Job — Chapter 22

1Then Eliphaz the Temanite answered and said:

2“Can a man be profitable to God? Surely he who is wise is profitable to himself.

3Is it any pleasure to the Almighty if you are in the right, or is it gain to him if you make your ways blameless?

4Is it for your fear of him that he reproves you and enters into judgment with you?

5Is not your evil abundant? There is no end to your iniquities.

6For you have exacted pledges of your brothers for nothing and stripped the naked of their clothing.

7You have given no water to the weary to drink, and you have withheld bread from the hungry.

8The man with power possessed the land, and the favored man lived in it.

9You have sent widows away empty, and the arms of the fatherless were crushed.

10Therefore snares are all around you, and sudden terror overwhelms you,

11or darkness, so that you cannot see, and a flood of water covers you.

12“Is not God high in the heavens? See the highest stars, how lofty they are!

13But you say, ‘What does God know? Can he judge through the deep darkness?

14Thick clouds veil him, so that he does not see, and he walks on the vault of heaven.’

15Will you keep to the old way that wicked men have trod?

16They were snatched away before their time; their foundation was washed away.

17They said to God, ‘Depart from us,’ and ‘What can the Almighty do to us?’

18Yet he filled their houses with good things— but the counsel of the wicked is far from me.

19The righteous see it and are glad; the innocent one mocks at them,

20saying, ‘Surely our adversaries are cut off, and what they left the fire has consumed.’

21“Agree with God, and be at peace; thereby good will come to you.

22Receive instruction from his mouth, and lay up his words in your heart.

23If you return to the Almighty you will be built up; if you remove injustice far from your tents,

24if you lay gold in the dust, and gold of Ophir among the stones of the torrent-bed,

25then the Almighty will be your gold and your precious silver.

26For then you will delight yourself in the Almighty and lift up your face to God.

27You will make your prayer to him, and he will hear you, and you will pay your vows.

28You will decide on a matter, and it will be established for you, and light will shine on your ways.

29For when they are humbled you say, ‘It is because of pride’; but he saves the lowly.

30He delivers even the one who is not innocent, who will be delivered through the cleanness of your hands.”

Summary

Eliphaz's third speech — abandons all subtlety and invents specific sins to accuse Job of: oppressing the poor, withholding bread from the hungry, and stripping the naked, then urges repentance.

Key Verses

v.5
"Is not your evil abundant? There is no end to your iniquities." (ESV) — Eliphaz's most brazen accusation. He has moved from implication to direct charge. This is the logical terminus of retribution theology: if suffering proves sin, then great suffering proves great sin.
v.12
"Is not God high in the heavens? See the highest stars, how lofty they are!" (ESV) — Eliphaz uses God's transcendence to argue that Job cannot hide from divine scrutiny. The irony: it is Eliphaz who fails to see clearly, not Job.
v.21
"Agree with God, and be at peace; thereby good will come unto you." (ESV) — Beautiful counsel tragically misapplied. Job already knows God — his suffering is not caused by distance from God but by a test of faith Job cannot see.
v.29
"For when they are humbled you say, 'It is because of pride'; but he saves the lowly." (ESV) — Eliphaz is not wrong that God saves the humble. He is wrong that Job is not humble. Ironically, this promise will be fulfilled in chapter 42.

Cross-References

  • v.2 — Psalm 16:2 — "I say to the LORD, 'You are my Lord; I have no good apart from you.'" (ESV) God is not enriched by human righteousness. v.5 — Job 1:8; 2:3 — God Himself declared Job blameless. Eliphaz contradicts God's own testimony. v.6 — Job 31:16-22 — Job's oath of innocence will systematically deny every charge Eliphaz raises here. v.7 — Isaiah 58:7 — "Is it not to share your bread with the hungry and bring the homeless poor into your house?" (ESV) True righteousness includes feeding the hungry and clothing the naked — precisely what Job did. v.12 — Psalm 139:7-12 — God is not hidden by distance or darkness; He sees all. Eliphaz's theology here is correct, but his accusation that Job denies this is false. v.21 — Psalm 46:10 — "Be still, and know that I am God." (ESV) The call to know God and find peace is genuine wisdom. v.29 — James 4:6; 1 Peter 5:5 — God gives grace to the humble.

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/job-22/ Guzik notes that Eliphaz's specific accusations are completely fabricated — there is no evidence anywhere in the book that Job committed these sins. Eliphaz has moved from theological argument to bearing false witness. His system requires Job to be guilty, so he invents the guilt. Guzik observes that vv.21-30 contain beautiful truths about knowing God, but they are cruelly misapplied to a righteous sufferer.
  • Charles Spurgeon: "Eliphaz has become a false accuser. When theology demands a sinner and reality provides none, the theologian may manufacture one. This is the peril of system over Spirit — when our framework for understanding God becomes more real to us than the God we claim to understand. Eliphaz's call to repentance is exquisite in its language and poisonous in its application. Truth in the wrong context becomes a weapon."

Translations

  • v.3 — KJV: "makest thy ways perfect" / ESV: "make your ways blameless" — Same Hebrew root (tam) used to describe Job in 1:1. Eliphaz sarcastically questions whether Job's perfection matters to God.
  • v.8 — KJV: "mighty man" / ESV: "man with power" — Hebrew "ish zeroa" (man of the arm/strength). Eliphaz implies Job used his power to oppress rather than protect.
  • v.21 — KJV: "Acquaint now thyself with him" / ESV: "Agree with God" — Hebrew "hasken" can mean to be familiar with, to benefit from, or to be at peace with. The KJV emphasizes relationship; the ESV emphasizes submission.
  • v.24 — KJV: "lay up gold as dust" / ESV: "lay gold in the dust" — The ESV reading suggests abandoning trust in gold (laying it down), while KJV suggests gaining abundant gold. The Hebrew supports the ESV: if you abandon gold, God becomes your treasure (v.25).

Reflection

  • 1. False accusation is the fruit of rigid theology (vv.5-9). When your system for understanding God cannot accommodate reality, you will distort reality to protect the system. Eliphaz invented sins because his theology demanded them. Beware of any framework that requires you to deny what you can plainly see in order to maintain what you believe.
  • 2. Truth misapplied becomes cruelty (vv.21-30). Everything Eliphaz says about knowing God and finding peace is theologically accurate. But spoken to a man who already knows God and suffers without cause, these truths become weapons. Context determines whether truth heals or wounds. The right word at the wrong time is the wrong word.
  • 3. God's transcendence does not equal God's indifference (v.12). God is high — but He is not far. He is above the stars — but He sees every sparrow. Transcendence and intimacy coexist in the God of Scripture. Never confuse sovereignty with detachment.
  • 4. The call to repentance must follow actual sin (v.23). We cannot repent of sins we did not commit. Calling someone to repentance without evidence of sin is spiritual abuse dressed in pious language. Genuine pastoral care begins with listening, not accusing.
