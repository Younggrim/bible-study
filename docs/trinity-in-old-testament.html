The Trinity

Trinity in the Old Testament

Understanding

While the doctrine of the Trinity is fully revealed in the New Testament, its seeds are planted throughout the Old Testament. From the very first chapter of Genesis, God speaks in the plural: "Let us make mankind in our image." This plurality within unity hints at the multi-personal nature of God that would later be revealed as Father, Son, and Holy Spirit.

The Old Testament contains numerous passages where God appears to speak to another divine person, where the Spirit of God acts as a distinct agent, and where mysterious figures — like the Angel of the Lord — receive worship reserved for God alone. These passages puzzled ancient interpreters but make perfect sense in light of the Trinity.

Isaiah 48:16 is particularly striking: "And now the Sovereign LORD has sent me, with his Spirit." Here a divine speaker distinguishes Himself from both the LORD who sends and the Spirit who accompanies — a clearly Trinitarian structure embedded in the Hebrew prophet's oracle. The Trinity did not begin at Pentecost; it was simply fully revealed there.

Key Scriptures

  • Genesis 1:26 — "Let us make mankind in our image, in our likeness" — God speaks in plural.
  • Isaiah 48:16 — "And now the Sovereign LORD has sent me, with his Spirit" — a Trinitarian statement from Isaiah.
  • Genesis 18:1-2 — "The LORD appeared to Abraham near the great trees of Mamre" — a theophany revealing God's nature.
  • Psalm 110:1 — "The LORD says to my lord: Sit at my right hand" — David recognizes two distinct persons.
  • Isaiah 63:10 — "They rebelled and grieved his Holy Spirit" — the Spirit presented as a distinct person who can be grieved.

Significance for Us

Recognizing the Trinity in the Old Testament shows us that God has always been Triune — Father, Son, and Spirit working together in creation, revelation, and redemption from the very beginning. The Old Testament is not a different God from the New but the same Triune God progressively revealing His nature across redemptive history.

This also strengthens our confidence in the unity of Scripture. The same divine community that creates in Genesis, speaks through prophets in Isaiah, and appears to Abraham in Mamre is the same Trinity that incarnates in Christ, descends at Pentecost, and indwells believers today. The Bible tells one coherent story of one Triune God's love for His creation.
