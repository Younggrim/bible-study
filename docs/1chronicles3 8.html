Summary

The royal line of David is documented, listing his sons born in Hebron and Jerusalem, then tracing the Davidic dynasty through Solomon to the exile and beyond. This chapter preserves the messianic lineage through the post-exilic generations.

Key Verses

  • v.1 — "These are the sons of David who were born to him in Hebron: the firstborn, Amnon" (ESV) — David's house begins in Hebron, where he reigned over Judah alone for seven years. The sons listed here include both glory (Solomon) and tragedy (Amnon, Absalom, Adonijah — all involved in violence and rebellion). David's house was great but not without grief.
  • v.5 — "Shimea, Shobab, Nathan and Solomon, four by Bath-shua, the daughter of Ammiel" (ESV) — Bath-shua here is Bathsheba (Bath-sheba = "daughter of the oath"; Bath-shua = "daughter of wealth/nobility"). Note that the Chronicler records her without scandal — no mention of Uriah, no mention of adultery. The Chronicler focuses on the legitimate royal line, not the sin that preceded it. Nathan's line is significant: Luke's genealogy of Jesus traces through Nathan, not Solomon (Luke 3:31). vv.10-14 — "The son of Solomon was Rehoboam, Abijah his son... Amon his son, Josiah his son." (ESV) — Twenty-one kings compressed into five verses. The entire history of the divided monarchy — its glories, its failures, its revivals and apostasies — is reduced to a list of names. What matters to the Chronicler is not the individual reigns but the unbroken succession: the promise to David (2 Samuel 7) was kept generation after generation.
  • v.17 — "and the sons of Jeconiah, the captive: Shealtiel his son" (ESV) — "The captive" — two devastating words that summarize the exile. Jeconiah (also called Jehoiachin or Coniah) was carried to Babylon in 597 BC. Jeremiah cursed his line: "no man of his offspring shall succeed in sitting on the throne of David" (Jeremiah 22:30, ESV). Yet the line continues. The curse applies to physical kingship, but God's promise finds fulfillment through another path — ultimately in Christ.
  • v.19 — "and the sons of Pedaiah: Zerubbabel and Shimei" (ESV) — Zerubbabel is the hero of the return. He led the first wave of exiles back to Jerusalem (Ezra 2:2) and rebuilt the Temple altar (Ezra 3:2). He is called "my servant" by God (Haggai 2:23) and given the signet ring — reversing Jeconiah's curse. For the Chronicler's audience, Zerubbabel was living proof that David's house endured.

Cross-References

  • v.1 → 2 Samuel 3:2-5 — The parallel list of David's sons born in Hebron. 2 Samuel gives more detail about the political context; Chronicles provides the official royal record.
  • v.5 → 2 Samuel 11-12 — The Bathsheba narrative (not mentioned by the Chronicler). The omission is deliberate: Chronicles focuses on David as the ideal king and Temple-planner, not his moral failures. The sin is real but not the point of this record.
  • v.5 → Luke 3:31 — "the son of Nathan, the son of David." (ESV) Jesus' genealogy through Mary traces through Nathan, not Solomon. Both Nathan and Solomon are sons of Bathsheba (v.5), so both lines have Davidic legitimacy. This resolves the Jeconiah curse: Jesus inherits David's throne through a non-Solomonic line.
  • v.10-14 → Matthew 1:7-11 — Matthew's genealogy of Jesus traces through the Solomonic kings listed here. Matthew's purpose is legal kingship (through Joseph); Luke's is biological descent (through Mary via Nathan).
  • v.17 → Jeremiah 22:24-30 — The curse on Coniah/Jeconiah: "Write this man down as childless... for none of his offspring shall succeed in sitting on the throne of David" (ESV). The Chronicler's listing of Jeconiah's sons shows the curse did not mean literal childlessness but exclusion from reigning.
  • v.19 → Ezra 3:2; Haggai 2:20-23 — Zerubbabel as the builder of the Second Temple and recipient of God's promise: "I will take you, O Zerubbabel... and make you like a signet ring." (ESV) The signet ring that was removed from Jeconiah (Jeremiah 22:24) is restored to his descendant.
  • v.19 → Matthew 1:12 — "and after the deportation to Babylon: Jechoniah was the father of Shealtiel, and Shealtiel the father of Zerubbabel" (ESV). Matthew traces Jesus' legal line through this very succession, placing Christ as heir to David's throne through the post-exilic line.

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): "https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/1-chronicles-3/" target="_blank">enduringword.com Guzik notes the tension between Jeremiah's curse on Jeconiah and the continuation of the Davidic line. He emphasizes that the Chronicler's inclusion of post-exilic descendants (vv.17-24) was an act of theological hope — declaring that God's promise to David survived even the catastrophe of exile. The line endured, and hope for a future king remained alive.
  • Charles Spurgeon: "Here is David's house — born in Hebron, expanded in Jerusalem, carried to Babylon, and yet surviving still. What power on earth could destroy a line that God Himself had sworn to preserve? Not the sins of Solomon, not the wickedness of Manasseh, not the flames of Nebuchadnezzar's anger could extinguish this lamp. God said to David, 'Thy throne shall be established for ever,' and here in this little genealogy we see the receipt of that promise — generation after generation, through exile and return, the house of David stands. And it stands still, for Jesus Christ, the Son of David, reigns eternally."

Translations

  • v.1 → KJV: "Daniel" / ESV: "Daniel" — This is David's second son, called "Chileab" in 2 Samuel 3:3. The reason for the different name is uncertain — "Daniel" may have been a throne name or the birth name while "Chileab" was a nickname. Both texts agree this was Abigail's son.
  • v.5 → KJV: "Bath-shua the daughter of Ammiel" / ESV: "Bath-shua, the daughter of Ammiel" — "Bath-shua" is a variant of "Bathsheba" (bath-sheba). "Ammiel" is "Eliam" reversed (both mean "my people is God" / "God is my people"). In 2 Samuel 11:3, she is "Bath-sheba the daughter of Eliam." The Chronicler uses alternate forms of both names.
  • v.15 → KJV: "Shallum" / ESV: "Shallum" — This is Josiah's fourth son, likely the same as Jehoahaz who reigned briefly (2 Kings 23:30-34). "Shallum" was his birth name; "Jehoahaz" his throne name. Despite being fourth-born, he reigned before his older brother Jehoiakim — chosen by the people.
  • v.17 → KJV: "Assir, Salathiel his son" / ESV: "Shealtiel his son" — KJV reads "Assir" as a proper name (a son of Jeconiah). ESV reads the Hebrew "assir" as "the captive" — a description of Jeconiah's status, not a son's name. The ESV reading is supported by the context and most modern scholarship: "the sons of Jeconiah, the captive: Shealtiel."
  • v.19 → Note: A genealogical puzzle exists here. Ezra 3:2 and Matthew 1:12 call Zerubbabel "son of Shealtiel," but this verse makes him son of Pedaiah (Shealtiel's brother). The most common explanation is levirate marriage: Shealtiel died childless, and Pedaiah raised up seed in his brother's name (Deuteronomy 25:5-6). Thus Zerubbabel was biologically Pedaiah's son but legally Shealtiel's.

Reflection

  • 1. God keeps His promises through catastrophe (vv.10-19). From Solomon to Zerubbabel, the Davidic line passes through the worst disaster in Israel's history — the exile. Kings become captives, palaces become prisons. Yet the line survives. God's promises are not cancelled by circumstances, no matter how devastating. If you feel your life has passed through its own "exile," know that God's purposes for you have not been destroyed — only redirected.
  • 2. The Chronicler focuses on grace, not disgrace (v.5). Bathsheba is recorded as a legitimate wife and mother of four royal sons — no mention of adultery or Uriah. This is not denial but theological perspective: God's grace truly covers sin. Forgiven sin does not define you. If God has forgiven your past, you are not obligated to be defined by it forever.
  • 3. Hope persists in the darkest times (v.17). "Jeconiah, the captive" — yet he has sons. A cursed king in a Babylonian prison still becomes a father. Life continues. God's plan continues. Even in your lowest, most confined, most "cursed" moments, God may be preparing the next generation of blessing through you.
  • 4. God resolves impossible contradictions (vv.17-19). How can Jeconiah be cursed from kingship AND David's throne be eternal? The answer unfolds across centuries — through Nathan's line, through levirate marriage, through the mystery of incarnation. God is not limited by our either/or logic. Trust Him with the contradictions in your life that seem unresolvable.
  • 5. Obscure faithfulness matters (vv.19-24). The post-exilic names in verses 19-24 are virtually unknown — Hashubah, Ohel, Berechiah, Hasadiah. Yet they preserved the Davidic line in obscurity until the fullness of time. Your faithfulness in obscurity may be preserving something precious for a future generation you will never see.
