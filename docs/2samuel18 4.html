2 Samuel — Chapter 18

1Then David mustered the men who were with him and set over them commanders of thousands and commanders of hundreds.

2And David sent out the army, one third under the command of Joab, one third under the command of Abishai the son of Zeruiah, Joab’s brother, and one third under the command of Ittai the Gittite. And the king said to the men, “I myself will also go out with you.”

3But the men said, “You shall not go out. For if we flee, they will not care about us. If half of us die, they will not care about us. But you are worth ten thousand of us. Therefore it is better that you send us help from the city.”

4The king said to them, “Whatever seems best to you I will do.” So the king stood at the side of the gate, while all the army marched out by hundreds and by thousands.

5And the king ordered Joab and Abishai and Ittai, “Deal gently for my sake with the young man Absalom.” And all the people heard when the king gave orders to all the commanders about Absalom.

6So the army went out into the field against Israel, and the battle was fought in the forest of Ephraim.

7And the men of Israel were defeated there by the servants of David, and the loss there was great on that day, twenty thousand men.

8The battle spread over the face of all the country, and the forest devoured more people that day than the sword.

9And Absalom happened to meet the servants of David. Absalom was riding on his mule, and the mule went under the thick branches of a great oak, and his head caught fast in the oak, and he was suspended between heaven and earth, while the mule that was under him went on.

10And a certain man saw it and told Joab, “Behold, I saw Absalom hanging in an oak.”

11Joab said to the man who told him, “What, you saw him! Why then did you not strike him there to the ground? I would have been glad to give you ten pieces of silver and a belt.”

12But the man said to Joab, “Even if I felt in my hand the weight of a thousand pieces of silver, I would not reach out my hand against the king’s son, for in our hearing the king commanded you and Abishai and Ittai, ‘For my sake protect the young man Absalom.’

13On the other hand, if I had dealt treacherously against his life (and there is nothing hidden from the king), then you yourself would have stood aloof.”

14Joab said, “I will not waste time like this with you.” And he took three javelins in his hand and thrust them into the heart of Absalom while he was still alive in the oak.

15And ten young men, Joab’s armor-bearers, surrounded Absalom and struck him and killed him.

16Then Joab blew the trumpet, and the troops came back from pursuing Israel, for Joab restrained them.

17And they took Absalom and threw him into a great pit in the forest and raised over him a very great heap of stones. And all Israel fled every one to his own home.

18Now Absalom in his lifetime had taken and set up for himself the pillar that is in the King’s Valley, for he said, “I have no son to keep my name in remembrance.” He called the pillar after his own name, and it is called Absalom’s monument to this day.

19Then Ahimaaz the son of Zadok said, “Let me run and carry news to the king that the LORD has delivered him from the hand of his enemies.”

20And Joab said to him, “You are not to carry news today. You may carry news another day, but today you shall carry no news, because the king’s son is dead.”

21Then Joab said to the Cushite, “Go, tell the king what you have seen.” The Cushite bowed before Joab, and ran.

22Then Ahimaaz the son of Zadok said again to Joab, “Come what may, let me also run after the Cushite.” And Joab said, “Why will you run, my son, seeing that you will have no reward for the news?”

23“Come what may,” he said, “I will run.” So he said to him, “Run.” Then Ahimaaz ran by the way of the plain, and outran the Cushite.

24Now David was sitting between the two gates, and the watchman went up to the roof of the gate by the wall, and when he lifted up his eyes and looked, he saw a man running alone.

25The watchman called out and told the king. And the king said, “If he is alone, there is news in his mouth.” And he drew nearer and nearer.

26The watchman saw another man running. And the watchman called to the gate and said, “See, another man running alone!” The king said, “He also brings news.”

27The watchman said, “I think the running of the first is like the running of Ahimaaz the son of Zadok.” And the king said, “He is a good man and comes with good news.”

28Then Ahimaaz cried out to the king, “All is well.” And he bowed before the king with his face to the earth and said, “Blessed be the LORD your God, who has delivered up the men who raised their hand against my lord the king.”

29And the king said, “Is it well with the young man Absalom?” Ahimaaz answered, “When Joab sent the king’s servant, your servant, I saw a great commotion, but I do not know what it was.”

30And the king said, “Turn aside and stand here.” So he turned aside and stood still.

31And behold, the Cushite came, and the Cushite said, “Good news for my lord the king! For the LORD has delivered you this day from the hand of all who rose up against you.”

32The king said to the Cushite, “Is it well with the young man Absalom?” And the Cushite answered, “May the enemies of my lord the king and all who rise up against you for evil be like that young man.”

33And the king was deeply moved and went up to the chamber over the gate and wept. And as he went, he said, “O my son Absalom, my son, my son Absalom! Would I had died instead of you, O Absalom, my son, my son!”

Summary

David's forces defeat Absalom's army in the forest of Ephraim; Absalom's hair catches in an oak and Joab kills him despite David's orders. David's agonized cry: "O my son Absalom! Would God I had died for thee!"

Key Verses

v.5
"Deal gently for my sake with the young man, even with Absalom." — David's impossible command reveals his divided heart. As king, he must defeat the rebellion; as father, he cannot bear to lose his son. This tension is the chapter's emotional engine. David's love for Absalom — despite everything — reflects something of God's love for rebellious humanity: a love that desires mercy even when justice demands punishment.
v.8
"The wood devoured more people that day than the sword devoured." — Creation itself fights against those who rebel against God's anointed king. The forest becomes God's instrument of judgment — an echo of how nature serves God's purposes throughout Scripture (the Red Sea, the Jordan, the storm on Jonah's ship). God needs no army when He has a forest.
v.14
"And he took three darts in his hand, and thrust them through the heart of Absalom, while he was yet alive in the midst of the oak." — Joab's brutal pragmatism. He knows that as long as Absalom lives, the rebellion can reignite. Joab serves David's kingdom but defies David's heart. This act will cost Joab eventually (1 Kings 2:5-6), but in the moment, it ends the war.
v.33
"O my son Absalom, my son, my son Absalom! would God I had died for you, O Absalom, my son, my son!" — The most heartbreaking lament in the Old Testament. David wishes he could have died in Absalom's place — a substitutionary desire that foreshadows the gospel. David could not die for his son, but God the Father sent His Son to die for rebels. What David wished, God accomplished in Christ.
v.18
"I have no son to keep my name in remembrance." — The tragic irony of Absalom's life. He had sons (14:27) who apparently died young. He built a monument to preserve his name, but his actual burial is a criminal's pit under a heap of stones. Pride builds monuments; God determines legacies.

Cross-References

Galatians 3:13 applies this to Christ, who bore the curse for us. v.14 — 1 Kings 2:5-6 — David's deathbed instruction to Solomon regarding Joab's bloodshed — including this act. v.17 — Joshua 7:26 — Achan buried under a heap of stones. The burial of the accursed and the rebel. v.18 — 2 Samuel 14:27 — Absalom had three sons and a daughter. His sons apparently died, leaving no heir — hence the memorial pillar. v.33 — Romans 5:8 — "While we were yet sinners, Christ died for us." David wished to die for his rebel son; God actually sent His Son to die for rebel sinners. v.33 — Genesis 37:35 — Jacob's grief for Joseph: "I will go down into the grave to my son mourning." Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/2-samuel-18/ Guzik notes that David's grief, while understandable as a father, was also problematic as a king. His excessive mourning demoralized the army that had just risked their lives for him (as Joab will point out in chapter 19). Guzik also observes that Absalom's death in the oak — suspended between heaven and earth — pictures a man who belonged to neither realm: rejected by heaven for his rebellion, removed from earth by God's judgment.
  • Charles Spurgeon: "David's cry, 'Would God I had died for you,' is the language of a father's love, but it is also the language of impossibility. David could not die for Absalom — no sinful father can atone for a sinful son. But there is One who could and did: the Lord Jesus Christ, who hung upon a tree not for His own rebellion but for ours. What David wished in vain, God accomplished in grace. The cross is the place where a Father's love and a rebel's need meet — and where substitution becomes reality, not merely a father's desperate wish."

Translations

  • v.3 — KJV: "thou succour us out of the city" / ESV: "you send us help from the city" — Hebrew "la'azor" (to help, support). KJV "succour" is archaic English for "help" or "aid" (from Latin succurrere, "to run to help"). ESV modernizes clearly. The idea is that David's strategic value is greater as a reserve commander than as a frontline fighter.
  • v.8 — KJV: "the wood devoured more people" / ESV: "the forest devoured more people" — Hebrew "ya'ar." Both "wood" and "forest" are valid translations. KJV uses "wood" in the older English sense of a forested area (not timber). ESV "forest" is clearer for modern readers. The verb "devoured" (Hebrew "akal" = to eat, consume) personifies the forest as a predator.
  • v.9 — KJV: "his head caught hold of the oak" / ESV: "his head caught fast in the oak" — Hebrew "vayechezaq rosho ba'elah" = literally "his head was strong/seized in the oak." The text says "head," not "hair" — though tradition (and 14:26's description of Absalom's heavy hair) has led many to assume his hair was involved. Both translations faithfully render "head." The Hebrew verb "chazaq" means to be firm, to seize, to hold fast.
  • v.14 — KJV: "three darts" / ESV: "three javelins" — Hebrew "shevatim" = rods, staffs, or shafts. KJV "darts" suggests thrown weapons; ESV "javelins" suggests thrusting weapons. The Hebrew is ambiguous — the word can mean either. Given that Joab "thrust them" (Hebrew "taqah" = to drive in, thrust), ESV's "javelins" better fits the action described.
  • v.22 — KJV: "thou hast no tidings ready" / ESV: "you will have no reward for the news" — Hebrew "ein lekha besorah motse'th" is ambiguous. KJV reads it as "you have no [good] news to bring." ESV reads it as "you will find no reward [for this news]." The ESV interpretation suggests Joab knows David will not reward the bearer of Absalom's death. Both readings are grammatically possible; ESV captures Joab's pragmatic concern.
  • v.33 — KJV: "would God I had died for thee" / ESV: "Would I had died instead of you" — Hebrew "mi-yitten muthi ani tachtekha" = literally "who will give my dying, I, in your place?" KJV's "would God" is an English idiom expressing a wish (not a direct reference to God). ESV's "Would I had died instead of you" is more literal. The Hebrew "tachtekha" (in your place, instead of you) is explicitly substitutionary language.

Reflection

  • 1. A father's love cannot save a rebel son (v.33). David's grief is real and devastating, but his love — however deep — could not prevent Absalom's choices or their consequences. Parents can love perfectly and still lose children to rebellion. But what David could not do, God did: He sent His own Son to die in the place of rebels. The gospel answers David's impossible wish.
  • 2. God uses unexpected instruments (v.8). The forest killed more than the sword. God is not limited to conventional means. He fights for His people through circumstances, terrain, timing, and providence — not only through human strength. When we face overwhelming odds, God has resources we cannot see.
  • 3. Disobedience to authority has consequences, even when it seems pragmatic (v.14). Joab killed Absalom against David's direct order. Politically, it was the right move — Absalom alive meant ongoing civil war. But Joab's defiance of his king eventually cost him his life (1 Kings 2:5-6). Pragmatism that violates God-given authority is still sin, even when the results seem beneficial.
  • 4. Pride builds monuments that God tears down (v.18). Absalom built a pillar to immortalize his name. God buried him in an unmarked pit under stones. The contrast is devastating: human attempts at self-exaltation end in divine humiliation. "God resisteth the proud, but giveth grace unto the humble" (James 4:6).
  • 5. Victory without peace is hollow (v.33). David won the battle but lost his son. The army's triumph brought the king only tears. This reminds us that earthly victories — career success, political wins, personal vindication — mean nothing if they cost us the people we love. The deepest human longing is not for triumph but for reconciliation.
