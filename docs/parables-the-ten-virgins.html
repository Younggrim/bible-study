Parables of Jesus

The Ten Virgins

The Parable

Jesus told this parable in the context of His teaching about the end times, immediately following His warnings about the day and hour that no one knows. "Then the kingdom of heaven will be like ten virgins who took their lamps and went to meet the bridegroom." In Jewish wedding custom, bridesmaids would wait with lit lamps to escort the bridegroom in a joyful procession to the wedding feast.

Five were wise and five were foolish. The foolish took their lamps but brought no extra oil. The wise brought flasks of oil along with their lamps. When the bridegroom was delayed, they all became drowsy and fell asleep. At midnight a cry rang out: "Here is the bridegroom! Come out to meet him!" All ten virgins woke and trimmed their lamps — but the foolish discovered their lamps were going out.

"Give us some of your oil," they begged the wise. But the wise replied: "There will not be enough for us and for you. Go to the dealers and buy for yourselves." While they were gone buying oil, the bridegroom came. Those who were ready went in with him to the wedding feast, and the door was shut. Later the foolish virgins arrived crying, "Lord, lord, open to us!" But he answered: "Truly, I say to you, I do not know you." Jesus concluded: "Watch therefore, for you know neither the day nor the hour."

Key Scriptures

  • Matthew 25:13 — "Watch therefore, for you know neither the day nor the hour."
  • 1 Thessalonians 5:2-6 — The day of the Lord comes like a thief; let us not sleep but stay awake and sober.
  • Revelation 3:3 — "If you will not wake up, I will come like a thief, and you will not know at what hour I will come."
  • Mark 13:35-37 — "Stay awake — you do not know when the master of the house will come."
  • Luke 12:40 — "You also must be ready, for the Son of Man is coming at an hour you do not expect."

What Jesus Is Teaching

Jesus is teaching that genuine spiritual readiness cannot be borrowed, faked, or acquired at the last minute. All ten virgins looked identical at the start — they all had lamps, they all went out to meet the bridegroom, they all fell asleep during the delay. The difference was invisible until the critical moment: the wise had oil; the foolish did not. Oil in Scripture often symbolizes the Holy Spirit and genuine, living faith. External religious appearance (having a lamp) is not enough — the inner reality (oil) determines who enters the feast.

The parable also warns that there comes a point when the door shuts and opportunity ends. The bridegroom's delay tests genuine readiness. The foolish virgins assumed the bridegroom would come on their schedule, or that they could prepare at the last minute. Both assumptions proved fatal. Jesus is urging His followers: don't presume on delay. Don't assume there will always be more time. Live today with the kind of authentic, oil-filled faith that is ready whenever He comes — whether that's tonight or in a thousand years. Readiness is not a last-minute act but a daily posture.

Reflection Questions

  • If Christ returned tonight, would you be among the wise or the foolish? What gives you confidence — or concern — about your answer?
  • What does "oil" represent in your spiritual life? Are you cultivating a genuine, personal relationship with God, or relying on borrowed faith?
  • How does the bridegroom's delay test your faith? Are there areas where you've grown spiritually drowsy or complacent?
  • What practical steps can you take to "keep your lamp trimmed" — maintaining spiritual readiness in the midst of daily life?
