Parables of Jesus

The Ten Virgins

The Parable

Jesus said the Kingdom of heaven would be like ten virgins who took their lamps and went to meet the bridegroom. In first-century Jewish weddings, the bridesmaids would wait at the bride's home for the groom's arrival — which could come at any hour, often late at night. Five of these virgins were wise and five were foolish. The foolish took their lamps but no extra oil; the wise took flasks of oil along with their lamps.

The bridegroom was delayed, and they all became drowsy and slept. At midnight, a cry rang out: "Here is the bridegroom! Come out to meet him." All ten woke and trimmed their lamps, but the foolish discovered theirs were going out — they had no oil. They asked the wise to share, but the wise replied, "There will not be enough for us and for you. Go to the dealers and buy for yourselves." While they went to buy, the bridegroom came, and those who were ready went in with him to the marriage feast, and the door was shut.

When the foolish virgins finally returned, they cried, "Lord, lord, open to us." But he answered, "Truly, I say to you, I do not know you." Jesus concluded: "Watch therefore, for you know neither the day nor the hour." The shut door is final — there is a point past which preparation is no longer possible.

Key Scriptures

  • Matthew 25:1-13 — The full parable: ten lamps, a delayed bridegroom, and a door that shuts permanently.
  • 1 Thessalonians 5:2-6 — "The day of the Lord will come like a thief in the night... So then let us not sleep, as others do, but let us keep awake."
  • Revelation 3:3 — "If you will not wake up, I will come like a thief, and you will not know at what hour I will come against you."
  • Matthew 24:42-44 — "Stay awake, for you do not know on what day your Lord is coming... You also must be ready."
  • Romans 13:11-12 — "The night is far gone; the day is at hand. So then let us cast off the works of darkness and put on the armor of light."

What Jesus Was Teaching

Jesus was teaching His disciples about the urgency of spiritual readiness. The oil represents genuine, personal relationship with God — something that cannot be borrowed, shared, or acquired at the last minute. The wise virgins were not smarter; they simply prepared in advance for what they knew was coming. Spiritual readiness is not a crisis-moment achievement but a daily cultivation. You cannot cram for eternity the night before. The inner life — prayer, faith, obedience, intimacy with Christ — must be nurtured long before it is tested.

The delay of the bridegroom is also significant. Jesus knew His return would not be immediate, and He warned that the waiting period would tempt people to drift into spiritual sleep. Both groups fell asleep — the difference was not in their alertness but in their preparation. When the moment came, the wise were ready because they had made prior provision. This parable is a loving but urgent call: do not assume you have unlimited time. The door will close. Be ready now, today, because the bridegroom's arrival is certain — only the timing is unknown.

Reflection Questions

  • If Christ returned tonight, would you be among those with oil in your lamp — or would you be scrambling to prepare?
  • What does "keeping oil in your lamp" look like practically — what daily habits sustain your spiritual readiness?
  • Are there areas of your spiritual life where you are coasting on borrowed faith — relying on others' relationship with God rather than cultivating your own?
  • How does the certainty of Christ's return — combined with the uncertainty of timing — change how you live today?
