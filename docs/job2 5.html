Job — Chapter 2

1Again there was a day when the sons of God came to present themselves before the LORD, and Satan also came among them to present himself before the LORD.

2And the LORD said to Satan, “From where have you come?” Satan answered the LORD and said, “From going to and fro on the earth, and from walking up and down on it.”

3And the LORD said to Satan, “Have you considered my servant Job, that there is none like him on the earth, a blameless and upright man, who fears God and turns away from evil? He still holds fast his integrity, although you incited me against him to destroy him without reason.”

4Then Satan answered the LORD and said, “Skin for skin! All that a man has he will give for his life.

5But stretch out your hand and touch his bone and his flesh, and he will curse you to your face.”

6And the LORD said to Satan, “Behold, he is in your hand; only spare his life.”

7So Satan went out from the presence of the LORD and struck Job with loathsome sores from the sole of his foot to the crown of his head.

8And he took a piece of broken pottery with which to scrape himself while he sat in the ashes.

9Then his wife said to him, “Do you still hold fast your integrity? Curse God and die.”

10But he said to her, “You speak as one of the foolish women would speak. Shall we receive good from God, and shall we not receive evil?” In all this Job did not sin with his lips.

11Now when Job’s three friends heard of all this evil that had come upon him, they came each from his own place, Eliphaz the Temanite, Bildad the Shuhite, and Zophar the Naamathite. They made an appointment together to come to show him sympathy and comfort him.

12And when they saw him from a distance, they did not recognize him. And they raised their voices and wept, and they tore their robes and sprinkled dust on their heads toward heaven.

13And they sat with him on the ground seven days and seven nights, and no one spoke a word to him, for they saw that his suffering was very great.

Summary

Satan's second challenge — God permits Satan to strike Job's body with boils; Job's wife urges him to curse God and die, but Job refuses, and three friends arrive to mourn in silence.

Key Verses

  • v.3 — "Have you considered my servant Job... He still holds fast his integrity, although you incited me against him to destroy him without reason." (ESV) — God Himself declares Job's suffering is "without cause." This demolishes the friends' entire argument before they speak a word. Suffering is not always punishment for sin.
  • v.4 — "Skin for skin! All that a man has he will give for his life." (ESV) — Satan's second accusation: Job only held on because his body was untouched. A man will sacrifice everything external to save his own skin. This proverb assumes all human motivation is ultimately selfish.
  • v.9 — "Do you still hold fast your integrity? Curse God and die." (ESV) — She urges the very thing Satan predicted. Augustine called her "the devil's assistant." Yet she too has lost everything — ten children, wealth, her husband's health. Her counsel may spring from despair rather than malice.
  • v.10 — "You speak as one of the foolish women would speak. Shall we receive good from God, and shall we not receive evil? In all this Job did not sin with his lips." (ESV) — Job's theology of sovereignty: God is the source of both good and adversity. Accepting one while rejecting the other is inconsistent. Note: "did not sin with his lips" — the struggle within may be fierce, but Job does not verbalize blasphemy.
  • v.13 — "And they sat with him on the ground seven days and seven nights, and no one spoke a word to him, for they saw that his suffering was very great." (ESV) — The friends' finest moment. Silent presence in suffering is an act of love. Their error begins when they open their mouths.

Cross-References

  • v.3 → Job 1:1 — God repeats the exact description of Job from the opening verse, adding "He still holds fast his integrity." Satan's first test failed completely.
  • v.3 → Job 42:7 — God later condemns the friends for not speaking "right" about Him — but never condemns Job for his complaints.
  • v.4 → Matthew 16:26 — "For what will it profit a man if he gains the whole world and forfeits his soul?" (ESV) — Jesus reverses Satan's proverb — the soul matters more than the skin.
  • v.7 → Isaiah 1:6 — "From the sole of the foot even to the head, there is no soundness in it, but bruises and sores and raw wounds." (ESV) — Isaiah's description of Israel echoes Job's condition.
  • v.9 → Job 1:11; 2:5 — Satan's prediction that Job would "curse you to your face" is echoed by Job's wife. She becomes Satan's unwitting mouthpiece.
  • v.10 → Isaiah 45:7 — "I form light and create darkness; I make well-being and create calamity." (ESV) — God's sovereignty over both good and adversity.
  • v.13 → Ezekiel 3:15 — Ezekiel sat among the exiles seven days "astonished" — the same period of silent grief.
  • v.13 → Romans 12:15 — "Weep with those who weep." (ESV) — The friends initially fulfill this command perfectly.

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): "https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/job-2/" target="_blank">enduringword.com Guzik emphasizes that God's statement "without cause" (v.3) is the interpretive key to the entire book. Every argument the friends make assumes Job suffers because of sin — but God has already declared there is no cause. Guzik also notes that Job's wife has lost everything Job has lost — she is a fellow sufferer who has reached her breaking point. Her counsel is wrong, but her pain is real. The seven days of silence represent the friends at their best — "when they saw that his grief was very great" they had the wisdom to simply be present.
  • Charles Spurgeon: "Job's wife was not altogether a bad woman, but she was a weak one. She could not endure to see her husband suffer. Her remedy was worse than the disease: 'Curse God, and die.' But notice Job's answer: he does not curse her, does not dismiss her, does not rage at her. He corrects her gently: 'Thou speakest as one of the foolish women.' In his deepest agony, Job maintained both his faith and his kindness. This is the mark of genuine grace — it does not lash out at those who fail us in our hour of need."

Translations

  • v.3 → KJV: "thou movedst me against him, to destroy him without cause" / ESV: "you incited me against him to destroy him without reason" — Hebrew "suth" means to incite or entice. The remarkable admission is that God attributes the incitement to Satan while acknowledging He Himself acted. Both translations capture this tension between divine sovereignty and satanic instigation.
  • v.4 → KJV: "Skin for skin" / ESV: "Skin for skin" — An ancient proverb whose exact meaning is debated. Most likely: a man will give up someone else's skin (property, family) to save his own skin. The meaning is clear from context: Job sacrificed his children's lives to preserve his own body. Now test the body itself.
  • v.7 → KJV: "sore boils" / ESV: "loathsome sores" — Hebrew "shechiyn" refers to inflamed, ulcerous skin conditions. The same word describes the plague on Egypt (Exodus 9:9) and the curse of Deuteronomy 28:35. ESV "loathsome" adds the element of revulsion that the Hebrew implies.
  • v.9 → KJV: "curse God, and die" / ESV: "Curse God and die" — Again the Hebrew uses the euphemism "barak" (bless) for curse. Both translations correctly interpret the euphemism. The imperative "die" may mean: cursing God will bring death as punishment, ending your suffering.
  • v.10 → KJV: "shall we not receive evil?" / ESV: "shall we not receive evil?" — Hebrew "ra" can mean moral evil or adversity/calamity. Job is not attributing moral evil to God but acknowledging that adversity comes from God's hand. Context demands "adversity" or "calamity" rather than moral wickedness.

Reflection

  • 1. Satan's strategy escalates (vv.4-5). When external loss fails to break faith, the enemy moves to personal suffering. Be prepared: if you have survived loss without losing faith, deeper testing may follow. Each test overcome invites a harder test — not because God is cruel, but because He is proving something magnificent about grace.
  • 2. "Without cause" changes everything (v.3). If you are suffering and cannot identify why, take comfort: God Himself may be saying "without cause" about your situation. Not all suffering is punishment. Some suffering is privilege — the privilege of proving that God is worth more than what He gives.
  • 3. Be careful whom you listen to in crisis (v.9). Job's wife gave the worst possible counsel in the worst possible moment. Those closest to you may not have the spiritual resources to sustain you. Their pain may produce counsel that would destroy you. Love them, but test their words against truth.
  • 4. Receiving good AND adversity from God (v.10). Job's theology is profoundly mature: if God is sovereign over blessings, He is sovereign over suffering. You cannot accept His hand in prosperity and reject His hand in pain. Both come from the same source — and that source is good.
  • 5. The ministry of silent presence (v.13). The friends' best ministry was their first seven days. They wept, they sat, they said nothing. Sometimes the most healing thing you can do for a suffering person is simply show up and stay. Words can wait. Presence cannot.
