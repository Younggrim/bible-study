Psalms — Chapter 73

1Truly God is good to Israel, to those who are pure in heart.

2But as for me, my feet had almost stumbled, my steps had nearly slipped.

3For I was envious of the arrogant when I saw the prosperity of the wicked.

4For they have no pangs until death; their bodies are fat and sleek.

5They are not in trouble as others are; they are not stricken like the rest of mankind.

6Therefore pride is their necklace; violence covers them as a garment.

7Their eyes swell out through fatness; their hearts overflow with follies.

8They scoff and speak with malice; loftily they threaten oppression.

9They set their mouths against the heavens, and their tongue struts through the earth.

10Therefore his people turn back to them, and find no fault in them.

11And they say, “How can God know? Is there knowledge in the Most High?”

12Behold, these are the wicked; always at ease, they increase in riches.

13All in vain have I kept my heart clean and washed my hands in innocence.

14For all the day long I have been stricken and rebuked every morning.

15If I had said, “I will speak thus,” I would have betrayed the generation of your children.

16But when I thought how to understand this, it seemed to me a wearisome task,

17until I went into the sanctuary of God; then I discerned their end.

18Truly you set them in slippery places; you make them fall to ruin.

19How they are destroyed in a moment, swept away utterly by terrors!

20Like a dream when one awakes, O Lord, when you rouse yourself, you despise them as phantoms.

21When my soul was embittered, when I was pricked in heart,

22I was brutish and ignorant; I was like a beast toward you.

23Nevertheless, I am continually with you; you hold my right hand.

24You guide me with your counsel, and afterward you will receive me to glory.

25Whom have I in heaven but you? And there is nothing on earth that I desire besides you.

26My flesh and my heart may fail, but God is the strength of my heart and my portion forever.

27For behold, those who are far from you shall perish; you put an end to everyone who is unfaithful to you.

28But for me it is good to be near God; I have made the Lord GOD my refuge, that I may tell of all your works.

Summary

A wisdom psalm of Asaph — opening Book III; the psalmist nearly stumbled envying the wicked's prosperity, until he entered God's sanctuary and understood their end; 'whom have I in heaven but thee?'

Key Verses

v.2-3
"But as for me, my feet were almost gone; my steps had well nigh slipped. For I was envious at the foolish, when I saw the prosperity of the wicked." (KJV) — Brutal honesty. A worship leader nearly lost his faith because the wicked were doing better than the righteous. This is the universal struggle of faith.
v.17
"Until I went into the sanctuary of God; then understood I their end." (KJV) — THE turning point. Everything changed in worship. The problem looked unsolvable UNTIL Asaph entered God's presence. Then he saw the END — their slippery destination, their sudden destruction. Perspective is everything.
v.25
"Whom have I in heaven but thee? and there is none upon earth that I desire beside thee." (KJV) — The greatest declaration of devotion in the OT. Having moved through envy to understanding, Asaph arrives at pure desire: God alone is enough. Nothing in heaven or earth compares.
v.26
"My flesh and my heart faileth: but God is the strength of my heart, and my portion for ever." (KJV) — Everything else will fail — body, emotions, circumstances. But God remains. "Portion" (cheleq) = inheritance, lot, share. God Himself is what Asaph inherits. Not God's gifts, but God Himself.
v.28
"But it is good for me to draw near to God." (KJV) — The psalm's final verdict. After wrestling with envy, doubt, and near-apostasy, the conclusion is simple: nearness to God is the supreme good. Not prosperity, not answers — but proximity.

Translations

  • v.4 — KJV: "there are no bands in their death" / ESV: "they have no pangs until death" — Hebrew is debated. KJV suggests the wicked die without pain or struggle; ESV similarly. The point: even their DEATHS are easy, adding to the righteous person's frustration.
  • v.22 — KJV: "I was as a beast before thee" / ESV: "I was like a beast toward you" — When Asaph envied the wicked, he was functioning at the level of an animal — reactive, instinctual, lacking eternal perspective. Only in the sanctuary did he recover his humanity.
  • v.24 — KJV: "afterward receive me to glory" / ESV: "afterward you will receive me to glory" — This may be the clearest OT expression of hope in an afterlife with God. "Glory" here points beyond death to eternal reception into God's presence.

Reflection

  • 1. It's normal to struggle with the prosperity of the wicked (vv.2-3). If even Asaph — the worship leader — nearly stumbled, you are not uniquely faithless for asking why the wicked thrive. The question is honest; just don't stop there.
  • 2. The sanctuary changes everything (v.17). What you cannot understand in isolation, you understand in worship. Get into God's presence. Perspective comes not from more thinking but from more worship. The answer to "why?" is found in "Who."
  • 3. God Himself is your portion (v.26). Not His blessings, His gifts, His answers — HIM. If everything else failed but you had God, you would have more than if everything succeeded without Him.
  • 4. Nearness to God is the definition of "good" (v.28). After wrestling with what is "good" (the wicked seem to have all the "good things"), Asaph redefines good: it is simply being near God. That is the supreme value. Everything else is negotiable.
  • 5. Think eschatologically (v.17). "Their END." The present chapter is not the whole story. The wicked prosper NOW; they perish THEN. Don't judge God's justice by chapter 3 when the book has 100 chapters.
