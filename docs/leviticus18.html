Summary

God forbids sexual immorality in all its forms — incest, adultery, homosexuality, and bestiality — distinguishing Israel from the surrounding nations.

Authorship & Background

Author: Moses. Leviticus is the third book of the Pentateuch, written during the wilderness period (approximately 1445-1405 BC). Hebrew title: "Vayikra" — "And He called." God speaks from the newly completed Tabernacle, giving Israel instructions for worship, sacrifice, and holy living.
Historical Context: Chapter 18 begins the section of Leviticus often called the "Holiness Code" (chapters 18-20, or more broadly 17-26). Having established the means of atonement (chapters 1-16) and the sanctity of blood (chapter 17), God now prescribes the holy life that must flow from atonement. The chapter deals with sexual ethics — specifically prohibited sexual relationships. The framing is crucial: "After the doings of the land of Egypt, wherein ye dwelt, shall ye not do: and after the doings of the land of Canaan, whither I bring you, shall ye not do" (v.3). Israel is coming FROM Egypt (where these practices were common) and going TO Canaan (where they were endemic). God's people must be different from both. The chapter lists prohibited relationships in careful detail (vv.6-23), then warns that the land itself is defiled by these practices and "vomiteth out her inhabitants" (v.25). The Canaanites are being expelled precisely because of these sins — and Israel will suffer the same fate if they follow the same path. The chapter establishes that sexual ethics are not culturally relative — they are grounded in God's character and enforced by the land itself.
The Framework: Neither Egypt Nor Canaan (vv.1-5): "I am the LORD your God" (v.2) — the basis of all that follows. God's identity grounds His commands. "After the doings of the land of Egypt... shall ye not do" (v.3) — what they came FROM. "After the doings of the land of Canaan... shall ye not do" (v.3) — what they are going TO. "Neither shall ye walk in their ordinances" (v.3). Instead: "Ye shall do my judgments, and keep mine ordinances" (v.4). The motivation: "which if a man do, he shall live in them: I am the LORD" (v.5). God's laws are life-giving, not restrictive. Obedience leads to life.
Prohibited Relationships — Incest (vv.6-18): The governing principle: "None of you shall approach to any that is near of kin to him, to uncover their nakedness" (v.6). "Uncover nakedness" = sexual relations. The list: mother (v.7), father's wife/stepmother (v.8), sister or half-sister (v.9), granddaughter (v.10), stepsister (v.11), father's sister/aunt (v.12), mother's sister/aunt (v.13), uncle's wife (v.14), daughter-in-law (v.15), brother's wife (v.16), a woman and her daughter or granddaughter (v.17), a wife's sister while the wife lives (v.18). Each prohibition protects family integrity and prevents exploitation of power dynamics within the household.
Other Sexual Prohibitions (vv.19-23): Sexual relations during menstruation (v.19) — connects to chapter 15. Adultery with a neighbor's wife (v.20). Child sacrifice to Molech (v.21) — placed among sexual sins because Molech worship involved ritual prostitution and the offering of children conceived in such unions. Homosexuality: "Thou shalt not lie with mankind, as with womankind: it is abomination" (v.22). Bestiality — for both men and women: "it is confusion" (KJV) / "it is perversion" (ESV) (v.23).
The Land Vomits Out Its Inhabitants (vv.24-30): "Defile not ye yourselves in any of these things" (v.24). The nations being driven out committed ALL these things (v.24). "The land is defiled: therefore I do visit the iniquity thereof upon it, and the land itself vomiteth out her inhabitants" (v.25). The land is personified — it cannot tolerate such defilement. God warns Israel: the land will vomit YOU out too if you practice these things (v.28). "Whosoever shall commit any of these abominations... shall be cut off" (v.29). Final charge: keep God's ordinances, do not commit these customs, do not defile yourselves: "I am the LORD your God" (v.30).

Map & Geography

  • Egypt (v.3): Land to the southwest; place of Israel's bondage and exodus.

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): enduringword.com Guzik emphasizes the framing: Israel must be different from both Egypt and Canaan. He notes that the inclusion of Molech worship among sexual sins shows the connection between sexual immorality and idolatry. He highlights the personification of the land — creation itself responds to moral corruption.
  • Charles Spurgeon: Sermon: "Separation from the World" "God says to His people: you shall not do as Egypt does, and you shall not do as Canaan does. You came FROM the world, and you live IN the world, but you are not OF the world. The world behind you practiced these things — do not look back. The world before you practices these things — do not conform. You are a peculiar people, a holy nation, set apart for God. The standard is not what the culture permits but what God commands. And His commands are not burdensome — they are LIFE. 'Which if a man do, he shall live in them.' God's boundaries are not prison walls; they are garden walls — keeping death out and life in."

Reflection

  • 1. "After the doings of Egypt... after the doings of Canaan... shall ye not do" (v.3). You came FROM a culture and you live IN a culture — but you are not defined by either. The standard for God's people is not "what everyone else does" but "what God says." Where are you conforming to the culture you came from or the culture you live in rather than to God's Word?
  • 2. "Which if a man do, he shall live in them" (v.5). God's commands are not arbitrary restrictions — they are the path to LIFE. Every boundary God sets is a protection. The fence is not a prison wall; it is a guardrail on a cliff. Obedience leads to flourishing; transgression leads to destruction.
  • 3. "I am the LORD" — repeated throughout. God does not negotiate His standards. He does not adjust them to cultural trends. He does not explain Himself to satisfy human curiosity. He declares: "I am the LORD." His character is the unchanging standard. Are you adjusting God's Word to fit your culture, or adjusting your life to fit God's Word?
  • 4. "The land itself vomiteth out her inhabitants" (v.25). Sin has consequences that extend beyond the individual. Moral corruption affects families, communities, nations, and even the physical land. The Canaanites were expelled for these very sins. Israel was later exiled for the same. No nation is exempt from moral accountability.
  • 5. The Canaanites' sin was "not yet full" in Abraham's day (Genesis 15:16). God waited 400 years before judging them. He is patient — but patience has a limit. The same sins that filled the Canaanites' cup will fill any nation's cup. God's patience is not permission.
