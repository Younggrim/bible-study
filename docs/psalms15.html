1A psalm of David. Lord, who may be a guest in your home? Who may live on your holy hill?

2Whoever lives a blameless life, does what is right, and speaks honestly.

3He does not slander, or do harm to others, or insult his neighbor.

Summary

An entrance liturgy — 'LORD, who shall abide in thy tabernacle?' The answer describes the person of integrity: truthful speech, no slander, keeps oaths even when it hurts, and does not take bribes.

Key Verses

v.1
"LORD, who shall abide in thy tabernacle? who shall dwell in thy holy hill?" (KJV) — The foundational question of worship: who has access to God? The answer is character- based, not ritual-based. This reframes worship as an ethical commitment, not merely a ceremonial act.
v.2
"He that walketh uprightly, and worketh righteousness, and speaketh the truth in his heart." (KJV) — Three spheres: conduct (walketh), action (worketh), and speech/thought (speaketh truth IN HIS HEART). Integrity begins internally — truth in the heart before truth on the lips.
v.4
"He that sweareth to his own hurt, and changeth not." (KJV) — One of Scripture's most challenging ethical standards: keeping your word even when it costs you. A promise made is binding regardless of changed circumstances or personal disadvantage.
v.5b
"He that doeth these things shall never be moved." (KJV) — The promise: stability, permanence, security. The ethical life produces the unshakable life. Character is foundation.

Cross-References

  • v.1 — Psalm 24:3-4 — "Who shall ascend into the hill of the LORD?" Same entrance-liturgy question with parallel answer. v.1 — Isaiah 33:14-16 — "Who among us shall dwell with the devouring fire?" Same question in prophetic context. v.2 — Micah 6:8 — "What doth the LORD require of thee? To do justly, love mercy, walk humbly." Same ethical summary. v.3 — James 3:5-8 — "The tongue is a fire." Speech control as character evidence. v.4 — Matthew 5:37 — "Let your yes be yes and your no be no." Jesus' ethic of keeping vows. v.4 — Joshua 9:18-19 — Israel kept its oath to the Gibeonites despite being deceived. Swearing to one's hurt and not changing. v.5 — Exodus 22:25 — "If thou lend money to any of my people that is poor... thou shalt not be to him as an usurer." OT prohibition on exploitative lending.

Translations

  • v.1 — KJV: "abide in thy tabernacle" / ESV: "sojourn in your tent" — Hebrew "gur" = sojourn/dwell as a guest. "Ohel" = tent. The imagery is of being a guest in God's own dwelling. Who is welcome in God's house?
  • v.3 — KJV: "backbiteth not" / ESV: "does not slander" — Hebrew "ragal" literally = to go about as a spy/foot-traveler, hence "going about gossiping." Both translations capture the sense of spreading harmful speech about others.
  • v.4 — KJV: "a vile person is contemned" / ESV: "a vile person is despised" — The righteous person makes moral distinctions. This is not social snobbery but moral discernment — refusing to honor what God rejects while honoring what God values (those who fear the LORD).
  • v.5 — KJV: "putteth not out his money to usury" / ESV: "does not put out his money at interest" — Hebrew "neshek" = interest/ usury. In the OT context, lending at interest to fellow Israelites (especially the poor) was prohibited. This is about exploitation of the vulnerable, not all financial transactions.

Reflection

  • 1. Access to God is ethical, not merely ceremonial (v.1). God does not ask "Did you perform the right ritual?" but "What is your character?" Worship without integrity is empty. Character IS the qualification for God's presence.
  • 2. Truth must be internal before it is external (v.2). "Speaketh the truth IN HIS HEART" — not just truth on the lips but truth in the mind. Self-deception disqualifies as much as lying to others. Are you honest with yourself?
  • 3. Keep your word even when it costs (v.4). This is perhaps the most practical and painful requirement. A promise made in optimism must be honored in adversity. Your word is your bond even when "the deal" turns against you.
  • 4. Financial ethics matter to God (v.5). How you use money reveals character. Exploiting the vulnerable through lending or taking bribes against the innocent — these are worship disqualifiers. Financial integrity is spiritual integrity.
  • 5. Character produces stability (v.5b). "Shall never be moved" is the reward of ethical living. The person of integrity has a foundation that circumstances cannot shake. Compromise creates instability; integrity creates permanence.
