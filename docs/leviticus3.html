Summary

Section 1 — Peace Offering from the Herd (vv.1-5): Male or female, without blemish. The worshipper lays his hand on the animal's head and kills it at the entrance of the tent of meeting. The priests sprinkle the blood on the altar. The fat covering the entrails, the two kidneys with their fat, and the long lobe of the liver are burned on the altar atop the burnt offering — a food offering with a pleasing aroma. Section 2 — Peace Offering from the Flock: A Lamb (vv.6-11): Male or female, without blemish. Same procedure: laying on of hands, killing, blood sprinkled. The fat, the entire fat tail (cut off close to the backbone), the fat on the entrails, kidneys, and liver lobe are burned. The priest burns it as "a food offering to the LORD." Section 3 — Peace Offering from the Flock: A Goat (vv.12-16): Same procedure as above. The fat, entrails, kidneys, and liver lobe are burned. "All fat is the LORD's" (v.16). Section 4 — The Perpetual Statute (v.17): A permanent command for all generations: "You shall eat neither fat nor blood." The fat belongs to God; the blood is the life and belongs to God alone.

Authorship & Background

  • Author: Moses. Leviticus is the third book of the Pentateuch, written during the wilderness period (approximately 1445-1405 BC). Hebrew title: "Vayikra" — "And He called." God speaks from the Tabernacle, giving instructions for worship, sacrifice, and holy living.
  • Historical Context: The peace offering (Hebrew: "shelamim," from "shalom" — peace, wholeness, well-being) is the third offering described in Leviticus. Unlike the burnt offering (entirely consumed) or the grain offering (shared between God and priests), the peace offering was a communal meal — portions were given to God (the fat burned on the altar), to the priest (the breast and right thigh, detailed in Lev 7:31-34), and to the worshipper and his family (the remaining meat). It was the only offering where the worshipper ate a portion.
  • This offering was voluntary — an expression of thanksgiving, fulfillment of a vow, or a freewill act of devotion (Lev 7:11-16). It celebrated fellowship and communion with God. The shared meal pictured reconciliation — God and man eating together in peace. This points forward to Christ, who is "our peace" (Eph 2:14), who broke down the dividing wall and reconciled us to God, making fellowship possible through His blood.

Reflection

  • 1. The peace offering was a shared meal — God, priest, and worshipper eating together. Through Christ, you have been invited to God's table. Do you enjoy fellowship with God, or do you treat your relationship as distant and formal? The Lord's Supper is your peace offering meal.
  • 2. "All fat is the LORD's" (v.16). The best belongs to God — not the leftovers. Do you give God the first and best of your time, energy, finances, and attention? Or does He receive what remains after you've served yourself?
  • 3. The worshipper laid his hand on the offering's head (v.2) — identification. You are identified with Christ: His death is your death; His acceptance is your acceptance. Do you live in the confidence of that identification, or do you still try to earn your standing before God?
  • 4. Peace with God (Rom 5:1) is the foundation for the peace of God (Phil 4:7). Have you settled the question of peace WITH God through faith in Christ? Only then can you experience the peace OF God in daily life.
  • 5. The peace offering was voluntary — an overflow of gratitude and joy. Is your worship obligatory or voluntary? Do you come to God because you must, or because you delight to? What would it look like to offer a "peace offering" of thanksgiving this week?
