Isaiah — Chapter 15

1An oracle concerning Moab. Because Ar of Moab is laid waste in a night, Moab is undone; because Kir of Moab is laid waste in a night, Moab is undone.

2He has gone up to the temple, and to Dibon, to the high places to weep; over Nebo and over Medeba Moab wails. On every head is baldness; every beard is shorn;

3in the streets they wear sackcloth; on the housetops and in the squares everyone wails and melts in tears.

4Heshbon and Elealeh cry out; their voice is heard as far as Jahaz; therefore the armed men of Moab cry aloud; his soul trembles.

5My heart cries out for Moab; her fugitives flee to Zoar, to Eglath-shelishiyah. For at the ascent of Luhith they go up weeping; on the road to Horonaim they raise a cry of destruction;

6the waters of Nimrim are a desolation; the grass is withered, the vegetation fails, the greenery is no more.

7Therefore the abundance they have gained and what they have laid up they carry away over the Brook of the Willows.

8For a cry has gone around the land of Moab; her wailing reaches to Eglaim; her wailing reaches to Beer-elim.

9For the waters of Dibon are full of blood; for I will bring upon Dibon even more, a lion for those of Moab who escape, for the remnant of the land.

Summary

The oracle against Moab — in a single night Moab's cities are destroyed, the entire nation weeps and wails, refugees flee southward, and even Isaiah's own heart cries out in compassion for Moab's devastation.

Key Verses

v.1
"In the night Ar of Moab is laid waste, and brought to silence; in the night Kir of Moab is laid waste" (KJV) — "In the night" — repeated. Destruction comes unexpectedly, while they sleep. Two major cities destroyed simultaneously before dawn.
v.5
"My heart shall cry out for Moab" (KJV) — The prophet's compassion for an enemy nation. Isaiah doesn't rejoice in Moab's judgment — he weeps. God's messengers should deliver hard truths with tender hearts.
v.6
"the waters of Nimrim shall be desolate: for the hay is withered away, the grass faileth, there is no green thing" (KJV) — Total ecological devastation: water dried up, vegetation dead. The land itself mourns — not just the people but the environment collapses.

Cross-References

  • v.1 — Jeremiah 48 — An expanded oracle against Moab using much of the same language and city names. v.2 — Numbers 21:30 — "Heshbon is perished" — an earlier prophecy of the same region's destruction. v.5 — Jeremiah 48:36 — "my heart shall sound for Moab like pipes" — Jeremiah shares Isaiah's compassion. v.7 — Isaiah 16:1-5 — Moab's refugees seek shelter in Judah.

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/isaiah-15/ Guzik notes the prophet's remarkable compassion for a pagan nation under judgment and traces the geographic sweep of destruction through Moab's cities.
  • Charles Spurgeon: "The prophet weeps for Moab. Learn from this: the preacher of judgment should never be dry-eyed. If you can announce damnation without tears, you have not the heart of Christ."

Translations

  • v.1 — "burden" (KJV) / "oracle" (ESV) — Hebrew "massa" = a weighty prophetic word (same as 13:1).
  • v.2 — "Dibon is gone up to the high places to weep" (KJV) — "high places" (bamot) = pagan worship sites. They weep at their temples — but their gods cannot help.
  • v.5 — "fugitives" (KJV) / "fugitives" (ESV) — Hebrew "bericheyha" = those who flee/escape. Refugees streaming south toward Edom, seeking asylum.
  • v.9 — "Dimon" (KJV/ESV) — likely a variant of "Dibon" with a wordplay on "dam" (blood). "The waters of Dimon shall be full of blood" — dam/Dimon sound-play.

Reflection

  • 1. God judges nations that aren't part of His covenant people. Moab had no Torah, no temple, no covenant — yet God held them accountable. No one escapes divine justice by claiming ignorance of God's specific revelation.
  • 2. "My heart shall cry out for Moab" (v.5). Do you weep for the people you know are under judgment? Or do you secretly enjoy watching enemies suffer? The prophetic heart delivers hard truths with real tears.
  • 3. Destruction came "in the night" (v.1) — unexpectedly, while Moab slept. Are you spiritually awake? "The day of the Lord so cometh as a thief in the night" (1 Thess 5:2). Vigilance isn't paranoia — it's wisdom.
