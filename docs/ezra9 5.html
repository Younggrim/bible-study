Ezra — Chapter 9

1After these things had been done, the officials approached me and said, “The people of Israel and the priests and the Levites have not separated themselves from the peoples of the lands with their abominations, from the Canaanites, the Hittites, the Perizzites, the Jebusites, the Ammonites, the Moabites, the Egyptians, and the Amorites.

2For they have taken some of their daughters to be wives for themselves and for their sons, so that the holy race has mixed itself with the peoples of the lands. And in this faithlessness the hand of the officials and chief men has been foremost.”

3As soon as I heard this, I tore my garment and my cloak and pulled hair from my head and beard and sat appalled.

4Then all who trembled at the words of the God of Israel, because of the faithlessness of the returned exiles, gathered around me while I sat appalled until the evening sacrifice.

5And at the evening sacrifice I rose from my fasting, with my garment and my cloak torn, and fell upon my knees and spread out my hands to the LORD my God,

6saying: “O my God, I am ashamed and blush to lift my face to you, my God, for our iniquities have risen higher than our heads, and our guilt has mounted up to the heavens.

7From the days of our fathers to this day we have been in great guilt. And for our iniquities we, our kings, and our priests have been given into the hand of the kings of the lands, to the sword, to captivity, to plundering, and to utter shame, as it is today.

8But now for a brief moment favor has been shown by the LORD our God, to leave us a remnant and to give us a secure hold within his holy place, that our God may brighten our eyes and grant us a little reviving in our slavery.

9For we are slaves. Yet our God has not forsaken us in our slavery, but has extended to us his steadfast love before the kings of Persia, to grant us some reviving to set up the house of our God, to repair its ruins, and to give us protection in Judea and Jerusalem.

10“And now, O our God, what shall we say after this? For we have forsaken your commandments,

11which you commanded by your servants the prophets, saying, ‘The land that you are entering, to take possession of it, is a land impure with the impurity of the peoples of the lands, with their abominations that have filled it from end to end with their uncleanness.

12Therefore do not give your daughters to their sons, neither take their daughters for your sons, and never seek their peace or prosperity, that you may be strong and eat the good of the land and leave it for an inheritance to your children forever.’

13And after all that has come upon us for our evil deeds and for our great guilt, seeing that you, our God, have punished us less than our iniquities deserved and have given us such a remnant as this,

14shall we break your commandments again and intermarry with the peoples who practice these abominations? Would you not be angry with us until you consumed us, so that there should be no remnant, nor any to escape?

15O LORD, the God of Israel, you are just, for we are left a remnant that has escaped, as it is today. Behold, we are before you in our guilt, for none can stand before you because of this.”

Summary

Ezra is informed that many Israelites, including priests and Levites, have intermarried with the surrounding pagan peoples. Devastated, he tears his garments and offers a passionate prayer of confession, acknowledging God's grace and the people's unfaithfulness.

Key Verses

v.2
"The holy race has mixed itself with the peoples of the lands. And in this faithlessness the hand of the officials and chief men has been foremost." (ESV) — "Holy race" — Israel was set apart (holy/qadosh) by covenant. Intermarriage threatened that distinctiveness at its most fundamental level — the family. That leaders were foremost in this faithlessness compounded the sin: those who should model faithfulness led the compromise.
v.3
"As soon as I heard this, I tore my garment and my cloak and pulled hair from my head and beard and sat appalled." (ESV) — Four expressions of grief escalating in severity: tearing outer garment, tearing inner cloak, pulling head hair, pulling beard hair. Ezra's response was not performance — it was the uncontrolled grief of a man who understood what this sin meant for the community's future.
v.6
"O my God, I am ashamed and blush to lift my face to you, my God, for our iniquities have risen higher than our heads, and our guilt has mounted up to the heavens." (ESV) — One of Scripture's most emotionally raw prayers. Ezra felt the sin personally though he didn't commit it. The image is of rising floodwaters — sin accumulated until it reached heaven itself. His shame was corporate identification with the community's guilt.
v.8
"But now for a brief moment favor has been shown by the LORD our God, to leave us a remnant and to give us a secure hold within his holy place" (ESV) — "A secure hold" — Hebrew "yathed" literally means a peg/tent stake. The remnant had barely established a foothold in the land — a tenuous stake of stability. And now they risk pulling even that stake out through sin.
v.13
"Seeing that you, our God, have punished us less than our iniquities deserved and have given us such a remnant as this" (ESV) — Perhaps the most important theological statement in the prayer: God's punishment was less than deserved, and His restoration was unearned grace. This perspective — we deserve worse and have received better — is the foundation of true repentance.
v.14
"Shall we break your commandments again and intermarry with the peoples who practice these abominations?" (ESV) — The rhetorical question requires no answer. The logic is irrefutable: having been judged for this sin before, having been graciously restored, to repeat the identical sin would be suicidal presumption upon grace.

Cross-References

  • v.1 — Deuteronomy 7:1-4 — The original prohibition: "You shall not intermarry with them... for they would turn away your sons from following me, to serve other gods." (ESV) The concern was always theological contamination through relational intimacy — not racial prejudice.
  • v.3 — Daniel 9:3 — Daniel's confessional prayer similarly arose from reading Scripture and recognizing corporate sin. Both leaders identified with the people's guilt ("our iniquities") and appealed to God's character rather than the people's merit.
  • v.6 — Psalm 38:4 — "For my iniquities have gone over my head; like a heavy burden, they are too heavy for me." (ESV) The same metaphor of sin as overwhelming flood/weight that Ezra employs in his prayer.
  • v.8 — Isaiah 1:9 — "If the LORD of hosts had not left us a few survivors, we should have been like Sodom, and become like Gomorrah." (ESV) The remnant theology pervades both Isaiah and Ezra — God's grace preserves a seed even through judgment.
  • v.13 — Lamentations 3:22 — "The steadfast love of the LORD never ceases; his mercies never come to an end." (ESV) Ezra's acknowledgment that punishment was "less than our iniquities deserved" echoes Jeremiah's post-destruction recognition of mercy within judgment.
  • v.15 — Romans 3:19 — "So that every mouth may be stopped, and the whole world may be held accountable to God." (ESV) Ezra's "none can stand before you" anticipates the universal human condition Paul describes — guilt before a righteous God that silences all self-justification.

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/ezra-9/ Guzik emphasizes that Ezra's prayer contains no request — only confession and acknowledgment. He did not tell God what to do; he simply told God the truth about the situation and left the response to divine wisdom. This is a model for prayers where the problem is so serious that no human solution seems adequate.
  • Charles Spurgeon: "Ezra's grief is the grief of a true shepherd. The sin was not his, yet he claims it — 'our iniquities.' This is the mark of intercession: to stand in the gap, bearing the burden of others' sins as though they were your own. His shame was real — 'I blush to lift up my face.' How different from those who expose others' sins with cold detachment! Ezra wept over what others did lightly. And mark his logic: God punished us for this sin with exile — shall we commit it again now that grace has restored us? The argument is unanswerable. He who has been forgiven much and returns to the forgiven sin is guilty beyond measure. God's mercy is not license to repeat the offense; it is the strongest possible motive to abandon it forever."

Translations

  • v.2 — KJV: "the holy seed have mingled themselves" / ESV: "the holy race has mixed itself" — Hebrew "zera ha-qodesh" (seed of holiness/the holy seed). KJV's "seed" is literal; ESV's "race" interprets it. "Holy" here means set apart by covenant, not inherently superior. The concern is covenant identity being diluted through marital alliance with those who worship other gods.
  • v.3 — KJV: "sat down astonied" / ESV: "sat appalled" — Hebrew "yoshev meshomem" (sitting desolate/stunned/appalled). KJV's archaic "astonied" (astonished to the point of paralysis) captures the intensity; ESV's "appalled" is more accessible but slightly less vivid.
  • v.8 — KJV: "a nail in his holy place" / ESV: "a secure hold within his holy place" — Hebrew "yathed" (peg/nail/tent stake). The metaphor is of a tent peg driven into ground — a small but essential point of stability. KJV preserves the vivid imagery; ESV interprets the metaphor. The community's hold on the land was precarious, like a tent secured by a single peg.
  • v.9 — KJV: "to give us a wall in Judah and in Jerusalem" / ESV: "to give us protection in Judea and Jerusalem" — Hebrew "gader" (a wall/fence, especially a low stone wall around a vineyard). KJV is literal; ESV interprets figuratively. Since Jerusalem's actual walls were not yet rebuilt (that awaits Nehemiah), the "wall" is metaphorical — God's protective boundary around the fragile community.
  • v.15 — KJV: "thou art righteous" / ESV: "you are just" — Hebrew "tsaddiq attah" (righteous/just you are). Ezra's closing affirmation is that God is right in whatever He does — whether in judgment or mercy. The prayer ends not with a request but with a declaration of God's character.

Reflection

  • 1. True leaders identify with their community's sin (v.6). Ezra said "our iniquities" — not "their sin." He was personally innocent of intermarriage, yet he bore the corporate guilt as a representative. If you lead, you share responsibility for the community's failures. Intercession means entering into others' guilt before God on their behalf, not distancing yourself from it.
  • 2. Repeating forgiven sin is the worst ingratitude (v.14). God judged Israel for this exact sin through exile, then graciously restored them — and now they do it again? The logic is devastating. When God has delivered you from a pattern of sin, returning to it is not merely weakness — it is trampling the grace that rescued you.
  • 3. Honest confession makes no excuses (vv.6-15). Ezra's prayer contains no rationalization, no blame-shifting, no comparison with worse sinners. Just raw, unqualified acknowledgment: "we cannot stand before thee because of this." True repentance doesn't negotiate with God; it simply tells the truth and submits to His justice.
  • 4. Grace received should increase holiness, not presumption (v.13). "Thou hast punished us less than our iniquities deserve." This awareness — that we've been treated better than we merit — should produce gratitude-driven obedience, not presumption that God will keep overlooking our sin. The proper response to mercy is not "I can get away with it" but "I dare not abuse such kindness."
  • 5. Sometimes the right response is grief, not action (vv.3-4). Ezra didn't immediately strategize, organize a committee, or deliver a sermon. He wept, tore his clothes, and sat in stunned silence. Some sins are so serious that the first response must be mourning — letting the gravity settle before rushing to solutions. Do you grieve over sin, or merely manage it?
