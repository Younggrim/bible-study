Judges — Chapter 4

1And the people of Israel again did what was evil in the sight of the LORD after Ehud died.

2And the LORD sold them into the hand of Jabin king of Canaan, who reigned in Hazor. The commander of his army was Sisera, who lived in Harosheth-hagoyim.

3Then the people of Israel cried out to the LORD for help, for he had 900 chariots of iron and he oppressed the people of Israel cruelly for twenty years.

4Now Deborah, a prophetess, the wife of Lappidoth, was judging Israel at that time.

5She used to sit under the palm of Deborah between Ramah and Bethel in the hill country of Ephraim, and the people of Israel came up to her for judgment.

6She sent and summoned Barak the son of Abinoam from Kedesh-naphtali and said to him, “Has not the LORD, the God of Israel, commanded you, ‘Go, gather your men at Mount Tabor, taking 10,000 from the people of Naphtali and the people of Zebulun.

7And I will draw out Sisera, the general of Jabin’s army, to meet you by the river Kishon with his chariots and his troops, and I will give him into your hand’?”

8Barak said to her, “If you will go with me, I will go, but if you will not go with me, I will not go.”

9And she said, “I will surely go with you. Nevertheless, the road on which you are going will not lead to your glory, for the LORD will sell Sisera into the hand of a woman.” Then Deborah arose and went with Barak to Kedesh.

10And Barak called out Zebulun and Naphtali to Kedesh. And 10,000 men went up at his heels, and Deborah went up with him.

11Now Heber the Kenite had separated from the Kenites, the descendants of Hobab the father-in-law of Moses, and had pitched his tent as far away as the oak in Zaanannim, which is near Kedesh.

12When Sisera was told that Barak the son of Abinoam had gone up to Mount Tabor,

13Sisera called out all his chariots, 900 chariots of iron, and all the men who were with him, from Harosheth-hagoyim to the river Kishon.

14And Deborah said to Barak, “Up! For this is the day in which the LORD has given Sisera into your hand. Does not the LORD go out before you?” So Barak went down from Mount Tabor with 10,000 men following him.

15And the LORD routed Sisera and all his chariots and all his army before Barak by the edge of the sword. And Sisera got down from his chariot and fled away on foot.

16And Barak pursued the chariots and the army to Harosheth-hagoyim, and all the army of Sisera fell by the edge of the sword; not a man was left.

17But Sisera fled away on foot to the tent of Jael, the wife of Heber the Kenite, for there was peace between Jabin the king of Hazor and the house of Heber the Kenite.

18And Jael came out to meet Sisera and said to him, “Turn aside, my lord; turn aside to me; do not be afraid.” So he turned aside to her into the tent, and she covered him with a rug.

19And he said to her, “Please give me a little water to drink, for I am thirsty.” So she opened a skin of milk and gave him a drink and covered him.

20And he said to her, “Stand at the opening of the tent, and if any man comes and asks you, ‘Is anyone here?’ say, ‘No.’”

21But Jael the wife of Heber took a tent peg, and took a hammer in her hand. Then she went softly to him and drove the peg into his temple until it went down into the ground while he was lying fast asleep from weariness. So he died.

22And behold, as Barak was pursuing Sisera, Jael went out to meet him and said to him, “Come, and I will show you the man whom you are seeking.” So he went in to her tent, and there lay Sisera dead, with the tent peg in his temple.

23So on that day God subdued Jabin the king of Canaan before the people of Israel.

24And the hand of the people of Israel pressed harder and harder against Jabin the king of Canaan, until they destroyed Jabin king of Canaan.

Summary

Deborah the prophetess summons Barak to fight Sisera's army, and God routs the Canaanite chariots at the river Kishon. Jael drives a tent peg through Sisera's skull, ending twenty years of oppression.

Key Verses

  • v.3 — "Then the people of Israel cried out to the LORD for help, for he had 900 chariots of iron and he oppressed the people of Israel cruelly for twenty years." (ESV) — The cry of desperation that precedes deliverance. Twenty years of cruel oppression — God allows the consequences to drive them back to Himself.
  • v.8 — "Barak said to her, 'If you will go with me, I will go, but if you will not go with me, I will not go.'" (ESV) — Barak's condition. He wants the prophetess present — wanting assurance of God's presence. His faith is real but incomplete.
  • v.9 — "The LORD will sell Sisera into the hand of a woman." (ESV) — Deborah's prophecy. The ultimate honor of the victory will go to a woman — a double shame for Sisera (killed by a woman) and a rebuke to Barak (the glory he could have had goes to another).
  • v.14 — "'Up! For this is the day in which the LORD has given Sisera into your hand. Does not the LORD go out before you?'" (ESV) — Deborah's battle cry. Note the past tense: "has given" — it is already done in God's purposes. Faith speaks of future victory as accomplished fact.
  • v.21 — "But Jael the wife of Heber took a tent peg, and took a hammer in her hand. Then she went softly to him and drove the peg into his temple." (ESV) — Jael's decisive act. A tent peg and hammer — the tools of a nomadic woman. God uses what is at hand.

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): enduringword.com Guzik notes that Deborah is unique among the judges — she is already functioning as judge before the crisis, not raised up in response to it. He sees Barak's request not as cowardice but as incomplete faith. The chapter shows God using women in leadership when men fail to lead.
  • Charles Spurgeon: "Sisera had nine hundred chariots of iron, but God had the river Kishon. All the iron in the world cannot stand against the waters of God's providence. The mightiest armies are nothing when God fights against them. Sisera fled on foot — his chariots became his coffin. So it is with every weapon formed against God's people: what the enemy trusts in becomes the instrument of his destruction."

Translations

  • v.2 → KJV: "Harosheth of the Gentiles" / ESV: "Harosheth-hagoyim" — Hebrew "Charoshet ha-Goyim." KJV translates "ha-Goyim" as "of the Gentiles." ESV transliterates the full Hebrew name. "Harosheth" may mean "woodland" or "craftsman's place." The location suggests a mixed population area.
  • v.4 → KJV: "the wife of Lapidoth" / ESV: "the wife of Lappidoth" — Hebrew "eshet Lappidot." "Lappidot" means "torches/flames." Some scholars suggest this could be a description ("woman of torches/ fiery woman") rather than a husband's name, but the traditional reading as her husband's name is standard.
  • v.9 → KJV: "the LORD shall sell Sisera into the hand of a woman" / ESV: "the LORD will sell Sisera into the hand of a woman" — Hebrew "b'yad ishah yimkor Adonai et-Sisera." "Makar" = to sell, hand over. The same verb used of God "selling" Israel to their enemies (v.2) — now God "sells" the enemy to a woman. Poetic justice.
  • v.15 → KJV: "the LORD discomfited Sisera" / ESV: "the LORD routed Sisera" — Hebrew "vayaham Adonai." "Hamam" = to confuse, throw into panic, rout. This word is used of God's supernatural intervention in battle (Exodus 14:24; Joshua 10:10; 1 Samuel 7:10). It implies divine action beyond natural military engagement — God Himself caused the panic and confusion.
  • v.18 → KJV: "she covered him with a mantle" / ESV: "she covered him with a rug" — Hebrew "ba-s'mikhah." This word occurs only here in the Old Testament. It likely refers to a thick covering — a blanket, rug, or heavy cloth. The covering conceals Sisera and gives him false security.
  • v.21 → KJV: "a nail of the tent" / ESV: "a tent peg" — Hebrew "y'ted ha-ohel." "Yated" = a peg, pin, or stake. These were large wooden or metal stakes used to anchor tent ropes — substantial enough to be driven through a man's skull into the ground. Nomadic women were skilled at driving tent pegs — this was Jael's expertise.

Reflection

  • 1. God uses women when men fail to lead (vv.4,9). Deborah's leadership is not presented as the ideal but as God's provision when men abdicate. She is faithful, courageous, and Spirit-led. God is not limited by human conventions — He uses whoever is willing and available.
  • 2. Incomplete faith is still faith (v.8). Barak's condition ("if you go, I'll go") shows imperfect faith — but he still went. He still obeyed. He still fought. Hebrews 11:32 lists him among the faithful. God uses imperfect faith. He does not demand perfection before He acts — He demands willingness.
  • 3. God turns the enemy's strength into weakness (v.15). Nine hundred iron chariots — Israel's greatest fear (cf. 1:19). But God draws them to the river where chariots become death traps. What the enemy trusts in becomes the instrument of his defeat. God is never intimidated by human technology or power.
  • 4. "Is not the LORD gone out before thee?" (v.14). Deborah's question to Barak is the question for every believer facing overwhelming odds. If God goes before you, what can stand against you? The issue is never the size of the enemy but the presence of God.
  • 5. Jael's decisive action (v.21). She did not hesitate, deliberate, or second-guess. When the moment came, she acted with precision and courage. Sometimes faithfulness requires decisive, uncomfortable action — not endless deliberation.
