Kings of Israel & Judah

Hezekiah

His Reign

Hezekiah inherited a kingdom in spiritual ruin from his father Ahaz, who had shut the Temple doors, set up altars to foreign gods, and even sacrificed his own children in pagan rituals. In his very first month as king, Hezekiah reopened and purified the Temple, reinstated proper worship, and called the nation to celebrate Passover for the first time in generations. His revival was not gradual but immediate and decisive — the mark of a leader who understood urgency.

Hezekiah's greatest test came when Sennacherib of Assyria — the superpower that had already destroyed the northern kingdom — surrounded Jerusalem with an overwhelming army. The Assyrian field commander mocked God publicly, and humanly speaking, Judah's situation was hopeless. Hezekiah's response was to take Sennacherib's threatening letter, spread it before the LORD in the Temple, and pray one of Scripture's most honest prayers of desperation.

God's answer was decisive: that very night, the angel of the LORD struck down 185,000 Assyrian soldiers, and Sennacherib retreated in disgrace to be murdered by his own sons. Hezekiah's trust in God was vindicated spectacularly. Yet his later years included the foolish display of his treasures to Babylonian envoys — planting the seeds of future exile. Even revival kings have blind spots, and pride after deliverance can undo the gains of faithfulness.

Key Scriptures

  • 2 Kings 18:5-6 — "He trusted in the LORD, the God of Israel, so that there was none like him among all the kings of Judah."
  • 2 Kings 19:15-19 — Hezekiah's prayer: spreading the threatening letter before the LORD and asking for deliverance.
  • Isaiah 37:36 — "The angel of the LORD went out and put to death a hundred and eighty-five thousand in the Assyrian camp."
  • 2 Chronicles 30:1 — Hezekiah invited all Israel and Judah to come to Jerusalem for Passover.
  • 2 Kings 20:5 — "I have heard your prayer and seen your tears; I will heal you."

Legacy and Lessons

Hezekiah demonstrates that spiritual revival often begins with a single leader willing to act decisively. He did not wait for consensus or popular opinion — he reopened the Temple because it was right, regardless of political cost. Revival in any generation requires leaders who will prioritize God's honor over personal comfort, institutional inertia, or fear of opposition. One person with courage can change the spiritual climate of an entire community.

Hezekiah also teaches us the proper response to overwhelming threat: bring it directly to God. He did not pretend the Assyrian army was not there, nor did he rely solely on military preparation. He took the actual letter of threat and laid it before God's presence. This physical act of surrender models what prayer should look like in crisis — specific, honest, and rooted in God's character rather than our capability.

Reflection Questions

  • What "Temple doors" in your life need to be reopened? Where has worship been neglected?
  • Is there a threatening situation you need to literally bring before God in prayer?
  • How do you guard against pride after God delivers you from crisis?
