Fruit of the Spirit

Love — Agape (ἀγάπη)

Understanding Agape Love

In the Greek New Testament, the word used for the love that is a fruit of the Spirit is agape (ἀγάπη). Unlike the emotional or romantic connotations that "love" carries in English, agape describes a deliberate, unconditional, and sacrificial commitment to the well-being of another person — regardless of whether that love is reciprocated or deserved. It is not based on feelings, attraction, or personal benefit. It is a choice rooted in the will, empowered by the Holy Spirit.

The Greek language distinguishes several types of love. Eros is romantic or passionate love between partners. Philia is the warm affection shared between friends — brotherly love. Storge is the natural affection found in family bonds, especially between parents and children. But agape stands above them all. It is the love that gives without expecting return, that serves without condition, and that endures when every other kind of love would falter. It is uniquely divine in origin.

God Himself demonstrates agape love toward us in the most profound way: "But God shows his love for us in that while we were still sinners, Christ died for us" (Romans 5:8). God did not wait for humanity to become lovable or worthy. He loved us at our worst — rebellious, broken, and hostile toward Him. This is the pattern and power of agape: it initiates, it sacrifices, and it transforms. When the Spirit produces this fruit in a believer's life, they begin to love others with the same selfless, persevering love that God has shown them.

Key Scriptures

  • 1 Corinthians 13:4-7 — The definitive description of what love looks like in action: patient, kind, not envious or boastful.
  • John 15:12-13 — Jesus commands His disciples to love one another as He has loved them — laying down one's life for friends.
  • Romans 5:8 — God demonstrates His own love toward us: while we were still sinners, Christ died for us.
  • 1 John 4:7-8 — Love originates from God. Everyone who loves is born of God and knows God, for God is love.
  • John 3:16 — The ultimate expression of agape: God gave His only Son so that whoever believes in Him would not perish.
  • Ephesians 5:25 — Husbands are called to love their wives as Christ loved the church and gave Himself up for her.

Living It Out

Practicing agape love begins with recognizing that it is not something we manufacture through willpower alone — it flows from an intimate relationship with Christ. As we abide in Him (John 15:4-5), His love naturally overflows through us toward others. This means daily surrender: asking the Spirit to give us eyes to see people as God sees them, and the willingness to act on their behalf even when it costs us something. Start small — choose one person today who is difficult to love, and serve them without expecting thanks or recognition.

Agape love is also intensely practical. It is not just a warm feeling during worship; it shows up in how we speak to our spouse when we are tired, how we respond to the coworker who frustrates us, how we treat the stranger who cuts us off in traffic. It means forgiving when an apology never comes. It means giving generously to someone who cannot repay you. It means staying present with someone in their pain rather than offering quick fixes. As you practice these things daily, you will find that the Spirit is reshaping your heart — making love less of an effort and more of a reflex.

Reflection Questions

  • Is there someone in my life I find difficult to love? What would it look like to show them agape love this week — not based on what they deserve, but on what Christ has shown me?
  • When I examine 1 Corinthians 13:4-7, which qualities of love do I struggle with most? Am I patient? Am I kind? Do I keep a record of wrongs?
  • How has God's unconditional love toward me changed the way I view myself and others? Am I receiving His love fully, or am I trying to earn it?
  • In what practical ways can I sacrificially serve someone today — with no expectation of return, recognition, or repayment?
