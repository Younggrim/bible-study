Genesis — Chapter 12

1Now the LORD said to Abram, “Go from your country and your kindred and your father’s house to the land that I will show you.

2And I will make of you a great nation, and I will bless you and make your name great, so that you will be a blessing.

3I will bless those who bless you, and him who dishonors you I will curse, and in you all the families of the earth shall be blessed.”

4So Abram went, as the LORD had told him, and Lot went with him. Abram was seventy-five years old when he departed from Haran.

5And Abram took Sarai his wife, and Lot his brother’s son, and all their possessions that they had gathered, and the people that they had acquired in Haran, and they set out to go to the land of Canaan. When they came to the land of Canaan,

6Abram passed through the land to the place at Shechem, to the oak of Moreh. At that time the Canaanites were in the land.

7Then the LORD appeared to Abram and said, “To your offspring I will give this land.” So he built there an altar to the LORD, who had appeared to him.

8From there he moved to the hill country on the east of Bethel and pitched his tent, with Bethel on the west and Ai on the east. And there he built an altar to the LORD and called upon the name of the LORD.

9And Abram journeyed on, still going toward the Negeb.

10Now there was a famine in the land. So Abram went down to Egypt to sojourn there, for the famine was severe in the land.

11When he was about to enter Egypt, he said to Sarai his wife, “I know that you are a woman beautiful in appearance,

12and when the Egyptians see you, they will say, ‘This is his wife.’ Then they will kill me, but they will let you live.

13Say you are my sister, that it may go well with me because of you, and that my life may be spared for your sake.”

14When Abram entered Egypt, the Egyptians saw that the woman was very beautiful.

15And when the princes of Pharaoh saw her, they praised her to Pharaoh. And the woman was taken into Pharaoh’s house.

16And for her sake he dealt well with Abram; and he had sheep, oxen, male donkeys, male servants, female servants, female donkeys, and camels.

17But the LORD afflicted Pharaoh and his house with great plagues because of Sarai, Abram’s wife.

18So Pharaoh called Abram and said, “What is this you have done to me? Why did you not tell me that she was your wife?

19Why did you say, ‘She is my sister,’ so that I took her for my wife? Now then, here is your wife; take her, and go.”

20And Pharaoh gave men orders concerning him, and they sent him away with his wife and all that he had.

Summary

God calls Abram to leave everything for an unknown land, promising to make him a great nation and bless all families through him.

Key Verses

v.1
"Now the LORD said to Abram, 'Go from your country and your kindred and your father's house to the land that I will show you.'" (ESV) — The call: leave everything for an unknown destination.
v.2
"And I will make of you a great nation, and I will bless you and make your name great, so that you will be a blessing." (ESV) — God's sevenfold promise.
v.3
"In you all the families of the earth shall be blessed." (ESV) — The gospel in seed form: blessing for ALL nations through Abram.
v.4
"So Abram went, as the LORD had told him." (ESV) — Faith expressed in obedience.
v.7
"To your offspring I will give this land." (ESV) — The land promise.

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/genesis-12/ Guzik covers the call, the sevenfold promise, Abram's obedience, and the Egypt failure.
  • Charles Spurgeon: Sermon: "The Call of Abraham" on vv.1-3 (MTP Vol. 14, No. 833) "Get thee OUT — leave. From thy country — the familiar. From thy kindred — the comfortable. From thy father's house — the secure. Unto a land that I WILL SHEW thee — not 'that I have shown' but 'will show.' He must leave before he sees. He must obey before he understands. This is faith: obeying God's command before seeing God's destination. And the promise: I WILL bless, I WILL make great, ALL families blessed. God's 'I will' is enough." Sermon: "In Thee Shall All Families Be Blessed" on v.3 (MTP Vol. 20) "ALL families of the earth — not just Israel, not just the Middle East — all. This is the gospel in Genesis: through one man's seed (Christ), every nation will have access to blessing. The Abrahamic covenant is the foundation of world missions."

Translations

  • v.1 — KJV: "Get thee out" / ESV: "Go from your country" — Hebrew "lek leka" — go for yourself, go for your own benefit. God's command is also for Abram's good. KJV's archaic "get thee out" and ESV's "go from" both capture the imperative; ESV loses the reflexive nuance of "leka" (for yourself).
  • v.1 — Three levels of separation: country (nation), kindred (extended family), father's house (immediate family). Each more costly than the last. Both translations identical in structure.
  • v.2 — KJV: "make thy name great" / ESV: "make your name great" — Both equivalent. Contrast with Babel: "let us make us a name" (11:4). At Babel, humans tried to make their own name; God makes Abram's name great. Self-exaltation fails; God-exaltation succeeds.
  • v.3 — KJV: "all families of the earth" / ESV: "all the families of the earth shall be blessed" — Both equivalent. Hebrew "kol mishpechoth ha'adamah" — every clan/family of the ground. Universal scope. The Abrahamic blessing is for everyone.
  • v.3 — KJV: "blessed" / ESV: "blessed" — Both identical. Hebrew "nibrechu" — a form that can mean "bless themselves" (reflexive) or "be blessed" (passive). Either way: Abram's seed brings blessing to all peoples. Fulfilled in Christ (Gal 3:16).
  • v.7 — KJV: "unto thy seed" / ESV: "To your offspring" — Hebrew "lezar'aka" — to your offspring/descendant. ESV uses "offspring" (modern English); KJV uses "seed" (closer to Hebrew "zera"). Both collective (Israel) and singular (Christ, Gal 3:16).
  • v.13 — KJV: "thou art my sister" / ESV: "you are my sister" — Both equivalent. A half-truth (Sarai was his half-sister, 20:12). But deceptive in intent. Fear displaced faith.
  • TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB) - v.1 — WEB: "Leave your country, and your relatives" — renders Hebrew "moladteka" as "relatives" rather than "kindred" (KJV/ASV). NET uses "your relatives" as well, modernizing the language. - v.2 — NET: "so that you will exemplify divine blessing" — a uniquely interpretive rendering of Hebrew "heyeh berakah" (KJV: "be thou a blessing" / ESV: "be a blessing"). NET makes Abram a visible model of what it looks like to be blessed by God. - v.3 — NET: "the one who treats you lightly I must curse" — renders Hebrew "meqallelka" as "treats you lightly" (vs. "curseth" KJV / "dishonors" ESV), capturing the root meaning of "qalal" (to make light/trivial). WEB: "treats you with contempt" — similar interpretive approach. - v.9 — NET: "Abram continually journeyed by stages down to the Negev" — adds "by stages" to render the Hebrew infinitive absolute construction, showing the gradual, progressive nature of the journey. - v.20 — NET: "they expelled him" — a harsher reading than "sent him away" (KJV/ESV) or "escorted him away" (WEB), emphasizing Pharaoh's anger.

Reflection

  • 1. "Get thee out... unto a land that I will shew thee" (v.1). God called Abram to leave before showing the destination. Has God ever called you to step out without knowing where you're going? Faith means obeying before seeing.
  • 2. "In thee shall all families of the earth be blessed" (v.3). You are part of this promise's fulfillment. Are you a blessing to the nations? How are you participating in God's plan to bless all peoples?
  • 3. "So Abram departed, as the LORD had spoken" (v.4). Simple, immediate obedience. When God speaks, do you depart? Or do you negotiate, delay, and make excuses?
  • 4. Abram built altars wherever he went (vv.7-8). He marked his journey with worship. Do you mark your life with worship — in every new place, every new season?
  • 5. Abram's Egypt failure (vv.10-20) shows that even great faith has weak moments. Fear displaced trust. Where does fear cause you to compromise your integrity? God protected His promise despite Abram's failure — He'll protect His purposes in your life too.
