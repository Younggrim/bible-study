Deuteronomy — Chapter 31

1So Moses continued to speak these words to all Israel.

2And he said to them, “I am 120 years old today. I am no longer able to go out and come in. The LORD has said to me, ‘You shall not go over this Jordan.’

3The LORD your God himself will go over before you. He will destroy these nations before you, so that you shall dispossess them, and Joshua will go over at your head, as the LORD has spoken.

4And the LORD will do to them as he did to Sihon and Og, the kings of the Amorites, and to their land, when he destroyed them.

5And the LORD will give them over to you, and you shall do to them according to the whole commandment that I have commanded you.

6Be strong and courageous. Do not fear or be in dread of them, for it is the LORD your God who goes with you. He will not leave you or forsake you.”

7Then Moses summoned Joshua and said to him in the sight of all Israel, “Be strong and courageous, for you shall go with this people into the land that the LORD has sworn to their fathers to give them, and you shall put them in possession of it.

8It is the LORD who goes before you. He will be with you; he will not leave you or forsake you. Do not fear or be dismayed.”

9Then Moses wrote this law and gave it to the priests, the sons of Levi, who carried the ark of the covenant of the LORD, and to all the elders of Israel.

10And Moses commanded them, “At the end of every seven years, at the set time in the year of release, at the Feast of Booths,

11when all Israel comes to appear before the LORD your God at the place that he will choose, you shall read this law before all Israel in their hearing.

12Assemble the people, men, women, and little ones, and the sojourner within your towns, that they may hear and learn to fear the LORD your God, and be careful to do all the words of this law,

13and that their children, who have not known it, may hear and learn to fear the LORD your God, as long as you live in the land that you are going over the Jordan to possess.”

14And the LORD said to Moses, “Behold, the days approach when you must die. Call Joshua and present yourselves in the tent of meeting, that I may commission him.” And Moses and Joshua went and presented themselves in the tent of meeting.

15And the LORD appeared in the tent in a pillar of cloud. And the pillar of cloud stood over the entrance of the tent.

16And the LORD said to Moses, “Behold, you are about to lie down with your fathers. Then this people will rise and whore after the foreign gods among them in the land that they are entering, and they will forsake me and break my covenant that I have made with them.

17Then my anger will be kindled against them in that day, and I will forsake them and hide my face from them, and they will be devoured. And many evils and troubles will come upon them, so that they will say in that day, ‘Have not these evils come upon us because our God is not among us?’

18And I will surely hide my face in that day because of all the evil that they have done, because they have turned to other gods.

19“Now therefore write this song and teach it to the people of Israel. Put it in their mouths, that this song may be a witness for me against the people of Israel.

20For when I have brought them into the land flowing with milk and honey, which I swore to give to their fathers, and they have eaten and are full and grown fat, they will turn to other gods and serve them, and despise me and break my covenant.

21And when many evils and troubles have come upon them, this song shall confront them as a witness (for it will live unforgotten in the mouths of their offspring). For I know what they are inclined to do even today, before I have brought them into the land that I swore to give.”

22So Moses wrote this song the same day and taught it to the people of Israel.

23And the LORD commissioned Joshua the son of Nun and said, “Be strong and courageous, for you shall bring the people of Israel into the land that I swore to give them. I will be with you.”

24When Moses had finished writing the words of this law in a book to the very end,

25Moses commanded the Levites who carried the ark of the covenant of the LORD,

26“Take this Book of the Law and put it by the side of the ark of the covenant of the LORD your God, that it may be there for a witness against you.

27For I know how rebellious and stubborn you are. Behold, even today while I am yet alive with you, you have been rebellious against the LORD. How much more after my death!

28Assemble to me all the elders of your tribes and your officers, that I may speak these words in their ears and call heaven and earth to witness against them.

29For I know that after my death you will surely act corruptly and turn aside from the way that I have commanded you. And in the days to come evil will befall you, because you will do what is evil in the sight of the LORD, provoking him to anger through the work of your hands.”

30Then Moses spoke the words of this song until they were finished, in the ears of all the assembly of Israel:

Summary

Moses commissions Joshua, writes the law, and commands it be read every seven years; God foretells Israel's coming unfaithfulness.

Key Verses

v.6
"Be strong and courageous, fear not, nor be afraid of them: for the LORD your God, he it is that does go with you; he will not fail you, nor forsake you." (ESV) — The foundation of courage: God's presence. Strength comes not from self but from the God who goes with us. Quoted in Hebrews 13:5.
v.8
"The LORD, he it is that does go before you; he will be with you, he will not fail you, neither forsake you: fear not, neither be dismayed." (ESV) — God goes BEFORE (leading) and is WITH (accompanying). Double assurance: He will not fail, He will not forsake.
v.12
"Gather the people together, men, and women, and children, and your stranger that is within your gates, that they may hear, and that they may learn, and fear the LORD your God, and observe to do all the words of this law." (ESV) — The law is for everyone: men, women, children, foreigners. No one is excluded from hearing God's word.
v.16
"This people will rise up, and go a whoring after the gods of the strangers of the land, where they go to be among them, and will forsake me, and break my covenant which I have made with them." (ESV) — God's foreknowledge of Israel's apostasy. He knows they will rebel yet still provides for them.
v.26
"Take this book of the law, and put it in the side of the ark of the covenant of the LORD your God, that it may be there for a witness against you." (ESV) — The law as witness. It testifies against those who violate it. Placed beside the ark — near God's presence.
v.29
"For I know that after my death you will utterly corrupt yourselves, and turn aside from the way which I have commanded you; and evil will befall you in the latter days." (ESV) — Moses' tragic prophecy. He knows the future and it is dark. Yet he still prepares them.

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/deuteronomy-31/ Guzik notes the tragic irony: Moses prepares Israel for a future he knows will go wrong. God commissions Joshua knowing Israel will rebel. Yet the preparations are still made — God's faithfulness is not contingent on Israel's faithfulness. He also emphasizes Hebrews 13:5 as the New Testament application of verse 6.
  • Charles Spurgeon: "'He will not fail thee, nor forsake thee.' Here is the believer's anchor in every storm. Not 'He might not fail thee' but 'He WILL NOT.' The double negative is absolute: not fail, not forsake. Whatever else may change — leaders die, circumstances shift, enemies arise — this remains: God will not leave His people. Moses dies, but God does not."

Translations

  • v.1 — KJV: "And Moses went and spake these words" / ESV: "So Moses continued to speak these words" — Hebrew "vayelekh Moshe." The Hebrew simply says "Moses went" — but where? Some read this as "went" (to the people) and others as "continued" (in speaking). ESV interprets as continuation; KJV translates literally.
  • v.6 — KJV: "he will not fail thee, nor forsake thee" / ESV: "He will not leave you or forsake you" — Hebrew "lo yarpekha v'lo ya'azvekha." "Raphah" = to let go, release, slacken. "Azav" = to leave, abandon, forsake. Two different Hebrew words for abandonment — God will neither let go His grip nor walk away. KJV's "fail" captures the idea of God's grip not weakening.
  • v.9 — KJV: "Moses wrote this law" / ESV: "Then Moses wrote this law" — Hebrew "vayikhtov Moshe et-ha-torah ha-zot." "Torah" here likely refers to the book of Deuteronomy specifically (or possibly the entire Pentateuch). Moses is identified as the writer.
  • v.10 — KJV: "the solemnity of the year of release" / ESV: "the set time in the year of release" — Hebrew "b'mo'ed sh'nat ha-sh'mittah." "Mo'ed" = appointed time, set feast. "Sh'mittah" = release (the sabbatical year when debts were released). Every seventh year at the Feast of Tabernacles.
  • v.16 — KJV: "go a whoring after the gods" / ESV: "whore after the foreign gods" — Hebrew "v'zanah acharei elohei nekhar." "Zanah" = to commit fornication/adultery. Idolatry is consistently described as spiritual adultery — unfaithfulness to the covenant "marriage" with God.
  • v.17 — KJV: "I will hide my face from them" / ESV: "I will hide my face from them" — Hebrew "v'histarti fanai." "Satar" = to hide, conceal. God hiding His face = withdrawing His favor, protection, and manifest presence. The worst judgment: not active punishment but divine absence.
  • v.29 — KJV: "ye will utterly corrupt yourselves" / ESV: "you will surely act corruptly" — Hebrew "hashchet tashchitun." Emphatic doubling of the verb "shachat" (to corrupt, destroy, ruin). The emphasis is absolute: they WILL corrupt themselves — it is certain.

Reflection

  • 1. God's presence is the foundation of courage (vv.6,8). "Be strong and of a good courage" is not a command to manufacture confidence from within. The reason for courage follows immediately: "for the LORD thy God, he it is that doth go with thee." Our strength is derivative — it comes from His presence. Without Him, courage is foolishness.
  • 2. Leaders change; God doesn't (vv.2-3). Moses dies, but God continues. Joshua takes over, but God is the same. Human leadership is temporary; divine faithfulness is permanent. We must not put our trust in leaders but in the God who outlasts them all.
  • 3. The law must be transmitted to every generation (vv.12-13). "That their children, which have not known any thing, may hear, and learn." Each generation must hear God's word fresh. Faith is not inherited automatically — it must be taught, heard, and embraced anew. Neglect of transmission produces apostasy.
  • 4. God provides even knowing we will fail (vv.16-22). God knows Israel will rebel. He tells Moses plainly. Yet He still commissions Joshua, still writes the law, still teaches the Song. God's faithfulness is not contingent on our faithfulness. He provides means of grace even for those He knows will abuse them.
  • 5. The law as witness against us (v.26). The written word placed beside the ark testifies against those who violate it. Scripture is not merely instruction — it is evidence. On the day of judgment, God's revealed word will testify that we knew what was right.
  • 6. Moses' tragic realism (v.29). "I know that after my death ye will utterly corrupt yourselves." Moses has no illusions about human nature. Forty years of leading this people has taught him what they are. Yet he still serves, still prepares, still speaks. Realism about human sin does not excuse us from faithful service.
