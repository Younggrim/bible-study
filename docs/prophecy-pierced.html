Prophecy & Fulfillment

Pierced

The Prophecy

Zechariah prophesied that God Himself would be pierced — and that those who pierced Him would one day mourn in deep repentance. This prophecy is remarkable because it identifies the pierced one as God speaking in first person: "They will look on me, the one they have pierced." The Messiah who would be pierced is God Himself, entering human history to be wounded for our sake.

Isaiah 53 explains the purpose of the piercing: "He was pierced for our transgressions." The wounds were not accidental or meaningless — they were substitutionary. Every nail, every thorn, every spear-thrust carried the weight of human sin. His piercing was our healing; His wounds were the price of our peace with God.

Psalm 22 adds physical detail to the prophecy: "They pierce my hands and my feet." Written centuries before crucifixion existed, this psalm describes the specific manner in which the Messiah's body would be wounded. The convergence of these three prophetic streams — Zechariah, Isaiah, and the Psalms — in the single event of Christ's crucifixion is compelling evidence of divine inspiration.

Key Scriptures

  • Zechariah 12:10 — "They will look on me, the one they have pierced, and they will mourn for him as one mourns for an only child."
  • John 19:34-37 — "One of the soldiers pierced Jesus' side with a spear... These things happened so that the scripture would be fulfilled."
  • Psalm 22:16 — "They pierce my hands and my feet."
  • Isaiah 53:5 — "He was pierced for our transgressions, he was crushed for our iniquities."
  • Revelation 1:7 — "Look, he is coming with the clouds, and every eye will see him, even those who pierced him."

The Fulfillment

John's Gospel records the literal fulfillment: after Jesus died, a Roman soldier pierced His side with a spear, and blood and water flowed out. John explicitly states this happened "so that the scripture would be fulfilled" and identifies himself as an eyewitness. The apostle saw Zechariah's words materialize before his eyes.

Revelation extends the prophecy into the future: "Every eye will see him, even those who pierced him." The piercing that accomplished salvation will one day be visible to all — and those who rejected Him will mourn. But for those who believe, His wounds are not a source of mourning but of eternal gratitude — visible proof that He loved us to the uttermost.

Reflection Questions

  • How does meditating on Christ being pierced for your transgressions affect your worship?
  • What does it mean that God Himself was the one who was pierced for humanity?
  • How do the wounds of Christ serve as both a warning and an invitation?
