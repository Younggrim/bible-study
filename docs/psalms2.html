Summary

A royal/Messianic psalm — the nations rage against the LORD and His Anointed, but God laughs from heaven, installs His King on Zion, and declares 'Thou art my Son'; blessed are all who take refuge in Him.

Key Verses

  • v.2 — "The kings of the earth set themselves, and the rulers take counsel together, against the LORD, and against his anointed" (KJV) — "Anointed" = Hebrew "mashiach" (Messiah). Opposition to God and to His Christ are one and the same rebellion. Applied to Jesus in Acts 4:25-27.
  • v.4 — "He that sitteth in the heavens shall laugh: the Lord shall have them in derision." (KJV) — God's response to human rebellion is not anxiety but laughter. Not cruel mockery but the response of absolute sovereignty to absurd pretension. The nations' conspiracy is laughable to the One who sits above all.
  • v.7 — "The LORD hath said unto me, Thou art my Son; this day have I begotten thee." (KJV) — The most quoted OT verse in the NT. Applied to Christ's resurrection (Acts 13:33), His superiority to angels (Hebrews 1:5), and His priestly appointment (Hebrews 5:5). "Today I have begotten you" refers to enthronement/coronation, not origin.
  • v.12 — "Kiss the Son, lest he be angry, and ye perish from the way, when his wrath is kindled but a little. Blessed are all they that put their trust in him." (KJV) — "Kiss" = submission/homage. The psalm ends as Psalm 1 began: with "blessed." The two psalms form a frame: blessed is the one who delights in Torah (Ps 1) and takes refuge in the Son (Ps 2).

Authorship & Background

  • Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed), plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1), Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89, 90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes: worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom, Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought before God.
  • Classification: Royal/Messianic Psalm Attributed Author: David (attributed in Acts 4:25) Key Themes: God's sovereign rule, rebellion of nations, the Anointed King (Messiah), divine sonship, judgment, refuge in God
  • Historical Context: Psalm 2 is the second "gateway" psalm, paired with Psalm 1 as a dual introduction to the Psalter. While Psalm 1 addresses individual righteousness, Psalm 2 addresses cosmic sovereignty. Though no superscription appears in the Hebrew text, Acts 4:25 attributes it to David. Its immediate context may be a coronation liturgy for Davidic kings, but its ultimate referent is Messianic — the NT quotes it more than any other psalm. The "decree" of v.7 was applied at each king's enthronement but finds its ultimate fulfillment in Christ. Four speakers alternate: the nations (v.3), God the Father (vv.6-9), the Son (v.7), and the psalmist/wisdom voice (vv.10-12).
  • Structure: - The Nations Rebel (vv.1-3) - God Laughs and Responds (vv.4-6) - The Son Declares the Decree (vv.7-9) - The Warning and Invitation (vv.10-12)

Cross-References

  • v.1-2 → Acts 4:25-28 — The early church explicitly identifies Herod, Pilate, Gentiles, and Israel as the "kings and rulers" who conspired against Jesus.
  • v.6 → Psalm 110:1-2 — "The LORD said unto my Lord, Sit thou at my right hand." Another Messianic enthronement psalm.
  • v.7 → Acts 13:33 — Paul applies "today I have begotten you" to Christ's resurrection — the moment of public declaration.
  • v.7 → Hebrews 1:5 — Applied to demonstrate Christ's superiority over angels.
  • v.7 → Hebrews 5:5 — Applied to Christ's priestly appointment.
  • v.8 → Philippians 2:9-11 — "God also hath highly exalted him, and given him a name which is above every name." The universal inheritance fulfilled.
  • v.9 → Revelation 2:27 — "He shall rule them with a rod of iron; as the vessels of a potter shall they be broken." Applied to the glorified church sharing Christ's authority.
  • v.9 → Revelation 19:15 — Christ returns with "a rod of iron" to smite the nations.
  • v.12 → Psalm 34:8 — "O taste and see that the LORD is good: blessed is the man that trusteth in him." Same refuge theology.

Translations

  • v.1 → KJV: "Why do the heathen rage" → ESV: "Why do the nations rage" — Hebrew "goyim" simply means nations/peoples. KJV "heathen" carries a pejorative connotation not present in the Hebrew. ESV "nations" is more accurate.
  • v.1 → KJV: "imagine a vain thing" → ESV: "plot in vain" — Hebrew "hagah" (same word as "meditate" in 1:2!) — here used negatively: the nations "mutter/plot" emptiness, just as the righteous "mutter/meditate" on Torah. Same activity, opposite content.
  • v.6 → KJV: "Yet have I set my king upon my holy hill of Zion" → ESV: "As for me, I have set my King on Zion, my holy hill" — ESV's "As for me" emphasizes the contrast: the nations plot, but AS FOR ME (God) — I have already installed my King. The rebellion is futile because the enthronement is past tense — already accomplished.
  • v.12 → KJV: "Kiss the Son" → ESV: "Kiss the Son" — Hebrew "bar" (Aramaic for "son") rather than the expected Hebrew "ben." Some scholars see this as evidence of early date or deliberate word choice. "Kiss" = an act of homage and submission to a ruler.
  • v.12 → KJV: "put their trust in him" → ESV: "take refuge in him" — ESV's "take refuge" captures the Hebrew "chasah" better — it implies running TO someone for shelter, not merely intellectual belief. Trust as active dependence.
  • ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB) - v.1 — NET: "Why do the nations rebel? Why are the countries devising plots that will fail?" — "rebel" is more specific than "rage" (KJV → ESV → ASV → WEB), and "plots that will fail" makes the futility explicit (Hebrew "riq" = empty/ vain). ASV → WEB: "Why do the nations rage, and the peoples meditate/plot a vain thing?" — "meditate" (ASV) deliberately echoes Psalm 1:2 — the wicked meditate on emptiness; the righteous meditate on Torah. - v.2 — WEB: "against Yahweh, and against his Anointed" — capitalizes "Anointed" (Mashiach/Messiah). NET: "the Lord and his anointed king" — adds "king" (interpretive, making the royal context explicit). ASV: "Against Jehovah, and against his anointed." - v.7 — NET: "You are my son. This very day I have become your father" — "I have become your father" interprets "begotten thee" (KJV → ASV) relationally rather than ontologically. This avoids implying the Son was created. WEB: "Today I have become your father" — same as NET. The decree is about official installation/declaration, not origin. Quoted at Jesus' baptism (Matt 3:17) and in Acts 13:33 (resurrection). - v.9 — NET: "You will break them with an iron scepter; you will smash them like a potter's jar" — "scepter" (vs. "rod" KJV → ESV → ASV → WEB) makes the royal authority explicit. "Smash" is more violent than "dash in pieces." Hebrew "tero'em" can also be read "tir'em" (you will shepherd them) — a textual variant the LXX follows (Revelation 2:27 quotes both ideas). - v.11-12 — NET: "Serve the Lord in fear. Repent in terror. Give sincere homage" — radically different from "Rejoice with trembling. Kiss the Son" (KJV → ASV). NET renders "nashqu-bar" (kiss the son) as "give sincere homage" because the Aramaic "bar" (son) is debated. Some read it as "bar" (purity) — thus "worship in purity." NET takes the interpretive meaning without committing to either reading. WEB: "Give sincere homage to the Son" — combines both: "sincere homage" for the verb, but retains "Son." - v.12 — ASV: "Kiss the son, lest he be angry, and ye perish in the way" — retains the powerful physical image of kissing (homage). WEB: "lest he be angry, and you perish on the way, for his wrath will soon be kindled" — "on the way" suggests perishing mid-journey, mid-rebellion. NET: "you will die because of your behavior" — makes the cause of death explicit.

Reflection

  • 1. Human rebellion against God is absurd (vv.1-4). The Creator of the universe does not tremble at human conspiracy. He laughs. Not because suffering is trivial, but because the outcome was never in doubt. When the world seems chaotic, remember: God is not worried.
  • 2. God's plan cannot be thwarted (v.6). "I HAVE set my King" — past tense, accomplished. The enthronement of Christ is not pending human permission. It is done. Opposition does not delay God's purposes.
  • 3. "Kiss the Son" — submission is wisdom (vv.10-12). The psalm does not end with wrath but with invitation. Kings are warned: be wise, submit, serve with reverent joy. The alternative is not punishment imposed but natural consequence — you "perish from the way" by choosing to oppose the way.
  • 4. The psalm ends with "blessed" (v.12b). This connects back to Psalm 1:1. The double blessing frames the introduction to the Psalter: blessed is the one who walks in Torah AND takes refuge in God's Anointed. Both are needed — obedience and trust.
  • 5. Refuge is the right response to divine power (v.12). The appropriate reaction to a God this powerful is not terror but refuge. His power becomes your protection when you run TO Him rather than FROM Him.
