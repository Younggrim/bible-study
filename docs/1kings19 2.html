1 Kings — Chapter 19

1Ahab told Jezebel all that Elijah had done, and how he had killed all the prophets with the sword.

2Then Jezebel sent a messenger to Elijah, saying, “So may the gods do to me and more also, if I do not make your life as the life of one of them by this time tomorrow.”

3Then he was afraid, and he arose and ran for his life and came to Beersheba, which belongs to Judah, and left his servant there.

4But he himself went a day’s journey into the wilderness and came and sat down under a broom tree. And he asked that he might die, saying, “It is enough; now, O LORD, take away my life, for I am no better than my fathers.”

5And he lay down and slept under a broom tree. And behold, an angel touched him and said to him, “Arise and eat.”

6And he looked, and behold, there was at his head a cake baked on hot stones and a jar of water. And he ate and drank and lay down again.

7And the angel of the LORD came again a second time and touched him and said, “Arise and eat, for the journey is too great for you.”

8And he arose and ate and drank, and went in the strength of that food forty days and forty nights to Horeb, the mount of God.

9There he came to a cave and lodged in it. And behold, the word of the LORD came to him, and he said to him, “What are you doing here, Elijah?”

10He said, “I have been very jealous for the LORD, the God of hosts. For the people of Israel have forsaken your covenant, thrown down your altars, and killed your prophets with the sword, and I, even I only, am left, and they seek my life, to take it away.”

11And he said, “Go out and stand on the mount before the LORD.” And behold, the LORD passed by, and a great and strong wind tore the mountains and broke in pieces the rocks before the LORD, but the LORD was not in the wind. And after the wind an earthquake, but the LORD was not in the earthquake.

12And after the earthquake a fire, but the LORD was not in the fire. And after the fire the sound of a low whisper.

13And when Elijah heard it, he wrapped his face in his cloak and went out and stood at the entrance of the cave. And behold, there came a voice to him and said, “What are you doing here, Elijah?”

14He said, “I have been very jealous for the LORD, the God of hosts. For the people of Israel have forsaken your covenant, thrown down your altars, and killed your prophets with the sword, and I, even I only, am left, and they seek my life, to take it away.”

15And the LORD said to him, “Go, return on your way to the wilderness of Damascus. And when you arrive, you shall anoint Hazael to be king over Syria.

16And Jehu the son of Nimshi you shall anoint to be king over Israel, and Elisha the son of Shaphat of Abel-meholah you shall anoint to be prophet in your place.

17And the one who escapes from the sword of Hazael shall Jehu put to death, and the one who escapes from the sword of Jehu shall Elisha put to death.

18Yet I will leave seven thousand in Israel, all the knees that have not bowed to Baal, and every mouth that has not kissed him.”

19So he departed from there and found Elisha the son of Shaphat, who was plowing with twelve yoke of oxen in front of him, and he was with the twelfth. Elijah passed by him and cast his cloak upon him.

20And he left the oxen and ran after Elijah and said, “Let me kiss my father and my mother, and then I will follow you.” And he said to him, “Go back again, for what have I done to you?”

21And he returned from following him and took the yoke of oxen and sacrificed them and boiled their flesh with the yokes of the oxen and gave it to the people, and they ate. Then he arose and went after Elijah and assisted him.

Summary

Elijah flees Jezebel's death threat and wishes to die under a broom tree, but God feeds him and sends him to Horeb. God reveals Himself not in wind, earthquake, or fire, but in a still small voice, and commissions Elisha as Elijah's successor.

Key Verses

v.4
"It is enough; now, O LORD, take away my life, for I am no better than my fathers." (ESV) — The honest prayer of an exhausted servant. Elijah does not abandon God — he surrenders to God. Even in despair, his theology is sound: he asks God to act rather than acting on his own. Depression is not the same as apostasy.
v.7
"Arise and eat, for the journey is too great for you." (ESV) — God's tender response to human weakness. No rebuke, no lecture — provision. God knows our frame and remembers that we are dust (Psalm 103:14). Sometimes the most spiritual thing you can do is eat and sleep.
v.12
"And after the fire the sound of a low whisper." (ESV) — God reveals Himself not only in the spectacular but in the quiet. The fire of Carmel and the whisper of Horeb are both God. He is sovereign over both the dramatic and the gentle. Learn to hear Him in the silence.
v.18
"Yet I will leave seven thousand in Israel, all the knees that have not bowed to Baal, and every mouth that has not kissed him." (ESV) — God's correction of Elijah's isolation. You are never as alone as you feel. God always preserves a remnant. Paul quotes this verse in Romans 11:4 to prove God's faithfulness to Israel.
v.20
"And he left the oxen and ran after Elijah." (ESV) — Elisha's immediate response to the prophetic call. He does not hesitate, negotiate, or delay. The call of God demands immediate, wholehearted response. What Elisha leaves behind (wealth, family, security) is nothing compared to what he gains (the Spirit of God in double portion).

Cross-References

  • v.2 — Revelation 2:20 — Jezebel's spirit persists: a woman who "calls herself a prophetess" and seduces God's servants. The name becomes a type of seductive false teaching. v.4 — Jonah 4:3 — "Take, I beseech you, my life from me." Both prophets ask for death after successful ministry. Spiritual exhaustion follows spiritual exertion. v.4 — Numbers 11:15 — Moses also asked God to kill him under the burden of leadership. Even the greatest servants reach their limits. v.8 — Exodus 34:28 — Moses fasted forty days on the same mountain. Elijah's journey echoes Moses — both meet God at Sinai after forty days without food. v.11 — Exodus 33:21-22 — Moses in the cleft of the rock as God "passed by." Elijah in the cave as God "passed by." The parallel is deliberate — Elijah is the new Moses. v.12 — Psalm 46:10 — "Be still, and know that I am God." The still small voice teaches what the Psalm commands: God is known in stillness, not only in spectacle. v.18 — Romans 11:4 — Paul quotes this verse directly to prove God's preservation of a faithful remnant in every generation. The principle endures. v.19 — 2 Kings 2:9 — Elisha asks for "a double portion" of Elijah's spirit. The mantle cast here is the same mantle that falls at Elijah's ascension. v.21 — Luke 9:62 — "No one who puts his hand to the plow and looks back is fit for the kingdom of God." Elisha literally leaves his plow — and never looks back.

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/1-kings-19/ Guzik emphasizes the physical dimension of Elijah's depression: he was exhausted, hungry, and alone. God's first response was not theological instruction but physical care — food and rest. Guzik notes that the "still small voice" does not replace the fire of Carmel but complements it. God works in both modes. The 7,000 who had not bowed to Baal remind us that God's work is always larger than any one person's perspective. Elijah's sense of isolation was sincere but factually wrong.
  • Charles Spurgeon: "Elijah under the juniper tree is the same Elijah who stood on Carmel. The man who called fire from heaven now asks for death. Let no one think himself above depression. If Elijah fell into it, so may you. But mark God's remedy: He did not scold His servant — He fed him. He did not argue with his despair — He gave him sleep. And then He gave him work. The cure for self-pity is always a new commission. God said, in effect, 'You are not finished. There is more to do. Get up and do it.' The best remedy for spiritual depression is obedient action."

Translations

  • v.4 — KJV: "juniper tree" / ESV: "broom tree" — Hebrew "rotem" is not a juniper but a desert broom plant (Retama raetam), a shrub that grows 6-10 feet tall in the Negev wilderness, providing shade. KJV follows an older botanical identification; ESV corrects to the accurate plant. The broom tree provides minimal shade — Elijah's shelter is meager, matching his despair.
  • v.12 — KJV: "a still small voice" / ESV: "the sound of a low whisper" — Hebrew "qol demamah daqqah." "Qol" = voice/ sound. "Demamah" = silence, stillness, calm. "Daqqah" = thin, fine, small. Literally: "a voice of thin silence" or "a sound of crushing stillness." KJV's "still small voice" has become iconic in English. ESV's "low whisper" interprets the paradox. Both struggle with the Hebrew, which describes something almost oxymoronic — a sound that is silence, a voice that is stillness.
  • v.13 — KJV: "wrapped his face in his mantle" / ESV: "wrapped his face in his cloak" — Hebrew "addereth" = a large outer garment, often of animal skin. This is the prophetic mantle — the same garment cast on Elisha (v.19) and later dropped at Elijah's ascension (2 Kings 2:13). Wrapping the face = reverence before God's presence (cf. Moses in Exodus 3:6).
  • v.19 — KJV: "cast his mantle upon him" / ESV: "cast his cloak upon him" — The same "addereth." This act is the prophetic commissioning — the mantle represents the prophetic office and authority. It is not merely clothing but a symbol of calling. When Elisha later picks up this same mantle (2 Kings 2:13), he inherits the office.
  • v.21 — KJV: "instruments of the oxen" / ESV: "yokes of the oxen" — Hebrew "keli ha-baqar" = the equipment/implements of the cattle, specifically the wooden yokes and plowing gear. Elisha burns his farming equipment as fuel to cook the sacrificed oxen — a dramatic, irreversible break with his former life. There is no plow to return to.

Reflection

  • 1. Spiritual depression is not spiritual failure (vv.3-4). Elijah was not in sin — he was exhausted. The greatest servants of God have experienced profound despair: Moses, David, Jeremiah, Paul. If you are in a dark place after faithful service, you are in good company. Depression does not disqualify you from God's service — it qualifies you for God's tenderness.
  • 2. God meets physical needs before addressing spiritual ones (vv.5-7). Before God spoke to Elijah about his mission, He fed him and let him sleep — twice. Sometimes the most spiritual thing you can do is take care of your body. Exhaustion distorts perspective. Rest is not laziness when you are depleted — it is obedience to your Creator's design.
  • 3. God is not only in the spectacular (v.12). After Carmel's fire, God reveals Himself in a whisper. Do not assume God is absent because the experience is not dramatic. He speaks in Scripture reading, in quiet prayer, in the counsel of faithful friends. Learn to recognize His voice in the ordinary — not only in the extraordinary.
  • 4. Your perception of isolation is almost always wrong (v.18). Elijah said "I only am left." God said "I have 7,000." When you feel alone in faithfulness, your feelings are lying to you. God always has His people. Seek them out. Isolation is the enemy's strategy — community is God's design.
  • 5. Total commitment requires burning the bridges (v.21). Elisha slaughtered his oxen and burned his plow. He made return impossible. Half-hearted following is not following at all. What "plows" do you need to burn? What fallback positions keep you from full commitment to God's call? Destroy them — and follow without reservation.
