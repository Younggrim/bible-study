Ezra — Chapter 4

1Now when the adversaries of Judah and Benjamin heard that the returned exiles were building a temple to the LORD, the God of Israel,

2they approached Zerubbabel and the heads of fathers’ houses and said to them, “Let us build with you, for we worship your God as you do, and we have been sacrificing to him ever since the days of Esarhaddon king of Assyria who brought us here.”

3But Zerubbabel, Jeshua, and the rest of the heads of fathers’ houses in Israel said to them, “You have nothing to do with us in building a house to our God; but we alone will build to the LORD, the God of Israel, as King Cyrus the king of Persia has commanded us.”

4Then the people of the land discouraged the people of Judah and made them afraid to build

5and bribed counselors against them to frustrate their purpose, all the days of Cyrus king of Persia, even until the reign of Darius king of Persia.

6And in the reign of Ahasuerus, in the beginning of his reign, they wrote an accusation against the inhabitants of Judah and Jerusalem.

7In the days of Artaxerxes, Bishlam and Mithredath and Tabeel and the rest of their associates wrote to Artaxerxes king of Persia. The letter was written in Aramaic and translated.

8Rehum the commander and Shimshai the scribe wrote a letter against Jerusalem to Artaxerxes the king as follows:

9Rehum the commander, Shimshai the scribe, and the rest of their associates, the judges, the governors, the officials, the Persians, the men of Erech, the Babylonians, the men of Susa, that is, the Elamites,

10and the rest of the nations whom the great and noble Osnappar deported and settled in the cities of Samaria and in the rest of the province Beyond the River.

11(This is a copy of the letter that they sent.) “To Artaxerxes the king: Your servants, the men of the province Beyond the River, send greeting. And now

12be it known to the king that the Jews who came up from you to us have gone to Jerusalem. They are rebuilding that rebellious and wicked city. They are finishing the walls and repairing the foundations.

13Now be it known to the king that if this city is rebuilt and the walls finished, they will not pay tribute, custom, or toll, and the royal revenue will be impaired.

14Now because we eat the salt of the palace and it is not fitting for us to witness the king’s dishonor, therefore we send and inform the king,

15in order that search may be made in the book of the records of your fathers. You will find in the book of the records and learn that this city is a rebellious city, hurtful to kings and provinces, and that sedition was stirred up in it from of old. That was why this city was laid waste.

16We make known to the king that if this city is rebuilt and its walls finished, you will then have no possession in the province Beyond the River.”

17The king sent an answer: “To Rehum the commander and Shimshai the scribe and the rest of their associates who live in Samaria and in the rest of the province Beyond the River, greeting. And now

18the letter that you sent to us has been plainly read before me.

19And I made a decree, and search has been made, and it has been found that this city from of old has risen against kings, and that rebellion and sedition have been made in it.

20And mighty kings have been over Jerusalem, who ruled over the whole province Beyond the River, to whom tribute, custom, and toll were paid.

21Therefore make a decree that these men be made to cease, and that this city be not rebuilt, until a decree is made by me.

22And take care not to be slack in this matter. Why should damage grow to the hurt of the king?”

23Then, when the copy of King Artaxerxes’ letter was read before Rehum and Shimshai the scribe and their associates, they went in haste to the Jews at Jerusalem and by force and power made them cease.

24Then the work on the house of God that is in Jerusalem stopped, and it ceased until the second year of the reign of Darius king of Persia.

Summary

Local adversaries offer to help build the Temple but are refused, then they actively oppose and discourage the builders through intimidation and political accusations. Letters to the Persian king result in a forced halt to the construction that lasts until the reign of Darius.

Key Verses

v.2
"Let us build with you, for we worship your God as you do" (ESV) — The most dangerous opposition often comes disguised as cooperation. These adversaries claimed shared faith but practiced syncretism (2 Kings 17:33 — "they feared the LORD, and served their own gods"). Their offer to "build with you" would have diluted pure worship with compromise.
v.3
"You have nothing to do with us in building a house to our God; but we alone will build to the LORD, the God of Israel, as King Cyrus the king of Persia has commanded us" (ESV) — Zerubbabel's response was clear, firm, and theologically grounded. He did not negotiate or seek a middle ground. Some situations require definitive separation, not diplomatic compromise. The authority of Cyrus's decree provided legal backing for the refusal.
v.4
"Then the people of the land discouraged the people of Judah and made them afraid to build" (ESV) — When cooperation was refused, the adversaries' true nature emerged. "Discouraged" translates the Hebrew idiom for demoralization. They used psychological warfare before resorting to political and legal tactics.
v.14
"Now because we eat the salt of the palace and it is not fitting for us to witness the king's dishonor" (ESV) — The accusers framed their treachery as loyalty to the king. The appeal to financial self-interest ("the revenue of the kings" v.13) was calculated to alarm. They weaponized bureaucracy against God's purposes.
v.24
"Then the work on the house of God that is in Jerusalem stopped" (ESV) — The work stopped. God allowed this delay. The prophets Haggai and Zechariah would later reveal that the people's response to opposition became sinful complacency — they stopped building God's house but built their own paneled houses (Haggai 1:4).

Cross-References

  • v.2 — 2 Kings 17:24-33 — The origin of the Samaritan peoples: Assyria repopulated the northern kingdom with foreigners who developed a syncretistic religion — fearing Yahweh while serving their own gods. Their claim to "seek your God" in Ezra 4:2 was technically true but theologically inadequate.
  • v.3 — 2 Corinthians 6:14 — "Do not be unequally yoked with unbelievers." (ESV) Zerubbabel's refusal embodies this principle centuries before Paul articulated it. Partnership in sacred work requires shared theological commitment, not merely shared activity.
  • v.4 — Nehemiah 4:1-6 — The same pattern of opposition recurs when Nehemiah rebuilds the walls: mockery, intimidation, conspiracy, and political manipulation. Satan's tactics against God's building projects are remarkably consistent.
  • v.15 — Jeremiah 52:3 — "For because of the anger of the LORD it came to the point in Jerusalem and Judah that he cast them out from his presence. And Zedekiah rebelled against the king of Babylon." (ESV) The accusers' claim that Jerusalem was historically rebellious was factually true — but they omitted that God Himself had ordained both the rebellion and the punishment, and had now authorized the return.
  • v.24 — Haggai 1:2-4 — "These people say the time has not yet come to rebuild the house of the LORD... Is it a time for you yourselves to dwell in your paneled houses, while this house lies in ruins?" (ESV) When opposition stopped the Temple work, the people redirected their energy to personal comfort. The 16-year delay was initially imposed by enemies but was prolonged by the people's own misplaced priorities.

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/ezra-4/ Guzik emphasizes that the first tactic of the enemy was partnership, not persecution — compromise is more dangerous than conflict. He notes that Zerubbabel's discernment to refuse help from syncretists was essential, and that the letter to Artaxerxes shows how truth can be manipulated for evil purposes. The historical facts cited were accurate but the interpretation was dishonest.
  • Charles Spurgeon: "The enemies first came as friends — 'Let us build with you.' Beware when the world offers to help the church! A partnership between truth and error can only end in the corruption of truth. Zerubbabel saw through the disguise and refused the polluted alliance. When friendly partnership failed, the adversary turned to intimidation and then to legal obstruction. Note the progression: flattery, then fear, then force. Satan has but three strategies, and he applies them in order. The work stopped — but not forever. God permits delays, but He does not permit defeat. What opposition halts, the Spirit of God will restart in His appointed time."

Translations

  • v.4 — KJV: "weakened the hands" / ESV: "discouraged" — Hebrew "merapim yedei" (making slack the hands). KJV preserves the vivid Hebrew idiom; ESV translates the meaning. The image is of strong hands losing their grip — morale and resolve draining away through persistent harassment.
  • v.7 — KJV: "written in the Syrian tongue, and interpreted in the Syrian tongue" / ESV: "written in Aramaic and translated" — From 4:8 through 6:18, the text shifts from Hebrew to Aramaic — the diplomatic language of the Persian Empire. Ezra preserves the original language of the official correspondence, lending documentary authenticity.
  • v.8 — KJV: "Rehum the chancellor" / ESV: "Rehum the commander" — Aramaic "beel-teem" — a Persian administrative title. Its exact meaning is debated: "lord of command/decree" or "master of report." KJV's "chancellor" and ESV's "commander" represent different interpretations of this Persian bureaucratic rank.
  • v.10 — KJV: "the great and noble Asnapper" / ESV: "the great and noble Osnappar" — Generally identified with Ashurbanipal, the last great Assyrian king (668-627 BC). The name is a corrupted form of "Ashurbanipal" filtered through Aramaic. He continued Esarhaddon's policy of deporting peoples into Samaria.
  • v.14 — KJV: "we have maintenance from the king's palace" / ESV: "we eat the salt of the palace" — Aramaic "melach hekalah melachnah" (the salt of the palace we eat). "Eating the king's salt" was a Persian idiom for being in the king's service/receiving his provisions. ESV preserves the idiom; KJV interprets it. The metaphor implies loyalty purchased through provision.

Reflection

  • 1. Beware of partnership disguised as cooperation (v.2). The most dangerous opposition comes wearing a friendly face. "Let us build with you" sounds generous, but it masks theological compromise. Not every offer of help should be accepted. Discernment means knowing when to say "You have nothing to do with us" — not out of arrogance, but out of fidelity to God's standards.
  • 2. Expect opposition when doing God's work (vv.4-5). Resistance to God's purposes is normal, not exceptional. If you face opposition in obedience, don't assume you've done something wrong. The enemy opposes what threatens his territory. Anticipate the pattern: flattery (offers to "help"), then discouragement, then outright attack.
  • 3. Truth can be weaponized (vv.12-16). The accusation letter contained factual truths — Jerusalem had rebelled. But facts without context become lies. Watch for this in your own life: selective truth-telling that paints a misleading picture. And guard against accepting accusations without investigating their full context.
  • 4. God permits delays but not defeat (v.24). The work stopped for 16 years. From a human perspective, the opposition won. But God was not finished — He raised up prophets to restart what enemies had stopped. If you're in a season where opposition has halted something you believe God initiated, wait for His timing. Delays test faith; they do not indicate abandonment.
  • 5. Don't let opposition become an excuse for complacency (v.24 + Haggai 1:4). The initial stoppage was forced by enemies, but it continued because the people grew comfortable. They built their own houses while God's house lay in ruins. Legitimate obstacles can become convenient excuses for neglecting God's priorities. Ask yourself: is opposition genuinely preventing me, or have I simply stopped trying?
