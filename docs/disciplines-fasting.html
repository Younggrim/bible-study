Spiritual Disciplines

Fasting

The Discipline

Fasting is the voluntary denial of a physical need — typically food — for a spiritual purpose. It is practiced throughout Scripture by those seeking God with urgency: Moses on Sinai, David in repentance, Esther before approaching the king, Jesus before His ministry began. Fasting declares that spiritual hunger surpasses physical hunger.

The purpose of fasting is not to impress God with our sacrifice or to twist His arm through deprivation. Rather, fasting creates space for focused prayer, heightened spiritual sensitivity, and deeper dependence on God. When we voluntarily set aside food, we declare that man does not live by bread alone but by every word that comes from God's mouth.

Jesus assumed His followers would fast — He said "when you fast," not "if you fast." Yet He warned against the hypocrisy of fasting for public recognition. Biblical fasting is a private discipline between the soul and God, not a public display of spirituality. Its power lies in the hidden transaction between a hungry body and a seeking heart.

Key Scriptures

  • Matthew 6:16-18 — "When you fast, put oil on your head and wash your face, so that it will not be obvious to others that you are fasting."
  • Isaiah 58:6 — "Is not this the kind of fasting I have chosen: to loose the chains of injustice?"
  • Acts 13:2-3 — "While they were worshiping the Lord and fasting, the Holy Spirit said, Set apart for me Barnabas and Saul."
  • Joel 2:12 — "Return to me with all your heart, with fasting and weeping and mourning."
  • Matthew 4:2 — "After fasting forty days and forty nights, he was hungry."

Practicing It

Begin with a simple fast — perhaps skipping one meal and using that time for prayer instead. As your body protests with hunger, let each pang become a prayer prompt, turning physical desire into spiritual conversation. Over time, you can extend fasts as the Spirit leads, always keeping health and medical needs in mind.

Fasting is most powerful when paired with a specific purpose: seeking guidance for a major decision, interceding for someone in crisis, repenting from sin, or preparing for ministry. Without purpose, fasting becomes mere dieting. With purpose, it becomes one of the most powerful spiritual weapons available to believers — as the early church demonstrated repeatedly.

Reflection Questions

  • Have you practiced fasting, and what has been your experience?
  • What purpose might God be calling you to pursue through a season of fasting?
  • How does physical hunger help you recognize your deeper spiritual hunger for God?
