Micah — Chapter 7

1Woe is me! For I have become as when the summer fruit has been gathered, as when the grapes have been gleaned: there is no cluster to eat, no first-ripe fig that my soul desires.

2The godly has perished from the earth, and there is no one upright among mankind; they all lie in wait for blood, and each hunts the other with a net.

3Their hands are on what is evil, to do it well; the prince and the judge ask for a bribe, and the great man utters the evil desire of his soul; thus they weave it together.

4The best of them is like a brier, the most upright of them a thorn hedge. The day of your watchmen, of your punishment, has come; now their confusion is at hand.

5Put no trust in a neighbor; have no confidence in a friend; guard the doors of your mouth from her who lies in your arms;

6for the son treats the father with contempt, the daughter rises up against her mother, the daughter-in-law against her mother-in-law; a man’s enemies are the men of his own house.

7But as for me, I will look to the LORD; I will wait for the God of my salvation; my God will hear me.

8Rejoice not over me, O my enemy; when I fall, I shall rise; when I sit in darkness, the LORD will be a light to me.

9I will bear the indignation of the LORD because I have sinned against him, until he pleads my cause and executes judgment for me. He will bring me out to the light; I shall look upon his vindication.

10Then my enemy will see, and shame will cover her who said to me, “Where is the LORD your God?” My eyes will look upon her; now she will be trampled down like the mire of the streets.

11A day for the building of your walls! In that day the boundary shall be far extended.

12In that day they will come to you, from Assyria and the cities of Egypt, and from Egypt to the River, from sea to sea and from mountain to mountain.

13But the earth will be desolate because of its inhabitants, for the fruit of their deeds.

14Shepherd your people with your staff, the flock of your inheritance, who dwell alone in a forest in the midst of a garden land; let them graze in Bashan and Gilead as in the days of old.

15As in the days when you came out of the land of Egypt, I will show them marvelous things.

16The nations shall see and be ashamed of all their might; they shall lay their hands on their mouths; their ears shall be deaf;

17they shall lick the dust like a serpent, like the crawling things of the earth; they shall come trembling out of their strongholds; they shall turn in dread to the LORD our God, and they shall be in fear of you.

18Who is a God like you, pardoning iniquity and passing over transgression for the remnant of his inheritance? He does not retain his anger forever, because he delights in steadfast love.

19He will again have compassion on us; he will tread our iniquities underfoot. You will cast all our sins into the depths of the sea.

20You will show faithfulness to Jacob and steadfast love to Abraham, as you have sworn to our fathers from the days of old.

Summary

Micah laments the moral decay of society where no one can be trusted, yet confesses faith in God who will have compassion, cast sins into the sea, and fulfill His covenant promises — "Who is a God like you, pardoning iniquity?"

Translations

  • "Who is a God like unto thee" (v. 18) — Hebrew: "mi-El kamocha." This is the etymological root of Micah's name. The entire book ends with the prophet's name becoming a theological declaration. His life and message answer his own name's question: no god compares to YHWH because no god forgives like YHWH.
  • "Delighteth in mercy" (KJV) vs. "delights in steadfast love" (ESV) — Hebrew: "chaphets chesed." The verb "chaphets" means to take pleasure in, to desire, to delight. God's mercy is not grudging obligation—it is what He loves to do. Hesed (mercy/steadfast love) is His native environment, His characteristic posture.
  • "Subdue our iniquities" (KJV) vs. "tread our iniquities underfoot" (ESV) — Hebrew: "yichbosh avonoteinu." The verb "kavash" means to subjugate, to conquer, to trample. It is a military term—God treats our sins as enemies to be defeated and crushed, not merely overlooked.
  • "The depths of the sea" (v. 19) — Hebrew: "metsulot yam." This recalls the Red Sea crossing where God drowned Pharaoh's army (Exodus 15). Just as Egypt's military power was swallowed by the sea and never recovered, so our sins are cast into irrecoverable depths.
  • "Firstripe fruit" (KJV) vs. "first-ripe fig" (ESV) — Hebrew: "bikkurah." The early fig was considered the choicest delicacy (cf. Isaiah 28:4, Hosea 9:10). Micah longs for even one righteous person the way one craves the first fruit of the season—but finds none.

Reflection

  • 1. When society seems to be collapsing around you—when corruption is everywhere and trustworthy people seem to have vanished—the answer is not despair but the deliberate choice: "I will look unto the LORD" (v. 7). Faith is a decision, not merely a feeling.
  • 2. "When I fall, I shall arise" (v. 8). Failure is not final for the child of God. The enemy celebrates your stumbling, but God specializes in resurrection. If you have fallen, the question is not whether you can rise but whether you will look to the One who lifts you up.
  • 3. Micah 7:18-20 is among the most comforting passages in all of Scripture for anyone burdened by guilt. God does not merely tolerate your sin—He delights in removing it. He actively enjoys showing mercy. Let this reshape how you approach Him after failure.
  • 4. "Cast all their sins into the depths of the sea" (v. 19). If God has thrown your sins into the ocean's deepest trench, stop diving for them. Forgiveness means they are gone—irrecoverably, permanently gone. Live in that freedom.
  • 5. The book ends with covenant faithfulness: "Thou wilt perform the truth to Jacob, and the mercy to Abraham" (v. 20). God's promises to Abraham still stand. What He swore to do, He will do. This is the foundation of all hope—not human performance but divine faithfulness to His own word.
