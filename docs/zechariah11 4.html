Zechariah — Chapter 11

1Open your doors, O Lebanon, that the fire may devour your cedars!

2Wail, O cypress, for the cedar has fallen, for the glorious trees are ruined! Wail, oaks of Bashan, for the thick forest has been felled!

3The sound of the wail of the shepherds, for their glory is ruined! The sound of the roar of the lions, for the thicket of the Jordan is ruined!

4Thus said the LORD my God: “Become shepherd of the flock doomed to slaughter.

5Those who buy them slaughter them and go unpunished, and those who sell them say, ‘Blessed be the LORD, I have become rich,’ and their own shepherds have no pity on them.

6For I will no longer have pity on the inhabitants of this land, declares the LORD. Behold, I will cause each of them to fall into the hand of his neighbor, and each into the hand of his king, and they shall crush the land, and I will deliver none from their hand.”

7So I became the shepherd of the flock doomed to be slaughtered by the sheep traders. And I took two staffs, one I named Favor, the other I named Union. And I tended the sheep.

8In one month I destroyed the three shepherds. But I became impatient with them, and they also detested me.

9So I said, “I will not be your shepherd. What is to die, let it die. What is to be destroyed, let it be destroyed. And let those who are left devour the flesh of one another.”

10And I took my staff Favor, and I broke it, annulling the covenant that I had made with all the peoples.

11So it was annulled on that day, and the sheep traders, who were watching me, knew that it was the word of the LORD.

12Then I said to them, “If it seems good to you, give me my wages; but if not, keep them.” And they weighed out as my wages thirty pieces of silver.

13Then the LORD said to me, “Throw it to the potter”—the lordly price at which I was priced by them. So I took the thirty pieces of silver and threw them into the house of the LORD, to the potter.

14Then I broke my second staff Union, annulling the brotherhood between Judah and Israel.

15Then the LORD said to me, “Take once more the equipment of a foolish shepherd.

16For behold, I am raising up in the land a shepherd who does not care for those being destroyed, or seek the young or heal the maimed or nourish the healthy, but devours the flesh of the fat ones, tearing off even their hoofs.

17“Woe to my worthless shepherd, who deserts the flock! May the sword strike his arm and his right eye! Let his arm be wholly withered, his right eye utterly blinded!”

Summary

Zechariah enacts a parable of two shepherd staffs — Favor and Union — breaking them to symbolize God's rejected covenant and the breaking of brotherhood between Judah and Israel, valued at thirty pieces of silver.

Key Verses

v.4
"Feed the flock of the slaughter" (KJV) / "Become shepherd of the flock doomed to slaughter" (ESV) — God commands Zechariah to shepherd a flock whose owners exploit and kill them without guilt. This pictures Israel under corrupt leaders who profit from the people's suffering. The Good Shepherd comes to a people already being destroyed.
v.7
"I took unto me two staves; the one I called Beauty, and the other I called Bands" (KJV) / "one I named Favor, the other I named Union" (ESV) — Two aspects of God's relationship with Israel: His gracious favor (Beauty/Favor) and the unity He provides (Bands/Union). When the shepherd is rejected, BOTH are broken.
v.12
"they weighed for my price thirty pieces of silver" (KJV) — The exact sum Judas received for betraying Christ (Matt 26:15). Thirty shekels was the compensation for a slave gored by an ox (Exod 21:32) — the lowest valuation possible. Israel valued the Good Shepherd at slave-price. This is what the world thought Jesus was worth.
v.13
"Cast it unto the potter: a goodly price that I was prised at of them" (KJV) — Dripping with divine sarcasm: "a lordly price!" The silver is thrown to the potter in the Temple — precisely what happened with Judas's blood money (Matt 27:5-10). Zechariah wrote this 500+ years before the event.
v.16
"I will raise up a shepherd in the land, which shall not visit those that be cut off, neither shall seek the young one, nor heal that that is broken" (KJV) — After rejecting the Good Shepherd, Israel receives a FOOLISH shepherd who devours rather than feeds. When you reject Christ, you get anti-Christ. History confirms this tragically in Israel's suffering under corrupt and oppressive leadership.
v.17
"Woe to the idol shepherd that leaveth the flock!" (KJV) / "Woe to my worthless shepherd, who deserts the flock!" (ESV) — The foolish shepherd's judgment is certain. His arm (strength) will wither; his eye (perception) will be blinded. Judgment comes to those who exploit God's people.

Cross-References

  • v.7 — Ezekiel 34:23 — "I will set up one shepherd over them... even my servant David" — God's solution for bad shepherds. v.12 — Matthew 26:15 — "They covenanted with him for thirty pieces of silver" — Judas's price for Christ. v.13 — Matthew 27:3-10 — Judas throwing the silver into the Temple; the priests buying the potter's field. v.13 — Exodus 21:32 — Thirty shekels: the price of a dead slave. v.10 — Romans 11:1-10 — The partial hardening/rejection of Israel after rejecting Messiah. v.14 — Ezekiel 37:15-22 — The two sticks (Judah and Israel) that will one day be reunited. v.16 — John 10:12-13 — "The hireling... careth not for the sheep" — the false shepherd who abandons the flock. v.16 — 2 Thessalonians 2:3-4 — The man of lawlessness — the ultimate "foolish shepherd."

Translations

  • v.7 — "Beauty" (KJV) / "Favor" (ESV) — Hebrew "no'am" = grace, pleasantness, beauty, favor. Represents God's gracious covenant relationship with His people.
  • v.7 — "Bands" (KJV) / "Union" (ESV) — Hebrew "choblim" = cords, bands, binding together. Represents the brotherhood that holds Judah and Israel together as one people.
  • v.12 — "thirty pieces of silver" — Hebrew "sheloshim kasef." The exact compensation for a slave (Exod 21:32). The insult is deliberate: the Good Shepherd is valued at the cheapest possible rate. This is contempt, not payment.
  • v.13 — "the potter" — Hebrew "yotser." Why "the potter"? Perhaps connected to the potter's field (Matt 27:7) or to God as the Potter who forms vessels (Jer 18:1-6). The connection between Zechariah's prophecy and the NT fulfillment is remarkably precise.
  • v.17 — "idol shepherd" (KJV) / "worthless shepherd" (ESV) — Hebrew "ro'i ha'elil" = shepherd of nothingness/worthless- ness. "Elil" is related to idols (worthless things). A shepherd who is himself an idol — empty, powerless, and destructive.

Reflection

  • 1. Rejecting the Good Shepherd has consequences (vv.8-10). When Israel despised Christ, they received oppressive leaders instead. Whom you reject determines whom you receive. If you reject Christ's gentle rule, you will end up under harsher masters — sin, self, or the world.
  • 2. God was valued at slave-price (v.12). Thirty pieces of silver — that's what the world thought Jesus was worth. How do YOU value Christ? Is He slave-price to you — an afterthought, a convenience? Or is He the Pearl of great price for whom you sell everything? (Matt 13:46)
  • 3. When "Beauty" is broken, grace departs (v.10). God's favor is not automatic. When persistently rejected, He withdraws His gracious protection. Don't take God's patience for granted. Covenant blessings have covenant conditions.
  • 4. When "Bands" is broken, unity dissolves (v.14). Rejection of Christ fractures community. Without the true Shepherd, the flock divides against itself. Unity among God's people is maintained by shared submission to Christ. When He is rejected, brotherhood disintegrates.
  • 5. The foolish shepherd devours what the Good Shepherd would feed (v.16). False leaders consume the people they should serve. Be discerning about who shepherds you. A true shepherd heals, seeks, and feeds. A false one exploits, abandons, and devours.
