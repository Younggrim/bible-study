Summary

A penitential lament — David pleads with God not to rebuke him in anger, weeping through the night on his sickbed, yet ends with sudden confidence that the LORD has heard his weeping.

Key Verses

v.1
"O LORD, rebuke me not in thine anger, neither chasten me in thy hot displeasure." (KJV) — David does not ask God to withhold discipline entirely — only to temper it. He accepts correction but asks it come from love, not wrath. This is the posture of a child who knows the Father's right to discipline.
v.3
"My soul is also sore vexed: but thou, O LORD, how long?" (KJV) — The incomplete sentence is powerful. "How long?" — how long until relief? Until healing? Until answer? The question trails off because there is no answer yet. The silence IS the suffering.
v.6
"I am weary with my groaning; all the night make I my bed to swim; I water my couch with my tears." (KJV) — Hyperbole expressing genuine extremity. David's nights are consumed with weeping. This is not stoic faith — this is raw, honest, exhausted anguish brought before God without sanitization.
v.8
"Depart from me, all ye workers of iniquity; for the LORD hath heard the voice of my weeping." (KJV) — The sudden pivot. Mid-psalm, David shifts from pleading to commanding. Something changed — he received assurance. The basis: God heard his WEEPING (not eloquent prayer, but tears).

Cross-References

  • v.1 — Psalm 38:1 — "O LORD, rebuke me not in thy wrath." Same opening plea — Psalm 38 expands on this theme. v.1 — Hebrews 12:5-6 — "Whom the Lord loveth he chasteneth." Discipline as evidence of sonship. v.3 — Psalm 13:1 — "How long wilt thou forget me, O LORD? for ever? How long?" The classic "how long" lament. v.5 — Psalm 30:9 — "What profit is there in my blood, when I go down to the pit? Shall the dust praise thee?" Same argument: keep me alive to praise You. v.5 — Isaiah 38:18 — "The grave cannot praise thee, death cannot celebrate thee." Hezekiah uses the same logic. v.6 — Psalm 42:3 — "My tears have been my meat day and night." Tears as constant companion in suffering. v.8 — Matthew 7:23 — "Depart from me, ye that work iniquity." Jesus echoes David's language of dismissal. v.9 — 1 John 5:14-15 — "If we know that he hear us... we know that we have the petitions." Assurance of answered prayer.

Translations

  • v.2 — KJV: "I am weak" / ESV: "I am languishing" — Hebrew "umlal" = withering, fading, languishing. ESV captures the image of something wilting/dying. KJV's "weak" is understated.
  • v.4 — KJV: "for thy mercies' sake" / ESV: "for the sake of your steadfast love" — Hebrew "chesed" = covenant faithfulness, loyal love. ESV's "steadfast love" is the standard modern rendering and better conveys the covenantal nature of God's mercy.
  • v.5 — KJV: "in the grave" / ESV: "in Sheol" — Hebrew "Sheol" = the realm of the dead. KJV translates interpretively; ESV preserves the Hebrew concept. David's argument: the dead cannot praise You, so keep me alive.
  • v.6 — KJV: "make I my bed to swim" / ESV: "I flood my bed with tears" — Both express the same hyperbole. KJV's image of a swimming bed is more vivid; ESV is clearer.
  • v.10 — KJV: "let them return and be ashamed suddenly" / ESV: "they shall turn back and be put to shame in a moment" — KJV uses jussive (a wish/prayer); ESV uses future indicative (confident prediction). The Hebrew supports both — David may be requesting or declaring.

Reflection

  • 1. It is right to ask God to temper discipline (v.1). David does not reject correction — he asks it come from love rather than wrath. You may ask God for mercy even when you know you deserve rebuke. A child can ask, "Please be gentle" without denying the Father's right.
  • 2. "How long?" is a legitimate prayer (v.3). The unfinished question is the most honest prayer of suffering. You do not need to complete the sentence. God knows what you are asking.
  • 3. Tears are heard by God (v.8). David's breakthrough came not from eloquent theology but from the "voice of my weeping." God hears tears as clearly as words. Sometimes tears ARE the prayer.
  • 4. Assurance can come suddenly (v.8). The shift from v.7 to v.8 is instantaneous. One moment David is weeping; the next he is commanding enemies to depart. The Spirit can bring confidence in a moment — mid-prayer, mid-tears, mid-crisis.
  • 5. Suffering is not sanitized in Scripture (v.6). The Bible does not present faith as emotional numbness. David floods his bed with tears, his eye wastes away from grief — and none of this disqualifies him before God. Bring your whole self.
