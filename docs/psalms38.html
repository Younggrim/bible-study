Psalms — Chapter 38

1O LORD, rebuke me not in your anger, nor discipline me in your wrath!

2For your arrows have sunk into me, and your hand has come down on me.

3There is no soundness in my flesh because of your indignation; there is no health in my bones because of my sin.

4For my iniquities have gone over my head; like a heavy burden, they are too heavy for me.

5My wounds stink and fester because of my foolishness,

6I am utterly bowed down and prostrate; all the day I go about mourning.

7For my sides are filled with burning, and there is no soundness in my flesh.

8I am feeble and crushed; I groan because of the tumult of my heart.

9O Lord, all my longing is before you; my sighing is not hidden from you.

10My heart throbs; my strength fails me, and the light of my eyes—it also has gone from me.

11My friends and companions stand aloof from my plague, and my nearest kin stand far off.

12Those who seek my life lay their snares; those who seek my hurt speak of ruin and meditate treachery all day long.

13But I am like a deaf man; I do not hear, like a mute man who does not open his mouth.

14I have become like a man who does not hear, and in whose mouth are no rebukes.

15But for you, O LORD, do I wait; it is you, O Lord my God, who will answer.

16For I said, “Only let them not rejoice over me, who boast against me when my foot slips!”

17For I am ready to fall, and my pain is ever before me.

18I confess my iniquity; I am sorry for my sin.

19But my foes are vigorous, they are mighty, and many are those who hate me wrongfully.

20Those who render me evil for good accuse me because I follow after good.

21Do not forsake me, O LORD! O my God, be not far from me!

22Make haste to help me, O Lord, my salvation!

Summary

A penitential lament — David groans under God's discipline for sin, his body afflicted, friends distant, enemies pressing; yet he waits for the LORD and confesses his iniquity, pleading 'forsake me not.'

Key Verses

Cross-References

  • v.1 — Psalm 6:1: Identical opening — both psalms plead for mercy within divine chastening, forming bookends of the penitential tradition.
  • v.2 — Job 6:4: "The arrows of the Almighty are within me" — Job uses the same imagery of God's arrows piercing the sufferer, connecting David's experience to the broader wisdom of suffering.
  • v.3 — Psalm 32:3-4: The same connection between sin and physical deterioration — bones wasting, strength failing under the weight of conviction and divine hand.
  • v.4 — Matthew 11:28: "Come unto me, all ye that labour and are heavy laden" — Christ's invitation directly addresses those crushed under burdens too heavy to bear, the very condition David describes here.
  • v.11 — Job 19:13-19: Job's friends and family withdrawing — the universal experience that suffering isolates, as others distance themselves from the afflicted.
  • v.13-14 — Isaiah 53:7: "He was oppressed, and he was afflicted, yet he opened not his mouth" — the Suffering Servant's silence echoes David's silent endurance before accusers.
  • v.18 — Psalm 32:5; 1 John 1:9: The pattern of confession — declaring iniquity and expressing sorrow for sin — as the path to restoration.
  • v.22 — Psalm 40:13,17; Psalm 70:1: The urgent plea "make haste to help me" — a cry of desperation that recognizes only God's swift intervention can save.

Translations

  • v.2 — "stick fast in me" (KJV) / "have sunk into me" (ESV): Hebrew nachatu means to descend, settle, or sink in. KJV's "stick fast" emphasizes being embedded permanently; ESV's "sunk into" conveys penetration and depth. The arrows are not superficial wounds — they have reached deep into David's being.
  • v.5 — "My wounds stink and are corrupt" (KJV) / "My wounds stink and fester" (ESV): Hebrew ba'ashu nimaqqu — literally "they stink, they are putrid/rotting." This graphic language may describe literal infection or metaphorically portray the nauseating reality of unhealed sin. Either way, the imagery is deliberately repulsive — sin left untreated rots.
  • v.7 — "my loins are filled with a loathsome disease" (KJV) / "my sides are filled with burning" (ESV): Hebrew niqleh — from qalah, meaning "to roast, burn, scorch." KJV interprets this as disease; ESV translates the literal sense of burning/inflammation. The burning in his "loins" (kesalim — flanks, lower back) suggests internal inflammation or pain in his midsection.
  • v.11 — "My lovers and my friends stand aloof from my sore" (KJV) / "My friends and companions stand aloof from my plague" (ESV): Hebrew nega can mean "plague, stroke, wound, affliction." KJV uses "sore"; ESV uses "plague." The word is the same used for leprosy signs in Leviticus 13-14, suggesting David's condition caused others to treat him as ritually unclean — socially quarantined.
  • v.13 — "I, as a deaf man, heard not; and I was as a dumb man" (KJV) / "I am like a deaf man; I do not hear, like a mute man" (ESV): David's silence is deliberate strategy, not inability. Before his enemies' taunts, he chooses not to respond — not because he cannot speak but because he trusts God to answer for him. This restraint mirrors Christ before Pilate.

Reflection

  • 1. David's honesty about the physical effects of sin and guilt (vv.3-8) validates the mind-body-spirit connection. Unresolved guilt, unconfessed sin, and divine discipline can manifest in physical symptoms — sleeplessness, chronic pain, exhaustion. Is your body telling you something your spirit has not yet addressed?
  • 2. The withdrawal of friends during suffering (v.11) is one of life's most painful realities. When you most need support, people often distance themselves. Have you experienced this? And conversely — have you been the friend who "stood afar off" from someone in their dark season?
  • 3. David's choice to remain silent before his enemies (vv.13-14) rather than defend himself is a profound act of trust. When falsely accused or misrepresented, the instinct is to fight back. But David's silence says: "God will vindicate me; I don't need to vindicate myself." Is there a situation where God is calling you to silent trust rather than self-defense?
  • 4. "For I will declare mine iniquity; I will be sorry for my sin" (v.18) — David does not blame his enemies, his circumstances, or God. He owns his sin without excuse. True repentance begins with ownership. Are you still making excuses for patterns God has clearly convicted you about?
  • 5. The psalm ends with desperate urgency: "Make haste to help me, O Lord my salvation" (v.22). Sometimes prayer is not eloquent — it is simply "Help me, quickly!" God does not require polished prayers. He responds to desperate, honest cries. When words fail, is "Help!" enough?
