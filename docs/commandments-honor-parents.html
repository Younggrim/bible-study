Ten Commandments

Honor Your Father and Mother

The Commandment

The fifth commandment — "Honor your father and your mother, that your days may be long in the land" (Exodus 20:12) — serves as the bridge between our duties toward God (commandments 1-4) and our duties toward others (commandments 6-10). It is the first commandment with a promise attached, indicating its foundational importance for both individual wellbeing and societal stability. The family is God's primary institution for transmitting faith, values, and blessing across generations.

The Hebrew word for "honor" (kabed) means to give weight to, to treat as significant and valuable. It encompasses respect, obedience (for children), care (for aging parents), gratitude, and a posture of esteem. This was countercultural in the ancient world where the elderly were often discarded once they could no longer contribute economically. God's command elevated parents to a place of dignity regardless of their usefulness.

Paul reaffirms this commandment for the Christian community in Ephesians 6:1-3, noting it as "the first commandment with a promise." Jesus also upheld its ongoing validity while condemning religious traditions that allowed people to avoid caring for parents under the guise of dedicating resources to God (Matthew 15:4-6). The New Testament teaching is clear: honoring parents remains a lifelong calling that adapts in expression but never expires.

Key Scriptures

  • Exodus 20:12 — "Honor your father and your mother, that your days may be long in the land that the LORD your God is giving you."
  • Ephesians 6:1-3 — "Children, obey your parents in the Lord, for this is right. 'Honor your father and mother' — this is the first commandment with a promise."
  • Colossians 3:20 — "Children, obey your parents in everything, for this pleases the Lord."
  • Proverbs 1:8-9 — "Hear, my son, your father's instruction, and forsake not your mother's teaching, for they are a graceful garland for your head."
  • Matthew 15:4-6 — "For God commanded, 'Honor your father and your mother'... But you say that whoever tells his father or mother, 'What you would have gained from me is given to God,' he need not honor his father."

Heart of the Law

At its deepest level, this commandment teaches us about the nature of authority, gratitude, and generational faithfulness. Honoring parents is training ground for honoring God — learning to respect authority we did not choose, to express gratitude for imperfect gifts, and to recognize that we stand on the shoulders of those who came before us. A culture that dishonors its parents will eventually dishonor all authority, including God's.

This commandment also carries special weight for those with difficult or broken parental relationships. Honoring does not mean excusing abuse or pretending dysfunction did not occur. It means refusing to carry bitterness, choosing forgiveness where possible, and recognizing the humanity and image-bearing dignity of our parents even when they failed us. It is ultimately about releasing them to God's judgment rather than carrying the weight of their failures as our identity.

Reflection Questions

  • What does "honoring" your parents look like in your current season of life — whether they are present, distant, or deceased?
  • How has your relationship with your earthly parents shaped your understanding of God as Father?
  • Are there areas of bitterness or unforgiveness toward your parents that need to be surrendered to God?
  • In what practical ways can you express honor, gratitude, and care toward your parents this week?
