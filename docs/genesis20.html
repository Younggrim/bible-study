Genesis — Chapter 20

1From there Abraham journeyed toward the territory of the Negeb and lived between Kadesh and Shur; and he sojourned in Gerar.

2And Abraham said of Sarah his wife, “She is my sister.” And Abimelech king of Gerar sent and took Sarah.

3But God came to Abimelech in a dream by night and said to him, “Behold, you are a dead man because of the woman whom you have taken, for she is a man’s wife.”

4Now Abimelech had not approached her. So he said, “Lord, will you kill an innocent people?

5Did he not himself say to me, ‘She is my sister’? And she herself said, ‘He is my brother.’ In the integrity of my heart and the innocence of my hands I have done this.”

6Then God said to him in the dream, “Yes, I know that you have done this in the integrity of your heart, and it was I who kept you from sinning against me. Therefore I did not let you touch her.

7Now then, return the man’s wife, for he is a prophet, so that he will pray for you, and you shall live. But if you do not return her, know that you shall surely die, you and all who are yours.”

8So Abimelech rose early in the morning and called all his servants and told them all these things. And the men were very much afraid.

9Then Abimelech called Abraham and said to him, “What have you done to us? And how have I sinned against you, that you have brought on me and my kingdom a great sin? You have done to me things that ought not to be done.”

10And Abimelech said to Abraham, “What did you see, that you did this thing?”

11Abraham said, “I did it because I thought, ‘There is no fear of God at all in this place, and they will kill me because of my wife.’

12Besides, she is indeed my sister, the daughter of my father though not the daughter of my mother, and she became my wife.

13And when God caused me to wander from my father’s house, I said to her, ‘This is the kindness you must do me: at every place to which we come, say of me, “He is my brother.”’”

14Then Abimelech took sheep and oxen, and male servants and female servants, and gave them to Abraham, and returned Sarah his wife to him.

15And Abimelech said, “Behold, my land is before you; dwell where it pleases you.”

16To Sarah he said, “Behold, I have given your brother a thousand pieces of silver. It is a sign of your innocence in the eyes of all who are with you, and before everyone you are vindicated.”

17Then Abraham prayed to God, and God healed Abimelech, and also healed his wife and female slaves so that they bore children.

18For the LORD had closed all the wombs of the house of Abimelech because of Sarah, Abraham’s wife.

Summary

Abraham deceives Abimelech by calling Sarah his sister; God protects Sarah and rebukes the king in a dream.

Key Verses

  • v.6 — "I also kept you from sinning against me. Therefore I did not let you touch her." (ESV) — God's sovereign protection of His plan.
  • v.7 — "He is a prophet, and he will pray for you." (ESV) — Abraham's identity despite his failure.
  • v.11 — "I did it because I thought, 'There is no fear of God at all in this place.'" (ESV) — Fear-based assumptions leading to sin.

Authorship & Background

  • Author: Moses (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).
  • Historical Context: Chapter 20 records Abraham's second "she is my sister" deception — this time with Abimelech king of Gerar (the first was with Pharaoh in ch.12). Remarkably, this happens AFTER God's promise of Isaac through Sarah (chs.17-18). Abraham's recurring sin of fear-driven deception threatens the very promise God made. Yet God protects Sarah (and the promise) despite Abraham's failure. The chapter reveals both Abraham's persistent weakness and God's persistent faithfulness.
  • Abraham's Deception (vv.1-2): Abraham moves to Gerar and again says Sarah is his sister (v.2). Abimelech takes Sarah (v.2). This is the same sin as chapter 12 — Abraham hasn't fully conquered this fear.
  • God Protects (vv.3-7): God warns Abimelech in a dream: "thou art but a dead man" (v.3). Abimelech protests his innocence (vv.4-5). God acknowledges: "I know... I also withheld thee from sinning" (v.6). God protected Sarah even from Abraham's failure. Command: restore her; Abraham will pray for you (v.7).
  • The Confrontation (vv.8-13): Abimelech rebukes Abraham (vv.9-10). Abraham's excuse: "I thought, Surely the fear of God is not in this place" (v.11). He reveals it's a half-truth: Sarah IS his half-sister (v.12). He admits this was a pre-arranged deception from the beginning (v.13).
  • Resolution (vv.14-18): Abimelech gives gifts and land (vv.14-15). Abraham prays; God heals Abimelech's household (vv.17-18).

Map & Geography

  • Gerar (v.1) — a Philistine city in the southern coastal plain, between Kadesh and Shur. Abraham moves southwest from Hebron into this region.
  • This is Philistine territory — the coastal lowlands of southern Canaan (distinct from the hill country where Abraham usually dwells).

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): enduringword.com Guzik covers the repeated deception, God's protection, Abimelech's integrity, and Abraham's intercession.
  • Charles Spurgeon: Sermon: "God's Overruling Providence" (MTP Vol. 14, No. 836) "God withheld Abimelech from sinning — sovereign protection. Even when Abraham failed to protect Sarah, God did not fail. The promise of Isaac was never in danger — because it depended on God's faithfulness, not Abraham's. Your failures cannot derail God's purposes. He protects His plan even from your mistakes."

Translations

Show translations:

v.2 — Hebrew "achoti hi" — she is my sister.
KJV: "She is my sister"
ESV: "She is my sister"
ASV: "She is my sister"
NET: "She is my sister"
WEB: "She is my sister"
Analysis: All five translations are identical. The same deception as 12:13.
A half-truth (v.12) used to deceive. Repeated sin — Abraham's persistent
weakness that threatened the very promise of Isaac.

v.3 — Hebrew "hinneka meth" — behold you are dead / you are a dead man.
KJV: "thou art but a dead man"
ESV: "you are a dead man"
ASV: "thou art but a dead man"
NET: "You are as good as dead because of the woman you have taken"
WEB: "you are a dead man"
Analysis: KJV/ASV/ESV/WEB give the direct Hebrew statement. NET uses the
strikingly colloquial "as good as dead," a modern idiom that captures the
urgency of the divine warning. Hebrew "Elohim" (not Yahweh) — God
communicates with pagans too, to protect His purposes. ESV adds "by night"
for contextual clarity about the dream.

v.4 — Hebrew "hagoy gam-tsaddiq taharog" — will you also slay a righteous nation?
KJV: "Lord, wilt thou slay also a righteous nation?"
ESV: "Lord, will you kill an innocent people?"
ASV: "Lord, wilt thou slay also a righteous nation?"
NET: "Lord, would you really slaughter an innocent nation?"
WEB: "Lord, will you kill even a righteous nation?"
Analysis: NET uses "slaughter" — stronger than "slay" or "kill" — and frames
it as a rhetorical question about God's justice. Abimelech's protest
parallels Abraham's intercession in 18:23-25 regarding divine justice.

v.7 — Hebrew "nabi" — prophet (first use of this term in Scripture).
KJV: "he is a prophet"
ESV: "he is a prophet"
ASV: "he is a prophet"
NET: "he is a prophet"
WEB: "he is a prophet"
Analysis: All identical. First use of "prophet" in Scripture. Despite his
failure, Abraham retains his prophetic identity and intercessory role. God
defines identity by calling, not by performance.

v.11 — Hebrew "en yir'ath Elohim" — there is no fear of God.
KJV: "the fear of God is not in this place"
ESV: "There is no fear of God at all in this place"
ASV: "Surely the fear of God is not in this place"
NET: "Surely no one fears God in this place"
WEB: "Surely the fear of God is not in this place"
Analysis: ESV adds "at all" for emphasis. NET personalizes it — "no one
fears God" — making it about people rather than an abstract quality of the
place. Ironically, Abimelech showed more integrity than Abraham in this
episode. Abraham's fear-based assumptions were wrong.

v.13 — Hebrew "hith'u" — caused to wander.
KJV: "God caused me to wander from my father's house"
ESV: "God caused me to wander from my father's house"
ASV: "God caused me to wander from my father's house"
NET: "When God made me wander from my father's house"
WEB: "when God caused me to wander from my father's house"
Analysis: All use "wander" for Hebrew "hith'u." Abraham reveals that he sees
his entire life as divinely-directed wandering — and that this deception was
a pre-arranged strategy from the very beginning of his journey.

v.16 — Hebrew "kesuth 'enayim" — a covering of the eyes (legal vindication).
KJV: "he is to thee a covering of the eyes"
ESV: "it is a sign of your innocence"
ASV: "it is for thee a covering of the eyes"
NET: "This is compensation for you so that you will stand vindicated"
WEB: "it is for you a covering of the eyes"
Analysis: The Hebrew idiom is difficult. KJV/ASV/WEB retain the literal
"covering of the eyes." ESV interprets as "sign of innocence." NET makes the
cultural/legal significance fully explicit — the silver payment serves as
legal vindication for Sarah's honor before all.

Reflection

  • 1. Abraham repeated the same sin from chapter 12. Do you have recurring sins — patterns you keep falling into? What would it take to break the cycle?
  • 2. God protected His plan despite Abraham's failure (v.6). Your failures cannot derail God's purposes. Does this comfort you? It should — but it shouldn't make you careless.
  • 3. Abraham assumed "the fear of God is not in this place" (v.11). He was wrong — Abimelech had more integrity than Abraham. Are you making assumptions about people that lead you to compromise?
  • 4. Despite his failure, Abraham was still called "a prophet" (v.7) and still interceded effectively (v.17). God uses imperfect people. Your failures don't disqualify you from service — but they do need to be addressed.
