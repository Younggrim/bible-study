Summary

Proverbs on fools, sluggards, and troublemakers — 'answer not a fool according to his folly' paired with 'answer a fool according to his folly'; the sluggard turns on his bed like a door on hinges; and the deceitful heart.

Key Verses

  • v.4-5 — "Answer not a fool according to his folly, lest you be like him yourself. Answer a fool according to his folly, lest he be wise in his own eyes." (ESV) — The famous "paradox." Both are true — wisdom lies in knowing WHEN to apply each. Sometimes engaging a fool drags you down to his level (v.4). Other times, silence lets him think he's won (v.5). Discernment, not a fixed rule, is required.
  • v.11 — "Like a dog that returns to his vomit is a fool who repeats his folly." (ESV) — One of Scripture's most visceral images. The fool is disgusting not because he sinned once but because he returns to the same sin repeatedly. He expelled it and voluntarily consumed it again. Peter quotes this in 2 Peter 2:22 regarding apostates who return to sin after knowing the truth.
  • v.12 — "Do you see a man who is wise in his own eyes? There is more hope for a fool than for him." (ESV) — The most hopeless condition is not foolishness — it's self-satisfied foolishness that THINKS it's wisdom. A fool might be corrected; a man who believes he's already wise CANNOT be reached. Pride + ignorance = terminal.
  • v.16 — "The sluggard is wiser in his own eyes than seven men who can answer sensibly." (ESV) — The lazy person is unteachable. Seven wise counselors could argue perfectly, and the sluggard would dismiss them all. He has an answer for everything — except why he hasn't accomplished anything. Laziness is not merely inactive; it's arrogant.
  • v.20 — "For lack of wood the fire goes out, and where there is no whisperer, quarreling ceases." (ESV) — Gossip is fuel for conflict. Remove the gossip and the conflict dies naturally — like a fire without wood. If strife continues in your environment, look for the talebearer who keeps feeding it. Remove the fuel; the fire dies.

  • v.11 → 2 Peter 2:22 — Peter quotes this verse directly regarding apostates who return to sin after knowing Christ.
  • v.12 → Proverbs 29:20 — "Do you see a man who is hasty in his words? There is more hope for a fool than for him." (ESV) Same structure of comparison.
  • v.13 → Proverbs 22:13 — "The sluggard says, 'There is a lion outside!'" (ESV) The identical sluggard excuse repeated.
  • v.17 → 2 Samuel 18:19-33 — Meddling in matters not your own brings danger — Ahimaaz running with news.
  • v.20 → James 3:5-6 — "How great a forest is set ablaze by such a small fire!" (ESV) The tongue as fire.
  • v.22 → Proverbs 18:8 — Identical proverb repeated: whisperer's words as "delicious morsels." (ESV)
  • v.27 → Psalm 7:15-16 — "He makes a pit, digging it out, and falls into the hole that he has made." (ESV) Evil boomerangs.

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): "https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/proverbs-26/" target="_blank">enduringword.com Guzik addresses the vv.4-5 paradox: "These are not contradictions but situational wisdom. Sometimes the fool must be answered so he doesn't mistake your silence for agreement. Other times he must NOT be answered because engaging him validates his foolish premise. The wise person reads the situation and responds accordingly." On
  • v.11, he notes: "The horror of this image is the RETURN. The fool doesn't merely fail — he fails, recovers, then deliberately goes back to the same failure. Repetition of folly is the dog's nature."
  • Charles Spurgeon: "As a dog returneth to his vomit, so a fool returneth to his folly. How many times we see this! A man repents — or seems to. He forsakes his sin — for a season. But when temptation returns, he goes back. Like the dog, he returns to what should disgust him. Oh, that men would learn that TRUE repentance is permanent! The dog returns because it is his NATURE. The fool returns because his heart was never changed. Only the new birth produces a new appetite."

Translations

  • v.11 → KJV: "As a dog returneth to his vomit" / ESV: "Like a dog that returns to his vomit" — Hebrew "kelev shav 'al qe'o" = a dog returning upon his vomit. Dogs were considered unclean animals in Israel. The image is deliberately revolting — the fool's behavior should provoke the same visceral disgust.
  • v.16 → KJV: "seven men that can render a reason" / ESV: "seven men who can answer sensibly" — "Seven" = completeness. "Render a reason" = Hebrew "ta'am" = taste, judgment, discernment. The sluggard thinks himself wiser than a perfect quorum of the most discerning counselors. His conceit exceeds even his laziness.
  • v.23 → KJV: "Burning lips and a wicked heart are like a potsherd covered with silver dross" / ESV: "Like the glaze covering an earthen vessel are fervent lips with an evil heart" — "Silver dross" (KJV) vs. "glaze" (ESV). Hebrew "keseph sigim" = silver dross (waste from smelting) or possibly a cheap glaze. Either way: an attractive exterior concealing worthless material underneath. Beautiful words + wicked heart = deception.
  • v.2 → KJV: "the curse causeless shall not come" / ESV: "a curse that is causeless does not alight" — Hebrew "lo tavo" = will not come/rest/land. An undeserved curse has no power — it flies around like a bird but never lands. Only curses that God authorizes find their target.

Reflection

  • 1. Learn the vv.4-5 paradox (when to engage, when to walk away). Not every fool deserves a response. Not every argument merits engagement. But sometimes silence lets error stand unchallenged. Ask God for discernment: when does engagement help, and when does it only dignify folly?
  • 2. Don't return to your vomit (v.11). What sin have you repented of and then returned to? The pattern of sin-repent-return- repeat is the dog's nature, not the Christian's. If you keep going back, ask whether your repentance was real or merely discomfort.
  • 3. Beware self-conceit (v.12). The most dangerous spiritual condition is thinking you're already wise. If you cannot receive correction, if you dismiss every counselor, if you always have an answer — you may be in a worse state than the fool you look down on.
  • 4. Remove the fuel from conflict (v.20). If strife keeps burning in your relationships, workplace, or church — identify the talebearer. Who is carrying stories? Who is fueling the fire? Remove the gossip and the conflict dies. Sometimes the talebearer is you.
  • 5. Beware attractive packaging on wicked content (v.23). Silver- coated potsherds. Flattering lips with a hateful heart. Learn to test what's underneath the surface. Not everyone who speaks beautifully means well. Look at fruit, not just words.
