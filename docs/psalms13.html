Summary

A brief lament — David cries 'How long, O LORD?' four times, feeling forgotten and overwhelmed by his enemy, yet ends trusting in God's steadfast love and singing because God has dealt bountifully.

Key Verses

v.1
"How long wilt thou forget me, O LORD? for ever? how long wilt thou hide thy face from me?" (KJV) — The most concentrated expression of lament in the Psalter. Four "how long" questions in two verses — directed at God (forgotten, hidden), at self (sorrow), and at enemies (exalted). Every dimension of suffering is covered.
v.5
"But I have trusted in thy mercy; my heart shall rejoice in thy salvation." (KJV) — The "BUT" of faith. Nothing in the psalm explains what changed between v.4 and v.5. The circumstances have not changed. The enemies have not fled. God has not audibly responded. Yet David DECIDES to trust. Faith is a choice, not a feeling.
v.6
"I will sing unto the LORD, because he hath dealt bountifully with me." (KJV) — Past tense: "he HATH dealt bountifully." David recalls past faithfulness as the basis for present trust. Even in current darkness, God's past goodness is evidence enough to sing.

Cross-References

  • v.1 — Psalm 10:1 — "Why standest thou afar off, O LORD?" Same sense of divine distance. v.1 — Psalm 44:24 — "Wherefore hidest thou thy face?" Communal version of the same question. v.3 — Psalm 6:5 — "In death there is no remembrance of thee." Same argument: keep me alive for Your glory. v.5 — Psalm 52:8 — "I trust in the mercy of God for ever and ever." Same anchor: God's mercy. v.5 — Lamentations 3:21-23 — "This I recall to my mind, therefore have I hope. It is of the LORD's mercies that we are not consumed." Same pivot from despair to hope via remembered mercy. v.6 — Psalm 116:7 — "The LORD hath dealt bountifully with thee." Same grateful memory of past provision.

Translations

  • v.1 — KJV: "How long wilt thou forget me" / ESV: "Will you forget me forever?" — Both capture the anguish. The Hebrew construction emphasizes duration — the suffering feels eternal. "Forever?" is the question suffering always asks.
  • v.3 — KJV: "lighten mine eyes" / ESV: "light up my eyes" — Hebrew "ha'ira eynay" = make my eyes shine/be bright. Dim eyes indicate approaching death (1 Sam 14:27-29). David asks for vitality — life-light in his eyes — lest death overtake him.
  • v.5 — KJV: "thy mercy" / ESV: "your steadfast love" — Hebrew "chesed" = covenant faithfulness, steadfast love. ESV's translation preserves the covenantal dimension — this is not general kindness but committed, unbreakable love.
  • v.6 — KJV: "he hath dealt bountifully" / ESV: "he has dealt bountifully" — Same. Hebrew "gamal" = dealt fully, repaid with good. Past-tense confidence: God HAS BEEN good, and that record is the basis for future trust.

Reflection

  • 1. "How long?" is the prayer of endurance (vv.1-2). The question implies duration — you have already been waiting. Long-term suffering produces this prayer. It is legitimate, biblical, and heard by God. Bring your "how long?" to Him.
  • 2. The psalm moves from complaint to praise with NO external change (vv.4→5). Nothing happened between verses 4 and 5. The enemies didn't leave. God didn't audibly answer. David simply CHOSE to trust. This teaches that the turn from despair to faith is often a choice, not a circumstantial change.
  • 3. Past faithfulness grounds present trust (v.6). "He HATH dealt bountifully" — David remembers. When the present is dark, recall the past. God's track record is evidence. If He was faithful then, He is faithful now.
  • 4. Six verses — complete emotional journey. From the bottom of despair to singing praise in six verses. This psalm gives permission to feel everything AND to choose worship. The two are not contradictory. You can hurt AND trust simultaneously.
  • 5. The brevity is the power. This psalm does not explain, theologize, or defend. It simply models the complete cycle of faith under pressure. Use it as a template: name your complaint, bring your request, choose trust, decide to sing.
