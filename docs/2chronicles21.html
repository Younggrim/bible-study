2 Chronicles — Chapter 21

1Jehoshaphat slept with his fathers and was buried with his fathers in the city of David, and Jehoram his son reigned in his place.

2He had brothers, the sons of Jehoshaphat: Azariah, Jehiel, Zechariah, Azariah, Michael, and Shephatiah; all these were the sons of Jehoshaphat king of Israel.

3Their father gave them great gifts of silver, gold, and valuable possessions, together with fortified cities in Judah, but he gave the kingdom to Jehoram, because he was the firstborn.

4When Jehoram had ascended the throne of his father and was established, he killed all his brothers with the sword, and also some of the princes of Israel.

5Jehoram was thirty-two years old when he became king, and he reigned eight years in Jerusalem.

6And he walked in the way of the kings of Israel, as the house of Ahab had done, for the daughter of Ahab was his wife. And he did what was evil in the sight of the LORD.

7Yet the LORD was not willing to destroy the house of David, because of the covenant that he had made with David, and since he had promised to give a lamp to him and to his sons forever.

8In his days Edom revolted from the rule of Judah and set up a king of their own.

9Then Jehoram passed over with his commanders and all his chariots, and he rose by night and struck the Edomites who had surrounded him and his chariot commanders.

10So Edom revolted from the rule of Judah to this day. At that time Libnah also revolted from his rule, because he had forsaken the LORD, the God of his fathers.

11Moreover, he made high places in the hill country of Judah and led the inhabitants of Jerusalem into whoredom and made Judah go astray.

12And a letter came to him from Elijah the prophet, saying, “Thus says the LORD, the God of David your father, ‘Because you have not walked in the ways of Jehoshaphat your father, or in the ways of Asa king of Judah,

13but have walked in the way of the kings of Israel and have enticed Judah and the inhabitants of Jerusalem into whoredom, as the house of Ahab led Israel into whoredom, and also you have killed your brothers, of your father’s house, who were better than you,

14behold, the LORD will bring a great plague on your people, your children, your wives, and all your possessions,

15and you yourself will have a severe sickness with a disease of your bowels, until your bowels come out because of the disease, day by day.’”

16And the LORD stirred up against Jehoram the anger of the Philistines and of the Arabians who are near the Ethiopians.

17And they came up against Judah and invaded it and carried away all the possessions they found that belonged to the king’s house, and also his sons and his wives, so that no son was left to him except Jehoahaz, his youngest son.

18And after all this the LORD struck him in his bowels with an incurable disease.

19In the course of time, at the end of two years, his bowels came out because of the disease, and he died in great agony. His people made no fire in his honor, like the fires made for his fathers.

20He was thirty-two years old when he began to reign, and he reigned eight years in Jerusalem. And he departed with no one’s regret. They buried him in the city of David, but not in the tombs of the kings.

19After about two years his intestines came out because of the disease, so that he died a very painful death. His people did not make a bonfire to honor him, as they had done for his ancestors.

Summary

Jehoram kills all his brothers upon taking the throne and follows the wicked ways of Ahab's house through his wife Athaliah. God brings Philistine and Arabian invasions, and Jehoram dies of a horrible bowel disease, unmourned by his people.

Key Verses

  • v.7 — "Yet the LORD was not willing to destroy the house of David, because of the covenant that he had made with David, and since he had promised to give a lamp to him and to his sons forever." (ESV)— Despite Jehoram's extreme wickedness, God preserved the Davidic line because of His unconditional covenant (2 Samuel 7:12-16). The ESV renders "a lamp to him and to his sons forever." The "lamp" metaphor represents continuing dynasty and hope. God's covenant faithfulness — not Jehoram's merit — prevented the extinction of David's line. This verse anchors the chapter's darkness in the certainty of God's promise.
  • v.12 — "And a letter came to him from Elijah the prophet, saying, “Thus says the LORD, the God of David your father, ‘Because you have not walked in the ways of Jehoshaphat your father, or in the ways of Asa king of Judah," (ESV)— Elijah's letter is extraordinary on multiple levels: a northern prophet writes to a southern king; the communication is written rather than spoken; and the indictment explicitly compares Jehoram to his godly forebears. The phrase "God of David thy father" emphasizes that Jehoram has abandoned his own family's God — the covenant God who blessed his ancestors.
  • v.15 — "and you yourself will have a severe sickness with a disease of your bowels, until your bowels come out because of the disease, day by day.’”" (ESV)— The specificity of the prophesied judgment is striking. This is likely a reference to a severe intestinal disease — possibly dysentery, colorectal cancer, or intestinal inflammation. The "day by day" indicates a slow, agonizing progression. The punishment's graphic nature matches the graphic evil of the crime — fratricidal murder and spiritual seduction deserve severe judgment.
  • v.20 — "He was thirty-two years old when he began to reign, and he reigned eight years in Jerusalem. And he departed with no one’s regret. They buried him in the city of David, but not in the tombs of the kings." (ESV)— "Departed without being desired" — the ESV renders "departed with no one's regret." The Hebrew phrase "lo lechemdah" (without desire/longing) means no one wished him alive. No mourning fires were lit (v.19), no royal tomb received his body. This is the ultimate epitaph of a wasted life — a king so wicked that death brought relief rather than grief. CROSS-REFERENCES ------------------------------------
  • v.4 — 2 Chronicles 11:22-23 — Rehoboam wisely distributed his sons throughout Judah's fortresses, perhaps to prevent fratricidal jealousy. Jehoshaphat similarly gave his sons "great gifts" and "fenced cities" (v.3). But Jehoram's evil was so extreme that neither provision nor distribution prevented his murderous power-grab. No human strategy can prevent what a wicked heart has determined.
  • v.6 — 1 Kings 16:31-33 — "He took to wife Jezebel... and went and served Baal." Jehoram walked "as the house of Ahab had done, for the daughter of Ahab was his wife." The marriage alliance with Ahab's house transmitted Jezebel's religion into Judah through Athaliah. The sins of the parents become the inheritance of the children.
  • v.7 — 2 Samuel 7:15-16 — "My mercy shall not depart away from him... thy throne shall be established for ever." God's covenant with David guaranteed dynastic survival regardless of individual kings' wickedness. This is pure grace — unmerited, unconditional preservation of God's redemptive plan despite human rebellion.
  • v.12 — 2 Kings 2:11 — Elijah was taken up to heaven in a chariot of fire. The timing of Elijah's letter presents a chronological question — how did Elijah write to Jehoram if he had already been translated? Possibilities include: the letter was written prophetically before his translation, or Elijah was translated later than commonly assumed. Either way, the prophetic word endures beyond the prophet's earthly presence.
  • v.16 — 2 Chronicles 17:11 — Under Jehoshaphat, the Philistines and Arabians brought tribute. Under Jehoram, these same nations invaded and plundered. The contrast is deliberate: faithfulness brings tribute; wickedness brings invasion. The same nations that feared God's presence now exploit God's absence.
  • v.20 — 2 Chronicles 16:14 — Asa received a great burning of spices at his death — public honor and mourning. Jehoram received "no burning" (v.19) — deliberate dishonor. How a king was mourned reflected how he was regarded. Legacy is built by character, not position.

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): "https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/2-chronicles-21/" target="_blank">https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/2-chronicles-21/ Guzik emphasizes the long-term consequences of Jehoshaphat's compromise: the marriage alliance with Ahab's house produced a son who walked in Ahab's ways and nearly extinguished the Davidic line. He notes the mercy of God in preserving one son (Jehoahaz/Ahaziah) and maintaining the covenant promise despite Jehoram's total apostasy. Guzik highlights that "departed without being desired" is the ultimate epitaph of a wicked life.
  • Charles Spurgeon: "Here is the bitter fruit of a father's compromise. Jehoshaphat allied himself with Ahab, and his son married Ahab's daughter, and his grandson walked in Ahab's ways, and his dynasty was nearly destroyed by Ahab's granddaughter. One unequal yoke produced generations of sorrow. Yet mark the faithfulness of God — 'the LORD would not destroy the house of David.' Not for Jehoram's sake, not for Judah's sake, but for David's sake and for the covenant's sake, God preserved a lamp in Jerusalem. When man's wickedness reaches its zenith, God's faithfulness remains unchanged. The candle flickered, but it did not go out — for Christ was in that flame, and He cannot be extinguished."

Translations

  • v.2 → KJV: "Jehoshaphat king of Israel" / ESV: "Jehoshaphat king of Israel" — Both preserve what appears to be a textual anomaly. Jehoshaphat was king of Judah, not Israel. This is likely either a scribal error (Judah written as Israel) or a theological statement — the Chronicler considered Judah the true Israel, the legitimate continuation of the covenant people.
  • v.7 → KJV: "give a light to him" / ESV: "give a lamp to him" — Hebrew "ner" (lamp/light). KJV uses the more general "light"; ESV uses the more literal "lamp." The "lamp" metaphor for dynastic continuation appears throughout Kings and Chronicles (1 Kings 11:36; 15:4; 2 Kings 8:19). A lamp that still burns means the dynasty still lives — God's promise still operates.
  • v.11 → KJV: "caused the inhabitants of Jerusalem to commit fornication" / ESV: "led the inhabitants of Jerusalem into whoredom" — Hebrew "vayyazne" (from "zanah," to commit harlotry/unfaithfulness). The primary reference is spiritual adultery — idolatry depicted as unfaithfulness to God the covenant husband. Both translations use the sexual metaphor to maintain the prophetic imagery of Israel's covenant relationship with God.
  • v.19 → KJV: "his people made no burning for him" / ESV: "His people made no fire in his honor" — Hebrew "lo asu lo serefah" (they did not make for him a burning). This refers to the customary burning of spices and incense at royal funerals (2 Chronicles 16:14; Jeremiah 34:5). The deliberate omission of this honor was a public statement of disgrace.
  • v.20 → KJV: "departed without being desired" / ESV: "departed with no one's regret" — Hebrew "halak belo chemdah" (he went without desire/longing). The word "chemdah" means desire, delight, or preciousness. KJV's "without being desired" is more literal; ESV's "with no one's regret" interprets the sense. Both convey the devastating reality: this king's death brought relief, not mourning. No one wished him back.

Reflection

  • 1. Compromise today produces catastrophe tomorrow (vv.4-6). Jehoshaphat's marriage alliance with Ahab seemed politically wise at the time, but it produced a son who murdered his brothers and led Judah into apostasy. The compromises you make today — the alliances, the small concessions, the "harmless" partnerships with ungodliness — may not bear fruit until the next generation. What are you building into your children's future through your present associations?
  • 2. God's covenant faithfulness transcends human failure (v.7). Jehoram was horrifically wicked, yet God did not destroy David's line "because of the covenant." God's promises are not contingent on your performance. This is not permission to sin — Jehoram suffered terribly. But it is assurance that God's ultimate purposes cannot be thwarted by human rebellion. If you belong to God through Christ, His covenant holds even when you fail.
  • 3. Sin is never private (vv.11, 13). Jehoram didn't merely sin privately — he "made Judah and the inhabitants of Jerusalem to go a whoring" (v.13, KJV). Leadership sin contaminates those being led. If you hold any position of influence — parent, teacher, pastor, employer, friend — your spiritual choices affect those who follow you. Your compromise gives others permission to compromise.
  • 4. The legacy you leave matters more than the power you hold (v.20). Jehoram held royal power for eight years but left no legacy worth remembering. He "departed without being desired." What will be said at your funeral? Will people grieve your absence or feel relief? Legacy is not built by authority and achievement alone but by how you treat people and whether your life pointed others toward God.
  • 5. God warns before He judges (vv.12-15). Elijah's letter gave Jehoram explicit warning of what would happen — naming the specific judgments before they fell. God is not capricious; He warns, gives time for repentance, and only then executes judgment. If you sense God's conviction about something in your life, don't ignore it. The warning is mercy; the silence after ignored warnings is terrifying.
