2 Samuel — Chapter 5

1Then all the tribes of Israel came to David at Hebron and said, “Behold, we are your bone and flesh.

2In times past, when Saul was king over us, it was you who led out and brought in Israel. And the LORD said to you, ‘You shall be shepherd of my people Israel, and you shall be prince over Israel.’”

3So all the elders of Israel came to the king at Hebron, and King David made a covenant with them at Hebron before the LORD, and they anointed David king over Israel.

4David was thirty years old when he began to reign, and he reigned forty years.

5At Hebron he reigned over Judah seven years and six months, and at Jerusalem he reigned over all Israel and Judah thirty-three years.

6And the king and his men went to Jerusalem against the Jebusites, the inhabitants of the land, who said to David, “You will not come in here, but the blind and the lame will ward you off”—thinking, “David cannot come in here.”

7Nevertheless, David took the stronghold of Zion, that is, the city of David.

8And David said on that day, “Whoever would strike the Jebusites, let him get up the water shaft to attack ‘the lame and the blind,’ who are hated by David’s soul.” Therefore it is said, “The blind and the lame shall not come into the house.”

9And David lived in the stronghold and called it the city of David. And David built the city all around from the Millo inward.

10And David became greater and greater, for the LORD, the God of hosts, was with him.

11And Hiram king of Tyre sent messengers to David, and cedar trees, also carpenters and masons who built David a house.

12And David knew that the LORD had established him king over Israel, and that he had exalted his kingdom for the sake of his people Israel.

13And David took more concubines and wives from Jerusalem, after he came from Hebron, and more sons and daughters were born to David.

14And these are the names of those who were born to him in Jerusalem: Shammua, Shobab, Nathan, Solomon,

15Ibhar, Elishua, Nepheg, Japhia,

16Elishama, Eliada, and Eliphelet.

17When the Philistines heard that David had been anointed king over Israel, all the Philistines went up to search for David. But David heard of it and went down to the stronghold.

18Now the Philistines had come and spread out in the Valley of Rephaim.

19And David inquired of the LORD, “Shall I go up against the Philistines? Will you give them into my hand?” And the LORD said to David, “Go up, for I will certainly give the Philistines into your hand.”

20And David came to Baal-perazim, and David defeated them there. And he said, “The LORD has broken through my enemies before me like a breaking flood.” Therefore the name of that place is called Baal-perazim.

21And the Philistines left their idols there, and David and his men carried them away.

22And the Philistines came up yet again and spread out in the Valley of Rephaim.

23And when David inquired of the LORD, he said, “You shall not go up; go around to their rear, and come against them opposite the balsam trees.

24And when you hear the sound of marching in the tops of the balsam trees, then rouse yourself, for then the LORD has gone out before you to strike down the army of the Philistines.”

25And David did as the LORD commanded him, and struck down the Philistines from Geba to Gezer.

Summary

All twelve tribes anoint David king at Hebron; he conquers Jerusalem and makes it his capital. The Philistines attack twice, but David inquires of the LORD and defeats them decisively both times.

Key Verses

  • v.2 — "You shall feed my people Israel, and you shall be a captain over Israel." — God's description of kingship: the king is a shepherd who feeds (provides for, protects) the people, and a prince (nagid = designated leader) over them. Kingship in Israel is service, not domination. David is to feed God's people — they belong to God, not to David.
  • v.10 — "And David went on, and grew great, and the LORD God of hosts was with him." — The chapter's theological summary. David's greatness is not self-made — it flows from God's presence. "The LORD God of hosts" = Yahweh, the God who commands all armies. When this God is "with" someone, no enemy can prevail.
  • v.12 — "David perceived that the LORD had established him king over Israel, and that he had exalted his kingdom for his people Israel's sake." — David's self-awareness: the kingdom is not for his benefit but for Israel's. God exalted David's throne to serve the people. Every leader must understand this — authority exists for those under it, not for the one who holds it.
  • v.19 — "Shall I go up against the Philistines? Will you give them into my hand?" (ESV) — David's pattern: inquire before acting. Even with a clear enemy and obvious military necessity, David asks God first. He does not presume on yesterday's guidance for today's battle.
  • v.24 — "When you hear the sound of marching in the tops of the balsam trees, then rouse yourself, for then the LORD has gone out before you." (ESV) — God fights first; David follows. The "sound of marching" in the treetops is the movement of God's invisible army. David must wait for God's timing, then act decisively. The battle is the LORD's — David is the instrument, not the initiator.

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): enduringword.com Guzik emphasizes the significance of Jerusalem's capture — a neutral city that united the tribes under David's rule. He also notes that David's two battles with the Philistines required different strategies, teaching that we must seek fresh guidance from God for each new challenge rather than relying on past methods.
  • Charles Spurgeon: "David inquired of the Lord the second time, and received a different answer. The same enemy, the same valley, but a different strategy. God does not always work in the same way. He who would follow the Lord must be willing to receive new instructions for new situations. Yesterday's method may be today's presumption. The sound in the mulberry trees was God's signal — David must wait for God to move first, then follow. So must we."

Translations

  • v.2 → KJV: "Thou shalt feed my people Israel, and thou shalt be a captain over Israel" / ESV: "You shall be shepherd of my people Israel, and you shall be prince over Israel" — Hebrew "ra'ah" = to shepherd, to feed, to tend. KJV "feed" is literal; ESV "shepherd" captures the full pastoral metaphor. Hebrew "nagid" = prince, designated ruler, leader. KJV "captain" suggests military command; ESV "prince" is closer to the Hebrew sense of a divinely designated leader.
  • v.6 → KJV: "Except thou take away the blind and the lame, thou shalt not come in hither" / ESV: "the blind and the lame will ward you off" — This verse is notoriously difficult in Hebrew. The Jebusites are taunting David — either saying their city is so strong that even disabled defenders could hold it, or that they have placed blind and lame people on the walls as a taunt/curse. Both translations capture the mockery; the exact meaning of the taunt remains debated.
  • v.8 → KJV: "getteth up to the gutter" / ESV: "get up the water shaft" — Hebrew "tsinnor" = a water channel, shaft, or tunnel. KJV "gutter" is archaic; ESV "water shaft" reflects the archaeological discovery of Warren's Shaft — a vertical tunnel the Jebusites used to access water without leaving the city walls. David's men likely entered through this shaft.
  • v.20 → KJV: "as the breach of waters" / ESV: "like a breaking flood" — Hebrew "perets mayim" = a bursting/breaking of waters. Both translations convey the image: God broke through the enemy like a dam bursting. "Baal-perazim" = "Lord/Master of breakings- through."
  • v.23 → KJV: "fetch a compass behind them, and come upon them over against the mulberry trees" / ESV: "go around to their rear, and come against them opposite the balsam trees" — KJV "fetch a compass" = go around (archaic). Hebrew "beka'im" = the identity of these trees is uncertain. KJV "mulberry" follows older tradition; ESV "balsam" follows more recent botanical identification. The exact species is unknown.
  • v.24 → KJV: "the sound of a going" / ESV: "the sound of marching" — Hebrew "qol tse'adah" = sound of stepping/marching. KJV "going" is vague; ESV "marching" better conveys the military imagery — the sound of God's invisible army advancing through the treetops.

Reflection

  • 1. God's timing is worth the wait (vv.1-3). David waited over fifteen years from his first anointing (1 Samuel 16) to this moment — king over all Israel. The waiting included persecution, exile, civil war, and grief. But God's promise was fulfilled completely. When God's timing feels unbearably slow, remember David's fifteen years.
  • 2. Authority exists for those under it (v.12). David "perceived that the LORD had established him king... for his people Israel's sake." The kingdom was not David's reward — it was Israel's provision. Every position of authority — in church, family, or work — exists to serve those under it, not to benefit the one who holds it.
  • 3. Seek fresh guidance for each new challenge (vv.19, 23). David inquired of God before both battles — and received different strategies each time. What worked at Baal-perazim would not work the second time. God's methods are not formulaic. We cannot reduce His guidance to a system. Each situation requires fresh dependence.
  • 4. Wait for God to move first (v.24). David had to wait for "the sound of marching in the tops of the trees" before attacking. God goes before us — our job is to follow His initiative, not to run ahead of Him. Patience is not passivity; it is readiness to act the moment God moves.
  • 5. The impossible becomes possible when God is with you (vv.6-7). The Jebusites mocked David — their fortress was impregnable. But "the LORD God of hosts was with him" (v.10). No fortress, no obstacle, no enemy is too strong when God is present. The question is never "How strong is the opposition?" but "Is God with us?"
