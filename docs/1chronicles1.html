Summary

A sweeping genealogy traces the line from Adam through Noah, Shem, and Abraham to Isaac's sons Esau and Israel, establishing the covenant lineage and affirming the post-exilic community's identity as God's chosen people. The inclusion of Esau's descendants and Edom's kings serves as contrast to the chosen line through Israel.

Key Verses

  • v.1 — "Adam, Seth, Enosh;" (ESV) — The Chronicler begins at the absolute beginning of human history. No introduction, no preamble — just names. This stark opening declares that Israel's story is humanity's story. God's purposes for this small post-exilic community are rooted in His purposes for all creation.
  • v.4 — "Noah, Shem, Ham, and Japheth." (ESV) — The post-flood restart of humanity. From Noah's three sons come all nations (vv.5-23). The Chronicler places Israel within the family of nations — not isolated but chosen from among them.
  • v.10 — "Cush fathered Nimrod. He was the first on earth to be a mighty man." (ESV) — The only editorial comment in the early genealogy. Nimrod represents human power and empire-building apart from God. His mention amidst the genealogy hints at the conflict between God's chosen line and worldly power — a theme that runs through all of Chronicles.
  • v.19 — "To Eber were born two sons: the name of the one was Peleg (for in his days the earth was divided), and his brother's name was Joktan." (ESV) — Another editorial insertion. "Peleg" means "division." Whether this refers to the scattering at Babel or a geographical/political division, it marks a turning point in human history that narrows toward the chosen line.
  • v.27 — "Abram, that is, Abraham." (ESV) — The genealogy arrives at its goal. From Adam through 26 verses of names, the line reaches Abraham — the father of the covenant, the man to whom God promised land, seed, and blessing. The name change (Abram to Abraham) is noted because it represents the covenant itself (Genesis 17:5).
  • v.34 — "Abraham fathered Isaac. The sons of Isaac: Esau and Israel." (ESV) — Note: "Israel," not "Jacob." The Chronicler uses the covenant name, emphasizing that this genealogy is about God's chosen people, not merely a family tree. The choice between Esau and Israel is the final narrowing before the tribes are listed in chapter 2.

Cross-References

  • v.1 → Genesis 5:1-32 — The genealogy from Adam to Noah. The Chronicler compresses ten generations into three verses, assuming the reader knows the full account. Genesis gives ages and narrative; Chronicles gives the essential line.
  • v.5-23 → Genesis 10:1-32 — The Table of Nations. The Chronicler reproduces much of this list, establishing that Israel exists within the context of all nations — all of whom descended from Noah's sons.
  • v.24-27 → Genesis 11:10-26 — The line from Shem to Abram. This is the narrowing from nations to the covenant family. Each generation moves closer to the promise.
  • v.28-31 → Genesis 25:12-16 — Ishmael's sons. Included to show completeness but quickly set aside as the non-chosen line.
  • v.32-33 → Genesis 25:1-4 — Keturah's sons. Abraham's later children through his concubine, also set aside from the covenant line.
  • v.35-37 → Genesis 36:1-19 — Esau's descendants. The rejected line is documented but not developed. Esau becomes Edom — Israel's perpetual rival and neighbor.
  • v.43-54 → Genesis 36:31-43 — Edom's kings and chiefs. These reigned "before any king reigned over the people of Israel" (v.43, ESV). This note looks forward to David — when Israel's king finally comes, he will eclipse all of Edom's rulers.

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): enduringword.com Guzik notes that the genealogies served a vital function for the returned exiles: proving their identity as God's covenant people. Without the Temple records (many lost in the exile), these lists preserved the community's connection to their ancestral heritage. He emphasizes that every name represents a real person known to God — "God knows His own."
  • Charles Spurgeon: "Here is a line of grace running through a line of names. Adam to Abraham — what a journey of sin and redemption lies beneath these simple records! Every name is a monument to God's patience. The line might have been broken a thousand times by human sin, but God preserved it, generation after generation, until it reached Abraham — and from Abraham it would reach David — and from David, Christ. These dry bones of genealogy are the skeleton of redemption's story."

Translations

  • v.1 → KJV: "Sheth" / ESV: "Seth" — Hebrew "Sheth." KJV preserves a more literal transliteration; ESV uses the familiar anglicized form established in Genesis. No difference in meaning — purely a spelling convention.
  • v.3 → KJV: "Henoch" / ESV: "Enoch" — Hebrew "Chanokh." KJV includes the initial "H" reflecting the Hebrew "chet"; ESV uses the conventional English spelling. Same person (Genesis 5:21-24) who "walked with God."
  • v.6 → KJV: "Ashchenaz" / ESV: "Ashkenaz" — A minor transliteration difference. This name became significant in later Jewish history as "Ashkenazi" Jews trace their name to this grandson of Japheth, associated with regions of central/eastern Europe.
  • v.7 → KJV: "Dodanim" / ESV: "Rodanim" — Hebrew manuscripts vary between "Dodanim" (dalet) and "Rodanim" (resh). These two Hebrew letters look nearly identical and are easily confused in copying. "Rodanim" likely refers to the people of Rhodes; "Dodanim" is of uncertain identification. Genesis 10:4 has "Dodanim" (MT) but the LXX and some manuscripts read "Rodanim."
  • v.8 → KJV: "Mizraim" / ESV: "Egypt" — "Mizraim" is the Hebrew name for Egypt. KJV transliterates the Hebrew; ESV translates it for clarity. Both are correct — Mizraim was Ham's son whose descendants populated Egypt. ESV's choice makes the geographical connection immediately clear.
  • v.10 → KJV: "begat" / ESV: "fathered" — Throughout Chronicles, KJV uses the archaic "begat" for Hebrew "yalad" (to bear, bring forth). ESV consistently uses "fathered." Both mean "became the father of" or "was the ancestor of."
  • v.17 → KJV: "Arphaxad" / ESV: "Arpachshad" — ESV preserves the Hebrew consonants more precisely (including the "ch" sound). This is the ancestor through whom the line continues to Abraham.
  • v.43 → KJV: "the children of Israel" / ESV: "the people of Israel" — KJV's "children" reflects Hebrew "bene" (sons of); ESV's "people" is a dynamic translation capturing the sense for modern readers. The Hebrew literally says "sons of Israel."

Reflection

  • 1. Every person matters to God (vv.1-54). This chapter contains over 200 names — most of whom we know nothing about beyond their place in a genealogy. Yet God recorded each one. In a world that values fame, influence, and visibility, this reminds us that God sees and remembers every life. You may feel like an obscure name in history, but you are known to God and placed in His story with purpose.
  • 2. God's plan spans all of human history (vv.1-27). From Adam to Abraham is roughly 2,000 years — compressed into 27 verses. God is never in a hurry. His purposes unfold across generations, and what looks like delay is actually patient faithfulness. If you are waiting on God, remember: He has been working since Adam, and He has never abandoned His plan.
  • 3. Identity matters (vv.1-34). The returned exiles needed to know who they were. In a culture of displacement and confusion, these genealogies grounded them in their covenant identity. Christians likewise need to know their spiritual heritage — grafted into Abraham's family by faith (Galatians 3:29), connected to the whole story of redemption. Know who you are in God's story.
  • 4. The narrowing of grace (vv.28-34). From all humanity, God chose one family. From Abraham's sons, He chose Isaac over Ishmael. From Isaac's sons, He chose Israel over Esau. This is not favoritism but purpose — God narrows to one line so that through that line all nations will be blessed (Genesis 12:3). Election is always for service, never merely for privilege.
  • 5. God works through imperfect lineages (v.34). The line from Adam to Israel passes through sinners, failures, and flawed humans. Tamar, Er, Judah's compromise — all are present in the broader genealogy. God does not require perfect people to accomplish His purposes. He works through broken vessels, and His plan succeeds despite human failure.
