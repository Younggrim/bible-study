Kings of Israel & Judah

Jehoshaphat

His Reign

Jehoshaphat was one of Judah's best kings — a man who sought the LORD, removed idols, and sent teachers throughout the land to instruct the people in God's law. His reign brought peace and prosperity to Judah, and even surrounding nations feared the God of Jehoshaphat. He reformed the judicial system, establishing judges in every fortified city with the charge: "Consider carefully what you do, because you are not judging for mere mortals but for the LORD."

His finest hour came when a vast army of Moabites, Ammonites, and Meunites marched against Judah. Jehoshaphat's response was not military strategy but prayer and fasting. His prayer before the assembled nation is a masterpiece of humble dependence: "We do not know what to do, but our eyes are on you." God's answer came through a Levite prophet: "The battle is not yours but God's." Judah went out singing, and God set ambushes that destroyed the enemy armies.

Yet Jehoshaphat's fatal weakness was his alliances with the wicked kings of Israel. He married his son to Ahab's daughter, joined Ahab in battle at Ramoth-Gilead (nearly dying in the process), and partnered with Ahaziah in a merchant fleet that God destroyed. The rebuke was clear: "Should you help the wicked and love those who hate the LORD?" His story shows that a heart devoted to God can still make costly errors in judgment about partnerships.

Key Scriptures

  • 2 Chronicles 20:3-4 — Alarmed, Jehoshaphat resolved to inquire of the LORD, and he proclaimed a fast for all Judah.
  • 2 Chronicles 20:12 — "We do not know what to do, but our eyes are on you."
  • 2 Chronicles 20:15 — "Do not be afraid... the battle is not yours but God's."
  • 2 Chronicles 19:2 — "Should you help the wicked and love those who hate the LORD?"
  • Psalm 37:5 — "Commit your way to the LORD; trust in him, and he will act."

Legacy and Lessons

Jehoshaphat teaches us the power of corporate prayer and worship in the face of overwhelming odds. When the army approached, he did not convene a war council — he called a prayer meeting. And God's strategy was unprecedented: send the worship team ahead of the army. Jehoshaphat's example shows that our first response to crisis should be turning our eyes to God rather than scrambling for human solutions. The battles we face are ultimately spiritual, and they require spiritual weapons.

Jehoshaphat also warns us that even godly people can make disastrous partnership decisions. His alliances with Israel's wicked kings were likely motivated by diplomacy and family loyalty, but they nearly cost him his life and compromised his legacy. Good intentions do not sanctify bad alliances. We must evaluate our partnerships not by their pragmatic benefits but by their spiritual compatibility with God's purposes.

Reflection Questions

  • When facing overwhelming circumstances, is your first instinct to strategize or to pray?
  • Are there partnerships in your life that, however well-intentioned, are compromising your faithfulness?
  • What would it look like to say 'our eyes are on you' in your current situation?
