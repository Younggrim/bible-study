Psalms — Chapter 42

1As a deer pants for flowing streams, so pants my soul for you, O God.

2My soul thirsts for God, for the living God. When shall I come and appear before God?

3My tears have been my food day and night, while they say to me all the day long, “Where is your God?”

4These things I remember, as I pour out my soul: how I would go with the throng and lead them in procession to the house of God with glad shouts and songs of praise, a multitude keeping festival.

5Why are you cast down, O my soul, and why are you in turmoil within me? Hope in God; for I shall again praise him, my salvation

6and my God. My soul is cast down within me; therefore I remember you from the land of Jordan and of Hermon, from Mount Mizar.

7Deep calls to deep at the roar of your waterfalls; all your breakers and your waves have gone over me.

8By day the LORD commands his steadfast love, and at night his song is with me, a prayer to the God of my life.

9I say to God, my rock: “Why have you forgotten me? Why do I go mourning because of the oppression of the enemy?”

10As with a deadly wound in my bones, my adversaries taunt me, while they say to me all the day long, “Where is your God?”

11Why are you cast down, O my soul, and why are you in turmoil within me? Hope in God; for I shall again praise him, my salvation and my God.

Summary

A lament of the Sons of Korah — 'As the hart panteth after the water brooks, so panteth my soul after thee, O God'; the psalmist, exiled from the temple, combats despair with the refrain: 'Why art thou cast down, O my soul? Hope thou in God.'

Key Verses

Cross-References

  • v. 1 — Psalm 63:1 — "O God, thou art my God; early will I seek thee: my soul thirsteth for thee, my flesh longeth for thee in a dry and thirsty land." David's similar cry of spiritual thirst.
  • v. 1 — John 7:37 — "If any man thirst, let him come unto me, and drink." Jesus offers Himself as the fulfillment of spiritual thirst.
  • v. 3 — Psalm 80:5 — "Thou feedest them with the bread of tears; and givest them tears to drink in great measure." Tears as sustenance during sorrow.
  • v. 5 — Psalm 43:5 — The identical refrain, confirming these psalms were composed as one unit.
  • v. 7 — Jonah 2:3 — "All thy billows and thy waves passed over me." Jonah uses nearly identical language from the belly of the great fish — the experience of being overwhelmed by God's waves.
  • v. 7 — Psalm 69:1-2 — "Save me, O God; for the waters are come in unto my soul." The imagery of drowning in sorrow recurs throughout the Psalms.
  • v. 8 — Psalm 32:7 — "Thou shalt compass me about with songs of deliverance." God surrounds the afflicted with His song even in trouble.
  • v. 10 — Psalm 22:1 — "My God, my God, why hast thou forsaken me?" The pattern of crying out to God in perceived abandonment — most fully expressed by Christ on the cross.

Translations

  • v. 1 — KJV "hart" vs. ESV "deer" — The Hebrew "ayyal" (אַיָּל) refers specifically to a male deer or stag. KJV's archaic "hart" preserves this masculine specificity, while ESV uses the more general modern term. The intensity of panting (arag) suggests the animal is in extremity.
  • v. 4 — KJV "I had gone with the multitude" vs. ESV "how I would go with the throng" — The Hebrew is difficult here. KJV renders it as a straightforward past tense memory. ESV captures the habitual aspect ("I would go") suggesting a repeated pattern of joyful worship now lost.
  • v. 5 — KJV "the help of his countenance" vs. ESV "my salvation" — The Hebrew is debated. KJV follows the Masoretic text more literally: "the salvations of his face/countenance." ESV simplifies for readability. The Hebrew connects salvation directly to seeing God's face — being in His presence is itself the deliverance.
  • v. 7 — KJV "waterspouts" vs. ESV "waterfalls" — The Hebrew "tsinnor" is rare and debated. It may refer to waterfalls, cataracts, water channels, or conduits. Given the northern geographic setting near Hermon's streams, "waterfalls" (ESV) fits the landscape, while "waterspouts" (KJV) evokes more violent water imagery.
  • v. 11 — KJV "the health of my countenance" vs. ESV "my salvation and my God" — KJV's rendering captures the Hebrew idea that God's saving presence literally shows on one's face — He is the health (healing, wholeness) that restores the downcast countenance.

Reflection

  • 1. Spiritual thirst is not a sign of failure but of life. Only living souls thirst. The psalmist's desperate longing for God reveals a heart that knows what it needs. When we feel spiritual dryness, it may be evidence of genuine faith recognizing its need.
  • 2. Preaching to yourself is a spiritual discipline. The refrain teaches us to speak truth to our own souls rather than passively listening to our emotions. When cast down, we can ask "Why?" and then command ourselves toward hope — not denying the pain but redirecting the soul toward God's faithfulness.
  • 3. Remembering past faithfulness sustains present faith. The psalmist recalls leading worship processions (v. 4) and uses those memories as anchors. Keeping a record of God's past dealings with us provides fuel for hope in present darkness.
  • 4. Tears and faith coexist. The psalmist weeps day and night (v. 3) while simultaneously affirming God's lovingkindness (v. 8). Biblical faith does not suppress grief — it grieves honestly while holding to God's character.
  • 5. Community matters. Part of the psalmist's anguish is isolation from corporate worship (v. 4). We were not designed to walk through spiritual valleys alone. The absence of gathered worship deepens spiritual pain; its restoration brings healing.
