Parables of Jesus

The Rich Fool

The Parable

Someone in the crowd asked Jesus to arbitrate an inheritance dispute with his brother. Jesus refused the role of judge but seized the moment to warn about covetousness: "Take care, and be on your guard against all covetousness, for one's life does not consist in the abundance of his possessions." Then He told this parable.

A rich man's land produced an extraordinary harvest — so abundant that his barns couldn't hold it all. He reasoned with himself: "What shall I do? I will tear down my barns and build larger ones, and there I will store all my grain and my goods. And I will say to my soul, 'Soul, you have ample goods laid up for many years; relax, eat, drink, be merry.'"

But God said to him: "Fool! This night your soul is required of you, and the things you have prepared, whose will they be?" Jesus concluded: "So is the one who lays up treasure for himself and is not rich toward God." The man's fatal error wasn't wealth itself — it was making wealth his security, his identity, and his god, while ignoring the One who gave it and could take it (along with his life) at any moment.

Key Scriptures

  • Luke 12:20-21 — "Fool! This night your soul is required of you… So is the one who is not rich toward God."
  • Matthew 6:19-21 — "Do not lay up treasures on earth… for where your treasure is, there your heart will be also."
  • 1 Timothy 6:7 — "We brought nothing into the world, and we cannot take anything out of it."
  • James 4:14 — "What is your life? You are a mist that appears for a little time and then vanishes."
  • Psalm 39:6 — "Man heaps up wealth and does not know who will gather it."

What Jesus Is Teaching

Jesus is exposing the dangerous illusion that material security equals life security. The rich fool's monologue is striking: he talks only to himself ("my barns," "my grain," "my goods," "my soul"). God, other people, eternity — none of these enter his thinking. His horizon extends only to earthly comfort. He plans for years of ease but has only hours left. The tragedy isn't that he was rich — it's that his riches were the sum total of his existence. He was wealthy in the eyes of the world and bankrupt in the eyes of God.

Being "rich toward God" means investing in things that outlast death: generosity, relationships, the gospel, eternal treasure. Jesus isn't condemning planning or saving — He's condemning a life built entirely on accumulation with no thought of God's purposes or eternity's reality. The question this parable forces on every hearer is: If God required your soul tonight, what would your life's investment be worth? Are you building bigger barns, or building a life that matters beyond the grave?

Reflection Questions

  • If you're honest, where does your sense of security really come from — God's provision, or the size of your "barns" (savings, possessions, career)?
  • What does it look like practically to be "rich toward God" in your current financial situation?
  • How would you live differently this week if you truly believed your life could be required of you tonight?
  • Are there ways you're hoarding what God has given you instead of generously investing it in others and His kingdom?
