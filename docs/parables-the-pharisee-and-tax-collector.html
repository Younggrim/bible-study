Parables of Jesus

The Pharisee and Tax Collector

The Parable

Jesus told this parable to those who "trusted in themselves that they were righteous, and treated others with contempt." Two men went up into the temple to pray — one a Pharisee and the other a tax collector. The Pharisee stood by himself and prayed: "God, I thank you that I am not like other men, extortioners, unjust, adulterers, or even like this tax collector. I fast twice a week; I give tithes of all that I get." His prayer was technically addressed to God but was really a performance review delivered to himself.

The tax collector, by contrast, stood far off. He would not even lift his eyes to heaven but beat his breast and said, "God, be merciful to me, a sinner!" In the original Greek, the phrase is even more pointed: "God, be propitiated toward me, the sinner" — as though he were the worst one in the room. He made no comparison, offered no resume, claimed no merit. He came empty-handed and broken.

Jesus delivered the verdict that shocked His audience: "I tell you, this man went down to his house justified, rather than the other. For everyone who exalts himself will be humbled, but the one who humbles himself will be exalted." The most religious man in the room left unjustified; the most despised man left right with God. The difference was not behavior but posture — self-reliance versus God-dependence.

Key Scriptures

  • Luke 18:9-14 — The full parable: two prayers, two postures, two outcomes — humility is justified, pride is not.
  • Isaiah 66:2 — "This is the one to whom I will look: he who is humble and contrite in spirit and trembles at my word."
  • James 4:6 — "God opposes the proud but gives grace to the humble." Grace flows downhill to the lowest place.
  • Proverbs 16:18 — "Pride goes before destruction, and a haughty spirit before a fall."
  • 1 Peter 5:5 — "Clothe yourselves, all of you, with humility toward one another" — humility is something we choose to put on.

What Jesus Was Teaching

Jesus was demolishing the foundation of self-righteousness. The Pharisee's prayer reveals the anatomy of spiritual pride: comparison with others ("not like other men"), religious performance as currency ("I fast... I give tithes"), and blindness to his own need. He came to God as a creditor, not a debtor. His obedience, real as it may have been, became a wall between him and grace because he used it to justify himself rather than glorify God. Religious activity without humility is the most dangerous form of self-deception.

The tax collector's prayer is the model for all genuine encounter with God: honest about sin, desperate for mercy, making no excuses and offering no comparisons. He represents the posture Paul later described: "Not having a righteousness of my own that comes from the law, but that which comes through faith in Christ" (Philippians 3:9). Justification — being declared righteous before God — comes not to those who perform but to those who confess. The door to God's presence is always humility, never achievement.

Reflection Questions

  • When you pray, do your prayers sound more like the Pharisee's (self-congratulatory) or the tax collector's (dependent on mercy)?
  • Are there people you mentally compare yourself to in order to feel spiritually superior? What does this reveal?
  • How can you cultivate the tax collector's posture — honest, humble, and desperate for grace — as a daily spiritual practice?
  • Is there an area of obedience in your life that has subtly become a source of pride rather than gratitude?
