1 Samuel — Chapter 28

1In those days the Philistines gathered their forces for war, to fight against Israel. And Achish said to David, “Understand that you and your men are to go out with me in the army.”

2David said to Achish, “Very well, you shall know what your servant can do.” And Achish said to David, “Very well, I will make you my bodyguard for life.”

3Now Samuel had died, and all Israel had mourned for him and buried him in Ramah, his own city. And Saul had put the mediums and the necromancers out of the land.

4The Philistines assembled and came and encamped at Shunem. And Saul gathered all Israel, and they encamped at Gilboa.

5When Saul saw the army of the Philistines, he was afraid, and his heart trembled greatly.

6And when Saul inquired of the LORD, the LORD did not answer him, either by dreams, or by Urim, or by prophets.

7Then Saul said to his servants, “Seek out for me a woman who is a medium, that I may go to her and inquire of her.” And his servants said to him, “Behold, there is a medium at En-dor.”

8So Saul disguised himself and put on other garments and went, he and two men with him. And they came to the woman by night. And he said, “Divine for me by a spirit and bring up for me whomever I shall name to you.”

9The woman said to him, “Surely you know what Saul has done, how he has cut off the mediums and the necromancers from the land. Why then are you laying a trap for my life to bring about my death?”

10But Saul swore to her by the LORD, “As the LORD lives, no punishment shall come upon you for this thing.”

11Then the woman said, “Whom shall I bring up for you?” He said, “Bring up Samuel for me.”

12When the woman saw Samuel, she cried out with a loud voice. And the woman said to Saul, “Why have you deceived me? You are Saul.”

13The king said to her, “Do not be afraid. What do you see?” And the woman said to Saul, “I see a god coming up out of the earth.”

14He said to her, “What is his appearance?” And she said, “An old man is coming up, and he is wrapped in a robe.” And Saul knew that it was Samuel, and he bowed with his face to the ground and paid homage.

15Then Samuel said to Saul, “Why have you disturbed me by bringing me up?” Saul answered, “I am in great distress, for the Philistines are warring against me, and God has turned away from me and answers me no more, either by prophets or by dreams. Therefore I have summoned you to tell me what I shall do.”

16And Samuel said, “Why then do you ask me, since the LORD has turned from you and become your enemy?

17The LORD has done to you as he spoke by me, for the LORD has torn the kingdom out of your hand and given it to your neighbor, David.

18Because you did not obey the voice of the LORD and did not carry out his fierce wrath against Amalek, therefore the LORD has done this thing to you this day.

19Moreover, the LORD will give Israel also with you into the hand of the Philistines, and tomorrow you and your sons shall be with me. The LORD will give the army of Israel also into the hand of the Philistines.”

20Then Saul fell at once full length on the ground, filled with fear because of the words of Samuel. And there was no strength in him, for he had eaten nothing all day and all night.

21And the woman came to Saul, and when she saw that he was terrified, she said to him, “Behold, your servant has obeyed you. I have taken my life in my hand and have listened to what you have said to me.

22Now therefore, you also obey your servant. Let me set a morsel of bread before you; and eat, that you may have strength when you go on your way.”

23He refused and said, “I will not eat.” But his servants, together with the woman, urged him, and he listened to their words. So he arose from the earth and sat on the bed.

24Now the woman had a fattened calf in the house, and she quickly killed it, and she took flour and kneaded it and baked unleavened bread of it,

25and she put it before Saul and his servants, and they ate. Then they rose and went away that night.

Summary

On the eve of battle, a terrified Saul consults the witch of Endor, and the spirit of Samuel appears with a devastating message: tomorrow Saul and his sons will die. Saul collapses in despair.

Key Verses

  • v.6 — "And when Saul enquired of the LORD, the LORD answered him not, neither by dreams nor by Urim, nor by prophets." — The most terrifying verse in the chapter. God is silent. Every channel of communication is closed. This is the consequence of persistent rejection of God's word: eventually God stops speaking. There is no more dreadful judgment than divine silence.
  • v.16 — "The LORD has departed from you and become your enemy." — Samuel's devastating summary. God has not merely withdrawn — He has become Saul's active opponent. When God fights against you, no human power can save you. This is the ultimate consequence of rebellion: God Himself becomes your adversary.
  • v.19 — "Tomorrow you and your sons shall be with me." — The death sentence. Saul and his sons will die in battle tomorrow. The finality is absolute — no offer of repentance, no possibility of escape. Saul's time has run out. God's patience, though long, is not infinite.
  • v.15 — "God is departed from me, and answereth me no more." — Saul's own confession. He knows God has left him. He knows God will not answer. Yet instead of repenting, he turns to necromancy. This is the pattern of the hardened heart: knowing the truth but refusing to submit to it, seeking alternatives to obedience rather than obedience itself.

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): "https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/1-samuel-28/" target="_blank">enduringword.com Guzik argues that this was genuinely Samuel (not a demon), based on the text's straightforward language and the medium's own shock. He notes that God permitted this extraordinary event as a final act of judgment — Saul sought the dead because the living God would not answer him.
  • Charles Spurgeon: "Saul enquired of the LORD, and the LORD answered him not. Here is the harvest of a life of disobedience. God will not be mocked. He who rejects God's word when it is freely given shall find God silent when he desperately needs to hear. Let us tremble at this: there is a point beyond which God's patience does not extend. Seek the Lord while He may be found; call upon Him while He is near. For the day may come when you call and He does not answer."

Translations

  • v.7 → KJV: "a woman that hath a familiar spirit" / ESV: "a woman who is a medium" — Hebrew "eshet ba'alat-ob" = a woman who is a mistress of a spirit/necromantic pit. KJV "familiar spirit" reflects the idea of a spirit that is "familiar" to the medium (a spirit guide). ESV "medium" translates the function rather than the mechanism.
  • v.13 → KJV: "I saw gods ascending out of the earth" / ESV: "I see a god coming up out of the earth" — Hebrew "elohim olim min ha-aretz." "Elohim" is plural in form but can refer to a single divine/supernatural being (as in Psalm 82:6). KJV "gods" is literal plural; ESV "a god" interprets as a singular supernatural being. The woman sees a supernatural figure rising from the earth.
  • v.14 → KJV: "covered with a mantle" / ESV: "wrapped in a robe" — Hebrew "me'il" = the prophetic robe/mantle. This is Samuel's characteristic garment — the same robe whose hem Saul tore (15:27). Even in death, Samuel is identified by his prophetic mantle.
  • v.19 → KJV: "tomorrow shalt thou and thy sons be with me" / ESV: "tomorrow you and your sons shall be with me" — Both agree. "With me" = in Sheol, the realm of the dead. This does not necessarily imply the same eternal destiny — it means they will join the dead. The prophecy is fulfilled in chapter 31.

Reflection

  • 1. Divine silence is the most terrifying judgment (v.6). God answered Saul "not... neither by dreams, nor by Urim, nor by prophets." Every channel closed. This is the consequence of persistently rejecting God's word: eventually He stops speaking. Do not take God's voice for granted. Respond when He speaks, lest He fall silent.
  • 2. Desperation without repentance leads to deeper sin (vv.7-8). Saul is desperate — but not repentant. Instead of humbling himself before God, he seeks a medium. Desperation that drives us to God is healthy; desperation that drives us to forbidden alternatives is deadly. When you are desperate, the question is: where do you turn?
  • 3. God's word does not change (vv.17-18). Samuel's message from beyond the grave is the same as his message in life: the kingdom is torn from Saul because of disobedience regarding Amalek. God does not revise His verdicts. What He said through His word remains true whether we accept it or not.
  • 4. There is a point of no return (v.19). Saul receives no offer of repentance, no possibility of restoration. His doom is sealed. While God's patience is extraordinary, it is not infinite. Do not presume upon grace. Today is the day of salvation (2 Corinthians 6:2).
  • 5. Sin's consequences are generational (v.19). "Thou and thy sons" will die. Saul's rebellion costs not only his own life but his sons' lives — including Jonathan, the faithful friend of David. Our sin does not affect only us. It touches our children, our families, our communities. The stakes of obedience are higher than we think.
