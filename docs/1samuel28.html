1And it came to pass in those days, that the Philistines gathered their armies together for warfare, to fight with Israel. And Achish said unto David, Know thou assuredly, that thou shalt go out with me to battle, thou and thy men.

2And David said to Achish, Surely thou shalt know what thy servant can do. And Achish said to David, Therefore will I make thee keeper of mine head for ever.

3Now Samuel was dead, and all Israel had lamented him, and buried him in Ramah, even in his own city. And Saul had put away those that had familiar spirits, and the wizards, out of the land.

4And the Philistines gathered themselves together, and came and pitched in Shunem: and Saul gathered all Israel together, and they pitched in Gilboa.

5And when Saul saw the host of the Philistines, he was afraid, and his heart greatly trembled.

6And when Saul enquired of the LORD, the LORD answered him not, neither by dreams nor by Urim, nor by prophets.

7Then said Saul unto his servants, Seek me a woman that hath a familiar spirit, that I may go to her, and enquire of her. And his servants said to him, Behold, there is a woman that hath a familiar spirit at Endor.

8And Saul disguised himself, and put on other raiment, and he went, and two men with him, and they came to the woman by night: and he said, I pray thee, divine unto me by the familiar spirit, and bring me him up, whom I shall name unto thee.

9And the woman said unto him, Behold, thou knowest what Saul hath done, how he hath cut off those that have familiar spirits, and the wizards, out of the land: wherefore then layest thou a snare for my life, to cause me to die?

10And Saul sware to her by the LORD, saying, As the LORD liveth, there shall no punishment happen to thee for this thing.

11Then said the woman, Whom shall I bring up unto thee? And he said, Bring me up Samuel.

12And when the woman saw Samuel, she cried with a loud voice: and the woman spake to Saul, saying, Why hast thou deceived me? for thou art Saul.

13And the king said unto her, Be not afraid: for what sawest thou? And the woman said unto Saul, I saw gods ascending out of the earth.

14And he said unto her, What form is he of? And she said, An old man cometh up; and he is covered with a mantle. And Saul perceived that it was Samuel, and he stooped with his face to the ground, and bowed himself.

15And Samuel said to Saul, Why hast thou disquieted me, to bring me up? And Saul answered, I am sore distressed; for the Philistines make war against me, and God is departed from me, and answereth me no more, neither by prophets, nor by dreams: therefore I have called thee, that thou mayest make known unto me what I shall do.

16Then said Samuel, Wherefore then dost thou ask of me, seeing the LORD is departed from thee, and is become thine enemy?

17And the LORD hath done to him, as he spake by me: for the LORD hath rent the kingdom out of thine hand, and given it to thy neighbour, even to David:

18Because thou obeyedst not the voice of the LORD, nor executedst his fierce wrath upon Amalek, therefore hath the LORD done this thing unto thee this day.

19Moreover the LORD will also deliver Israel with thee into the hand of the Philistines: and to morrow shalt thou and thy sons be with me: the LORD also shall deliver the host of Israel into the hand of the Philistines.

20Then Saul fell straightway all along on the earth, and was sore afraid, because of the words of Samuel: and there was no strength in him; for he had eaten no bread all the day, nor all the night.

21And the woman came unto Saul, and saw that he was sore troubled, and said unto him, Behold, thine handmaid hath obeyed thy voice, and I have put my life in my hand, and have hearkened unto thy words which thou spakest unto me.

22Now therefore, I pray thee, hearken thou also unto the voice of thine handmaid, and let me set a morsel of bread before thee; and eat, that thou mayest have strength, when thou goest on thy way.

23But he refused, and said, I will not eat. But his servants, together with the woman, compelled him; and he hearkened unto their voice. So he arose from the earth, and sat upon the bed.

24And the woman had a fat calf in the house; and she hasted, and killed it, and took flour, and kneaded it, and did bake unleavened bread thereof:

He does not answer me anymore—not by the prophets nor by dreams. So I have called on you to tell me what I should do.”

16Samuel said, “Why are you asking me, now that the Lord has turned away from you and has become your enemy?

21When the woman came to Saul and saw how terrified he was, she said to him, “Your servant has done what you asked. I took my life into my own hands and did what you told me.

22Now it’s your turn to listen to your servant! Let me set before you a bit of bread so that you can eat. When you regain your strength, you can go on your way.”

23But he refused, saying, “I won’t eat!” Both his servants and the woman urged him to eat, so he gave in. He got up from the ground and sat down on the bed.

Summary

On the eve of battle, a terrified Saul consults the witch of Endor, and the spirit of Samuel appears with a devastating message: tomorrow Saul and his sons will die. Saul collapses in despair.

Authorship & Background

Author: Unknown, traditionally attributed to Samuel (for the early chapters), Nathan, and Gad (1 Chronicles 29:29). Originally one book with 2 Samuel in the Hebrew Bible. Written approximately 1050-950 BC. The book covers the transition from the judges to the monarchy — from the birth of Samuel through the death of Saul (approximately 1105-1010 BC). Key themes: God's sovereignty in raising and removing leaders, the danger of rejecting God's rule, the contrast between Saul (man's choice) and David (God's choice), and the principle that 'man looketh on the outward appearance, but the LORD looketh on the heart' (16:7).
Historical Context: Chapter 28 is one of the most dramatic and theologically debated chapters in the Old Testament. It records Saul's final descent — from fear (v.5) to divine silence (v.6) to forbidden necromancy (v.7) to doom (v.19). The Philistines mass for a major invasion, camping at Shunem in the Jezreel Valley. Saul gathers Israel at Mount Gilboa. When he sees the Philistine army, he is terrified (v.5). He seeks God — but God does not answer by any means: dreams, Urim, or prophets (v.6). In desperation, Saul does what he himself has forbidden: he seeks a medium (a woman with a "familiar spirit") at Endor. He asks her to bring up Samuel from the dead. What happens next is extraordinary: Samuel actually appears (v.12) — the medium herself is shocked, suggesting this is not her usual experience. Samuel's message is devastating: God has become Saul's enemy (v.16), the kingdom is given to David (v.17), and "tomorrow shalt thou and thy sons be with me" (v.19) — Saul and his sons will die in battle. Saul collapses in terror (v.20). The chapter is Saul's darkest hour: the king who rejected God's word through Samuel now seeks Samuel through forbidden means — and receives only confirmation of his doom. The theological irony is complete: Saul banned mediums from the land (v.3) but now uses one himself; he could not hear God through legitimate means, so he seeks Him through illegitimate ones; and the message he receives is the same one Samuel delivered in life (15:28) — the kingdom is torn from him.
David's Dilemma (vv.1-2): The chapter opens with David's crisis: Achish expects him to fight against Israel (v.1). David's ambiguous answer — "thou shalt know what thy servant can do" (v.2) — can be read as loyalty to Achish or as a veiled threat. Achish takes it positively and makes David his bodyguard (v.2). David is trapped by his own deception — the consequences of chapter 27's faithless decision. God will resolve this in chapter 29.
Saul's Desperation (vv.3-7): Samuel is dead (v.3). Saul has banned mediums and wizards (v.3) — ironically, one of his few acts of obedience to the law (Deuteronomy 18:10-12). The Philistines camp at Shunem; Israel at Gilboa (v.4). Saul sees the enemy and is terrified (v.5). He inquires of the LORD — but receives no answer by any channel: dreams, Urim (the priestly oracle — but Abiathar and the ephod are with David), or prophets (v.6). God's silence is judgment. Having rejected God's word repeatedly, Saul now finds God will not speak to him at all. In desperation, he seeks what he has forbidden: "Seek me a woman that hath a familiar spirit" (v.7).
The Medium at Endor (vv.8-14): Saul disguises himself and goes to Endor by night (v.8) — darkness, disguise, and disobedience. He asks the woman to bring up Samuel (v.11). When Samuel appears, the woman cries out in shock (v.12) — this is not her normal experience. She recognizes Saul (perhaps because only a king would dare summon a prophet). She sees "gods ascending out of the earth" (v.13 KJV) / "a god coming up" (ESV) — Hebrew "elohim" (divine being/beings). Samuel appears as "an old man... covered with a mantle" (v.14) — his characteristic prophetic garment. The theological question: is this really Samuel? The text says "Samuel said to Saul" (v.15) — the narrator treats it as genuinely Samuel. Most conservative interpreters conclude that God permitted Samuel's actual spirit to appear (not a demonic impersonation) as a final act of judgment on Saul.
Samuel's Message of Doom (vv.15-19): Samuel's words are harsh and final: "Why hast thou disquieted me?" (v.15). Saul explains his desperation: the Philistines attack, God is silent (v.15). Samuel's response: "Wherefore then dost thou ask of me, seeing the LORD is departed from thee, and is become thine enemy?" (v.16). The message repeats what Samuel said in life: the kingdom is torn from Saul and given to David (v.17), because Saul disobeyed regarding Amalek (v.18). Then the death sentence: "tomorrow shalt thou and thy sons be with me: the LORD also shall deliver the host of Israel into the hand of the Philistines" (v.19). "With me" = in the realm of the dead (Sheol). Saul's doom is sealed.
Saul's Collapse (vv.20-25): Saul falls full-length on the ground, terrified and physically spent — he has not eaten all day (v.20). The medium shows him unexpected kindness: she urges him to eat, kills a fattened calf, and bakes bread (vv.21-24). This is Saul's last meal — a condemned man fed by a woman he should have executed. He eats and leaves into the night (v.25) — walking toward his death at Gilboa.

Map & Geography

  • Endor: A village on the north side of Mount Moreh, behind the Philistine camp — Saul crosses enemy lines to reach it at night
  • Mount Gilboa: Where Saul's army camps facing the Philistines in the Jezreel Valley
  • The Philistines camp at Shunem, across the valley from Gilboa — Saul is terrified by their numbers

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): enduringword.com Guzik argues that this was genuinely Samuel (not a demon), based on the text's straightforward language and the medium's own shock. He notes that God permitted this extraordinary event as a final act of judgment — Saul sought the dead because the living God would not answer him.
  • Charles Spurgeon: "Saul enquired of the LORD, and the LORD answered him not. Here is the harvest of a life of disobedience. God will not be mocked. He who rejects God's word when it is freely given shall find God silent when he desperately needs to hear. Let us tremble at this: there is a point beyond which God's patience does not extend. Seek the Lord while He may be found; call upon Him while He is near. For the day may come when you call and He does not answer."

Reflection

  • 1. Divine silence is the most terrifying judgment (v.6). God answered Saul "not... neither by dreams, nor by Urim, nor by prophets." Every channel closed. This is the consequence of persistently rejecting God's word: eventually He stops speaking. Do not take God's voice for granted. Respond when He speaks, lest He fall silent.
  • 2. Desperation without repentance leads to deeper sin (vv.7-8). Saul is desperate — but not repentant. Instead of humbling himself before God, he seeks a medium. Desperation that drives us to God is healthy; desperation that drives us to forbidden alternatives is deadly. When you are desperate, the question is: where do you turn?
  • 3. God's word does not change (vv.17-18). Samuel's message from beyond the grave is the same as his message in life: the kingdom is torn from Saul because of disobedience regarding Amalek. God does not revise His verdicts. What He said through His word remains true whether we accept it or not.
  • 4. There is a point of no return (v.19). Saul receives no offer of repentance, no possibility of restoration. His doom is sealed. While God's patience is extraordinary, it is not infinite. Do not presume upon grace. Today is the day of salvation (2 Corinthians 6:2).
  • 5. Sin's consequences are generational (v.19). "Thou and thy sons" will die. Saul's rebellion costs not only his own life but his sons' lives — including Jonathan, the faithful friend of David. Our sin does not affect only us. It touches our children, our families, our communities. The stakes of obedience are higher than we think.
