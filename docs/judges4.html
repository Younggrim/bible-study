Judges — Chapter 4

1And the children of Israel again did evil in the sight of the LORD, when Ehud was dead.

2And the LORD sold them into the hand of Jabin king of Canaan, that reigned in Hazor; the captain of whose host was Sisera, which dwelt in Harosheth of the Gentiles.

3And the children of Israel cried unto the LORD: for he had nine hundred chariots of iron; and twenty years he mightily oppressed the children of Israel.

4And Deborah, a prophetess, the wife of Lapidoth, she judged Israel at that time.

5And she dwelt under the palm tree of Deborah between Ramah and Bethel in mount Ephraim: and the children of Israel came up to her for judgment.

6And she sent and called Barak the son of Abinoam out of Kedesh-naphtali, and said unto him, Hath not the LORD God of Israel commanded, saying, Go and draw toward mount Tabor, and take with thee ten thousand men of the children of Naphtali and of the children of Zebulun?

7And I will draw unto thee to the river Kishon Sisera, the captain of Jabin’s army, with his chariots and his multitude; and I will deliver him into thine hand.

8And Barak said unto her, If thou wilt go with me, then I will go: but if thou wilt not go with me, then I will not go.

9And she said, I will surely go with thee: notwithstanding the journey that thou takest shall not be for thine honour; for the LORD shall sell Sisera into the hand of a woman. And Deborah arose, and went with Barak to Kedesh.

10And Barak called Zebulun and Naphtali to Kedesh; and he went up with ten thousand men at his feet: and Deborah went up with him.

11Now Heber the Kenite, which was of the children of Hobab the father in law of Moses, had severed himself from the Kenites, and pitched his tent unto the plain of Zaanaim, which is by Kedesh.

12And they shewed Sisera that Barak the son of Abinoam was gone up to mount Tabor.

13And Sisera gathered together all his chariots, even nine hundred chariots of iron, and all the people that were with him, from Harosheth of the Gentiles unto the river of Kishon.

14And Deborah said unto Barak, Up; for this is the day in which the LORD hath delivered Sisera into thine hand: is not the LORD gone out before thee? So Barak went down from mount Tabor, and ten thousand men after him.

15And the LORD discomfited Sisera, and all his chariots, and all his host, with the edge of the sword before Barak; so that Sisera lighted down off his chariot, and fled away on his feet.

16But Barak pursued after the chariots, and after the host, unto Harosheth of the Gentiles: and all the host of Sisera fell upon the edge of the sword; and there was not a man left.

17Howbeit Sisera fled away on his feet to the tent of Jael the wife of Heber the Kenite: for there was peace between Jabin the king of Hazor and the house of Heber the Kenite.

18And Jael went out to meet Sisera, and said unto him, Turn in, my lord, turn in to me; fear not. And when he had turned in unto her into the tent, she covered him with a mantle.

19And he said unto her, Give me, I pray thee, a little water to drink; for I am thirsty. And she opened a bottle of milk, and gave him drink, and covered him.

20Again he said unto her, Stand in the door of the tent, and it shall be, when any man doth come and enquire of thee, and say, Is there any man here? that thou shalt say, No.

21Then Jael Heber’s wife took a nail of the tent, and took an hammer in her hand, and went softly unto him, and smote the nail into his temples, and fastened it into the ground: for he was fast asleep and weary. So he died.

22And, behold, as Barak pursued Sisera, Jael came out to meet him, and said unto him, Come, and I will shew thee the man whom thou seekest. And when he came into her tent, behold, Sisera lay dead, and the nail was in his temples.

23So God subdued on that day Jabin the king of Canaan before the children of Israel.

24And the hand of the children of Israel prospered, and prevailed against Jabin the king of Canaan, until they had destroyed Jabin king of Canaan.

20He said to her, “Stand watch at the entrance to the tent. If anyone comes along and asks you, ‘Is there a man here?’ say, ‘No.’”

Summary

Deborah the prophetess summons Barak to fight Sisera's army, and God routs the Canaanite chariots at the river Kishon. Jael drives a tent peg through Sisera's skull, ending twenty years of oppression.

Authorship & Background

Author: Unknown, traditionally attributed to Samuel. Judges covers approximately 350 years (1380-1050 BC) between Joshua's death and the rise of the monarchy. Hebrew title: 'Shophetim' — 'Judges' (deliverers/ rulers raised by God). The book records Israel's repeated cycle: sin → oppression → crying out → deliverance → rest → sin again. The refrain: 'In those days there was no king in Israel: every man did that which was right in his own eyes' (17:6, 21:25). The book demonstrates humanity's need for a righteous king — ultimately fulfilled in Christ.
Historical Context: Chapter 4 records the deliverance of Israel through Deborah and Barak from the oppression of Jabin king of Canaan and his military commander Sisera. This is the only narrative in Judges where a woman serves as judge — Deborah is both prophetess and judge, the spiritual and civil leader of Israel. The chapter features two remarkable women: Deborah, who commissions and accompanies Barak to battle, and Jael, who kills Sisera with a tent peg. Deborah's prophecy is fulfilled: "the LORD shall sell Sisera into the hand of a woman" (v.9). The military situation is dire — Sisera commands 900 iron chariots, the ancient equivalent of tanks. Israel has been "mightily oppressed" for twenty years (v.3). Yet God draws Sisera to the river Kishon where his chariots become a liability (cf. 5:21 — the river sweeps them away). The chapter demonstrates that God's deliverance is not limited by gender, military technology, or human weakness — He uses whomever He chooses.
Deborah the Judge (vv.1-5): After Ehud's death, Israel sins again (v.1). God sells them to Jabin king of Canaan (v.2) — a dynasty name; an earlier Jabin was defeated by Joshua (Joshua 11:1-11). Sisera, his general, has 900 iron chariots and oppresses Israel cruelly for twenty years (v.3). Deborah — "a prophetess, the wife of Lapidoth" — judges Israel (v.4). She sits under a palm tree between Ramah and Bethel, and Israel comes to her for judgment (v.5). She is the only judge described as actually adjudicating disputes — she functions as both spiritual leader (prophetess) and civil authority (judge).
Barak's Commission and Condition (vv.6-10): Deborah summons Barak and delivers God's command: gather 10,000 men from Naphtali and Zebulun at Mount Tabor (v.6). God promises to draw Sisera to the river Kishon and deliver him into Barak's hand (v.7). Barak's response: "If thou wilt go with me, then I will go: but if thou wilt not go with me, then I will not go" (v.8). This is not necessarily cowardice — it may reflect his desire for the prophetess's presence as assurance of God's presence. But Deborah pronounces the consequence: "the journey that thou takest shall not be for thine honour; for the LORD shall sell Sisera into the hand of a woman" (v.9). The glory of the final kill will go to a woman — not Deborah, but Jael.
The Battle (vv.12-16): Sisera musters his 900 chariots at the river Kishon (v.13). Deborah gives the command: "Up; for this is the day in which the LORD hath delivered Sisera into thine hand: is not the LORD gone out before thee?" (v.14). Barak descends from Mount Tabor with 10,000 men. "The LORD discomfited Sisera, and all his chariots" (v.15). The word "discomfited" (Hebrew "vayaham") suggests supernatural confusion and panic — God Himself routs the enemy. Sisera abandons his chariot and flees on foot (v.15). Barak pursues and destroys the entire army — "there was not a man left" (v.16).
Jael Kills Sisera (vv.17-22): Sisera flees to the tent of Jael, wife of Heber the Kenite — there was peace between Jabin and Heber's house (v.17). Jael invites him in: "Turn in, my lord... fear not" (v.18). She covers him, gives him milk (v.19), and he falls asleep from exhaustion. Then "Jael Heber's wife took a nail of the tent, and took an hammer in her hand, and went softly unto him, and smote the nail into his temples, and fastened it into the ground" (v.21). When Barak arrives pursuing Sisera, Jael shows him the dead commander (v.22). Deborah's prophecy is fulfilled — Sisera is sold "into the hand of a woman."

Map & Geography

  • Hazor: Jabin's capital in upper Galilee (rebuilt since Joshua 11) — oppressing the northern tribes
  • Harosheth-hagoiim: Sisera's base, likely at the western end of the Jezreel Valley near the Kishon River
  • Kedesh (Naphtali): Where Barak is summoned; Mount Tabor: The staging ground overlooking the Jezreel plain

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): enduringword.com Guzik notes that Deborah is unique among the judges — she is already functioning as judge before the crisis, not raised up in response to it. He sees Barak's request not as cowardice but as incomplete faith. The chapter shows God using women in leadership when men fail to lead.
  • Charles Spurgeon: "Sisera had nine hundred chariots of iron, but God had the river Kishon. All the iron in the world cannot stand against the waters of God's providence. The mightiest armies are nothing when God fights against them. Sisera fled on foot — his chariots became his coffin. So it is with every weapon formed against God's people: what the enemy trusts in becomes the instrument of his destruction."

Reflection

  • 1. God uses women when men fail to lead (vv.4,9). Deborah's leadership is not presented as the ideal but as God's provision when men abdicate. She is faithful, courageous, and Spirit-led. God is not limited by human conventions — He uses whoever is willing and available.
  • 2. Incomplete faith is still faith (v.8). Barak's condition ("if you go, I'll go") shows imperfect faith — but he still went. He still obeyed. He still fought. Hebrews 11:32 lists him among the faithful. God uses imperfect faith. He does not demand perfection before He acts — He demands willingness.
  • 3. God turns the enemy's strength into weakness (v.15). Nine hundred iron chariots — Israel's greatest fear (cf. 1:19). But God draws them to the river where chariots become death traps. What the enemy trusts in becomes the instrument of his defeat. God is never intimidated by human technology or power.
  • 4. "Is not the LORD gone out before thee?" (v.14). Deborah's question to Barak is the question for every believer facing overwhelming odds. If God goes before you, what can stand against you? The issue is never the size of the enemy but the presence of God.
  • 5. Jael's decisive action (v.21). She did not hesitate, deliberate, or second-guess. When the moment came, she acted with precision and courage. Sometimes faithfulness requires decisive, uncomfortable action — not endless deliberation.
