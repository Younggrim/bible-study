2 Kings — Chapter 22

1Josiah was eight years old when he began to reign, and he reigned thirty-one years in Jerusalem. His mother’s name was Jedidah the daughter of Adaiah of Bozkath.

2And he did what was right in the eyes of the LORD and walked in all the way of David his father, and he did not turn aside to the right or to the left.

3In the eighteenth year of King Josiah, the king sent Shaphan the son of Azaliah, son of Meshullam, the secretary, to the house of the LORD, saying,

4“Go up to Hilkiah the high priest, that he may count the money that has been brought into the house of the LORD, which the keepers of the threshold have collected from the people.

5And let it be given into the hand of the workmen who have the oversight of the house of the LORD, and let them give it to the workmen who are at the house of the LORD, repairing the house

6(that is, to the carpenters, and to the builders, and to the masons), and let them use it for buying timber and quarried stone to repair the house.

7But no accounting shall be asked from them for the money that is delivered into their hand, for they deal honestly.”

8And Hilkiah the high priest said to Shaphan the secretary, “I have found the Book of the Law in the house of the LORD.” And Hilkiah gave the book to Shaphan, and he read it.

9And Shaphan the secretary came to the king, and reported to the king, “Your servants have emptied out the money that was found in the house and have delivered it into the hand of the workmen who have the oversight of the house of the LORD.”

10Then Shaphan the secretary told the king, “Hilkiah the priest has given me a book.” And Shaphan read it before the king.

11When the king heard the words of the Book of the Law, he tore his clothes.

12And the king commanded Hilkiah the priest, and Ahikam the son of Shaphan, and Achbor the son of Micaiah, and Shaphan the secretary, and Asaiah the king’s servant, saying,

13“Go, inquire of the LORD for me, and for the people, and for all Judah, concerning the words of this book that has been found. For great is the wrath of the LORD that is kindled against us, because our fathers have not obeyed the words of this book, to do according to all that is written concerning us.”

14So Hilkiah the priest, and Ahikam, and Achbor, and Shaphan, and Asaiah went to Huldah the prophetess, the wife of Shallum the son of Tikvah, son of Harhas, keeper of the wardrobe (now she lived in Jerusalem in the Second Quarter), and they talked with her.

15And she said to them, “Thus says the LORD, the God of Israel: ‘Tell the man who sent you to me,

16Thus says the LORD, Behold, I will bring disaster upon this place and upon its inhabitants, all the words of the book that the king of Judah has read.

17Because they have forsaken me and have made offerings to other gods, that they might provoke me to anger with all the work of their hands, therefore my wrath will be kindled against this place, and it will not be quenched.

18But to the king of Judah, who sent you to inquire of the LORD, thus shall you say to him, Thus says the LORD, the God of Israel: Regarding the words that you have heard,

19because your heart was penitent, and you humbled yourself before the LORD, when you heard how I spoke against this place and against its inhabitants, that they should become a desolation and a curse, and you have torn your clothes and wept before me, I also have heard you, declares the LORD.

20Therefore, behold, I will gather you to your fathers, and you shall be gathered to your grave in peace, and your eyes shall not see all the disaster that I will bring upon this place.’” And they brought back word to the king.

1Josiah was eight years old when he became king, and he reigned for thirty-one years in Jerusalem. His mother was Jedidah, daughter of Adaiah, from Bozkath.

3In the eighteenth year of King Josiah’s reign, the king sent the scribe Shaphan son of Azaliah, son of Meshullam, to the Lord’s temple with these orders:

4“Go up to Hilkiah the high priest and have him melt down the silver that has been brought by the people to the Lord’s temple and has been collected by the guards at the door.

5Have them hand it over to the construction foremen assigned to the Lord’s temple. They in turn should pay the temple workers to repair it,

6including craftsmen, builders, and masons, and should buy wood and chiseled stone for the repair work.

7Do not audit the foremen who disburse the silver, for they are honest.”

8Hilkiah the high priest informed Shaphan the scribe, “I found the scroll of the law in the Lord’s temple.” Hilkiah gave the scroll to Shaphan and he read it.

9Shaphan the scribe went to the king and reported, “Your servants melted down the silver in the temple and handed it over to the construction foremen assigned to the Lord’s temple.”

14So Hilkiah the priest, Ahikam, Achbor, Shaphan, and Asaiah went to Huldah the prophetess, the wife of Shullam son of Tikvah, the son of Harhas, the supervisor of the wardrobe. (She lived in Jerusalem in the Mishneh district.) They stated their business,

15and she said to them: “This is what the Lord God of Israel has said: ‘Say this to the man who sent you to me:

17This will happen because they have abandoned me and offered sacrifices to other gods, angering me with all the idols they have made. My anger will ignite against this place and will not be extinguished!’”

18Say this to the king of Judah, who sent you to seek an oracle from the Lord: “This is what the Lord God of Israel has said concerning the words you have heard:

Summary

Eight-year-old Josiah becomes king; at eighteen he repairs the temple and the Book of the Law is discovered. Josiah tears his robes, and Huldah the prophetess confirms that judgment is coming—but not in Josiah's lifetime.

Key Verses

  • v.2 — "And he did that which was right in the sight of the LORD, and walked in all the way of David his father, and turned not aside to the right hand or to the left." (ESV) — The fullest commendation given to any king. Unlike others who did right "yet the high places were not removed," Josiah receives unqualified praise. "Turned not aside to the right hand or to the left" — language drawn from Deuteronomy 17:20 and Joshua 1:7, describing perfect covenant faithfulness. Josiah walked the straight path of David without deviation.
  • v.8 — "And Hilkiah the high priest said to Shaphan the scribe, I have found the book of the law in the house of the LORD." (ESV) — The most momentous discovery in Israel's history since the giving of the Law itself. That God's word could be lost in God's house is both tragic and instructive. Manasseh's corruption was so thorough that Scripture itself was buried. Yet the word was not destroyed — only hidden. It surfaces at the right time, in the right hands, before the right king.
  • v.11 — "And it happened, when the king had heard the words of the book of the law, that he rent his clothes." (ESV) — The proper response to God's word is conviction, not complacency. Josiah tore his clothes — the most dramatic expression of grief and repentance. He heard the curses of Deuteronomy 28 and recognized that Judah stood condemned. His reaction reveals the condition of his heart: soft, teachable, humble before God's authority.
  • v.13 — "Go you, inquire of the LORD for me, and for the people, and for all Judah, concerning the words of this book that is found: for great is the wrath of the LORD that is kindled against us, because our fathers have not hearkened to the words of this book." (ESV) — Josiah's immediate response is corporate: not "for me" alone but "for the people and for all Judah." He recognizes generational accountability — "our fathers have not hearkened." True leaders feel the weight of their people's sin and seek God on behalf of the community.
  • v.19 — "Because your heart was tender, and you have humbled thyself before the LORD, when you heardest what I spake against this place... and have rent your clothes, and wept before me; I also have heard you, says the LORD." (ESV) — God responds to the tender heart. The Hebrew "rakkah" (tender) means soft, pliable, responsive. Josiah's heart was not hardened by power or numbed by prosperity. He wept — and God heard. The pattern is consistent throughout Scripture: God resists the proud but gives grace to the humble (James 4:6). A tender heart is more powerful than a mighty army.

Cross-References

  • v.2 → Deuteronomy 17:18-20 — The law for kings: to write a copy of the Torah, read it daily, and not turn "to the right hand or to the left." Josiah fulfilled what generations of kings neglected. He is the Deuteronomic ideal of kingship — the king who submits to the Book.
  • v.8 → Deuteronomy 31:24-26 — Moses commanded that the Book of the Law be placed "in the side of the ark of the covenant." By Josiah's day it had been displaced, lost, forgotten. The gap between Moses' command and Josiah's discovery spans centuries of neglect.
  • v.11 → Ezra 9:3-5 — Ezra tears his garments when he learns of Israel's intermarriage after the exile. The same pattern: hearing God's word, recognizing disobedience, and responding with visible, physical grief. True repentance involves the whole person — body and soul.
  • v.14 → 2 Chronicles 34:22-28 — The parallel account of Huldah's prophecy with nearly identical wording, confirming both the event's historicity and its centrality to the theological narrative.
  • v.14 → Judges 4:4 — Deborah, another prophetess whom God used at a critical moment in Israel's history. Huldah and Deborah demonstrate that God speaks through women when the word is needed, regardless of cultural expectations. Male prophets were available; God chose Huldah.
  • v.19 → Isaiah 66:2 — "But to this man will I look, even to him that is poor and of a contrite spirit, and trembleth at my word." Josiah's tender heart and trembling before Scripture are exactly what God looks for. Tenderness before God's word is the precondition for hearing God.
  • v.19 → James 4:6,10 — "God resisteth the proud, but gives grace to the humble... Humble yourselves in the sight of the Lord, and he shall lift you up." Josiah humbled himself and received personal mercy within national judgment.
  • v.20 → 2 Kings 23:29 — Josiah's death at Megiddo. "Gathered to your grave in peace" meant he would not witness Jerusalem's fall — not that his death would be peaceful. God's promises require careful reading; they are always true but not always in the way we expect.

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): enduringword.com Guzik highlights the remarkable fact that God's word can be lost even within God's house — the Temple still functioned, priests still served, but the Scripture that was supposed to govern it all lay forgotten in a storeroom. He draws the parallel to churches today where religious activity continues while the actual authority of Scripture has been buried under tradition, programs, or cultural accommodation.
  • Charles Spurgeon: "When Josiah heard the words of the Book, he rent his clothes. He did not debate whether the text was authentic; he did not appoint a committee to study its implications; he did not suggest that perhaps the words applied to another time. He heard, he believed, he trembled, he repented. Would to God that all who hear the Scriptures read would respond with such honest tenderness! The Book has lost none of its power — but we have often lost the tenderness of heart that feels its edge. A hard heart can sit under the thunder of Sinai and feel nothing; a tender heart needs only one verse to fall prostrate. Pray for tenderness — it is the soil in which all other graces grow."

Translations

  • v.3 → KJV: "Shaphan the son of Azaliah, the son of Meshullam, the scribe" / ESV: "Shaphan the son of Azaliah, son of Meshullam, the secretary" — KJV "scribe" and ESV "secretary" both translate Hebrew "sopher." In this period, the sopher was a high-ranking royal official (secretary of state), not merely a copyist. Shaphan's family became influential supporters of Jeremiah's ministry (Jeremiah 26:24; 36:10-12).
  • v.7 → KJV: "they dealt faithfully" / ESV: "they deal honestly" — Hebrew "be'emunah" means "in faithfulness/integrity." Both translations capture the meaning. The detail shows that Josiah's reform had already produced trustworthy workers — a contrast to the corruption of previous reigns. Integrity in small matters (financial accountability) reflects deeper spiritual health.
  • v.14 → KJV: "now she dwelt in Jerusalem in the college" / ESV: "now she lived in Jerusalem in the Second Quarter" — Hebrew "mishneh" means "second" or "double." KJV "college" follows the Targum's interpretation (a place of teaching). ESV "Second Quarter" identifies it as a newer section of Jerusalem — the expansion area north of the original city. Modern scholarship favors the geographic reading: a residential district, not an educational institution.
  • v.16 → KJV: "I will bring evil upon this place" / ESV: "I will bring disaster upon this place" — Hebrew "ra'ah" means calamity, disaster, or harm. KJV "evil" can be misunderstood as moral evil done by God. ESV's "disaster" clarifies: God brings judgment (consequences), not moral wickedness. The distinction is theologically significant — God is righteous in all His acts, including judgment.

Reflection

  • 1. God's word can be lost in God's house (v.8). The Temple functioned for decades without the Book of the Law. Religious activity continued — priests served, offerings were made, festivals were observed — but the actual Scripture that should have governed it all was buried and forgotten. This is the danger of religion without revelation: you can maintain all the forms while losing the substance. Is God's word truly central in your life, or has it been displaced by religious routine?
  • 2. The right response to God's word is conviction, not comfort (v.11). Josiah tore his clothes. He did not rationalize, explain away, or defer action. When Scripture confronts sin, the tender heart breaks before it. If you read the Bible without ever being disturbed, challenged, or grieved, you may not be truly hearing it. Ask God for the tenderness that trembles at His word.
  • 3. True leaders take responsibility for corporate sin (v.13). Josiah did not say "this was my grandfather's fault" — he said "our fathers have not hearkened." He owned the generational failure and sought God on behalf of all Judah. Leadership means bearing the weight of others' disobedience without deflecting blame. Intercede for your community's sins, not just your own.
  • 4. God sends His word at strategic moments (vv.3-8). The Book was found during Temple repair — when someone was doing the work of restoration. God's revelatory moments often come when we are already in motion — already obedient, already working, already seeking. You don't find the Book sitting idle; you find it while rebuilding what was broken. Faithfulness in the work you know opens doors to truth you haven't yet discovered.
  • 5. A tender heart receives personal mercy even within corporate judgment (v.19). Josiah could not save the nation from its accumulated sin, but his humility before God brought personal grace. You may not be able to change your culture, your family system, or your generation's trajectory — but your own tender heart before God is never wasted. God sees individual faith even when collective judgment is certain.
