Exodus — Chapter 21

1“Now these are the rules that you shall set before them.

2When you buy a Hebrew slave, he shall serve six years, and in the seventh he shall go out free, for nothing.

3If he comes in single, he shall go out single; if he comes in married, then his wife shall go out with him.

4If his master gives him a wife and she bears him sons or daughters, the wife and her children shall be her master’s, and he shall go out alone.

5But if the slave plainly says, ‘I love my master, my wife, and my children; I will not go out free,’

6then his master shall bring him to God, and he shall bring him to the door or the doorpost. And his master shall bore his ear through with an awl, and he shall be his slave forever.

7“When a man sells his daughter as a slave, she shall not go out as the male slaves do.

8If she does not please her master, who has designated her for himself, then he shall let her be redeemed. He shall have no right to sell her to a foreign people, since he has broken faith with her.

9If he designates her for his son, he shall deal with her as with a daughter.

10If he takes another wife to himself, he shall not diminish her food, her clothing, or her marital rights.

11And if he does not do these three things for her, she shall go out for nothing, without payment of money.

12“Whoever strikes a man so that he dies shall be put to death.

13But if he did not lie in wait for him, but God let him fall into his hand, then I will appoint for you a place to which he may flee.

14But if a man willfully attacks another to kill him by cunning, you shall take him from my altar, that he may die.

15“Whoever strikes his father or his mother shall be put to death.

16“Whoever steals a man and sells him, and anyone found in possession of him, shall be put to death.

17“Whoever curses his father or his mother shall be put to death.

18“When men quarrel and one strikes the other with a stone or with his fist and the man does not die but takes to his bed,

19then if the man rises again and walks outdoors with his staff, he who struck him shall be clear; only he shall pay for the loss of his time, and shall have him thoroughly healed.

20“When a man strikes his slave, male or female, with a rod and the slave dies under his hand, he shall be avenged.

21But if the slave survives a day or two, he is not to be avenged, for the slave is his money.

22“When men strive together and hit a pregnant woman, so that her children come out, but there is no harm, the one who hit her shall surely be fined, as the woman’s husband shall impose on him, and he shall pay as the judges determine.

23But if there is harm, then you shall pay life for life,

24eye for eye, tooth for tooth, hand for hand, foot for foot,

25burn for burn, wound for wound, stripe for stripe.

26“When a man strikes the eye of his slave, male or female, and destroys it, he shall let the slave go free because of his eye.

27If he knocks out the tooth of his slave, male or female, he shall let the slave go free because of his tooth.

28“When an ox gores a man or a woman to death, the ox shall be stoned, and its flesh shall not be eaten, but the owner of the ox shall not be liable.

29But if the ox has been accustomed to gore in the past, and its owner has been warned but has not kept it in, and it kills a man or a woman, the ox shall be stoned, and its owner also shall be put to death.

30If a ransom is imposed on him, then he shall give for the redemption of his life whatever is imposed on him.

31If it gores a man’s son or daughter, he shall be dealt with according to this same rule.

32If the ox gores a slave, male or female, the owner shall give to their master thirty shekels of silver, and the ox shall be stoned.

33“When a man opens a pit, or when a man digs a pit and does not cover it, and an ox or a donkey falls into it,

34the owner of the pit shall make restoration. He shall give money to its owner, and the dead beast shall be his.

35“When one man’s ox butts another’s, so that it dies, then they shall sell the live ox and share its price, and the dead beast also they shall share.

36Or if it is known that the ox has been accustomed to gore in the past, and its owner has not kept it in, he shall repay ox for ox, and the dead beast shall be his.

Summary

God gives laws governing servants, personal injury, and property rights — justice that protects the vulnerable and values human life.

Key Verses

  • v.2 — "If you buy a Hebrew slave, six years he shall serve, and in the seventh he shall go out free." (ESV) — Limits on servitude; freedom built into the system.
  • v.5-6 — "I love my master... I will not go out free... he shall serve him forever." (ESV) — The voluntary bond-servant: a picture of Christ's willing service.
  • v.23-25 — "Life for life, eye for eye, tooth for tooth." (ESV) — Proportional justice: the punishment fits the crime — no more, no less.
  • v.32 — "The owner shall give to their master thirty shekels of silver." (ESV) — The price of a slave — the price paid for Christ.

Authorship & Background

  • Author: Moses (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).
  • Historical Context: Chapter 21 begins the "Book of the Covenant" (21:1-23:33) — the detailed case law that applies the Ten Commandments to daily life in ancient Israel. These are "judgments" (mishpatim) — legal rulings for specific situations. Chapter 21 covers: (1) Laws regarding Hebrew servants (vv.1-11); (2) Capital offenses (vv.12-17); (3) Laws of personal injury (vv.18-27); (4) Laws regarding dangerous animals and property (vv.28-36). These laws were revolutionary for the ancient Near East — protecting slaves, women, and the vulnerable in ways no other ancient code did. They establish the principle of proportional justice ("eye for eye") which LIMITS vengeance rather than encouraging it.
  • Laws Regarding Servants (vv.1-11): Hebrew servants serve six years, go free in the seventh (v.2). If a servant loves his master and chooses to stay permanently, his ear is pierced at the doorpost — a voluntary bond-servant (vv.5-6). Female servants have additional protections: they cannot be sold to foreigners, must be treated as daughters if betrothed to the master's son, and go free if their rights are violated (vv.7-11).
  • Capital Offenses (vv.12-17): Murder (v.12), striking parents (v.15), kidnapping (v.16), cursing parents (v.17). Distinction between murder and manslaughter: unintentional killing allows flight to a city of refuge (v.13); premeditated murder has no sanctuary (v.14).
  • Laws of Personal Injury (vv.18-27): Non-fatal assault: the attacker pays for lost time and healing (vv.18-19). Striking a slave fatally: the slave is "avenged" (v.20). Injury to a pregnant woman: if no harm, a fine; if harm, "life for life, eye for eye, tooth for tooth" (vv.22-25). Injuring a slave's eye or tooth: the slave goes free (vv.26-27).
  • Laws Regarding Animals and Property (vv.28-36): A goring ox: first offense, the ox is stoned but the owner is not liable (v.28); known dangerous ox, the owner is liable for death (v.29). A slave killed by an ox: thirty shekels of silver to the master (v.32) — the price Judas received for Christ (Matthew 26:15). Open pits and ox injuries: restitution required (vv.33-36).

Map & Geography

  • Israel remains encamped at Mount Sinai. These civil laws (the "Book of the Covenant") are given at the mountain where God spoke the Ten Commandments.

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): enduringword.com Guzik covers the humanitarian advances of these laws over other ancient codes, the bond-servant as a type of Christ, and "eye for eye" as limiting (not encouraging) vengeance.
  • Charles Spurgeon: Sermon: "The Willing Servant" on vv.5-6 (MTP Vol. 14, No. 800) "I LOVE my master — I will not go out free. Here is the bond-servant who CHOOSES slavery out of love. His ear is pierced at the doorpost — marked forever as belonging to his master. And here is Christ: He could have gone free. He was under no obligation. But He said, 'I love my Father. I love my bride. I will not go out free.' And His ear was pierced — not with an awl but with a nail. At the doorpost of the cross, He was marked forever as the willing Servant. 'Lo, I come to do thy will, O God' (Hebrews 10:9). He serves us forever — not by compulsion but by love."

Reflection

  • 1. "I love my master... I will not go out free" (v.5). Christ chose to serve us — not by compulsion but by love. His ear was "pierced" at the cross. How do you respond to a Savior who CHOSE bondage for your freedom?
  • 2. "Eye for eye" (v.24). Justice must be proportional — not excessive vengeance, not inadequate response. In your relationships, do you seek proportional justice or disproportionate revenge? Jesus goes further: "resist not evil" (Matthew 5:39) — grace exceeds justice.
  • 3. These laws protect the vulnerable: slaves, women, the unborn, the injured. God cares about justice for the powerless. Do you? Are you advocating for those who cannot advocate for themselves?
  • 4. The servant goes free in the seventh year (v.2). God builds freedom into the system. Bondage is never meant to be permanent. What bondage in your life has an expiration date that you haven't claimed?
  • 5. Thirty shekels — the price of a slave (v.32). That's what Judas received for Jesus. The world valued God's Son at a slave's price. How do YOU value Him?
