The 12 Apostles

Simon the Zealot

Who Was Simon the Zealot

Simon is distinguished from Simon Peter by the title "the Zealot" (Luke and Acts) or "the Cananaean" (Matthew and Mark — from the Aramaic word qan'an, meaning "zealous one"). This designation almost certainly refers to his political background before following Jesus. The Zealots were a Jewish faction fiercely committed to overthrowing Roman occupation through violent resistance. They viewed collaboration with Rome as treason against God and were willing to assassinate, riot, and die for Israel's political freedom.

That Jesus chose a Zealot to be among His twelve apostles is remarkable — especially when we consider that Matthew the tax collector sat in the same group. A Zealot and a tax collector represented opposite poles of Jewish society. Zealots would have considered tax collectors worthy of death for collaborating with the enemy. Yet Jesus placed them side by side, forging a community united not by politics but by allegiance to Him. Simon's inclusion among the twelve is a living parable of the reconciling power of the gospel.

Simon's transformation from political revolutionary to follower of Jesus required a fundamental reorientation of his understanding of God's kingdom. He came to Jesus expecting a military Messiah who would defeat Rome. Instead, he found a Savior who defeated sin and death through a cross. The zeal that once drove Simon toward violence was not extinguished but redirected — channeled into passionate devotion to Christ's kingdom, which advances not through swords but through love, sacrifice, and the proclamation of truth.

Key Scriptures

  • Luke 6:15 — Simon is called "the Zealot," identifying his pre-conversion political affiliation among the twelve apostles.
  • Acts 1:13 — Simon the Zealot is present in the upper room after the ascension, united in prayer with the other apostles.
  • Matthew 10:4 — Simon the Cananaean is listed among the twelve whom Jesus sends out with authority to proclaim the kingdom.
  • Mark 3:18 — Mark names Simon the Cananaean among those Jesus appointed to be with Him and to be sent out to preach.

Lessons from Simon the Zealot

Simon teaches us that Jesus transforms our deepest passions rather than eliminating them. Simon's zeal for God's honor and Israel's freedom was not wrong in itself — it was misdirected. Jesus did not ask Simon to stop caring deeply; He redirected that intensity toward the true kingdom. In the same way, God takes our strongest drives — whether for justice, achievement, creativity, or influence — and reorients them toward His purposes. The question is not "Am I too passionate?" but "Is my passion submitted to Christ?"

From Simon we also learn that the gospel creates community across the deepest political and social divides. Simon the Zealot and Matthew the tax collector — mortal enemies by every earthly measure — broke bread together, traveled together, served together, and loved one another as brothers in Christ. This is the scandal of the church: people who have every reason to hate each other are united by something greater than their differences. Simon's presence among the twelve challenges us to ask whether our political identities have become more important to us than our identity in Christ.

Reflection Questions

  • Are there passions in your life — political, social, professional — that need to be redirected rather than eliminated? How is Jesus calling you to channel them differently?
  • Simon sat at the same table as Matthew, his natural enemy. Are there people in your church or community you struggle to fellowship with because of political or social differences?
  • Simon had to redefine his understanding of God's kingdom — from political to spiritual. Are there ways your expectations of God need to be reshaped by Scripture?
  • The Zealots sought change through force. Jesus brings change through love. In what areas of your life are you trying to force change rather than trusting God's methods?
