Names of God

El Shaddai

Understanding El Shaddai

El Shaddai, translated "God Almighty," is the name God used when He appeared to Abraham in Genesis 17:1, saying, "I am God Almighty; walk before me faithfully and be blameless." The exact etymology of "Shaddai" is debated among scholars — some connect it to the Hebrew word for mountain (suggesting immovable strength), others to the word for breast (suggesting nourishment and sufficiency), and still others to a root meaning "to overpower." Together, these possibilities paint a picture of a God who is both overwhelmingly powerful and tenderly sufficient.

This name appears most frequently in the book of Job (31 times) and in the patriarchal narratives of Genesis. It is the name associated with God's dealings with the patriarchs before the full revelation of Yahweh at Sinai. When God called Himself El Shaddai, He was assuring Abraham that His power was more than adequate to fulfill seemingly impossible promises — a son to a barren couple, descendants as numerous as the stars, and a land flowing with milk and honey.

El Shaddai speaks of God's all-sufficiency: He is enough. He is enough power for any obstacle, enough provision for any need, enough comfort for any sorrow, and enough grace for any failure. The God who sustained the patriarchs through decades of wandering, waiting, and wondering is the same God who sustains us today — almighty in power yet intimate in care.

Key Scriptures

  • Genesis 17:1 — "I am God Almighty; walk before me faithfully." God reveals Himself as El Shaddai to 99-year-old Abraham, reaffirming His impossible promise.
  • Genesis 49:24-25 — "Because of the hand of the Mighty One... because of the Almighty, who blesses you." Jacob's blessing invokes El Shaddai's abundant provision.
  • Ruth 1:20-21 — "The Almighty has made my life very bitter." Naomi's honest lament to El Shaddai in her grief — yet He was working redemption all along.
  • Psalm 91:1 — "Whoever dwells in the shelter of the Most High will rest in the shadow of the Almighty." El Shaddai provides ultimate refuge and rest.
  • Revelation 4:8 — "Holy, holy, holy is the Lord God Almighty, who was, and is, and is to come." The eternal worship of El Shaddai in heaven.

What This Name Means for Us

When we face situations that seem impossible — a diagnosis with no cure, a relationship beyond repair, a calling beyond our abilities — El Shaddai reminds us that our God specializes in the impossible. He gave a son to a barren woman of ninety. He brought a nation out of slavery. He raised His Son from the dead. There is no limitation, no obstacle, and no impossibility that can exhaust His almighty power or deplete His all-sufficient resources.

El Shaddai also invites us to rest. The imagery of shelter and shadow in Psalm 91 suggests not frantic striving but peaceful trust. We do not need to generate our own sufficiency — He is sufficient for us. In our weakness, His power is made perfect. In our emptiness, He fills. In our inadequacy, He more than compensates. To know God as El Shaddai is to stop striving and start trusting that the Almighty God has everything we need.

Reflection Questions

  • What "impossible" situation in your life needs the intervention of El Shaddai — God Almighty?
  • Like Naomi, have you ever felt that God's dealings were bitter? How might He be working redemption in ways you cannot yet see?
  • In what areas are you trying to be self-sufficient rather than resting in God's all-sufficiency?
  • How does knowing that El Shaddai sustained the patriarchs through decades of waiting encourage you in your own seasons of waiting?
