Ten Commandments

You Shall Not Murder

The Commandment

The sixth commandment, "You shall not murder" (Exodus 20:13), establishes the sanctity of human life as a core principle of God's moral order. The Hebrew word used here (ratsach) refers specifically to unlawful killing — the intentional, unjustified taking of human life. Every human being is created in the image of God (Genesis 9:6), and to destroy that image-bearer is to assault the Creator Himself. This commandment places an absolute boundary around the value of human life.

In the Old Testament context, this prohibition distinguished lawful killing (self-defense, just war, capital punishment administered by the state) from murder — the unauthorized, malicious destruction of another person. Israel's legal system included cities of refuge for those who killed accidentally, demonstrating that intent and circumstance mattered deeply. The commandment protected the vulnerable and ensured that human life could never be treated as cheap or disposable.

Jesus radically expanded this commandment in the Sermon on the Mount, teaching that murder begins in the heart long before it reaches the hands. "Everyone who is angry with his brother will be liable to judgment" (Matthew 5:22). The New Testament reveals that hatred, contempt, dehumanizing language, and indifference to suffering are all violations of the spirit of this law. John states it plainly: "Everyone who hates his brother is a murderer" (1 John 3:15).

Key Scriptures

  • Exodus 20:13 — "You shall not murder."
  • Matthew 5:21-22 — "You have heard that it was said, 'You shall not murder'... But I say to you that everyone who is angry with his brother will be liable to judgment."
  • 1 John 3:15 — "Everyone who hates his brother is a murderer, and you know that no murderer has eternal life abiding in him."
  • Romans 12:19-21 — "Beloved, never avenge yourselves, but leave it to the wrath of God... Do not be overcome by evil, but overcome evil with good."
  • James 4:1-2 — "What causes quarrels and what causes fights among you? Is it not this, that your passions are at war within you?"

Heart of the Law

The heart of this commandment is not merely the absence of violence but the presence of active love for human life. It calls us to see every person — regardless of their utility, status, or likability — as bearing the sacred image of God. This means the commandment is violated not only by taking life but by failing to protect it, by dehumanizing others through words, by nursing hatred in our hearts, and by remaining indifferent when we could act to preserve life.

For followers of Jesus, the sixth commandment becomes a call to radical peacemaking. It means refusing to repay evil with evil, choosing forgiveness over vengeance, and actively seeking the flourishing of even those who have wronged us. It means examining our hearts for the seeds of murder — contempt, rage, bitterness — and bringing them to the cross before they bear their deadly fruit. In Christ, we are called not merely to refrain from killing but to become agents of life in a world marked by death.

Reflection Questions

  • Are there people toward whom you harbor anger, contempt, or hatred that Jesus would identify as violating this commandment?
  • How does recognizing every person as an image-bearer of God change the way you speak about and treat others?
  • What practical steps can you take to become an agent of life and peace in your relationships and community?
