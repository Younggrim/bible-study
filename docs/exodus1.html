Exodus — Chapter 1

1These are the names of the sons of Israel who came to Egypt with Jacob, each with his household:

2Reuben, Simeon, Levi, and Judah,

3Issachar, Zebulun, and Benjamin,

4Dan and Naphtali, Gad and Asher.

5All the descendants of Jacob were seventy persons; Joseph was already in Egypt.

6Then Joseph died, and all his brothers and all that generation.

7But the people of Israel were fruitful and increased greatly; they multiplied and grew exceedingly strong, so that the land was filled with them.

8Now there arose a new king over Egypt, who did not know Joseph.

9And he said to his people, “Behold, the people of Israel are too many and too mighty for us.

10Come, let us deal shrewdly with them, lest they multiply, and, if war breaks out, they join our enemies and fight against us and escape from the land.”

11Therefore they set taskmasters over them to afflict them with heavy burdens. They built for Pharaoh store cities, Pithom and Raamses.

12But the more they were oppressed, the more they multiplied and the more they spread abroad. And the Egyptians were in dread of the people of Israel.

13So they ruthlessly made the people of Israel work as slaves

14and made their lives bitter with hard service, in mortar and brick, and in all kinds of work in the field. In all their work they ruthlessly made them work as slaves.

15Then the king of Egypt said to the Hebrew midwives, one of whom was named Shiphrah and the other Puah,

16“When you serve as midwife to the Hebrew women and see them on the birthstool, if it is a son, you shall kill him, but if it is a daughter, she shall live.”

17But the midwives feared God and did not do as the king of Egypt commanded them, but let the male children live.

18So the king of Egypt called the midwives and said to them, “Why have you done this, and let the male children live?”

19The midwives said to Pharaoh, “Because the Hebrew women are not like the Egyptian women, for they are vigorous and give birth before the midwife comes to them.”

20So God dealt well with the midwives. And the people multiplied and grew very strong.

21And because the midwives feared God, he gave them families.

22Then Pharaoh commanded all his people, “Every son that is born to the Hebrews you shall cast into the Nile, but you shall let every daughter live.”

Summary

Israel multiplies in Egypt despite a new Pharaoh's brutal slavery and genocide decree, but God preserves His people through courageous midwives who fear Him more than the king.

Key Verses

  • v.7 — "But the people of Israel were fruitful and increased greatly; they multiplied and grew exceedingly strong, so that the land was filled with them." (ESV) — God's covenant promise fulfilled despite circumstances.
  • v.8 — "Now there arose a new king over Egypt, who did not know Joseph." (ESV) — The turning point: from favor to slavery.
  • v.12 — "But the more they were oppressed, the more they multiplied and the more they spread abroad." (ESV) — God's blessing cannot be stopped by human oppression.
  • v.17 — "But the midwives feared God and did not do as the king of Egypt commanded them, but let the male children live." (ESV) — Civil disobedience grounded in the fear of God.
  • v.22 — "Every son that is born to the Hebrews you shall cast into the Nile." (ESV) — Satan's attempt to destroy the covenant line — and the deliverer (Moses).

Authorship & Background

Author: Moses. Exodus is the second of the five books of Moses (the
Pentateuch/Torah). Internal evidence: Exodus 17:14, 24:4, 34:27 — God
commands Moses to write. Jesus attributes Exodus to Moses (Mark 7:10,
12:26). The book covers events Moses personally witnessed and participated
in — from his birth through the construction of the Tabernacle.

Date of Writing: Approximately 1445-1405 BC (early date of the Exodus),
written during the 40 years of wilderness wandering.

Title: "Exodus" comes from the Greek "exodos" — "a going out" / "departure."
The Hebrew title is "Shemot" — "Names" — from the opening words: "these are
the names."

Historical Context: Chapter 1 bridges Genesis and Exodus. Approximately 350-
400 years pass between Genesis 50 and Exodus 1. The 70 souls who entered
Egypt have become a nation of perhaps 2-3 million. A new dynasty ("a new
king... which knew not Joseph" — v.8) enslaves Israel. The chapter introduces
the conflict that drives the entire book: Pharaoh's oppression vs. God's
covenant faithfulness. Despite slavery, genocide attempts, and brutal
affliction — "the more they afflicted them, the more they multiplied" (v.12).

Israel's Growth (vv.1-7): The chapter opens by naming the sons of Jacob who
came to Egypt — 70 souls (v.5). Joseph and his generation die (v.6). Then
the explosion: "fruitful, and increased abundantly, and multiplied, and waxed
exceeding mighty" (v.7) — five verbs of growth, echoing God's creation
mandate (Genesis 1:28) and His promise to Abraham (Genesis 15:5).

Pharaoh's Oppression (vv.8-14): A new king "which knew not Joseph" (v.8) —
a dynasty change. He fears Israel's numbers (v.9) and enslaves them with
"taskmasters to afflict them" (v.11). They build Pithom and Raamses (v.11).
But oppression backfires: "the more they afflicted them, the more they
multiplied" (v.12). So Egypt intensifies: "rigour" — crushing, breaking
labor (vv.13-14).

The Midwives' Courage (vv.15-21): Pharaoh commands the Hebrew midwives
Shiphrah and Puah to kill all male babies (vv.15-16). "But the midwives
feared God" (v.17) — they obey God rather than Pharaoh. When questioned,
they claim Hebrew women deliver too quickly (v.19). "God dealt well with the
midwives" and "made them houses" — established their families (vv.20-21).

Pharaoh's Final Decree (v.22): Failing with the midwives, Pharaoh commands
ALL his people: "Every son that is born ye shall cast into the river" (v.22)
— state-sponsored genocide. This sets the stage for Moses' birth in chapter 2.

Map & Geography

  • Goshen: Region in the eastern Nile Delta where Israel settled (Genesis 46:28-34). Fertile land, separate from Egyptian population centers.
  • Egypt (the land): The broader kingdom under Pharaoh's rule; Israel dwelt in Goshen but labored on Egyptian building projects.
  • Pithom and Raamses (v.11): Store-cities built by Israelite slaves, located in the eastern Delta region.

Glossary

  • Exodus — Greek "exodos" — departure, going out. The book of Israel's
  • deliverance from Egypt — a picture of salvation from sin.
  • Taskmasters — Egyptian overseers of forced labor. Organized oppression designed to break Israel's spirit and limit their growth.
  • Rigour (perekh) — Crushing, breaking severity. Labor designed not just to produce but to destroy.
  • Shiphrah and Puah — Hebrew midwives who feared God more than Pharaoh. Models of courageous civil disobedience when human law contradicts divine law.

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): enduringword.com Guzik covers the transition from Genesis, the growth of Israel, Pharaoh's escalating oppression, and the midwives' faith as a model of godly civil disobedience.
  • Charles Spurgeon: Sermon: "The More They Afflicted Them" on v.12 (MTP Vol. 14, No. 793) "The more they afflicted them, the MORE THEY MULTIPLIED. This is God's law for His church in every age. Persecution does not destroy the church — it multiplies it. The blood of the martyrs is the seed of the church. Pharaoh's bricks and mortar could not stop God's promise. Rome's lions could not stop the gospel. And whatever affliction the enemy brings against you — it will only multiply what God is doing in your life. The devil is a fool: every blow he strikes against God's people produces more growth, more faith, more fruit."

Reflection

  • 1. "The more they afflicted them, the more they multiplied" (v.12). God's purposes cannot be stopped by opposition. What opposition are you facing? Trust that God can use it to multiply His work in your life.
  • 2. "The midwives feared God" (v.17). When human authority contradicts God's commands, whom do you obey? The midwives chose God over Pharaoh — and God honored them. Where might you need this courage?
  • 3. "A new king... which knew not Joseph" (v.8). Favor with the world is temporary. The world's gratitude fades. Don't build your security on human favor — build it on God's covenant.
  • 4. Pharaoh's plan escalates: taskmasters → midwife murder → public genocide (vv.11, 16, 22). Evil escalates when unchecked. Are you tolerating "small" sins that are growing? Address them now.
  • 5. God is silent in this chapter — no theophany, no voice, no miracle. Yet He is clearly at work: multiplying Israel, empowering the midwives, overruling Pharaoh. Sometimes God works silently. His silence is not absence.
