Numbers — Chapter 12

1Miriam and Aaron spoke against Moses because of the Cushite woman whom he had married, for he had married a Cushite woman.

2And they said, “Has the LORD indeed spoken only through Moses? Has he not spoken through us also?” And the LORD heard it.

3Now the man Moses was very meek, more than all people who were on the face of the earth.

4And suddenly the LORD said to Moses and to Aaron and Miriam, “Come out, you three, to the tent of meeting.” And the three of them came out.

5And the LORD came down in a pillar of cloud and stood at the entrance of the tent and called Aaron and Miriam, and they both came forward.

6And he said, “Hear my words: If there is a prophet among you, I the LORD make myself known to him in a vision; I speak with him in a dream.

7Not so with my servant Moses. He is faithful in all my house.

8With him I speak mouth to mouth, clearly, and not in riddles, and he beholds the form of the LORD. Why then were you not afraid to speak against my servant Moses?”

9And the anger of the LORD was kindled against them, and he departed.

10When the cloud removed from over the tent, behold, Miriam was leprous, like snow. And Aaron turned toward Miriam, and behold, she was leprous.

11And Aaron said to Moses, “Oh, my lord, do not punish us because we have done foolishly and have sinned.

12Let her not be as one dead, whose flesh is half eaten away when he comes out of his mother’s womb.”

13And Moses cried to the LORD, “O God, please heal her—please.”

14But the LORD said to Moses, “If her father had but spit in her face, should she not be shamed seven days? Let her be shut outside the camp seven days, and after that she may be brought in again.”

15So Miriam was shut outside the camp seven days, and the people did not set out on the march till Miriam was brought in again.

16After that the people set out from Hazeroth, and camped in the wilderness of Paran.

Summary

Miriam and Aaron challenge Moses' authority; God rebukes them, strikes Miriam with leprosy, and affirms Moses' unique intimacy with Him.

Key Verses

v.2
"Has the LORD indeed spoken only through Moses? Has he not spoken through us also? And the LORD heard it." (ESV) — Jealousy disguised as a question. God hears challenges to His appointed servants.
v.3
"Now the man Moses was very meek, above all the men which were upon the face of the earth." — True meekness: strength under control. Moses does not defend himself.
v.7
"My servant Moses is not so, who is faithful in all mine house." — God's testimony about Moses. Faithful in ALL God's house. The highest commendation.
v.8
"With him will I speak mouth to mouth, even apparently, and not in dark speeches." — Moses' unique relationship with God: direct, clear, face-to-face.
v.8
"Why then were you not afraid to speak against my servant Moses?" (ESV) — God defends His servant. To speak against God's chosen is to provoke God's anger.
v.13
"O God, please heal her—please." (ESV) — Moses intercedes for his attacker. Grace in action.

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/numbers-12/ Guzik emphasizes that the real issue is not the Cushite wife but jealousy over Moses' authority. He highlights Moses' meekness as extraordinary — the man who could call down plagues on Egypt does not lift a finger to defend himself against his siblings. He notes that Moses' intercession for Miriam is a picture of Christ interceding for those who reject Him.
  • Charles Spurgeon: "Moses was meek — the meekest man on earth. And what did his meekness do? It left room for God to act. Moses did not need to defend himself because God defended him. When you fight your own battles, you fight alone. When you leave your defense to God, the Almighty fights for you. 'Vengeance is mine; I will repay, saith the Lord' (Romans 12:19). And mark this: when God had judged Miriam, Moses prayed for her. He did not say, 'She deserved it.' He said, 'Heal her.' This is the spirit of Christ. This is grace."

Translations

  • v.1 — KJV: "the Ethiopian woman" / ESV: "the Cushite woman" — Hebrew "ha-ishah ha-kushit." "Cush" in the Old Testament can refer to Ethiopia/Nubia (south of Egypt) or to a region in Midian/Arabia. KJV translates geographically ("Ethiopian"); ESV transliterates ("Cushite"). The identity of this woman is debated — she may be Zipporah or a second wife.
  • v.3 — KJV: "very meek" / ESV: "very meek" — Hebrew "anav me'od." "Anav" means humble, lowly, gentle, afflicted. Not weakness but strength under control — power that does not assert itself. Both translations use "meek." The word describes one who does not grasp for rights or retaliate when wronged.
  • v.6 — KJV: "I the LORD will make myself known unto him in a vision" / ESV: "I the LORD make myself known to him in a vision" — Hebrew "b'mar'ah elav etvadda" — in a vision to him I make myself known. God communicates with ordinary prophets through visions and dreams — indirect, symbolic, requiring interpretation.
  • v.7 — KJV: "faithful in all mine house" / ESV: "faithful in all my house" — Hebrew "ne'eman b'khol beiti" — trusted/faithful in all my house. "House" = God's household (Israel) or God's dwelling (the Tabernacle). Moses is entrusted with the whole — not just a portion. Hebrews 3:5 quotes this directly.
  • v.8 — KJV: "mouth to mouth, even apparently" / ESV: "mouth to mouth, clearly" — Hebrew "peh el peh... u-mar'eh" — mouth to mouth... and appearance/clearly. "Apparently" (KJV) means "openly, visually" (archaic). ESV's "clearly" captures the meaning: God speaks to Moses directly, without obscurity.
  • v.8 — KJV: "not in dark speeches" / ESV: "not in riddles" — Hebrew "v'lo b'chidot" — not in riddles/enigmas. God does not speak to Moses in parables or puzzles. His communication is plain and direct.
  • v.8 — KJV: "the similitude of the LORD shall he behold" / ESV: "he beholds the form of the LORD" — Hebrew "u-t'munat Yahweh yabit" — the form/likeness of the LORD he gazes upon. Moses sees God's "form" — not His full glory (Exodus 33:20) but a visible manifestation. An extraordinary privilege.
  • v.12 — KJV: "as one dead, of whom the flesh is half consumed" / ESV: "as one dead, whose flesh is half eaten away" — Hebrew "k'met asher b'tzeito me-rechem immo va-ye'akhel chatzi b'saro." Aaron compares Miriam's leprous state to a stillborn child whose flesh is already decaying. A graphic, desperate plea.
  • v.13 — KJV: "Heal her now, O God, I beseech thee" / ESV: "O God, please heal her—please" — Hebrew "el na r'fa na lah" — God, please, heal, please, her. Five words. Two "please" (na). The shortest prayer in the Torah. Urgent, compassionate, selfless.
  • v.14 — KJV: "If her father had but spit in her face" / ESV: "If her father had but spit in her face" — Hebrew "v'aviha yaroq yaraq b'faneha." Spitting in the face = extreme paternal rebuke/shame. If a human father's rebuke brings seven days of shame, God's rebuke requires at least the same.

Reflection

  • 1. Jealousy disguised as concern (vv.1-2). The Cushite wife is the excuse; the real issue is "Has God spoken ONLY through Moses?" Jealousy often hides behind legitimate-sounding complaints. Examine your criticisms of others — is the stated reason the real reason?
  • 2. Meekness is strength (v.3). Moses does not defend himself. He does not need to — God defends him. True strength is not self-assertion but self-restraint. "The meek shall inherit the earth" (Matthew 5:5) — not because they are weak, but because God fights for them.
  • 3. God hears (v.2). "And the LORD heard it." Every word spoken against God's servants reaches God's ears. Be careful how you speak about those God has appointed — even when you disagree with them. "Touch not mine anointed, and do my prophets no harm" (Psalm 105:15).
  • 4. Moses intercedes for his attacker (v.13). The one wronged prays for the one who wronged him. This is the spirit of Christ: "Father, forgive them" (Luke 23:34). Can you pray for those who speak against you? This is the test of true meekness.
  • 5. Grace with discipline (v.14). God heals Miriam but requires seven days outside the camp. Forgiveness does not eliminate consequences. God restores, but He also disciplines. "Whom the Lord loveth he chasteneth" (Hebrews 12:6). The seven days are not punishment but purification.
