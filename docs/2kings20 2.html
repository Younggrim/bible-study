2 Kings — Chapter 20

1In those days Hezekiah became sick and was at the point of death. And Isaiah the prophet the son of Amoz came to him and said to him, “Thus says the LORD, ‘Set your house in order, for you shall die; you shall not recover.’”

2Then Hezekiah turned his face to the wall and prayed to the LORD, saying,

3“Now, O LORD, please remember how I have walked before you in faithfulness and with a whole heart, and have done what is good in your sight.” And Hezekiah wept bitterly.

4And before Isaiah had gone out of the middle court, the word of the LORD came to him:

5“Turn back, and say to Hezekiah the leader of my people, Thus says the LORD, the God of David your father: I have heard your prayer; I have seen your tears. Behold, I will heal you. On the third day you shall go up to the house of the LORD,

6and I will add fifteen years to your life. I will deliver you and this city out of the hand of the king of Assyria, and I will defend this city for my own sake and for my servant David’s sake.”

7And Isaiah said, “Bring a cake of figs. And let them take and lay it on the boil, that he may recover.”

8And Hezekiah said to Isaiah, “What shall be the sign that the LORD will heal me, and that I shall go up to the house of the LORD on the third day?”

9And Isaiah said, “This shall be the sign to you from the LORD, that the LORD will do the thing that he has promised: shall the shadow go forward ten steps, or go back ten steps?”

10And Hezekiah answered, “It is an easy thing for the shadow to lengthen ten steps. Rather let the shadow go back ten steps.”

11And Isaiah the prophet called to the LORD, and he brought the shadow back ten steps, by which it had gone down on the steps of Ahaz.

12At that time Merodach-baladan the son of Baladan, king of Babylon, sent envoys with letters and a present to Hezekiah, for he heard that Hezekiah had been sick.

13And Hezekiah welcomed them, and he showed them all his treasure house, the silver, the gold, the spices, the precious oil, his armory, all that was found in his storehouses. There was nothing in his house or in all his realm that Hezekiah did not show them.

14Then Isaiah the prophet came to King Hezekiah, and said to him, “What did these men say? And from where did they come to you?” And Hezekiah said, “They have come from a far country, from Babylon.”

15He said, “What have they seen in your house?” And Hezekiah answered, “They have seen all that is in my house; there is nothing in my storehouses that I did not show them.”

16Then Isaiah said to Hezekiah, “Hear the word of the LORD:

17Behold, the days are coming, when all that is in your house, and that which your fathers have stored up till this day, shall be carried to Babylon. Nothing shall be left, says the LORD.

18And some of your own sons, who will come from you, whom you will father, shall be taken away, and they shall be eunuchs in the palace of the king of Babylon.”

19Then Hezekiah said to Isaiah, “The word of the LORD that you have spoken is good.” For he thought, “Why not, if there will be peace and security in my days?”

20The rest of the deeds of Hezekiah and all his might and how he made the pool and the conduit and brought water into the city, are they not written in the Book of the Chronicles of the Kings of Judah?

21And Hezekiah slept with his fathers, and Manasseh his son reigned in his place.

Summary

Hezekiah falls deathly ill and God adds fifteen years to his life, confirmed by the shadow retreating ten steps. He then foolishly shows Babylon's envoys all his treasures, and Isaiah prophesies the Babylonian exile.

Key Verses

  • v.3 — "I beseech you, O LORD, remember now how I have walked before you in truth and with a perfect heart, and have done that which is good in your sight. And Hezekiah wept sore." (ESV) — Hezekiah appeals not to personal merit as grounds for salvation, but to covenant faithfulness as evidence of a genuine relationship with God. His "perfect heart" (ESV: "whole heart") means undivided loyalty, not sinless perfection. The weeping reveals both his humanity and the depth of his communion with God — he brings his whole self, including grief, into prayer.
  • v.5 — "Turn again, and tell Hezekiah the captain of my people, Thus says the LORD, the God of David your father, I have heard your prayer, I have seen your tears: behold, I will heal you." (ESV) — God calls Hezekiah "captain of my people" — a military title emphasizing his role as leader under God's authority. "The God of David your father" invokes the covenant promises. God responds to both prayer and tears — He is not unmoved by genuine emotion. The healing is certain: "I will heal you."
  • v.11 — "And Isaiah the prophet cried to the LORD: and he brought the shadow ten degrees backward, by which it had gone down in the dial of Ahaz." (ESV) — A miracle that defies natural law. The "dial of Ahaz" (ESV: "steps of Ahaz") was likely a stairway sundial. God who controls time and creation uses Ahaz's own construction — that idolatrous king's legacy — to confirm His power. What Ahaz built for timekeeping, God uses for sign-giving.
  • v.13 — "And Hezekiah hearkened to them, and showed them all the house of his precious things... there was nothing in his house, nor in all his dominion, that Hezekiah showed them not." (ESV) — The repetition of "all" and "nothing" emphasizes the totality of Hezekiah's disclosure. Pride and political ambition replace the humility that characterized his prayer life. The man who laid Sennacherib's letter before God now lays his treasures before Babylon. What you display to the world's powers becomes what they eventually take from you.
  • v.17 — "Behold, the days come, that all that is in your house, and that which your fathers have laid up in store to this day, shall be carried into Babylon: nothing shall be left, says the LORD." (ESV) — The first explicit prophecy of the Babylonian exile in 2 Kings. "Nothing shall be left" — the totality of loss matches the totality of display. What Hezekiah showed, Babylon will take. Generations of accumulated treasure, destroyed in a single judgment. This prophecy was fulfilled in 586 BC, roughly a century after it was spoken.

Cross-References

  • v.1 → Isaiah 38:1-8 — The parallel account with Hezekiah's psalm of thanksgiving (Isaiah 38:9-20), which provides his personal reflection on facing death and receiving life. The psalm reveals the depth of his spiritual crisis.
  • v.3 → Psalm 26:1-3 — "Vindicate me, O LORD, for I have walked in my integrity." Like Hezekiah, David appeals to faithful conduct not as merit but as evidence of genuine faith. Both men approach God on the basis of relationship, not performance.
  • v.5 → 2 Samuel 7:12-16 — The Davidic covenant. God heals Hezekiah "for my own sake, and for my servant David's sake" (v.6). The covenant promises sustain even individual kings — God's faithfulness to David extends to his descendants.
  • v.7 → James 5:14-16 — "Is any sick among you? let him call for the elders." God often heals through means (figs, oil, medicine) combined with prayer. The natural remedy does not diminish the supernatural healing — God works through both.
  • v.11 → Joshua 10:12-14 — Joshua's long day, when the sun stood still. Both miracles demonstrate God's sovereignty over celestial bodies. What He did for Joshua in battle, He does for Hezekiah in sickness — nature serves God's redemptive purposes.
  • v.12 → Isaiah 39:1-8 — The parallel account of the Babylonian embassy. Isaiah 39 provides identical details, confirming the historical reliability of both accounts and the centrality of this event to Isaiah's prophetic message.
  • v.17 → 2 Kings 24:13 — The fulfillment: "He carried out from there all the treasures of the house of the LORD... as the LORD had said." Isaiah's prophecy finds exact fulfillment when Nebuchadnezzar strips the Temple in 597 BC.
  • v.19 → Philippians 2:4 — "Look not every man on his own things, but every man also on the things of others." Hezekiah's failure was caring only about peace in his own generation. Genuine faith considers the inheritance we leave to those who come after us.

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): enduringword.com Guzik notes that Hezekiah's prayer is remarkable for its bold honesty — he does not passively accept death but wrestles with God for life. However, Guzik also observes that the additional fifteen years produced Manasseh (born during this period), the worst king in Judah's history. God gave Hezekiah what he asked for, but the unintended consequences were devastating.
  • Charles Spurgeon: "Hezekiah's tears moved the heart of God, and fifteen years were added like a postscript to a life that was nearly finished. But mark this: in those fifteen years he fathered Manasseh, who undid all his father's good. And he showed his treasures to Babylon, who would one day take them all. Sometimes God gives us what we ask, and we find the gift more costly than the denial. Let us pray not merely for longer life, but for wisdom to use whatever days we are given. The brevity of faithful service outweighs the length of careless years."

Translations

  • v.1 → KJV: "sick unto death" / ESV: "sick and was at the point of death" — Both convey mortal illness. The Hebrew phrase indicates not merely severe sickness but a condition with no natural hope of recovery. The ESV's expanded phrasing makes the immediacy clearer for modern readers.
  • v.5 → KJV: "the captain of my people" / ESV: "the leader of my people" — Hebrew "nagid" means ruler, prince, or commander. KJV's "captain" emphasizes military leadership; ESV's "leader" is more general. The term is significant — God still claims Hezekiah as His appointed ruler despite the death sentence.
  • v.9 → KJV: "shall the shadow go forward ten degrees, or go back ten degrees?" / ESV: "shall the shadow go forward ten steps, or go back ten steps?" — Hebrew "ma'aloth" means "steps" or "ascents." KJV interprets it as degrees on a dial; ESV retains the literal "steps" (likely a stairway used as a sundial). The distinction matters: "steps of Ahaz" suggests a physical staircase whose shadow served as a timepiece.
  • v.12 → KJV: "Berodach-baladan" / ESV: "Merodach-baladan" — A textual variant in the first letter. The correct Babylonian name is Marduk-apla-iddina II ("Merodach" = Marduk). The KJV follows a Hebrew manuscript variant with "B" instead of "M." Isaiah 39:1 has "Merodach" in both translations.
  • v.19 → KJV: "Is it not good, if peace and truth be in my days?" / ESV: "Why not, if there will be peace and security in my days?" — The ESV renders Hezekiah's response more candidly. KJV's "peace and truth" (Hebrew: shalom ve'emeth) could imply stability and faithfulness; ESV's "peace and security" suggests Hezekiah's relief that judgment falls after his lifetime — a more critical reading of his attitude.

Reflection

  • 1. God hears desperate prayer (vv.2-5). Hezekiah turned his face to the wall — shutting out the world to face God alone — and wept bitterly. Before Isaiah left the courtyard, God had already reversed the sentence. There is no prayer too desperate, no tears too many, no situation too final for God to hear and respond. Do not mistake God's initial word as His final word when you have not yet brought your whole heart to Him in prayer.
  • 2. Pride follows deliverance if we are not careful (vv.12-13). Hezekiah survived death, saw Assyria defeated, and then — in his prosperity — showed off to Babylon. The most dangerous moment spiritually is often just after God's greatest victory in your life. Success, healing, deliverance can breed a pride that undoes the very faith that brought the blessing. Stay humble after the miracle.
  • 3. What you display to the world, the world will eventually take (v.13,17). Hezekiah showed everything; Babylon took everything. This is a spiritual principle: whatever you flaunt before worldly powers — your resources, your strategies, your vulnerabilities — becomes what they exploit. Wisdom does not parade its treasures before those who would steal them. Guard what God has entrusted to you.
  • 4. Short-term peace is not faithfulness (v.19). Hezekiah's relief that judgment wouldn't come in his lifetime reveals a failure of generational responsibility. Faithfulness cares about what we leave behind, not just what we experience now. Ask yourself: Am I making decisions that bless future generations, or am I content with peace in my own days while sowing seeds of destruction for my children?
  • 5. God's sovereignty includes natural means (v.7). The fig poultice did not diminish the miracle — God chose to heal through both His word and a natural remedy. Faith does not reject medicine, wisdom, or practical means. It trusts that God works through all things — supernatural and natural — to accomplish His purposes. Use the means God provides while trusting the God who provides them.
