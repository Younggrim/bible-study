Prayers in the Bible

Moses' Intercession (Exodus 32:11-14)

The Prayer

When Israel worshiped the golden calf while Moses was on Mount Sinai receiving the law, God's righteous anger burned against them. He told Moses He would destroy the nation and start over with Moses' descendants. What happened next is one of the most remarkable acts of intercession in all of Scripture — Moses stood between God and the people and pleaded for mercy.

Moses' intercession was not sentimental — it was theological. He appealed to God's reputation among the nations, His covenant promises to Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob, and His own character as a God of mercy. Moses did not argue that the people deserved mercy — they clearly did not — but that God's own glory and faithfulness were at stake.

The result is stunning: "The LORD relented." This does not mean Moses changed God's eternal plan, but that prayer is a means God uses to accomplish His purposes. Moses' intercession was itself part of God's sovereign plan — demonstrating both His justice (He does not ignore sin) and His mercy (He responds to intercession with compassion).

Key Scriptures

  • Exodus 32:11-14 — "Moses sought the favor of the LORD his God... Then the LORD relented and did not bring the disaster he had threatened."
  • Numbers 14:13-19 — "In accordance with your great love, forgive the sin of these people, just as you have pardoned them from Egypt until now."
  • Deuteronomy 9:25-29 — "I lay prostrate before the LORD those forty days and forty nights because the LORD had said he would destroy you."
  • Psalm 106:23 — "He said he would destroy them — had not Moses, his chosen one, stood in the breach before him."
  • 1 Timothy 2:1 — "I urge that petitions, prayers, intercession and thanksgiving be made for all people."

What We Learn About Prayer

Moses teaches us the power and responsibility of intercession — standing in the gap for those who cannot or will not pray for themselves. There are times when God looks for someone to stand in the breach, and the intercessor's prayer becomes the channel through which mercy flows. This is a sacred calling available to every believer.

We also learn to base our intercession on God's character and promises rather than human merit. Like Moses, we appeal not to the worthiness of those we pray for but to the faithfulness, compassion, and covenant love of God. This approach gives us confidence in prayer even when those we intercede for seem undeserving — because intercession is always ultimately about God's glory.

Reflection Questions

  • Who in your life needs you to stand in the gap for them in intercessory prayer?
  • How does Moses' example encourage you to persist in prayer for those who seem beyond hope?
  • What attributes of God's character can you appeal to when interceding for others?
  • How does understanding intercession as part of God's sovereign plan affect your prayer life?
