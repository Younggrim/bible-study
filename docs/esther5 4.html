Esther — Chapter 5

1On the third day Esther put on her royal robes and stood in the inner court of the king’s palace, in front of the king’s quarters, while the king was sitting on his royal throne inside the throne room opposite the entrance to the palace.

2And when the king saw Queen Esther standing in the court, she won favor in his sight, and he held out to Esther the golden scepter that was in his hand. Then Esther approached and touched the tip of the scepter.

3And the king said to her, “What is it, Queen Esther? What is your request? It shall be given you, even to the half of my kingdom.”

4And Esther said, “If it please the king, let the king and Haman come today to a feast that I have prepared for the king.”

5Then the king said, “Bring Haman quickly, so that we may do as Esther has asked.” So the king and Haman came to the feast that Esther had prepared.

6And as they were drinking wine after the feast, the king said to Esther, “What is your wish? It shall be granted you. And what is your request? Even to the half of my kingdom, it shall be fulfilled.”

7Then Esther answered, “My wish and my request is:

8If I have found favor in the sight of the king, and if it please the king to grant my wish and fulfill my request, let the king and Haman come to the feast that I will prepare for them, and tomorrow I will do as the king has said.”

9And Haman went out that day joyful and glad of heart. But when Haman saw Mordecai in the king’s gate, that he neither rose nor trembled before him, he was filled with wrath against Mordecai.

10Nevertheless, Haman restrained himself and went home, and he sent and brought his friends and his wife Zeresh.

11And Haman recounted to them the splendor of his riches, the number of his sons, all the promotions with which the king had honored him, and how he had advanced him above the officials and the servants of the king.

12Then Haman said, “Even Queen Esther let no one but me come with the king to the feast she prepared. And tomorrow also I am invited by her together with the king.

13Yet all this is worth nothing to me, so long as I see Mordecai the Jew sitting at the king’s gate.”

14Then his wife Zeresh and all his friends said to him, “Let a gallows fifty cubits high be made, and in the morning tell the king to have Mordecai hanged upon it. Then go joyfully with the king to the feast.” This idea pleased Haman, and he had the gallows made.

Summary

Esther risks her life by approaching the king unsummoned and is received with favor; she invites the king and Haman to a banquet, then a second. Haman leaves elated but furious at Mordecai, and his wife suggests building a gallows to hang him.

Key Verses

v.2
"And when the king saw Queen Esther standing in the court, she won favor in his sight, and he held out to Esther the golden scepter that was in his hand." (ESV) — The golden scepter extended means life granted. Esther's "if I perish" became "I have found favor." The moment of greatest risk became the moment of divine provision — God turned the king's heart toward His servant at the critical moment.
v.3
"And the king said to her, 'What is it, Queen Esther? What is your request? It shall be given you, even to the half of my kingdom.'" (ESV) — The king's extravagant offer ("half of the kingdom") is formulaic royal generosity — expressing willingness to grant any reasonable request. Yet the irony is profound: what Esther will actually request is her own life and her people's survival — far less than half a kingdom, yet infinitely more valuable.
v.9
"And Haman went out that day joyful and glad of heart. But when Haman saw Mordecai in the king's gate, that he neither rose nor trembled before him, he was filled with wrath against Mordecai." (ESV) — The chapter's psychological pivot. Haman's joy instantly collapses at the sight of one man's defiance. Pride cannot coexist with any opposition — it demands absolute acknowledgment.
v.13
"Yet all this is worth nothing to me, so long as I see Mordecai the Jew sitting at the king's gate." (ESV) — The definitive statement of pride's misery. Haman has wealth, children, royal favor, and exclusive invitation to the queen's banquet — yet one man's refusal to bow nullifies it all. This is the bitter fruit of pride: it can never have enough because it demands everything.
v.14
"Let a gallows fifty cubits high be made, and in the morning tell the king to have Mordecai hanged upon it. Then go joyfully with the king to the feast." (ESV) — The gallows that will become Haman's own execution device is constructed on his family's counsel. "Fifty cubits" (approximately 75 feet) was grotesquely excessive — designed for public spectacle, visible throughout the city. Pride desires not just victory but humiliation of the enemy.

Cross-References

  • v.2 — Proverbs 21:1 — "The king's heart is a stream of water in the hand of the LORD; he turns it wherever he will." (ESV) The king's favorable reception of Esther was not chance but divine influence. The same God who moves kings' hearts turned Ahasuerus toward grace at the precise moment Esther risked death.
  • v.3 — 1 Kings 3:5 — "Ask what I shall give you." (ESV) Both Solomon and Esther received blank-check offers from authority figures. Solomon asked for wisdom; Esther would ask for her people's lives. The test of character lies in what you request when given unlimited options.
  • v.9 — Proverbs 27:4 — "Wrath is cruel, anger is overwhelming, but who can stand before jealousy?" (ESV) Haman's rage at Mordecai is rooted in wounded pride and envy — he cannot bear that one person refuses him the deference he craves.
  • v.13 — Ecclesiastes 5:10 — "He who loves money will not be satisfied with money." (ESV) Haman's insatiable dissatisfaction despite enormous privilege mirrors the Preacher's wisdom: the prideful heart can never have enough because it always fixates on what it lacks.
  • v.14 — Proverbs 26:27 — "Whoever digs a pit will fall into it." (ESV) The gallows Haman built for Mordecai became his own execution device (7:10). Scripture consistently teaches that traps set for the righteous ensnare the wicked themselves.
  • v.14 — Psalm 7:15 — "He makes a pit, digging it out, and falls into the hole that he has made." (ESV) The principle of reversal — evil's instruments turned against the evildoer — is a cornerstone of biblical justice and the dominant theme of Esther.

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/esther-5/ Guzik notes Esther's remarkable strategic patience — she did not blurt her request immediately but created conditions for maximum impact. Her delay also unwittingly created the space for God's providential intervention in chapter 6. He emphasizes that Haman's misery (v.13) despite extraordinary privilege reveals the self- destructive nature of pride: it converts blessing into torment by fixating on the one thing it cannot control.
  • Charles Spurgeon: "'Yet all this availeth me nothing.' Here sits a man with wealth beyond counting, favor above all peers, children in abundance, and the queen's exclusive invitation — yet one Jew's refusal to bow empties it all of satisfaction. Such is pride's bitter harvest. The proud man's cup of joy has a hole in its bottom — it can never be filled because it always leaks. One slight, one refusal, one competitor not conquered, and all his millions become worthless. But the humble man, who seeks God's approval alone, is satisfied with the least crumb from the heavenly table. Learn, O soul: the secret of contentment is not more honor from men but less dependence on it."

Translations

  • v.1 — KJV: "Esther put on her royal apparel" / ESV: "Esther put on her royal robes" — Hebrew "vattilbash Esther malkhut" (Esther clothed herself [with] royalty). The Hebrew is striking: she literally "put on royalty" — not just clothes but authority. She entered the king's presence as queen, not as supplicant. Both translations capture this, with ESV's "robes" and KJV's "apparel" being equivalent.
  • v.6 — KJV: "at the banquet of wine" / ESV: "as they were drinking wine after the feast" — Hebrew "b'mishteh ha-yayin" (at the feast/drinking of wine). Persian banquets involved separate courses of food and wine. The "banquet of wine" was the drinking portion following the meal — a relaxed social setting where the king might be most receptive to requests.
  • v.9 — KJV: "he stood not up, nor moved for him" / ESV: "he neither rose nor trembled before him" — Hebrew "lo-qam v'lo za" (not rose and not trembled/moved). ESV's "trembled" captures the Hebrew "za" more precisely — Mordecai showed no physical reaction at all, neither standing nor even flinching. His composure was as defiant as active refusal.
  • v.14 — KJV: "a gallows be made of fifty cubits high" / ESV: "a gallows fifty cubits high be made" — Hebrew "etz gavoha chamishim ammah" (a tree/pole high fifty cubits). "Etz" means tree or wooden structure. The execution method was likely impalement on a tall stake (Persian practice) rather than hanging by rope. Fifty cubits (75 feet) was designed for maximum public visibility.
  • v.14 — KJV: "go thou in merrily with the king" / ESV: "go joyfully with the king" — Hebrew "vo sameyach im ha-melekh" (enter joyful with the king). Both translations convey Zeresh's counsel: dispatch Mordecai in the morning, then enjoy the queen's banquet with a light heart. The word "sameyach" (joyful) forms an ironic contrast with the "joyful" (v.9) that was instantly destroyed by seeing Mordecai.

Reflection

  • 1. Patient wisdom may accomplish more than immediate action (vv.4, 7-8). Esther delayed her request through two banquets — and that delay created space for God to orchestrate the providential events of chapter 6. Not every crisis requires instant action. Sometimes the wisest response is strategic patience — creating conditions for God to work before you move.
  • 2. The extended scepter represents grace (v.2). Esther deserved death by law but received favor by grace. This is the gospel pattern: we approach a holy King uninvited, deserving judgment, and He extends His scepter of grace. Come boldly to God's throne — not because you deserve welcome but because He chooses to extend it.
  • 3. Pride poisons abundance (v.13). Haman had everything — yet "all this availeth me nothing." A heart consumed with ego cannot enjoy genuine blessings because it obsesses over the one thing it cannot control. If you find yourself unable to enjoy real blessings because of one unresolved grievance or one person's disapproval, check your heart for pride's poison.
  • 4. Those who build gallows for others often hang on them themselves (v.14). The principle of reaping what you sow is active in this narrative. The trap you set for others tends to catch you. The weapon you forge against the innocent tends to wound its maker. Instead of plotting harm, leave justice to God.
  • 5. Courage and wisdom work together (vv.1-8). Esther was courageous (approaching uninvited) and wise (timing her revelation strategically). Courage without wisdom can be reckless; wisdom without courage can be cowardly. The most effective servants of God combine both — acting boldly but thoughtfully, with Spirit-directed timing.
