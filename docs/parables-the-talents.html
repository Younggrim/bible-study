Parables of Jesus

The Talents

The Parable

A man going on a journey called his servants and entrusted to them his property. To one he gave five talents, to another two, and to another one — each according to his ability. A talent was not a coin but a weight of precious metal worth roughly twenty years of wages for a common laborer. Even the servant with one talent received an enormous sum. The master then departed, leaving no detailed instructions — only trust.

The servant with five talents traded and doubled his money. Likewise the one with two talents gained two more. But the servant with one talent dug a hole and buried his master's money in the ground. When the master returned, he commended the first two identically: "Well done, good and faithful servant. You have been faithful over a little; I will set you over much. Enter into the joy of your master." The reward was not proportional to the amount but to the faithfulness.

The third servant offered excuses rooted in fear: "Master, I knew you to be a hard man... so I was afraid, and I went and hid your talent." The master called him wicked and lazy — not for failing spectacularly, but for doing nothing at all. His talent was taken and given to the one with ten. The parable ends with a sobering principle: "To everyone who has, more will be given... but from the one who has not, even what he has will be taken away."

Key Scriptures

  • Matthew 25:14-30 — The full parable: three servants, varying gifts, one standard — faithfulness with what you've been given.
  • Luke 19:12-27 — The Parable of the Minas — a parallel account where ten servants each receive one mina, emphasizing equal opportunity.
  • 1 Corinthians 4:2 — "It is required of stewards that they be found faithful." God measures faithfulness, not flashiness.
  • 1 Peter 4:10 — "As each has received a gift, use it to serve one another, as good stewards of God's varied grace."
  • Romans 12:6-8 — "Having gifts that differ according to the grace given to us, let us use them" — prophecy, service, teaching, generosity.

What Jesus Was Teaching

Jesus was teaching that everything we have — time, abilities, resources, influence, the gospel itself — belongs to God and is entrusted to us for Kingdom purposes. We are not owners but stewards. The master didn't demand identical results from each servant; he expected faithful engagement proportional to what was given. The five-talent servant and the two-talent servant received the same commendation because both were equally faithful with their differing capacities. God never compares us to others — only to our own calling.

The buried talent reveals the most dangerous spiritual posture: not rebellion, but passivity. The third servant's problem was a distorted view of God — he saw his master as harsh and demanding rather than generous and trustworthy. Fear of failure led to inaction, and inaction was judged more severely than risky failure would have been. Jesus is warning His followers: the greatest danger is not making mistakes in service but refusing to serve at all. We will give an account not only for what we did wrong but for what we failed to do with what God gave us.

Reflection Questions

  • What "talents" has God entrusted to you — gifts, resources, relationships, opportunities? Are you actively investing them or burying them?
  • Do you struggle with comparing your "two talents" to someone else's "five"? How does this parable free you from that comparison?
  • Is there an area where fear of failure or a distorted view of God is causing you to play it safe rather than step out in faith?
  • What would it look like to hear "Well done, good and faithful servant" at the end of your life? What needs to change today?
