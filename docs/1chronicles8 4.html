1 Chronicles — Chapter 8

1Benjamin fathered Bela his firstborn, Ashbel the second, Aharah the third,

2Nohah the fourth, and Rapha the fifth.

3And Bela had sons: Addar, Gera, Abihud,

4Abishua, Naaman, Ahoah,

5Gera, Shephuphan, and Huram.

6These are the sons of Ehud (they were heads of fathers’ houses of the inhabitants of Geba, and they were carried into exile to Manahath):

7Naaman, Ahijah, and Gera, that is, Heglam, who fathered Uzza and Ahihud.

8And Shaharaim fathered sons in the country of Moab after he had sent away Hushim and Baara his wives.

9He fathered sons by Hodesh his wife: Jobab, Zibia, Mesha, Malcam,

10Jeuz, Sachia, and Mirmah. These were his sons, heads of fathers’ houses.

11He also fathered sons by Hushim: Abitub and Elpaal.

12The sons of Elpaal: Eber, Misham, and Shemed, who built Ono and Lod with its towns,

13and Beriah and Shema (they were heads of fathers’ houses of the inhabitants of Aijalon, who caused the inhabitants of Gath to flee);

14and Ahio, Shashak, and Jeremoth.

15Zebadiah, Arad, Eder,

16Michael, Ishpah, and Joha were sons of Beriah.

17Zebadiah, Meshullam, Hizki, Heber,

18Ishmerai, Izliah, and Jobab were the sons of Elpaal.

19Jakim, Zichri, Zabdi,

20Elienai, Zillethai, Eliel,

21Adaiah, Beraiah, and Shimrath were the sons of Shimei.

22Ishpan, Eber, Eliel,

23Abdon, Zichri, Hanan,

24Hananiah, Elam, Anthothijah,

25Iphdeiah, and Penuel were the sons of Shashak.

26Shamsherai, Shehariah, Athaliah,

27Jaareshiah, Elijah, and Zichri were the sons of Jeroham.

28These were the heads of fathers’ houses, according to their generations, chief men. These lived in Jerusalem.

29Jeiel the father of Gibeon lived in Gibeon, and the name of his wife was Maacah.

30His firstborn son: Abdon, then Zur, Kish, Baal, Nadab,

31Gedor, Ahio, Zecher,

32and Mikloth (he fathered Shimeah). Now these also lived opposite their kinsmen in Jerusalem, with their kinsmen.

33Ner was the father of Kish, Kish of Saul, Saul of Jonathan, Malchi-shua, Abinadab and Eshbaal;

34and the son of Jonathan was Merib-baal; and Merib-baal was the father of Micah.

35The sons of Micah: Pithon, Melech, Tarea, and Ahaz.

36Ahaz fathered Jehoaddah, and Jehoaddah fathered Alemeth, Azmaveth, and Zimri. Zimri fathered Moza.

37Moza fathered Binea; Raphah was his son, Eleasah his son, Azel his son.

38Azel had six sons, and these are their names: Azrikam, Bocheru, Ishmael, Sheariah, Obadiah, and Hanan. All these were the sons of Azel.

39The sons of Eshek his brother: Ulam his firstborn, Jeush the second, and Eliphelet the third.

40The sons of Ulam were men who were mighty warriors, bowmen, having many sons and grandsons, 150. All these were Benjaminites.

Summary

The genealogy of Benjamin is expanded in detail, listing the families who settled in Jerusalem and the surrounding areas. The chapter concludes with the lineage of King Saul, transitioning from the genealogical section to the narrative of Israel's monarchy.

Key Verses

v.12
"The sons of Elpaal: Eber, Misham, and Shemed, who built Ono and Lod with its towns" (ESV) — Benjamites built Ono and Lod — cities that appear in the post-exilic records (Ezra 2:33; Nehemiah 7:37). This connects the ancient genealogy to cities the returned exiles would recognize and inhabit.
v.13
"and Beriah and Shema (they were heads of fathers' houses of the inhabitants of Aijalon, who caused the inhabitants of Gath to flee)" (ESV) — Benjamite warriors who defeated the Philistines of Gath. This military achievement establishes Benjamin as a tribe of warriors — fitting for the tribe that produced Saul and his mighty son Jonathan.
v.28
"These were the heads of fathers' houses, according to their generations, chief men. These lived in Jerusalem." (ESV) — Benjamites in Jerusalem. This validates Benjamin's claim on the holy city alongside Judah and establishes the tribe's presence in the capital from ancient times.
v.33
"Ner was the father of Kish, Kish of Saul, Saul of Jonathan, Malchi-shua, Abinadab and Eshbaal." (ESV) — The royal line of Benjamin. Saul and his sons — including Jonathan, David's beloved friend, and Eshbaal (Ish- bosheth), who briefly rivaled David for the throne. The Chronicler records them without prejudice, preserving their place in Israel's history.
v.34
"and the son of Jonathan was Merib-baal; and Merib-baal was the father of Micah." (ESV) — Merib-baal is Mephibosheth — the lame son of Jonathan whom David showed covenant loyalty (2 Samuel 9). His line continues for several generations, showing that David's kindness bore lasting fruit.
v.40
"The sons of Ulam were men who were mighty warriors, bowmen, having many sons and grandsons, 150." (ESV) — The chapter ends with a note of military strength: 150 mighty archer-warriors from one family. Benjamin's reputation for archery and left-handed warriors (Judges 20:16) persisted across generations.

Cross-References

  • v.12 — Nehemiah 11:35 — Lod and Ono appear as post-exilic settlements. The Chronicler traces these cities to their Benjamite founders, establishing ancient claims for current residents.
  • v.13 — 1 Samuel 17:4 — Gath, the city of Goliath. That Benjamites drove away Gath's inhabitants shows military prowess that predated David's famous victory over the Philistine giant.
  • v.29-38 — 1 Chronicles 9:35-44 — This genealogy of Saul is repeated almost verbatim in chapter 9. The repetition frames the post-exilic resident list and prepares for chapter 10's narrative of Saul's death.
  • v.33 — 1 Samuel 9:1-2 — Saul's introduction: "There was not a man among the people of Israel more handsome than he." (ESV) The genealogy here gives his family context.
  • v.34 — 2 Samuel 4:4; 9:1-13 — Mephibosheth (Merib-baal), lamed at age five when his nurse dropped him fleeing the news of Saul and Jonathan's deaths. David later restored him to his table in covenant faithfulness.
  • v.40 — Judges 20:16 — "Among all these were 700 chosen men who were left-handed; every one could sling a stone at a hair and not miss." (ESV) Benjamin's martial tradition, especially in archery and slinging, was legendary from early times.

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/1-chronicles-8/ Guzik notes that Benjamin receives extended treatment because of the tribe's dual significance: the tribe of the first king (Saul) and the tribe that remained loyal to Judah after the division of the kingdom. He emphasizes that the Chronicler's use of "Baal" names (Esh-baal, Merib-baal) reflects historical accuracy rather than theological endorsement.
  • Charles Spurgeon: "Benjamin, the smallest tribe, yet dwelling with Judah in the holy city. Here is a principle of grace: God does not measure value by size. The little tribe that nearly perished in the civil war of Judges 19-21 is here given extensive record, honored with Jerusalem residence, and credited with mighty warriors. God delights in using the small, the nearly-destroyed, the remnant. If Benjamin can survive near-extinction and produce both a king and mighty archers, then no situation is too hopeless for God's restoring grace."

Translations

  • v.33 — KJV: "Esh-baal" / ESV: "Eshbaal" — The original name meaning "man of Baal" or "fire of Baal." In 2 Samuel, this is changed to "Ish-bosheth" (man of shame). The Chronicler preserves the original, either for accuracy or because the name was understood without pagan connotation by post- exilic readers.
  • v.34 — KJV: "Merib-baal" / ESV: "Merib-baal" — Original name meaning "contender with Baal" or "Baal contends." Changed to "Mephibosheth" (dispeller of shame) in 2 Samuel. Again, the Chronicler uses the historical name.
  • v.36 — KJV: "Jehoadah" / ESV: "Jehoaddah" — In 9:42 this same person is called "Jarah." Such variation in names across genealogical lists is common in ancient Near Eastern records and reflects different manuscript traditions or alternate name forms.
  • v.29 — KJV: does not name Jeiel / ESV: does not name Jeiel — Both translations leave the "father of Gibeon" unnamed here (cf. 9:35 ESV: "Jeiel"). The Hebrew text of 8:29 appears to have lost the name through scribal error; 9:35 preserves it.
  • v.40 — KJV: "mighty men of valour, archers" / ESV: "mighty warriors, bowmen" — Hebrew "gibbore chayil, dorekhe qeshet" (mighty warriors, benders of the bow). Both translations convey the dual qualification: general military excellence and specific skill in archery.

Reflection

  • 1. God restores what was nearly destroyed (vv.1-40). Benjamin nearly perished in Judges 19-21 — reduced to 600 men. Yet here the tribe fills an entire chapter with clans, warriors, and city-builders. God's ability to restore is not limited by how far something has fallen. What seems beyond recovery is not beyond His power.
  • 2. Original names may carry shame, but God redefines identity (vv.33-34). Esh-baal and Merib-baal bore names with "Baal" — yet they were Israelites, not pagans. The names reflected their culture, not their faith. God looks at the heart, not the label. Don't judge others by names, backgrounds, or external markers that may not reflect their true character.
  • 3. Covenant faithfulness bears generational fruit (v.34). David's kindness to Merib-baal/Mephibosheth produced descendants who lasted for generations. Your acts of faithfulness today — to friends, to promises, to covenants — may bless people for generations you'll never meet.
  • 4. Small tribes can produce great things (v.40). Benjamin was tiny but produced mighty warriors. Size is not destiny. A small church, a small family, a small influence can produce disproportionate impact when God's blessing rests upon it.
  • 5. History is God's preparation for the future (vv.28-33). The Chronicler records ancient Benjamites in Jerusalem to validate current Benjamites in Jerusalem. Your past — your family's history, your community's heritage — matters because it establishes your present identity and future calling.
