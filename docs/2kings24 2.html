2 Kings — Chapter 24

1In his days, Nebuchadnezzar king of Babylon came up, and Jehoiakim became his servant for three years. Then he turned and rebelled against him.

2And the LORD sent against him bands of the Chaldeans and bands of the Syrians and bands of the Moabites and bands of the Ammonites, and sent them against Judah to destroy it, according to the word of the LORD that he spoke by his servants the prophets.

3Surely this came upon Judah at the command of the LORD, to remove them out of his sight, for the sins of Manasseh, according to all that he had done,

4and also for the innocent blood that he had shed. For he filled Jerusalem with innocent blood, and the LORD would not pardon.

5Now the rest of the deeds of Jehoiakim and all that he did, are they not written in the Book of the Chronicles of the Kings of Judah?

6So Jehoiakim slept with his fathers, and Jehoiachin his son reigned in his place.

7And the king of Egypt did not come again out of his land, for the king of Babylon had taken all that belonged to the king of Egypt from the Brook of Egypt to the river Euphrates.

8Jehoiachin was eighteen years old when he became king, and he reigned three months in Jerusalem. His mother’s name was Nehushta the daughter of Elnathan of Jerusalem.

9And he did what was evil in the sight of the LORD, according to all that his father had done.

10At that time the servants of Nebuchadnezzar king of Babylon came up to Jerusalem, and the city was besieged.

11And Nebuchadnezzar king of Babylon came to the city while his servants were besieging it,

12and Jehoiachin the king of Judah gave himself up to the king of Babylon, himself and his mother and his servants and his officials and his palace officials. The king of Babylon took him prisoner in the eighth year of his reign

13and carried off all the treasures of the house of the LORD and the treasures of the king’s house, and cut in pieces all the vessels of gold in the temple of the LORD, which Solomon king of Israel had made, as the LORD had foretold.

14He carried away all Jerusalem and all the officials and all the mighty men of valor, 10,000 captives, and all the craftsmen and the smiths. None remained, except the poorest people of the land.

15And he carried away Jehoiachin to Babylon. The king’s mother, the king’s wives, his officials, and the chief men of the land he took into captivity from Jerusalem to Babylon.

16And the king of Babylon brought captive to Babylon all the men of valor, 7,000, and the craftsmen and the metal workers, 1,000, all of them strong and fit for war.

17And the king of Babylon made Mattaniah, Jehoiachin’s uncle, king in his place, and changed his name to Zedekiah.

18Zedekiah was twenty-one years old when he became king, and he reigned eleven years in Jerusalem. His mother’s name was Hamutal the daughter of Jeremiah of Libnah.

19And he did what was evil in the sight of the LORD, according to all that Jehoiakim had done.

20For because of the anger of the LORD it came to the point in Jerusalem and Judah that he cast them out from his presence. And Zedekiah rebelled against the king of Babylon.

Summary

Nebuchadnezzar besieges Jerusalem, deports King Jehoiachin, the royal family, and 10,000 leading citizens to Babylon. He strips the temple of its gold and installs Zedekiah as a puppet king—the beginning of the end.

Key Verses

  • v.2 — "And the LORD sent against him bands of the Chaldees, and bands of the Syrians, and bands of the Moabites, and bands of the children of Ammon, and sent them against Judah to destroy it, according to the word of the LORD, which he spake by his servants the prophets." (ESV) — The LORD sent them. Not Babylon's ambition, not geopolitical accident — God actively deployed multiple nations as instruments of His judgment. "According to the word" — nothing happens that was not foretold. The prophets warned; the people ignored; the word was fulfilled.
  • v.3 — "Surely at the commandment of the LORD came this upon Judah, to remove them out of his sight, for the sins of Manasseh, according to all that he did." (ESV) — "At the commandment of the LORD" — the theological interpretation of history. Secular historians see Babylonian imperialism; the biblical author sees divine sovereignty. "To remove them out of his sight" — language of divorce, of rejection, of casting away. "For the sins of Manasseh" — the proximate cause is named one final time. The sins of one generation bear fruit in another.
  • v.4 — "And also for the innocent blood that he shed: for he filled Jerusalem with innocent blood; which the LORD would not pardon." (ESV) — "The LORD would not pardon." These are some of the most solemn words in Scripture. Not "could not" but "would not." God's patience with Judah had reached its limit. The blood of the innocent cries out (Genesis 4:10), and there comes a point where mercy gives way to justice. This does not mean individual forgiveness is impossible — but corporate judgment, once decreed, will not be averted.
  • v.13 — "And he carried out from there all the treasures of the house of the LORD, and the treasures of the king's house, and cut in pieces all the vessels of gold which Solomon king of Israel had made in the temple of the LORD, as the LORD had said." (ESV) — "As the LORD had said" (cf. 2 Kings 20:17). The gold vessels Solomon crafted for God's worship are cut to pieces by pagans — the physical destruction mirrors the spiritual destruction that preceded it. What Hezekiah displayed, Nebuchadnezzar dismantled.
  • v.14 — "And he carried away all Jerusalem, and all the princes, and all the mighty men of valour, even ten thousand captives, and all the craftsmen and smiths: none remained, save the poorest sort of the people of the land." (ESV) — Babylon's deportation policy was strategic: remove every capable person — leaders, soldiers, artisans — leaving only those too poor and unskilled to resist. This creates permanent dependency. Among these 10,000 captives was Ezekiel (Ezekiel 1:1-3), who would prophesy from Babylon. God goes with His people into exile.

Cross-References

  • v.2 → Jeremiah 25:8-11 — Jeremiah explicitly names Nebuchadnezzar as God's "servant" and prophesies 70 years of Babylonian dominion. The same theology: God uses pagan empires as instruments of judgment against His own disobedient people.
  • v.3 → 2 Kings 21:10-15 — The original pronouncement of judgment for Manasseh's sins. Chapter 24 is the fulfillment of chapter 21's prophecy. The gap between pronouncement and fulfillment spans multiple reigns — God's patience is evident even in judgment.
  • v.4 → Genesis 4:10 — "The voice of your brother's blood cries to me from the ground." Innocent blood has a voice before God. Manasseh filled Jerusalem with it; God heard it; judgment came. The same principle that judged Cain judges nations.
  • v.12 → Ezekiel 17:12-13 — Ezekiel's parable of the eagles describes Nebuchadnezzar taking Jehoiachin to Babylon and installing Zedekiah. The prophet, himself among the deportees, interprets these events from within exile.
  • v.13 → 2 Kings 20:17 — Isaiah's prophecy to Hezekiah: "All that is in your house... shall be carried into Babylon." Now fulfilled with devastating precision. The same treasures Hezekiah showed to Merodach-baladan's envoys are seized by Merodach-baladan's successors.
  • v.14 → Ezekiel 1:1-3 — Ezekiel was among those deported in this 597 BC captivity. "By the river Chebar" in Babylon, God called him to prophetic ministry. God's word does not cease in exile — it follows His people wherever they go.
  • v.17 → Jeremiah 52:1-3 — The parallel account of Zedekiah's installation and eventual rebellion. Jeremiah provides additional detail about the political dynamics and repeatedly warned Zedekiah not to rebel — warnings that were ignored.
  • v.20 → Ezekiel 17:15-20 — God condemns Zedekiah's rebellion against Babylon as breaking a covenant oath. Though Babylon was the oppressor, Zedekiah had sworn loyalty — and breaking that oath was sin before God. Even oaths to pagans must be honored.

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): enduringword.com Guzik emphasizes the phrase "the LORD would not pardon" as one of the most sobering in Scripture. He notes that this does not contradict God's forgiving nature but demonstrates that corporate sin has corporate consequences that persist even when individuals repent. He also highlights how Babylon's deportation strategy systematically dismantled Judah's capacity for independence — removing precisely those people (leaders, warriors, craftsmen) needed to maintain a functioning state.
  • Charles Spurgeon: "Here we read the most dreadful words: 'which the LORD would not pardon.' Not that He cannot pardon — His mercy endures forever. But that He would not — because there comes a time when justice must speak, when the cry of innocent blood must be answered, when patience exhausted must yield to righteousness offended. Let no man presume upon God's long-suffering as though it were weakness. The same God who waited through Manasseh's fifty-five years of abomination is the God who said 'I will not pardon.' Mercy has a door — and the hand that holds it open can also shut it. Turn while the door stands open; tomorrow is promised to no one."

Translations

  • v.1 → KJV: "Jehoiakim became his servant three years: then he turned and rebelled against him" / ESV: "Jehoiakim became his servant for three years. Then he turned and rebelled against him" — Both are identical in meaning. Jehoiakim's rebellion (c. 601 BC) likely occurred after Nebuchadnezzar's failed Egyptian campaign. He misjudged Babylon as weakened — a fatal miscalculation.
  • v.6 → KJV: "So Jehoiakim slept with his fathers" / ESV: "So Jehoiakim slept with his fathers" — The standard death formula, but notably without the usual "was buried in the city of David." Jeremiah 22:18-19 prophesied Jehoiakim would receive "the burial of a donkey" — dragged outside Jerusalem's gates. The absence of burial details in Kings may silently confirm Jeremiah's prophecy.
  • v.12 → KJV: "the king of Babylon took him in the eighth year of his reign" / ESV: "The king of Babylon took him prisoner in the eighth year of his reign" — Ambiguity exists: "his reign" could refer to Nebuchadnezzar's eighth year (597 BC) or to the eighth year since Jehoiachin became king. Context favors Nebuchadnezzar's eighth year — Jehoiachin only reigned three months. ESV adds "prisoner" for clarity.
  • v.14 → KJV: "ten thousand captives" / ESV: "10,000 captives" — The number includes the 7,000 warriors and 1,000 craftsmen/ smiths mentioned in v.16, plus the royal household and officials. This was the cream of Judah's society — a deliberate decapitation of the nation's leadership and productive capacity.
  • v.17 → KJV: "Mattaniah his father's brother" / ESV: "Mattaniah, Jehoiachin's uncle" — Both translate the same Hebrew. KJV's "his father's brother" is more literal; ESV's "uncle" clarifies the relationship. Nebuchadnezzar chose a puppet from the royal family — old enough to reign (21) but weak enough to control. Changing his name to Zedekiah ("the LORD is my righteousness") was ironic given his subsequent evil.

Reflection

  • 1. God uses unexpected instruments for judgment (v.2). Chaldeans, Syrians, Moabites, Ammonites — nations Judah despised — became God's agents against them. God is not limited to "religious" means. He uses secular powers, natural disasters, economic collapse, and even enemy nations to accomplish His purposes. When hardship comes through unexpected channels, ask whether God is speaking through the very thing you would not have chosen to hear Him in.
  • 2. There are sins God will not pardon corporately (v.4). Individual forgiveness is always available through repentance (1 John 1:9). But corporate, national, institutional sin accumulates until consequences become unavoidable. Churches, nations, and institutions that persist in systemic injustice — particularly the shedding of innocent blood — will face reckoning. Do not assume that because God has been patient, He will be patient forever. Corporate repentance is as necessary as individual repentance.
  • 3. What you show off, you eventually lose (v.13; cf. 20:13-17). Hezekiah's display became Babylon's plunder. This principle operates in personal life too: what you boast about often becomes what is taken from you. Prosperity that breeds pride rather than gratitude becomes a target rather than a blessing. Hold your blessings with open hands, acknowledging them as God's gifts rather than displaying them as personal trophies.
  • 4. God's people go into exile but God goes with them (v.14-16). Among the 10,000 captives was Ezekiel, who received the most dramatic visions of God's glory — in Babylon, not Jerusalem. God is not confined to the Temple. When circumstances take you to places you never chose — loss, displacement, unwanted change — God is already there. The river of Chebar becomes the location of revelation. Exile can become the place of encounter.
  • 5. Weak leaders who rebel without strength bring ruin (v.20). Zedekiah was installed as a puppet but tried to act as a sovereign. He rebelled against Babylon without the military, economic, or spiritual resources to sustain independence. The result was catastrophe. Wisdom knows the difference between courageous resistance and foolish defiance. Not every rebellion is righteous; some are merely reckless. Count the cost before you fight — and make sure you are fighting on God's side, not merely your own pride.
