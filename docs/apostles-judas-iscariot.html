The 12 Apostles

Judas Iscariot

Who Was Judas Iscariot

Judas Iscariot is the most infamous of the twelve apostles — the man who betrayed Jesus Christ for thirty pieces of silver. His surname "Iscariot" likely refers to his hometown of Kerioth in southern Judea, making him the only apostle who was not from Galilee. Judas served as the group's treasurer, carrying the money bag that supported Jesus and the twelve during their ministry. Yet John's Gospel reveals that Judas was a thief who helped himself to the funds entrusted to his care (John 12:6).

The tragedy of Judas is that he had every opportunity to know and love Jesus. He walked with Christ for three years, heard every teaching, witnessed every miracle, and was trusted with responsibility among the twelve. He was even sent out with the others to preach and heal. Yet beneath the surface of outward discipleship, Judas harbored a heart that was never truly surrendered to Christ. His greed grew in secret, unchecked and unconfessed, until it consumed him entirely. When Satan entered him at the Last Supper, it was the culmination of a long spiritual decline, not a sudden fall.

After betraying Jesus with a kiss in Gethsemane, Judas was seized with remorse. He returned the thirty silver coins to the chief priests, confessing "I have sinned by betraying innocent blood." But his remorse was not repentance — it was despair. Unlike Peter, who wept bitterly and returned to Christ, Judas turned inward and hanged himself. The apostles later chose Matthias to replace him, restoring the number to twelve. Judas stands as a sobering warning that proximity to Jesus is not the same as surrender to Jesus.

Key Scriptures

  • Matthew 26:14-16 — Judas goes to the chief priests and agrees to betray Jesus for thirty pieces of silver.
  • Matthew 27:3-5 — Judas is seized with remorse, returns the silver, and hangs himself in despair rather than seeking forgiveness.
  • John 12:4-6 — Judas objects to the anointing of Jesus with expensive perfume — not from concern for the poor, but because he was a thief.
  • John 6:70-71 — Jesus reveals that He chose the twelve knowing one of them was a devil, speaking of Judas who would betray Him.
  • Acts 1:15-26 — Peter explains Judas' fate to the gathered believers, and Matthias is chosen by lot to take his place among the twelve.

Lessons from Judas Iscariot

Judas is a warning that religious activity without heart transformation leads to destruction. He preached, he healed (when sent out with the twelve), he appeared devoted — yet his heart was never surrendered to Christ. He followed Jesus for what he could get, not for who Jesus was. His story warns us against the danger of playing the part of a disciple while harboring secret sin. The most dangerous spiritual condition is not open rebellion — it is hidden corruption beneath a veneer of faithfulness. Judas challenges every believer to examine whether their faith is genuine or merely performed.

The contrast between Judas and Peter is instructive. Both failed catastrophically — one betrayed, the other denied. But Peter turned back to Christ in brokenness, while Judas turned inward in despair. The difference was not the severity of their sin but the direction of their response. Remorse says "I feel terrible about what I did." Repentance says "I return to Christ and receive His mercy." Judas also warns us about the cumulative power of unchecked sin. His betrayal did not come from nowhere — it grew from small acts of dishonesty, greed nurtured in secret, and a refusal to bring his struggles into the light. Sin left in the dark always grows.

Reflection Questions

  • Are there areas of your life where you are maintaining an outward appearance of faithfulness while harboring secret sin? What would it look like to bring those into the light?
  • Judas followed Jesus for personal gain rather than genuine love. What are your true motivations for following Christ? Are you seeking Him or seeking what He can give you?
  • The difference between Judas and Peter was the direction they turned after failure — inward despair vs. returning to Christ. When you fail, which direction do you tend to go?
  • Judas' greed grew unchecked over time. Are there "small" sins in your life that you are tolerating, which could grow into something destructive if left unaddressed?
