2 Chronicles — Chapter 32

1After these things and these acts of faithfulness, Sennacherib king of Assyria came and invaded Judah and encamped against the fortified cities, thinking to win them for himself.

2And when Hezekiah saw that Sennacherib had come and intended to fight against Jerusalem,

3he planned with his officers and his mighty men to stop the water of the springs that were outside the city; and they helped him.

4A great many people were gathered, and they stopped all the springs and the brook that flowed through the land, saying, “Why should the kings of Assyria come and find much water?”

5He set to work resolutely and built up all the wall that was broken down and raised towers upon it, and outside it he built another wall, and he strengthened the Millo in the city of David. He also made weapons and shields in abundance.

6And he set combat commanders over the people and gathered them together to him in the square at the gate of the city and spoke encouragingly to them, saying,

7“Be strong and courageous. Do not be afraid or dismayed before the king of Assyria and all the horde that is with him, for there are more with us than with him.

8With him is an arm of flesh, but with us is the LORD our God, to help us and to fight our battles.” And the people took confidence from the words of Hezekiah king of Judah.

9After this, Sennacherib king of Assyria, who was besieging Lachish with all his forces, sent his servants to Jerusalem to Hezekiah king of Judah and to all the people of Judah who were in Jerusalem, saying,

10“Thus says Sennacherib king of Assyria, ‘On what are you trusting, that you endure the siege in Jerusalem?

11Is not Hezekiah misleading you, that he may give you over to die by famine and by thirst, when he tells you, “The LORD our God will deliver us from the hand of the king of Assyria”?

12Has not this same Hezekiah taken away his high places and his altars and commanded Judah and Jerusalem, “Before one altar you shall worship, and on it you shall burn your sacrifices”?

13Do you not know what I and my fathers have done to all the peoples of other lands? Were the gods of the nations of those lands at all able to deliver their lands out of my hand?

14Who among all the gods of those nations that my fathers devoted to destruction was able to deliver his people from my hand, that your God should be able to deliver you from my hand?

15Now, therefore, do not let Hezekiah deceive you or mislead you in this fashion, and do not believe him, for no god of any nation or kingdom has been able to deliver his people from my hand or from the hand of my fathers. How much less will your God deliver you out of my hand!’”

16And his servants said still more against the LORD God and against his servant Hezekiah.

17And he wrote letters to cast contempt on the LORD, the God of Israel, and to speak against him, saying, “Like the gods of the nations of the lands who have not delivered their people from my hands, so the God of Hezekiah will not deliver his people from my hand.”

18And they shouted it with a loud voice in the language of Judah to the people of Jerusalem who were on the wall, to frighten and terrify them, in order that they might take the city.

19And they spoke of the God of Jerusalem as they spoke of the gods of the peoples of the earth, which are the work of men’s hands.

20Then Hezekiah the king and Isaiah the prophet, the son of Amoz, prayed because of this and cried to heaven.

21And the LORD sent an angel, who cut off all the mighty warriors and commanders and officers in the camp of the king of Assyria. So he returned with shame of face to his own land. And when he came into the house of his god, some of his own sons struck him down there with the sword.

22So the LORD saved Hezekiah and the inhabitants of Jerusalem from the hand of Sennacherib king of Assyria and from the hand of all his enemies, and he provided for them on every side.

23And many brought gifts to the LORD to Jerusalem and precious things to Hezekiah king of Judah, so that he was exalted in the sight of all nations from that time onward.

24In those days Hezekiah became sick and was at the point of death, and he prayed to the LORD, and he answered him and gave him a sign.

25But Hezekiah did not make return according to the benefit done to him, for his heart was proud. Therefore wrath came upon him and Judah and Jerusalem.

26But Hezekiah humbled himself for the pride of his heart, both he and the inhabitants of Jerusalem, so that the wrath of the LORD did not come upon them in the days of Hezekiah.

27And Hezekiah had very great riches and honor, and he made for himself treasuries for silver, for gold, for precious stones, for spices, for shields, and for all kinds of costly vessels;

28storehouses also for the yield of grain, wine, and oil; and stalls for all kinds of cattle, and sheepfolds.

29He likewise provided cities for himself, and flocks and herds in abundance, for God had given him very great possessions.

30This same Hezekiah closed the upper outlet of the waters of Gihon and directed them down to the west side of the city of David. And Hezekiah prospered in all his works.

31And so in the matter of the envoys of the princes of Babylon, who had been sent to him to inquire about the sign that had been done in the land, God left him to himself, in order to test him and to know all that was in his heart.

32Now the rest of the acts of Hezekiah and his good deeds, behold, they are written in the vision of Isaiah the prophet, the son of Amoz, in the Book of the Kings of Judah and Israel.

33And Hezekiah slept with his fathers, and they buried him in the upper part of the tombs of the sons of David, and all Judah and the inhabitants of Jerusalem did him honor at his death. And Manasseh his son reigned in his place.

Summary

Sennacherib of Assyria invades Judah, and Hezekiah fortifies Jerusalem and encourages the people to trust God rather than fear the Assyrian army. The Lord sends an angel who destroys 185,000 Assyrian soldiers, vindicating Hezekiah's faith; the chapter also notes Hezekiah's pride and eventual death.

Key Verses

v.7-8
"Be strong and courageous, be not afraid nor dismayed for the king of Assyria, nor for all the multitude that is with him: for there be more with us than with him: With him is an arm of flesh; but with us is the LORD our God to help us, and to fight our battles." (KJV) — The theological centerpiece of the chapter. Hezekiah reframes the military situation in spiritual terms. Assyria's vast army is merely "an arm of flesh" — temporary, limited, mortal. God's presence with Judah is infinite, omnipotent, and decisive. Numbers are irrelevant when God fights.
v.20
"Then Hezekiah the king and Isaiah the prophet, the son of Amoz, prayed because of this and cried to heaven." (ESV)— The king and the prophet unite in prayer — the partnership of civil and prophetic leadership in crisis. Their response to blasphemy was not military counterattack but intercession. Prayer was their primary weapon.
v.21
"And the LORD sent an angel, who cut off all the mighty warriors and commanders and officers in the camp of the king of Assyria. So he returned with shame of face to his own land. And when he came into the house of his god, some of his own sons struck him down there with the sword." (ESV)— God's response is swift and devastating. One angel accomplished what no army could. The parallel in 2 Kings 19:35 specifies 185,000 died in one night. Sennacherib returned in disgrace and was murdered by his own sons — divine justice completed.
v.26
"But Hezekiah humbled himself for the pride of his heart, both he and the inhabitants of Jerusalem, so that the wrath of the LORD did not come upon them in the days of Hezekiah." (ESV)— Even after failure (pride, v.25), humility averts judgment. The pattern of 2 Chronicles 7:14 plays out again: humbling oneself before God turns away wrath.
v.31
"And so in the matter of the envoys of the princes of Babylon, who had been sent to him to inquire about the sign that had been done in the land, God left him to himself, in order to test him and to know all that was in his heart." (ESV)— A remarkable theological statement: God sometimes withdraws His manifest guidance to reveal what is truly in a person's heart. Testing exposes character. Prosperity and attention from foreign powers revealed Hezekiah's latent pride. CROSS-REFERENCES
v.7-8
2 Kings 6:16 — Elisha's words to his servant: "Fear not: for they that be with us are more than they that be with them." The same theology of invisible divine resources outweighing visible human threats. God's army is always greater than the enemy's.
v.8
Jeremiah 17:5,7 — "Cursed be the man that trusteth in man, and maketh flesh his arm... Blessed is the man that trusteth in the LORD." Hezekiah's "arm of flesh" language directly parallels Jeremiah's curse/blessing contrast. Trusting human strength is cursed; trusting God is blessed.
v.19
Isaiah 37:23 — "Whom hast thou reproached and blasphemed? and against whom hast thou exalted thy voice?... even against the Holy One of Israel." Isaiah's response to Sennacherib's taunting — the Assyrian's fundamental error was equating YHWH with the impotent gods of conquered nations.
v.21
2 Kings 19:35 — "And it came to pass that night, that the angel of the LORD went out, and smote in the camp of the Assyrians an hundred fourscore and five thousand." The fuller account specifying the 185,000 killed — one angel, one night, total victory.
v.30
2 Kings 20:20 — "And the rest of the acts of Hezekiah... and how he made a pool, and a conduit, and brought water into the city." The Siloam Tunnel — a 1,750-foot tunnel cut through solid rock, confirmed by the Siloam Inscription discovered in 1880.
v.31
2 Kings 20:12-19 — The full account of the Babylonian envoys' visit, where Hezekiah showed them all his treasures. Isaiah prophesied that everything shown would be carried to Babylon — a prophecy fulfilled in 586 BC.

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/2-chronicles-32/ Guzik highlights the contrast between Sennacherib's propaganda (equating YHWH with pagan gods) and the reality of God's power. He notes that Hezekiah combined faith with practical action — trusting God did not mean neglecting defensive preparations. The testing by Babylonian envoys (v.31) illustrates that success can be more spiritually dangerous than adversity.
  • Charles Spurgeon: "With him is an arm of flesh; but with us is the LORD our God. Here is the believer's arithmetic — the sum which faith alone can calculate. The world counts battalions; faith counts promises. The world weighs armies; faith weighs the Almighty. An arm of flesh — however muscled with battalions and armored with iron chariots — is still flesh, still mortal, still limited, still subject to the worm and the grave. But with us is the LORD — omnipotent, eternal, infinite in resource. One angel emptied the Assyrian camp of 185,000 warriors in a single night. What cannot our God do? Let us rest, as Hezekiah's people rested, upon these words. The battle is not ours but God's."

Translations

  • v.1 — KJV: "After these things, and the establishment thereof" / ESV: "After these things and these acts of faithfulness" — Hebrew "ha-emeth ha-zoth" (this truth/faithfulness). KJV's "establishment" suggests the reforms were firmly set; ESV's "acts of faithfulness" interprets "emeth" as Hezekiah's faithful conduct. Both are valid — the Hebrew word encompasses reliability, truth, and faithfulness. The theological point is that the invasion came AFTER faithfulness, not because of unfaithfulness.
  • v.8 — KJV: "the people rested themselves upon the words" / ESV: "the people took confidence from the words" — Hebrew "vayyissamku" (they leaned/supported themselves). KJV's "rested" captures the Hebrew image of leaning one's weight upon something — complete reliance. ESV's "took confidence" interprets the result of that leaning. The Hebrew verb "samak" means to lean upon, support oneself — suggesting the people transferred the weight of their fear onto Hezekiah's theological declaration.
  • v.18 — KJV: "in the Jews' speech" / ESV: "in the language of Judah" — Hebrew "Yehudit" (Judean/Jewish language). This was the common dialect of the people on the walls — the Assyrians deliberately spoke in the vernacular to maximize psychological impact on the civilian population, rather than using Aramaic (the diplomatic language) which only officials understood (cf. 2 Kings 18:26-28).
  • v.25 — KJV: "his heart was lifted up" / ESV: "his heart was proud" — Hebrew "gavah libbo" (his heart was high/ exalted). Both capture the core meaning, though KJV's literal "lifted up" preserves the spatial metaphor of the Hebrew — the heart rising above its proper station. Pride is the heart positioning itself higher than it belongs.
  • v.31 — KJV: "God left him, to try him" / ESV: "God left him to himself, in order to test him" — Hebrew "azavo ha-Elohim le-nassotho" (God left/abandoned him to test him). ESV adds "to himself" for clarity — God withdrew His guiding presence to see what Hezekiah would do independently. The verb "nasah" (test/prove) is the same used of Abraham's testing in Genesis 22:1.

Reflection

  • 1. Faithfulness does not prevent trials — it prepares you for them (v.1). The invasion came "after" Hezekiah's reforms, not because of sin but as a test of faith. Do not assume that obedience guarantees a trouble-free life. Rather, your faithfulness positions you to face crisis with God's presence and power. The storm comes; what matters is whether God is with you in it.
  • 2. Combine practical preparation with spiritual trust (vv.3-8). Hezekiah stopped springs, built walls, made weapons, AND declared "with us is the LORD our God." Faith does not mean passivity. Prepare wisely, work diligently, plan practically — but place your ultimate confidence in God, not your preparations. The walls were necessary; God was sufficient.
  • 3. The enemy's strategy is to equate your God with powerless alternatives (vv.13-15, 19). Sennacherib's argument: "No god has delivered anyone from me; yours won't either." The world constantly argues that your God is no different from any other philosophy, religion, or coping mechanism. Refuse this equation. YHWH is not one among many — He is the living God who sends angels and overthrows armies.
  • 4. Pride is the danger that follows deliverance (v.25). After God's miraculous salvation, Hezekiah's heart "was lifted up." Blessing received without sustained humility becomes the seedbed of pride. After every spiritual victory, guard your heart against self-exaltation. The deliverance was God's work, not yours. Stay low in gratitude after God lifts you high in blessing.
  • 5. God sometimes withdraws to reveal what is in your heart (v.31). When the Babylonian envoys came, "God left him, to try him." Seasons when God seems distant may be testing seasons — revealing whether your faith depends on God's manifest presence or is genuinely rooted in your character. What you do when God seems silent reveals who you truly are.
