The Gospel

Man's Sin

The Truth

Against the backdrop of God's perfect holiness, the reality of human sin becomes starkly clear. Sin is not merely breaking rules — it is falling short of God's glorious standard. Every human being, without exception, has missed the mark. The problem is universal: "all have sinned." No culture, no religion, no amount of education has produced a single sinless person.

Scripture's diagnosis of the human condition is unflinching: our hearts are deceitful, our best efforts are insufficient, and our natural inclination bends toward selfishness and rebellion against God. This is not a popular message, but it is an honest one. The first step toward healing is always an accurate diagnosis, and the Bible does not sugarcoat humanity's spiritual condition.

Sin affects every dimension of human existence: our minds (darkened understanding), our wills (enslaved to self), our relationships (broken by pride and selfishness), and our standing before God (guilty and condemned). This total effect of sin — what theologians call "total depravity" — does not mean we are as bad as possible, but that sin touches every part of who we are.

Key Scriptures

  • Romans 3:23 — "For all have sinned and fall short of the glory of God."
  • Isaiah 64:6 — "All of us have become like one who is unclean, and all our righteous acts are like filthy rags."
  • Romans 3:10-12 — "There is no one righteous, not even one; there is no one who understands; there is no one who seeks God."
  • Genesis 6:5 — "Every inclination of the thoughts of the human heart was only evil all the time."
  • Jeremiah 17:9 — "The heart is deceitful above all things and beyond cure. Who can understand it?"

Why It Matters

Understanding the depth of our sin matters because it prevents two dangerous errors: self-righteousness (thinking we are good enough for God) and despair (thinking we are too bad for God). The gospel speaks to both: we are worse than we think (no one is righteous) yet more loved than we dare hope (God saves the undeserving by grace).

The universal nature of sin also creates a level playing field before God. No one stands above another in moral superiority — all have sinned, all fall short, all need a Savior. This truth demolishes human pride and creates the humble posture necessary to receive grace. We come to God not as achievers presenting résumés but as beggars receiving bread.

Reflection Questions

  • How does understanding the universal nature of sin affect the way you view yourself and others?
  • In what ways do you see sin's effects on your thoughts, desires, and relationships?
  • Why is acknowledging our sinfulness essential before we can receive the gospel?
