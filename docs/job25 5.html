Job — Chapter 25

1Then Bildad the Shuhite answered and said:

2“Dominion and fear are with God; he makes peace in his high heaven.

3Is there any number to his armies? Upon whom does his light not arise?

4How then can man be in the right before God? How can he who is born of woman be pure?

5Behold, even the moon is not bright, and the stars are not pure in his eyes;

6how much less man, who is a maggot, and the son of man, who is a worm!”

Summary

Bildad's third speech — the shortest speech in the dialogues (only 6 verses), asserting God's dominion and purity while declaring that no mortal can be righteous before Him.

Key Verses

v.2
"Dominion and fear are with God; he makes peace in his high heaven." (ESV) — God's sovereignty is absolute. He maintains order even among heavenly beings. Bildad's point: if God governs the cosmos, who are you to question Him?
v.4
"How then can man be in the right before God? How can he who is born of woman be pure?" (ESV) — The question is legitimate but misapplied. Bildad uses human depravity as a club to silence Job rather than as a truth that points toward grace.
v.6
"How much less man, who is a maggot, and the son of man, who is a worm!" (ESV) — Bildad reduces humanity to nothing. While human smallness before God is real, reducing man to a "worm" contradicts God's own valuation of man (Psalm 8:4-6). God made man in His image — not as a worm.

Cross-References

  • v.2 — Colossians 1:20 — Christ "made peace through the blood of his cross" — the ultimate fulfillment of God making peace. v.3 — Revelation 5:11 — "Ten thousand times ten thousand" angels — God's armies are innumerable. v.4 — Job 9:2 — Job himself asked this same question earlier: "How can a man be in the right before God?" (ESV) The question is valid; the answer is grace, not despair. v.4 — Romans 3:23-24 — "All have sinned... justified by his grace as a gift." (ESV) The answer to Bildad's question is not "man cannot be justified" but "man is justified by grace." v.5 — Isaiah 64:6 — "All our righteous deeds are like a polluted garment" (ESV) — human righteousness is insufficient, but God provides His own. v.6 — Psalm 8:4-6 — "What is man that you are mindful of him ...you have made him a little lower than the heavenly beings." (ESV) God's view of man is far higher than Bildad's. v.6 — Psalm 22:6 — "I am a worm and not a man" (ESV) — David uses this language prophetically of Christ's humiliation, not of human nature in general.

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/job-25/ Guzik observes that Bildad's brevity reveals the bankruptcy of the friends' position. They have run out of arguments. Bildad essentially repeats what Eliphaz said in 4:17-19 and 15:14-16 without adding anything new. His reduction of man to a "worm" is theologically one-sided — it acknowledges human depravity but ignores the image of God in man.
  • Charles Spurgeon: "Bildad has nothing left to say, and he says it in six verses. His theology has been a cistern with no water — it looked deep but was empty. The question 'How can man be just with God?' is the right question asked by the wrong man for the wrong reason. The answer is not 'man cannot' — the answer is 'God justifies.' But Bildad does not know grace. He knows only law, and law without grace produces either pride or despair — never peace."

Translations

  • v.2 — KJV: "he maketh peace in his high places" / ESV: "he makes peace in his high heaven" — Hebrew "marom" (heights). This likely refers to God maintaining order among heavenly beings (cf. Isaiah 24:21). If God governs angels, how can man contend with Him?
  • v.4 — KJV: "justified" / ESV: "in the right" — Hebrew "tsadaq" — to be righteous, to be declared just. The forensic sense (declared righteous) is prominent: how can man win a legal case before God?
  • v.6 — KJV: "man, that is a worm... son of man, which is a worm" — Two different Hebrew words: "rimmah" (maggot) and "toleah" (worm/crimson worm). The ESV renders the first as "maggot." Bildad uses the most degrading possible language for humanity.

Reflection

  • 1. When arguments run out, silence is wiser than repetition (6 verses). Bildad has nothing new to contribute but speaks anyway. Sometimes the most faithful thing is to stop talking. The friends' failure is not that they spoke — it is that they kept speaking when they had nothing true left to say.
  • 2. Human smallness does not negate human value (v.6). Bildad calls man a worm. God calls man His image-bearer (Genesis 1:27). Both truths exist: we are small before God's majesty AND precious in God's sight. Theology that emphasizes one while ignoring the other is incomplete.
  • 3. The right question deserves the right answer (v.4). "How can man be justified with God?" is Scripture's central question. But the answer is not despair ("you can't") — it is grace ("God justifies the ungodly," Romans 4:5). Bildad asks the question that the gospel answers.
  • 4. Theology without compassion is empty (entire chapter). Bildad's six verses contain no comfort, no empathy, no practical help. They are theologically abstract and personally useless. Right doctrine that produces no love is dead doctrine.
