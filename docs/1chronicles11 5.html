1Then all Israel gathered together to David at Hebron and said, “Behold, we are your bone and flesh.

2In times past, even when Saul was king, it was you who led out and brought in Israel. And the LORD your God said to you, ‘You shall be shepherd of my people Israel, and you shall be prince over my people Israel.’”

3So all the elders of Israel came to the king at Hebron, and David made a covenant with them at Hebron before the LORD. And they anointed David king over Israel, according to the word of the LORD by Samuel.

4And David and all Israel went to Jerusalem, that is, Jebus, where the Jebusites were, the inhabitants of the land.

5The inhabitants of Jebus said to David, “You will not come in here.” Nevertheless, David took the stronghold of Zion, that is, the city of David.

6David said, “Whoever strikes the Jebusites first shall be chief and commander.” And Joab the son of Zeruiah went up first, so he became chief.

7And David lived in the stronghold; therefore it was called the city of David.

8And he built the city all around from the Millo in complete circuit, and Joab repaired the rest of the city.

9And David became greater and greater, for the LORD of hosts was with him.

10Now these are the chiefs of David’s mighty men, who gave him strong support in his kingdom, together with all Israel, to make him king, according to the word of the LORD concerning Israel.

11This is an account of David’s mighty men: Jashobeam, a Hachmonite, was chief of the three. He wielded his spear against 300 whom he killed at one time.

12And next to him among the three mighty men was Eleazar the son of Dodo, the Ahohite.

13He was with David at Pas-dammim when the Philistines were gathered there for battle. There was a plot of ground full of barley, and the men fled from the Philistines.

14But he took his stand in the midst of the plot and defended it and killed the Philistines. And the LORD saved them by a great victory.

15Three of the thirty chief men went down to the rock to David at the cave of Adullam, when the army of Philistines was encamped in the Valley of Rephaim.

16David was then in the stronghold, and the garrison of the Philistines was then at Bethlehem.

17And David said longingly, “Oh that someone would give me water to drink from the well of Bethlehem that is by the gate!”

18Then the three mighty men broke through the camp of the Philistines and drew water out of the well of Bethlehem that was by the gate and took it and brought it to David. But David would not drink it. He poured it out to the LORD

19and said, “Far be it from me before my God that I should do this. Shall I drink the lifeblood of these men? For at the risk of their lives they brought it.” Therefore he would not drink it. These things did the three mighty men.

20Now Abishai, the brother of Joab, was chief of the thirty. And he wielded his spear against 300 men and killed them and won a name beside the three.

21He was the most renowned of the thirty and became their commander, but he did not attain to the three.

22And Benaiah the son of Jehoiada was a valiant man of Kabzeel, a doer of great deeds. He struck down two heroes of Moab. He also went down and struck down a lion in a pit on a day when snow had fallen.

23And he struck down an Egyptian, a man of great stature, five cubits tall. The Egyptian had in his hand a spear like a weaver’s beam, but Benaiah went down to him with a staff and snatched the spear out of the Egyptian’s hand and killed him with his own spear.

24These things did Benaiah the son of Jehoiada and won a name beside the three mighty men.

25He was renowned among the thirty, but he did not attain to the three. And David set him over his bodyguard.

Summary

All Israel gathers at Hebron to anoint David king, and he immediately captures Jerusalem (the Jebusite stronghold) to establish it as his capital. The chapter also lists David's mighty men and their heroic exploits.

Key Verses

  • v.1 — "Then all Israel gathered together to David at Hebron and said, 'Behold, we are your bone and flesh.'" (ESV) — "All Israel" — the Chronicler's favorite phrase for national unity under David. The people recognize kinship ("bone and flesh") and choose David willingly. The ideal king is not imposed but recognized.
  • v.2 — "'You shall be shepherd of my people Israel, and you shall be prince over my people Israel.'" (ESV) — God's word to David defines kingship as shepherding. The king is not a despot but a shepherd — feeding, guiding, protecting. This is the Davidic ideal that points to Christ, the Good Shepherd.
  • v.3 — "and they anointed David king over Israel, according to the word of the LORD by Samuel." (ESV) — Three authorities converge: the people's choice, the elders' covenant, and God's prior word through Samuel. Legitimate kingship requires divine appointment confirmed by human recognition.
  • v.9 — "And David became greater and greater, for the LORD of hosts was with him." (ESV) — The Chronicler's thesis statement for David's reign. David's greatness was not self-generated but divinely empowered. "The LORD of hosts" — the God of armies — backed David. Every victory, every expansion, every achievement came from God's presence. vv.17-19 — "And David said longingly, 'Oh that someone would give me water to drink from the well of Bethlehem that is by the gate!'... Then the three mighty men broke through the camp of the Philistines... But David would not drink it. He poured it out to the LORD." (ESV) — One of the most beautiful episodes in David's life. His men risked their lives for a casual wish. David, recognizing the cost, refused to drink what was purchased with blood and offered it to God instead. This reveals David's character: he valued his men's lives above his own comfort and consecrated their sacrifice to God.
  • v.41 — "Uriah the Hittite" (KJV/ESV) — Listed among David's mighty men. The Chronicler includes this name without comment — yet every reader knows the story. Uriah was one of David's most loyal warriors, and David had him killed. The Chronicler omits the Bathsheba narrative but preserves Uriah's name in the honor roll — a silent witness.

Cross-References

  • vv.1-3 — 2 Samuel 5:1-3 — The parallel account. Both versions are nearly identical, emphasizing that this event was central to Israel's self-understanding: the moment all tribes united under David.
  • v.4-8 → 2 Samuel 5:6-10 — The capture of Jerusalem. 2 Samuel includes the Jebusites' taunt about "the blind and the lame"; Chronicles simplifies to their declaration "You will not come in here." (ESV)
  • v.6 → 2 Samuel 5:8 — In Samuel, David offers the position to whoever "goes up by the water shaft." (ESV) Chronicles says "whoever strikes the Jebusites first." Both result in Joab's promotion.
  • vv.10-47 — 2 Samuel 23:8-39 — The parallel mighty men list. Chronicles adds approximately ten additional names (vv.41- 47), suggesting access to fuller records.
  • v.17 → 2 Samuel 23:15-17 — The Bethlehem water incident. Identical in both accounts — clearly a beloved tradition preserved in multiple sources.
  • v.22 → 2 Samuel 23:20-21 — Benaiah's exploits: lion-killing and Egyptian-slaying. Chronicles adds the detail of the Egyptian's height (five cubits = 7.5 feet).

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): "https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/1-chronicles-11/" target="_blank">https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/1-chronicles-11/ Guzik highlights the Chronicler's omission of the civil war period (2 Samuel 2-4) — presenting David as immediately recognized by all Israel. He notes that the Bethlehem water episode reveals why men would die for David: he was a leader who valued them more than himself.
  • Charles Spurgeon: "David poured out the water as a drink offering to the LORD — and in so doing, he showed himself a true king. A lesser man would have drunk it and praised his warriors' bravery. David could not. What cost his men's blood was too sacred for his lips — it belonged to God alone. Here is the mark of greatness: recognizing that the sacrifices others make for you are not yours to consume but God's to receive. Every leader should learn this: the devotion of those who serve you is a sacred trust, not a personal entitlement."

Translations

  • v.2 → KJV: "Thou shalt feed my people" / ESV: "You shall be shepherd of my people" — Hebrew "tir'eh" (you shall shepherd/tend). KJV's "feed" is one aspect of shepherding; ESV's "shepherd" captures the full range: feeding, leading, protecting. The metaphor is royal: kings as shepherds was common in the ancient Near East, but Israel's king shepherds God's people, not his own.
  • v.5 → KJV: "the castle of Zion" / ESV: "the stronghold of Zion" — Hebrew "metsudah" (stronghold, fortress). KJV's "castle" is slightly anachronistic; ESV's "stronghold" better reflects the fortified citadel that was ancient Jebus.
  • v.9 → KJV: "David waxed greater and greater" / ESV: "David became greater and greater" — Hebrew "halokh v'gadol" (going and becoming great). The continuous verbal form shows progressive growth — David's ascent was not a single event but a sustained trajectory of increasing greatness.
  • v.11 → KJV: "three hundred slain by him at one time" / ESV: "300 whom he killed at one time" — 2 Samuel 23:8 says "eight hundred." This is a well-known textual variant between the parallel accounts. The difference may reflect different incidents or a scribal copying error in transmission.
  • v.19 → KJV: "shall I drink the blood of these men that have put their lives in jeopardy?" / ESV: "Shall I drink the lifeblood of these men?" — Hebrew "dam" (blood). David equates the water with his men's blood — to drink it would be to consume their life-sacrifice for his pleasure. The metaphor reveals profound respect for their risk.
  • v.22 → KJV: "two lionlike men of Moab" / ESV: "two heroes of Moab" — Hebrew "ariel" (lion of God, or hero). The meaning is debated: KJV takes it literally as "lion-like" warriors; ESV interprets as "heroes" (mighty ones). Both are linguistically possible. The word "ariel" appears also as a name for Jerusalem (Isaiah 29:1).

Reflection

  • 1. True leadership is shepherding (v.2). God told David to "feed" (shepherd) His people — not exploit them, not dominate them. Biblical leadership in every sphere — home, church, workplace — is serving those under your care. Are you leading like a shepherd or like a tyrant?
  • 2. God's presence is the source of all true greatness (v.9). "David became greater and greater, for the LORD of hosts was with him." The correlation is explicit: David's greatness was not talent, strategy, or luck — it was God's presence. Whatever genuine greatness you achieve comes from the same source. Stay close to God, and growth will follow.
  • 3. Value people's sacrifices properly (vv.17-19). David refused to casually consume what cost his men their lives. Do you take for granted the sacrifices others make for you? Your spouse's patience, your parents' investments, your colleagues' support — these are not cheap commodities but life-offerings. Treat them as sacred.
  • 4. The right people rally around God's anointed (vv.10-47). David's mighty men came from every tribe, every background — even foreigners (Zelek the Ammonite, Uriah the Hittite, Ithmah the Moabite). God draws diverse people to His purposes. Don't be surprised when unlikely allies appear in your mission.
  • 5. Human loyalty can inspire extraordinary sacrifice (vv.17-19). These men risked death for their king's casual wish. What inspires such devotion? A leader worth following. David's character drew out the best in others. Your character — not your position — determines whether people will sacrifice for your vision.
