Summary

A mighty angel with a little scroll announces that when the seventh trumpet sounds, the mystery of God will be finished — John eats the scroll which is sweet in his mouth but bitter in his stomach, and is told to prophesy again.

Authorship & Background

Author: The Apostle John (see Chapter 1 notes for full details).
Historical Context: Chapter 10 is an interlude between the sixth and seventh trumpets (like chapter 7 was between the sixth and seventh seals). A mighty angel descends with a little scroll, seven thunders speak (but are sealed), and John is told to eat the scroll — sweet in the mouth but bitter in the stomach. This chapter prepares for the final trumpet and the completion of God's mystery.
The Mighty Angel (vv.1-4): A powerful angel descends — clothed with a cloud, rainbow on his head, face like the sun, feet like fire (v.1). He straddles sea and land (v.2) — claiming authority over all creation. He cries with a lion's voice; seven thunders respond (v.3). John is told NOT to write what the thunders said — some things remain sealed (v.4).
The Oath (vv.5-7): The angel swears by the eternal Creator: "there should be time no longer" (v.6) — no more delay. When the seventh trumpet sounds, "the mystery of God should be finished" (v.7).
The Little Scroll (vv.8-11): John is told to eat the scroll (v.8). It is sweet as honey in his mouth but bitter in his stomach (vv.9-10). He must "prophesy again" to many peoples and nations (v.11). God's word is sweet (truth, promise) but bitter (judgment, suffering).

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): enduringword.com Guzik covers the mighty angel's identity, the sealed thunders, the oath of no more delay, and the sweet/bitter scroll.
  • Charles Spurgeon: Sermon: "The Sweet and the Bitter" on vv.9-10 (MTP Vol. 25, No. 1502) "Sweet in the mouth — God's word is delightful to receive. Truth is sweet. Promise is sweet. The gospel is sweet. But bitter in the belly — when you digest it, when you live it out, when you proclaim judgment, when you see its implications for a lost world — it becomes bitter. The prophet who truly digests God's word will know both sweetness and sorrow."

Reflection

  • 1. "There should be time no longer" (v.6). God's patience has a limit. Are you living with urgency, knowing that delay will end and God's plan will be completed?
  • 2. "Seal up those things" (v.4). God has secrets — things He chooses not to reveal. Can you accept that some questions won't be answered this side of eternity? Can you trust what He hasn't revealed?
  • 3. "Sweet as honey... belly was bitter" (vv.9-10). God's word is both sweet and bitter. Do you experience both? The sweetness of His promises AND the bitterness of His judgments? A balanced diet of Scripture includes both.
  • 4. "Thou must prophesy again" (v.11). After receiving God's word, you must share it. Are you keeping truth to yourself or proclaiming it to others?
