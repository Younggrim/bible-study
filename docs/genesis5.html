Essentially literal. Modern English, highly accurate. Best for deep study.

Traditional (1611). Beautiful, poetic language. The classic English Bible.
ASV — American Standard Version
Formal equivalent (1901). Very literal, precise. Good for word studies.

Study Bible with extensive translator notes. Balances accuracy and readability.

2Male and female he created them, and he blessed them and named them Man when they were created.

3When Adam had lived 130 years, he fathered a son in his own likeness, after his image, and named him Seth.

Summary

The genealogy from Adam to Noah traces ten generations, showing both long life and the relentless refrain 'and he died' — death reigns after the Fall.

Key Verses

v.1
"When God created man, he made him in the likeness of God." (ESV) — Reaffirming the image of God despite the fall.
v.3
"When Adam had lived 130 years, he fathered a son in his own likeness, after his image." (ESV) — Now Adam's likeness (fallen), not just God's.
v.5
"Thus all the days that Adam lived were 930 years, and he died." (ESV) — Death's reality.
v.24
"Enoch walked with God, and he was not, for God took him." (ESV) — Life beyond death through walking with God.
v.29
"He called his name Noah, saying, 'Out of the ground that the LORD has cursed, this one shall bring us relief.'" (ESV) — Hope for relief from the curse.

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): "https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/genesis-5/" target="_blank">https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/genesis-5/ Guzik covers the genealogy's purpose, the refrain of death, Enoch's translation, and Noah's prophetic name.
  • Charles Spurgeon: Sermon: "Enoch Walked with God" on v.24 (MTP Vol. 11, No. 635) "Enoch WALKED with God — not ran, not flew, not leaped — walked. The steady, daily, consistent pace of a life lived in God's company. Not occasional visits but habitual companionship. And the result: he was not, for God took him. Walking with God leads to being with God. The daily walk becomes the eternal presence. Start walking today — and one day, without interruption, you'll find yourself home."

Translations

  • v.1 → KJV: "This is the book of the generations of Adam. In the day that God created man, in the likeness of God made he him" / ESV: "This is the book of the generations of Adam. When God created man, he made him in the likeness of God" — Both equivalent. Hebrew "sepher toledoth" — the written record of the descendants. The second "toledoth" section in Genesis (the first is 2:4). ESV restructures for modern English word order.
  • v.2 → KJV: "called their name Adam" / ESV: "named them Man" — Hebrew "Adam" means "man/mankind." ESV translates the name to show its meaning; KJV retains the proper name. Both are valid — "Adam" is both a name and the Hebrew word for "man."
  • v.3 → KJV: "begat a son in his own likeness, after his image" / ESV: "fathered a son in his own likeness, after his image" — Both equivalent. KJV's "begat" vs. ESV's "fathered" — same Hebrew "yalad" (to beget/father). Adam's son bears Adam's (fallen) image, not just God's original image. The image of God continues but is now transmitted through a fallen nature.
  • v.22 → KJV: "walked with God" / ESV: "walked with God" — Both identical. Hebrew "hithhallek eth ha-Elohim" — walked habitually with God. The hithpael (reflexive) form suggests ongoing, deliberate, intimate companionship.
  • v.24 → KJV: "he was not; for God took him" / ESV: "he was not, for God took him" — Both identical. Hebrew "einennu ki laqach oto Elohim" — he was no more because God took/received him. Enoch was translated directly to God's presence without dying.
  • v.27 → Methuselah's name may mean "when he dies, it shall come" — possibly prophetic of the flood. He died the year the flood came. Both KJV and ESV render the name identically.
  • v.29 → KJV: "This same shall comfort us concerning our work and toil of our hands, because of the ground which the LORD hath cursed" / ESV: "Out of the ground that the LORD has cursed, this one shall bring us relief from our work and from the painful toil of our hands" — Hebrew "yenachamenu" — shall comfort/bring relief. ESV restructures for clarity and uses "relief" and "painful toil" to make the connection to the curse of 3:17-19 more explicit. "Noah" — Hebrew "Noach" — rest, comfort. Related to "nacham" (comfort).
  • TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB) - v.1 — NET: "the record of the family line of Adam" — renders Hebrew "sepher toledoth" as "record of the family line" rather than "book of the generations" (KJV/ESV/ASV), modernizing the genealogical formula. - v.2 — NET: "named them 'humankind'" — translates Hebrew "Adam" as the common noun "humankind" rather than the proper name, emphasizing the collective sense (male and female together named "Adam/humanity"). - v.24 — NET: "he disappeared because God took him away" — uniquely renders "he was not" as "he disappeared," making the supernatural event more vivid and explicit for modern readers. WEB: "he was not found, for God took him" — adds "found" (not in the Hebrew), clarifying that people looked for Enoch but could not locate him. - v.29 — ASV brackets "[which cometh]" to show this is supplied for clarity and not in the Hebrew, demonstrating ASV's practice of marking translator additions. This transparency about the Hebrew text is unique to ASV.

Reflection

  • 1. "And he died" (repeated 8 times). Death is the universal human experience since the fall. Does the reality of death create urgency in your life? Are you living with eternity in view?
  • 2. "Enoch walked with God" (v.24). One man in a chapter of death didn't die — because he walked with God. What does "walking with God" look like for you daily? Is it habitual or occasional?
  • 3. "In his own likeness, after his image" (v.3). We inherit a fallen nature. But through Christ, we are being renewed in God's image (Col 3:10). Are you being transformed, or just passing on the old nature?
  • 4. "This same shall comfort us" (v.29). Even in a world under curse, there is hope for comfort and rest. Where do you find rest from the curse's effects? Ultimately, only in Christ.
  • 5. The genealogy connects Adam to Noah — showing God's faithfulness across generations. God keeps His promises over centuries. Can you trust Him with your lifetime?
