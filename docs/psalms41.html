Psalms — Chapter 41

1Blessed is the one who considers the poor! In the day of trouble the LORD delivers him;

2the LORD protects him and keeps him alive; he is called blessed in the land; you do not give him up to the will of his enemies.

3The LORD sustains him on his sickbed; in his illness you restore him to full health.

4As for me, I said, “O LORD, be gracious to me; heal me, for I have sinned against you!”

5My enemies say of me in malice, “When will he die, and his name perish?”

6And when one comes to see me, he utters empty words, while his heart gathers iniquity; when he goes out, he tells it abroad.

7All who hate me whisper together about me; they imagine the worst for me.

8They say, “A deadly thing is poured out on him; he will not rise again from where he lies.”

9Even my close friend in whom I trusted, who ate my bread, has lifted his heel against me.

10But you, O LORD, be gracious to me, and raise me up, that I may repay them!

11By this I know that you delight in me: my enemy will not shout in triumph over me.

12But you have upheld me because of my integrity, and set me in your presence forever.

13Blessed be the LORD, the God of Israel, from everlasting to everlasting! Amen and Amen.

Summary

A lament and trust psalm closing Book I — blessed is he who considers the poor; David's enemies wish him dead, and even his close friend (prophetic of Judas) has lifted his heel against him, yet the LORD upholds him.

Key Verses

Cross-References

  • v. 1 — Proverbs 19:17 — "He that hath pity upon the poor lendeth unto the LORD; and that which he hath given will he pay him again." God rewards compassion for the needy.
  • v. 4 — Psalm 6:2 — "Have mercy upon me, O LORD; for I am weak: O LORD, heal me; for my bones are vexed." David's repeated pattern of crying out for mercy in sickness.
  • v. 9 — John 13:18 — "I speak not of you all: I know whom I have chosen: but that the scripture might be fulfilled, He that eateth bread with me hath lifted up his heel against me." Jesus identifies this verse as Messianic prophecy fulfilled in Judas.
  • v. 9 — 2 Samuel 15:12 — Ahithophel's betrayal of David parallels Judas's betrayal of Christ, both trusted companions who turned traitor.
  • v. 11 — Psalm 35:19 — "Let not them that are mine enemies wrongfully rejoice over me." The righteous trust that God will not allow final triumph to the wicked.
  • v. 12 — Psalm 25:21 — "Let integrity and uprightness preserve me; for I wait on thee." Integrity as a hallmark of those God sustains.
  • v. 13 — Psalm 72:18-19 — The doxology closing Book II, showing the pattern of praise concluding each division of the Psalter.
  • v. 13 — 1 Chronicles 16:36 — "Blessed be the LORD God of Israel for ever and ever. And all the people said, Amen, and praised the LORD." The same doxological formula used in corporate worship.

Translations

  • v. 1 — KJV "considereth the poor" vs. ESV "considers the poor" — The Hebrew "maskil" (שָׂכַל) means to act wisely, to give thoughtful attention to. Both translations capture this, but the Hebrew emphasizes prudent, discerning compassion rather than mere pity. The word "poor" (dal) refers to the weak, thin, or helpless.
  • v. 3 — KJV "thou wilt make all his bed in his sickness" vs. ESV "in his illness you restore him to full health" — The KJV preserves the vivid Hebrew metaphor of God literally turning or rearranging the sick person's bed (transforming a bed of illness into a bed of comfort). The ESV interprets the meaning more dynamically.
  • v. 8 — KJV "An evil disease" vs. ESV "A deadly thing" — The Hebrew "debar beliyyaal" literally means "a thing of Belial" or "a worthless/wicked thing." KJV interprets this as a specific disease; ESV renders it more generally as something deadly or destructive poured upon him.
  • v. 9 — KJV "mine own familiar friend" vs. ESV "my close friend" — The Hebrew phrase "ish shelomi" literally means "man of my peace" — one with whom David had a covenant of peace. Both translations capture the intimacy, but the Hebrew carries overtones of a formally established peaceful relationship.
  • v. 12 — KJV "settest me before thy face for ever" vs. ESV "set me in your presence forever" — Nearly identical. The Hebrew "paneh" (face) conveys intimate, personal access to God — being in His direct sight and favor perpetually.

Reflection

  • 1. Compassion brings divine protection. The psalm opens with a promise: those who thoughtfully consider the poor and weak will find God considering them in their time of trouble. How am I actively, wisely caring for those in need around me?
  • 2. Honest confession accompanies faithful prayer. David does not hide his sin from God (v. 4: "heal my soul; for I have sinned against thee"). Authentic prayer requires acknowledging our own failures even while crying out against injustice done to us.
  • 3. Betrayal by close friends is among the deepest wounds. David's pain — and ultimately Christ's — teaches us that intimate betrayal is not outside God's sovereign plan. If we experience such treachery, we are in the company of David and of Christ Himself.
  • 4. God's final word is praise, not pain. Book I of the Psalms contains many laments, yet it concludes with a doxology. No matter how deep the valley, the trajectory of the faithful life bends toward eternal praise. Our present sufferings are not the final chapter.
  • 5. Integrity does not prevent suffering but sustains us through it. David was upheld "in mine integrity" (v. 12) — not exempted from trial but preserved through it. Walking uprightly is not a guarantee against hardship but a foundation that holds firm when hardship comes.
