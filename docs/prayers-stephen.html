Prayers in the Bible

Stephen's Prayer (Acts 7:59-60)

The Prayer

Stephen's prayer is remarkable for its setting: he was being stoned to death. As rocks crushed his body, the first Christian martyr looked up to see heaven open and Jesus standing at the right hand of God. With his final breaths, he prayed two things — for his own spirit to be received by Christ, and for his murderers to be forgiven.

The parallel to Jesus' words on the cross is unmistakable. Just as Jesus prayed "Father, forgive them," Stephen prayed "Lord, do not hold this sin against them." The Spirit of Christ living in Stephen produced the same response Christ Himself displayed — supernatural love for enemies in the very moment of their cruelty.

Among those watching Stephen die was a young man named Saul, who approved of the execution. Church tradition has long connected Stephen's prayer to Saul's later conversion — suggesting that this prayer of forgiveness planted a seed that eventually blossomed on the Damascus road. Prayers of forgiveness may not change our persecutors immediately, but they are never wasted.

Key Scriptures

  • Acts 7:59-60 — "Lord Jesus, receive my spirit... Lord, do not hold this sin against them."
  • Luke 23:34 — "Father, forgive them, for they do not know what they are doing."
  • Matthew 5:44 — "Love your enemies and pray for those who persecute you."
  • Romans 12:14 — "Bless those who persecute you; bless and do not curse."
  • Acts 7:55-56 — "Stephen, full of the Holy Spirit, looked up to heaven and saw the glory of God, and Jesus standing at the right hand of God."

What We Learn About Prayer

Stephen teaches us that the ultimate test of our prayer life is how we pray under persecution. It is easy to pray for enemies in comfort; it is supernatural to pray for them while they are actively harming us. This capacity comes not from natural goodness but from being filled with the Holy Spirit, as Stephen was.

We also learn that forgiveness in prayer does not mean our pain does not matter — it means we entrust justice to God rather than demanding vengeance ourselves. Stephen's prayer released his killers into God's hands, freeing his own spirit from bitterness even as his body was being destroyed. This is the freedom of radical forgiveness.

Reflection Questions

  • Is there someone who has wronged you that you need to release through a prayer of forgiveness?
  • How does Stephen's vision of Christ standing to receive him encourage you in facing opposition?
  • What would it take for you to pray blessings over those who have caused you harm?
