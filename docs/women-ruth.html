Women of the Bible

Ruth

Her Story

Ruth was a Moabite woman — a foreigner from a nation that had historically been an enemy of Israel. When famine drove Naomi's family to Moab, Ruth married one of Naomi's sons. But tragedy struck: Naomi's husband and both sons died, leaving three widows destitute. When Naomi decided to return to Bethlehem, she urged her daughters-in-law to go back to their own families. Orpah turned back, but Ruth clung to Naomi with words that echo through the ages: "Where you go I will go, and where you stay I will stay. Your people will be my people and your God my God."

Ruth's declaration was not mere sentiment — it was a covenant commitment that cost her everything familiar. She left her homeland, her gods, her family, and her future prospects to follow Naomi into poverty and uncertainty. In Bethlehem, she humbled herself to glean in the fields behind the harvesters — the ancient equivalent of searching for food in dumpsters. Yet her faithfulness caught the eye of Boaz, a kinsman-redeemer who recognized her extraordinary character.

Through Boaz's redemption, Ruth — the foreign widow with no standing and no future — became the great-grandmother of King David and an ancestor of Jesus Christ. Her story is one of the most beautiful pictures of redemption in all of Scripture: God took the outsider and made her an insider, took the destitute and made her an ancestor of royalty. No one is too far outside God's story to be written into its most important chapters.

Key Scriptures

  • Ruth 1:16-17 — "Where you go I will go... Your people will be my people and your God my God."
  • Ruth 2:12 — "May you be richly rewarded by the LORD... under whose wings you have come to take refuge."
  • Ruth 4:13-17 — Ruth marries Boaz and becomes grandmother of Jesse, father of David.
  • Proverbs 31:10 — "A wife of noble character who can find? She is worth far more than rubies."
  • Matthew 1:5 — Ruth named in the genealogy of Jesus Christ.

Lessons from Her Life

Ruth teaches us that loyalty and faithfulness can transform our destiny. Her commitment to Naomi was not strategic — it offered no visible advantage. Yet this selfless devotion placed her in the exact field, at the exact time, where God's provision awaited her. When we are faithful in the small, unglamorous choices — choosing love over convenience, commitment over comfort — God weaves those choices into a larger story we cannot yet see.

Ruth also demonstrates that God's family has always been larger than ethnic or national boundaries. As a Moabite woman in Israel's royal lineage, Ruth prefigures the gospel truth that God's grace extends to all people. No background, no past, no nationality can exclude someone from God's redemptive purposes. The inclusion of Ruth in Christ's genealogy is God's permanent statement that outsiders are welcome at His table.

Reflection Questions

  • Who in your life needs the kind of selfless loyalty Ruth showed Naomi?
  • Have you ever felt like an outsider in God's story? How does Ruth's inclusion encourage you?
  • What small act of faithfulness might God be using to position you for something greater?
  • How does Ruth's story shape your understanding of God's view of foreigners and outsiders?
