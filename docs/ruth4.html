Ruth — Chapter 4

1Now Boaz had gone up to the gate and sat down there. And behold, the redeemer, of whom Boaz had spoken, came by. So Boaz said, “Turn aside, friend; sit down here.” And he turned aside and sat down.

2And he took ten men of the elders of the city and said, “Sit down here.” So they sat down.

3Then he said to the redeemer, “Naomi, who has come back from the country of Moab, is selling the parcel of land that belonged to our relative Elimelech.

4So I thought I would tell you of it and say, ‘Buy it in the presence of those sitting here and in the presence of the elders of my people.’ If you will redeem it, redeem it. But if you will not, tell me, that I may know, for there is no one besides you to redeem it, and I come after you.” And he said, “I will redeem it.”

5Then Boaz said, “The day you buy the field from the hand of Naomi, you also acquire Ruth the Moabite, the widow of the dead, in order to perpetuate the name of the dead in his inheritance.”

6Then the redeemer said, “I cannot redeem it for myself, lest I impair my own inheritance. Take my right of redemption yourself, for I cannot redeem it.”

7Now this was the custom in former times in Israel concerning redeeming and exchanging: to confirm a transaction, the one drew off his sandal and gave it to the other, and this was the manner of attesting in Israel.

8So when the redeemer said to Boaz, “Buy it for yourself,” he drew off his sandal.

9Then Boaz said to the elders and all the people, “You are witnesses this day that I have bought from the hand of Naomi all that belonged to Elimelech and all that belonged to Chilion and to Mahlon.

10Also Ruth the Moabite, the widow of Mahlon, I have bought to be my wife, to perpetuate the name of the dead in his inheritance, that the name of the dead may not be cut off from among his brothers and from the gate of his native place. You are witnesses this day.”

11Then all the people who were at the gate and the elders said, “We are witnesses. May the LORD make the woman, who is coming into your house, like Rachel and Leah, who together built up the house of Israel. May you act worthily in Ephrathah and be renowned in Bethlehem,

12and may your house be like the house of Perez, whom Tamar bore to Judah, because of the offspring that the LORD will give you by this young woman.”

13So Boaz took Ruth, and she became his wife. And he went in to her, and the LORD gave her conception, and she bore a son.

14Then the women said to Naomi, “Blessed be the LORD, who has not left you this day without a redeemer, and may his name be renowned in Israel!

15He shall be to you a restorer of life and a nourisher of your old age, for your daughter-in-law who loves you, who is more to you than seven sons, has given birth to him.”

16Then Naomi took the child and laid him on her lap and became his nurse.

17And the women of the neighborhood gave him a name, saying, “A son has been born to Naomi.” They named him Obed. He was the father of Jesse, the father of David.

18Now these are the generations of Perez: Perez fathered Hezron,

19Hezron fathered Ram, Ram fathered Amminadab,

20Amminadab fathered Nahshon, Nahshon fathered Salmon,

21Salmon fathered Boaz, Boaz fathered Obed,

22Obed fathered Jesse, and Jesse fathered David.

Summary

Boaz redeems Naomi's land and takes Ruth as his wife after the nearer kinsman declines. Ruth bears Obed, the grandfather of David, placing this Moabite woman in the royal and messianic lineage.

Key Verses

  • v.1 — "Then went Boaz up to the gate, and sat him down there: and, behold, the kinsman of whom Boaz spake came by." — Boaz acts immediately — Naomi was right (3:18). And the nearer kinsman "came by" — another instance of providence wearing the mask of coincidence. God arranges the encounter Boaz needs.
  • v.4 — "And I thought to advertise thee, saying, Buy it before the inhabitants, and before the elders of my people. If thou wilt redeem it, redeem it: but if thou wilt not redeem it, then tell me, that I may know: for there is none to redeem it beside thee; and I am after thee." — Boaz's legal precision: he gives the nearer kinsman full opportunity. No manipulation, no shortcuts. He trusts that God's purpose will prevail through proper process.
  • v.5 — "Then said Boaz, What day thou buyest the field of the hand of Naomi, thou must buy it also of Ruth the Moabitess, the wife of the dead, to raise up the name of the dead upon his inheritance." — The full cost of redemption revealed. The land is attractive; the obligation is costly. True redemption always costs the redeemer something. It is never merely acquisition — it is sacrifice.
  • v.6 — "And the kinsman said, I cannot redeem it for myself, lest I mar mine own inheritance: redeem thou my right to thyself; for I cannot redeem it." — The nearer kinsman counts the cost and declines. He is unwilling to diminish his own estate for the sake of the dead. His refusal is not sinful but it is self- preserving. Boaz's willingness to bear the cost is what makes him the true redeemer.
  • v.10 — "Moreover Ruth the Moabitess, the wife of Mahlon, have I purchased to be my wife, to raise up the name of the dead upon his inheritance, that the name of the dead be not cut off from among his brethren, and from the gate of his place: ye are witnesses this day." — Boaz's declaration is both legal and theological. He redeems the land, takes the wife, and commits to preserving the dead man's name. This is the go'el in full action — a picture of Christ who redeems, takes His bride, and gives new life to the dead.
  • v.14 — "And the women said unto Naomi, Blessed be the LORD, which hath not left thee this day without a kinsman, that his name may be famous in Israel." — The community recognizes God's faithfulness. Naomi, who said God had "testified against" her (1:21), now receives testimony of His blessing. God's hesed has not failed.
  • v.15 — "And he shall be unto thee a restorer of thy life, and a nourisher of thine old age: for thy daughter in law, which loveth thee, which is better to thee than seven sons, hath born him." — "Better than seven sons" — the ultimate superlative in Israelite culture. Seven = completeness; sons = security and legacy. Ruth surpasses the ideal. And through her, Naomi's life is restored — the bitter has become sweet.
  • v.17 — "And the women her neighbours gave it a name, saying, There is a son born to Naomi; and they called his name Obed: he is the father of Jesse, the father of David." — The book's final revelation: this child is David's grandfather. The entire story — famine, Moab, death, loyalty, gleaning, the threshing floor, redemption — has been leading to David. And beyond David, to Christ.
  • v.22 — "And Obed begat Jesse, and Jesse begat David." — The final word of the book is "David." Everything in Ruth points forward to the king. And the king points forward to the King of Kings. The Moabite widow's great-grandson will sit on Israel's throne. The outsider's descendant will rule God's people.

Authorship & Background

Author: Unknown, traditionally attributed to Samuel. Ruth is set 'in the
days when the judges ruled' (1:1) but provides a beautiful contrast to
the moral chaos of Judges. While Judges ends with 'every man did that
which was right in his own eyes,' Ruth shows ordinary people living
faithfully in dark times. The book demonstrates God's providence, the
kinsman-redeemer (go'el) concept, and the inclusion of Gentiles in God's
plan. Ruth, a Moabite woman, becomes the great-grandmother of David and
an ancestor of Christ (Matthew 1:5).

Historical Context: Chapter 4 brings the book to its climax and
resolution at the city gate — the place of legal transactions, judicial
decisions, and public witness in ancient Israel. Boaz wastes no time:
"Then went Boaz up to the gate, and sat him down there" (v.1). The gate
was the ancient equivalent of a courthouse — elders sat there to witness
contracts, settle disputes, and validate transactions. Boaz assembles
ten elders as witnesses (v.2) — later Jewish tradition required ten men
(a "minyan") for legal proceedings, and this passage may be the origin
of that practice. The legal negotiation that follows is masterful. Boaz
first presents only the land transaction: Naomi is selling Elimelech's
parcel (v.3). The nearer kinsman agrees to buy it — land acquisition is
profitable. Then Boaz reveals the full obligation: "What day thou buyest
the field of the hand of Naomi, thou must buy it also of Ruth the
Moabitess, the wife of the dead, to raise up the name of the dead upon
his inheritance" (v.5). The land comes with Ruth — and with the
obligation to produce an heir in Mahlon's name. This changes everything.
The heir would inherit the land, meaning the kinsman would pay for
property that would ultimately pass to another man's line. "I cannot
redeem it for myself, lest I mar mine own inheritance" (v.6). The nearer
kinsman's refusal is economically rational but spiritually revealing: he
prioritizes his own inheritance over his obligation to the dead. He is
willing to acquire land but not to sacrifice for family. The sandal
ceremony (v.7-8) — removing and giving one's sandal — confirmed the
transfer of rights. This custom was already ancient by the narrator's
time ("this was the manner in former time in Israel"). Boaz then makes
his public declaration: he has bought all that belonged to Elimelech,
Chilion, and Mahlon, and he has acquired Ruth as his wife "to raise up
the name of the dead upon his inheritance" (v.10). The people and elders
respond with a remarkable blessing: "The LORD make the woman that is
come into thine house like Rachel and like Leah, which two did build the
house of Israel" (v.11). Ruth the Moabitess is compared to the
matriarchs of Israel — the outsider is placed alongside the founders.
They also invoke Perez — the son of Judah and Tamar (v.12) — another
irregular union that produced the line of Judah. The book's conclusion
is swift and beautiful: Boaz takes Ruth, the LORD gives conception, and
Obed is born. The women of Bethlehem bless Naomi: "Blessed be the LORD,
which hath not left thee this day without a kinsman" (v.14). The woman
who said "call me Mara" now holds a grandson in her arms. Ruth is
declared "better to thee than seven sons" (v.15) — the highest possible
praise, since seven sons represented the ideal family. The genealogy
(vv.18-22) traces ten generations from Perez to David, placing Ruth's
story in the grand narrative of God's redemptive plan. This Moabite
widow, who chose Israel's God on a road in Moab, becomes the great-
grandmother of Israel's greatest king — and an ancestor of Christ.

The Gate Transaction (vv.1-6): Boaz's legal strategy is deliberate. He
presents the land first because it is attractive — the nearer kinsman
sees profit. Only then does he reveal the full cost: Ruth and the
obligation to raise an heir for the dead. The kinsman's refusal — "lest
I mar mine own inheritance" — reveals his values. He will not risk his
estate for the sake of his dead relative's name. His unnamed status in
the text is telling: the man who refused to preserve another's name has
his own name forgotten by Scripture. He is simply "such a one" (KJV) or
"friend" (ESV) — anonymous forever. Meanwhile, Boaz, who sacrificed to
preserve Mahlon's name, has his own name preserved eternally in the
genealogy of Christ.

The Sandal Ceremony (vv.7-8): The narrator explains this custom for
readers unfamiliar with it — suggesting the book was written after the
practice had fallen out of common use. Removing the sandal symbolized
relinquishing one's right to "walk on" (possess) the land. Compare
Deuteronomy 25:9-10, where the widow removes the refusing kinsman's
sandal in disgrace — here the transfer is voluntary and without shame.
The sandal is both a legal instrument (confirming the transaction) and
a symbol (transferring the right to walk the land).

Boaz's Declaration and the People's Blessing (vv.9-12): Boaz's public
statement is comprehensive: he has bought the land AND acquired Ruth as
wife, with the explicit purpose of raising up the dead man's name. The
community responds with witness ("We are witnesses") and blessing. The
blessing invokes Rachel and Leah — the mothers of the twelve tribes —
placing Ruth in their company. It invokes Perez — born of Judah and
Tamar's unusual union — connecting Ruth's story to another instance
where God worked through unconventional means to preserve the line of
promise. The blessing is prophetic: Ruth will indeed "build the house
of Israel" through David's line.

Birth of Obed and Restoration of Naomi (vv.13-17): "The LORD gave her
conception" (v.13) — after ten childless years with Mahlon in Moab, Ruth
conceives immediately with Boaz. The narrator attributes this directly
to God. The women's blessing to Naomi (vv.14-15) completes her
restoration: she who was "empty" (1:21) now has a redeemer, a grandson,
and a daughter-in-law "better than seven sons." Naomi takes the child
to her bosom and becomes his nurse (v.16) — the grandmother who thought
God had abandoned her now holds the future king's grandfather. The
neighbors name the child Obed ("servant/worshipper") and declare: "There
is a son born to Naomi" — legally, the child continues Elimelech's line
through Mahlon, fulfilling the purpose of redemption.

The Genealogy of David (vv.18-22): The book closes with ten generations:
Perez — Hezron — Ram — Amminadab — Nahshon — Salmon — Boaz — Obed —
Jesse — David. This genealogy connects Ruth's story to the larger
narrative of God's plan. Perez was born of Judah and Tamar (Genesis 38)
— another story of levirate obligation, unconventional means, and God's
preservation of the messianic line. Nahshon was the leader of Judah
during the wilderness wandering (Numbers 2:3). Salmon (Boaz's father)
married Rahab according to Matthew 1:5 — another Gentile woman of
faith. The genealogy demonstrates that God's redemptive plan runs
through ordinary people, unexpected unions, and faithful outsiders. It
culminates in David — and beyond David, in Christ (Matthew 1:5-6).

Map & Geography

  • The city gate of Bethlehem: The legal and commercial center where elders sat and transactions were publicly witnessed
  • Bethlehem ("house of bread"): Now fulfilled — famine has turned to harvest, emptiness to fullness
  • Ruth's journey is complete: from Moab to Bethlehem's fields to the city gate to the lineage of David and Christ

Glossary

  • The Gate — The main entrance to an ancient walled city, typically a complex structure with chambers, benches, and open areas. It served as courthouse, marketplace, and public forum. Elders sat at the gate to conduct business, witness transactions, and render judgments (Deuteronomy 21:19; Proverbs 31:23). "In the gate" meant "in public, with legal authority."
  • Go'el — Hebrew = kinsman-redeemer. The near relative with (Kinsman- responsibility to: (1) redeem family land sold due to Redeemer) poverty (Leviticus 25:25); (2) redeem relatives from
  • slavery (Leviticus 25:47 — 49); (3) avenge blood (Numbers 35:19); (4) marry a kinsman's widow to preserve his name (Ruth 4:5,10). The go'el must be: (a) a near relative, (b) able to redeem (having sufficient resources), (c) willing to redeem. Christ fulfills all three: He became our kinsman through incarnation, He is able to save
  • completely, and He is willing — "I will redeem you."
  • Obed — Hebrew "Oved" = servant, worshipper. The son of Boaz and Ruth, grandfather of David. His name suggests one
  • who serves God — appropriate for the ancestor of Israel's greatest king and of the Messiah who came "not to be ministered unto, but to minister" (Matthew 20:28).
  • Perez/Pharez — Hebrew "Perets" = breach, breaking through. Son of Judah and Tamar (Genesis 38:29). His birth involved breaking through ahead of his twin Zerah. The line of Perez became the royal line of Judah, leading to David and to Christ. The genealogy of Ruth begins with Perez, connecting Ruth's story to the larger narrative of Judah's line.
  • Ephrathah — An older name for Bethlehem or its surrounding district (Genesis 35:19; Micah 5:2). "Ephrathite" = a native of this region. The name may derive from "parah" (to be fruitful). Micah 5:2 prophesies that the Messiah will come from "Bethlehem Ephratah" — connecting Ruth's story directly to messianic prophecy.
  • Sandal — The removal and transfer of a sandal confirmed the Ceremony relinquishing of property rights. The sandal
  • symbolized the right to walk on (possess) land — giving it to another transferred that right. In Deuteronomy 25:9-10, the widow removes the refusing kinsman's sandal
  • in disgrace. Here in Ruth 4:7 — 8, the transfer is voluntary and dignified. The narrator explains the custom, indicating it was no longer practiced in his day.
  • Levirate — From Latin "levir" = husband's brother. The obligation
  • Obligation (Deuteronomy 25:5 — 10) to marry a deceased brother's childless widow and produce an heir in the dead man's name. The firstborn son of this union would legally be considered the dead man's son, inheriting his property and continuing his name. In Ruth, the principle extends
  • beyond brothers to the broader kinsman — redeemer role.

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): enduringword.com Guzik emphasizes the typology of Boaz as kinsman-redeemer pointing to Christ: willing to pay the full price, taking the bride, raising up new life from death. He notes that the unnamed kinsman represents the Law — which identifies the obligation but cannot fulfill it — while Boaz represents grace, which both identifies and accomplishes redemption.
  • Charles Spurgeon: "The nearer kinsman said, 'I cannot redeem it, lest I mar mine own inheritance.' He counted the cost and found it too great. But Boaz counted the cost and found it worth paying. Here is the difference between law and grace. The law says, 'I cannot redeem — the cost is too high, the obligation too great, the sacrifice too much.' But grace says, 'I will redeem. I will pay the price. I will take the bride. I will raise up the dead man's name.' Christ is our Boaz. He did not say, 'I cannot redeem, lest I mar mine own inheritance.' He said, 'I will redeem, though it cost me everything.' He bought us with His blood. He took us as His bride. He gives us His name. And the inheritance He gains is not diminished but multiplied — for we are His inheritance, and He is ours."

Translations

  • v.1 → KJV: "Ho, such a one! turn aside, sit down here" → ESV: "Turn aside, friend; sit down here" — Hebrew "surah sh'vah-poh p'loni almoni." The phrase "p'loni almoni" is deliberately vague — it means "so-and-so" or "a certain one." The narrator intentionally withholds the kinsman's name. KJV's "such a one" captures this vagueness; ESV's "friend" is more natural English but loses the deliberate anonymity. The man who refused to preserve another's name has his own name erased from the record.
  • v.3 → KJV: "selleth a parcel of land" → ESV: "is selling the parcel of land" — Hebrew "mak'rah chelqat hassadeh." The tense is debated: is Naomi currently selling, or offering to sell? The Hebrew participle can indicate either ongoing action or imminent intention. The legal situation is complex: as a widow, Naomi may have had the right to sell her deceased husband's land, or she may be transferring the right of redemption.
  • v.4 → KJV: "I thought to advertise thee" → ESV: "So I thought I would tell you of it" — Hebrew "amarti egleh oznekha." Literally "I said I would uncover your ear." "Galah ozen" = to uncover the ear, meaning to reveal, disclose, inform. KJV's "advertise" (make known publicly) captures the formal, legal disclosure; ESV simplifies to "tell you." The idiom suggests removing something that blocks hearing — making the information clearly known.
  • v.5 → KJV: "thou must buy it also of Ruth the Moabitess" → ESV: "you also acquire Ruth the Moabite" — Hebrew "u-me'et Rut hammo'aviyyah... qanitah." There is a significant textual variant here. The Hebrew Ketiv (written text) reads "qanithi" (I have acquired) while the Qere (read text) has "qanitah" (you acquire). KJV follows the Qere; ESV also follows the Qere. The difference affects whether Boaz is saying "you must also acquire Ruth" or "I have already acquired Ruth." Most translations follow the Qere.
  • v.6 → KJV: "lest I mar mine own inheritance" → ESV: "lest I impair my own inheritance" — Hebrew "pen-ashchit et-nachalati." "Shachat" = to destroy, corrupt, ruin, mar. The kinsman fears that producing an heir for Mahlon would divide his estate — the child would inherit Elimelech's land (which the kinsman would have purchased) and potentially claim a share of the kinsman's own property. KJV "mar" and ESV "impair" both capture the sense of damage to his financial position.
  • v.7 → KJV: "a man plucked off his shoe" → ESV: "the one drew off his sandal" — Hebrew "shalaf ish na'alo." "Na'al" = sandal, shoe. The sandal symbolized the right to walk on (possess) land. Its removal and transfer confirmed the relinquishing of that right. The narrator's explanatory note ("this was the manner in former time") indicates this custom was no longer practiced when the book was written — suggesting a date well after the events.
  • v.11 → KJV: "do thou worthily in Ephratah, and be famous in Bethlehem" → ESV: "May you act worthily in Ephrathah and be renowned in Bethlehem" — Hebrew "va'aseh-chayil b'Efratah uq'ra-shem b'Veit Lachem." "Aseh chayil" = do worthily, act with valor, prosper. "Q'ra shem" = call a name, be renowned. Ephrathah and Bethlehem are the same place (or Ephrathah is the district containing Bethlehem). The blessing is prophetic: Boaz's name will indeed be renowned — in the genealogy of David and of Christ.
  • v.12 → KJV: "the house of Pharez, whom Tamar bare unto Judah" → ESV: "the house of Perez, whom Tamar bore to Judah" — "Pharez" (KJV) / "Perez" (ESV) — Hebrew "Perets" = breach, breaking through. The reference to Tamar and Judah (Genesis 38) is deliberate: another story where the line of promise was preserved through unconventional means, involving a woman who took bold action to secure her rights within the levirate system.

Reflection

  • 1. True redemption costs the redeemer (vv.5-6,10). The nearer kinsman was willing to acquire land (profit) but not to bear the full cost of redemption (sacrifice). Boaz paid the price willingly. Christ, our Kinsman-Redeemer, did not count the cost too great. He took our debt, our shame, our death — and gave us His inheritance. Redemption is never free for the redeemer; it is always free for the redeemed.
  • 2. The one who preserves another's name has his own name preserved (vv.1,10,22). The unnamed kinsman refused to preserve Mahlon's name — and Scripture erases his own. Boaz sacrificed to preserve the dead man's name — and his own name stands forever in the genealogy of Christ. Those who seek to save their life lose it; those who lose their life for others find it (Matthew 16:25).
  • 3. God works through the outsider (vv.11-12,17). Ruth the Moabitess is blessed alongside Rachel and Leah. A woman from a cursed nation (Deuteronomy 23:3) enters the line of David and of Christ. God's grace demolishes every boundary that human religion constructs. No one is too far outside for God to bring in. The genealogy of Christ includes Tamar, Rahab, Ruth, and Bathsheba — women whose stories defy conventional expectations.
  • 4. God fills what He empties (vv.14-16). Naomi went out full and came back empty (1:21). Now she holds a grandson — a "restorer of life." God's emptying is never His final word. What He takes away in one season, He restores — often with greater abundance — in another. The bitter becomes sweet. The empty becomes full. But the filling often comes through paths we would never have chosen.
  • 5. Ordinary faithfulness produces extraordinary legacy (vv.17-22). Ruth gleaned barley. Boaz managed his fields. Naomi counseled her daughter-in-law. None of them knew they were building the lineage of David and of Christ. Faithfulness in small, daily, unglamorous tasks produces outcomes we cannot foresee. The significance of our obedience is rarely visible in our lifetime. Trust that God is weaving your ordinary faithfulness into His extraordinary plan.
