Formal equivalent (1901). Very literal, precise. Good for word studies.

Study Bible with extensive translator notes. Balances accuracy and readability.

Psalms — Chapter 74

1O God, why do you cast us off forever? Why does your anger smoke against the sheep of your pasture?

2Remember your congregation, which you have purchased of old, which you have redeemed to be the tribe of your heritage! Remember Mount Zion, where you have dwelt.

3Direct your steps to the perpetual ruins; the enemy has destroyed everything in the sanctuary!

4Your foes have roared in the midst of your meeting place; they set up their own signs for signs.

5They were like those who swing axes in a forest of trees.

6And all its carved wood they broke down with hatchets and hammers.

7They set your sanctuary on fire; they profaned the dwelling place of your name, bringing it down to the ground.

8They said to themselves, “We will utterly subdue them”; they burned all the meeting places of God in the land.

9We do not see our signs; there is no longer any prophet, and there is none among us who knows how long.

10How long, O God, is the foe to scoff? Is the enemy to revile your name forever?

11Why do you hold back your hand, your right hand? Take it from the fold of your garment and destroy them!

12Yet God my King is from of old, working salvation in the midst of the earth.

13You divided the sea by your might; you broke the heads of the sea monsters on the waters.

14You crushed the heads of Leviathan; you gave him as food for the creatures of the wilderness.

15You split open springs and brooks; you dried up ever-flowing streams.

16Yours is the day, yours also the night; you have established the heavenly lights and the sun.

17You have fixed all the boundaries of the earth; you have made summer and winter.

18Remember this, O LORD, how the enemy scoffs, and a foolish people reviles your name.

19Do not deliver the soul of your dove to the wild beasts; do not forget the life of your poor forever.

20Have regard for the covenant, for the dark places of the land are full of the habitations of violence.

21Let not the downtrodden turn back in shame; let the poor and needy praise your name.

22Arise, O God, defend your cause; remember how the foolish scoff at you all the day!

23Do not forget the clamor of your foes, the uproar of those who rise against you, which goes up continually!

Summary

A communal lament — the psalmist cries over the destroyed temple (perhaps by Babylon): enemies roar in God's meeting place, no prophet remains; yet God is king from of old who crushed Leviathan and divided the sea.

Key Verses

  • v.1 — "O God, why hast thou cast us off for ever? why doth thine anger smoke against the sheep of thy pasture?" (KJV) — The agonizing "why" and "forever." When suffering feels permanent, when God seems actively hostile toward His own people. The metaphor: even angry shepherds don't abandon their sheep.
  • v.9 — "We see not our signs: there is no more any prophet: neither is there among us any that knoweth how long." (KJV) — Triple absence: no signs (evidences of God's presence), no prophet (voice from God), no knowledge (of when it will end). Total prophetic silence. This is the darkest spiritual condition: divine silence.
  • v.12 — "For God is my King of old, working salvation in the midst of the earth." (KJV) — The pivot. Despite present devastation, God's track record remains. He IS king. He HAS worked salvation. Past faithfulness is the ground for present hope.
  • v.20 — "Have respect unto the covenant: for the dark places of the earth are full of the habitations of cruelty." (KJV) — The appeal to covenant. God bound Himself by promise. The psalmist calls on God's covenant obligation — not personal merit but divine commitment.

Authorship & Background

Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Community Lament Psalm
Attributed Author: Asaph (or a descendant in the Asaphite guild)
Key Themes: Destruction of the temple, God's apparent absence,
"how long?", covenant faithfulness appealed to, God's power over
creation, cry for divine intervention

Historical Context: This psalm describes the destruction of the
sanctuary — most likely the Babylonian destruction of Solomon's
temple in 586 BC (2 Kings 25:8-9), though some suggest the
Philistine destruction of Shiloh or Antiochus IV's desecration
in 167 BC. The details — axes breaking carved woodwork, fire in
the sanctuary, burning all meeting places, no prophet — best fit
the Babylonian destruction. If Asaph wrote this prophetically or
if an Asaphite descendant composed it later using the family name
is debated. The psalm's anguish is raw: God seems to have
permanently rejected His people. Yet the psalmist appeals to God's
covenant, His creation power, and His honor.

Structure:
- Why Have You Cast Us Off? (vv.1-3)
- Description of Temple Destruction (vv.4-8)
- No Signs, No Prophet, No Knowledge (vv.9-11)
- God's Power Recalled (vv.12-17)
- Appeal to God's Honor and Covenant (vv.18-23)

Cross-References

  • v.1 → Psalm 44:23-24 — "Awake, why sleepest thou, O Lord?... Wherefore hidest thou thy face?" Same apparent divine abandonment.
  • v.7 → 2 Kings 25:8-9 — "He burnt the house of the LORD." The historical fulfillment of this psalm's description.
  • v.9 → 1 Samuel 3:1 — "The word of the LORD was precious in those days; there was no open vision." Prophetic silence.
  • v.13-14 → Exodus 14:21-28 — The sea divided, the enemies destroyed. God's creation power deployed for salvation.
  • v.14 → Isaiah 27:1 — "The LORD with his sore and great and strong sword shall punish leviathan." Same chaos-monster imagery.
  • v.20 → Genesis 15:18 — God's covenant with Abraham. The covenant appealed to here.

Translations

  • v.5-6 → KJV: "A man was famous according as he had lifted up axes upon the thick trees. But now they break down the carved work" → ESV: "They were like those who swing axes in a forest of trees. And all its carved wood they broke down" — The image: what was once the work of skilled craftsmen is now treated like trees to be chopped down. Axe-wielding destroyers treat holy artistry like firewood.
  • v.13 → KJV: "thou brakest the heads of the dragons in the waters" → ESV: "you broke the heads of the sea monsters on the waters" — "Dragons" (tanninim) = sea monsters/chaos creatures. This creation-combat imagery draws on ancient Near Eastern mythology, which Israel used poetically to describe God's sovereignty over all powers of chaos.
  • v.19 → KJV: "O deliver not the soul of thy turtledove" → ESV: "Do not deliver the soul of your dove" — Israel as God's vulnerable dove amid the "wild beasts" (predatory nations). A tender image of helplessness needing divine protection.
  • ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB) - v.1 — NET: "Why, O God, have you permanently rejected us?" — NET makes "forever" explicit as "permanently," interpretively clarifying the Hebrew's ambiguity about whether this is a question or accusation.
  • - v.8 — NET: "We will oppress all of them" → WEB: "We will crush them completely" → KJV: "Let us destroy them together" — NET and WEB interpret the Hebrew as total annihilation language; KJV's "together" is more ambiguous (together in time or in totality?).
  • - v.11 — NET: "Why do you remain inactive? Intervene and destroy him" — NET significantly paraphrases what KJV renders literally as "Why withdrawest thou thy hand...pluck it out of thy bosom." NET loses the vivid image of God's hand hidden in His robe but gains clarity.
  • - v.14 — NET: "you fed him to the people who live along the coast" → KJV: "gavest him to be meat to the people inhabiting the wilderness" — The Hebrew is debated; NET reads "coast-dwellers" while KJV → ASV read "wilderness-dwellers." Different understanding of the same Hebrew word.
  • - v.18 — ASV: "O Jehovah" → WEB: "Yahweh" → KJV → NET: "O LORD" — The divine name YHWH rendered differently: ASV uses the traditional English "Jehovah," WEB uses the scholarly transliteration "Yahweh," while KJV and NET use the conventional "LORD."
  • - v.20 — ASV: "habitations of violence" → KJV: "habitations of cruelty" → NET: "places where violence rules" → WEB: "haunts of violence" — All capture the Hebrew חָמָס (chamas), but NET's interpretive "where violence rules" adds personification absent from the Hebrew.
  • - v.19 — ASV: "wild beast" → NET: "wild animal" → KJV: "multitude of the wicked" — KJV reads the Hebrew differently here, understanding it as wicked people rather than a wild creature. ASV, WEB, and NET all read "wild beast/animal," which better parallels "dove."

Reflection

  • 1. "How long?" is a valid prayer (v.10). When God seems absent and silent, asking "how long?" is not faithlessness — it is faith reaching for God in the dark. The question assumes God CAN answer; it just doesn't know when He will.
  • 2. God's past faithfulness grounds present hope (v.12). When you cannot see God in the present, remember Him in the past. He divided seas. He broke monsters. He established day and night. That God has not changed.
  • 3. Appeal to covenant, not merit (v.20). When praying through devastation, don't lean on "I deserve better." Lean on "You promised." God's covenant commitments outlast every disaster.
  • 4. Divine silence is not divine absence. The psalm describes no signs, no prophet, no knowledge — yet the psalmist still prays. If God were truly absent, prayer would be impossible. The very act of lament proves His presence.
