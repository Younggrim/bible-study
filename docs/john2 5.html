John — Chapter 2

1On the third day there was a wedding at Cana in Galilee, and the mother of Jesus was there.

2Jesus also was invited to the wedding with his disciples.

3When the wine ran out, the mother of Jesus said to him, “They have no wine.”

4And Jesus said to her, “Woman, what does this have to do with me? My hour has not yet come.”

5His mother said to the servants, “Do whatever he tells you.”

6Now there were six stone water jars there for the Jewish rites of purification, each holding twenty or thirty gallons.

7Jesus said to the servants, “Fill the jars with water.” And they filled them up to the brim.

8And he said to them, “Now draw some out and take it to the master of the feast.” So they took it.

9When the master of the feast tasted the water now become wine, and did not know where it came from (though the servants who had drawn the water knew), the master of the feast called the bridegroom

10and said to him, “Everyone serves the good wine first, and when people have drunk freely, then the poor wine. But you have kept the good wine until now.”

11This, the first of his signs, Jesus did at Cana in Galilee, and manifested his glory. And his disciples believed in him.

12After this he went down to Capernaum, with his mother and his brothers and his disciples, and they stayed there for a few days.

13The Passover of the Jews was at hand, and Jesus went up to Jerusalem.

14In the temple he found those who were selling oxen and sheep and pigeons, and the money-changers sitting there.

15And making a whip of cords, he drove them all out of the temple, with the sheep and oxen. And he poured out the coins of the money-changers and overturned their tables.

16And he told those who sold the pigeons, “Take these things away; do not make my Father’s house a house of trade.”

17His disciples remembered that it was written, “Zeal for your house will consume me.”

18So the Jews said to him, “What sign do you show us for doing these things?”

19Jesus answered them, “Destroy this temple, and in three days I will raise it up.”

20The Jews then said, “It has taken forty-six years to build this temple, and will you raise it up in three days?”

21But he was speaking about the temple of his body.

22When therefore he was raised from the dead, his disciples remembered that he had said this, and they believed the Scripture and the word that Jesus had spoken.

23Now when he was in Jerusalem at the Passover Feast, many believed in his name when they saw the signs that he was doing.

24But Jesus on his part did not entrust himself to them, because he knew all people

25and needed no one to bear witness about man, for he himself knew what was in man.

Summary

Jesus performs His first sign by turning water into wine at the wedding in Cana, then cleanses the temple in Jerusalem and speaks of raising "this temple" in three days — pointing to His resurrection.

Key Verses

v.5
"Do whatever he tells you." (ESV) — Mary's instruction to the servants. One of the most practical statements of faith in Scripture.
v.11
"This, the first of his signs, Jesus did at Cana in Galilee, and manifested his glory. And his disciples believed in him." (ESV) — The purpose of miracles: to reveal Christ's glory and produce faith.
v.16
"Do not make my Father's house a house of trade." (ESV) — Jesus' zeal for pure worship and His claim of unique relationship with God ("my Father").
v.19
"Destroy this temple, and in three days I will raise it up." (ESV) — A veiled prophecy of His death and resurrection, misunderstood by His hearers.
v.25
"He himself knew what was in man." (ESV) — Jesus' omniscience — He sees through superficial belief to the heart.

Cross-References

  • v.5 — Genesis 41:55 — Pharaoh tells the Egyptians "Go to Joseph; do whatever he tells you." A parallel to Mary's instruction to trust and obey. v.11 — John 20:30-31 — Signs are recorded so that we may believe. v.16 — Psalm 69:9 — "Zeal for your house has consumed me" — quoted in v.17. v.17 — Psalm 69:9 — Fulfilled in Jesus' passion for the Temple. v.19 — Matthew 26:61 — This statement used against Jesus at His trial. v.19 — 1 Corinthians 3:16 — Believers are now the temple of God. v.21 — John 10:17-18 — Jesus lays down His life and takes it up again. v.24 — Jeremiah 17:9-10 — God knows the heart of man. v.25 — Psalm 139:1-4 — God searches and knows us completely.

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/john-2/ Guzik addresses the meaning of "mine hour is not yet come" (v.4), the significance of the six stone waterpots, and the two Temple cleansings (John's early cleansing vs. the synoptic Gospels' later one).
  • Charles Spurgeon: Sermon: "Obedience to Christ" on v.5 (MTP Vol. 12, No. 680) "Whatever He says to you, do it — not part of it, not a modified version of it, not what you think He probably means — but what He says. This is the secret of blessing: simple, prompt, complete obedience." Sermon: "The Zeal of Thine House" (MTP Vol. 9, No. 496) Spurgeon on the Temple cleansing: "Christ's anger was not the anger of wounded pride but of wounded love — love for His Father's honor, love for the worshippers being robbed, love for the Gentiles whose court had been turned into a market."

Translations

  • v.4 — KJV: "Woman, what have I to do with thee?" / ESV: "Woman, what does this have to do with me?" — Greek "ti emoi kai soi" — A Hebrew idiom meaning "what is that to us?" or "that is not our concern." It is not disrespectful — "Woman" (gune) was a respectful address in Greek. Jesus is gently indicating His actions are governed by the Father's timing, not family requests. [ASV/NET/WEB]: NET: "Woman, why are you saying this to me? My time has not yet come" — "Woman" (gynai) is respectful address in Greek (not rude). KJV: "Woman, what have I to do with thee?" WEB: "Woman, what does that have to do with you and me?" ASV: "Woman, what have I to do with thee?"
  • v.6 — KJV: "two or three firkins apiece" / ESV: "twenty or thirty gallons" — Greek "metretas" — KJV uses the archaic "firkins"; ESV converts to modern gallons. Each pot held 20-30 gallons, meaning Jesus created 120-180 gallons of wine — an extravagant abundance, a picture of God's generosity. [ASV/NET/WEB]: NET: "six stone water jars were sitting there, meant for Jewish ceremonial washing" — "meant for" explains their purpose. WEB: "for the Jews' purification." KJV: "after the manner of the purifying of the Jews." Jesus transforms vessels of ritual law into vessels of celebratory wine.
  • v.10 — KJV: "when men have well drunk" / ESV: "when people have drunk freely" — Greek "methusthosin" — Can mean drunk or simply having drunk freely. The point is that the best wine was saved for last — opposite of human custom.
  • v.11 — KJV: "This beginning of miracles" / ESV: "This, the first of his signs" — Greek "semeion" — KJV uses "miracles"; ESV uses "signs." John consistently uses "signs" to indicate miracles that point to deeper truth about Jesus' identity. [ASV/NET/WEB]: NET: "Jesus did this as the first of his miraculous signs" — John calls miracles "signs" (sēmeia) — they point beyond themselves to who Jesus is. WEB: "the first of his signs." KJV: "This beginning of miracles." John's theology of signs is distinctive.
  • v.15 — KJV: "a scourge of small cords" / ESV: "a whip of cords" — Both equivalent. Jesus deliberately made the whip — this was not an impulsive act but a deliberate, righteous response.
  • v.16 — KJV: "make not my Father's house an house of merchandise" / ESV: "do not make my Father's house a house of trade" — Both equivalent. Jesus claims unique relationship with God ("my Father") and zeal for pure worship.
  • v.19 — KJV: "Destroy this temple, and in three days I will raise it up" / ESV: "Destroy this temple, and in three days I will raise it up" — Both identical. [ASV/NET/WEB]: NET: "Destroy this temple and in three days I will raise it up" — "I will raise it" — Jesus claims to raise Himself. WEB: "Destroy this temple, and in three days I will raise it up." All versions preserve the ambiguity that confused His hearers but was clarified by His resurrection (v.22).
  • v.24 — KJV: "Jesus did not commit himself unto them" / ESV: "Jesus on his part did not entrust himself to them" — Greek "episteusen" — Same root as "believe" (pisteuo). The wordplay is intentional: they "believed" in Him (v.23), but He did not "believe in/entrust" Himself to them. [ASV/NET/WEB]: NET: "Jesus would not entrust himself to them, because he knew all people" — "entrust himself" (episteusen) — same Greek word as "believe." They believed in Him (v.23) but He didn't believe/entrust Himself to them. WEB: "didn't trust himself to them." The wordplay is deliberate.

Reflection

  • 1. The servants obeyed Jesus' instruction to fill the pots with water before they saw any miracle. They acted in faith before they saw results. What step of obedience is God asking you to take right now, before you see the outcome?
  • 2. Jesus' first miracle was at a wedding — an ordinary social event. What does it say about God that He cares about the everyday details and potential embarrassments of our lives?
  • 3. Jesus drove out the money changers with a whip He made Himself. This was deliberate, not impulsive. What is the difference between righteous anger and sinful anger? How do you tell the difference in your own life?
  • 4. Many believed in Jesus because of His miracles (v.23), but Jesus didn't entrust Himself to them because He knew their hearts (v.24-25). What is the difference between being impressed by Jesus and truly trusting Him?
  • 5. The disciples didn't fully understand Jesus' words about the Temple until after the resurrection (v.22). Is there something in Scripture you don't fully understand yet that you're trusting God to make clear in time?
  • 1. Mary's instruction — "Whatever He says to you, do it" (v.5) — is the simplest and most complete description of the Christian life. Where are you currently hearing something from Christ that you haven't fully acted on yet?
  • 2. Jesus saved the best wine for last (v.10). God's provision often exceeds our expectations and comes at the right time, not our time. Where in your life are you struggling to trust God's timing?
  • 3. Jesus' zeal for His Father's house (v.17) shows that righteous anger has a place — when God's honor is at stake. As a man, are there things in your home, workplace, or community that should stir righteous anger in you? What would it look like to act on that?
  • 4. "He knew what was in man" (v.25). Jesus doesn't need your resume or your reputation — He sees exactly who you are. That can be humbling or freeing depending on where you stand with Him. How does being fully known by Christ affect how you approach Him?
