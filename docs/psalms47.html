Summary

An enthronement psalm — 'Clap your hands, all ye people'; God has gone up with a shout, the LORD with the sound of a trumpet; He reigns over the nations and sits upon His holy throne.

Key Verses

Authorship & Background

  • Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed), plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1), Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89, 90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes: worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom, Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought before God.
  • Classification: Hymn of Praise / Enthronement Psalm Attributed Author: Sons of Korah (To the chief Musician, A Psalm for the sons of Korah) Key Themes: Universal kingship of God, exuberant praise, God's ascension, nations gathered to God, joyful worship
  • Historical Context: Psalm 47 is one of the "Enthronement Psalms" (alongside Psalms 93, 95-99) celebrating God's kingship over all the earth. It continues the triumphant tone of Psalm 46 and forms part of a cluster of praise psalms by the Sons of Korah (46-48). While some scholars connect it to the Ark's processional entry into Jerusalem (2 Samuel 6) or to a New Year's celebration of God's sovereignty, its language transcends any single historical occasion.
  • The psalm is remarkable for its universal scope — "all ye people" (v. 1), "all the earth" (v. 2, 7), "the heathen/nations" (v. 8), "the people of the God of Abraham" (v. 9). This is not merely Israelite nationalism but a vision of God reigning over every nation. The mention of "the God of Abraham" (v. 9) rather than "the God of Moses" points to the Abrahamic promise: "in thee shall all families of the earth be blessed" (Genesis 12:3).
  • Verse 5 — "God is gone up with a shout, the LORD with the sound of a trumpet" — has been traditionally associated with Christ's Ascension. The early church connected this psalm to Ascension Day liturgy, understanding God's "going up" as fulfilled in Christ's ascent to the Father's right hand (Acts 1:9-11). The psalm thus looks forward to the day when every knee shall bow and the nations gather under the rule of the ascended King.
  • Structure: - Call to Exuberant Praise (vv. 1-4) - God's Ascension with Shout and Trumpet (vv. 5-7) - God's Universal Reign Over All Nations (vv. 8-9)

Cross-References

  • v. 1 — Isaiah 55:12 — "Ye shall go out with joy... the mountains and the hills shall break forth before you into singing, and all the trees of the field shall clap their hands." Creation itself joins the applause.
  • v. 2 — Psalm 95:3 — "For the LORD is a great God, and a great King above all gods." God's supremacy over all other claimed authorities.
  • v. 5 — Acts 1:9-11 — "While they beheld, he was taken up; and a cloud received him out of their sight." Christ's ascension fulfills the imagery of God going up with a shout.
  • v. 5 — Ephesians 4:8 — "When he ascended up on high, he led captivity captive, and gave gifts unto men." Paul connects ascension to triumph and generosity.
  • v. 7 — 1 Corinthians 14:15 — "I will sing with the spirit, and I will sing with the understanding also." Worship that engages the mind, not just emotion.
  • v. 8 — Revelation 11:15 — "The kingdoms of this world are become the kingdoms of our Lord, and of his Christ; and he shall reign for ever and ever." The ultimate fulfillment of God's universal reign.
  • v. 9 — Galatians 3:28-29 — "If ye be Christ's, then are ye Abraham's seed, and heirs according to the promise." All nations gathered as Abraham's children through faith.
  • v. 9 — Genesis 12:3 — "In thee shall all families of the earth be blessed." The Abrahamic promise of universal blessing fulfilled in the gathering of all peoples to God.

Translations

  • v. 2 — KJV "terrible" vs. ESV "to be feared" — The Hebrew "nora" (נוֹרָא) means awe-inspiring, fearsome, or dreadful. KJV's "terrible" carried this meaning in 1611 (inspiring terror/awe) but now suggests "bad" in modern English. ESV's "to be feared" is clearer for contemporary readers. The word describes reverential dread before God's majesty.
  • v. 4 — KJV "the excellency of Jacob" vs. ESV "the pride of Jacob" — The Hebrew "ga'on" can mean pride, majesty, excellency, or glory. KJV uses the positive sense (excellency — the land as Israel's glorious inheritance); ESV's "pride" can sound negative in English but here means "glory" or "honored possession."
  • v. 6 — KJV "sing praises with understanding" vs. ESV "sing praises with a psalm" — The Hebrew "maskil" (v. 7) is debated. It can mean "with understanding/skill" (KJV) or refer to a specific type of psalm — a "maskil" (ESV). The KJV reading emphasizes intelligent, thoughtful worship; the ESV reading sees a musical term. Both are grammatically possible.
  • v. 9 — KJV "the people of the God of Abraham" vs. ESV "the people of the God of Abraham" — Identical translation. The Hebrew construction is significant: the princes of the peoples are gathered AS the people of Abraham's God — indicating their conversion and inclusion, not mere political submission.
  • ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB) - v.2 — ASV: "Jehovah Most High is terrible" → WEB: "Yahweh Most High is awesome" — The divine name difference is striking: ASV uses "Jehovah," WEB uses "Yahweh," KJV uses "the LORD." WEB's "awesome" updates KJV's archaic "terrible" (awe-inspiring) for modern readers.
  • - v.3 — NET: "He subdued nations beneath us and countries under our feet" — NET uses past tense ("subdued") where KJV uses future ("shall subdue"). This reflects a textual variant: whether God's conquest is a completed historical act or a future promise. ASV → WEB use present tense ("subdues/subdues").
  • - v.4 — NET: "He picked out for us a special land to be a source of pride for Jacob" — NET heavily paraphrases, adding "special land" (not in Hebrew) and rendering "ga'on" as "source of pride." ASV → WEB preserve the more literal "the glory of Jacob" / "the glory of Jacob."
  • - v.5 — ASV: "Jehovah with the sound of a trumpet" → WEB: "Yahweh with the sound of a trumpet" — Both preserve the divine name where KJV has "the LORD." NET paraphrases: "the Lord has ascended amid the blaring of ram's horns," specifying the instrument type.
  • - v.5 — NET: "God has ascended his throne amid loud shouts" — NET adds "his throne" (not in the Hebrew), interpreting the ascension as enthronement. KJV → ASV → WEB simply say "God is gone up with a shout," leaving the destination unstated and more mysterious.
  • - v.7 — NET: "Sing a well-written song" vs. KJV: "sing ye praises with understanding" — NET interprets "maskil" as a genre designation (a well-crafted psalm) rather than a manner of worship (with understanding). This is a significant interpretive choice affecting whether the verse commands intelligent worship or skilled composition.
  • - v.9 — NET: "for God has authority over the rulers of the earth" vs. KJV: "for the shields of the earth belong unto God" — NET interprets "shields" (maginney) as "rulers" (those who shield/protect nations), making the metaphor explicit. ASV → WEB preserve the literal "shields of the earth."

Reflection

  • 1. Worship should be exuberant. This psalm commands clapping, shouting, and singing — physical expressions of joy. While reverence has its place, so does jubilation. If God is truly King of all the earth, our response should sometimes overflow in visible, audible celebration. Subdued worship is not always more spiritual.
  • 2. God's reign is universal, not tribal. "King of all the earth" (v. 7) — not merely King of our denomination, nation, or tradition. His sovereignty extends over every people, culture, and government. This expands our prayer life, our missions vision, and our understanding of God's purposes.
  • 3. Christ ascended in triumph. Verse 5 reminds us that Jesus did not merely depart — He ascended as conquering King, enthroned at the Father's right hand with all authority. His current position is one of supreme authority, and He awaits the day when every knee will bow.
  • 4. Worship should engage the mind. "Sing with understanding" (v. 7) calls us beyond emotional experience to theological comprehension. We should know what we're singing, why we're singing it, and whom we're singing to. Mindless worship dishonors the God who gave us minds.
  • 5. All nations will be gathered. The vision of verse 9 — princes of every people assembled as God's own — fuels hope for global redemption. Every tongue, tribe, and nation has representatives destined for God's kingdom. This is not wishful thinking but prophetic certainty.
