Summary

God prescribes offerings for the fall feasts — Trumpets, Day of Atonement, and the Feast of Tabernacles with its abundant sacrifices.

Key Verses

  • v.1 — "And in the seventh month, on the first day of the month... It is a day for you to blow the trumpets." (ESV) — The trumpet awakens, assembles, and announces. Prophetically: the trumpet of Christ's return (1 Thessalonians 4:16; 1 Corinthians 15:52).
  • v.7 — "And you shall afflict yourselves. You shall not do any work." (ESV) — The Day of Atonement: the only required fast in the Mosaic law. Self-denial accompanies atonement — you cannot celebrate while sin remains unaddressed.
  • v.12 — "you shall keep a feast to the LORD seven days." (ESV) — Tabernacles is the feast of joy — after atonement comes celebration. After the cross comes resurrection life.
  • v.35 — "On the eighth day you shall have a solemn assembly." (ESV) — The eighth day: beyond the seven of creation, pointing to new creation. Jesus chooses this day for His greatest invitation (John 7:37-38).

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): "https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/numbers-29/" target="_blank">https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/numbers-29/ Guzik notes the prophetic significance of the seventh-month festivals: Trumpets points to the rapture/second coming, Atonement to Israel's national repentance, and Tabernacles to the joy of the Millennial Kingdom. He highlights the 70 bulls as possibly representing the nations.
  • Charles Spurgeon: "Seventy bullocks for the seventy nations — Israel standing as priest for the world, offering sacrifice on behalf of all peoples. And on the eighth day, one bullock — Israel alone with her God in intimate communion. First the world is blessed through Israel's intercession; then Israel herself rests in God's presence. So it shall be: through Israel's Messiah, all nations are blessed, and then comes the eternal rest where God and His people dwell together, and the sacrifices cease because the Lamb Himself is the temple's light."

Translations

  • v.1 → KJV: "it is a day of blowing the trumpets unto you" / ESV: "It is a day for you to blow the trumpets" — Hebrew "yom teru'ah yihyeh lakhem." "Teru'ah" means a shout, blast, or alarm — specifically the staccato blasts of the shofar (ram's horn). KJV and ESV both capture the meaning; the Hebrew emphasizes the sound itself rather than the instrument.
  • v.7 → KJV: "ye shall afflict your souls" / ESV: "afflict yourselves" — Hebrew "v'innitem et nafshoteikhem." "Innah" means to humble, deny, afflict. "Nefesh" (soul) here means the whole self — body and soul. Traditionally understood as fasting, but includes all forms of self-denial. ESV's "yourselves" is simpler; KJV's "your souls" preserves the Hebrew "nefesh."
  • v.11 → KJV: "beside the sin offering of atonement" / ESV: "besides the sin offering of atonement" — This refers to the special Yom Kippur goat of Leviticus 16 (the scapegoat ritual). The offerings listed here in Numbers 29 are additional public offerings on the same day.
  • v.12 → KJV: "ye shall keep a feast unto the LORD seven days" / ESV: "you shall keep a feast to the LORD seven days" — Hebrew "v'chagotem chag la-YHWH shiv'at yamim." "Chag" (feast/festival) comes from a root meaning "to dance, to make a pilgrimage." This is the most joyous festival — pure celebration after the solemnity of Atonement.
  • v.35 → KJV: "ye shall have a solemn assembly" / ESV: "you shall have a solemn assembly" — Hebrew "atzeret tihyeh lakhem." "Atzeret" comes from "atzar" — to restrain, hold back, close. It is a closing ceremony — the conclusion of the festival season. The word suggests being held back in God's presence — lingering rather than rushing away.
  • v.39 → KJV: "in your set feasts" / ESV: "at your appointed feasts" — Hebrew "b'mo'adeikhem." "Mo'ed" means appointed time, fixed season, meeting. These are not arbitrary celebrations but divinely scheduled appointments with God. ESV's "appointed" captures the intentionality.

Reflection

  • 1. The trumpet sounds (v.1). Before atonement comes awakening. The shofar blast says: "Wake up! Prepare! God is coming!" Are you spiritually awake, or do you need the trumpet's call to rouse you from complacency?
  • 2. "Afflict your souls" (v.7). The Day of Atonement requires self-denial — you cannot feast while sin is being addressed. There is a time for sober self-examination, fasting, and repentance. When did you last truly "afflict your soul" before God?
  • 3. After atonement, celebration (vv.7→12). The order matters: first the solemnity of Yom Kippur, then the joy of Tabernacles. You cannot celebrate rightly until sin is dealt with. But once it is — rejoice! The Christian life moves from the cross to resurrection joy.
  • 4. Seventy bulls for the nations (vv.13-34). Israel's worship was never meant to be insular. As a priestly nation, they intercede for the world. The church likewise exists not for itself but for the nations. Is your worship self-focused or world-encompassing?
  • 5. The eighth day — intimate communion (vv.35-38). After the grand public festival, one quiet day with God. After the crowds disperse, the individual remains. Corporate worship is essential, but so is personal, intimate time alone with God. Do you linger in His presence after the "festival" ends?
  • 6. "If any man thirst" (John 7:37). Jesus chose the eighth day — the climax of the festival calendar — to offer Himself as the fulfillment of everything the sacrifices pointed toward. Every bull, ram, and lamb in this chapter whispers His name. He is the feast.
