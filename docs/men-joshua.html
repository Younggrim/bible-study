Men of the Bible

Joshua

His Story

Joshua served as Moses' assistant for forty years before being commissioned to lead Israel into the Promised Land. He was one of only two spies — along with Caleb — who believed God could deliver Canaan into Israel's hands when the other ten returned with a report of fear. That faith, tested through decades of wilderness wandering while an entire generation died around him, positioned Joshua to inherit the mantle of leadership when Moses could go no further.

God's charge to Joshua rings across the centuries: "Be strong and courageous. Do not be frightened, and do not be dismayed, for the LORD your God is with you wherever you go." This was not a suggestion but a command — because the task ahead was genuinely terrifying. Fortified cities, giant warriors, and countless enemy nations stood between Israel and their inheritance. Joshua's courage was not the absence of fear but obedience in the face of it.

Under Joshua's leadership, the walls of Jericho fell, the sun stood still at Gibeon, and the land was systematically conquered and divided among the tribes. His final address to Israel — "Choose this day whom you will serve... as for me and my house, we will serve the LORD" — remains one of Scripture's most memorable declarations of devotion. Joshua proved that faithfulness in following prepares us for faithfulness in leading.

Key Scriptures

  • Joshua 1:9 — "Be strong and courageous. Do not be frightened... for the LORD your God is with you."
  • Joshua 24:15 — "Choose this day whom you will serve... as for me and my house, we will serve the LORD."
  • Deuteronomy 31:6 — "Be strong and courageous... the LORD your God goes with you; he will never leave you."
  • Joshua 1:5 — "No man shall be able to stand before you all the days of your life."
  • Numbers 27:18-20 — God commands Moses to commission Joshua, a man in whom is the Spirit.

Lessons from His Life

Joshua teaches us that leadership begins with followership. Before he ever led a nation, he faithfully served Moses for four decades — carrying his sandals, standing guard at the tent of meeting, learning the ways of God through patient apprenticeship. In a culture that celebrates self-made leaders, Joshua reminds us that the best preparation for leading others is learning to follow well. Those who have served under authority understand how to wield it with humility.

Joshua also demonstrates that courage is a choice rooted in theology, not temperament. God did not say "feel brave" but "be strong and courageous" — because He knew that feelings follow faith, not the other way around. Joshua's courage was anchored in God's promise of presence: "I will be with you." When we know that God goes before us, we can face giants and fortified cities without being paralyzed by fear. The command to choose is itself evidence that courage is available to everyone willing to trust.

Reflection Questions

  • What "Promised Land" is God calling you to step into that requires courage?
  • How are you developing as a faithful follower before seeking to lead?
  • What declaration of commitment, like Joshua's "as for me and my house," do you need to make today?
