Ezra — Chapter 10

1While Ezra prayed and made confession, weeping and casting himself down before the house of God, a very great assembly of men, women, and children, gathered to him out of Israel, for the people wept bitterly.

2And Shecaniah the son of Jehiel, of the sons of Elam, addressed Ezra: “We have broken faith with our God and have married foreign women from the peoples of the land, but even now there is hope for Israel in spite of this.

3Therefore let us make a covenant with our God to put away all these wives and their children, according to the counsel of my lord and of those who tremble at the commandment of our God, and let it be done according to the Law.

4Arise, for it is your task, and we are with you; be strong and do it.”

5Then Ezra arose and made the leading priests and Levites and all Israel take an oath that they would do as had been said. So they took the oath.

6Then Ezra withdrew from before the house of God and went to the chamber of Jehohanan the son of Eliashib, where he spent the night, neither eating bread nor drinking water, for he was mourning over the faithlessness of the exiles.

7And a proclamation was made throughout Judah and Jerusalem to all the returned exiles that they should assemble at Jerusalem,

8and that if anyone did not come within three days, by order of the officials and the elders all his property should be forfeited, and he himself banned from the congregation of the exiles.

9Then all the men of Judah and Benjamin assembled at Jerusalem within the three days. It was the ninth month, on the twentieth day of the month. And all the people sat in the open square before the house of God, trembling because of this matter and because of the heavy rain.

10And Ezra the priest stood up and said to them, “You have broken faith and married foreign women, and so increased the guilt of Israel.

11Now then make confession to the LORD, the God of your fathers and do his will. Separate yourselves from the peoples of the land and from the foreign wives.”

12Then all the assembly answered with a loud voice, “It is so; we must do as you have said.

13But the people are many, and it is a time of heavy rain; we cannot stand in the open. Nor is this a task for one day or for two, for we have greatly transgressed in this matter.

14Let our officials stand for the whole assembly. Let all in our cities who have taken foreign wives come at appointed times, and with them the elders and judges of every city, until the fierce wrath of our God over this matter is turned away from us.”

15Only Jonathan the son of Asahel and Jahzeiah the son of Tikvah opposed this, and Meshullam and Shabbethai the Levite supported them.

16Then the returned exiles did so. Ezra the priest selected men, heads of fathers’ houses, according to their fathers’ houses, each of them designated by name. On the first day of the tenth month they sat down to examine the matter;

17and by the first day of the first month they had come to the end of all the men who had married foreign women.

18Now there were found some of the sons of the priests who had married foreign women: Maaseiah, Eliezer, Jarib, and Gedaliah, some of the sons of Jeshua the son of Jozadak and his brothers.

19They pledged themselves to put away their wives, and their guilt offering was a ram of the flock for their guilt.

20Of the sons of Immer: Hanani and Zebadiah.

Summary

The people respond to Ezra's prayer with weeping and repentance, making a covenant to put away their foreign wives. A systematic investigation is conducted over three months, and those guilty of intermarriage separate from their pagan spouses.

Authorship & Background

Author: Ezra the priest and scribe (likely author/compiler). Written approximately 450-400 BC. Ezra covers two returns from Babylonian exile: (1) Zerubbabel's return and Temple rebuilding (538-516 BC, chapters 1-6); (2) Ezra's return and spiritual reform (458 BC, chapters 7-10). Key themes: God's faithfulness to His covenant promises, the sovereignty of God over pagan kings, the centrality of worship/Temple, and the danger of compromise with surrounding peoples.
Historical Context: Chapter 10 records the community's response to Ezra's confessional prayer — collective repentance and reform. The initiative came not from Ezra imposing a solution but from Shecaniah's voluntary proposal (v.2): "yet now there is hope in Israel concerning this thing." The people covenanted to "put away" (divorce) the foreign wives. This action was completed over three months (from the tenth month through the first month) through a systematic legal process — appointed committees examined each case individually. The names of 113 guilty men are recorded: 17 priests, 6 Levites, 1 singer, 3 gatekeepers, and 86 laypeople. The book ends abruptly with this list — no triumphal conclusion, no celebration. The somber ending reflects the painful reality that obedience sometimes requires costly sacrifice. This chapter raises difficult pastoral questions about the divorced women and children. The text does not address their fate in detail, and ancient Near Eastern custom would have required financial provision for divorced wives. The theological point is clear: covenant faithfulness to God must take priority over every other relationship, however painful the cost. The historical setting — a tiny, fragile remnant surrounded by hostile syncretistic peoples — made this a survival issue for the covenant community.

Map & Geography

  • The geographic arc: Babylon → Jerusalem (~900-mile journey through the Fertile Crescent). Two returns: 538 BC under Zerubbabel and 458 BC under Ezra.
  • Jerusalem is the destination — the Temple is rebuilt (516 BC) and worship restored.
  • The journey followed major trade routes along the Euphrates River, through Syria, then south into Judah — a 4-month trek (Ezra 7:9).

Cross-References

  • v.2 → 1 John 1:9 — "If we confess our sins, he is faithful and just to forgive us our sins and to cleanse us from all unrighteousness." (ESV) Shecaniah's "there is hope" anticipates the New Testament promise that confession opens the door to cleansing.
  • v.3 → 2 Corinthians 6:17 — "Therefore go out from their midst, and be separate from them, says the Lord." (ESV) The principle of separation from spiritual compromise carries from Old Testament to New — though the form changes (divorce of pagan spouses vs. separation from unbelieving fellowship practices).
  • v.6 → Joel 2:12-13 — "Return to me with all your heart, with fasting, with weeping, and with mourning; and rend your hearts and not your garments." (ESV) Ezra's continued fasting (v.6) after the assembly showed that his grief was not performative — it was sustained, heartfelt mourning.
  • v.11 → Matthew 3:8 — "Bear fruit in keeping with repentance." (ESV) John the Baptist's demand for visible evidence of genuine repentance echoes Ezra's requirement — true repentance produces concrete action, not merely emotional regret.
  • v.19 → Leviticus 5:15-16 — The guilt/trespass offering required restitution plus a ram. The priests in v.19 offered a ram as their guilt offering — acknowledging their sin formally through the sacrificial system they themselves administered.

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): enduringword.com Guzik acknowledges the difficulty of this chapter while emphasizing its theological necessity for the remnant's survival. He notes that Ezra did not command the divorces — the proposal came from within the community (Shecaniah), and the process was orderly, legal, and case-by-case. He also observes that only 113 men out of 42,000+ were guilty — a small percentage, but in leadership positions that amplified the damage.
  • Charles Spurgeon: "There is hope for Israel — blessed word! Not hope because the sin was light, but hope because God is merciful and repentance is genuine. Shecaniah's faith saw beyond the disaster to the God who forgives. But mark well: hope was attached to action — 'let us make a covenant to put away.' Hope without obedience is presumption. The people wept, confessed, and then acted — they did the hard thing that obedience required. Sin always costs more to leave than we imagine. These men lost wives, perhaps children, certainly reputation. But they gained something worth more: a clear conscience before God and the preservation of the covenant community. Let the Christian learn: whatever must go for Christ's sake — go it must. No relationship, however dear, may stand between the soul and obedience to God."

Reflection

  • 1. There is always hope when there is genuine repentance (v.2). No matter how serious the sin or how deep the compromise, "there is hope in Israel concerning this thing." If you have strayed from God's commands, the door of repentance remains open. Do not let shame or despair convince you that restoration is impossible. Where there is confession, there is hope.
  • 2. Repentance requires action, not just emotion (vv.3-4). The people wept (emotion), confessed (acknowledgment), and put away the foreign wives (action). Tears without change are sentimentality. True repentance moves from feeling to doing — it addresses the actual source of compromise, however painful the surgery.
  • 3. Leaders must go first in obedience (vv.18-19). The priestly families are named first in the list — they who should have led in holiness had led in sin, and now they led in repentance. If you're in leadership and you've failed, don't hide behind your position — confess first, repent publicly, and accept the consequences.
  • 4. Obedience sometimes costs relationships (v.44). These men lost wives and some lost daily access to children. The cost was enormous. Jesus said, "If any man come to me, and hate not his father, and mother, and wife, and children... he cannot be my disciple" (Luke 14:26). Nothing — no relationship however dear — may stand between you and obedience to God.
  • 5. Corporate accountability strengthens individual resolve (vv.12-14). The community agreed together, appointed leaders to oversee the process, and held each other accountable over three months. Sin is rarely overcome alone. Place yourself in a community of accountability where others can support your obedience and challenge your compromise.
