Malachi — Chapter 1

1The oracle of the word of the LORD to Israel by Malachi.

2“I have loved you,” says the LORD. But you say, “How have you loved us?” “Is not Esau Jacob’s brother?” declares the LORD. “Yet I have loved Jacob

3but Esau I have hated. I have laid waste his hill country and left his heritage to jackals of the desert.”

4If Edom says, “We are shattered but we will rebuild the ruins,” the LORD of hosts says, “They may build, but I will tear down, and they will be called ‘the wicked country,’ and ‘the people with whom the LORD is angry forever.’”

5Your own eyes shall see this, and you shall say, “Great is the LORD beyond the border of Israel!”

6“A son honors his father, and a servant his master. If then I am a father, where is my honor? And if I am a master, where is my fear? says the LORD of hosts to you, O priests, who despise my name. But you say, ‘How have we despised your name?’

7By offering polluted food upon my altar. But you say, ‘How have we polluted you?’ By saying that the LORD’s table may be despised.

8When you offer blind animals in sacrifice, is that not evil? And when you offer those that are lame or sick, is that not evil? Present that to your governor; will he accept you or show you favor? says the LORD of hosts.

9And now entreat the favor of God, that he may be gracious to us. With such a gift from your hand, will he show favor to any of you? says the LORD of hosts.

10Oh that there were one among you who would shut the doors, that you might not kindle fire on my altar in vain! I have no pleasure in you, says the LORD of hosts, and I will not accept an offering from your hand.

11For from the rising of the sun to its setting my name will be great among the nations, and in every place incense will be offered to my name, and a pure offering. For my name will be great among the nations, says the LORD of hosts.

12But you profane it when you say that the Lord’s table is polluted, and its fruit, that is, its food, may be despised.

13But you say, ‘What a weariness this is,’ and you snort at it, says the LORD of hosts. You bring what has been taken by violence or is lame or sick, and this you bring as your offering! Shall I accept that from your hand? says the LORD.

14Cursed be the cheat who has a male in his flock, and vows it, and yet sacrifices to the Lord what is blemished. For I am a great King, says the LORD of hosts, and my name will be feared among the nations.

Summary

God declares His love for Israel over Edom, then rebukes the priests for offering blemished sacrifices and despising His table — "My name will be great among the nations" yet they profane it.

Key Verses

  • v.2 — "I have loved you, saith the LORD. Yet ye say, Wherein hast thou loved us?" (KJV) / "'I have loved you,' says the LORD. But you say, 'How have you loved us?'" (ESV) — THE opening declaration of Malachi. God's FIRST word to His apathetic people is not rebuke but LOVE. "I have loved you" — past tense, present reality, eternal truth. And their response? Skepticism. "How? Show us the evidence." This is the attitude of spiritual dullness: blind to love, deaf to grace, demanding proof of what surrounds them.
  • v.6 — "A son honoureth his father, and a servant his master: if then I be a father, where is mine honour? and if I be a master, where is my fear?" (KJV) — A devastating analogy. Even human relationships demand honor. If God is Father, He deserves a son's honor. If God is Master, He deserves a servant's fear. The priests give Him neither. They call Him "Father" and "Lord" but treat Him with contempt.
  • v.8 — "offer it now unto thy governor; will he be pleased with thee?" (KJV) — The test of sincerity: would you give your governor what you give God? Would you present a blind, lame animal to a human authority? You wouldn't dare. Yet you dare offer it to the King of kings. You give God what you wouldn't give your boss.
  • v.11 — "from the rising of the sun even unto the going down of the same my name shall be great among the Gentiles" (KJV) — Stunning: while Israel dishonors God, the NATIONS will honor Him. A prophecy of worldwide worship — fulfilled in the church, where God's name is praised from every corner of the earth. Israel's failure opens the door to global worship.
  • v.13 — "Ye said also, Behold, what a weariness is it!" (KJV) / "But you say, 'What a weariness this is'" (ESV) — The attitude behind the action. They found worship BORING. Tedious. A chore. They "snuffed at it" — rolled their eyes at God's altar. When worship becomes a weariness, something has died in the heart.
  • v.14 — "I am a great King, saith the LORD of hosts, and my name is dreadful among the heathen" (KJV) — God's self- assertion. While Israel yawns, the nations tremble. While priests offer leftovers, God declares: I AM a great King. The problem is not God's worthiness — it is their blindness.

Cross-References

  • v.2 → Romans 9:13 — "Jacob have I loved, but Esau have I hated" — Paul quotes this in discussing God's sovereign election.
  • v.2 → Deuteronomy 7:6-8 — "The LORD did not set his love upon you because ye were more in number... but because the LORD loved you."
  • v.6 → Exodus 20:12 — "Honour thy father" — if human fathers deserve honor, how much more the heavenly Father?
  • v.8 → Leviticus 22:20-22 — The law requiring unblemished sacrifices — no blind, broken, or diseased animals.
  • v.11 → Isaiah 60:3 — "The Gentiles shall come to thy light" — worldwide worship of Israel's God.
  • v.11 → John 4:21-23 — "Neither in this mountain, nor yet at Jerusalem... true worshippers shall worship the Father in spirit and in truth."
  • v.13 → Amos 8:5 — "When will the new moon be gone, that we may sell corn?" — worship as impatient obligation.

Translations

  • v.1 → "Malachi" — Hebrew "mal'aki" = "my messenger." Some debate whether this is a proper name or a title (since 3:1 uses the same word: "I will send my messenger"). Most traditions accept it as a name, fitting for the prophet whose message announces THE coming messenger.
  • v.2 → "hated" — Hebrew "sane." In this context, "love/hate" is covenantal language meaning "chose/did not choose" (as in Luke 14:26). It refers to God's sovereign election of Jacob over Esau for covenant purposes, not emotional hatred.
  • v.7 → "polluted bread" — Hebrew "lechem mego'al" = defiled food. The sacrifices ARE the "bread" on God's table. Defiled animals = defiled food offered to God. They treated God's table like a garbage dump.
  • v.11 → "incense" — Hebrew "muqtar" = that which goes up in smoke, incense or offerings. This verse envisions worship arising to God from EVERY place — not just Jerusalem. A radical prophecy of the universal worship that came through Christ.
  • v.13 → "snuffed at it" (KJV) / "snort at it" (ESV) — Hebrew "hippachtem" = to blow, to puff, to snort in contempt. The gesture of dismissal — rolling eyes, sighing impatiently. They expressed physical disdain for worship.

Reflection

  • 1. "I have loved you" (v.2). God's first word to a cold people is not anger but affection. Before the rebuke comes the reminder: I LOVE YOU. If you feel distant from God, hear His first word: love. But don't respond like Israel — demanding proof while surrounded by evidence. Open your eyes to the love that is already there.
  • 2. "Where is my honour?" (v.6). If you call God "Father" and "Lord," does your life reflect a son's honor and a servant's reverence? Titles without corresponding behavior are empty. Words without worship are noise. Honor God not just with your lips but with your LIFE.
  • 3. "Offer it to your governor" (v.8). The God-test: would you give this to someone you truly respect? The leftover time, the half-hearted attention, the minimal effort — would you give that to your boss? Your spouse? Then why give it to God? He deserves your BEST, not your remainders.
  • 4. "What a weariness!" (v.13). When worship becomes boring, the problem is never God — it is the state of your heart. A heart alive to grace finds worship compelling. If church, prayer, or Scripture feels like drudgery, don't blame the format — examine your heart. Reignite your first love.
  • 5. "I am a great King" (v.14). God is not diminished by our neglect. He remains a great King whether we honor Him or not. The nations will praise Him even if Israel does not. The question is not whether God will be worshipped — it is whether YOU will be among the worshippers.
