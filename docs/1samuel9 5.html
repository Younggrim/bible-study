1 Samuel — Chapter 9

1There was a man of Benjamin whose name was Kish, the son of Abiel, son of Zeror, son of Becorath, son of Aphiah, a Benjaminite, a man of wealth.

2And he had a son whose name was Saul, a handsome young man. There was not a man among the people of Israel more handsome than he. From his shoulders upward he was taller than any of the people.

3Now the donkeys of Kish, Saul’s father, were lost. So Kish said to Saul his son, “Take one of the young men with you, and arise, go and look for the donkeys.”

4And he passed through the hill country of Ephraim and passed through the land of Shalishah, but they did not find them. And they passed through the land of Shaalim, but they were not there. Then they passed through the land of Benjamin, but did not find them.

5When they came to the land of Zuph, Saul said to his servant who was with him, “Come, let us go back, lest my father cease to care about the donkeys and become anxious about us.”

6But he said to him, “Behold, there is a man of God in this city, and he is a man who is held in honor; all that he says comes true. So now let us go there. Perhaps he can tell us the way we should go.”

7Then Saul said to his servant, “But if we go, what can we bring the man? For the bread in our sacks is gone, and there is no present to bring to the man of God. What do we have?”

8The servant answered Saul again, “Here, I have with me a quarter of a shekel of silver, and I will give it to the man of God to tell us our way.”

9(Formerly in Israel, when a man went to inquire of God, he said, “Come, let us go to the seer,” for today’s “prophet” was formerly called a seer.)

10And Saul said to his servant, “Well said; come, let us go.” So they went to the city where the man of God was.

11As they went up the hill to the city, they met young women coming out to draw water and said to them, “Is the seer here?”

12They answered, “He is; behold, he is just ahead of you. Hurry. He has come just now to the city, because the people have a sacrifice today on the high place.

13As soon as you enter the city you will find him, before he goes up to the high place to eat. For the people will not eat till he comes, since he must bless the sacrifice; afterward those who are invited will eat. Now go up, for you will meet him immediately.”

14So they went up to the city. As they were entering the city, they saw Samuel coming out toward them on his way up to the high place.

15Now the day before Saul came, the LORD had revealed to Samuel:

16“Tomorrow about this time I will send to you a man from the land of Benjamin, and you shall anoint him to be prince over my people Israel. He shall save my people from the hand of the Philistines. For I have seen my people, because their cry has come to me.”

17When Samuel saw Saul, the LORD told him, “Here is the man of whom I spoke to you! He it is who shall restrain my people.”

18Then Saul approached Samuel in the gate and said, “Tell me where is the house of the seer?”

19Samuel answered Saul, “I am the seer. Go up before me to the high place, for today you shall eat with me, and in the morning I will let you go and will tell you all that is on your mind.

20As for your donkeys that were lost three days ago, do not set your mind on them, for they have been found. And for whom is all that is desirable in Israel? Is it not for you and for all your father’s house?”

21Saul answered, “Am I not a Benjaminite, from the least of the tribes of Israel? And is not my clan the humblest of all the clans of the tribe of Benjamin? Why then have you spoken to me in this way?”

22Then Samuel took Saul and his young man and brought them into the hall and gave them a place at the head of those who had been invited, who were about thirty persons.

23And Samuel said to the cook, “Bring the portion I gave you, of which I said to you, ‘Put it aside.’”

24So the cook took up the leg and what was on it and set them before Saul. And Samuel said, “See, what was kept is set before you. Eat, because it was kept for you until the hour appointed, that you might eat with the guests.” So Saul ate with Samuel that day.

25And when they came down from the high place into the city, a bed was spread for Saul on the roof, and he lay down to sleep.

26Then at the break of dawn Samuel called to Saul on the roof, “Up, that I may send you on your way.” So Saul arose, and both he and Samuel went out into the street.

27As they were going down to the outskirts of the city, Samuel said to Saul, “Tell the servant to pass on before us, and when he has passed on, stop here yourself for a while, that I may make known to you the word of God.”

Summary

Saul, a tall Benjamite searching for lost donkeys, is directed by God to Samuel, who anoints him privately as Israel's first king. God gives Saul a new heart and confirms the anointing with signs.

Key Verses

v.2
"And he had a son, whose name was Saul, a choice young man, and a goodly: and there was not among the children of Israel a goodlier person than he: from his shoulders and upward he was higher than any of the people." — Saul is introduced by his appearance — tall, handsome, impressive. This is what Israel wanted: a king who looks the part. But appearance is deceptive. The book will later contrast this with God's choice of David: "the LORD looketh on the heart" (16:7).
v.16
"Tomorrow about this time I will send to you a man from the land of Benjamin, and you shall anoint him to be prince over my people Israel, that he may save my people out of the hand of the Philistines: for I have looked upon my people, because their cry is come unto me." — God's sovereignty in the appointment. Despite Israel's sinful demand, God still provides for His people. He hears their cry even when they have rejected Him. Grace operates even within judgment.
v.17
"Here is the man of whom I spoke to you! He it is who shall reign over my people." — God identifies Saul to Samuel. There is no ambiguity — God is directing this process. Saul's kingship is not accidental but divinely appointed, even though it arises from Israel's rebellion.
v.20
"And for whom is all that is desirable in Israel? Is it not for you and for all your father's house?" — Samuel hints at Saul's destiny. "The desire of Israel" — the nation's longing for a king — will be fulfilled in Saul. The phrase is both honor and burden: Israel's expectations will rest on this man's shoulders.
v.21
"Am not I a Benjamite, of the smallest of the tribes of Israel? and my family the least of all the families of the tribe of Benjamin?" — Saul's initial humility. He recognizes his insignificance. This is the right posture — but it will not last. The man who begins by saying "I am least" will end by building himself a monument (15:12).

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/1-samuel-9/ Guzik notes the irony that Saul cannot find donkeys but is about to be given a kingdom. He emphasizes God's providence in the "ordinary" events — lost animals, a servant's suggestion, timing — that bring Saul to Samuel at exactly the right moment.
  • Charles Spurgeon: "Saul went out to find his father's asses and found a kingdom. So does God often work — through the ordinary, the mundane, the seemingly accidental. We go about our daily business, unaware that God is directing our steps toward His appointed purpose. The lost donkeys were God's means of bringing Saul to Samuel. What 'lost donkeys' is God using in your life to bring you to His appointed place?"

Translations

  • v.1 — KJV: "a mighty man of power" / ESV: "a man of wealth" — Hebrew "gibbor chayil." This phrase can mean a mighty warrior, a man of valor, or a man of substance/wealth. KJV emphasizes military prowess; ESV emphasizes economic status. In context, Kish is a man of standing in his community — whether through military reputation or material wealth (or both).
  • v.2 — KJV: "a choice young man, and a goodly" / ESV: "a handsome young man" — Hebrew "bachur vatov." "Bachur" = choice, select, young man in his prime. "Tov" = good, in this context meaning good-looking. KJV preserves both descriptors; ESV combines them into "handsome." The emphasis is on Saul's physical impressiveness — he looks like royalty.
  • v.16 — KJV: "anoint him to be captain" / ESV: "anoint him to be prince" — Hebrew "nagid." This word means a designated leader, one appointed by a higher authority — distinct from "melekh" (king). KJV "captain" emphasizes military leadership; ESV "prince" emphasizes royal designation. The theological point: even Israel's king is subordinate to God — he is God's appointed leader, not an autonomous sovereign.
  • v.17 — KJV: "this same shall reign over my people" / ESV: "He it is who shall restrain my people" — Hebrew "ya'atsor." This verb means to restrain, hold back, or rule. KJV "reign" and ESV "restrain" reflect different nuances of the same word. The idea may be that Saul will "hold together" or "govern" the people — keeping them in order.
  • v.24 — KJV: "the shoulder" / ESV: "the leg" — Hebrew "ha-shoq." This refers to the upper leg/thigh portion — the priest's portion of the sacrifice (Leviticus 7:32-34). By giving Saul the priestly portion, Samuel honors him as God's chosen leader. KJV "shoulder" and ESV "leg" reflect different understandings of the cut of meat.

Reflection

  • 1. God works through ordinary circumstances (vv.3-6). Lost donkeys led to a kingdom. God's providence operates through the mundane — a wrong turn, a chance meeting, a servant's suggestion. We often cannot see God's hand until we look backward. Trust that God is directing your "ordinary" days toward His purposes.
  • 2. Appearance is not destiny (v.2). Saul looked like a king — tall, handsome, impressive. But looking the part does not mean being the part. External qualifications do not guarantee internal character. We must learn to evaluate people (and ourselves) by heart, not by appearance.
  • 3. God provides even within judgment (v.16). Israel's demand for a king was sinful (chapter 8), yet God still hears their cry and provides a deliverer. Grace operates even within the consequences of our rebellion. God does not abandon His people even when they reject His rule.
  • 4. Initial humility is no guarantee of lasting humility (v.21). Saul begins with genuine self-deprecation: "I am from the smallest tribe." But humility that depends on low position often disappears when position rises. True humility is not thinking less of yourself because you have less — it is thinking rightly of yourself regardless of what you have.
  • 5. God prepares before He reveals (vv.15-17). God told Samuel about Saul the day before Saul arrived. God had prepared the feast, the reserved portion, the rooftop conversation. God works ahead of us, preparing what we will need before we know we need it. His providence is always ahead of our awareness.
