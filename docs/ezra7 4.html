Ezra — Chapter 7

1Now after this, in the reign of Artaxerxes king of Persia, Ezra the son of Seraiah, son of Azariah, son of Hilkiah,

2son of Shallum, son of Zadok, son of Ahitub,

3son of Amariah, son of Azariah, son of Meraioth,

4son of Zerahiah, son of Uzzi, son of Bukki,

5son of Abishua, son of Phinehas, son of Eleazar, son of Aaron the chief priest—

6this Ezra went up from Babylonia. He was a scribe skilled in the Law of Moses that the LORD, the God of Israel, had given, and the king granted him all that he asked, for the hand of the LORD his God was on him.

7And there went up also to Jerusalem, in the seventh year of Artaxerxes the king, some of the people of Israel, and some of the priests and Levites, the singers and gatekeepers, and the temple servants.

8And Ezra came to Jerusalem in the fifth month, which was in the seventh year of the king.

9For on the first day of the first month he began to go up from Babylonia, and on the first day of the fifth month he came to Jerusalem, for the good hand of his God was on him.

10For Ezra had set his heart to study the Law of the LORD, and to do it and to teach his statutes and rules in Israel.

11This is a copy of the letter that King Artaxerxes gave to Ezra the priest, the scribe, a man learned in matters of the commandments of the LORD and his statutes for Israel:

12“Artaxerxes, king of kings, to Ezra the priest, the scribe of the Law of the God of heaven. Peace. And now

13I make a decree that anyone of the people of Israel or their priests or Levites in my kingdom, who freely offers to go to Jerusalem, may go with you.

14For you are sent by the king and his seven counselors to make inquiries about Judah and Jerusalem according to the Law of your God, which is in your hand,

15and also to carry the silver and gold that the king and his counselors have freely offered to the God of Israel, whose dwelling is in Jerusalem,

16with all the silver and gold that you shall find in the whole province of Babylonia, and with the freewill offerings of the people and the priests, vowed willingly for the house of their God that is in Jerusalem.

17With this money, then, you shall with all diligence buy bulls, rams, and lambs, with their grain offerings and their drink offerings, and you shall offer them on the altar of the house of your God that is in Jerusalem.

18Whatever seems good to you and your brothers to do with the rest of the silver and gold, you may do, according to the will of your God.

19The vessels that have been given you for the service of the house of your God, you shall deliver before the God of Jerusalem.

20And whatever else is required for the house of your God, which it falls to you to provide, you may provide it out of the king’s treasury.

21“And I, Artaxerxes the king, make a decree to all the treasurers in the province Beyond the River: Whatever Ezra the priest, the scribe of the Law of the God of heaven, requires of you, let it be done with all diligence,

22up to 100 talents of silver, 100 cors of wheat, 100 baths of wine, 100 baths of oil, and salt without prescribing how much.

23Whatever is decreed by the God of heaven, let it be done in full for the house of the God of heaven, lest his wrath be against the realm of the king and his sons.

24We also notify you that it shall not be lawful to impose tribute, custom, or toll on anyone of the priests, the Levites, the singers, the doorkeepers, the temple servants, or other servants of this house of God.

25“And you, Ezra, according to the wisdom of your God that is in your hand, appoint magistrates and judges who may judge all the people in the province Beyond the River, all such as know the laws of your God. And those who do not know them, you shall teach.

26Whoever will not obey the law of your God and the law of the king, let judgment be strictly executed on him, whether for death or for banishment or for confiscation of his goods or for imprisonment.”

27Blessed be the LORD, the God of our fathers, who put such a thing as this into the heart of the king, to beautify the house of the LORD that is in Jerusalem,

28and who extended to me his steadfast love before the king and his counselors, and before all the king’s mighty officers. I took courage, for the hand of the LORD my God was on me, and I gathered leading men from Israel to go up with me.

Summary

Ezra the priest and scribe is introduced, and King Artaxerxes grants him a letter authorizing his journey to Jerusalem with extensive authority and resources to teach and enforce God's law. Ezra praises God for putting this in the king's heart.

Key Verses

v.6
"This Ezra went up from Babylonia. He was a scribe skilled in the Law of Moses... and the king granted him all that he asked, for the hand of the LORD his God was on him." (ESV) — Two realities explain Ezra's success: personal competence ("scribe skilled") and divine favor ("the hand of the LORD his God was on him"). Skill and sovereignty worked together. Ezra's diligent preparation met God's providential opening.
v.10
"For Ezra had set his heart to study the Law of the LORD, and to do it and to teach his statutes and rules in Israel." (ESV) — This is the definitive verse on Ezra's character and method. Three steps in order: (1) study, (2) do/practice, (3) teach. He would not teach what he had not practiced, and he would not practice what he had not studied. This sequence — study, obey, instruct — is the model for all biblical teaching.
v.23
"Whatever is decreed by the God of heaven, let it be done in full for the house of the God of heaven, lest his wrath be against the realm of the king and his sons." (ESV) — Artaxerxes' theology was pragmatic but real — he feared the consequences of offending Israel's God. Even incomplete understanding of God can produce obedience-shaped behavior. God uses imperfect reverence to accomplish perfect purposes.
v.27
"Blessed be the LORD, the God of our fathers, who put such a thing as this into the heart of the king" (ESV) — Ezra's first words in first person are praise. His interpretation of royal favor is theological: God put it in the king's heart. Human generosity is attributed to divine influence. This echoes 1:1 where God "stirred up the spirit of Cyrus."
v.28
"I took courage, for the hand of the LORD my God was on me" (ESV) — The repeated phrase "the hand of the LORD" (vv.6, 9, 28) is Ezra's personal testimony of experienced divine enablement. The "hand of God" is both protective (providential care) and empowering (supernatural strength for the mission).

Cross-References

  • v.1 — 1 Chronicles 6:3-14 — The high priestly genealogy from Aaron to the exile. Ezra's lineage in vv.1-5 is an abbreviated form of this list, establishing his credentials as a direct descendant of Aaron through Zadok — the legitimate priestly line.
  • v.6 — Nehemiah 2:8 — "The king granted me, according to the good hand of my God upon me." Nehemiah uses the same phrase as Ezra — "the hand of God" explaining royal favor. Both men experienced the same divine pattern: inner preparation leading to divinely arranged opportunity.
  • v.10 — 2 Timothy 2:15 — "Do your best to present yourself to God as one approved, a worker who has no need to be ashamed, rightly handling the word of truth." (ESV) Paul's charge to Timothy reflects the same principle Ezra embodied: diligent study as the foundation for faithful ministry.
  • v.10 — James 1:22 — "But be doers of the word, and not hearers only, deceiving yourselves." (ESV) Ezra's middle step — "to do it" — prevents the separation of knowledge from obedience that James warns against. Study without practice is hypocrisy.
  • v.25 — Deuteronomy 16:18 — "You shall appoint judges and officers in all your towns." (ESV) Artaxerxes' authorization for Ezra to appoint judges fulfilled the Mosaic pattern — God's law required institutional enforcement, and a pagan king authorized it.

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/ezra-7/ Guzik emphasizes the remarkable nature of Artaxerxes' letter — essentially giving Ezra a blank check from the Persian treasury and unlimited judicial authority. He highlights v.10 as the key to Ezra's influence: the man who studies, practices, and teaches — in that order — carries divine authority regardless of his political position.
  • Charles Spurgeon: "Ezra 'prepared his heart to seek the law of the LORD, and to do it, and to teach in Israel.' Here is the true order of the ministry: first, the man studies for himself — not to compose sermons but to feed his own soul. Second, he practices what he learns — his life becomes his first sermon. Third, and only third, does he presume to teach others. The man who reverses this order is a fraud and a danger. He who teaches what he does not practice is a Pharisee; he who practices what he has not studied is a fanatic. But the man who seeks, does, and then teaches — this is Ezra, and this is the pattern for every minister of God's Word. Note further: 'the hand of the LORD was upon him.' All his preparation availed nothing without divine anointing. The skilled scribe needed God's hand as much as the humblest servant."

Translations

  • v.6 — KJV: "a ready scribe" / ESV: "a scribe skilled" — Hebrew "sopher mahir" — a scribe who is quick, skilled, fluent. The word "mahir" implies both expertise and readiness — not merely learned but prepared for action. KJV's "ready" captures both senses; ESV's "skilled" emphasizes competence.
  • v.9 — KJV: "according to the good hand of his God upon him" / ESV: "for the good hand of his God was on him" — This phrase ("yad-Elohav ha-tovah") is Ezra's signature expression, appearing five times in Ezra-Nehemiah. It attributes every success to God's favorable, active presence — His "good hand" guiding, protecting, and empowering.
  • v.12 — KJV: "perfect peace, and at such a time" / ESV: "Peace. And now" — Aramaic "gemira uk'eth" — a diplomatic greeting formula. KJV's "perfect peace" overtranslates "gemira" (complete/finished) which may simply mean "etc." or function as a closing salutation formula. ESV simplifies.
  • v.22 — KJV: "an hundred talents of silver" / ESV: "100 talents of silver" — A talent was approximately 75 pounds; 100 talents = approximately 7,500 pounds (3.75 tons) of silver. This represents an enormous sum — the equivalent of millions in modern currency — given from the royal treasury for Temple service.
  • v.25 — KJV: "after the wisdom of thy God, that is in thine hand" / ESV: "according to the wisdom of your God that is in your hand" — "In thine hand" means "in your possession/at your disposal." The Torah was literally in Ezra's hand — he carried the scrolls and carried their authority. The "wisdom of God" was not abstract philosophy but the concrete Law.

Reflection

  • 1. Study, do, teach — in that order (v.10). Ezra's pattern is non-negotiable for any teacher of God's word. You cannot effectively teach what you have not personally studied and practiced. If you teach others, ask: Am I living what I'm teaching? If you're a student, ask: Am I practicing what I'm learning, or merely accumulating knowledge?
  • 2. Preparation meets opportunity (v.6). Ezra was "ready" when the moment came. His years of quiet study in Babylon prepared him for a role he could not have anticipated. Don't despise seasons of preparation that seem to lack visible purpose. When God's timing arrives, your readiness determines your usefulness.
  • 3. Give God credit for human favor (v.27). When a boss promotes you, a door opens unexpectedly, or an authority figure shows unusual generosity — the correct interpretation is Ezra's: "God put this in his heart." Acknowledge the human kindness, but attribute it to divine orchestration. This prevents both pride and ingratitude.
  • 4. God's hand is not magic — it accompanies obedience (vv.9-10). "The hand of the LORD was upon him... for Ezra had prepared his heart." Note the sequence: Ezra prepared, then God's hand was upon him. Divine favor is not random; it accompanies diligent preparation and wholehearted devotion. Don't expect God's hand without preparing your heart.
  • 5. The joy of divine provision calls for worship (v.27). Ezra's first response to overwhelming favor is not planning or strategizing — it's praise. "Blessed be the LORD." When God opens doors, let your first words be doxology. The proper response to received grace is expressed worship.
