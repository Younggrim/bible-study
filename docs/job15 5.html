Job — Chapter 15

1Then Eliphaz the Temanite answered and said:

2“Should a wise man answer with windy knowledge, and fill his belly with the east wind?

3Should he argue in unprofitable talk, or in words with which he can do no good?

4But you are doing away with the fear of God and hindering meditation before God.

5For your iniquity teaches your mouth, and you choose the tongue of the crafty.

6Your own mouth condemns you, and not I; your own lips testify against you.

7“Are you the first man who was born? Or were you brought forth before the hills?

8Have you listened in the council of God? And do you limit wisdom to yourself?

9What do you know that we do not know? What do you understand that is not clear to us?

10Both the gray-haired and the aged are among us, older than your father.

11Are the comforts of God too small for you, or the word that deals gently with you?

12Why does your heart carry you away, and why do your eyes flash,

13that you turn your spirit against God and bring such words out of your mouth?

14What is man, that he can be pure? Or he who is born of a woman, that he can be righteous?

15Behold, God puts no trust in his holy ones, and the heavens are not pure in his sight;

16how much less one who is abominable and corrupt, a man who drinks injustice like water!

17“I will show you; hear me, and what I have seen I will declare

18(what wise men have told, without hiding it from their fathers,

19to whom alone the land was given, and no stranger passed among them).

20The wicked man writhes in pain all his days, through all the years that are laid up for the ruthless.

21Dreadful sounds are in his ears; in prosperity the destroyer will come upon him.

22He does not believe that he will return out of darkness, and he is marked for the sword.

23He wanders abroad for bread, saying, ‘Where is it?’ He knows that a day of darkness is ready at his hand;

24distress and anguish terrify him; they prevail against him, like a king ready for battle.

25Because he has stretched out his hand against God and defies the Almighty,

26running stubbornly against him with a thickly bossed shield;

27because he has covered his face with his fat and gathered fat upon his waist

28and has lived in desolate cities, in houses that none should inhabit, which were ready to become heaps of ruins;

29he will not be rich, and his wealth will not endure, nor will his possessions spread over the earth;

30he will not depart from darkness; the flame will dry up his shoots, and by the breath of his mouth he will depart.

31Let him not trust in emptiness, deceiving himself, for emptiness will be his payment.

32It will be paid in full before his time, and his branch will not be green.

33He will shake off his unripe grape like the vine, and cast off his blossom like the olive tree.

34For the company of the godless is barren, and fire consumes the tents of bribery.

35They conceive trouble and give birth to evil, and their womb prepares deceit.”

Summary

Eliphaz's second speech — abandons gentle persuasion and becomes accusatory, charging Job with undermining religion and describing in vivid detail the torments that await the wicked.

Key Verses

v.4
"But you are doing away with the fear of God and hindering meditation before God." (ESV) — Eliphaz's most serious charge: Job is undermining religion. By questioning God's justice and maintaining innocence, Job allegedly destroys other people's faith. This is the charge of the religious establishment against every honest questioner.
v.7
"Are you the first man who was born? Or were you brought forth before the hills?" (ESV) — Eliphaz's sarcasm: do you have primordial wisdom? Were you there at creation? The irony: God will ask Job similar questions in chapter 38 — but with a different purpose and a different spirit.
v.14
"What is man, that he can be pure? Or he who is born of a woman, that he can be righteous?" (ESV) — Eliphaz repeats his chapter 4 theology: no human can be righteous. The statement is true in absolute terms but false as applied to Job's specific situation — God called Job "blameless" (1:8).
v.16
"How much less one who is abominable and corrupt, a man who drinks injustice like water!" (ESV) — Eliphaz escalates his description of human depravity. Earlier he said humans are fragile (houses of clay); now they are "abominable and filthy." The gloves are fully off.

Cross-References

  • v.4 — Jude 1:3 — "Contend for the faith." There is a difference between undermining faith and honestly wrestling with God. Job does the latter; Eliphaz accuses him of the former. v.7 — Job 38:4 — "Where were you when I laid the foundation of the earth?" (ESV) God will ask similar questions — but from love and authority, not from spite. v.8 — Jeremiah 23:18 — "For who among them has stood in the council of the LORD?" (ESV) The prophetic council is real — but access to it is by God's initiative, not human achievement. v.14 — Job 4:17 — Eliphaz repeats his earlier theological point virtually word for word — he has not progressed in his thinking. v.15 — 2 Peter 2:4 — "God did not spare angels when they sinned." (ESV) Even angels can fail — but this does not prove Job's specific guilt. v.25 — Isaiah 45:9 — "Woe to him who strives with him who formed him." (ESV) Eliphaz accuses Job of striving against God — which is true, but Job's striving leads to encounter, not destruction. v.35 — Isaiah 59:4 — "They conceive mischief and give birth to iniquity." (ESV) The imagery of sin as pregnancy and birth appears in both Isaiah and Eliphaz.

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/job-15/ Guzik notes that Eliphaz's second speech reveals how threatened he is by Job's arguments. Job has challenged the entire framework Eliphaz relies on — and rather than reconsidering his theology, Eliphaz escalates his attacks. This is a common pattern: when our theology is threatened by reality, we often double down rather than reconsider. Guzik also observes that the description of the wicked (vv.20-35) is intended as a mirror for Job: "This is what you look like to us."
  • Charles Spurgeon: "Eliphaz has become angry — and angry theologians are dangerous men. In his first speech he was gentle; now he is savage. Job's honest protests have not humbled Eliphaz — they have enraged him. When a man's system is challenged by facts, he has two options: revise his system or attack the facts. Eliphaz chose to attack. His description of the wicked man is a thinly veiled portrait of Job — and for this, God will eventually require repentance."

Translations

  • v.2 — KJV: "vain knowledge" / ESV: "windy knowledge" — Hebrew "da'at ruach" = knowledge of wind. Both mean empty, insubstantial wisdom. ESV's "windy" connects to the "east wind" in the same verse — Job's words are like the scorching sirocco wind from the Arabian desert.
  • v.11 — KJV: "Are the consolations of God small with thee?" / ESV: "Are the comforts of God too small for you?" — Same meaning. Eliphaz implies that his own words ARE "the consolations of God" — therefore rejecting Eliphaz's counsel equals rejecting God's comfort. The arrogance is stunning.
  • v.26 — KJV: "He runneth upon him, even on his neck, upon the thick bosses of his bucklers" / ESV: "running stubbornly against him with a thickly bossed shield" — The image: a warrior charging God with shield raised, attacking the Almighty. KJV is difficult to parse; ESV clarifies: the wicked man stubbornly assaults God with a heavily reinforced shield.
  • v.33 — KJV: "He shall shake off his unripe grape as the vine" / ESV: "He will shake off his unripe grape like the vine" — The image of fruitlessness: grapes that never ripen, dropped before maturity. The wicked man's efforts never come to fruition — his "fruit" falls before it matures.

Reflection

  • 1. When your theology is threatened, examine it rather than attack the questioner (vv.1-6). Eliphaz cannot handle Job's challenge to his system. Rather than reconsidering whether retribution theology is adequate, he attacks Job personally. When reality challenges your beliefs, humility examines the beliefs; arrogance attacks the reality.
  • 2. Honest questioning is not "undermining religion" (v.4). Eliphaz says Job "casts off fear" and "restrains prayer." In reality, Job is the one still praying — fiercely, honestly, to God's face. The friends pray polished prayers; Job prays raw ones. Which is more real? Do not let anyone silence your honest questions by calling them dangerous to the faith.
  • 3. Describing the wicked to wound the sufferer (vv.20-35). This is spiritual manipulation: painting a detailed picture of the wicked and letting the sufferer see themselves in it. Be aware of this tactic. When someone describes general spiritual dangers while looking at you, they are accusing without taking responsibility for the accusation.
  • 4. The escalation pattern of bad counselors (ch. 4 vs. ch. 15). Eliphaz began gently; now he is harsh. When counsel is rejected, bad counselors escalate rather than reconsider. If your first words didn't help, speaking louder and more harshly will not help either. Escalation is a sign you are wrong, not that the other person is stubborn.
  • 5. None righteous — but still called blameless (v.14). Yes, no human is perfectly righteous. But God still calls people "blameless" (tam) in a relative, functional sense. Do not use total depravity doctrine to flatten all moral distinctions. There are genuinely upright people suffering genuinely undeserved affliction — and they do not need your lecture on universal sin.
