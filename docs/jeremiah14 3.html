Jeremiah — Chapter 14

1The word of the LORD that came to Jeremiah concerning the drought:

2“Judah mourns, and her gates languish; her people lament on the ground, and the cry of Jerusalem goes up.

3Her nobles send their servants for water; they come to the cisterns; they find no water; they return with their vessels empty; they are ashamed and confounded and cover their heads.

4Because of the ground that is dismayed, since there is no rain on the land, the farmers are ashamed; they cover their heads.

5Even the doe in the field forsakes her newborn fawn because there is no grass.

6The wild donkeys stand on the bare heights; they pant for air like jackals; their eyes fail because there is no vegetation.

7“Though our iniquities testify against us, act, O LORD, for your name’s sake; for our backslidings are many; we have sinned against you.

8O you hope of Israel, its savior in time of trouble, why should you be like a stranger in the land, like a traveler who turns aside to tarry for a night?

9Why should you be like a man confused, like a mighty warrior who cannot save? Yet you, O LORD, are in the midst of us, and we are called by your name; do not leave us.”

10Thus says the LORD concerning this people: “They have loved to wander thus; they have not restrained their feet; therefore the LORD does not accept them; now he will remember their iniquity and punish their sins.”

11The LORD said to me: “Do not pray for the welfare of this people.

12Though they fast, I will not hear their cry, and though they offer burnt offering and grain offering, I will not accept them. But I will consume them by the sword, by famine, and by pestilence.”

13Then I said: “Ah, Lord GOD, behold, the prophets say to them, ‘You shall not see the sword, nor shall you have famine, but I will give you assured peace in this place.’”

14And the LORD said to me: “The prophets are prophesying lies in my name. I did not send them, nor did I command them or speak to them. They are prophesying to you a lying vision, worthless divination, and the deceit of their own minds.

15Therefore thus says the LORD concerning the prophets who prophesy in my name although I did not send them, and who say, ‘Sword and famine shall not come upon this land’: By sword and famine those prophets shall be consumed.

16And the people to whom they prophesy shall be cast out in the streets of Jerusalem, victims of famine and sword, with none to bury them—them, their wives, their sons, and their daughters. For I will pour out their evil upon them.

17“You shall say to them this word: ‘Let my eyes run down with tears night and day, and let them not cease, for the virgin daughter of my people is shattered with a great wound, with a very grievous blow.

18If I go out into the field, behold, those pierced by the sword! And if I enter the city, behold, the diseases of famine! For both prophet and priest ply their trade through the land and have no knowledge.’”

19Have you utterly rejected Judah? Does your soul loathe Zion? Why have you struck us down so that there is no healing for us? We looked for peace, but no good came; for a time of healing, but behold, terror.

20We acknowledge our wickedness, O LORD, and the iniquity of our fathers, for we have sinned against you.

21Do not spurn us, for your name’s sake; do not dishonor your glorious throne; remember and do not break your covenant with us.

22Are there any among the false gods of the nations that can bring rain? Or can the heavens give showers? Are you not he, O LORD our God? We set our hope on you, for you do all these things.

Summary

A devastating drought hits Judah — even animals abandon their young. Jeremiah intercedes, but God says: don't pray for them. False prophets promise rain and peace; God says "I sent them not." Judgment by sword, famine, and pestilence is decreed. Jeremiah pours out his grief and God promises the lying prophets will face the very judgment they denied.

Key Verses

v.7
"O LORD, though our iniquities testify against us, do thou it for thy name's sake" (KJV) — Jeremiah's prayer strategy: he can't appeal to Israel's goodness (there is none) so he appeals to GOD'S NAME. "We don't deserve help — but Your reputation is at stake."
v.9
"thou art in the midst of us, and we are called by thy name; leave us not" (KJV) — The intercessor's argument: God's presence and God's name are connected to this people. If they're destroyed, God's reputation suffers.
v.11
"Then said the LORD unto me, Pray not for this people for their good" (KJV) — Third prohibition against intercession (see 7:16; 11:14). Even the greatest intercessor's prayers are refused when judgment is final.
v.14
"The prophets prophesy lies in my name: I sent them not, neither have I commanded them" (KJV) — God's explicit denial: these prophets are UNAUTHORIZED. Their credentials are self-issued. Their message is self-invented.
v.22
"Art not thou he, O LORD our God? therefore we will wait upon thee: for thou hast made all these things" (KJV) — Despite God's refusal, Jeremiah KEEPS praying. The intercessor's persistence — even when told to stop. His theology: you're the Creator; weather is yours to command.

Cross-References

  • v.7 — Daniel 9:18-19 — "we do not present our supplications before thee for our righteousnesses, but for thy great mercies." v.9 — Joel 2:17 — "Spare thy people, O LORD, and give not thine heritage to reproach." v.11 — Jeremiah 7:16; 11:14 — Previous prayer prohibitions. v.12 — Isaiah 1:15 — "when ye make many prayers, I will not hear." v.14 — Jeremiah 23:21 — "I have not sent these prophets, yet they ran." v.14 — Deuteronomy 18:20 — "the prophet, which shall presume to speak a word in my name, which I have not commanded." v.22 — Psalm 147:8 — "who covereth the heaven with clouds, who prepareth rain for the earth."

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/jeremiah-14/
  • Charles Spurgeon: "'Pray not for this people.' Terrible words from God to a prophet whose WHOLE LIFE is intercession. When God says 'stop praying for them,' the case is terminal. Yet even here, the intercessor cannot stop — he keeps going (v.19). Some people are built to intercede even when forbidden."

Translations

  • v.2 — "gates" (KJV/ESV) — Hebrew "she'areyha" = the city gates. Metonymy: the gates represent the cities. "The gates languish" = the cities are in mourning/distress.
  • v.4 — "chapt" (KJV) / "cracked" (ESV) — Hebrew "chatah" = dismayed/cracked. The ground itself is distressed — even soil has no moisture.
  • v.6 — "snuffed up the wind like dragons" (KJV) / "panting for air like jackals" (ESV) — Hebrew "tannim" = jackals (not dragons). Wild donkeys gasping on hilltops like panting jackals — desperate for any air or moisture.
  • v.14 — "divination" (KJV/ESV) — Hebrew "qesem" = the practice of seeking omens or supernatural knowledge apart from God. False prophets' messages come from divination, not revelation.

Reflection

  • 1. "Do it for thy name's sake" (v.7). When you can't appeal to your own goodness (and you usually can't), appeal to God's REPUTATION. His name is at stake in your life. Pray on the basis of who HE is, not who you are.
  • 2. False prophets say what people want to hear (v.14). In any era, there are voices claiming God's authority while contradicting God's word. Test: does the message match Scripture, or just your preferences?
  • 3. God controls weather (v.22). Modern ears resist this — but biblically, weather is in God's hands. Drought, flood, storm — God is sovereign over nature. Whatever you're experiencing externally may have spiritual dimensions.
  • 4. Even refused prayer matters. Jeremiah was told THREE TIMES to stop praying — and he kept praying anyway (v.19). Sometimes the intercessor can't stop, even when heaven says "no." Persistent prayer honors the intercessor's calling even when the answer is "no."
  • 5. "I sent them not" (v.14). Not everyone who says "God told me" was actually told by God. False spiritual authority is real and dangerous. Question credentials. Check messages against Scripture. Guard yourself.
