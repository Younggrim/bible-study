Job — Chapter 3

1After this Job opened his mouth and cursed the day of his birth.

2And Job said:

3“Let the day perish on which I was born, and the night that said, ‘A man is conceived.’

4Let that day be darkness! May God above not seek it, nor light shine upon it.

5Let gloom and deep darkness claim it. Let clouds dwell upon it; let the blackness of the day terrify it.

6That night—let thick darkness seize it! Let it not rejoice among the days of the year; let it not come into the number of the months.

7Behold, let that night be barren; let no joyful cry enter it.

8Let those curse it who curse the day, who are ready to rouse up Leviathan.

9Let the stars of its dawn be dark; let it hope for light, but have none, nor see the eyelids of the morning,

10because it did not shut the doors of my mother’s womb, nor hide trouble from my eyes.

11“Why did I not die at birth, come out from the womb and expire?

12Why did the knees receive me? Or why the breasts, that I should nurse?

13For then I would have lain down and been quiet; I would have slept; then I would have been at rest,

14with kings and counselors of the earth who rebuilt ruins for themselves,

15or with princes who had gold, who filled their houses with silver.

16Or why was I not as a hidden stillborn child, as infants who never see the light?

17There the wicked cease from troubling, and there the weary are at rest.

18There the prisoners are at ease together; they hear not the voice of the taskmaster.

19The small and the great are there, and the slave is free from his master.

20“Why is light given to him who is in misery, and life to the bitter in soul,

21who long for death, but it comes not, and dig for it more than for hidden treasures,

22who rejoice exceedingly and are glad when they find the grave?

23Why is light given to a man whose way is hidden, whom God has hedged in?

24For my sighing comes instead of my bread, and my groanings are poured out like water.

25For the thing that I fear comes upon me, and what I dread befalls me.

26I am not at ease, nor am I quiet; I have no rest, but trouble comes.”

Summary

Job's first speech — he breaks his silence to curse the day of his birth, wishing he had never existed rather than endure such suffering.

Key Verses

  • v.3 — "Let the day perish on which I was born, and the night that said, 'A man is conceived.'" (ESV) — Job's opening words after seven days of silence. Not a curse on God but a curse on his own existence. He wishes himself uncreated. The intensity reveals that faith and anguish can coexist in the same breath.
  • v.11 — "Why did I not die at birth, come out from the womb and expire?" (ESV) — The question moves from cursing birth to questioning why death did not come immediately. If life leads to this suffering, what was the purpose of living at all?
  • v.20 — "Why is light given to him who is in misery, and life to the bitter in soul" (ESV) — Job expands his personal cry into a universal question. Why does God give existence to those who will only suffer? This is the problem of theodicy stated with brutal clarity.
  • v.23 — "Why is light given to a man whose way is hidden, whom God has hedged in?" (ESV) — Bitter irony: in 1:10, Satan said God "hedged" Job with protection. Now Job uses the same image for entrapment. The very God who fenced him in with blessing now fences him in with suffering.
  • v.25 — "For the thing that I fear comes upon me, and what I dread befalls me." (ESV) — A haunting confession: even in prosperity, Job carried a deep anxiety that it could all be taken away. The fear itself did not cause the loss, but it reveals that Job's faith was never naive optimism.

Cross-References

  • v.3 → Jeremiah 20:14-18 — Jeremiah likewise cursed the day of his birth in nearly identical language. Prophets and righteous men can reach this depth of despair without losing their faith.
  • v.4 → Genesis 1:3-4 — "Let there be light... and God divided the light from the darkness." Job's curse systematically reverses creation language.
  • v.8 → ESV: "who are ready to rouse up Leviathan" — A mythic image of cosmic chaos. Job invokes the most powerful forces of destruction against the day of his birth.
  • v.13 → Ecclesiastes 4:2-3 — "I thought the dead who are already dead more fortunate than the living." (ESV) — The same bitter conclusion.
  • v.17 → Revelation 14:13 — "Blessed are the dead who die in the Lord from now on... that they may rest from their labors." (ESV) — Rest in death is a biblical theme.
  • v.20 → Job 10:18 — Job will repeat this question: why bring me from the womb only to suffer?
  • v.23 → Job 1:10 — The "hedge" language deliberately echoes Satan's complaint, but now Job experiences the hedge as a prison rather than protection.
  • v.25 → Proverbs 10:24 — "What the wicked dreads will come upon him." (ESV) — But Job is not wicked — this shows the proverb does not apply neatly to every situation.

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): "https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/job-3/" target="_blank">enduringword.com Guzik observes that Job's speech is carefully structured anti- creation poetry. He does not randomly vent — he deliberately inverts the language of Genesis 1. This shows theological sophistication even in despair. Guzik also notes that Job never curses God (Satan's prediction fails), never asks to die by his own hand (he does not contemplate suicide — he wishes God had never created him, which is different), and never abandons his relationship with God. His complaint is directed TO God, not AWAY from God.
  • Charles Spurgeon: "Here is a man in the very depths, and yet he does not sin in the way Satan predicted. He does not curse God — he curses the day. There is a vast difference. Many a saint has wished he had never been born without ever wishing God did not exist. The language of extreme suffering is not the language of apostasy. God hears the groan that comes from faith's dark night and does not condemn it. He who made us knows our frame and remembers that we are dust."

Translations

  • v.5 → KJV: "shadow of death" / ESV: "deep darkness" — Hebrew "tsalmaveth" was traditionally understood as a compound (tsel + maveth = shadow of death). Modern scholars often read it as an intensive form meaning "deep/utter darkness." KJV preserves the traditional reading; ESV follows modern linguistic analysis. Both convey the idea of impenetrable darkness.
  • v.8 → KJV: "who are ready to raise up their mourning" / ESV: "who are ready to rouse up Leviathan" — Hebrew "livyathan" in ESV vs. a different vowel pointing yielding "mourning" in KJV. The ESV follows the more likely reading: ancient sorcerers who claimed power to curse days and awaken cosmic sea monsters. This fits the mythic, cosmic tone of Job's curse.
  • v.17 → KJV: "the weary be at rest" / ESV: "the weary are at rest" — Hebrew "yegi'e koach" = those exhausted in strength. Both translations capture the image of death as cessation of labor. The weary find in death what they could not find in life: rest.
  • v.24 → KJV: "my roarings are poured out like the waters" / ESV: "my groanings are poured out like water" — Hebrew "she'agah" can mean roaring (like a lion) or groaning. KJV "roarings" captures the volume and intensity; ESV "groanings" captures the inward anguish. Both are valid.

Reflection

  • 1. Faith permits honest anguish (vv.1-3). Job's curse on his birthday is included in inspired Scripture. God did not condemn these words. The psalms of lament, Jeremiah's curses on his birth, and Job's anguish all teach that honest pain expressed TO God is not sin. You do not need to pretend you are fine. God would rather hear your raw cry than your polished silence.
  • 2. Despair and faith are not mutually exclusive (v.23). Job questions God while still speaking to God. He complains about God's actions while still acknowledging God is the one acting. If you find yourself in darkness and cannot find God, keep talking to Him anyway. The very complaint proves the relationship still exists.
  • 3. The problem of purposeless suffering (v.20). "Why give light to the miserable?" is a question every sufferer asks. The book of Job does not answer it with a formula — it answers it with a Person (chapters 38-41). When no explanation suffices, only the presence of God satisfies.
  • 4. You are not alone in darkness (v.26). Job's description of his inner state — no safety, no rest, no quiet, yet trouble comes — is the experience of millions. Scripture includes this chapter so that sufferers know: even the most righteous person in the earth felt exactly what you feel. Your darkness does not disqualify you from God's purposes.
  • 5. There is a difference between wanting to die and wanting to cease existing (vv.11-16). Job does not contemplate suicide — he wishes he had never been created. The distinction matters: he still acknowledges God's sovereignty over life and death. He will not take what is not his to take.
