Psalms — Chapter 3

1O LORD, how many are my foes! Many are rising against me;

2many are saying of my soul, “There is no salvation for him in God.” Selah

3But you, O LORD, are a shield about me, my glory, and the lifter of my head.

4I cried aloud to the LORD, and he answered me from his holy hill. Selah

5I lay down and slept; I woke again, for the LORD sustained me.

6I will not be afraid of many thousands of people who have set themselves against me all around.

7Arise, O LORD! Save me, O my God! For you strike all my enemies on the cheek; you break the teeth of the wicked.

8Salvation belongs to the LORD; your blessing be on your people! Selah

Summary

A lament psalm of David fleeing Absalom — surrounded by enemies who say God will not help him, yet David declares the LORD is his shield and sustainer; he slept and woke because the LORD sustained him.

Key Verses

v.3
"But thou, O LORD, art a shield for me; my glory, and the lifter up of mine head." (KJV) — The great "BUT" — the pivot from crisis to confidence. Enemies are many, taunts are loud, but GOD is shield, glory, and head-lifter. David fled with his head covered in shame (2 Sam 15:30); God lifts it again.
v.5
"I laid me down and slept; I awaked; for the LORD sustained me." (KJV) — Astonishing in context. A king fleeing his own son with an army in pursuit — and he SLEPT. This is not denial but trust. Sleep is the ultimate indicator of inner peace (cf. Mark 4:38, Jesus sleeping in the storm).
v.8
"Salvation belongeth unto the LORD: thy blessing is upon thy people. Selah." (KJV) — The psalm's theological summary. Salvation is God's domain — not David's strategy, army, or political maneuvering. And it extends beyond David to "thy people" — communal blessing from personal trust.

Cross-References

  • v.1 — 2 Samuel 15:12-13 — "The conspiracy was strong; for the people increased continually with Absalom." The historical backdrop — David's supporters defecting. v.2 — 2 Samuel 16:7-8 — Shimei's curse: "Come out, come out, thou bloody man." The taunt that God abandoned David. v.3 — Genesis 15:1 — "Fear not, Abram: I am thy shield." God as shield — Abrahamic promise. v.3 — Psalm 27:6 — "Now shall mine head be lifted up above mine enemies." Same head-lifting imagery. v.5 — Psalm 4:8 — "I will both lay me down in peace, and sleep." The evening companion psalm echoes this trust. v.5 — Mark 4:38 — Jesus asleep in the storm — the same radical trust expressed in rest. v.7 — Psalm 58:6 — "Break their teeth, O God, in their mouth." Similar imprecatory language — breaking teeth = removing power to harm. v.8 — Jonah 2:9 — "Salvation is of the LORD." Same theological declaration from another desperate situation.

Translations

  • v.2 — KJV: "There is no help for him in God" / ESV: "There is no salvation for him in God" — Hebrew "yeshuah" = salvation/ deliverance. ESV's "salvation" is more precise. The taunt is theological: God cannot or will not save him. This is the deepest cut — not just physical danger but spiritual abandonment.
  • v.3 — KJV: "a shield for me" / ESV: "a shield about me" — Hebrew "ba'adi" could mean "for me" or "around me." ESV suggests surrounding protection; KJV suggests advocacy. Both are legitimate readings.
  • v.4 — KJV: "he heard me" / ESV: "he answered me" — Hebrew "anah" = answered/responded. ESV is more precise — God did not merely hear passively but actively responded. The answer came "from his holy hill" — Zion, which David has just LEFT. God is not confined to the geographic location David departed.
  • v.7 — KJV: "thou hast smitten all mine enemies upon the cheek bone" / ESV: "you strike all my enemies on the cheek" — Striking the cheek = shaming and defanging. Breaking teeth = removing the power to devour. The imagery is of a predator rendered harmless.
  • Selah — Appears three times (vv.2, 4, 8). Meaning uncertain — likely a musical or liturgical pause. Functionally: stop and consider what was just said.

Reflection

  • 1. The world will tell you God cannot help (v.2). "There is no salvation for him in God" — this is the taunt of every circumstance that seems too large, every failure that seems disqualifying, every enemy that seems overwhelming. The taunt is always a lie.
  • 2. The word "BUT" changes everything (v.3). Enemies are real, danger is real, taunts are real — BUT God is shield, glory, head-lifter. Faith does not deny the crisis; it introduces a greater reality into the crisis.
  • 3. You can sleep when God is your shield (v.5). David's sleep is not escapism — it is the most practical demonstration of trust. If you truly believe God is your protector, you can close your eyes in the presence of ten thousand enemies. Insomnia often reveals where trust has eroded.
  • 4. Personal trust becomes communal blessing (v.8). "Thy blessing is upon thy people." David's faith did not serve only David. A leader who trusts God becomes a channel of blessing to everyone under their care.
  • 5. Salvation BELONGS to the Lord (v.8). It is His possession, His prerogative, His gift. You cannot manufacture it, earn it, or lose it through human failure. It belongs to Him — and He gives it freely to those who cry out (v.4).
