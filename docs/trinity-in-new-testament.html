The Trinity

Trinity in the New Testament

Understanding

The New Testament reveals the Trinity with unmistakable clarity. At Jesus' baptism, all three persons are simultaneously present and active: the Son emerges from the water, the Spirit descends as a dove, and the Father speaks from heaven. This is not one God playing three roles but three distinct persons united in one divine being.

Jesus' farewell discourse in John 14-16 unfolds the Trinitarian relationships with beautiful intimacy. The Son asks the Father, who sends "another Advocate" — the Spirit. The Spirit glorifies the Son, the Son reveals the Father, and the Father loves the Son and those who love Him. Each person has distinct actions yet works in perfect harmony with the others.

The baptismal formula of Matthew 28:19 — "in the name (singular) of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit" — captures the doctrine perfectly. One name, three persons. Not three gods, not one person wearing three masks, but one God who exists eternally as three distinct, co-equal, co-eternal persons in perfect love and unity.

Key Scriptures

  • Matthew 28:19 — "Go and make disciples of all nations, baptizing them in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit."
  • 2 Corinthians 13:14 — "May the grace of the Lord Jesus Christ, and the love of God, and the fellowship of the Holy Spirit be with you all."
  • Matthew 3:16-17 — "Jesus was baptized... the Spirit descended like a dove... a voice from heaven said, This is my Son."
  • John 14:16-17 — "I will ask the Father, and he will give you another advocate — the Spirit of truth."
  • Ephesians 4:4-6 — "There is one body and one Spirit... one Lord... one God and Father of all."

Significance for Us

The New Testament revelation of the Trinity means that our entire Christian experience is Trinitarian. We are loved by the Father, saved by the Son, and empowered by the Spirit. We pray to the Father, through the Son, in the Spirit. We are chosen by the Father, redeemed by the Son, and sealed by the Spirit. Every aspect of salvation involves all three persons.

Understanding the Trinity also reveals that God is inherently relational. Before creation, within the Godhead, there was love, communication, and fellowship. God did not create us because He was lonely — He was already in perfect relationship within Himself. He created us to share in the joy of that divine love, inviting us into the fellowship of the Trinity.

Reflection Questions

  • How does the baptism of Jesus help you understand the distinct persons of the Trinity?
  • In what ways do you experience each person of the Trinity in your daily spiritual life?
  • How does the Trinitarian nature of God assure you that love is at the very heart of reality?
