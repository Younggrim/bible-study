Women of the Bible

Hannah

Her Story

Hannah's story begins in grief. Year after year she accompanied her husband Elkanah to Shiloh for worship, and year after year she returned home without the child her heart longed for. Her rival Peninnah taunted her relentlessly about her barrenness, adding emotional cruelty to physical heartbreak. Even Elkanah's love — "Am I not more to you than ten sons?" — could not fill the void in her soul.

One year at Shiloh, Hannah's grief reached a breaking point. She poured out her soul before the LORD with such intensity that the priest Eli thought she was drunk. Her prayer was not polished or composed — it was raw, desperate, and honest. She made a vow: if God would give her a son, she would give him back to the LORD for all the days of his life. It was a prayer born not of bargaining but of surrender.

God answered Hannah's prayer, and Samuel was born. True to her word, as soon as the boy was weaned, Hannah brought him to the tabernacle at Shiloh and left him there to serve the LORD under Eli's care. Her prayer of thanksgiving — recorded in 1 Samuel 2 — is one of Scripture's most beautiful songs of praise, celebrating God as the one who lifts the needy from the ash heap and seats them among princes.

Key Scriptures

  • 1 Samuel 1:10-11 — In her deep anguish Hannah prayed to the LORD, weeping bitterly, and made a vow.
  • 1 Samuel 1:27-28 — "I prayed for this child, and the LORD has granted me what I asked. So now I give him to the LORD."
  • 1 Samuel 2:1 — "My heart rejoices in the LORD; in the LORD my horn is lifted high."
  • Psalm 37:4 — "Delight yourself in the LORD, and he will give you the desires of your heart."
  • Philippians 4:6 — "Do not be anxious about anything, but in everything by prayer present your requests to God."

Lessons from Her Life

Hannah teaches us that God welcomes raw, unfiltered prayer. She did not come to God with composed liturgy but with gut-wrenching honesty. Her example gives permission to every believer who has been told to "be strong" or "have more faith" — it is okay to weep before God, to pour out bitterness and longing without pretense. God is not offended by our desperation; He is moved by it.

Hannah also demonstrates the radical nature of true surrender. She prayed for a son, received him, and then gave him back. This was not giving God leftovers — it was offering her greatest treasure. Yet God honored her sacrifice abundantly, giving her five more children and using Samuel to anoint kings and lead a nation. When we offer God our best with open hands, He multiplies what we could never have imagined.

Reflection Questions

  • What are you desperate enough to pray about with Hannah's intensity?
  • Is there something God has given you that He is now asking you to surrender back to Him?
  • How does Hannah's example change your understanding of what 'acceptable' prayer looks like?
  • What might it look like to give God your greatest desire with open hands?
