2 Corinthians — Chapter 8

1We want you to know, brothers, about the grace of God that has been given among the churches of Macedonia,

2for in a severe test of affliction, their abundance of joy and their extreme poverty have overflowed in a wealth of generosity on their part.

3For they gave according to their means, as I can testify, and beyond their means, of their own accord,

4begging us earnestly for the favor of taking part in the relief of the saints—

5and this, not as we expected, but they gave themselves first to the Lord and then by the will of God to us.

6Accordingly, we urged Titus that as he had started, so he should complete among you this act of grace.

7But as you excel in everything—in faith, in speech, in knowledge, in all earnestness, and in our love for you—see that you excel in this act of grace also.

8I say this not as a command, but to prove by the earnestness of others that your love also is genuine.

9For you know the grace of our Lord Jesus Christ, that though he was rich, yet for your sake he became poor, so that you by his poverty might become rich.

10And in this matter I give my judgment: this benefits you, who a year ago started not only to do this work but also to desire to do it.

11So now finish doing it as well, so that your readiness in desiring it may be matched by your completing it out of what you have.

12For if the readiness is there, it is acceptable according to what a person has, not according to what he does not have.

13For I do not mean that others should be eased and you burdened, but that as a matter of fairness

14your abundance at the present time should supply their need, so that their abundance may supply your need, that there may be fairness.

15As it is written, “Whoever gathered much had nothing left over, and whoever gathered little had no lack.”

16But thanks be to God, who put into the heart of Titus the same earnest care I have for you.

17For he not only accepted our appeal, but being himself very earnest he is going to you of his own accord.

18With him we are sending the brother who is famous among all the churches for his preaching of the gospel.

19And not only that, but he has been appointed by the churches to travel with us as we carry out this act of grace that is being ministered by us, for the glory of the Lord himself and to show our good will.

20We take this course so that no one should blame us about this generous gift that is being administered by us,

21for we aim at what is honorable not only in the Lord’s sight but also in the sight of man.

22And with them we are sending our brother whom we have often tested and found earnest in many matters, but who is now more earnest than ever because of his great confidence in you.

23As for Titus, he is my partner and fellow worker for your benefit. And as for our brothers, they are messengers of the churches, the glory of Christ.

24So give proof before the churches of your love and of our boasting about you to these men.

Summary

Paul holds up the Macedonians' generous giving out of deep poverty as a model, appeals to Christ's example ("though He was rich, yet for your sakes He became poor"), and organizes the collection for the Jerusalem saints with transparency and integrity.

Key Verses

v.2
"In a severe test of affliction, their abundance of joy and their extreme poverty have overflowed in a wealth of generosity." (ESV) — Joy + poverty = generosity. Giving is not about wealth but about heart.
v.5
"They gave themselves first to the Lord." (ESV) — Self-surrender precedes financial giving.
v.7
"See that you excel in this act of grace also." (ESV) — Giving is a grace — a spiritual gift to be cultivated.
v.9
"Though he was rich, yet for your sake he became poor, so that you by his poverty might become rich." (ESV) — Christ's generosity as the model.
v.12
"For if the readiness is there, it is acceptable according to what a person has, not according to what he does not have." (ESV) — God measures willingness, not amount.

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/2-corinthians-8/ Guzik examines the Macedonian example, Christ's generosity, the principles of giving, and financial accountability.
  • Charles Spurgeon: Sermon: "The Grace of Giving" on v.9 (MTP Vol. 10, No. 666) "Though He was rich — infinitely rich, owning everything — yet for your sakes He became poor. Not just less rich — poor. Homeless, dependent, crucified. Why? That you through His poverty might be rich. That is the model for all giving: sacrificial, voluntary, for others' benefit."

Translations

  • v.1 — KJV: "the grace of God bestowed on the churches" / ESV: "the grace of God that has been given among the churches" — Giving is described as "grace" (charis) — not obligation but a gift from God that enables generosity.
  • v.2 — KJV: "deep poverty abounded unto the riches of their liberality" / ESV: "poverty have overflowed in a wealth of generosity on their part" — A paradox: extreme poverty producing extreme generosity. Joy overflowed into giving despite having almost nothing.
  • v.9 — KJV: "though he was rich" / ESV: Both equivalent. — Christ's pre-incarnate glory and wealth. "Became poor" — the Incarnation, the cross. "That ye might be rich" — spiritual riches in Christ.
  • v.12 — KJV: "according to that a man hath" / ESV: "according to what a person has, not" — God evaluates giving by what you have, not by what you don't have. Proportional, not equal amounts.
  • ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB) v.2 — NET: "their abundant joy and their extreme poverty have overflowed in the wealth of their generosity" — "extreme poverty" + "abundant joy" = "wealth of generosity" — the equation defies logic. WEB: "their deep poverty abounded to the riches of their generosity." Grace-powered giving.
  • v.5 — NET: "they first gave themselves to the Lord and to us by the will of God" — "gave themselves first" — self-giving precedes money-giving. WEB: "first they gave their own selves to the Lord." The order matters: self, then substance.
  • v.9 — NET: "For you know the grace of our Lord Jesus Christ, that although he was rich, for your sakes he became poor, so that you by his poverty could become rich" — the Incarnation as economic language. WEB: "though he was rich, yet for your sakes he became poor." Christ's poverty = our riches. The theological foundation of generosity.
  • v.12 — NET: "For if the eagerness is present, the gift itself is acceptable according to whatever one has, not according to what he does not have" — proportional giving, not equal amounts. WEB: "it is accepted according to what you have." God measures generosity by capacity, not quantity.
  • v.21 — NET: "For we are concerned about what is right not only before the Lord but also before people" — "before people" (enOpion anthrOpOn). WEB: "providing honorable things, not only in the sight of the Lord, but also in the sight of men." Financial transparency as ministry principle.

Reflection

  • 1. The Macedonians "first gave their own selves to the Lord" (v.5). Financial giving flows from self-surrender. Have you given YOURSELF to God first? Money follows the heart.
  • 2. "Though he was rich, yet for your sakes he became poor" (v.9). Christ's generosity is the model. He gave everything — not from surplus but from sacrifice. How does His example challenge your giving?
  • 3. "If there be first a willing mind, it is accepted" (v.12). God doesn't measure the amount — He measures the willingness. Is your giving willing and cheerful, or reluctant and obligatory?
  • 4. "See that ye abound in this grace also" (v.7). Giving is a grace to be cultivated — like faith, knowledge, and love. Are you growing in the grace of generosity?
