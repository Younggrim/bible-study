1Wisdom has built her house; she has hewn her seven pillars.

2She has slaughtered her beasts; she has mixed her wine; she has also set her table.

3She has sent out her young women to call from the highest places in the town,

4“Whoever is simple, let him turn in here!” To him who lacks sense she says,

5“Come, eat of my bread and drink of the wine I have mixed.

6Leave your simple ways, and live, and walk in the way of insight.”

7Whoever corrects a scoffer gets himself abuse, and he who reproves a wicked man incurs injury.

8Do not reprove a scoffer, or he will hate you; reprove a wise man, and he will love you.

9Give instruction to a wise man, and he will be still wiser; teach a righteous man, and he will increase in learning.

10The fear of the LORD is the beginning of wisdom, and the knowledge of the Holy One is insight.

11For by me your days will be multiplied, and years will be added to your life.

12If you are wise, you are wise for yourself; if you scoff, you alone will bear it.

13The woman Folly is loud; she is seductive and knows nothing.

14She sits at the door of her house; she takes a seat on the highest places of the town,

15calling to those who pass by, who are going straight on their way,

16“Whoever is simple, let him turn in here!” And to him who lacks sense she says,

17“Stolen water is sweet, and bread eaten in secret is pleasant.”

18But he does not know that the dead are there, that her guests are in the depths of Sheol.

Summary

The final contrast of the discourse section — Lady Wisdom builds her house and hosts a feast, inviting the simple; Folly also sits and calls, but her guests end in Sheol. 'The fear of the LORD is the beginning of wisdom.'

Key Verses

  • v.1 — "Wisdom has built her house; she has hewn her seven pillars." (ESV) — Wisdom is established, prepared, stable. Seven pillars = perfection, completeness. Her house is not hastily constructed but carefully built. To enter wisdom is to enter security and abundance.
  • v.8 — "Do not reprove a scoffer, or he will hate you; reprove a wise man, and he will love you." (ESV) — The definitive test of character: how do you respond to correction? The wise man loves the person who corrects him. The scoffer hates the very one trying to help. Your response to rebuke reveals whether you are wise or foolish.
  • v.10 — "The fear of the LORD is the beginning of wisdom, and the knowledge of the Holy One is insight." (ESV) — The motto restated (cf. 1:7). Here "beginning" is "techillah" (the starting point) rather than "re'shith" (first principle/ chief part) as in 1:7. Together: the fear of the LORD is both the foundation and the starting point of wisdom.
  • v.17 — "Stolen water is sweet, and bread eaten in secret is pleasant." (ESV) — Folly's only argument: forbidden things taste better. The thrill of the illicit, the excitement of secrecy. It is true — sin has pleasure (Hebrews 11:25). But v.18 reveals the price.
  • v.18 — "But he does not know that the dead are there, that her guests are in the depths of Sheol." (ESV) — The hidden truth behind Folly's banquet: her dining room is a morgue. Her guests are corpses. The "he" doesn't know — that's the horror. He walks in blind.

Cross-References

  • v.1 → 1 Timothy 3:15 — "The church of the living God, a pillar and buttress of the truth." (ESV) God's house also associated with pillars and truth.
  • v.2 → Matthew 22:2-4 — "The kingdom of heaven may be compared to a king who gave a wedding feast." (ESV) The gospel as an invitation to a prepared banquet.
  • v.5 → Isaiah 55:1 — "Come...buy wine and milk without money." (ESV) The same free invitation to abundance.
  • v.8 → Psalm 141:5 — "Let a righteous man strike me—it is a kindness." (ESV) David welcomed correction.
  • v.10 → Proverbs 1:7 — The bookend. The discourse section begins and ends with the same motto.
  • v.10 → Job 28:28 — "The fear of the Lord, that is wisdom." (ESV) Job's conclusion after searching for wisdom.
  • v.17 → Hebrews 11:25 — "The fleeting pleasures of sin." (ESV) Temporary sweetness, eternal bitterness.
  • v.18 → Proverbs 2:18-19 — "Her house sinks down to death." (ESV) Same verdict on folly's destination.

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): "https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/proverbs-9/" target="_blank">enduringword.com Guzik emphasizes the parallel structure: both Wisdom and Folly call from the same high places to the same audience. The simple person must choose which invitation to accept. He notes that Folly's only selling point is transgression itself — "stolen waters are sweet." She has no genuine product, only the thrill of the forbidden. On v.8, Guzik states the practical test: "If you can't receive correction, you're not wise — regardless of how much you know."
  • Charles Spurgeon: "Rebuke a wise man, and he will love thee. This is the infallible mark of wisdom: the love of correction. Not the mere tolerance of it — the LOVE of it. The wise man sees the rebuker as a friend, not an enemy. He knows that faithful wounds are better than deceitful kisses. If you want to know whether you are growing in wisdom, ask yourself: am I more or less receptive to correction than I was a year ago? The direction of your answer reveals the direction of your soul."

Translations

  • v.1 → KJV: "hewn out her seven pillars" / ESV: "hewn her seven pillars" — Hebrew "chatsab" = to cut, hew, quarry stone. Wisdom's house is built with cut stone — permanent, solid, crafted. The seven pillars have been variously interpreted as the seven days of creation, seven liberal arts, etc. Most likely: seven = completeness/perfection.
  • v.10 → KJV: "knowledge of the holy" / ESV: "knowledge of the Holy One" — Hebrew "da'ath qedoshim" (plural). KJV leaves it ambiguous; ESV capitalizes and singularizes: "the Holy One" (God). The plural "qedoshim" could be a plural of majesty (referring to God) or a general plural (holy things/beings). Most interpreters agree: knowing GOD is the essence of understanding.
  • v.13 → KJV: "A foolish woman is clamorous" / ESV: "The woman Folly is loud" — ESV personalizes her as "Woman Folly" (matching "Lady Wisdom"), making the parallel clearer. Both are loud — both compete for attention. The difference is their character and destination.
  • v.17 → KJV: "Stolen waters are sweet" / ESV: "Stolen water is sweet" — Identical sense. "Water" likely refers to sexual pleasure (cf. 5:15-18 where water = marital intimacy). Stolen water = sexual pleasure taken outside of marriage covenant.

Reflection

  • 1. Both wisdom and folly are calling (vv.3-4, 14-16). Every day you hear both voices. Both sound inviting. Both promise satisfaction. The question is: which invitation will you accept? Learn to recognize the voices. Wisdom calls openly with truth; Folly lures secretly with the thrill of the forbidden.
  • 2. How you respond to correction reveals everything (v.8). Do you love correction or hate it? Do you thank the person who points out your error, or do you resent them? This single test separates the wise from the foolish more reliably than any other. Cultivate a heart that welcomes rebuke.
  • 3. "If thou be wise, thou shalt be wise for thyself" (v.12). The benefits of wisdom accrue to YOU. The costs of folly are paid by YOU. No one else can be wise for you, and no one else suffers your foolishness as much as you do. This is deeply personal. Take ownership.
  • 4. Sin's only appeal is the thrill of transgression (v.17). "Stolen waters are sweet." If it weren't forbidden, it wouldn't be exciting. Recognize this: sin has no genuine substance — only the adrenaline of rebellion. When you strip away the illicit thrill, what remains? Nothing. Emptiness. Death.
  • 5. Know what's behind the door (v.18). Before you accept any invitation — to a relationship, a lifestyle, a decision — ask: who else accepted this invitation, and where are they now? Folly's guest list is the dead. Check the outcomes before you RSVP.
