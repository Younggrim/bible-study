Prayers in the Bible

Daniel's Prayer (Daniel 9:1-19)

The Prayer

Daniel's prayer in chapter 9 was triggered by his study of Scripture — specifically Jeremiah's prophecy that the exile would last seventy years. Rather than passively waiting for the prophecy to fulfill itself, Daniel responded with urgent, passionate prayer. He understood that God's promises and human prayer work together, not in opposition.

The content of Daniel's prayer is remarkable for its identification with the sins of his people. Though Daniel was personally righteous, he prayed "we have sinned, we have been wicked." He did not separate himself from the community's failures but carried them before God as his own. This corporate identification is a hallmark of mature intercession.

Daniel approached God with fasting, sackcloth, and ashes — physical expressions of spiritual urgency. His prayer was not casual or routine but desperate, humble, and wholly dependent on God's mercy. The result was one of the most significant prophetic revelations in all of Scripture — demonstrating that earnest prayer opens doors to divine understanding.

Key Scriptures

  • Daniel 9:3-5 — "I turned to the Lord God and pleaded with him in prayer and petition, in fasting, and in sackcloth and ashes."
  • Daniel 9:17-19 — "Give ear, our God, and hear; open your eyes and see the desolation of the city that bears your Name."
  • Daniel 10:12 — "From the first day that you set your mind to gain understanding and to humble yourself before your God, your words were heard."
  • James 5:16 — "The prayer of a righteous person is powerful and effective."
  • 2 Chronicles 7:14 — "If my people, who are called by my name, will humble themselves and pray... I will hear from heaven."

What We Learn About Prayer

Daniel teaches us that Scripture study and prayer are meant to work together. His reading of Jeremiah did not replace prayer — it fueled it. When we discover God's promises in His Word, the appropriate response is not passive waiting but active, faith-filled prayer that aligns our hearts with His purposes and asks Him to fulfill what He has spoken.

We also learn the power of humility and identification in prayer. Daniel could have prayed as a righteous man looking down on sinful Israel. Instead, he included himself in the confession, recognizing that we are part of a community and bear responsibility for one another. This kind of humble, corporate prayer moves the heart of God in ways individual self-righteousness never will.

Reflection Questions

  • How does your study of Scripture inform and fuel your prayer life?
  • Are you willing to identify with the sins of your community and pray for corporate repentance?
  • What does Daniel's example teach you about the relationship between God's sovereignty and human prayer?
