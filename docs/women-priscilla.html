Women of the Bible

Priscilla

Her Story

Priscilla and her husband Aquila are always mentioned together in Scripture — a partnership in marriage, ministry, and mission that modeled the early church at its best. Tentmakers by trade, they were among the Jewish Christians expelled from Rome under Emperor Claudius. In Corinth, they met Paul and opened their home and workshop to him, beginning a partnership that would span years and cross continents.

When the eloquent Apollos arrived in Ephesus, preaching powerfully but with incomplete understanding, it was Priscilla and Aquila who "took him aside and explained to him the way of God more accurately." The fact that Priscilla is often named first in this couple suggests her prominent role in teaching and leadership. She corrected a brilliant orator — not publicly to embarrass him, but privately to equip him for greater effectiveness.

Paul called Priscilla and Aquila his "fellow workers in Christ Jesus" who "risked their necks" for his life. They hosted house churches in every city they lived — Corinth, Ephesus, and Rome. Their home was not merely a dwelling but a center of gospel ministry wherever they went. They represent the countless unnamed believers whose hospitality, teaching, and sacrifice built the early church from the ground up.

Key Scriptures

  • Acts 18:26 — Priscilla and Aquila took Apollos aside and explained to him the way of God more accurately.
  • Romans 16:3-4 — "Greet Prisca and Aquila, my fellow workers in Christ Jesus, who risked their necks for my life."
  • Acts 18:2-3 — Paul went to see them, and because he was a tentmaker as they were, he stayed and worked with them.
  • 1 Corinthians 16:19 — "The churches in the province of Asia send you greetings. Aquila and Prisca greet you warmly."
  • 2 Timothy 4:19 — "Greet Prisca and Aquila" — Paul remembers them among his dearest co-laborers to the end.

Lessons from Her Life

Priscilla teaches us that effective ministry often happens in the margins — in homes, workshops, and private conversations rather than from platforms and pulpits. She and Aquila built the church one relationship at a time, one household gathering at a time, one private correction at a time. Their model challenges our obsession with visible, large-scale ministry and reminds us that the most transformative work often happens in intimate, personal settings.

Priscilla also demonstrates the power of partnership in ministry. She and Aquila worked as a seamless team — in business, in hospitality, and in teaching. Their marriage was not merely a personal arrangement but a ministry vehicle. For couples today, their example invites reflection on how marriage can become a platform for gospel work together rather than a retreat from it.

Reflection Questions

  • How is your home being used as a place of ministry and hospitality?
  • Is there someone in your life who needs gentle, private correction to grow in their faith?
  • How can you and your spouse (or close ministry partner) work together more effectively for the gospel?
  • What does Priscilla's example teach you about the value of behind-the-scenes ministry?
