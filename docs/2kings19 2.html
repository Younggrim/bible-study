2 Kings — Chapter 19

1As soon as King Hezekiah heard it, he tore his clothes and covered himself with sackcloth and went into the house of the LORD.

2And he sent Eliakim, who was over the household, and Shebna the secretary, and the senior priests, covered with sackcloth, to the prophet Isaiah the son of Amoz.

3They said to him, “Thus says Hezekiah, This day is a day of distress, of rebuke, and of disgrace; children have come to the point of birth, and there is no strength to bring them forth.

4It may be that the LORD your God heard all the words of the Rabshakeh, whom his master the king of Assyria has sent to mock the living God, and will rebuke the words that the LORD your God has heard; therefore lift up your prayer for the remnant that is left.”

5When the servants of King Hezekiah came to Isaiah,

6Isaiah said to them, “Say to your master, ‘Thus says the LORD: Do not be afraid because of the words that you have heard, with which the servants of the king of Assyria have reviled me.

7Behold, I will put a spirit in him, so that he shall hear a rumor and return to his own land, and I will make him fall by the sword in his own land.’”

8The Rabshakeh returned, and found the king of Assyria fighting against Libnah, for he heard that the king had left Lachish.

9Now the king heard concerning Tirhakah king of Cush, “Behold, he has set out to fight against you.” So he sent messengers again to Hezekiah, saying,

10“Thus shall you speak to Hezekiah king of Judah: ‘Do not let your God in whom you trust deceive you by promising that Jerusalem will not be given into the hand of the king of Assyria.

11Behold, you have heard what the kings of Assyria have done to all lands, devoting them to destruction. And shall you be delivered?

12Have the gods of the nations delivered them, the nations that my fathers destroyed, Gozan, Haran, Rezeph, and the people of Eden who were in Telassar?

13Where is the king of Hamath, the king of Arpad, the king of the city of Sepharvaim, the king of Hena, or the king of Ivvah?’”

14Hezekiah received the letter from the hand of the messengers and read it; and Hezekiah went up to the house of the LORD and spread it before the LORD.

15And Hezekiah prayed before the LORD and said: “O LORD, the God of Israel, enthroned above the cherubim, you are the God, you alone, of all the kingdoms of the earth; you have made the heavens and the earth.

16Incline your ear, O LORD, and hear; open your eyes, O LORD, and see; and hear the words of Sennacherib, which he has sent to mock the living God.

17Truly, O LORD, the kings of Assyria have laid waste the nations and their lands

18and have cast their gods into the fire, for they were not gods, but the work of men’s hands, wood and stone. Therefore they were destroyed.

19So now, O LORD our God, save us, please, from his hand, that all the kingdoms of the earth may know that you, O LORD, are God alone.”

20Then Isaiah the son of Amoz sent to Hezekiah, saying, “Thus says the LORD, the God of Israel: Your prayer to me about Sennacherib king of Assyria I have heard.

21This is the word that the LORD has spoken concerning him: “She despises you, she scorns you— the virgin daughter of Zion; she wags her head behind you— the daughter of Jerusalem.

22“Whom have you mocked and reviled? Against whom have you raised your voice and lifted your eyes to the heights? Against the Holy One of Israel!

23By your messengers you have mocked the Lord, and you have said, ‘With my many chariots I have gone up the heights of the mountains, to the far recesses of Lebanon; I felled its tallest cedars, its choicest cypresses; I entered its farthest lodging place, its most fruitful forest.

24I dug wells and drank foreign waters, and I dried up with the sole of my foot all the streams of Egypt.’

25“Have you not heard that I determined it long ago? I planned from days of old what now I bring to pass, that you should turn fortified cities into heaps of ruins,

26while their inhabitants, shorn of strength, are dismayed and confounded, and have become like plants of the field and like tender grass, like grass on the housetops, blighted before it is grown.

27“But I know your sitting down and your going out and coming in, and your raging against me.

28Because you have raged against me and your complacency has come into my ears, I will put my hook in your nose and my bit in your mouth, and I will turn you back on the way by which you came.

29“And this shall be the sign for you: this year eat what grows of itself, and in the second year what springs of the same. Then in the third year sow and reap and plant vineyards, and eat their fruit.

30And the surviving remnant of the house of Judah shall again take root downward and bear fruit upward.

31For out of Jerusalem shall go a remnant, and out of Mount Zion a band of survivors. The zeal of the LORD will do this.

32“Therefore thus says the LORD concerning the king of Assyria: He shall not come into this city or shoot an arrow there, or come before it with a shield or cast up a siege mound against it.

33By the way that he came, by the same he shall return, and he shall not come into this city, declares the LORD.

34For I will defend this city to save it, for my own sake and for the sake of my servant David.”

35And that night the angel of the LORD went out and struck down 185,000 in the camp of the Assyrians. And when people arose early in the morning, behold, these were all dead bodies.

36Then Sennacherib king of Assyria departed and went home and lived at Nineveh.

37And as he was worshiping in the house of Nisroch his god, Adrammelech and Sharezer, his sons, struck him down with the sword and escaped into the land of Ararat. And Esarhaddon his son reigned in his place.

Summary

Hezekiah trusts God when Sennacherib of Assyria besieges Jerusalem; Isaiah prophesies deliverance. The Angel of the LORD kills 185,000 Assyrian soldiers in one night, and Sennacherib retreats to be murdered by his own sons.

Key Verses

v.1
"And it happened, when king Hezekiah heard it, that he rent his clothes, and covered himself with sackcloth, and went into the house of the LORD." (ESV) — The model response to crisis: grief expressed honestly, humility demonstrated physically, and refuge sought in God's presence. Hezekiah did not call a war council or send to Egypt — he went to the Temple. The first response to overwhelming threat should be worship, not strategy.
v.15
"And Hezekiah prayed before the LORD, and said, O LORD God of Israel, which dwellest between the cherubims, you are the God, even you alone, of all the kingdoms of the earth: you have made heaven and earth." (ESV) — Prayer grounded in theology. Hezekiah begins not with his problem but with God's character: sovereign, unique, Creator. Right theology produces right prayer. When you know who God is, you know what to ask.
v.19
"Now therefore, O LORD our God, I beseech you, save you us out of his hand, that all the kingdoms of the earth may know that you are the LORD God, even you only." (ESV) — The motive of Hezekiah's prayer: not merely survival but God's glory among the nations. He asks for deliverance so that the world will know God is God. This transforms a desperate plea into a missionary prayer.
v.28
"Because your raging against me and your complacency have come to my ears, I will put my hook in your nose and my bit in your mouth, and I will turn you back on the way by which you came." (ESV) — God addresses Sennacherib directly: the conqueror of nations is merely an animal on God's leash. The hook and bridle imagery reduces the most powerful man on earth to a beast led by its master. God's sovereignty is absolute over all earthly power.
v.35
"And it happened that night, that the angel of the LORD went out, and smote in the camp of the Assyrians an hundred fourscore and five thousand: and when they arose early in the morning, behold, they were all dead corpses." (ESV) — The most dramatic divine intervention since the Exodus. No human army fought. No siege was required. One angel, one night, total destruction. "The battle is the LORD's" (1 Samuel 17:47) demonstrated in its most literal form.

Cross-References

  • v.1 — 2 Chronicles 20:3-4 — Jehoshaphat's similar response to military threat: fasting, prayer, and seeking God. The pattern of faithful kings in crisis is consistent: turn to God first, not last.
  • v.14 — Psalm 31:14-15 — "I trusted in you, O LORD... My times are in your hand: deliver me from the hand of mine enemies." Hezekiah's act of spreading the letter before God embodies the Psalms' theology of casting burdens on the LORD.
  • v.15 — Psalm 80:1 — "You that dwellest between the cherubims, shine forth." Hezekiah's prayer language echoes the Psalms' address to God enthroned above the ark. He prays from Israel's worship tradition.
  • v.19 — Exodus 9:16 — "For this cause have I raised you up, for to show in you my power; and that my name may be declared throughout all the earth." God's purpose in confronting Pharaoh parallels His purpose in confronting Sennacherib: His name declared among the nations.
  • v.25 — Isaiah 10:5-7,15 — "Ah, Assyria, the rod of my anger... Shall the axe boast over him who hews with it?" God's extended commentary on Assyria as His unwitting instrument. Sennacherib thought he conquered by his own power; God reveals he was merely a tool.
  • v.30 — Isaiah 37:31 — The identical prophecy in Isaiah's parallel account. "Take root downward, and bear fruit upward" — the remnant theology that sustains hope through exile and beyond. Roots before fruit; depth before productivity.
  • v.35 — Exodus 12:29 — "At midnight the LORD smote all the firstborn in the land of Egypt." The angel's destruction of the Assyrian army echoes the Passover — God striking the oppressor overnight while His people are preserved. The new exodus pattern: God delivers His people by destroying their enemies while they sleep.
  • v.37 — Isaiah 37:38 — The parallel account confirms Sennacherib's assassination by his sons Adrammelech and Sharezer. Assyrian records confirm a succession crisis after Sennacherib's death. His god Nisroch could not save him in his own temple.

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/2-kings-19/ Guzik emphasizes that Hezekiah's prayer is a model of crisis intercession: it begins with God's character (v.15), acknowledges the reality of the threat (v.17), identifies the theological issue (v.18 — the other gods were not real gods), and makes a God-centered request (v.19 — that all nations may know). Guzik notes that God's response through Isaiah is both poetry and prophecy — mocking Sennacherib's arrogance while declaring God's absolute sovereignty over history. The 185,000 dead demonstrate that God does not need human armies to accomplish His purposes.
  • Charles Spurgeon: "Hezekiah spread the letter before the LORD. Here is the secret of prevailing prayer: take your trouble, whatever it is, and spread it before God. Do not merely tell Him about it — show it to Him. Lay it open. Hide nothing. Let Him read every threatening word, every impossible demand, every blasphemous boast. And then leave it there. Hezekiah did not take the letter back. He spread it and prayed. The man who can spread his case before God and leave it there has learned the highest lesson of faith. One hundred and eighty-five thousand dead by morning — that is what God does with the problems we leave in His hands."

Translations

  • v.3 — KJV: "a day of trouble, and of rebuke, and blasphemy" / ESV: "a day of distress, of rebuke, and of disgrace" — Hebrew "ne'atsah" can mean blasphemy, contempt, or disgrace. KJV's "blasphemy" interprets it as the Rabshakeh's words against God; ESV's "disgrace" interprets it as Judah's humiliation. Both meanings are present in the context — the Rabshakeh's blasphemy has brought disgrace upon Judah.
  • v.7 — KJV: "I will send a blast upon him" / ESV: "I will put a spirit in him" — Hebrew "ruach" means wind, spirit, or breath. KJV's "blast" suggests a destructive force; ESV's "spirit" suggests an inner disposition (fear or anxiety) that causes him to retreat. The ESV reading fits the fulfillment better: Sennacherib heard a "rumour" (about Tirhakah) and withdrew — an internal motivation rather than an external force.
  • v.21 — KJV: "The virgin the daughter of Zion hath despised thee, and laughed thee to scorn" / ESV: "She despises you, she scorns you — the virgin daughter of Zion" — The ESV restructures for clarity, placing the subject identification after the verbs. Both convey the same meaning: Jerusalem, personified as an unconquered virgin, mocks the would-be conqueror. The "virgin" imagery emphasizes that Jerusalem has never been violated by Assyria — and never will be.
  • v.25 — KJV: "Hast thou not heard long ago how I have done it, and of ancient times that I have formed it?" / ESV: "Have you not heard that I determined it long ago? I planned from days of old what now I bring to pass" — The ESV makes explicit what the KJV leaves compressed: God planned Assyria's conquests in eternity past and is now executing that plan. Sennacherib's victories were God's predetermined purpose, not Assyria's independent achievement.
  • v.35 — KJV: "an hundred fourscore and five thousand" / ESV: "185,000" — KJV uses the traditional English number-word format; ESV uses numerals. The number is identical: 185,000 Assyrian soldiers killed in one night. Some scholars have debated whether this was plague, divine fire, or direct angelic action — the text simply says "the angel of the LORD went out, and smote."

Reflection

  • 1. Spread it before the LORD (v.14). Hezekiah took the threatening letter and physically laid it before God. This is the model for prayer in crisis: bring the specific problem — the exact words, the precise threat, the actual document — and spread it before God. Do not generalize your prayers. Be specific. Show God the letter. Name the fear. Lay it open and leave it in His presence.
  • 2. Right theology produces right prayer (v.15). Hezekiah's prayer begins with who God is: Creator, sovereign, unique. He does not start with his problem but with God's character. When your theology is right, your prayers align with reality. Before you ask God to act, remind yourself who He is. The size of your God determines the size of your faith.
  • 3. God's purposes transcend your survival (v.19). Hezekiah prayed for deliverance "that all the kingdoms of the earth may know that thou art the LORD God." His prayer was not merely "save me" but "glorify yourself through saving me." When your prayers become about God's reputation rather than merely your comfort, you have entered the realm of prayers God delights to answer.
  • 4. The most powerful enemies are still on God's leash (v.28). Sennacherib conquered nations — but God put a hook in his nose and led him home. No enemy, no circumstance, no power on earth operates outside God's sovereign control. The threat that terrifies you is already under God's authority. He has a hook in its nose. It can go no further than He permits, and He will turn it back when His purpose is accomplished.
  • 5. God fights for His own name's sake (v.34). "I will defend this city... for mine own sake, and for my servant David's sake." God's deliverance is ultimately grounded in His own glory and His covenant faithfulness — not in our worthiness. This is liberating: you do not have to earn God's protection. He acts for His name. When you belong to Him, His reputation is bound up with your deliverance. He will not let His name be mocked through your destruction.
