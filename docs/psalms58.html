Psalms — Chapter 58

1Do you indeed decree what is right, you gods? Do you judge the children of man uprightly?

2No, in your hearts you devise wrongs; your hands deal out violence on earth.

3The wicked are estranged from the womb; they go astray from birth, speaking lies.

4They have venom like the venom of a serpent, like the deaf adder that stops its ear,

5so that it does not hear the voice of charmers or of the cunning enchanter.

6O God, break the teeth in their mouths; tear out the fangs of the young lions, O LORD!

7Let them vanish like water that runs away; when he aims his arrows, let them be blunted.

8Let them be like the snail that dissolves into slime, like the stillborn child who never sees the sun.

9Sooner than your pots can feel the heat of thorns, whether green or ablaze, may he sweep them away!

10The righteous will rejoice when he sees the vengeance; he will bathe his feet in the blood of the wicked.

11Mankind will say, “Surely there is a reward for the righteous; surely there is a God who judges on earth.”

Summary

An imprecatory psalm — David confronts unjust rulers who speak not righteousness, praying God will break their teeth and make them vanish; the righteous will rejoice when they see vengeance, for God judges the earth.

Key Verses

v.1
"Do ye indeed speak righteousness, O congregation? do ye judge uprightly, O ye sons of men?" (KJV) — A rhetorical challenge to those in authority. The expected answer is no. Those entrusted with justice have perverted it. ESV renders "you gods" — viewing rulers as divine representatives who have failed their mandate.
v.3
"The wicked are estranged from the womb: they go astray as soon as they be born, speaking lies." (KJV) — Like Psalm 51:5, this affirms innate sinfulness. Wickedness is not merely learned behavior but a condition present from birth. The propensity to lie is natural to fallen humanity.
v.6
"Break their teeth, O God, in their mouth: break out the great teeth of the young lions, O LORD." (KJV) — The wicked are compared to predatory beasts. Breaking their teeth = removing their power to devour the weak. This is not personal vindictiveness but a cry for the protection of victims.
v.11
"So that a man shall say, Verily there is a reward for the righteous: verily he is a God that judgeth in the earth." (KJV) — The ultimate purpose of divine judgment: to demonstrate that righteousness is rewarded and that God actively governs. Justice is not an illusion.

Cross-References

  • v.1 — Psalm 82:1-2 — "God standeth in the congregation of the mighty; he judgeth among the gods." Same address to unjust rulers as "gods." v.3 — Psalm 51:5 — "In sin did my mother conceive me." Same doctrine of innate sinfulness. v.4 — Romans 3:13 — "The poison of asps is under their lips." Paul uses this serpent imagery for universal sin. v.6 — Psalm 3:7 — "Thou hast broken the teeth of the ungodly." Same imagery of disarming the wicked. v.10 — Revelation 19:2 — "True and righteous are his judgments... he hath avenged the blood of his servants." Ultimate vindication. v.11 — Psalm 9:16 — "The LORD is known by the judgment which he executeth." God revealed through justice.

Translations

  • v.1 — KJV: "O congregation" / ESV: "you gods" — The Hebrew "elem" is highly debated. It could be: (1) a scribal variant of "elim" (gods/mighty ones = rulers); (2) related to "elem" (silence); or (3) a form of "eylam" (congregation). ESV follows the interpretation of rulers as God's representatives.
  • v.8 — KJV: "As a snail which melteth" / ESV: "like the snail that dissolves into slime" — The ancient understanding was that snails melted as they moved (their trail being their substance disappearing). The image is of gradual dissolution and disappearance.
  • v.9 — KJV: "Before your pots can feel the thorns" / ESV: "Sooner than your pots can feel the heat of thorns" — An image of swiftness: before a cooking fire of thorns can even heat the pot, God's judgment comes. Thorns burn quickly but even that speed is too slow for God's justice.

Reflection

  • 1. Those in authority will answer for how they use their power (vv.1-2). Position carries responsibility. Every judge, ruler, and leader will give account for whether they used their authority for justice or for personal gain.
  • 2. Human wickedness is innate, not merely environmental (v.3). This is uncomfortable but biblical. We are not born neutral and corrupted by society — we are born with a bent toward sin. This makes grace necessary, not optional.
  • 3. It is right to pray for justice against oppressors (v.6). The imprecatory psalms are not about personal revenge but about God's protection of the vulnerable. When the powerful crush the weak, it is righteous to cry out for God to intervene.
  • 4. God's judgment vindicates His character (v.11). When justice finally comes, it proves that God is real, that He sees, and that righteousness matters. Delayed justice is not denied justice — God WILL judge the earth.
