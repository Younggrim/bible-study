1 John — Chapter 1

1That which was from the beginning, which we have heard, which we have seen with our eyes, which we looked upon and have touched with our hands, concerning the word of life—

2the life was made manifest, and we have seen it, and testify to it and proclaim to you the eternal life, which was with the Father and was made manifest to us—

3that which we have seen and heard we proclaim also to you, so that you too may have fellowship with us; and indeed our fellowship is with the Father and with his Son Jesus Christ.

4And we are writing these things so that our joy may be complete.

5This is the message we have heard from him and proclaim to you, that God is light, and in him is no darkness at all.

6If we say we have fellowship with him while we walk in darkness, we lie and do not practice the truth.

7But if we walk in the light, as he is in the light, we have fellowship with one another, and the blood of Jesus his Son cleanses us from all sin.

8If we say we have no sin, we deceive ourselves, and the truth is not in us.

9If we confess our sins, he is faithful and just to forgive us our sins and to cleanse us from all unrighteousness.

10If we say we have not sinned, we make him a liar, and his word is not in us.

Summary

John declares the Word of life which was from the beginning — seen, heard, and touched — so that readers may have fellowship with the Father and Son, walking in the light where Christ's blood cleanses from all sin, and confessing sin to find faithful forgiveness.

Authorship & Background

Author: The Apostle John (unnamed but identified by style, theology, and early church tradition). The same author as the Gospel of John and Revelation. Written approximately AD 85-95 from Ephesus, where John spent his later years.
Recipient: Believers in Asia Minor (likely churches around Ephesus). John writes as an elderly pastor ("my little children") to a community threatened by false teachers who denied Christ's true humanity (early Gnosticism).
Purpose: To give assurance of salvation (5:13), to combat false teaching about Christ's nature, and to call believers to walk in the light, love one another, and keep God's commandments. John provides "tests of life" — how to know you truly know God.
Theme: Fellowship with God and assurance of salvation. Three tests repeated: the doctrinal test (believing rightly about Christ), the moral test (obeying God's commands), and the social test (loving one another).

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): enduringword.com Guzik covers the eyewitness testimony, fellowship, God as light, walking in light, and the promise of confession and cleansing.
  • Charles Spurgeon: Sermon: "If We Confess Our Sins" on v.9 (MTP Vol. 24, No. 1439) "If we CONFESS — not hide, not excuse, not minimize — confess. Agree with God about your sin. Call it what He calls it. He is FAITHFUL — He promised to forgive, and He keeps His promises. And JUST — the cross satisfied justice, so forgiveness is not unjust but righteous. To FORGIVE — pardon, release. And to CLEANSE — not just forgive but purify. From ALL unrighteousness — no sin too great, no stain too deep. Confess and be clean."

Reflection

  • 1. "God is light, and in him is no darkness at all" (v.5). Is there darkness in your life that you're hiding from God? He is pure light — nothing is hidden from Him. Are you walking in the light or hiding in shadows?
  • 2. "If we walk in the light... the blood of Jesus Christ his Son cleanseth us from all sin" (v.7). Walking in the light doesn't mean sinless perfection — it means living openly before God. When you do, Christ's blood keeps cleansing you. Are you walking openly?
  • 3. "If we confess our sins" (v.9). Is there unconfessed sin in your life? Confession is agreeing with God — calling sin what He calls it. He promises to forgive AND cleanse. What do you need to confess today?
  • 4. "If we say that we have no sin, we deceive ourselves" (v.8). Self- deception about sin is dangerous. Are you honest about your sinfulness? Or do you minimize, excuse, or deny?
  • 5. "That your joy may be full" (v.4). John writes so that joy would be complete. Is your joy full? Fellowship with God and honest living produce full joy. What's blocking yours?
