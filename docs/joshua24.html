Joshua — Chapter 24

1Joshua gathered all the tribes of Israel to Shechem and summoned the elders, the heads, the judges, and the officers of Israel. And they presented themselves before God.

2And Joshua said to all the people, “Thus says the LORD, the God of Israel, ‘Long ago, your fathers lived beyond the Euphrates, Terah, the father of Abraham and of Nahor; and they served other gods.

3Then I took your father Abraham from beyond the River and led him through all the land of Canaan, and made his offspring many. I gave him Isaac.

4And to Isaac I gave Jacob and Esau. And I gave Esau the hill country of Seir to possess, but Jacob and his children went down to Egypt.

5And I sent Moses and Aaron, and I plagued Egypt with what I did in the midst of it, and afterward I brought you out.

6“‘Then I brought your fathers out of Egypt, and you came to the sea. And the Egyptians pursued your fathers with chariots and horsemen to the Red Sea.

7And when they cried to the LORD, he put darkness between you and the Egyptians and made the sea come upon them and cover them; and your eyes saw what I did in Egypt. And you lived in the wilderness a long time.

8Then I brought you to the land of the Amorites, who lived on the other side of the Jordan. They fought with you, and I gave them into your hand, and you took possession of their land, and I destroyed them before you.

9Then Balak the son of Zippor, king of Moab, arose and fought against Israel. And he sent and invited Balaam the son of Beor to curse you,

10but I would not listen to Balaam. Indeed, he blessed you. So I delivered you out of his hand.

11And you went over the Jordan and came to Jericho, and the leaders of Jericho fought against you, and also the Amorites, the Perizzites, the Canaanites, the Hittites, the Girgashites, the Hivites, and the Jebusites. And I gave them into your hand.

12And I sent the hornet before you, which drove them out before you, the two kings of the Amorites; it was not by your sword or by your bow.

13I gave you a land on which you had not labored and cities that you had not built, and you dwell in them. You eat the fruit of vineyards and olive orchards that you did not plant.’

14“Now therefore fear the LORD and serve him in sincerity and in faithfulness. Put away the gods that your fathers served beyond the River and in Egypt, and serve the LORD.

15And if it is evil in your eyes to serve the LORD, choose this day whom you will serve, whether the gods your fathers served in the region beyond the River, or the gods of the Amorites in whose land you dwell. But as for me and my house, we will serve the LORD.”

16Then the people answered, “Far be it from us that we should forsake the LORD to serve other gods,

17for it is the LORD our God who brought us and our fathers up from the land of Egypt, out of the house of slavery, and who did those great signs in our sight and preserved us in all the way that we went, and among all the peoples through whom we passed.

18And the LORD drove out before us all the peoples, the Amorites who lived in the land. Therefore we also will serve the LORD, for he is our God.”

19But Joshua said to the people, “You are not able to serve the LORD, for he is a holy God. He is a jealous God; he will not forgive your transgressions or your sins.

20If you forsake the LORD and serve foreign gods, then he will turn and do you harm and consume you, after having done you good.”

21And the people said to Joshua, “No, but we will serve the LORD.”

22Then Joshua said to the people, “You are witnesses against yourselves that you have chosen the LORD, to serve him.” And they said, “We are witnesses.”

23He said, “Then put away the foreign gods that are among you, and incline your heart to the LORD, the God of Israel.”

24And the people said to Joshua, “The LORD our God we will serve, and his voice we will obey.”

25So Joshua made a covenant with the people that day, and put in place statutes and rules for them at Shechem.

26And Joshua wrote these words in the Book of the Law of God. And he took a large stone and set it up there under the terebinth that was by the sanctuary of the LORD.

27And Joshua said to all the people, “Behold, this stone shall be a witness against us, for it has heard all the words of the LORD that he spoke to us. Therefore it shall be a witness against you, lest you deal falsely with your God.”

28So Joshua sent the people away, every man to his inheritance.

29After these things Joshua the son of Nun, the servant of the LORD, died, being 110 years old.

30And they buried him in his own inheritance at Timnath-serah, which is in the hill country of Ephraim, north of the mountain of Gaash.

31Israel served the LORD all the days of Joshua, and all the days of the elders who outlived Joshua and had known all the work that the LORD did for Israel.

32As for the bones of Joseph, which the people of Israel brought up from Egypt, they buried them at Shechem, in the piece of land that Jacob bought from the sons of Hamor the father of Shechem for a hundred pieces of money. It became an inheritance of the descendants of Joseph.

33And Eleazar the son of Aaron died, and they buried him at Gibeah, the town of Phinehas his son, which had been given him in the hill country of Ephraim.

10I refused to respond to Balaam; he kept prophesying good things about you, and I rescued you from his power.

11You crossed the Jordan and came to Jericho. The leaders of Jericho, as well as the Amorites, Perizzites, Canaanites, Hittites, Girgashites, Hivites, and Jebusites, fought against you, but I handed them over to you.

12I sent terror ahead of you to drive out before you the two Amorite kings. I gave you the victory; it was not by your swords or bows.

13I gave you a land in which you had not worked hard; you took up residence in cities you did not build, and you are eating the produce of vineyards and olive groves you did not plant.’

14“Now obey the Lord and worship him with integrity and loyalty. Put aside the gods your ancestors worshiped beyond the Euphrates and in Egypt, and worship the Lord.

Summary

Joshua gathers all Israel at Shechem, recounts God's faithfulness from Abraham to the conquest, and calls them to choose whom they will serve. The people covenant to serve the LORD, and Joshua dies at 110 years old.

Key Verses

v.2
"Your fathers dwelt on the other side of the flood in old time, even Terah, the father of Abraham, and the father of Nachor: and they served other gods." (ESV) — The starting point: Abraham's family were idolaters in Mesopotamia. Israel's story begins not with their merit but with God's sovereign choice. Grace chose Abraham out of paganism.
v.12
"I sent the hornet before you, which drave them out from before you... but not with your sword, nor with your bow." (ESV) — God's emphatic claim: the victory was MINE, not yours. Israel's weapons did not win the land — God's power did. This prevents all boasting.
v.13
"I have given you a land for which you did not labour, and cities which you built not, and you dwell in them; of the vineyards and oliveyards which you planted not do you eat." (ESV) — Pure grace. Every blessing is unearned. The land, the cities, the vineyards — all gifts. This is the foundation for the call to serve: you owe everything to God.
v.14
"Now therefore fear the LORD, and serve him in sincerity and in truth: and put away the gods which your fathers served on the other side of the flood, and in Egypt; and serve you the LORD." (ESV) — The demand that follows grace. Because God has done all this, therefore serve Him — sincerely, truthfully, exclusively. Note: "put away the gods" — some Israelites still had foreign gods among them!
v.15
"And if it is evil in your eyes to serve the LORD, choose this day whom you will serve, whether the gods your fathers served in the region beyond the River, or the gods of the Amorites in whose land you dwell. But as for me and my house, we will serve the LORD." (ESV) — The most famous verse in Joshua. The choice is stark: ancestral gods, local gods, or the LORD. There is no fourth option. Neutrality is impossible. Joshua declares his choice first — leading by example.
v.19
"You cannot serve the LORD: for he is an holy God; he is a jealous God; he will not forgive your transgressions nor your sins." (ESV) — Joshua's shocking response to their pledge. He is not discouraging them but testing the depth of their commitment. Serving God is not casual — He demands absolute loyalty. This is not a God who tolerates divided hearts.
v.23
"Now therefore put away, said he, the strange gods which are among you, and incline your heart to the LORD God of Israel." (ESV) — The practical test: if you mean what you say, ACT. Put away the foreign gods you still possess. Commitment without action is empty words.
v.27
"Behold, this stone shall be a witness to us; for it has heard all the words of the LORD which he spoke to us: it shall be therefore a witness to you, lest you deny your God." (ESV) — The witness stone. A permanent, silent testimony to the covenant made this day. If Israel breaks faith, the stone will testify against them.
v.29
"Joshua the son of Nun, the servant of the LORD, died, being an hundred and ten years old." (ESV) — Joshua's title at death: "the servant of the LORD" — the same title given to Moses (Deuteronomy 34:5). The highest honor: faithful service to God until the end.
v.31
"Israel served the LORD all the days of Joshua, and all the days of the elders that overlived Joshua." (ESV) — Joshua's lasting influence. His generation remained faithful. But the ominous implication: after those elders died, faithfulness ended. The book of Judges begins where this verse ends.

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/joshua-24/ Guzik emphasizes that Joshua's "choose you this day" is not an invitation to consider options but a demand for decisive commitment. The choice is not whether to serve — everyone serves something — but WHOM to serve. He also notes Joshua's surprising "Ye cannot serve the LORD" (v.19) as a test of sincerity: Joshua wants them to count the cost before pledging. Cheap commitment is worthless to a holy God.
  • Charles Spurgeon: "'Choose you this day whom ye will serve.' Not tomorrow — today. Not eventually — now. Joshua does not say, 'Think about it.' He says, 'Choose.' And then he leads: 'As for me and my house, we will serve the LORD.' He does not wait for a committee vote. He does not take a poll. He declares his allegiance and invites others to follow. This is leadership. This is faith. This is the courage of conviction that changes nations."

Translations

  • v.2 — KJV: "on the other side of the flood" / ESV: "beyond the Euphrates" — Hebrew "b'ever ha-nahar." "Nahar" = river (the Euphrates). KJV's "flood" is archaic English for "river" (not Noah's flood). ESV correctly identifies it as the Euphrates. Abraham's family lived in Mesopotamia (Ur, then Haran) — beyond the great river.
  • v.2 — KJV: "they served other gods" / ESV: "they served other gods" — A startling admission: Abraham's own family were idolaters. Jewish tradition (based on this verse) says Terah made and sold idols. God's choice of Abraham was pure grace — not a reward for prior faithfulness.
  • v.12 — KJV: "I sent the hornet before you" / ESV: "I sent the hornet before you" — Hebrew "ha-tsir'ah." "Tsir'ah" = hornet, wasp. Debated whether this is literal (actual hornets/wasps) or figurative (terror, panic, or Egypt — whose symbol was a bee/ hornet). Either way, God claims the victory: "not with thy sword, nor with thy bow."
  • v.14 — KJV: "in sincerity and in truth" / ESV: "in sincerity and in faithfulness" — Hebrew "b'tamim u-v'emet." "Tamim" = complete, whole, without blemish (same word used of sacrificial animals). "Emet" = truth, faithfulness, reliability. KJV's "truth" and ESV's "faithfulness" both translate "emet" — which encompasses both concepts.
  • v.15 — KJV: "choose you this day" / ESV: "choose this day" — Hebrew "bacharu lakhem ha-yom." "Bachar" = to choose, select, prefer. The imperative is plural — addressed to all Israel. The choice is individual but made publicly, corporately, decisively.
  • v.19 — KJV: "Ye cannot serve the LORD" / ESV: "You are not able to serve the LORD" — Hebrew "lo tukh'lu la'avod et YHWH." "Yakol" = to be able. Joshua's statement is provocative — designed to test their resolve. He is saying: "Do you understand what you're committing to? This God is holy and jealous. Half-hearted service is worse than none."
  • v.26 — KJV: "under an oak" / ESV: "under the terebinth" — Hebrew "tachat ha-elah." "Elah" = terebinth (a large tree, not technically an oak). KJV's "oak" is a common but imprecise translation. This may be the same tree under which Jacob buried the foreign gods (Genesis 35:4) — a fitting location for covenant renewal.
  • v.29 — KJV: "the servant of the LORD" / ESV: "the servant of the LORD" — Hebrew "eved YHWH." Joshua's final title — the same given to Moses (Deuteronomy 34:5). In chapter 1:1, Joshua was "Moses' minister/assistant." Now he is "the servant of the LORD" — he has grown into his own direct relationship with God.
  • v.32 — KJV: "for an hundred pieces of silver" / ESV: "for a hundred pieces of money" — Hebrew "b'me'ah q'sitah." "Qesitah" is an ancient unit of value — its exact worth is unknown. KJV's "silver" is interpretive; ESV's "money" is more cautious. The term appears only here, Genesis 33:19, and Job 42:11.

Reflection

  • 1. "Choose you this day whom ye will serve" (v.15). The most urgent question any person faces. There is no neutrality — everyone serves something. The only question is WHAT. Joshua presents the options clearly: dead gods of the past, local gods of the culture, or the living LORD. Choose. Today. Not tomorrow.
  • 2. "As for me and my house, we will serve the LORD" (v.15). Joshua does not wait for consensus. He leads by declaring his own commitment first. Leaders go first. Fathers go first. The household follows the head. What have you declared for your house?
  • 3. Grace precedes demand (vv.2-13). Before God asks for commitment, He recounts His gifts. "I took... I gave... I sent... I brought... I delivered." The call to serve is grounded in what God has already done. We do not serve to earn His favor — we serve because we have already received it.
  • 4. "Ye cannot serve the LORD" (v.19). Joshua's shocking statement tests their sincerity. Serving God is not casual. He is holy, jealous, and will not tolerate rivals. Count the cost before you pledge (Luke 14:28). Better to not vow than to vow and not pay (Ecclesiastes 5:5).
  • 5. "Put away the strange gods which are among you" (v.23). Even after the conquest, some Israelites still had foreign gods! Commitment to the LORD requires active removal of rivals. What "gods" do we still harbor — comfort, approval, control, pleasure? Put them away. Today.
  • 6. Joseph's bones buried at Shechem (v.32). A 400-year-old promise fulfilled. Joseph believed God would bring Israel to Canaan — and made them swear to bring his bones. Faith looks beyond death. Faith makes plans for a future it will not see. Joseph's bones testify: God keeps His word across centuries.
  • 7. "Israel served the LORD all the days of Joshua" (v.31). One generation's faithfulness. But the next generation "knew not the LORD" (Judges 2:10). Faithfulness is not automatically inherited. Each generation must choose for itself. We cannot coast on our parents' faith.
  • 8. Three burials close the book (vv.29-33). Joshua, Joseph, Eleazar — the leader, the patriarch, the priest. An era ends. The generation that conquered Canaan passes away. What remains? The land, the covenant, the choice — and the question: will the next generation serve the LORD?
