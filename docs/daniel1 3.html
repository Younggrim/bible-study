Daniel — Chapter 1

1In the third year of the reign of Jehoiakim king of Judah, Nebuchadnezzar king of Babylon came to Jerusalem and besieged it.

2And the Lord gave Jehoiakim king of Judah into his hand, with some of the vessels of the house of God. And he brought them to the land of Shinar, to the house of his god, and placed the vessels in the treasury of his god.

3Then the king commanded Ashpenaz, his chief eunuch, to bring some of the people of Israel, both of the royal family and of the nobility,

4youths without blemish, of good appearance and skillful in all wisdom, endowed with knowledge, understanding learning, and competent to stand in the king’s palace, and to teach them the literature and language of the Chaldeans.

5The king assigned them a daily portion of the food that the king ate, and of the wine that he drank. They were to be educated for three years, and at the end of that time they were to stand before the king.

6Among these were Daniel, Hananiah, Mishael, and Azariah of the tribe of Judah.

7And the chief of the eunuchs gave them names: Daniel he called Belteshazzar, Hananiah he called Shadrach, Mishael he called Meshach, and Azariah he called Abednego.

8But Daniel resolved that he would not defile himself with the king’s food, or with the wine that he drank. Therefore he asked the chief of the eunuchs to allow him not to defile himself.

9And God gave Daniel favor and compassion in the sight of the chief of the eunuchs,

10and the chief of the eunuchs said to Daniel, “I fear my lord the king, who assigned your food and your drink; for why should he see that you were in worse condition than the youths who are of your own age? So you would endanger my head with the king.”

11Then Daniel said to the steward whom the chief of the eunuchs had assigned over Daniel, Hananiah, Mishael, and Azariah,

12“Test your servants for ten days; let us be given vegetables to eat and water to drink.

13Then let our appearance and the appearance of the youths who eat the king’s food be observed by you, and deal with your servants according to what you see.”

14So he listened to them in this matter, and tested them for ten days.

15At the end of ten days it was seen that they were better in appearance and fatter in flesh than all the youths who ate the king’s food.

16So the steward took away their food and the wine they were to drink, and gave them vegetables.

17As for these four youths, God gave them learning and skill in all literature and wisdom, and Daniel had understanding in all visions and dreams.

18At the end of the time, when the king had commanded that they should be brought in, the chief of the eunuchs brought them in before Nebuchadnezzar.

19And the king spoke with them, and among all of them none was found like Daniel, Hananiah, Mishael, and Azariah. Therefore they stood before the king.

20And in every matter of wisdom and understanding about which the king inquired of them, he found them ten times better than all the magicians and enchanters that were in all his kingdom.

21And Daniel was there until the first year of King Cyrus.

Summary

Daniel and three young Hebrew nobles are carried captive to Babylon in 605 BC during Nebuchadnezzar's first siege. Selected for royal training, they refuse the king's food and wine, choosing faithfulness over compromise. God rewards their resolve with wisdom and understanding ten times greater than all the magicians of Babylon.

Key Verses

v.2
"And the Lord gave Jehoiakim king of Judah into his hand, with part of the vessels of the house of God: which he carried into the land of Shinar to the house of his god" (KJV) — "The Lord GAVE." Exile wasn't Nebuchadnezzar's victory — it was God's sovereign act of discipline. Even the temple vessels moving to Babylon were under God's direction.
v.8
"But Daniel purposed in his heart that he would not defile himself with the portion of the king's meat, nor with the wine which he drank" (KJV) — "Purposed in his heart" — the decision was made BEFORE the temptation arrived. This is the key: resolve precedes resistance. Daniel didn't deliberate in the moment; he had already decided.
v.9
"Now God had brought Daniel into favour and tender love with the prince of the eunuchs" (KJV) — God works in pagan hearts. The same God who allowed the exile now softens the captor. Favor in hostile territory is God's specialty (cf. Joseph in Egypt, Esther in Persia).
v.17
"As for these four children, God gave them knowledge and skill in all learning and wisdom: and Daniel had understanding in all visions and dreams" (KJV) — God GAVE. Every advantage was divine gift. Their faithfulness didn't earn the wisdom — God's grace bestowed it.
v.20
"And in all matters of wisdom and understanding, that the king enquired of them, he found them ten times better than all the magicians and astrologers that were in all his realm" (KJV) — TEN TIMES. Not marginally better — an order of magnitude. God's wisdom doesn't just match the world's; it dwarfs it entirely.

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/daniel-1/
  • Charles Spurgeon: "Daniel 'purposed in his heart.' Here is the secret of all holy living: the PURPOSE comes before the practice. The young man who waits until temptation arrives to decide what he will do has already decided — he will fall. But he who has settled the matter with God beforehand stands like a rock in the flood."

Translations

  • v.2 — "Shinar" (KJV/ESV) — Hebrew "Shin'ar" = the ancient name for Babylonia (cf. Genesis 11:2 — the Tower of Babel was built in Shinar). The use of this archaic name links Nebuchadnezzar's empire to humanity's oldest rebellion against God.
  • v.4 — "Chaldeans" (KJV/ESV) — Hebrew "Kasdim" = both an ethnic group and a priestly/scholarly class in Babylon. These were the learned elite — astronomers, diviners, interpreters. Daniel was being trained to JOIN their ranks.
  • v.7 — "Belteshazzar" (KJV/ESV) — Babylonian "Balat-su-usur" = "Bel, protect his life." Daniel's Hebrew name meant "God is my judge." The renaming was an attempt to transfer allegiance from Yahweh to Bel (Marduk). It failed.
  • v.8 — "defile himself" (KJV) / "defile himself" (ESV) — Hebrew "yitga'al" = to make oneself ceremonially unclean. The food had been offered to idols (Deuteronomy 32:38) and included unclean animals (Leviticus 11). This wasn't preference — it was Torah obedience.

Reflection

  • 1. "Daniel purposed in his heart" (v.8). The battle is won or lost BEFORE the temptation arrives. What have you already decided? What convictions are settled so deeply that no pressure can move them? Purpose it NOW — before the test.
  • 2. God gives favour in hostile territory (v.9). You may be in a workplace, school, or family that is spiritually hostile. God can soften hearts you thought were stone. Don't assume hostility is permanent — pray for favour.
  • 3. Faithfulness in small things precedes promotion (vv.17-20). Daniel was faithful with FOOD before God trusted him with KINGDOMS. What "small" area of obedience are you rationalizing away? The big calling requires small faithfulness first.
  • 4. The world will try to rename you (v.7). Culture wants to redefine your identity — strip away your God-given purpose and replace it with its own labels. But Daniel never forgot who he was. Don't let the world's renaming stick.
  • 5. "The Lord GAVE" (v.2). Even exile was God's sovereign act. If you're in a season that feels like captivity — job loss, illness, relocation — remember: God gave you into this season. He has purposes IN it, not just AFTER it.
