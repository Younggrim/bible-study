Ten Commandments

Do Not Take God's Name in Vain

The Commandment

The third commandment, "You shall not take the name of the LORD your God in vain" (Exodus 20:7), guards the holiness of God's revealed name. In ancient Israel, a name was not merely a label but a revelation of character and essence. God's name — YHWH — represented His covenant faithfulness, His power, His very presence among His people. To "take up" this name in vain meant to carry it emptily, to invoke it without weight or reverence, to use it as a tool for personal advantage.

This prohibition extends far beyond casual profanity or thoughtless exclamations. It encompasses swearing falsely by God's name, making vows in His name without intending to keep them, invoking His authority to support lies, and claiming to speak for God when He has not spoken. It addresses religious hypocrisy — bearing the name of God's people while living in contradiction to His character. The seriousness of this command is underscored by its unique warning: "The LORD will not hold him guiltless who takes his name in vain."

Jesus deepened this commandment in the Sermon on the Mount, teaching His disciples to simply let their "yes" be yes and their "no" be no, rather than invoking elaborate oaths (Matthew 5:33-37). The New Testament vision is of people whose integrity is so consistent that they need not invoke God's name as a guarantor — their character itself bears witness to the God whose name they carry.

Key Scriptures

  • Exodus 20:7 — "You shall not take the name of the LORD your God in vain, for the LORD will not hold him guiltless who takes his name in vain."
  • Matthew 6:9 — "Pray then like this: Our Father in heaven, hallowed be your name."
  • Philippians 2:9-11 — "God has highly exalted him and bestowed on him the name that is above every name."
  • James 5:12 — "Do not swear, either by heaven or by earth or by any other oath, but let your 'yes' be yes and your 'no' be no."
  • Leviticus 19:12 — "You shall not swear by my name falsely, and so profane the name of your God: I am the LORD."

Heart of the Law

The heart of this commandment is reverence — a deep, settled awareness that God's name represents His very person, and that to invoke it is to stand in His presence. When we pray "hallowed be Your name," we are asking that God's name would be treated as holy — set apart, weighty, honored — in our speech, our worship, and our lives. This is not about fear-based avoidance but about love-based honor.

For Christians who bear the name of Christ, this commandment carries special weight. We are "Christ-ians" — name-bearers. Every interaction, every business dealing, every relationship either hallows or profanes the name we carry. Living in alignment with this commandment means asking: does my life make God's name look beautiful, trustworthy, and good? Or does my conduct cause others to blaspheme the name I claim to revere?

Reflection Questions

  • In what ways might you be "carrying" God's name emptily — professing faith without living it out?
  • How does the awareness that you bear Christ's name change the way you approach everyday conversations and decisions?
  • What would it look like to "hallow" God's name not just in worship but in your workplace, relationships, and private thoughts?
