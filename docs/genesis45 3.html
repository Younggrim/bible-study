Formal equivalent (1901). Very literal, precise. Good for word studies.

Modern, public domain. Based on the Majority Text. Free to use and share.

Genesis — Chapter 45

1Then Joseph could not control himself before all those who stood by him. He cried, “Make everyone go out from me.” So no one stayed with him when Joseph made himself known to his brothers.

2And he wept aloud, so that the Egyptians heard it, and the household of Pharaoh heard it.

3And Joseph said to his brothers, “I am Joseph! Is my father still alive?” But his brothers could not answer him, for they were dismayed at his presence.

4So Joseph said to his brothers, “Come near to me, please.” And they came near. And he said, “I am your brother, Joseph, whom you sold into Egypt.

5And now do not be distressed or angry with yourselves because you sold me here, for God sent me before you to preserve life.

6For the famine has been in the land these two years, and there are yet five years in which there will be neither plowing nor harvest.

7And God sent me before you to preserve for you a remnant on earth, and to keep alive for you many survivors.

8So it was not you who sent me here, but God. He has made me a father to Pharaoh, and lord of all his house and ruler over all the land of Egypt.

9Hurry and go up to my father and say to him, ‘Thus says your son Joseph, God has made me lord of all Egypt. Come down to me; do not tarry.

10You shall dwell in the land of Goshen, and you shall be near me, you and your children and your children’s children, and your flocks, your herds, and all that you have.

11There I will provide for you, for there are yet five years of famine to come, so that you and your household, and all that you have, do not come to poverty.’

12And now your eyes see, and the eyes of my brother Benjamin see, that it is my mouth that speaks to you.

13You must tell my father of all my honor in Egypt, and of all that you have seen. Hurry and bring my father down here.”

14Then he fell upon his brother Benjamin’s neck and wept, and Benjamin wept upon his neck.

15And he kissed all his brothers and wept upon them. After that his brothers talked with him.

16When the report was heard in Pharaoh’s house, “Joseph’s brothers have come,” it pleased Pharaoh and his servants.

17And Pharaoh said to Joseph, “Say to your brothers, ‘Do this: load your beasts and go back to the land of Canaan,

18and take your father and your households, and come to me, and I will give you the best of the land of Egypt, and you shall eat the fat of the land.’

19And you, Joseph, are commanded to say, ‘Do this: take wagons from the land of Egypt for your little ones and for your wives, and bring your father, and come.

20Have no concern for your goods, for the best of all the land of Egypt is yours.’”

21The sons of Israel did so: and Joseph gave them wagons, according to the command of Pharaoh, and gave them provisions for the journey.

22To each and all of them he gave a change of clothes, but to Benjamin he gave three hundred shekels of silver and five changes of clothes.

23To his father he sent as follows: ten donkeys loaded with the good things of Egypt, and ten female donkeys loaded with grain, bread, and provision for his father on the journey.

24Then he sent his brothers away, and as they departed, he said to them, “Do not quarrel on the way.”

25So they went up out of Egypt and came to the land of Canaan to their father Jacob.

26And they told him, “Joseph is still alive, and he is ruler over all the land of Egypt.” And his heart became numb, for he did not believe them.

27But when they told him all the words of Joseph, which he had said to them, and when he saw the wagons that Joseph had sent to carry him, the spirit of their father Jacob revived.

28And Israel said, “It is enough; Joseph my son is still alive. I will go and see him before I die.”

Summary

Joseph reveals himself to his brothers, weeping, and declares that God sent him ahead to preserve life; he invites all of Jacob's family to Egypt.

Key Verses

  • v.3 — "I am Joseph! Is my father still alive?" (ESV) — The great revelation.
  • v.5 — "Do not be distressed or angry with yourselves because you sold me here, for God sent me before you to preserve life." (ESV) — Forgiveness grounded in God's sovereignty.
  • v.7 — "God sent me before you to preserve for you a remnant on earth, and to keep alive for you many survivors." (ESV) — God's purpose: preservation of the covenant line.
  • v.8 — "So it was not you who sent me here, but God." (ESV) — The clearest statement of divine sovereignty over human sin.
  • v.24 — "Do not quarrel on the way." (ESV) — Don't let guilt and blame destroy the reconciliation.
  • v.28 — "It is enough; Joseph my son is still alive. I will go and see him before I die." (ESV) — Faith revived.

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): enduringword.com Guzik emphasizes Joseph's theology of providence (vv.5-8) as a model for how believers should view suffering — God's purpose behind human evil.
  • Charles Spurgeon: Sermon: "Joseph Made Known to His Brethren" (MTP Vol. 18, No. 1048) "I am Joseph — three words that shook the world of those eleven men. Three words that turned terror into joy, guilt into grace, famine into feast. And one day, the greater Joseph — the Lord Jesus Christ — shall make Himself known to His brethren according to the flesh. Israel shall look upon Him whom they pierced, and they shall mourn (Zechariah 12:10). And He shall say, as Joseph said, 'Be not grieved — for God did send me.' The cross was not man's victory over God but God's victory through man's sin. 'It was not you that sent me hither, but God.'"

Translations

  • v.1 → KJV: "could not refrain himself" / ESV: "could not control himself" — Hebrew "lo yakol lehit'appek" — could not contain/restrain himself. The ESV's "control himself" is more natural in modern English. The dam of emotion breaks after Judah's plea.
  • v.3 → KJV: "troubled at his presence" / ESV: "dismayed at his presence" — Hebrew "nivhalu" from "bahal" — terrified, dismayed, panic-stricken. The ESV's "dismayed" captures the terror. Not mere surprise but panic — the man they sold is the most powerful man in Egypt.
  • v.5 → KJV: "God did send me" / ESV: "God sent me" — Hebrew "shalach" — to send with purpose, to commission. Both equivalent. The same word used for God sending prophets. Joseph sees himself as divinely commissioned.
  • v.6 → KJV: "earing" / ESV: "plowing" — The KJV's "earing" is archaic English for "plowing." The ESV uses the modern word. There will be neither plowing nor harvest for five more years.
  • v.24 → KJV: "fall not out by the way" / ESV: "do not quarrel on the way" — Hebrew "al tirgezu" — do not quarrel, do not be agitated. The ESV is immediately clear; the KJV's "fall not out" is archaic for quarreling.
  • v.27 → KJV: "the spirit of Jacob their father revived" / ESV: "the spirit of their father Jacob revived" — Hebrew "vattechi ruach" — his spirit lived again. Both equivalent. Jacob was spiritually dead with grief; the news of Joseph brings resurrection of spirit.
  • TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB) - v.3 — NET: "His brothers could not answer him because they were dumbfounded before him" — "dumbfounded" captures the shock vividly (KJV: "troubled at his presence" / ESV: "dismayed"). WEB: "terrified at his presence." - v.5 — NET: "God sent me ahead of you to preserve life!" — all translations agree on this theological interpretation of Joseph's suffering. The divine purpose language is consistent across ASV, WEB, and NET. - v.7 — NET: "God sent me ahead of you to preserve you on the earth and to save your lives by a great deliverance" — "great deliverance" renders Hebrew "peletah gedolah" (KJV: "a great deliverance" / ESV: "great escape"). All translations preserve this critical theological statement. - v.8 — NET: "He has made me an adviser to Pharaoh" — renders Hebrew "ab" (father) as "adviser" where KJV/ESV/ASV/WEB have "father to Pharaoh." NET interprets the title's functional meaning.

Reflection

  • 1. "It was not you that sent me hither, but God" (v.8). Joseph sees God's hand in his suffering. Can you look back at painful seasons and see God's purpose? This doesn't minimize the sin done to you — it magnifies God's sovereignty over it.
  • 2. "Be not grieved, nor angry with yourselves" (v.5). Joseph releases his brothers from guilt. Is there someone you need to release? Forgiveness doesn't deny the wrong — it refuses to hold it against them.
  • 3. "See that ye fall not out by the way" (v.24). Forgiven people shouldn't quarrel about who was most guilty. In your family, your church — do you let past guilt fuel present conflict?
  • 4. "The spirit of Jacob their father revived" (v.27). Good news brings life. The gospel — "Jesus is alive" — revives dead spirits. Have you experienced this revival? Are you sharing news that brings life to others?
  • 5. "It is enough; Joseph my son is yet alive" (v.28). After 22 years of grief, Jacob receives his son back. God's timing is not our timing — but His faithfulness endures. What are you still waiting for? Don't give up.
