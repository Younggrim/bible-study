Kings of Israel & Judah

Josiah

His Reign

Josiah became king at eight years old, inheriting a nation steeped in idolatry after the disastrous reigns of his grandfather Manasseh and father Amon. Yet at sixteen, he "began to seek the God of his father David," and at twenty, he began purging Judah of its high places, Asherah poles, and carved images. His passion for reform was not merely political — it flowed from genuine devotion to the God his ancestors had abandoned.

The turning point came when Josiah was twenty-six. During Temple repairs, the high priest Hilkiah discovered the Book of the Law — likely Deuteronomy — buried and forgotten in the Temple itself. When Josiah heard its words read aloud, he tore his robes in grief, recognizing how far the nation had drifted from God's commands. His response was not defensiveness but brokenness: "Great is the wrath of the LORD that is poured out on us, because our fathers have not kept the word of the LORD."

Josiah led the most thorough national repentance in Judah's history. He read the entire Book of the Law publicly, renewed the covenant, destroyed every idol and high place in the land, and celebrated a Passover unprecedented since the days of the judges. Scripture's verdict is extraordinary: "Before him there was no king like him, who turned to the LORD with all his heart and with all his soul and with all his might." His life proves that youth is no barrier to radical faithfulness.

Key Scriptures

  • 2 Kings 22:2 — "He did what was right in the eyes of the LORD and walked in all the way of David his father."
  • 2 Kings 22:11 — When the king heard the words of the Book of the Law, he tore his clothes.
  • 2 Kings 23:25 — "Before him there was no king like him, who turned to the LORD with all his heart."
  • 2 Chronicles 34:3 — "While he was still young, he began to seek the God of his father David."
  • Deuteronomy 6:5 — "You shall love the LORD your God with all your heart and with all your soul and with all your might."

Legacy and Lessons

Josiah teaches us that the discovery (or rediscovery) of God's Word should produce immediate, radical response. He did not form a committee to study the implications or schedule a gradual implementation plan. He tore his clothes and acted. When Scripture confronts us with truth we have been ignoring, the appropriate response is not intellectual acknowledgment but repentant action. The distance between hearing and doing is the distance between religion and revival.

Josiah also demonstrates that godly heritage is not required for godly living. His grandfather Manasseh was arguably Judah's most wicked king, and his father Amon was no better. Yet Josiah chose a different path — seeking God while still a teenager, before anyone expected reform from him. Our family of origin does not determine our spiritual destiny. Every generation has the opportunity to break the cycle of unfaithfulness and chart a new course toward God.

Reflection Questions

  • When was the last time God's Word genuinely grieved you over something in your life? How did you respond?
  • Are there generational patterns of unfaithfulness you need to break? What would that look like practically?
  • What does Josiah's wholehearted devotion challenge in your current level of commitment to God?
