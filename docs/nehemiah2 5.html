Nehemiah — Chapter 2

1In the month of Nisan, in the twentieth year of King Artaxerxes, when wine was before him, I took up the wine and gave it to the king. Now I had not been sad in his presence.

2And the king said to me, “Why is your face sad, seeing you are not sick? This is nothing but sadness of the heart.” Then I was very much afraid.

3I said to the king, “Let the king live forever! Why should not my face be sad, when the city, the place of my fathers’ graves, lies in ruins, and its gates have been destroyed by fire?”

4Then the king said to me, “What are you requesting?” So I prayed to the God of heaven.

5And I said to the king, “If it pleases the king, and if your servant has found favor in your sight, that you send me to Judah, to the city of my fathers’ graves, that I may rebuild it.”

6And the king said to me (the queen sitting beside him), “How long will you be gone, and when will you return?” So it pleased the king to send me when I had given him a time.

7And I said to the king, “If it pleases the king, let letters be given me to the governors of the province Beyond the River, that they may let me pass through until I come to Judah,

8and a letter to Asaph, the keeper of the king’s forest, that he may give me timber to make beams for the gates of the fortress of the temple, and for the wall of the city, and for the house that I shall occupy.” And the king granted me what I asked, for the good hand of my God was upon me.

9Then I came to the governors of the province Beyond the River and gave them the king’s letters. Now the king had sent with me officers of the army and horsemen.

10But when Sanballat the Horonite and Tobiah the Ammonite servant heard this, it displeased them greatly that someone had come to seek the welfare of the people of Israel.

11So I went to Jerusalem and was there three days.

12Then I arose in the night, I and a few men with me. And I told no one what my God had put into my heart to do for Jerusalem. There was no animal with me but the one on which I rode.

13I went out by night by the Valley Gate to the Dragon Spring and to the Dung Gate, and I inspected the walls of Jerusalem that were broken down and its gates that had been destroyed by fire.

14Then I went on to the Fountain Gate and to the King’s Pool, but there was no room for the animal that was under me to pass.

15Then I went up in the night by the valley and inspected the wall, and I turned back and entered by the Valley Gate, and so returned.

16And the officials did not know where I had gone or what I was doing, and I had not yet told the Jews, the priests, the nobles, the officials, and the rest who were to do the work.

17Then I said to them, “You see the trouble we are in, how Jerusalem lies in ruins with its gates burned. Come, let us build the wall of Jerusalem, that we may no longer suffer derision.”

18And I told them of the hand of my God that had been upon me for good, and also of the words that the king had spoken to me. And they said, “Let us rise up and build.” So they strengthened their hands for the good work.

19But when Sanballat the Horonite and Tobiah the Ammonite servant and Geshem the Arab heard of it, they jeered at us and despised us and said, “What is this thing that you are doing? Are you rebelling against the king?”

20Then I replied to them, “The God of heaven will make us prosper, and we his servants will arise and build, but you have no portion or right or claim in Jerusalem.”

Summary

Nehemiah boldly asks King Artaxerxes for permission and resources to rebuild Jerusalem's walls, then travels to Judah and secretly surveys the broken walls by night. He rallies the people with confidence that God will prosper their work despite enemy mockery.

Key Verses

v.4
"Then the king said to me, 'What are you requesting?' So I prayed to the God of heaven." (ESV) — The most famous "arrow prayer" in Scripture. In the split second between the king's question and his own answer, Nehemiah lifted his heart to God. This was possible because he had been praying for four months — the arrow prayer was the culmination of sustained intercession, not a substitute for it.
v.8
"And the king granted me what I asked, for the good hand of my God was upon me." (ESV) — The same phrase Ezra used (Ezra 7:6). Both men attributed royal favor to divine causation. The king granted because God's hand moved. Human decisions are instruments of divine providence.
v.12
"And I told no one what my God had put into my heart to do for Jerusalem." (ESV) — Strategic silence. Nehemiah didn't announce his mission immediately — he first gathered intelligence. Wisdom dictates that visionary leaders assess reality privately before casting vision publicly. Premature announcement invites premature opposition.
v.17
"Come, let us build the wall of Jerusalem, that we may no longer suffer derision." (ESV) — Nehemiah's rallying cry was brief, direct, and motivating. He identified the problem ("we are in distress"), the solution ("let us build"), and the outcome ("no more a reproach"). Great leadership communication is clear, inclusive ("us"), and vision-casting.
v.18
"Let us rise up and build." So they strengthened their hands for the good work." (ESV) — The people's response was immediate and committed. Nehemiah's combination of personal testimony (God's hand), royal authorization (king's words), and clear vision (rebuild the wall) produced unified, enthusiastic action.
v.20
"The God of heaven will make us prosper, and we his servants will arise and build, but you have no portion or right or claim in Jerusalem." (ESV) — Nehemiah's response to mockery was confident, theological, and definitive. He claimed God's prosperity, asserted their servant identity, and excluded the opponents from any claim to Jerusalem. No negotiation, no compromise, no fear.

Cross-References

  • v.4 — 1 Thessalonians 5:17 — "Pray without ceasing." (ESV) Nehemiah's instantaneous prayer mid-conversation demonstrates what "unceasing prayer" looks like in practice — a heart so habitually oriented toward God that prayer occurs naturally in every moment.
  • v.8 — Ezra 7:6 — "The king granted him all that he asked, for the hand of the LORD his God was on him." (ESV) The identical phrase and dynamic — God's hand securing royal favor for His servant's mission.
  • v.10 — Psalm 2:1-2 — "Why do the nations rage and the peoples plot in vain?... against the LORD and against his Anointed." (ESV) Sanballat and Tobiah's grief at someone seeking Israel's welfare echoes the pattern of opposition to God's purposes by surrounding powers.
  • v.17 — Psalm 44:13-14 — "You make us a reproach to our neighbors." (ESV) The "reproach" of broken walls echoed the psalmic laments over Israel's humiliation. Nehemiah resolved to end the reproach through concrete action.
  • v.20 — Zechariah 2:8-9 — "He who touches you touches the apple of his eye." (ESV) Nehemiah's confidence that God would prosper them and exclude their enemies rested on the covenant promise that Jerusalem was God's special possession.

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/nehemiah-2/ Guzik highlights the "arrow prayer" of v.4 as evidence that Nehemiah's spiritual life was so developed that instant prayer was natural — the overflow of months of sustained intercession. He also notes the wisdom of the nighttime inspection: vision without information is wishful thinking.
  • Charles Spurgeon: "'So I prayed to the God of heaven.' In the twinkling of an eye, between the king's question and Nehemiah's answer, a prayer ascended to the throne of God. Here is a man whose prayer life was so constant that he could shoot an arrow of petition to heaven in a single heartbeat. This is not the prayer of a beginner — this is the instant reflex of one who has lived on his knees for months. And mark: the prayer did not replace planning. Nehemiah had his request ready, his logistics prepared, his timeline calculated. Prayer without preparation is laziness; preparation without prayer is presumption. Nehemiah combined both with masterful balance. Then see his nighttime ride — alone, silent, observing. The wise builder counts the cost before announcing the project. Nehemiah inspected before he inspired."

Translations

  • v.1 — KJV: "I had not been beforetime sad in his presence" / ESV: "I had not been sad in his presence" — Nehemiah had maintained composure for four months of prayer (Chislev to Nisan). His sadness finally broke through — whether by divine timing or human limitation, the king noticed what Nehemiah had been concealing.
  • v.3 — KJV: "the place of my fathers' sepulchres" / ESV: "the place of my fathers' graves" — Nehemiah framed his request in terms a Persian king would understand — ancestral burial sites were sacred in every culture. He didn't initially mention religious motivation but appealed to universal filial piety.
  • v.8 — KJV: "the palace which appertained to the house" / ESV: "the fortress of the temple" — Hebrew "ha-birah asher la-bayith" (the fortress/citadel that belongs to the house [of God]). This refers to the fortified structure adjacent to the Temple — later known as the Antonia Fortress in Herod's time.
  • v.10 — KJV: "Sanballat the Horonite" / ESV: "Sanballat the Horonite" — Sanballat is confirmed in the Elephantine papyri as governor of Samaria. "Horonite" likely indicates origin from Beth-horon or Horonaim (Moab). Tobiah "the Ammonite servant" was likely a Persian-appointed official of Ammonite origin.
  • v.13 — KJV: "the dragon well" / ESV: "the Dragon Spring" — Hebrew "ein ha-tannin" (spring/fountain of the dragon/ jackal). Location uncertain — possibly the same as En-rogel or a spring in the Kidron Valley. "Tannin" can mean dragon, sea serpent, or jackal — the name may reflect local legend or the spring's serpentine course.

Reflection

  • 1. Arrow prayers depend on sustained prayer lives (v.4). Nehemiah could pray instantly because he had been praying for months. If you want to respond to crises with faith, build a prayer life in the ordinary times. Daily communion with God enables instant communion in emergencies.
  • 2. Plan while you pray (vv.5-8). Nehemiah's request was detailed: travel letters, timber, specific building materials. He hadn't just been praying vaguely — he'd been planning concretely. Prayer does not excuse you from preparation. Do your research, form your plan, then ask God to open the door your preparation has identified.
  • 3. Assess before you announce (vv.12-16). The nighttime inspection shows wisdom: gather facts privately before casting vision publicly. If Nehemiah had announced his plan without knowing the wall's condition, he might have made promises he couldn't keep. Leaders who inspire without investigating build on sand.
  • 4. Cast clear, inclusive, hope-filled vision (v.17). "Come, let us build" — three elements: clarity (what we're doing), inclusion (we're doing it together), and motivation (no more reproach). When rallying others to God's work, be brief, be clear, be inclusive, and paint the outcome.
  • 5. Expect opposition and refuse to negotiate with it (vv.19-20). The moment vision was cast, mockers appeared. Nehemiah didn't argue, defend, or negotiate — he declared God's sovereignty and the opponents' exclusion. Not every critic deserves a response. Some opposition requires only confident declaration of whose work this is.
