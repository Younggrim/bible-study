Parables of Jesus

The Unforgiving Servant

The Parable

Peter came to Jesus and asked: "Lord, how often will my brother sin against me, and I forgive him? As many as seven times?" Peter thought he was being generous — the rabbis taught three times was sufficient. Jesus answered: "I do not say to you seven times, but seventy-seven times." Then He told this parable to illustrate what unlimited forgiveness looks like — and what happens when we refuse it.

A king settled accounts with his servants. One owed him ten thousand talents — an absurdly impossible debt, equivalent to roughly 200,000 years of wages. The servant couldn't pay, so the king ordered him sold along with his family. The servant fell on his knees and begged: "Have patience with me, and I will pay you everything." Out of pity, the master released him and forgave the entire debt — every last coin, wiped clean.

But that same servant went out and found a fellow servant who owed him a hundred denarii — about three months' wages. He seized him by the throat and demanded: "Pay what you owe!" His fellow servant begged with the exact same words. But he refused, and threw him into prison. When the king heard, he was furious: "You wicked servant! I forgave you all that debt because you pleaded with me. Should you not have had mercy on your fellow servant, as I had mercy on you?" He delivered him to the jailers until he should pay all his debt.

Key Scriptures

  • Matthew 18:32-35 — The master's rebuke and Jesus' warning: "So also my heavenly Father will do to every one of you, if you do not forgive your brother from your heart."
  • Ephesians 4:32 — "Be kind to one another, tenderhearted, forgiving one another, as God in Christ forgave you."
  • Colossians 3:13 — "Bearing with one another… forgiving each other; as the Lord has forgiven you, so you also must forgive."
  • Mark 11:25 — "Whenever you stand praying, forgive, if you have anything against anyone, so that your Father also may forgive you."
  • Matthew 6:14-15 — "If you forgive others their trespasses, your heavenly Father will also forgive you."

What Jesus Is Teaching

Jesus is teaching that forgiven people must become forgiving people — and that a refusal to forgive reveals we haven't truly grasped the magnitude of what we've been forgiven. The math is intentionally extreme: ten thousand talents versus a hundred denarii is a ratio of roughly 600,000 to 1. Our sin against God is an infinite debt — cosmic treason against the Creator of the universe. The offenses others commit against us, however painful, are comparatively tiny. This doesn't minimize our hurt; it magnifies God's grace and puts our grievances in proper perspective.

The parable also warns that unforgiveness has consequences. The unforgiving servant didn't lose his salvation in the story — but he lost his freedom and his peace. He was "delivered to the jailers." Bitterness is its own prison. When we refuse to forgive, we chain ourselves to the person who hurt us. Jesus isn't teaching that forgiveness is easy or that it means pretending nothing happened. He's teaching that because we've been forgiven an unpayable debt, we have both the obligation and the supernatural power (through the Spirit) to release others from what they owe us. Forgiveness isn't optional for the forgiven.

Reflection Questions

  • Is there someone you're currently refusing to forgive? How does comparing their offense to God's forgiveness of your "ten thousand talents" change your perspective?
  • Have you fully grasped the enormity of your own debt before God — and the completeness of His forgiveness through Christ?
  • In what ways might unforgiveness be acting as a "prison" in your life — stealing your peace, poisoning relationships, or hindering your prayers?
  • What practical step can you take this week toward forgiving someone who has wronged you — even if the feelings haven't fully caught up yet?
