Psalms — Chapter 57

1Be merciful to me, O God, be merciful to me, for in you my soul takes refuge; in the shadow of your wings I will take refuge, till the storms of destruction pass by.

2I cry out to God Most High, to God who fulfills his purpose for me.

3He will send from heaven and save me; he will put to shame him who tramples on me. Selah God will send out his steadfast love and his faithfulness!

4My soul is in the midst of lions; I lie down amid fiery beasts— the children of man, whose teeth are spears and arrows, whose tongues are sharp swords.

5Be exalted, O God, above the heavens! Let your glory be over all the earth!

6They set a net for my steps; my soul was bowed down. They dug a pit in my way, but they have fallen into it themselves. Selah

7My heart is steadfast, O God, my heart is steadfast! I will sing and make melody!

8Awake, my glory! Awake, O harp and lyre! I will awake the dawn!

9I will give thanks to you, O Lord, among the peoples; I will sing praises to you among the nations.

10For your steadfast love is great to the heavens, your faithfulness to the clouds.

11Be exalted, O God, above the heavens! Let your glory be over all the earth!

Summary

A lament turning to praise — David hides in a cave from Saul, taking refuge under God's wings until destruction passes; his heart is steadfast: 'Awake, my glory! I will praise thee among the nations.'

Key Verses

v.1
"Be merciful unto me, O God, be merciful unto me: for my soul trusteth in thee: yea, in the shadow of thy wings will I make my refuge, until these calamities be overpast." (KJV) — The double "be merciful" expresses urgency. "Shadow of thy wings" — like a mother bird sheltering her young. "Until these calamities be overpast" — storms are temporal; God's refuge is permanent. David expects the trial to end.
v.5
"Be thou exalted, O God, above the heavens; let thy glory be above all the earth." (KJV) — The refrain (repeated in v.11). Even in a cave, David's vision is cosmic. His prayer extends beyond personal deliverance to God's universal glory. True worship looks beyond self.
v.7
"My heart is fixed, O God, my heart is fixed: I will sing and give praise." (KJV) — "Fixed" (nachon) = established, prepared, steadfast. The double declaration is emphatic. David's circumstances are chaotic, but his heart is settled. A fixed heart can sing in any situation.
v.10
"For thy mercy is great unto the heavens, and thy truth unto the clouds." (KJV) — God's mercy (hesed) and truth (emeth) are immeasurable — they reach from earth to heaven. No situation is beyond the reach of God's covenant love.

Cross-References

  • v.1 — 1 Samuel 22:1; 24:1-3 — The cave settings that prompt this psalm. v.1 — Ruth 2:12 — "Under whose wings thou art come to trust." Same protective wing imagery. v.1 — Matthew 23:37 — Jesus: "How often would I have gathered thy children together, even as a hen gathereth her chickens under her wings." God's maternal protection. v.4 — Psalm 120:4 — "Sharp arrows of the mighty, with coals of juniper." Tongue as weapon theme. v.6 — Psalm 7:15 — "He made a pit, and digged it, and is fallen into the ditch which he made." Enemies falling into their own traps. v.7 — Psalm 108:1-5 — Nearly identical to vv.7-11. Same psalm reused in a different context. v.10 — Psalm 36:5 — "Thy mercy, O LORD, is in the heavens; and thy faithfulness reacheth unto the clouds."

Translations

  • v.1 — KJV: "until these calamities be overpast" / ESV: "till the storms of destruction pass by" — Hebrew "havvot" = destruction, calamity, ruin. ESV's "storms" adds vivid meteorological imagery not explicitly in the Hebrew but captures the sense of passing turbulence.
  • v.7 — KJV: "My heart is fixed" / ESV: "My heart is steadfast" — Hebrew "nachon" = firm, established, prepared. Both work well. "Steadfast" (ESV) emphasizes endurance; "fixed" (KJV) emphasizes immovability.
  • v.8 — KJV: "Awake up, my glory" / ESV: "Awake, my glory!" — "Glory" here likely means David's soul or his tongue (the instrument of praise). He commands his whole being to rise and worship. "I myself will awake early" / "I will awake the dawn" — David's praise is so eager it precedes sunrise.

Reflection

  • 1. God's wings are a refuge until storms pass (v.1). The storms WILL pass. This is temporary. God's protection is the shelter during the storm, not necessarily the removal of the storm. Wait it out under His wings.
  • 2. A fixed heart can praise in any circumstance (v.7). David is in a cave, hunted, uncertain of his future — and his heart is "fixed." Stability of heart is not a product of stable circumstances but of settled trust.
  • 3. Enemies' traps become their own judgment (v.6). Those who dig pits for the righteous fall into them. God turns evil strategies against their authors. You don't need to avenge yourself — wickedness is self-defeating.
  • 4. Worship should be cosmic in scope (v.5). Even personal prayers should lift our eyes to God's universal glory. Our problems are real, but God is bigger than our problems. Let worship expand your vision beyond your cave.
  • 5. Wake up early to praise (v.8). "I will awake the dawn" — don't let the day get ahead of your worship. The first voice of your morning should be praise, not anxiety.
