1Then the LORD answered Job out of the whirlwind, and said,

2Who is this that darkeneth counsel by words without knowledge?

3Gird up now thy loins like a man; for I will demand of thee, and answer thou me.

4Where wast thou when I laid the foundations of the earth? declare, if thou hast understanding.

5Who hath laid the measures thereof, if thou knowest? or who hath stretched the line upon it?

7When the morning stars sang together, and all the sons of God shouted for joy?

8Or who shut up the sea with doors, when it brake forth, as if it had issued out of the womb?

9When I made the cloud the garment thereof, and thick darkness a swaddlingband for it,

10And brake up for it my decreed place, and set bars and doors,

11And said, Hitherto shalt thou come, but no further: and here shall thy proud waves be stayed?

12Hast thou commanded the morning since thy days; and caused the dayspring to know his place;

13That it might take hold of the ends of the earth, that the wicked might be shaken out of it?

14It is turned as clay to the seal; and they stand as a garment.

15And from the wicked their light is withholden, and the high arm shall be broken.

16Hast thou entered into the springs of the sea? or hast thou walked in the search of the depth?

17Have the gates of death been opened unto thee? or hast thou seen the doors of the shadow of death?

18Hast thou perceived the breadth of the earth? declare if thou knowest it all.

19Where is the way where light dwelleth? and as for darkness, where is the place thereof,

20That thou shouldest take it to the bound thereof, and that thou shouldest know the paths to the house thereof?

21Knowest thou it, because thou wast then born? or because the number of thy days is great?

22Hast thou entered into the treasures of the snow? or hast thou seen the treasures of the hail,

23Which I have reserved against the time of trouble, against the day of battle and war?

24By what way is the light parted, which scattereth the east wind upon the earth?

26To cause it to rain on the earth, where no man is; on the wilderness, wherein there is no man;

28Hath the rain a father? or who hath begotten the drops of dew?

29Out of whose womb came the ice? and the hoary frost of heaven, who hath gendered it?

30The waters are hid as with a stone, and the face of the deep is frozen.

31Canst thou bind the sweet influences of Pleiades, or loose the bands of Orion?

32Canst thou bring forth Mazzaroth in his season? or canst thou guide Arcturus with his sons?

33Knowest thou the ordinances of heaven? canst thou set the dominion thereof in the earth?

34Canst thou lift up thy voice to the clouds, that abundance of waters may cover thee?

35Canst thou send lightnings, that they may go, and say unto thee, Here we are?

36Who hath put wisdom in the inward parts? or who hath given understanding to the heart?

37Who can number the clouds in wisdom? or who can stay the bottles of heaven,

38When the dust groweth into hardness, and the clods cleave fast together?

39Wilt thou hunt the prey for the lion? or fill the appetite of the young lions,

40When they couch in their dens, and abide in the covert to lie in wait?

41Who provideth for the raven his food? when his young ones cry unto God, they wander for lack of meat.

Summary

God's first speech begins — the LORD answers Job out of the whirlwind, overwhelming him with questions about creation: the earth's foundations, the sea, dawn, snow, stars, and weather — 'Where were you?'

Authorship & Background

Author: Unknown (see Chapter 1 notes for full discussion).
Historical Context: Chapter 38 is the climax the entire book has been building toward: GOD SPEAKS. After 37 chapters of human dialogue — friends arguing, Job protesting, Elihu intervening — God Himself breaks into the conversation "out of the whirlwind" (v.1). This is one of the most powerful theophanies in Scripture. Yet God's speech is not what anyone expected. He does not explain Job's suffering. He does not vindicate Job against the friends. He does not reveal the heavenly council scene from chapters 1-2. Instead, He interrogates Job with a relentless series of questions about CREATION. "Where were you?" "Can you?" "Do you know?" God answers Job's demand for explanation with a demand for humility. The implicit argument: if you cannot understand how I made the world, how can you understand how I govern it?
The Opening Challenge (vv.1-3): "Who is this that darkeneth counsel by words without knowledge?" (v.2). God does not address Job gently — He confronts him. Job's words have been "without knowledge" — not sinful (God will vindicate Job in 42:7) but presumptuous. Job spoke rightly about God's character but wrongly in demanding that God explain Himself. "Gird up now thy loins like a man" (v.3) — prepare yourself, because God is about to ask questions Job cannot answer.
The Foundation of the Earth (vv.4-7): "Where wast thou when I laid the foundations of the earth?" (v.4). The first question establishes the fundamental disproportion: God was creating while Job did not yet exist. The morning stars sang at creation (v.7), the sons of God shouted for joy — but Job was not there. He cannot explain the beginning; how can he explain the middle?
The Sea (vv.8-11): God "shut up the sea with doors" (v.8), made clouds its garment (v.9), and decreed: "Hitherto shalt thou come, but no further" (v.11). God controls the most powerful, chaotic force in nature. If He can set boundaries for the ocean, can He not set boundaries for suffering?
Dawn (vv.12-15): Has Job ever commanded morning or caused dawn to know its place? Can he shake the wicked out of the earth? Dawn is God's daily miracle that Job takes for granted.
The Deep, Death, Light, and Darkness (vv.16-21): Has Job entered the springs of the sea, seen the gates of death, comprehended the earth's expanse, or found where light and darkness dwell? God's sarcasm in v.21 is sharp: "Knowest thou it, because thou wast then born? or because the number of thy days is great?" — You must know all this, since you're so old and wise!
Snow, Hail, Rain, and Ice (vv.22-30): Has Job entered God's storehouses of snow and hail? (v.22). Does he know how light is distributed or wind scattered? (v.24). Who sends rain on empty deserts? (v.26). Who fathers the dew, births the ice? (vv.28-29). Each question reveals God's sovereign control over phenomena Job cannot even explain, much less replicate.
Stars and Weather (vv.31-38): Can Job bind the Pleiades, loose Orion, bring forth Mazzaroth, or guide Arcturus? (vv.31-32). Does he know heaven's ordinances? (v.33). Can he command clouds and lightning? (vv.34-35). Who put wisdom in the inward parts? (v.36). The questions multiply, overwhelming Job's presumption.
Animals (vv.39-41): Can Job hunt for the lion or provide for the raven? God feeds creatures Job never sees. His providence operates continuously in places Job will never go.

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): enduringword.com Guzik emphasizes that God never answers Job's "why." Instead, He overwhelms Job with "who" and "how" — who made this? who governs that? The unstated argument: the God who designed and sustains all creation can be trusted to govern one man's life wisely. Guzik notes that God's questions are not cruel but corrective — they reframe Job's perspective from demanding answers to receiving God's presence.
  • Charles Spurgeon: "God answers Job — but not with answers. He answers with questions. And the questions are not interrogation but revelation. Every question reveals something Job did not know, could not do, and cannot control. And the cumulative effect is not humiliation but worship. For if God governs the stars and the sea, the dawn and the deep, the snow and the raven — then He surely governs the life of one suffering man. 'Where wast thou?' — I was not there. I do not understand. But He was there. He does understand. And that is enough."

Reflection

  • 1. God's answer to suffering is not explanation but presence (v.1). Job asked "why?" God answered "I AM." The theophany itself is the answer — not information about suffering but encounter with the Sufferer's God. Sometimes God's presence IS the answer, even without words.
  • 2. You were not there (v.4). This is humbling and freeing. You were not consulted at creation; you will not be consulted about providence. God does not need your input to be just. Your inability to understand is not a flaw — it is a feature of being finite before the Infinite.
  • 3. God governs chaos (v.11). The sea obeys. The deep has boundaries. Darkness has limits. Whatever feels chaotic and overwhelming in your life is still within God's decreed boundaries. "Hitherto shalt thou come, but no further."
  • 4. Creation reveals enough of God to trust Him (entire chapter). Every question God asks points to a truth Job can observe: God designed, sustains, and governs all things. If you can trust Him with the cosmos, you can trust Him with your crisis.
  • 5. Questions can be more powerful than answers. God teaches Job through interrogation, not lecture. Each question forces Job to encounter his own limits — and in encountering his limits, to encounter God's limitlessness. Sometimes the best pastoral response is not an answer but a better question.
