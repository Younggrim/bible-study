Summary

The genealogy of Levi is presented in detail, tracing the priestly line through Aaron and the Levitical musicians appointed by David, along with the cities assigned to the Levites throughout Israel. This chapter establishes the legitimacy of post-exilic worship leadership.

Key Verses

v.3
"And the children of Amram; Aaron, and Moses, and Miriam." (KJV/ESV) — The three great leaders of the Exodus — all from one family, all Levites. Aaron the priest, Moses the prophet/lawgiver, Miriam the prophetess. God concentrated leadership gifts in one household to accomplish the Exodus, then distributed priestly service throughout the tribe for all generations.
v.10
"and Johanan fathered Azariah (it was he who served as priest in the house that Solomon built in Jerusalem)." (ESV) — The only editorial note in the high priestly genealogy. This Azariah likely opposed King Uzziah when he tried to burn incense (2 Chronicles 26:17-20). His mention highlights that priests had authority even over kings in matters of worship.
v.15
"and Jehozadak went into exile when the LORD sent Judah and Jerusalem into exile by the hand of Nebuchadnezzar." (ESV) — The priestly line enters exile — but it does not end. Jehozadak's son Jeshua would return to rebuild the altar. The priesthood survived Babylon because God preserved it for restoration.
v.31
"These are the men whom David put in charge of the service of song in the house of the LORD after the ark rested there." (ESV) — David organized Temple music. The phrase "after the ark rested there" connects to 2 Samuel 6 — once the ark was in Jerusalem, worship could be formalized. Music in worship was not an afterthought but a central ministry requiring dedicated, qualified personnel.
v.49
"But Aaron and his sons made offerings on the altar of burnt offering and on the altar of incense for all the work of the Most Holy Place, and to make atonement for Israel, according to all that Moses the servant of God had commanded." (ESV) — The distinction between priests (Aaron's line) and Levites is clear: only priests could offer sacrifices and make atonement. Levites served in support roles — music, gatekeeping, maintenance. This distinction mattered enormously for the post-exilic Temple.

Cross-References

  • v.3 — Exodus 6:16-20 — The genealogy of Moses and Aaron from Levi. The Chronicler's version is condensed but confirms the same line: Levi → Kohath → Amram → Aaron/Moses.
  • v.10 — 2 Chronicles 26:17-20 — Azariah the priest who confronted King Uzziah for unlawful incense-burning. "It is not for you, Uzziah, to burn incense" (ESV). Priestly authority in worship was absolute.
  • v.13 — 2 Kings 22:8 — Hilkiah the high priest who found the Book of the Law during Josiah's Temple reforms. This discovery sparked the last great revival before exile.
  • v.15 — Ezra 3:2 — "Then stood up Jeshua the son of Jozadak [Jehozadak]." The priestly line resumed in the return from exile, validating the Second Temple's ministry.
  • v.31 — 1 Chronicles 15:16-24 — David's appointment of Levitical musicians when the ark was brought to Jerusalem. The Chronicler will detail this further in chapters 15-16.
  • v.33 — 1 Samuel 1:1 — Samuel (Shemuel) is here placed in the Kohathite line through Elkanah. 1 Samuel identifies Elkanah as an Ephraimite (by residence), but his Levitical ancestry is confirmed here.
  • v.54-81 — Joshua 21:1-42 — The parallel account of Levitical cities assigned by Joshua. The Chronicler reproduces this list to show that the original allotment was still valid for post-exilic Levites returning to their ancestral cities.

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/1-chronicles-6/ Guzik notes that the Chronicler gives Levi more space than any other tribe because his concern is worship. The post-exilic community needed to know that its priests were legitimate, its musicians were authorized, and its worship was in continuity with David's original pattern. This chapter is the Chronicler's charter for proper worship.
  • Charles Spurgeon: "The musicians of the Temple are given genealogies as careful and complete as the priests themselves. What does this tell us? That in God's economy, the ministry of praise is as sacred as the ministry of sacrifice. The singer stands alongside the priest, and his lineage matters to God. Worship is not a prelude to the important thing — worship IS the important thing. David knew this, and the Chronicler preserves it for every generation: those who lead God's people in praise serve a holy calling."

Translations

  • v.1 — KJV: "Gershon" / ESV: "Gershon" — Note: verse 16 has "Gershom" in both translations. The same person is called "Gershon" and "Gershom" interchangeably in Hebrew texts. "Gershom" is the more common form (meaning "exile/stranger there"), while "Gershon" is a variant.
  • v.10 — KJV: "executed the priest's office in the temple" / ESV: "served as priest in the house" — Hebrew "kihen" (to function as priest). KJV's "executed the priest's office" is formal and precise; ESV's "served as priest" is simpler. Both convey active priestly ministry in Solomon's Temple.
  • v.28 — KJV: "the firstborn Vashni, and Abiah" / ESV: "Joel his firstborn, the second Abijah" — A famous textual difficulty. The Hebrew text reads "the firstborn Vashni" but "Vashni" may be a corruption of "and the second" (Hebrew "v'hasheni"). ESV follows the correction from 1 Samuel 8:2, which names Joel and Abijah as Samuel's sons. KJV follows the Masoretic text literally.
  • v.33 — KJV: "Shemuel" / ESV: "Samuel" — "Shemuel" is the Hebrew form; "Samuel" is the anglicized form. Both refer to the prophet Samuel. His grandson Heman became the chief musician — from a family of prophets came a family of worship leaders.
  • v.54 — KJV: "their castles in their coasts" / ESV: "their settlements within their borders" — KJV's "castles" is archaic English for "encampments" or "settlements" (not fortified castles). "Coasts" is archaic for "borders" or "territories." ESV translates both terms into modern equivalents.
  • v.57 — KJV: "the city of refuge" / ESV: "the cities of refuge" — Hebron was designated a city of refuge (Numbers 35:6; Joshua 20:7). These were cities where someone who killed accidentally could flee and receive protection until trial. That the Levitical priestly cities included cities of refuge is theologically rich: mercy and priesthood coexisted.

Reflection

  • 1. Worship leaders need legitimate calling (vv.31-48). David didn't just pick talented singers — he appointed Levites with proper genealogical credentials. Ministry in worship is not about mere talent but about calling and consecration. Those who lead worship today should be people of proven character and genuine calling, not simply skilled performers.
  • 2. Music is ministry, not entertainment (vv.31-32). The Levitical musicians "ministered before the dwelling place" and "waited on their office according to their order." Worship music was an office — a formal, ordered, consecrated service. This elevates worship music beyond aesthetic preference to sacred ministry. How you approach worship singing — whether leading or participating — reflects your theology of God's presence.
  • 3. God preserves what He values through exile (v.15). The priestly line went into captivity but survived. God preserved the priesthood through 70 years of Babylon because proper worship required legitimate priests. God preserves the institutions and people necessary for His purposes, even through catastrophe. What God has called you to will survive the trials that threaten it.
  • 4. The distinction between roles matters (vv.48-49). Levites served the tabernacle; only priests could offer sacrifices. Different roles, different authority, same purpose. In the body of Christ, not everyone has the same function (Romans 12:4-8; 1 Corinthians 12). Honor your role without envying another's, and respect the boundaries God has established.
  • 5. God spreads His presence through His servants (vv.54-81). The Levites were distributed throughout every tribe — God's ministers embedded in every community. God's plan was never to concentrate all spiritual life in one place but to permeate the whole land with His presence through His servants. You are placed where you are to be God's representative in that place.
