1 Chronicles — Chapter 28

1David assembled at Jerusalem all the officials of Israel, the officials of the tribes, the officers of the divisions that served the king, the commanders of thousands, the commanders of hundreds, the stewards of all the property and livestock of the king and his sons, together with the palace officials, the mighty men and all the seasoned warriors.

2Then King David rose to his feet and said: “Hear me, my brothers and my people. I had it in my heart to build a house of rest for the ark of the covenant of the LORD and for the footstool of our God, and I made preparations for building.

3But God said to me, ‘You may not build a house for my name, for you are a man of war and have shed blood.’

4Yet the LORD God of Israel chose me from all my father’s house to be king over Israel forever. For he chose Judah as leader, and in the house of Judah my father’s house, and among my father’s sons he took pleasure in me to make me king over all Israel.

5And of all my sons (for the LORD has given me many sons) he has chosen Solomon my son to sit on the throne of the kingdom of the LORD over Israel.

6He said to me, ‘It is Solomon your son who shall build my house and my courts, for I have chosen him to be my son, and I will be his father.

7I will establish his kingdom forever if he continues strong in keeping my commandments and my rules, as he is today.’

8Now therefore in the sight of all Israel, the assembly of the LORD, and in the hearing of our God, observe and seek out all the commandments of the LORD your God, that you may possess this good land and leave it for an inheritance to your children after you forever.

9“And you, Solomon my son, know the God of your father and serve him with a whole heart and with a willing mind, for the LORD searches all hearts and understands every plan and thought. If you seek him, he will be found by you, but if you forsake him, he will cast you off forever.

10Be careful now, for the LORD has chosen you to build a house for the sanctuary; be strong and do it.”

11Then David gave Solomon his son the plan of the vestibule of the temple, and of its houses, its treasuries, its upper rooms, and its inner chambers, and of the room for the mercy seat;

12and the plan of all that he had in mind for the courts of the house of the LORD, all the surrounding chambers, the treasuries of the house of God, and the treasuries for dedicated gifts;

13for the divisions of the priests and of the Levites, and all the work of the service in the house of the LORD; for all the vessels for the service in the house of the LORD,

14the weight of gold for all golden vessels for each service, the weight of silver vessels for each service,

15the weight of the golden lampstands and their lamps, the weight of gold for each lampstand and its lamps, the weight of silver for a lampstand and its lamps, according to the use of each lampstand in the service,

16the weight of gold for each table for the showbread, the silver for the silver tables,

17and pure gold for the forks, the basins and the cups; for the golden bowls and the weight of each; for the silver bowls and the weight of each;

18for the altar of incense made of refined gold, and its weight; also his plan for the golden chariot of the cherubim that spread their wings and covered the ark of the covenant of the LORD.

19“All this he made clear to me in writing from the hand of the LORD, all the work to be done according to the plan.”

20Then David said to Solomon his son, “Be strong and courageous and do it. Do not be afraid and do not be dismayed, for the LORD God, even my God, is with you. He will not leave you or forsake you, until all the work for the service of the house of the LORD is finished.

21And behold the divisions of the priests and the Levites for all the service of the house of God; and with you in all the work will be every willing man who has skill for any kind of service; also the officers and all the people will be wholly at your command.”

Summary

David publicly charges Solomon before all Israel to build the Temple, giving him the divinely inspired plans for every detail of its construction. He emphasizes that Solomon must know God personally and serve Him wholeheartedly to see the work completed.

Key Verses

v.3
"But God said unto me, Thou shalt not build an house for my name, because thou hast been a man of war, and hast shed blood." (KJV) — David publicly acknowledges the limitation God placed on him. A lesser man might have resented this prohibition or hidden it. David embraces it transparently, teaching Israel that submission to God's "no" is as important as pursuing God's "yes." The Temple of peace required a man of peace to build it — a typological pointer to Christ, the Prince of Peace, who builds the true Temple (His Church).
v.9
"And thou, Solomon my son, know thou the God of thy father, and serve him with a perfect heart and with a willing mind: for the LORD searcheth all hearts, and understandeth all the imaginations of the thoughts: if thou seek him, he will be found of thee; but if thou forsake him, he will cast thee off for ever." (KJV) — The greatest verse in this chapter. "Know" (yada) means intimate, experiential knowledge — not mere intellectual acknowledgment. "Perfect heart" (lev shalem) means a whole, undivided heart. "The LORD searcheth all hearts" — God's omniscience makes hypocrisy impossible. The conditional promise and warning are stark: seek and find, or forsake and be cast off.
v.12
"And the pattern of all that he had by the spirit, of the courts of the house of the LORD." (KJV) — The Temple plans came "by the spirit" — whether this means the Holy Spirit revealed them or that David received them in his own spirit through divine inspiration, the result is the same: the Temple design was God's work, not man's. This parallels Exodus 25:9 where Moses received the tabernacle pattern on Sinai. Both worship structures originated in heaven.
v.19
"All this, said David, the LORD made me understand in writing by his hand upon me, even all the works of this pattern." (KJV) — David claims divine inspiration for the architectural plans — "the LORD made me understand in writing by his hand upon me." This is a claim of inscripturation — God communicated the pattern in written form, just as He wrote the commandments on stone tablets. The Temple was as much a product of divine revelation as the Torah itself.
v.20
"And David said to Solomon his son, Be strong and of good courage, and do it: fear not, nor be dismayed: for the LORD God, even my God, will be with thee; he will not fail thee, nor forsake thee, until thou hast finished all the work for the service of the house of the LORD." (KJV) — David echoes God's charge to Joshua (Joshua 1:5-9). The language is nearly identical — "be strong," "fear not," "I will not fail thee nor forsake thee." Solomon's commission to build the Temple parallels Joshua's commission to take the land. Both require courage rooted in divine presence, not human adequacy.

Cross-References

  • v.3 — 2 Samuel 7:5-13 — Nathan's prophecy: God told David "Thou shalt not build me an house" but promised that his son would build it and God would establish his throne forever. Chapter 28 is the public fulfillment and transmission of this private revelation.
  • v.9 — Jeremiah 29:13 — "And ye shall seek me, and find me, when ye shall search for me with all your heart." The conditional promise David gives Solomon echoes through the prophets — God is findable, but only by those who seek with undivided hearts.
  • v.12 — Exodus 25:9 — "According to all that I shew thee, after the pattern of the tabernacle, and the pattern of all the instruments thereof, even so shall ye make it." Moses received the tabernacle pattern on Sinai; David received the Temple pattern by the Spirit. Both worship structures were divinely designed, not humanly conceived.
  • v.18 — Exodus 25:18-22 — The original instructions for the cherubim over the mercy seat. David's "pattern of the chariot of the cherubims" (v.18) extends the original design — the Temple's cherubim are larger and more elaborate, but the concept comes from the same divine source.
  • v.19 — Hebrews 8:5 — "Who serve unto the example and shadow of heavenly things, as Moses was admonished of God when he was about to make the tabernacle... Make all things according to the pattern shewed to thee in the mount." The New Testament confirms that earthly worship structures are copies of heavenly realities — what David received "by the spirit" was a glimpse of that heavenly pattern.
  • v.20 — Joshua 1:5-9 — "Be strong and of a good courage... I will not fail thee, nor forsake thee." David's charge to Solomon deliberately echoes God's charge to Joshua. The mission is different (building vs. conquering), but the source of courage is identical: God's unfailing presence.
  • v.20 — Hebrews 13:5 — "I will never leave thee, nor forsake thee." The promise given to Joshua, repeated to Solomon, is extended to all believers in Christ. God's commitment to His servants endures across all dispensations.
  • v.9 — Psalm 139:1-4 — "O LORD, thou hast searched me, and known me... thou understandest my thought afar off." David's statement that "the LORD searcheth all hearts" reflects his own experience expressed in Psalm 139. He teaches Solomon what he himself has lived.

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/1-chronicles-28/ Guzik emphasizes the remarkable parallel between David receiving the Temple plans "by the spirit" and Moses receiving the tabernacle pattern on Sinai — both worship structures were divinely designed rather than humanly imagined. He highlights verse 9 as one of the most important single verses in Chronicles, combining the themes of heart-knowledge of God, willing service, divine omniscience, and the conditional nature of sustained blessing.
  • Charles Spurgeon: "David could not build the Temple, but he could prepare everything for the man who would. What a lesson for those whose hands are tied by providence! You cannot do the great work yourself? Then do all you can to equip the one who will. David gave Solomon the pattern, the materials, the personnel, and the encouragement. He could not lay the first stone, but he laid everything else in readiness. Be not idle because your specific dream is denied you. God has a Solomon coming after you, and your preparation is his provision. And mark those words: 'The LORD made me understand in writing by his hand upon me.' The Temple came from heaven before it rose from earth. Every true work of God begins in divine revelation, not human imagination."

Translations

  • v.2 — KJV: "an house of rest for the ark of the covenant of the LORD, and for the footstool of our God" / ESV: "a house of rest for the ark of the covenant of the LORD and for the footstool of our God" — Both render identically. The Hebrew "beth-menuchah" (house of rest) is theologically rich — the ark had been mobile since Sinai; the Temple would give it permanent rest. "Footstool" (hadom) presents the Temple as God's throne room — heaven is His seat, the ark is where His feet rest on earth (cf. Isaiah 66:1).
  • v.9 — KJV: "a perfect heart and with a willing mind" / ESV: "a whole heart and with a willing mind" — Hebrew "lev shalem ve-nephesh chaphetsah." KJV's "perfect" translates "shalem" (complete, whole, undivided); ESV's "whole" is more precise since "shalem" does not imply sinless perfection but undivided devotion. "Willing mind" — Hebrew "nephesh chaphetsah" (a soul that delights/desires) — service must flow from desire, not mere duty.
  • v.11 — KJV: "the pattern of the porch" / ESV: "the plan of the vestibule" — Hebrew "tavnith ha-ulam." "Tavnith" means pattern, model, or architectural plan. KJV's "pattern" and ESV's "plan" are essentially synonymous. "Ulam" refers to the entrance hall or vestibule of the Temple — KJV's "porch" reflects older English architectural vocabulary.
  • v.12 — KJV: "the pattern of all that he had by the spirit" / ESV: "the plan of all that he had in mind" — This is the most theologically significant translation difference in the chapter. Hebrew "tavnith kol asher hayah ba-ruach immo." KJV takes "ba-ruach" as "by the Spirit" (divine inspiration); ESV takes it as "in [his] mind/spirit" (David's own mental conception). The Hebrew "ruach" can mean Spirit (of God), spirit (of man), or mind. Context and verse 19 ("the LORD made me understand") support the KJV's reading — divine revelation rather than mere human planning.
  • v.18 — KJV: "the pattern of the chariot of the cherubims" / ESV: "his plan for the golden chariot of the cherubim" — Hebrew "tavnith ha-merkavah ha-keruvim." The cherubim over the ark are called a "chariot" (merkavah) — connecting to Ezekiel's vision of God's throne-chariot and suggesting the ark was understood as God's vehicle/throne. This rich imagery presents the mercy seat as a divine conveyance, not merely furniture.
  • v.19 — KJV: "the LORD made me understand in writing by his hand upon me" / ESV: "All this he made clear to me in writing from the hand of the LORD" — Hebrew "haskil ba-ketav mi-yad YHWH alay." The phrase "from the hand of the LORD upon me" echoes prophetic language (Ezekiel 1:3; 3:22). Both translations convey divine communication through writing, but KJV's word order emphasizes God's hand resting on David as the medium of revelation.

Reflection

  • 1. God's "no" to you may be His "yes" to the next generation (v.3). David desired to build the Temple but was told "thou shalt not." Rather than sulking or forcing his way, David poured everything into preparing what another would build. When God closes a door in your life, consider that He may be positioning you to prepare the way for someone else's calling. Not every vision you receive is yours to execute — some are yours to prepare and pass on.
  • 2. Know God, not just know about Him (v.9). David's charge is "know the God of thy father" — personal, experiential, intimate knowledge. Head knowledge of doctrine, theology, and Bible facts is insufficient. God searches hearts, not merely orthodoxy. Do you know God Himself — His character, His voice, His presence — or merely truths about Him? Religion stays at information; relationship pursues the Person behind the information.
  • 3. God searches the heart behind the action (v.9). "The LORD searcheth all hearts, and understandeth all the imaginations of the thoughts." External obedience with internal reluctance does not satisfy God. He examines motivation, not merely behavior. This should produce both humility (we cannot deceive God) and freedom (we need not perform for Him — He already knows us completely). Serve with a willing mind because the unwilling servant's work is known to be hollow.
  • 4. Divine blueprints exist for God's work (vv.12, 19). The Temple was not David's design but God's revelation. This principle applies to every genuine work of God — there is a divine pattern, and our task is to receive it, not invent it. In your ministry, family, or calling, seek God's design rather than imposing your own. Ask: "Lord, what is Your pattern for this?" rather than assuming your ideas are automatically His.
  • 5. Courage for great tasks comes from God's presence, not personal ability (v.20). "Be strong and of good courage, and do it: fear not, nor be dismayed." The grounds for courage are not Solomon's wisdom or resources but "the LORD God, even my God, will be with thee." When God assigns a task, He does not leave you to accomplish it alone. His presence is both the command and the resource. Whatever God has called you to — be strong and do it. He who called you is faithful, and He will not fail you.
