Ten Commandments

You Shall Not Steal

The Commandment

The eighth commandment, "You shall not steal" (Exodus 20:15), establishes the principle of private property and the dignity of honest labor. It affirms that what belongs to another person — their possessions, their wages, their time, their ideas — is not ours to take. This simple prohibition undergirds all economic justice: without respect for ownership, society collapses into a war of all against all, where the strong simply take from the weak.

In Israel's law, theft encompassed far more than pickpocketing or burglary. It included fraudulent business practices, withholding wages from workers, moving boundary stones to steal land, and even kidnapping (stealing persons). The prophets thundered against those who "join house to house" and "add field to field" through unjust means (Isaiah 5:8). God's concern extended beyond individual acts of theft to systemic injustice — economic structures that robbed the poor of their livelihood.

Paul transforms this commandment from mere prohibition into positive calling: "Let the thief no longer steal, but rather let him labor, doing honest work with his own hands, so that he may have something to share with anyone in need" (Ephesians 4:28). The New Testament vision moves beyond "don't take" to "work to give." The opposite of stealing is not merely keeping your hands to yourself but becoming generous — using your labor to bless others.

Key Scriptures

  • Exodus 20:15 — "You shall not steal."
  • Ephesians 4:28 — "Let the thief no longer steal, but rather let him labor, doing honest work with his own hands, so that he may have something to share with anyone in need."
  • Romans 13:7-8 — "Pay to all what is owed to them... Owe no one anything, except to love each other."
  • Proverbs 11:1 — "A false balance is an abomination to the LORD, but a just weight is his delight."
  • Luke 19:8 — "And Zacchaeus stood and said to the Lord, 'Behold, Lord, the half of my goods I give to the poor. And if I have defrauded anyone of anything, I restore it fourfold.'"

Heart of the Law

The heart of the eighth commandment is contentment and trust in God's provision. People steal — whether through outright theft, dishonest business, or exploitation — because they believe God's provision is insufficient or that they deserve what belongs to others. This commandment calls us to radical trust: God will provide what we need through honest labor, and we need not grasp at what He has given to our neighbor.

For modern believers, theft takes many subtle forms: padding expense reports, taking credit for others' work, wasting an employer's time, pirating content, or exploiting others' generosity without reciprocating. The eighth commandment invites self-examination: Am I giving honest value in my work? Am I respecting what belongs to others? Am I content with God's provision? The transformed life moves from taking to giving, from grasping to generosity, from scarcity-thinking to gospel abundance.

Reflection Questions

  • Are there subtle ways you might be "stealing" — taking credit, wasting time, or failing to give honest effort in your work?
  • How does contentment with God's provision protect you from the temptation to take what belongs to others?
  • What would it look like to move beyond "not stealing" toward active generosity in your daily life?
  • Is there anything you need to restore or make right — relationships, resources, or credit owed to others?
