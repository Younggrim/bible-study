1 Chronicles — Chapter 29

1And David the king said to all the assembly, “Solomon my son, whom alone God has chosen, is young and inexperienced, and the work is great, for the palace will not be for man but for the LORD God.

2So I have provided for the house of my God, so far as I was able, the gold for the things of gold, the silver for the things of silver, and the bronze for the things of bronze, the iron for the things of iron, and wood for the things of wood, besides great quantities of onyx and stones for setting, antimony, colored stones, all sorts of precious stones and marble.

3Moreover, in addition to all that I have provided for the holy house, I have a treasure of my own of gold and silver, and because of my devotion to the house of my God I give it to the house of my God:

43,000 talents of gold, of the gold of Ophir, and 7,000 talents of refined silver, for overlaying the walls of the house,

5and for all the work to be done by craftsmen, gold for the things of gold and silver for the things of silver. Who then will offer willingly, consecrating himself today to the LORD?”

6Then the leaders of fathers’ houses made their freewill offerings, as did also the leaders of the tribes, the commanders of thousands and of hundreds, and the officers over the king’s work.

7They gave for the service of the house of God 5,000 talents and 10,000 darics of gold, 10,000 talents of silver, 18,000 talents of bronze and 100,000 talents of iron.

8And whoever had precious stones gave them to the treasury of the house of the LORD, in the care of Jehiel the Gershonite.

9Then the people rejoiced because they had given willingly, for with a whole heart they had offered freely to the LORD. David the king also rejoiced greatly.

10Therefore David blessed the LORD in the presence of all the assembly. And David said: “Blessed are you, O LORD, the God of Israel our father, forever and ever.

11Yours, O LORD, is the greatness and the power and the glory and the victory and the majesty, for all that is in the heavens and in the earth is yours. Yours is the kingdom, O LORD, and you are exalted as head above all.

12Both riches and honor come from you, and you rule over all. In your hand are power and might, and in your hand it is to make great and to give strength to all.

13And now we thank you, our God, and praise your glorious name.

14“But who am I, and what is my people, that we should be able thus to offer willingly? For all things come from you, and of your own have we given you.

15For we are strangers before you and sojourners, as all our fathers were. Our days on the earth are like a shadow, and there is no abiding.

16O LORD our God, all this abundance that we have provided for building you a house for your holy name comes from your hand and is all your own.

17I know, my God, that you test the heart and have pleasure in uprightness. In the uprightness of my heart I have freely offered all these things, and now I have seen your people, who are present here, offering freely and joyously to you.

18O LORD, the God of Abraham, Isaac, and Israel, our fathers, keep forever such purposes and thoughts in the hearts of your people, and direct their hearts toward you.

19Grant to Solomon my son a whole heart that he may keep your commandments, your testimonies, and your statutes, performing all, and that he may build the palace for which I have made provision.”

20Then David said to all the assembly, “Bless the LORD your God.” And all the assembly blessed the LORD, the God of their fathers, and bowed their heads and paid homage to the LORD and to the king.

21And they offered sacrifices to the LORD, and on the next day offered burnt offerings to the LORD, 1,000 bulls, 1,000 rams, and 1,000 lambs, with their drink offerings, and sacrifices in abundance for all Israel.

22And they ate and drank before the LORD on that day with great gladness. And they made Solomon the son of David king the second time, and they anointed him as prince for the LORD, and Zadok as priest.

23Then Solomon sat on the throne of the LORD as king in place of David his father. And he prospered, and all Israel obeyed him.

24All the leaders and the mighty men, and also all the sons of King David, pledged their allegiance to King Solomon.

25And the LORD made Solomon very great in the sight of all Israel and bestowed on him such royal majesty as had not been on any king before him in Israel.

26Thus David the son of Jesse reigned over all Israel.

27The time that he reigned over Israel was forty years. He reigned seven years in Hebron and thirty-three years in Jerusalem.

28Then he died at a good age, full of days, riches, and honor. And Solomon his son reigned in his place.

29Now the acts of King David, from first to last, are written in the Chronicles of Samuel the seer, and in the Chronicles of Nathan the prophet, and in the Chronicles of Gad the seer,

30with accounts of all his rule and his might and of the circumstances that came upon him and upon Israel and upon all the kingdoms of the countries.

Summary

David leads the assembly in generous giving for the Temple construction, offering his personal wealth and inspiring the leaders to give willingly. He closes with a magnificent prayer of praise, Solomon is anointed king a second time, and David dies after a forty-year reign.

Key Verses

v.5
"And who then is willing to consecrate his service this day unto the LORD?" (KJV) — David's challenge. The Hebrew for "consecrate his service" is literally "fill his hand" — a phrase used for priestly ordination (Exodus 28:41). David invites the people to fill their hands with offerings as priests fill their hands at consecration. Giving becomes a priestly act — worship through sacrifice. The question is not "who can afford it?" but "who is willing?"
v.11
"Thine, O LORD, is the greatness, and the power, and the glory, and the victory, and the majesty: for all that is in the heaven and in the earth is thine; thine is the kingdom, O LORD, and thou art exalted as head above all." (KJV) — The supreme doxology of 1 Chronicles. Five attributes ascribed to God: greatness, power, glory, victory, majesty. "All that is in the heaven and in the earth is thine" — absolute divine ownership eliminates human possessiveness. "Thine is the kingdom" — echoed in the Lord's Prayer (Matthew 6:13). David ends where every believer should end: with everything belonging to God.
v.14
"But who am I, and what is my people, that we should be able to offer so willingly after this sort? for all things come of thee, and of thine own have we given thee." (KJV) — The definitive theology of stewardship. David recognizes that even the ability to give is God's gift. "Of thine own have we given thee" — we never give God anything that did not first come from Him. This eliminates pride in generosity and transforms giving from human achievement to grateful return.
v.15
"For we are strangers before thee, and sojourners, as were all our fathers: our days on the earth are as a shadow, and there is none abiding." (KJV) — David, king over all Israel for forty years, calls himself a "stranger and sojourner." The man with the greatest earthly position acknowledges the brevity and transience of human life. "None abiding" — nothing in this life is permanent. This honest eschatology empowered radical generosity: those who know they are passing through hold loosely what they cannot keep.
v.17
"I know also, my God, that thou triest the heart, and hast pleasure in uprightness. As for me, in the uprightness of mine heart I have willingly offered all these things: and now have I seen with joy thy people, which are present here, to offer willingly unto thee." (KJV) — God tests hearts and delights in integrity. David's joy is doubled: his own willing offering and the sight of his people offering willingly. A leader's greatest joy is seeing those he leads respond to God with the same wholehearted devotion he models.

Cross-References

  • v.5 — Exodus 35:5, 21-29 — Moses' call for tabernacle offerings: "Whosoever is of a willing heart, let him bring it." The pattern is consistent — God's house is built by willing hearts, never coerced giving. David's appeal echoes Moses' appeal, and both produced overwhelming, joyful generosity.
  • v.11 — Matthew 6:13 — "For thine is the kingdom, and the power, and the glory, for ever." The doxology of the Lord's Prayer (in its liturgical form) echoes David's prayer directly. What David declared at the end of his life, Jesus taught His disciples to pray daily. The kingdom always belongs to God — we merely serve in it.
  • v.14 — James 1:17 — "Every good gift and every perfect gift is from above, and cometh down from the Father of lights." David's principle restated in the New Testament: everything we have — including the ability and desire to give — originates with God. Generosity is returning a fraction of what we have received.
  • v.15 — Hebrews 11:13 — "These all died in faith... confessing that they were strangers and pilgrims on the earth." David's self-description as "stranger and sojourner" places him in the great company of faith — those who lived for an eternal city while passing through a temporal world.
  • v.15 — Psalm 39:12 — "I am a stranger with thee, and a sojourner, as all my fathers were." David's own psalm expresses the same theology — human life is brief and borrowed. This conviction was not morbid despair but liberating reality that freed him to give generously.
  • v.18 — Philippians 2:13 — "For it is God which worketh in you both to will and to do of his good pleasure." David's prayer that God would "prepare their heart" unto Himself anticipates Paul's teaching that even the desire to obey God is God's work within us. Sanctification is divine initiative met by human cooperation.
  • v.22 — 1 Kings 1:38-40 — The first anointing of Solomon during Adonijah's attempted coup. Chapter 29's "second time" anointing was the public, national coronation after the emergency was past — the formal transfer of power with all Israel assembled and consenting.
  • v.28 — Genesis 25:8 — Abraham "died in a good old age, an old man, and full of years." David's death description echoes Abraham's — both patriarchs died "full" (satisfied, with nothing lacking). To die "full of days, riches, and honour" is the covenant blessing of a completed life.

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/1-chronicles-29/ Guzik highlights that David's prayer in vv.10-19 is one of the greatest prayers in Scripture, combining praise, theological depth, humility, and intercession in a single address. He emphasizes that "of thine own have we given thee" (v.14) is the foundational principle of all Christian stewardship — we are never donors, only stewards returning what was entrusted. Guzik also notes the beauty of David's death: a man who finished well after a life of both great triumph and great failure.
  • Charles Spurgeon: "'Thine, O LORD, is the greatness, and the power, and the glory.' Here is a man who possessed all three — greatness as Israel's champion, power as her king, glory as her conqueror — and he lays every crown at Jehovah's feet. David knew what many wealthy givers forget: we do not bestow upon God from our abundance; we merely unclasp our fingers from what was never ours. 'Of thine own have we given thee.' Every gold talent came from God's earth, refined by God-given skill, accumulated under God-given prosperity. The giver has nothing that the Giver did not first give. And mark this: David gave 'in the uprightness of his heart' and saw his people give 'willingly' — and his joy was complete. The leader who gives first and most opens a river of generosity in those who follow."

Translations

  • v.1 — KJV: "young and tender" / ESV: "young and inexperienced" — Hebrew "na'ar va-rakh" (young and tender/soft). KJV's "tender" preserves the Hebrew metaphor of softness (unformed, not yet hardened by experience); ESV's "inexperienced" interprets the metaphor for modern readers. Both convey that Solomon is not yet proven — the work requires maturity he has not yet developed.
  • v.2 — KJV: "glistering stones, and of divers colours" / ESV: "antimony, colored stones" — Hebrew "avney-puk" and "avney riqmah." "Puk" refers to antimony (a metallic element used for cosmetic eye-paint and decorative inlay); KJV's "glistering stones" is an interpretive guess. "Riqmah" means variegated/multicolored. ESV's "antimony" is technically more accurate but less evocative.
  • v.5 — KJV: "consecrate his service" / ESV: "consecrating himself" — Hebrew "le-malle yado" literally "to fill his hand." This idiom was used specifically for priestly ordination (Exodus 28:41; Leviticus 8:33). KJV's "consecrate his service" and ESV's "consecrating himself" both capture the priestly overtone — giving is presented as a form of self-offering to God's service.
  • v.7 — KJV: "ten thousand drams" / ESV: "10,000 darics" — Hebrew "adarkonim." These are Persian gold coins (darics), which is an acknowledged anachronism — darics did not exist in David's time. The Chronicler converts the value into currency his post-exilic readers would understand. KJV's "drams" (drachmas) is less precise; ESV's "darics" is historically specific to the Persian period.
  • v.18 — KJV: "keep this for ever in the imagination of the thoughts of the heart of thy people, and prepare their heart unto thee" / ESV: "keep forever such purposes and thoughts in the hearts of your people, and direct their hearts toward you" — Hebrew "yetser machshevoth lev." KJV's "imagination of the thoughts" preserves the Hebrew layering (yetser = inclination/formation; machshevoth = thoughts/plans). ESV simplifies to "purposes and thoughts." David prays that God would preserve the generous inclination permanently in the people's hearts — acknowledging that human devotion fades without divine preservation.
  • v.22 — KJV: "anointed him unto the LORD to be the chief governor" / ESV: "they anointed him as prince for the LORD" — Hebrew "nagid la-YHWH" (prince/ruler for the LORD). KJV's "chief governor" is an unusual rendering; ESV's "prince for the LORD" is more standard. The key phrase is "for the LORD" — Solomon's kingship belongs to God; he rules as God's representative, not as autonomous sovereign.

Reflection

  • 1. Everything we have comes from God and belongs to God (v.14). "Of thine own have we given thee" — this single statement transforms the entire theology of giving. We are not generous benefactors bestowing our possessions on a needy God. We are stewards returning a portion of what was entrusted. Every dollar, every talent, every hour was God's before it was ours. True generosity begins with recognizing that ownership was always an illusion. Give freely, because you are merely releasing what never belonged to you.
  • 2. We are strangers and sojourners — hold this world loosely (v.15). David, at the pinnacle of earthly success (forty-year reign, enormous wealth, national adoration), declares himself a temporary resident. If the greatest king of Israel recognized his transience, how much more should we? The brevity of life is not depressing but liberating — it frees us from accumulating what we cannot keep and redirects our investment toward what is eternal. What are you clinging to that you cannot take with you?
  • 3. Willing, joyful giving marks a healthy heart (v.9). "The people rejoiced, for that they offered willingly, because with perfect heart they offered willingly to the LORD." Joy accompanied their giving — it was not reluctant obligation but delighted worship. If giving feels like loss rather than worship, examine your heart. Generous giving from a willing spirit produces deep joy; reluctant giving from a grudging heart produces only resentment. Ask God to transform your heart until generosity becomes your greatest pleasure.
  • 4. Leaders who give first inspire generosity in others (vv.1-9). David gave from his personal treasury before challenging the people. His example opened the floodgates — the leaders gave, then the people gave, and all rejoiced. Leadership in generosity is the most powerful form of leadership. Those who call others to give while withholding themselves are hypocrites; those who give first and most create a culture of generosity. Lead with your own offering before asking for anyone else's.
  • 5. Pray that God would preserve the desire to serve Him (v.18). David's prayer reveals a profound awareness: human devotion is fragile. He asks God to "keep this for ever" in the people's hearts — to preserve the generous, willing spirit they currently feel. David knows that today's passion can become tomorrow's indifference without divine preservation. Pray this prayer for yourself and your community: "Lord, keep this devotion alive in our hearts. We cannot sustain our own zeal — preserve what You have stirred."
