Parables of Jesus

The Sower

The Parable

Jesus told this parable to a large crowd gathered by the Sea of Galilee. A farmer went out to sow seed, and it fell on four types of ground: the path (where birds ate it), rocky soil (where it sprang up quickly but withered), thorns (where it was choked), and good soil (where it produced a crop — thirty, sixty, or a hundredfold).

This is one of the few parables Jesus explained directly. The seed is the Word of God. The four soils represent four responses people have when they hear the gospel. The path represents hardened hearts where Satan snatches the Word away. Rocky soil represents emotional responders who fall away when difficulty comes. Thorns represent those choked by worldly worries, wealth, and desires. Good soil represents those who hear, receive, and bear lasting fruit.

Jesus told this parable at a turning point in His ministry — when opposition was growing and many were rejecting His message. He was explaining why the same Word produces different results in different people. The condition of the heart determines the harvest.

Key Scriptures

  • Matthew 13:3-9 — Jesus tells the parable of the four soils to the crowd by the sea.
  • Matthew 13:18-23 — Jesus privately explains the meaning to His disciples.
  • Mark 4:13-20 — Mark's account with the emphasis that understanding this parable unlocks all others.
  • Luke 8:11-15 — Luke's version emphasizing honest and good hearts that hold fast to the Word.
  • Isaiah 55:10-11 — God's Word never returns void — it accomplishes His purpose.

What Jesus Is Teaching

Jesus is teaching that the gospel's effectiveness depends not on the quality of the seed (God's Word is always powerful) but on the condition of the soil (the human heart). This parable confronts us with an uncomfortable truth: not everyone who hears the Word will respond with lasting faith. Some rejection is immediate, some gradual, some hidden beneath apparent growth. Only the heart that receives the Word with understanding, holds onto it through difficulty, and refuses to let worldly concerns choke it out will bear the fruit God intends.

For believers, this parable is both a diagnostic tool and a call to ongoing cultivation. We must honestly assess which soil best describes our current heart condition — and actively tend our spiritual ground through prayer, confession, and removal of thorns (distractions, anxieties, competing loves). The good news: soil can be broken up, rocks removed, and thorns pulled. A heart that was once hard can become fertile through the work of the Holy Spirit.

Reflection Questions

  • Which soil best describes your heart right now? Are there areas where the Word is being choked by worries, wealth, or worldly desires?
  • What "thorns" need to be pulled from your life to give God's Word room to grow and bear fruit?
  • How can you cultivate "good soil" — an honest, receptive heart that holds fast to God's Word through trials?
  • Are you sharing the seed (God's Word) with others, trusting that God will bring the harvest — even when some soil appears unresponsive?
