Job — Chapter 11

1Then Zophar the Naamathite answered and said:

2“Should a multitude of words go unanswered, and a man full of talk be judged right?

3Should your babble silence men, and when you mock, shall no one shame you?

4For you say, ‘My doctrine is pure, and I am clean in God’s eyes.’

5But oh, that God would speak and open his lips to you,

6and that he would tell you the secrets of wisdom! For he is manifold in understanding. Know then that God exacts of you less than your guilt deserves.

7“Can you find out the deep things of God? Can you find out the limit of the Almighty?

8It is higher than heaven—what can you do? Deeper than Sheol—what can you know?

9Its measure is longer than the earth and broader than the sea.

10If he passes through and imprisons and summons the court, who can turn him back?

11For he knows worthless men; when he sees iniquity, will he not consider it?

12But a stupid man will get understanding when a wild donkey’s colt is born a man!

13“If you prepare your heart, you will stretch out your hands toward him.

14If iniquity is in your hand, put it far away, and let not injustice dwell in your tents.

15Surely then you will lift up your face without blemish; you will be secure and will not fear.

16You will forget your misery; you will remember it as waters that have passed away.

17And your life will be brighter than the noonday; its darkness will be like the morning.

18And you will feel secure, because there is hope; you will look around and take your rest in security.

19You will lie down, and none will make you afraid; many will court your favor.

20But the eyes of the wicked will fail; all way of escape will be lost to them, and their hope is to breathe their last.”

Summary

Zophar's first speech — the bluntest of the three friends, he accuses Job of empty talk and insists God is actually punishing him less than his sin deserves.

Key Verses

  • v.6 — "Know then that God exacts of you less than your guilt deserves." (ESV) — The harshest accusation in the entire dialogue. Zophar doesn't merely say Job has sinned — he says Job's punishment is MILD compared to what he deserves. This statement contradicts God's own assessment: Job suffers "without cause" (2:3).
  • v.7 — "Can you find out the deep things of God? Can you find out the limit of the Almighty?" (ESV) — A profound question with a correct answer (no) but wrong application. God IS unsearchable — but Zophar uses this to shut down legitimate questioning rather than to inspire humble trust.
  • v.12 — "But a stupid man will get understanding when a wild donkey's colt is born a man!" (ESV) — Pure insult. Zophar compares Job to a wild donkey — untamed, stupid, incapable of wisdom. This is not comfort or counsel — it is contempt.
  • v.13-14 — "If you prepare your heart, you will stretch out your hands toward him. If iniquity is in your hand, put it far away." (ESV) — Zophar's prescription: repent. It sounds spiritual but assumes what has not been proven. Job has asked: "show me my sin" (6:24). No one can.

Cross-References

  • v.6 → Job 2:3 — God: "without cause." Zophar: "less than your iniquity deserves." The contrast is total — God says innocent; Zophar says doubly guilty.
  • v.7 → Romans 11:33 — "Oh, the depth of the riches and wisdom and knowledge of God! How unsearchable are his judgments." (ESV) Paul echoes Zophar's theology but in a context of worship, not accusation.
  • v.7 → Isaiah 55:8-9 — "For my thoughts are not your thoughts." (ESV) God IS beyond human searching — but this fact should produce humility in the counselor, not certainty.
  • v.8 → Psalm 139:8 — "If I ascend to heaven, you are there! If I make my bed in Sheol, you are there!" (ESV) The dimensions of God's presence — higher than heaven, deeper than Sheol.
  • v.12 → Job 39:5-8 — God will later speak of the wild donkey with respect — it is free, not stupid. Zophar's contempt is not God's view.
  • v.13 → 2 Chronicles 7:14 — "If my people who are called by my name humble themselves, and pray." (ESV) Preparation of heart IS biblical — but it applies to the actually guilty, not the falsely accused.
  • v.20 → Proverbs 11:7 — "When the wicked dies, his hope will perish." (ESV) The fate of the wicked is real — but Job is not wicked.

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): "https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/job-11/" target="_blank">enduringword.com Guzik notes that Zophar combines the worst elements of both previous friends: he has Eliphaz's certainty without his initial gentleness, and Bildad's dogmatism without his appeal to authority. Zophar speaks from pure arrogance. His theology of God's transcendence (vv.7-9) is correct, but he uses it as a blunt instrument rather than a source of comfort. The irony: Zophar says God's ways are beyond finding out, then proceeds to explain exactly what God is doing with Job.
  • Charles Spurgeon: "Zophar was the worst kind of comforter — the kind who kicks a man when he is down and then calls it spiritual counsel. 'God is punishing you less than you deserve' — what sort of friend says this to a man covered in sores who has buried ten children? If this is comfort, then a rack is a bed of ease. Zophar had the right God but the wrong heart. He knew the Almighty was great but did not know that the Almighty was also gracious."

Translations

  • v.3 → KJV: "Should thy lies make men hold their peace?" / ESV: "Should your babble silence men?" — Hebrew "bad" = idle talk, prattle, boasting. KJV's "lies" is stronger than the Hebrew warrants. ESV's "babble" captures the dismissive tone better: Zophar considers Job's words empty noise rather than deliberate falsehood.
  • v.6 → KJV: "they are double to that which is" / ESV: "he is manifold in understanding" — This phrase is notoriously difficult in Hebrew. KJV suggests wisdom has "two sides" (God knows what you don't). ESV reads it as "manifold" — God's wisdom is multidimensional. Both lead to the same conclusion: God sees what Job cannot.
  • v.12 → KJV: "though man be born like a wild ass's colt" / ESV: "when a wild donkey's colt is born a man!" — A difficult verse. KJV: vain man is born untamed (like a wild donkey). ESV: a stupid man will gain wisdom when a donkey is born human (i.e., never). ESV's reading is sharper and more insulting — it says Job will NEVER understand.
  • v.20 → KJV: "their hope shall be as the giving up of the ghost" / ESV: "their hope is to breathe their last" — Both mean the same: the wicked's only "hope" is death. Their final expectation is merely to die — no future, no rescue.

Reflection

  • 1. Beware the certainty that condemns (v.6). Zophar is absolutely certain — and absolutely wrong. He does not say "perhaps you have sinned" but "God punishes you less than you deserve." When you are most certain about someone else's spiritual condition, pause. Certainty about another person's guilt before God is almost always presumption.
  • 2. God's transcendence should humble the counselor, not embolden him (vv.7-9). "Can you find out the limit of the Almighty?" The correct response to this question is: "Then I too cannot fully explain what God is doing with Job." Zophar acknowledges God is beyond finding out, then immediately claims to know exactly what God is doing. The inconsistency is breathtaking.
  • 3. "Less than you deserve" is never comfort (v.6). Even if theologically true in some ultimate sense (all sinners deserve judgment), this statement never helps a suffering person. In Job's case, it is factually wrong. Resist the urge to tell suffering people they deserve worse — even if your theology says so. The place for that truth is private conviction, not public accusation.
  • 4. Simple prescriptions for complex problems (vv.13-14). "Prepare your heart, put away iniquity, and all will be well." This is the ancient equivalent of "have you tried praying more?" Simple answers to complex suffering are not just inadequate — they are insulting. Honor the complexity of another person's pain.
  • 5. The wicked's fate is real but not everyone who suffers is wicked (v.20). Zophar's warning about the wicked is theologically valid — the wicked will face judgment. But applying it to Job as a veiled threat reveals Zophar's true assessment: he thinks Job is wicked. Never use general theological truths as personal threats against specific individuals whose situations you do not fully understand.
