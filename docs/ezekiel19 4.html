Ezekiel — Chapter 19

1And you, take up a lamentation for the princes of Israel,

2and say: What was your mother? A lioness! Among lions she crouched; in the midst of young lions she reared her cubs.

3And she brought up one of her cubs; he became a young lion, and he learned to catch prey; he devoured men.

4The nations heard about him; he was caught in their pit, and they brought him with hooks to the land of Egypt.

5When she saw that she waited in vain, that her hope was lost, she took another of her cubs and made him a young lion.

6He prowled among the lions; he became a young lion, and he learned to catch prey; he devoured men,

7and seized their widows. He laid waste their cities, and the land was appalled and all who were in it at the sound of his roaring.

8Then the nations set against him from provinces on every side; they spread their net over him; he was taken in their pit.

9With hooks they put him in a cage and brought him to the king of Babylon; they brought him into custody, that his voice should no more be heard on the mountains of Israel.

10Your mother was like a vine in a vineyard planted by the water, fruitful and full of branches by reason of abundant water.

11Its strong stems became rulers’ scepters; it towered aloft among the thick boughs; it was seen in its height with the mass of its branches.

12But the vine was plucked up in fury, cast down to the ground; the east wind dried up its fruit; they were stripped off and withered. As for its strong stem, fire consumed it.

13Now it is planted in the wilderness, in a dry and thirsty land.

14And fire has gone out from the stem of its shoots, has consumed its fruit, so that there remains in it no strong stem, no scepter for ruling. This is a lamentation and has become a lamentation.

Summary

A funeral dirge for the princes of Israel. Ezekiel uses two vivid parables: first, a lioness who raises cubs (kings) that are captured and carried away in chains; second, a fruitful vine planted by the waters that is uprooted, withered by the east wind, and consumed by fire. The chapter mourns what could have been — royal potential destroyed by sin and foreign conquest.

Key Verses

v.1
"Moreover take thou up a lamentation for the princes of Israel" (KJV) — The command to sing a funeral song. The Hebrew word "qinah" is a formal dirge with a specific meter — the rhythm of weeping. God Himself commissions the funeral of a dynasty.
v.4
"The nations also heard of him; he was taken in their pit, and they brought him with chains into the land of Egypt" (KJV) — The first cub (Jehoahaz) — briefly king, quickly captured. Three months on the throne, then chains to Egypt. A young lion who never reached his potential.
v.12
"But she was plucked up in fury, she was cast down to the ground, and the east wind dried up her fruit: her strong rods were broken and withered; the fire consumed them" (KJV) — The vine uprooted. "East wind" = Babylon. God's fury tears up what He once planted. The vine that should have filled the earth (Psalm 80) is now kindling.
v.14
"And fire is gone out of a rod of her branches, which hath devoured her fruit, so that she hath no strong rod to be a sceptre to rule. This is a lamentation, and shall be for a lamentation" (KJV) — The most tragic line: the fire comes from WITHIN. Self-destruction. No sceptre remains. The dynasty is over. "This IS a lamentation" — present tense. Already true.

Cross-References

  • v.1 — Genesis 49:9 — "Judah is a lion's whelp" — the tribal identity of Judah as lion. v.2 — 2 Kings 23:31-34 — Jehoahaz's three-month reign and deportation to Egypt. v.5-9 — 2 Kings 24:8-15 — Jehoiachin's capture and exile to Babylon. v.10 — Psalm 80:8-16 — Israel as a vine planted by God, now burned and cut down. v.12 — Jeremiah 18:17 — "I will scatter them as with an east wind before the enemy." v.14 — 2 Kings 24:20 — Zedekiah's rebellion that triggered Jerusalem's final destruction. v.14 — Judges 9:15 — Jotham's parable: fire from the bramble consuming the cedars — self-destruction imagery.

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/ezekiel-19/
  • Charles Spurgeon: "God sings the funeral of kings who would not bow before Him. He composes the dirge for dynasties that spent their strength on pride instead of obedience. The lioness raised her cubs to roar, but they roared against the wrong enemy — they roared against heaven, and heaven swallowed them whole."

Translations

  • v.1 — "lamentation" (KJV) — Hebrew "qinah" = a formal funeral dirge composed in a specific 3:2 meter (three beats followed by two beats). The uneven rhythm mimics the catch in the breath of someone weeping. The entire chapter follows this poetic pattern.
  • v.2 — "lioness" (KJV/ESV) — Hebrew "l'biyya" = specifically a female lion. The mother of the dynasty. In the ancient Near East, the lion was a royal symbol; Judah's lion imagery goes back to Jacob's blessing (Genesis 49:9).
  • v.12 — "east wind" (KJV/ESV) — Hebrew "ruach haqadim" = the sirocco wind from the eastern desert, hot and destructive. Consistently used as a metaphor for Babylon's invasion (cf. Jeremiah 18:17; Hosea 13:15).
  • v.14 — "sceptre" (KJV) — Hebrew "shebet" = rod of authority, ruling staff. "No strong rod to be a sceptre" = no one left qualified to rule. The dynasty is extinct.

Reflection

  • 1. God sings funerals for wasted potential (v.1). This is a lamentation — grief, not gloating. God mourns what the kings of Judah COULD have been. He mourns wasted gifts in your life the same way. Don't make Him sing your funeral dirge.
  • 2. The fire came from WITHIN (v.14). The vine's own branches produce the fire that destroys it. External enemies only finish what internal corruption begins. Self-destruction is always the real danger.
  • 3. Three months of glory, then chains (v.4). Jehoahaz ruled for 90 days. Brief power without God leads to captivity. Don't confuse a short season of success with lasting significance.
  • 4. The east wind dries what water once nourished (v.12). A vine that was "planted by the waters" can still be destroyed when it forgets the Source. Proximity to God's blessings doesn't guarantee permanence — faithfulness does.
  • 5. "This IS a lamentation" (v.14). Present tense. The tragedy is already happening. Don't wait until the funeral to recognize what's dying. Wake up NOW to what you're losing through disobedience or neglect.
