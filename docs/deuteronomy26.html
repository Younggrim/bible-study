Deuteronomy — Chapter 26

1“When you come into the land that the LORD your God is giving you for an inheritance and have taken possession of it and live in it,

2you shall take some of the first of all the fruit of the ground, which you harvest from your land that the LORD your God is giving you, and you shall put it in a basket, and you shall go to the place that the LORD your God will choose, to make his name to dwell there.

3And you shall go to the priest who is in office at that time and say to him, ‘I declare today to the LORD your God that I have come into the land that the LORD swore to our fathers to give us.’

4Then the priest shall take the basket from your hand and set it down before the altar of the LORD your God.

5“And you shall make response before the LORD your God, ‘A wandering Aramean was my father. And he went down into Egypt and sojourned there, few in number, and there he became a nation, great, mighty, and populous.

6And the Egyptians treated us harshly and humiliated us and laid on us hard labor.

7Then we cried to the LORD, the God of our fathers, and the LORD heard our voice and saw our affliction, our toil, and our oppression.

8And the LORD brought us out of Egypt with a mighty hand and an outstretched arm, with great deeds of terror, with signs and wonders.

9And he brought us into this place and gave us this land, a land flowing with milk and honey.

10And behold, now I bring the first of the fruit of the ground, which you, O LORD, have given me.’ And you shall set it down before the LORD your God and worship before the LORD your God.

11And you shall rejoice in all the good that the LORD your God has given to you and to your house, you, and the Levite, and the sojourner who is among you.

14I have not eaten of the tithe while I was mourning, or removed any of it while I was unclean, or offered any of it to the dead. I have obeyed the voice of the LORD my God. I have done according to all that you have commanded me.

15Look down from your holy habitation, from heaven, and bless your people Israel and the ground that you have given us, as you swore to our fathers, a land flowing with milk and honey.’

18And the LORD has declared today that you are a people for his treasured possession, as he has promised you, and that you are to keep all his commandments,

19and that he will set you in praise and in fame and in honor high above all nations that he has made, and that you shall be a people holy to the LORD your God, as he promised.”

Summary

God commands firstfruits and tithe offerings accompanied by a confession of His faithfulness — remembering that He brought them from slavery to abundance.

Key Verses

  • v.5 — "A Syrian ready to perish was my father, and he went down into Egypt, and sojourned there with a few, and became there a nation, great, mighty, and populous." (ESV) — The oldest creed in Israel. Every Israelite confesses: we came from nothing. God made us everything. Humility is the foundation of worship.
  • v.8 — "And the LORD brought us forth out of Egypt with a mighty hand, and with an outstretched arm, and with great terribleness, and with signs, and with wonders." (ESV) — God's salvation is powerful, visible, and undeniable. The Exodus is the defining act of redemption in the Old Testament.
  • v.10 — "And now, behold, I have brought the firstfruits of the land, which you, O LORD, hast given me." (ESV) — The logic of giving: because God gave first, we give back. Firstfruits are acknowledgment that everything comes from God.
  • v.15 — "Look down from your holy habitation, from heaven, and bless your people Israel, and the land which you hast given us." (ESV) — Prayer grounded in obedience. The worshiper has been faithful; now he asks God to bless.
  • v.17 — "You hast avouched the LORD this day to be your God, and to walk in his ways, and to keep his statutes, and his commandments, and his judgments, and to listen to his voice." (ESV) — Israel's solemn declaration of covenant loyalty. Five commitments: walk, keep statutes, keep commandments, keep judgments, listen.
  • v.18 — "And the LORD has avouched you this day to be his peculiar people, as he has promised you." (ESV) — God's reciprocal declaration. He claims Israel as His own treasured possession. The covenant is bilateral — both parties commit.
  • v.19 — "And to make you high above all nations which he has made, in praise, and in name, and in honour; and that you may be an holy people to the LORD your God." (ESV) — God's purpose for Israel: exaltation and holiness. Not for pride but for witness.

Authorship & Background

  • Author: Moses. Deuteronomy is the fifth and final book of the Pentateuch, written at the end of the 40 years of wilderness wandering (approximately 1406 BC). Hebrew title: "Devarim" — "Words/Things." Greek title: "Deuteronomion" — "Second Law" (a repetition/expansion of the Law for the new generation). The book consists of Moses' farewell speeches to Israel on the plains of Moab, just before they enter the Promised Land. Moses will not enter with them — these are his final words to the nation he has led for 40 years.
  • Historical Context: Chapter 26 concludes the specific legislation section of Deuteronomy (chapters 12-26). It is a fitting climax: after all the individual laws, Moses prescribes two liturgical confessions that Israel must make when they enter the land — the firstfruits confession (vv.1-11) and the tithe declaration (vv.12-15). These are not mere rituals but theological statements of identity. The firstfruits confession (vv.5-10) is one of the oldest and most important creedal statements in the Old Testament — a miniature salvation history recited by every Israelite farmer. It begins with "A Syrian ready to perish was my father" (KJV) / "A wandering Aramean was my father" (ESV) — a reference to Jacob — and traces God's saving acts from Egypt to the Promised Land. The chapter concludes with a mutual "avouching" (vv.16-19): Israel declares God as their God, and God declares Israel as His people. This is covenant ratification language — a bilateral commitment. Chapter 26 thus forms a theological bookend to the law section: the laws are not arbitrary rules but the terms of a relationship between God and His chosen people.
  • The Firstfruits Confession (vv.1-11): "When thou art come in unto the land" (v.1) — this ceremony begins only after settlement. The worshiper brings "the first of all the fruit of the earth" (v.2) to "the place which the LORD thy God shall choose" (v.2). He declares to the priest: "I profess this day unto the LORD thy God, that I am come unto the country which the LORD sware unto our fathers for to give us" (v.3). Then the great confession (vv.5-10): "A Syrian ready to perish was my father" (v.5) — Jacob, the wandering Aramean, nearly destroyed. "He went down into Egypt... with a few, and became there a nation, great, mighty, and populous" (v.5). "The Egyptians evil entreated us, and afflicted us" (v.6). "We cried unto the LORD... the LORD heard our voice" (v.7). "The LORD brought us forth out of Egypt with a mighty hand" (v.8). "He hath brought us into this place, and hath given us this land" (v.9). "Now, behold, I have brought the firstfruits" (v.10). The logic: God saved us from nothing, therefore we give Him the first of everything. The ceremony concludes with worship and rejoicing — shared with the Levite and the stranger (v.11).
  • The Tithe Declaration (vv.12-15): Every third year ("the year of tithing," v.12), after distributing the tithe to the Levite, stranger, fatherless, and widow, the Israelite makes a solemn declaration before God: "I have brought away the hallowed things out of mine house" (v.13). He affirms complete obedience: "I have not transgressed thy commandments, neither have I forgotten them" (v.13). Three negatives of purity: "I have not eaten thereof in my mourning, neither have I taken away ought thereof for any unclean use, nor given ought thereof for the dead" (v.14). Then a prayer: "Look down from thy holy habitation, from heaven, and bless thy people Israel" (v.15). This is remarkable: the worshiper declares his faithfulness and then asks God to bless based on that faithfulness.
  • The Mutual Avouching (vv.16-19): "This day the LORD thy God hath commanded thee to do these statutes and judgments" (v.16). "Thou hast avouched the LORD this day to be thy God" (v.17) — Israel declares God as their God. "And the LORD hath avouched thee this day to be his peculiar people" (v.18) — God declares Israel as His people. The word "avouched" (Hebrew "he'emir") means to declare publicly, to affirm solemnly. This is bilateral covenant language: both parties make commitments. God commits to exalt Israel "high above all nations... in praise, and in name, and in honour" (v.19). Israel commits to "walk in his ways, and to keep his statutes" (v.17).

Map & Geography

  • Moses speaks from the plains of Moab. The firstfruits liturgy recites Israel's geographic story: "A Syrian ready to perish was my father, and he went down into Egypt... and the LORD brought us forth out of Egypt" (vv.5-8).

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): enduringword.com Guzik highlights the firstfruits confession as one of the most important theological statements in the Old Testament — a creed that every Israelite recited, grounding worship in historical redemption. He notes the mutual avouching as covenant language: God and Israel publicly commit to each other.
  • Charles Spurgeon: "A Syrian ready to perish was my father.' This is where all true worship begins — with the confession that we were nothing, had nothing, deserved nothing. The man who brings his firstfruits to God must first remember that he was a beggar whom God made rich. Gratitude is the soil in which generosity grows. Forget your poverty and you will forget your God."

Translations

Show translations:

v.5 — Hebrew "Arami oved avi" — an Aramean perishing/wandering was my father.
KJV: "A Syrian ready to perish was my father"
ESV: "A wandering Aramean was my father"
ASV: "A Syrian ready to perish was my father"
NET: "A wandering Aramean was my ancestor"
WEB: "A Syrian ready to perish was my father"
Analysis: One of the most debated translations in Deuteronomy.
"Oved" can mean "perishing/ready to perish" (KJV/ASV/WEB) or
"wandering/lost" (ESV/NET). The LXX has "My father abandoned Syria."
KJV/ASV/WEB emphasize Jacob's vulnerability; ESV/NET emphasize his
nomadic status. NET updates "father" to "ancestor." Both readings
capture aspects of Jacob's condition: homeless wanderer on the verge
of destruction. "Arami" = Aramean/Syrian — Jacob lived in Aram
(Paddan-aram) with Laban.

v.8 — Hebrew "u-v'mora gadol" — and with great terror/dread.
KJV: "with great terribleness"
ESV: "with great deeds of terror"
ASV: "with great terribleness"
NET: "with great power"
WEB: "with great terror"
Analysis: "Mora" = terror, awesome dread. KJV/ASV use the archaic
"terribleness." ESV expands to "deeds of terror." WEB uses simple
"terror." NET diverges significantly with "great power" — losing
the terror imagery in favor of an emphasis on might. Refers to the
plagues and Red Sea crossing — events that terrified Egypt and
surrounding nations.

v.13 — Hebrew "ha-qodesh" — the holy/sacred portion.
KJV: "I have brought away the hallowed things out of mine house"
ESV: "I have removed the sacred portion out of my house"
ASV: "I have put away the hallowed things out of my house"
NET: "I have removed the sacred offering from my house"
WEB: "I have put away the holy things out of my house"
Analysis: The tithe is holy — set apart for God's purposes. KJV/WEB
use "hallowed/holy things." ESV uses "sacred portion." NET uses
"sacred offering." ASV uses "hallowed things." All convey that the
tithe belongs to God and is distributed to those He designates.

v.14 — Hebrew "v'lo natati mimmennu l'met" — and I have not given from it for the dead.
KJV: "nor given ought thereof for the dead"
ESV: "or offered any of it to the dead"
ASV: "nor given thereof for the dead"
NET: "I have not offered any of it to the dead"
WEB: "neither have I given of it for the dead"
Analysis: KJV/ASV/WEB use "given for the dead." ESV/NET use
"offered to the dead" — implying a religious ritual directed at
deceased persons. This likely refers to pagan funeral practices
where food was offered to or on behalf of the dead. The tithe must
not be contaminated by death rituals.

v.17 — Hebrew "he'emarta" — you have declared/avouched.
KJV: "Thou hast avouched the LORD this day to be thy God"
ESV: "You have declared today that the LORD is your God"
ASV: "Thou hast avouched Jehovah this day to be thy God"
NET: "Today you have declared the LORD to be your God"
WEB: "You have declared Yahweh this day to be your God"
Analysis: This rare verb (used only here and v.18 in this form)
means to declare solemnly, to affirm publicly. KJV/ASV retain
"avouched" — an archaic legal term meaning to affirm/guarantee.
ESV/NET/WEB use "declared" — modern equivalent. Note divine names:
ASV uses "Jehovah," WEB uses "Yahweh."

v.18 — Hebrew "am s'gullah" — a people of treasured possession.
KJV: "his peculiar people"
ESV: "a people for his treasured possession"
ASV: "a people for his own possession"
NET: "his treasured people"
WEB: "a people for his own possession"
Analysis: "S'gullah" = treasured possession, special property.
KJV's "peculiar" means "specially one's own" (not "strange" as in
modern usage). ESV is most explicit: "treasured possession."
ASV/WEB use "own possession." NET condenses to "treasured people."
Israel is God's personal treasure among the nations (cf. 1 Peter
2:9).

The Bible Project — Deuteronomy Overview

Reflection

  • 1. Worship begins with remembering our poverty (v.5). "A Syrian ready to perish was my father." Before we bring anything to God, we must remember what we were without Him — lost, wandering, perishing. Genuine worship is rooted in gratitude for undeserved grace. Pride kills worship.
  • 2. The firstfruits principle (vv.2,10). God gets the first and best, not the remainder. This applies to time, money, energy, and attention. When we give God the leftovers, we declare that He is not first in our lives.
  • 3. Confession of faith is historical (vv.5-10). Israel's creed is not abstract theology but a recounting of God's acts in history. Our faith is grounded in what God has done — creation, exodus, incarnation, cross, resurrection. Christianity is a historical faith, not a philosophy.
  • 4. Obedience enables bold prayer (vv.13-15). The tithe declaration shows a worshiper who has been faithful asking God to bless. This is not earning God's favor but living in the confidence that obedience and blessing are connected. Disobedience weakens our confidence in prayer (1 John 3:21-22).
  • 5. Covenant is bilateral (vv.17-18). God commits to Israel AND Israel commits to God. Grace is free but not passive. God avouches us; we must avouch Him. The relationship requires response, commitment, and active faithfulness from both parties.
  • 6. Rejoicing is commanded (v.11). "Thou shalt rejoice in every good thing which the LORD thy God hath given unto thee." Joy is not optional — it is the proper response to God's goodness. Joyless religion dishonors the Giver.
