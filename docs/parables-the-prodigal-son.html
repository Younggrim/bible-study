Parables of Jesus

The Prodigal Son

The Parable

Jesus told this parable in response to the Pharisees grumbling about His fellowship with sinners. A father had two sons. The younger demanded his inheritance early — an act of profound disrespect, essentially wishing his father dead — and traveled to a far country where he squandered everything in reckless living. When a severe famine struck, he found himself feeding pigs, so desperate he longed to eat their food. No one gave him anything.

Coming to his senses, the younger son rehearsed a speech of repentance and headed home, hoping only to be made a hired servant. But while he was still a long way off, his father saw him, was filled with compassion, ran to him, embraced him, and kissed him. Before the son could finish his speech, the father called for the best robe, a ring, sandals, and a fattened calf — throwing a feast to celebrate his return.

The elder brother, working faithfully in the fields, heard the celebration and was furious. He refused to go in, complaining that despite his years of obedience, he'd never received such a party. The father gently reminded him: "Son, you are always with me, and everything I have is yours. But we had to celebrate — your brother was dead and is alive again; he was lost and is found."

Key Scriptures

  • Luke 15:20-24 — The father runs to his returning son, restoring him fully with robe, ring, and celebration.
  • Ephesians 2:4-5 — God, rich in mercy, made us alive with Christ even when we were dead in sin.
  • Romans 5:8 — God demonstrates His love: while we were still sinners, Christ died for us.
  • 2 Corinthians 5:17 — Anyone in Christ is a new creation; the old has gone, the new has come.
  • Isaiah 55:7 — Let the wicked forsake their ways; the Lord will have mercy and freely pardon.

What Jesus Is Teaching

At its heart, this parable reveals the extravagant, pursuing love of God the Father. The younger son represents every sinner who wanders far from God — demanding autonomy, squandering blessings, and ending up in spiritual ruin. But the stunning center of the story is the father's response: he doesn't wait with crossed arms. He runs. He doesn't demand the rehearsed speech. He restores fully — robe (honor), ring (authority), sandals (sonship), and a feast (joy). This is grace that exceeds anything we dare to ask.

Yet Jesus also confronts the elder brother's sin — a self-righteousness that resents grace given to others. The Pharisees saw themselves in this son: dutiful, rule-keeping, but joyless and bitter. Jesus exposes that both sons were lost — one in rebellion, the other in religion. The father's heart is for both to come in and celebrate. The parable ends without telling us whether the elder brother entered the feast — leaving the question open for every hearer: Will you receive the Father's grace, or will you stand outside resenting it?

Reflection Questions

  • Do you identify more with the younger son (rebellion and wandering) or the elder brother (duty without joy, resentment toward grace given to others)?
  • Is there an area of your life where you are "in a far country" — distant from God, trying to find satisfaction apart from Him?
  • How does the father's running, embracing response change your view of how God receives repentant sinners?
  • Are there people you secretly resent God showing grace to? How does the elder brother's story challenge that attitude?
