Parables of Jesus

The Prodigal Son

The Parable

A man had two sons. The younger demanded his share of the inheritance — an unthinkable insult in ancient culture, essentially saying, "I wish you were dead." Yet the father divided his property between them. The younger son gathered everything and traveled to a distant country, where he squandered his wealth in reckless living. When a severe famine struck, he found himself feeding pigs — the lowest possible degradation for a Jewish man — and longing to eat even the pods the swine consumed.

"When he came to himself," the text says — a moment of brutal clarity — he remembered his father's house where even the hired servants had food to spare. He rehearsed a speech: "Father, I have sinned against heaven and before you. I am no longer worthy to be called your son." He rose and headed home, expecting nothing more than servant status.

But while he was still a great distance away, his father saw him and was filled with compassion. The father ran — undignified for a patriarch in that culture — threw his arms around his son and kissed him. Before the boy could finish his rehearsed speech, the father called for the best robe, a ring, sandals, and a feast. "This my son was dead, and is alive again; he was lost, and is found." Meanwhile, the older brother refused to come in, angry at the celebration, revealing his own kind of lostness — serving out of obligation rather than love.

Key Scriptures

  • Luke 15:11-24 — The younger son's rebellion, ruin, repentance, and the father's extravagant restoration.
  • Luke 15:25-32 — The elder brother's resentment reveals that proximity to the father doesn't guarantee intimacy with him.
  • Romans 5:8 — "While we were still sinners, Christ died for us" — the running father in theological form.
  • Ephesians 2:4-5 — "Because of his great love... even when we were dead in our trespasses, made us alive together with Christ."
  • 1 John 1:9 — "If we confess our sins, he is faithful and just to forgive us" — the promise that coming home always works.

What Jesus Was Teaching

Jesus told this parable in response to the Pharisees grumbling that He received sinners and ate with them (Luke 15:2). The story answers their complaint at every level. The father represents God — not an angry judge waiting to condemn, but a watching father longing for reunion. His running shatters the image of a distant deity. The robe, ring, and sandals are symbols of full sonship restored — not partial acceptance, not probation, but complete reinstatement. Grace is not measured or cautious; it is lavish.

But the parable has a second target: the elder brother represents the Pharisees themselves — those who stayed home, kept the rules, yet never truly knew the father's heart. They served for reward rather than love. Their obedience had become a ledger they kept against others. Jesus leaves the elder brother's story unresolved — the father pleads with him, but we never learn if he comes in. It's an open invitation: will the religious insiders join the party, or will their self-righteousness keep them outside while sinners feast at the father's table?

Reflection Questions

  • Do you identify more with the younger son (wandering from God) or the elder brother (serving God resentfully)? Why?
  • Is there an area of your life where you need to "come to yourself" and return to the Father?
  • How does the father's running challenge your image of God — do you see Him as eager to forgive or reluctant?
  • Are there people in your life whose restoration makes you uncomfortable, like the elder brother? What does that reveal about your heart?
