Psalms — Chapter 27

1The LORD is my light and my salvation; whom shall I fear? The LORD is the stronghold of my life; of whom shall I be afraid?

2When evildoers assail me to eat up my flesh, my adversaries and foes, it is they who stumble and fall.

3Though an army encamp against me, my heart shall not fear; though war arise against me, yet I will be confident.

4One thing have I asked of the LORD, that will I seek after: that I may dwell in the house of the LORD all the days of my life, to gaze upon the beauty of the LORD and to inquire in his temple.

5For he will hide me in his shelter in the day of trouble; he will conceal me under the cover of his tent; he will lift me high upon a rock.

6And now my head shall be lifted up above my enemies all around me, and I will offer in his tent sacrifices with shouts of joy; I will sing and make melody to the LORD.

7Hear, O LORD, when I cry aloud; be gracious to me and answer me!

8You have said, “Seek my face.” My heart says to you, “Your face, LORD, do I seek.”

9Hide not your face from me. Turn not your servant away in anger, O you who have been my help. Cast me not off; forsake me not, O God of my salvation!

10For my father and my mother have forsaken me, but the LORD will take me in.

11Teach me your way, O LORD, and lead me on a level path because of my enemies.

12Give me not up to the will of my adversaries; for false witnesses have risen against me, and they breathe out violence.

13I believe that I shall look upon the goodness of the LORD in the land of the living!

14Wait for the LORD; be strong, and let your heart take courage; wait for the LORD!

Summary

A psalm of trust and longing — 'The LORD is my light and my salvation; whom shall I fear?' David's one desire is to dwell in God's house; though father and mother forsake him, the LORD will take him up. 'Wait on the LORD.'

Key Verses

v.1
"The LORD is my light and my salvation; whom shall I fear?" (KJV) — Three declarations produce two rhetorical questions. Light (against darkness), salvation (against danger), stronghold (against assault). If God is all three, fear becomes irrational.
v.4
"One thing have I desired of the LORD, that will I seek after; that I may dwell in the house of the LORD all the days of my life, to behold the beauty of the LORD" (KJV) — David's supreme desire: perpetual presence with God. Not protection, victory, or prosperity — but BEHOLDING God's beauty. This is the soul's deepest longing distilled.
v.10
"When my father and my mother forsake me, then the LORD will take me up." (KJV) — Even the most primal human relationship (parent-child) may fail. But God never abandons. He "takes up" (asaph = gathers, receives, adopts) those whom even family rejects.
v.13-14
"I had fainted, unless I had believed to see the goodness of the LORD in the land of the living. Wait on the LORD: be of good courage" (KJV) — Faith preserves from despair: believe you WILL see God's goodness — not just in eternity but in THIS life. Then wait.

Cross-References

  • v.1 — John 8:12 — "I am the light of the world." Christ as the fulfillment of God as "light." v.1 — Micah 7:8 — "When I sit in darkness, the LORD shall be a light unto me." Same light-in-darkness theology. v.4 — Luke 10:42 — "One thing is needful." Mary chose the same "one thing" — presence with Jesus. v.4 — Philippians 3:13 — "This one thing I do." Paul's singular focus echoes David's. v.8 — 2 Chronicles 7:14 — "If my people... shall seek my face." God's invitation to face-seeking. v.10 — Isaiah 49:15 — "Can a woman forget her sucking child?... yet will I not forget thee." God surpasses even maternal faithfulness. v.14 — Isaiah 40:31 — "They that wait upon the LORD shall renew their strength." Same waiting-and-strength theology.

Translations

  • v.1 — KJV: "the strength of my life" / ESV: "the stronghold of my life" — Hebrew "ma'oz" = stronghold, fortress, refuge. ESV's "stronghold" is more precise than KJV's abstract "strength."
  • v.4 — KJV: "to behold the beauty of the LORD" / ESV: "to gaze upon the beauty of the LORD" — Hebrew "no'am" = pleasantness, beauty, graciousness. David wants to GAZE (not glance) at God's loveliness. Sustained contemplation, not brief acknowledgment.
  • v.10 — KJV: "the LORD will take me up" / ESV: "the LORD will take me in" — Hebrew "ya'aspheni" = will gather me, receive me. The image is of adoption — God gathers the orphaned/ abandoned into His own family.
  • v.13 — KJV places "I had fainted, unless" in italics (not in Hebrew). The Hebrew literally reads: "[Surely] I believe I will see the goodness of the LORD in the land of the living." The KJV adds the implied clause for sense. ESV: "I believe that I shall look upon the goodness of the LORD" — simple confidence statement.

Reflection

  • 1. If God is your light, fear is irrational (v.1). The logic is complete: if the LORD handles darkness, danger, and attack — what is left to fear? Fear persists when we forget who God is. Remind yourself of His identity, and fear loses its basis.
  • 2. Reduce life to "one thing" (v.4). David had many needs, many enemies, many responsibilities — but ONE desire dominated: God's presence. Simplify. What is the one thing? Everything else flows from or is secondary to this.
  • 3. God adopts the abandoned (v.10). Even if every human relationship fails — parents, spouse, friends — God receives you. You cannot be orphaned by God. He gathers what others scatter.
  • 4. Believe you will see goodness IN THIS LIFE (v.13). Not only in heaven. David expected to see God's goodness "in the land of the living" — this side of eternity. Hope is not only eschatological — God is good NOW.
  • 5. Wait — and then wait again (v.14). Repeated: "wait on the LORD... wait, I say, on the LORD." The repetition acknowledges that waiting is hard enough to need reinforcement. Be strong. Take courage. And wait.
