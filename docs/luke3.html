8Bring forth therefore fruits worthy of repentance, and begin not to say within yourselves, We have Abraham to our father: for I say unto you, That God is able of these stones to raise up children unto Abraham.

9And now also the axe is laid unto the root of the trees: every tree therefore which bringeth not forth good fruit is hewn down, and cast into the fire.

10And the people asked him, saying, What shall we do then?

11He answereth and saith unto them, He that hath two coats, let him impart to him that hath none; and he that hath meat, let him do likewise.

12Then came also publicans to be baptized, and said unto him, Master, what shall we do?

13And he said unto them, Exact no more than that which is appointed you.

14And the soldiers likewise demanded of him, saying, And what shall we do? And he said unto them, Do violence to no man, neither accuse any falsely; and be content with your wages.

Summary

John the Baptist preaches repentance with specific ethical demands for each class of people, baptizes Jesus as heaven opens with the Father's voice and the Spirit's descent, and Luke traces Jesus' genealogy back through David to Adam and God.

Authorship & Background

Author: Luke (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).
Historical Context: Chapter 3 records the ministry of John the Baptist and the baptism of Jesus, followed by Luke's genealogy of Christ. Luke the historian anchors the narrative in specific historical time — naming the emperor (Tiberius), the governor (Pilate), the tetrarchs (Herod, Philip, Lysanias), and the high priests (Annas and Caiaphas). This is approximately AD 26-27.
Luke's Genealogy (vv.23-38): Unlike Matthew's genealogy (which goes from Abraham forward to Jesus through Joseph's legal line), Luke traces Jesus' lineage backward from Jesus all the way to Adam and to God. Most scholars believe Luke traces the biological line through Mary (Heli being Mary's father), while Matthew traces the legal line through Joseph. Luke's genealogy emphasizes Jesus' connection to all humanity — He is the Son of Adam, the Son of God.
John's Practical Ethics (vv.10-14): Unique to Luke — John gives specific, practical instructions to different groups: the crowds (share your possessions), tax collectors (don't extort), soldiers (don't abuse power). Repentance produces concrete behavioral change, not just religious feeling.

Map & Geography

  • The Jordan River: John baptized in the region around the Jordan, likely near Bethany beyond the Jordan (Bethabara), in the lower Jordan Valley near Jericho.
  • Luke's genealogy traces from Nazareth (where Jesus lived) back to Adam — spanning the entire geography of human history from Eden to Galilee.

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): enduringword.com Guzik examines Luke's historical precision, John's practical ethics for different groups, the baptism of Jesus, and the differences between Luke's and Matthew's genealogies.
  • Charles Spurgeon: Sermon: "Fruits Worthy of Repentance" on v.8 (MTP Vol. 22, No. 1297) "Bring forth fruits — not leaves, not blossoms, not promises — fruits. Visible, tangible evidence that something has changed inside. The tax collector stops cheating. The soldier stops bullying. The man with two coats gives one away. That is what repentance looks like when it's real." Sermon: "The Beloved Son" on v.22 (MTP Vol. 37, No. 2218) "Thou art my beloved Son — spoken before one miracle, before one sermon, before one act of public ministry. The Father's love is not earned by performance. It is declared over identity. And in Christ, that same declaration is spoken over you."

Reflection

  • 1. John gave different instructions to different groups (vv.10-14) — practical, specific, tailored to their situation. Repentance looks different for different people. What does repentance look like specifically in YOUR life, in YOUR situation, with YOUR particular temptations?
  • 2. John told the soldiers "be content with your wages" (v.14). Discontentment drives much of our sin — greed, envy, dishonesty. Where is discontentment driving sinful behavior in your life? What would contentment look like?
  • 3. The Father said "Thou art my beloved Son; in thee I am well pleased" (v.22) — before Jesus had done anything publicly. Your identity in Christ is not based on your performance. Do you live from approval or for approval?
  • 4. Luke traces Jesus' lineage to Adam (v.38) — connecting Him to all humanity. Jesus is not just the Jewish Messiah but the Savior of the world. How does the universal scope of the Gospel affect how you view people who are different from you?
  • 5. Luke anchors his narrative in specific historical facts (v.1-2). Christianity is not a myth or philosophy — it is rooted in real history. How does the historical grounding of the Gospel strengthen your confidence in its truth?
  • 6. John told people with two coats to give one away (v.11). Simple, practical generosity. What do you have two of that someone else has none of? What would it look like to share it this week?
  • 7. John rebuked Herod for his sin (v.19) — and it cost him his freedom and eventually his life. Speaking truth to power is costly. Is there a situation where you need to speak truth even though it will cost you something?
  • 8. The genealogy goes all the way back to "Adam, which was the son of God" (v.38). Every human being is descended from Adam — made in God's image. How does this truth shape how you treat every person you encounter, regardless of their background?
  • 9. Jesus was "about thirty years of age" (v.23) when He began His ministry — 30 years of preparation for 3 years of public work. God often prepares us longer than we expect. Are you in a season of preparation? How are you stewarding it?
