The 12 Apostles

James — Son of Alphaeus

Who Was James son of Alphaeus

James the son of Alphaeus is one of the least-known apostles. He is sometimes called "James the Less" (or "James the Younger") to distinguish him from James the son of Zebedee. The designation "the Less" likely refers to his shorter stature or younger age rather than lesser importance. Beyond his inclusion in every apostolic list, Scripture tells us almost nothing specific about his words or deeds — and yet Jesus chose him among the twelve, and he was present for every major moment of Christ's ministry.

Some scholars identify James son of Alphaeus with "James the brother of the Lord" who led the Jerusalem church, though this is debated. What is clear is that this James was a faithful, consistent presence among the apostles. He walked with Jesus for three years, witnessed His miracles, heard His teachings, saw His death and resurrection, and was present in the upper room at Pentecost. His faithfulness did not depend on being noticed or celebrated — it depended on being present and obedient.

The very obscurity of James son of Alphaeus speaks volumes about the nature of Christian discipleship. Not every follower of Jesus will be a prominent leader, a prolific writer, or a famous martyr. Some are called to serve faithfully in the background — to be present, to pray, to support, to endure. The kingdom of God advances not only through the Peters and Pauls but through the countless unnamed believers who show up day after day in quiet obedience. James represents all of them.

Key Scriptures

  • Matthew 10:3 — James son of Alphaeus is listed among the twelve apostles whom Jesus appointed and sent out with authority.
  • Mark 3:18 — Mark includes James the son of Alphaeus in his record of the twelve whom Jesus appointed to be with Him.
  • Luke 6:15 — Luke names James son of Alphaeus among the apostles chosen by Jesus after a night of prayer.
  • Acts 1:13 — After the ascension, James son of Alphaeus is among those gathered in the upper room, devoted to prayer.
  • Mark 15:40 — Mary the mother of James the younger and Joses watches the crucifixion from a distance, possibly connecting to this James.

Lessons from James son of Alphaeus

James the Less teaches us that faithful obscurity is not lesser discipleship. In a culture that prizes visibility, influence, and platform, James reminds us that God sees and values the quiet servant just as much as the prominent leader. He was chosen by Jesus, trained by Jesus, and sent by Jesus — the same commission given to Peter and John. His lack of recorded sermons or miracles does not diminish the significance of his calling. God does not measure faithfulness by fame.

From James we learn the discipline of simply showing up. He was present in the upper room. He was present at Pentecost. He was present through three years of itinerant ministry with Jesus. In a world of flashy ministry and viral moments, there is profound holiness in consistent presence — in being the one who always shows up for prayer meeting, who serves without seeking credit, who endures hardship without complaint. James challenges us to ask: Am I faithful in the unseen places? Am I content to serve where no one applauds?

Reflection Questions

  • Are you content serving God in obscurity, or do you find yourself craving recognition and visibility? What does faithful hiddenness look like in your life?
  • James was chosen by Jesus just as surely as Peter was. How does knowing that God values quiet faithfulness change how you view your own calling?
  • What does it mean to "just show up" in your spiritual life — in prayer, in church, in service — even when no one notices or thanks you?
  • Are there people in your community who serve faithfully in the background? How can you honor and encourage them this week?
