1And Moses went and spake these words unto all Israel.

2And he said unto them, I am an hundred and twenty years old this day; I can no more go out and come in: also the LORD hath said unto me, Thou shalt not go over this Jordan.

3The LORD thy God, he will go over before thee, and he will destroy these nations from before thee, and thou shalt possess them: and Joshua, he shall go over before thee, as the LORD hath said.

4And the LORD shall do unto them as he did to Sihon and to Og, kings of the Amorites, and unto the land of them, whom he destroyed.

5And the LORD shall give them up before your face, that ye may do unto them according unto all the commandments which I have commanded you.

6Be strong and of a good courage, fear not, nor be afraid of them: for the LORD thy God, he it is that doth go with thee; he will not fail thee, nor forsake thee.

7And Moses called unto Joshua, and said unto him in the sight of all Israel, Be strong and of a good courage: for thou must go with this people unto the land which the LORD hath sworn unto their fathers to give them; and thou shalt cause them to inherit it.

8And the LORD, he it is that doth go before thee; he will be with thee, he will not fail thee, neither forsake thee: fear not, neither be dismayed.

9And Moses wrote this law, and delivered it unto the priests the sons of Levi, which bare the ark of the covenant of the LORD, and unto all the elders of Israel.

10And Moses commanded them, saying, At the end of every seven years, in the solemnity of the year of release, in the feast of tabernacles,

11When all Israel is come to appear before the LORD thy God in the place which he shall choose, thou shalt read this law before all Israel in their hearing.

12Gather the people together, men, and women, and children, and thy stranger that is within thy gates, that they may hear, and that they may learn, and fear the LORD your God, and observe to do all the words of this law:

13And that their children, which have not known any thing, may hear, and learn to fear the LORD your God, as long as ye live in the land whither ye go over Jordan to possess it.

14And the LORD said unto Moses, Behold, thy days approach that thou must die: call Joshua, and present yourselves in the tabernacle of the congregation, that I may give him a charge. And Moses and Joshua went, and presented themselves in the tabernacle of the congregation.

15And the LORD appeared in the tabernacle in a pillar of a cloud: and the pillar of the cloud stood over the door of the tabernacle.

16And the LORD said unto Moses, Behold, thou shalt sleep with thy fathers; and this people will rise up, and go a whoring after the gods of the strangers of the land, whither they go to be among them, and will forsake me, and break my covenant which I have made with them.

17Then my anger shall be kindled against them in that day, and I will forsake them, and I will hide my face from them, and they shall be devoured, and many evils and troubles shall befall them; so that they will say in that day, Are not these evils come upon us, because our God is not among us?

18And I will surely hide my face in that day for all the evils which they shall have wrought, in that they are turned unto other gods.

19Now therefore write ye this song for you, and teach it the children of Israel: put it in their mouths, that this song may be a witness for me against the children of Israel.

20For when I shall have brought them into the land which I sware unto their fathers, that floweth with milk and honey; and they shall have eaten and filled themselves, and waxen fat; then will they turn unto other gods, and serve them, and provoke me, and break my covenant.

21And it shall come to pass, when many evils and troubles are befallen them, that this song shall testify against them as a witness; for it shall not be forgotten out of the mouths of their seed: for I know their imagination which they go about, even now, before I have brought them into the land which I sware.

22Moses therefore wrote this song the same day, and taught it the children of Israel.

23And he gave Joshua the son of Nun a charge, and said, Be strong and of a good courage: for thou shalt bring the children of Israel into the land which I sware unto them: and I will be with thee.

24And it came to pass, when Moses had made an end of writing the words of this law in a book, until they were finished,

25That Moses commanded the Levites, which bare the ark of the covenant of the LORD, saying,

26Take this book of the law, and put it in the side of the ark of the covenant of the LORD your God, that it may be there for a witness against thee.

27For I know thy rebellion, and thy stiff neck: behold, while I am yet alive with you this day, ye have been rebellious against the LORD; and how much more after my death?

28Gather unto me all the elders of your tribes, and your officers, that I may speak these words in their ears, and call heaven and earth to record against them.

29For I know that after my death ye will utterly corrupt yourselves, and turn aside from the way which I have commanded you; and evil will befall you in the latter days; because ye will do evil in the sight of the LORD, to provoke him to anger through the work of your hands.

30And Moses spake in the ears of all the congregation of Israel the words of this song, until they were ended.

Summary

Moses commissions Joshua, writes the law, and commands it be read every seven years; God foretells Israel's coming unfaithfulness.

Authorship & Background

Author: Moses. Deuteronomy is the fifth and final book of the Pentateuch, written at the end of the 40 years of wilderness wandering (approximately 1406 BC). Hebrew title: "Devarim" — "Words/Things." Greek title: "Deuteronomion" — "Second Law" (a repetition/expansion of the Law for the new generation). The book consists of Moses' farewell speeches to Israel on the plains of Moab, just before they enter the Promised Land. Moses will not enter with them — these are his final words to the nation he has led for 40 years.
Historical Context: Chapter 31 is the beginning of the end — Moses' final preparations before his death. The chapter records four critical actions: (1) Moses' public charge to Israel and Joshua — "Be strong and of a good courage" (vv.1-8), (2) the writing of the law and its entrustment to the priests with instructions for public reading every seven years (vv.9-13), (3) God's commissioning of Joshua in the tabernacle and His sobering prophecy of Israel's future apostasy (vv.14-22), and (4) the placement of the written law beside the ark as a witness against Israel, with Moses' final prediction of their corruption (vv.23-30). The chapter is pervaded by a tragic tension: Moses prepares for a future he knows will go wrong. God Himself tells Moses: "this people will rise up, and go a whoring after the gods of the strangers of the land" (v.16). Moses tells the people: "I know that after my death ye will utterly corrupt yourselves" (v.29). Yet the preparations are made anyway — the law is written, Joshua is commissioned, the Song is taught. God provides for His people even knowing they will rebel. The chapter also marks the transition of leadership from Moses to Joshua — the end of one era and the beginning of another.
Moses' Charge to Israel and Joshua (vv.1-8): "I am an hundred and twenty years old this day; I can no more go out and come in" (v.2). Moses acknowledges his age and God's decree: "Thou shalt not go over this Jordan" (v.2). But God will go: "The LORD thy God, he will go over before thee" (v.3). Joshua will lead: "Joshua, he shall go over before thee" (v.3). The charge to all Israel: "Be strong and of a good courage, fear not, nor be afraid of them: for the LORD thy God, he it is that doth go with thee; he will not fail thee, nor forsake thee" (v.6). Then to Joshua personally: "Be strong and of a good courage: for thou must go with this people unto the land" (v.7). The promise repeated: "The LORD, he it is that doth go before thee; he will be with thee, he will not fail thee, neither forsake thee: fear not, neither be dismayed" (v.8). Hebrews 13:5 quotes this promise: "I will never leave thee, nor forsake thee."
The Law Written and Entrusted (vv.9-13): "Moses wrote this law, and delivered it unto the priests the sons of Levi" (v.9). The law is to be read publicly "at the end of every seven years, in the solemnity of the year of release, in the feast of tabernacles" (v.10). The audience: "all Israel" (v.11) — "men, and women, and children, and thy stranger" (v.12). The purpose: "that they may hear, and that they may learn, and fear the LORD your God, and observe to do all the words of this law" (v.12). And for the next generation: "that their children, which have not known any thing, may hear, and learn to fear the LORD your God" (v.13). The law must be transmitted — each generation must hear it fresh.
God's Prophecy of Apostasy (vv.14-22): God summons Moses and Joshua to the tabernacle (v.14). The LORD appears in a pillar of cloud (v.15). The prophecy: "this people will rise up, and go a whoring after the gods of the strangers of the land... and will forsake me, and break my covenant" (v.16). God's response: "my anger shall be kindled against them... and I will forsake them, and I will hide my face from them" (v.17). Israel will then say: "Are not these evils come upon us, because our God is not among us?" (v.17). But God will still hide His face "for all the evils which they shall have wrought" (v.18). Therefore: "write ye this song for you, and teach it the children of Israel" (v.19) — the Song of Moses (chapter 32) will serve as a witness against them when they rebel.
The Law Beside the Ark (vv.23-30): Joshua is charged: "Be strong and of a good courage: for thou shalt bring the children of Israel into the land which I sware unto them: and I will be with thee" (v.23). Moses commands the Levites: "Take this book of the law, and put it in the side of the ark of the covenant of the LORD your God, that it may be there for a witness against thee" (v.26). Moses' sobering assessment: "For I know thy rebellion, and thy stiff neck: behold, while I am yet alive with you this day, ye have been rebellious against the LORD; and how much more after my death?" (v.27). His prophecy: "I know that after my death ye will utterly corrupt yourselves, and turn aside from the way which I have commanded you; and evil will befall you in the latter days" (v.29).

Map & Geography

  • Moses speaks from the plains of Moab. He will "not go over this Jordan" (v.2). Joshua will lead Israel across. The book of the law is placed beside the ark of the covenant.

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): enduringword.com Guzik notes the tragic irony: Moses prepares Israel for a future he knows will go wrong. God commissions Joshua knowing Israel will rebel. Yet the preparations are still made — God's faithfulness is not contingent on Israel's faithfulness. He also emphasizes Hebrews 13:5 as the New Testament application of verse 6.
  • Charles Spurgeon: "'He will not fail thee, nor forsake thee.' Here is the believer's anchor in every storm. Not 'He might not fail thee' but 'He WILL NOT.' The double negative is absolute: not fail, not forsake. Whatever else may change — leaders die, circumstances shift, enemies arise — this remains: God will not leave His people. Moses dies, but God does not."

Reflection

  • 1. God's presence is the foundation of courage (vv.6,8). "Be strong and of a good courage" is not a command to manufacture confidence from within. The reason for courage follows immediately: "for the LORD thy God, he it is that doth go with thee." Our strength is derivative — it comes from His presence. Without Him, courage is foolishness.
  • 2. Leaders change; God doesn't (vv.2-3). Moses dies, but God continues. Joshua takes over, but God is the same. Human leadership is temporary; divine faithfulness is permanent. We must not put our trust in leaders but in the God who outlasts them all.
  • 3. The law must be transmitted to every generation (vv.12-13). "That their children, which have not known any thing, may hear, and learn." Each generation must hear God's word fresh. Faith is not inherited automatically — it must be taught, heard, and embraced anew. Neglect of transmission produces apostasy.
  • 4. God provides even knowing we will fail (vv.16-22). God knows Israel will rebel. He tells Moses plainly. Yet He still commissions Joshua, still writes the law, still teaches the Song. God's faithfulness is not contingent on our faithfulness. He provides means of grace even for those He knows will abuse them.
  • 5. The law as witness against us (v.26). The written word placed beside the ark testifies against those who violate it. Scripture is not merely instruction — it is evidence. On the day of judgment, God's revealed word will testify that we knew what was right.
  • 6. Moses' tragic realism (v.29). "I know that after my death ye will utterly corrupt yourselves." Moses has no illusions about human nature. Forty years of leading this people has taught him what they are. Yet he still serves, still prepares, still speaks. Realism about human sin does not excuse us from faithful service.
