1And Balaam said to Balak, “Build for me here seven altars, and prepare for me here seven bulls and seven rams.”

2Balak did as Balaam had said. And Balak and Balaam offered on each altar a bull and a ram.

6And he returned to him, and behold, he and all the princes of Moab were standing beside his burnt offering.

8How can I curse whom God has not cursed? How can I denounce whom the LORD has not denounced?

9For from the top of the crags I see him, from the hills I behold him; behold, a people dwelling alone, and not counting itself among the nations!

10Who can count the dust of Jacob or number the fourth part of Israel? Let me die the death of the upright, and let my end be like his!”

12And he answered and said, “Must I not take care to speak what the LORD puts in my mouth?”

25And Balak said to Balaam, “Do not curse them at all, and do not bless them at all.”

26But Balaam answered Balak, “Did I not tell you, ‘All that the LORD says, that I must do’?”

27And Balak said to Balaam, “Come now, I will take you to another place. Perhaps it will please God that you may curse them for me from there.”

28So Balak took Balaam to the top of Peor, which overlooks the desert.

Summary

Balaam blesses Israel instead of cursing them — twice he pronounces God's blessing, unable to reverse what God has declared.

Key Verses

v.8
"How can I curse whom God has not cursed? How can I denounce whom the LORD has not denounced?" (ESV) — The impossibility of cursing what God has blessed. Human words cannot override divine decree.
v.9
"The people shall dwell alone, and shall not be reckoned among the nations." — Israel's uniqueness: set apart, distinct, not assimilated into the nations. This remains true of the Jewish people to this day.
v.10
"Let me die the death of the righteous, and let my last end be like his!" — Balaam wants the righteous man's reward without the righteous man's life. He desires the destination without the journey. Tragically, he dies in battle against Israel (31:8).
v.19
"God is not man, that he should lie, or a son of man, that he should change his mind. Has he said, and will he not do it?" (ESV) — One of the most important theological statements in Scripture. God's character: truthful, unchanging, faithful to His word.
v.21
"He has not beheld misfortune in Jacob, nor has he seen trouble in Israel." (ESV) — Not that Israel is sinless, but that God's covenant covers their sin. He looks at them through grace.
v.23
"For there is no enchantment against Jacob, no divination against Israel." (ESV) — No spiritual weapon can touch God's people. Curses, spells, divination — all powerless against those God has blessed.

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/numbers-23/ Guzik emphasizes verse 19 as one of the foundational statements about God's nature in all of Scripture. He notes the irony: this truth about God's faithfulness comes through the mouth of a man who IS a liar and who DOES change his mind for money. God's truth is not diminished by the imperfection of the messenger. He also highlights v.21 as a picture of justification: God does not "see" Israel's sin because He has chosen to cover it.
  • Charles Spurgeon: "'God is not a man, that he should lie.' Here is the rock on which faith stands. Men lie — God does not. Men change — God does not. Men promise and forget — God promises and performs. Has He said He will save you? He will. Has He said He will keep you? He will. Has He said nothing shall separate you from His love? Nothing shall. Rest here, trembling soul: God is not a man. His word is as certain as His nature. And His nature is eternal, unchangeable, and true."

Translations

  • v.3 — KJV: "he went to an high place" / ESV: "he went to a bare height" — Hebrew "vayyeelekh shefi." "Shefi" = a bare, windswept height. Balaam goes to an elevated, exposed place to meet God — a common practice for diviners seeking revelation.
  • v.7 — KJV: "he took up his parable" / ESV: "Balaam took up his discourse" — Hebrew "vayyissa m'shalo." "Mashal" = a proverb, oracle, parable, or prophetic discourse. KJV's "parable" and ESV's "discourse" both capture aspects of this poetic, prophetic speech.
  • v.10 — KJV: "the death of the righteous" / ESV: "the death of the upright" — Hebrew "mot y'sharim." "Yesharim" = the upright, the straight ones. Balaam recognizes Israel's blessed state and wishes for their end — but will not live their life. He wants heaven without holiness.
  • v.19 — KJV: "that he should repent" / ESV: "that he should change his mind" — Hebrew "v'yitnecham." "Nacham" = to be sorry, to relent, to change one's mind. KJV's "repent" can confuse modern readers (God does not repent of sin); ESV's "change his mind" is clearer. The point: God does not vacillate or reverse His declared purposes.
  • v.21 — KJV: "He hath not beheld iniquity in Jacob, neither hath he seen perverseness in Israel" / ESV: "He has not beheld misfortune in Jacob, nor has he seen trouble in Israel" — Hebrew "lo hibit aven b'Ya'aqov v'lo ra'ah amal b'Yisra'el." This verse is debated: "aven" can mean "iniquity" or "misfortune/trouble"; "amal" can mean "perverseness" or "trouble/toil." KJV reads it as God not imputing sin; ESV reads it as God not allowing trouble/misfortune. Both are theologically valid; the KJV reading connects more directly to justification theology.
  • v.22 — KJV: "the strength of an unicorn" / ESV: "the horns of the wild ox" — Hebrew "k'to'afot r'em." "Re'em" is an extinct wild ox (aurochs), not a mythical unicorn. KJV followed the Septuagint's "monokeros" (one-horned); ESV correctly identifies the animal. The point: God's strength for Israel is like the unstoppable power of a wild ox.
  • v.23 — KJV: "there is no enchantment against Jacob, neither is there any divination against Israel" / ESV: "there is no enchantment against Jacob, no divination against Israel" — Hebrew "lo nachash b'Ya'aqov v'lo qesem b'Yisra'el." "Nachash" = enchantment, sorcery, snake-charming. "Qesem" = divination. No form of occult power can touch God's people. This is spoken by a diviner — who admits his own craft is powerless against Israel.

Reflection

  • 1. "God is not a man, that he should lie" (v.19). When you doubt God's promises, remember: He is not like humans who forget, exaggerate, or break their word. What He has said, He will do. What He has spoken, He will fulfill. Your doubts do not change His faithfulness.
  • 2. "He hath not beheld iniquity in Jacob" (v.21). This is justification: God chooses not to see Israel's sin because of His covenant. In Christ, God does not "behold iniquity" in you — not because you are sinless, but because Christ's righteousness covers you. "There is therefore now no condemnation" (Romans 8:1).
  • 3. "There is no enchantment against Jacob" (v.23). No spiritual weapon formed against God's people can succeed. No curse, no spell, no divination, no demonic strategy can override God's blessing. If you belong to Christ, you are untouchable by the enemy's curses.
  • 4. "Let me die the death of the righteous" (v.10). Balaam wants the righteous man's end without the righteous man's life. Many desire heaven who will not walk the narrow road. You cannot have the destination without the journey. Balaam dies in battle against Israel (31:8) — the opposite of what he wished.
  • 5. Balak's strategy: change the location (vv.13, 27). When the first oracle blesses, Balak moves to a new spot — as if geography determines God's word. We do the same: changing churches, changing circumstances, hoping God will say something different. But God's word does not change with your vantage point.
  • 6. God speaks through imperfect instruments (v.5). Balaam is corrupt — yet God puts true words in his mouth. This is both encouraging (God's truth is not limited by the messenger's character) and sobering (being used by God does not validate your life). Judge truth by its content, not its source.
