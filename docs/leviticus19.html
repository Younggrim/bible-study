Leviticus — Chapter 19

1And the LORD spoke to Moses, saying,

2“Speak to all the congregation of the people of Israel and say to them, You shall be holy, for I the LORD your God am holy.

3Every one of you shall revere his mother and his father, and you shall keep my Sabbaths: I am the LORD your God.

4Do not turn to idols or make for yourselves any gods of cast metal: I am the LORD your God.

5“When you offer a sacrifice of peace offerings to the LORD, you shall offer it so that you may be accepted.

6It shall be eaten the same day you offer it or on the day after, and anything left over until the third day shall be burned up with fire.

7If it is eaten at all on the third day, it is tainted; it will not be accepted,

8and everyone who eats it shall bear his iniquity, because he has profaned what is holy to the LORD, and that person shall be cut off from his people.

9“When you reap the harvest of your land, you shall not reap your field right up to its edge, neither shall you gather the gleanings after your harvest.

10And you shall not strip your vineyard bare, neither shall you gather the fallen grapes of your vineyard. You shall leave them for the poor and for the sojourner: I am the LORD your God.

11“You shall not steal; you shall not deal falsely; you shall not lie to one another.

12You shall not swear by my name falsely, and so profane the name of your God: I am the LORD.

13“You shall not oppress your neighbor or rob him. The wages of a hired worker shall not remain with you all night until the morning.

14You shall not curse the deaf or put a stumbling block before the blind, but you shall fear your God: I am the LORD.

15“You shall do no injustice in court. You shall not be partial to the poor or defer to the great, but in righteousness shall you judge your neighbor.

16You shall not go around as a slanderer among your people, and you shall not stand up against the life of your neighbor: I am the LORD.

17“You shall not hate your brother in your heart, but you shall reason frankly with your neighbor, lest you incur sin because of him.

18You shall not take vengeance or bear a grudge against the sons of your own people, but you shall love your neighbor as yourself: I am the LORD.

19“You shall keep my statutes. You shall not let your cattle breed with a different kind. You shall not sow your field with two kinds of seed, nor shall you wear a garment of cloth made of two kinds of material.

20“If a man lies sexually with a woman who is a slave, assigned to another man and not yet ransomed or given her freedom, a distinction shall be made. They shall not be put to death, because she was not free;

21but he shall bring his compensation to the LORD, to the entrance of the tent of meeting, a ram for a guilt offering.

22And the priest shall make atonement for him with the ram of the guilt offering before the LORD for his sin that he has committed, and he shall be forgiven for the sin that he has committed.

23“When you come into the land and plant any kind of tree for food, then you shall regard its fruit as forbidden. Three years it shall be forbidden to you; it must not be eaten.

24And in the fourth year all its fruit shall be holy, an offering of praise to the LORD.

25But in the fifth year you may eat of its fruit, to increase its yield for you: I am the LORD your God.

26“You shall not eat any flesh with the blood in it. You shall not interpret omens or tell fortunes.

27You shall not round off the hair on your temples or mar the edges of your beard.

28You shall not make any cuts on your body for the dead or tattoo yourselves: I am the LORD.

29“Do not profane your daughter by making her a prostitute, lest the land fall into prostitution and the land become full of depravity.

30You shall keep my Sabbaths and reverence my sanctuary: I am the LORD.

31“Do not turn to mediums or necromancers; do not seek them out, and so make yourselves unclean by them: I am the LORD your God.

32“You shall stand up before the gray head and honor the face of an old man, and you shall fear your God: I am the LORD.

33“When a stranger sojourns with you in your land, you shall not do him wrong.

34You shall treat the stranger who sojourns with you as the native among you, and you shall love him as yourself, for you were strangers in the land of Egypt: I am the LORD your God.

35“You shall do no wrong in judgment, in measures of length or weight or quantity.

36You shall have just balances, just weights, a just ephah, and a just hin: I am the LORD your God, who brought you out of the land of Egypt.

37And you shall observe all my statutes and all my rules, and do them: I am the LORD.”

Summary

God calls Israel to holiness in everyday life — love your neighbor as yourself, care for the poor, deal honestly, and revere God in all things.

Authorship & Background

Author: Moses. Leviticus is the third book of the Pentateuch, written during the wilderness period (approximately 1445-1405 BC). Hebrew title: "Vayikra" — "And He called." God speaks from the newly completed Tabernacle, giving Israel instructions for worship, sacrifice, and holy living.
Historical Context: Chapter 19 is the heart of the Holiness Code and one of the most remarkable chapters in the entire Bible. It opens with the foundational command: "Ye shall be holy: for I the LORD your God am holy" (v.2). What follows is a sweeping collection of laws that touch every area of life — family, worship, economics, agriculture, justice, speech, relationships, and love. The chapter is addressed to "all the congregation" (v.2) — not just priests, not just leaders, but every Israelite. Holiness is not reserved for a spiritual elite; it is the calling of the entire community. The chapter contains the second greatest commandment: "thou shalt love thy neighbour as thyself" (v.18) — quoted by Jesus as the summary of the law's second table (Matthew 22:39; Mark 12:31). Many of the Ten Commandments are echoed here (honor parents, keep Sabbath, no idols, no stealing, no lying, no false witness), but they are expanded and applied to practical daily life. The refrain "I am the LORD" (or "I am the LORD your God") appears sixteen times — grounding every command in God's identity. Holiness is not abstract — it is concrete, practical, and all-encompassing.
The Foundation: Be Holy (vv.1-4): God speaks to Moses for "all the congregation" (v.1-2). "Ye shall be holy: for I the LORD your God am holy" (v.2) — the thesis statement of the entire Holiness Code. Holiness is not optional; it is the nature of God and therefore the calling of His people. Immediately practical: "Ye shall fear every man his mother, and his father" (v.3) — honor parents (note: mother listed first, unusual). "Keep my sabbaths" (v.3). "Turn ye not unto idols, nor make to yourselves molten gods" (v.4). Family, worship, and loyalty to God — the foundations.
Sacrifice and Worship (vv.5-8): Peace offerings must be eaten within two days; anything remaining on the third day must be burned (vv.5-6). Eating it on the third day is "abominable" — the offering is profaned and the person bears iniquity and is cut off (vv.7-8). Worship must follow God's instructions precisely.
Economic Justice and Compassion (vv.9-10): Gleaning laws — do not harvest to the very edges of your field; do not pick up what falls (v.9). Do not strip your vineyard bare or gather fallen grapes (v.10). Leave them "for the poor and stranger" (v.10). This is institutionalized compassion — built into the economic system. The story of Ruth depends on this law (Ruth 2:2-3).
The Ethical Commands (vv.11-18): A rapid-fire series of moral commands: No stealing, no lying, no false dealing (v.11). No false swearing by God's name (v.12). No oppression or robbery; pay wages on time (v.13). No cursing the deaf or tripping the blind (v.14) — protecting the vulnerable who cannot defend themselves. No injustice in court — neither favoring the poor nor deferring to the rich (v.15). No slander; no standing against your neighbor's life (v.16). No hatred in the heart; rebuke your neighbor openly rather than harboring sin (v.17). No vengeance or grudge-bearing (v.18). THE CLIMAX: "thou shalt love thy neighbour as thyself: I am the LORD" (v.18). Love is the fulfillment of all these commands.
Laws of Separation (v.19): "Ye shall keep my statutes" — then three prohibitions: no crossbreeding cattle, no sowing mixed seed, no garments of mixed fabric (linen and wool). These laws teach the principle of distinction — God separates, and His people maintain distinctions. They are object lessons in holiness (separation/distinction).
Sexual Sin and Its Remedy (vv.20-22): A man who lies with a betrothed slave woman — "she shall be scourged" (KJV) / "a distinction shall be made" (ESV) (v.20). They are not put to death because she was not free (v.20). He brings a guilt/trespass offering — a ram (v.21). The priest makes atonement and he is forgiven (v.22). Even in cases of sexual sin, there is a path to forgiveness through sacrifice.
Agricultural Laws (vv.23-25): Fruit trees in the land — the fruit is "uncircumcised" (forbidden) for three years (v.23). In the fourth year, all fruit is holy to the LORD (v.24). In the fifth year, they may eat it (v.25). Patience, consecration, then enjoyment. First fruits belong to God.
Miscellaneous Holiness Laws (vv.26-31): No eating with blood (v.26). No divination or fortune-telling (v.26). No rounding the corners of the head or marring the beard (v.27) — likely prohibiting pagan mourning customs. No cutting the body for the dead or tattoo marks (v.28). Do not prostitute your daughter (v.29). Keep Sabbaths and reverence the sanctuary (v.30). Do not consult mediums or necromancers (v.31).
Honor, Love, and Justice (vv.32-37): Rise before the elderly and honor them (v.32). Do not wrong the stranger — love him as yourself, "for ye were strangers in the land of Egypt" (vv.33-34). No dishonesty in measurements — just balances, just weights, just measures (vv.35-36). "I am the LORD your God, which brought you out of the land of Egypt" (v.36) — the exodus is the motivation for justice. Final summary: "observe all my statutes, and all my judgments, and do them: I am the LORD" (v.37).

Map & Geography

  • Egypt (v.34): Land to the southwest; place of Israel's bondage and exodus.

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): enduringword.com Guzik calls this chapter "a remarkable collection of laws" that shows holiness touching every area of life. He emphasizes that "love your neighbor as yourself" is not a New Testament invention but an Old Testament command — Jesus was quoting Moses. He notes the balance of justice in v.15: neither favoring the poor nor the rich.
  • Charles Spurgeon: Sermon: "Love Thy Neighbour" "Love thy neighbour as thyself — this is not a suggestion, it is a COMMAND. And it is not a new command — it is as old as Leviticus. Our Lord did not invent it; He quoted it. Moses wrote it by the Spirit of God a thousand years before Christ walked the earth. And what does it mean? It means: treat your neighbour's interests as you treat your own. His reputation — guard it as you guard yours. His property — respect it as you respect yours. His feelings — consider them as you consider yours. His needs — attend to them as you attend to yours. This is not natural to fallen man. It requires a new heart, a new nature, the very Spirit of God within. But it is the standard — and Christ both commands it and enables it."

The Bible Project — Leviticus Overview

Reflection

  • 1. "Be ye holy: for I the LORD your God am holy" (v.2). Holiness is not optional for believers — it is the family resemblance. God's children bear His character. And holiness is not abstract — this chapter shows it is intensely practical: how you treat your parents, your employees, the disabled, the poor, the foreigner, your neighbor. Where is your holiness merely theoretical rather than practical?
  • 2. "Leave them for the poor and stranger" (v.10). God builds compassion into the economic system. Generosity is not merely voluntary charity — it is structural justice. Are you using all your resources for yourself, or leaving "edges" for those in need?
  • 3. "Thou shalt not curse the deaf, nor put a stumblingblock before the blind" (v.14). Do not exploit those who cannot defend themselves or detect your exploitation. God sees what the deaf cannot hear and what the blind cannot see. "Fear thy God" — He is the witness when no one else is watching.
  • 4. "Love thy neighbour as thyself" (v.18). The standard is not "love your neighbor as much as you can manage" but "as yourself." With the same urgency, the same concern, the same investment you naturally give to your own interests. This is impossible without the Spirit of God. But it is the standard — and it is the fulfillment of the entire law (Romans 13:9; Galatians 5:14).
  • 5. "The stranger that dwelleth with you shall be unto you as one born among you" (v.34). Love is not limited to your own people, your own tribe, your own kind. The foreigner is to be loved "as yourself" — the same standard as the neighbor. "For ye were strangers in the land of Egypt" — remember where you came from. You were once the outsider.
