Zechariah — Chapter 7

1In the fourth year of King Darius, the word of the LORD came to Zechariah on the fourth day of the ninth month, which is Chislev.

2Now the people of Bethel had sent Sharezer and Regem-melech and their men to entreat the favor of the LORD,

3saying to the priests of the house of the LORD of hosts and the prophets, “Should I weep and abstain in the fifth month, as I have done for so many years?”

4Then the word of the LORD of hosts came to me:

5“Say to all the people of the land and the priests, ‘When you fasted and mourned in the fifth month and in the seventh, for these seventy years, was it for me that you fasted?

6And when you eat and when you drink, do you not eat for yourselves and drink for yourselves?

7Were not these the words that the LORD proclaimed by the former prophets, when Jerusalem was inhabited and prosperous, with her cities around her, and the South and the lowland were inhabited?’”

8And the word of the LORD came to Zechariah, saying,

9“Thus says the LORD of hosts, Render true judgments, show kindness and mercy to one another,

10do not oppress the widow, the fatherless, the sojourner, or the poor, and let none of you devise evil against another in your heart.”

11But they refused to pay attention and turned a stubborn shoulder and stopped their ears that they might not hear.

12They made their hearts diamond-hard lest they should hear the law and the words that the LORD of hosts had sent by his Spirit through the former prophets. Therefore great anger came from the LORD of hosts.

13“As I called, and they would not hear, so they called, and I would not hear,” says the LORD of hosts,

14“and I scattered them with a whirlwind among all the nations that they had not known. Thus the land they left was desolate, so that no one went to and fro, and the pleasant land was made desolate.”

Summary

A delegation asks about continuing fast days, and God responds that their fasting was self-serving — what He truly requires is justice, mercy, and compassion, which their fathers refused and reaped exile.

Key Verses

v.5
"When ye fasted and mourned in the fifth and seventh month, even those seventy years, did ye at all fast unto me, even to me?" (KJV) — God's piercing question exposes the heart. Religious activity can be entirely self- referential — done for ourselves, our reputation, our comfort — while bypassing God entirely. The repetition "unto ME, even to ME" emphasizes that God was never the true audience of their worship.
v.9
"Execute true judgment, and shew mercy and compassions every man to his brother" (KJV) / "Render true judgments, show kindness and mercy to one another" (ESV) — What God ACTUALLY wants. Not fasting rituals but relational righteousness. True religion is measured by how you treat people — especially the vulnerable.
v.10
"oppress not the widow, nor the fatherless, the stranger, nor the poor" (KJV) — The four categories of the vulnerable that recur throughout the prophets. God measures a society by its treatment of those who cannot defend themselves.
v.12
"they made their hearts as an adamant stone, lest they should hear" (KJV) / "They made their hearts diamond-hard lest they should hear" (ESV) — The ultimate hardness: deliberate, chosen resistance. They didn't fail to hear — they refused to hear. The hardening was voluntary. This is the most dangerous spiritual state.
v.13
"as he cried, and they would not hear; so they cried, and I would not hear" (KJV) — A terrifying reciprocity. Those who refuse to listen to God will find that God refuses to listen to them. The day of grace has limits.

Cross-References

  • v.5 — Isaiah 58:3-7 — God's exposé of empty fasting: "Is not this the fast that I have chosen? to loose the bands of wickedness." v.5 — Matthew 6:16-18 — Jesus' teaching on fasting for God's approval, not human attention. v.9 — Micah 6:8 — "What doth the LORD require of thee, but to do justly, and to love mercy, and to walk humbly?" v.10 — James 1:27 — "Pure religion... is this, To visit the fatherless and widows in their affliction." v.12 — Hebrews 3:7-8 — "Harden not your hearts, as in the provocation." v.13 — Proverbs 1:24-28 — "Then shall they call upon me, but I will not answer" — the consequence of rejecting wisdom. v.14 — Deuteronomy 28:64 — The scattering among nations as covenant consequence for disobedience.

Translations

  • v.3 — "separating myself" (KJV) / "abstain" (ESV) — Hebrew "hinnazar" = to consecrate oneself by abstaining (same root as "Nazirite"). The fasting involved specific acts of self- denial — but the motivation was wrong.
  • v.11 — "pulled away the shoulder" (KJV) / "turned a stubborn shoulder" (ESV) — Hebrew "nathan kateph sorerot" = literally "gave a turning/rebellious shoulder." The image of an ox refusing the yoke — turning its shoulder away from the load. Willful resistance to God's direction.
  • v.12 — "adamant stone" (KJV) / "diamond-hard" (ESV) — Hebrew "shamir." Used elsewhere for the hardest cutting stone. The heart became impenetrable — no word could break through. A self-inflicted condition with devastating consequences.
  • v.14 — "pleasant land" — Hebrew "eretz chemdah" = the land of delight/desire. The Promised Land — so beautiful, so blessed — made desolate by disobedience. The land reflects the spiritual state of its people.

Reflection

  • 1. "Did ye fast unto ME?" (v.5). Examine your spiritual disciplines. Is your prayer life about connecting with GOD or about feeling religious? Is your church attendance for HIM or for your social identity? The crucial question is not whether you do spiritual things but WHO they are actually for.
  • 2. God wants justice and mercy, not just worship services (vv.9-10). You cannot sing on Sunday and oppress on Monday. True worship produces just behavior. If your religion doesn't change how you treat the vulnerable, it isn't real religion (James 1:27).
  • 3. A hard heart is chosen, not inherited (v.12). They MADE their hearts diamond-hard. Hardness is a decision — repeated refusals to listen calcify into inability to hear. Every time you dismiss God's conviction, your heart gets a little harder. Respond NOW while your heart is still soft.
  • 4. God's patience has limits (v.13). He called; they refused. Then THEY called; HE refused. This is sobering. God is patient — but persistent rejection exhausts even divine patience. Don't presume on grace. Respond to God while He is still calling.
  • 5. Disobedience desolates what was once beautiful (v.14). The "pleasant land" became wasteland. Sin ALWAYS desolates. What was once flourishing — your marriage, your joy, your calling — can become wasteland through persistent disobedience. Choose repentance before desolation.
