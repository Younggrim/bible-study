1 Samuel — Chapter 22

1David departed from there and escaped to the cave of Adullam. And when his brothers and all his father’s house heard it, they went down there to him.

2And everyone who was in distress, and everyone who was in debt, and everyone who was bitter in soul, gathered to him. And he became commander over them. And there were with him about four hundred men.

3And David went from there to Mizpeh of Moab. And he said to the king of Moab, “Please let my father and my mother stay with you, till I know what God will do for me.”

4And he left them with the king of Moab, and they stayed with him all the time that David was in the stronghold.

5Then the prophet Gad said to David, “Do not remain in the stronghold; depart, and go into the land of Judah.” So David departed and went into the forest of Hereth.

6Now Saul heard that David was discovered, and the men who were with him. Saul was sitting at Gibeah under the tamarisk tree on the height with his spear in his hand, and all his servants were standing about him.

7And Saul said to his servants who stood about him, “Hear now, people of Benjamin; will the son of Jesse give every one of you fields and vineyards, will he make you all commanders of thousands and commanders of hundreds,

8that all of you have conspired against me? No one discloses to me when my son makes a covenant with the son of Jesse. None of you is sorry for me or discloses to me that my son has stirred up my servant against me, to lie in wait, as at this day.”

9Then answered Doeg the Edomite, who stood by the servants of Saul, “I saw the son of Jesse coming to Nob, to Ahimelech the son of Ahitub,

10and he inquired of the LORD for him and gave him provisions and gave him the sword of Goliath the Philistine.”

11Then the king sent to summon Ahimelech the priest, the son of Ahitub, and all his father’s house, the priests who were at Nob, and all of them came to the king.

12And Saul said, “Hear now, son of Ahitub.” And he answered, “Here I am, my lord.”

13And Saul said to him, “Why have you conspired against me, you and the son of Jesse, in that you have given him bread and a sword and have inquired of God for him, so that he has risen against me, to lie in wait, as at this day?”

14Then Ahimelech answered the king, “And who among all your servants is so faithful as David, who is the king’s son-in-law, and captain over your bodyguard, and honored in your house?

15Is today the first time that I have inquired of God for him? No! Let not the king impute anything to his servant or to all the house of my father, for your servant has known nothing of all this, much or little.”

16And the king said, “You shall surely die, Ahimelech, you and all your father’s house.”

17And the king said to the guard who stood about him, “Turn and kill the priests of the LORD, because their hand also is with David, and they knew that he fled and did not disclose it to me.” But the servants of the king would not put out their hand to strike the priests of the LORD.

18Then the king said to Doeg, “You turn and strike the priests.” And Doeg the Edomite turned and struck down the priests, and he killed on that day eighty-five persons who wore the linen ephod.

19And Nob, the city of the priests, he put to the sword; both man and woman, child and infant, ox, donkey and sheep, he put to the sword.

20But one of the sons of Ahimelech the son of Ahitub, named Abiathar, escaped and fled after David.

21And Abiathar told David that Saul had killed the priests of the LORD.

22And David said to Abiathar, “I knew on that day, when Doeg the Edomite was there, that he would surely tell Saul. I have occasioned the death of all the persons of your father’s house.

23Stay with me; do not be afraid, for he who seeks my life seeks your life. With me you shall be in safekeeping.”

Summary

David gathers a band of 400 distressed and discontented men at the cave of Adullam. Saul massacres eighty-five priests at Nob for helping David; only Abiathar escapes to join David's company.

Key Verses

  • v.2 — "And every one that was in distress, and every one that was in debt, and every one that was discontented, gathered themselves unto him; and he became a captain over them." — David's first followers are not the elite but the rejected. This is a picture of Christ's kingdom: He gathers the broken, the burdened, and the bitter, and transforms them into an army. David's 400 misfits will become his "mighty men" (2 Samuel 23:8-39).
  • v.17 — "But the servants of the king would not put forth their hand to fall upon the priests of the LORD." — Even Saul's own soldiers have more conscience than their king. There are limits that even loyal servants will not cross. When a leader commands what God forbids, disobedience is faithfulness.
  • v.18 — "And Doeg the Edomite turned, and he fell upon the priests, and slew on that day fourscore and five persons that did wear a linen ephod." — 85 priests murdered. An Edomite does what no Israelite would do. This is Saul's darkest act — the slaughter of God's servants by a pagan mercenary at the king's command.
  • v.22 — "I have caused the death of all the persons of your father's house." — David accepts responsibility. He does not blame Saul alone or excuse his own deception. True repentance acknowledges the full chain of consequences. David's lie at Nob (21:2) led to this massacre — a sobering reminder that our sins have victims beyond ourselves.

Authorship & Background

  • Author: Unknown, traditionally attributed to Samuel (for the early chapters), Nathan, and Gad (1 Chronicles 29:29). Originally one book with 2 Samuel in the Hebrew Bible. Written approximately 1050-950 BC. The book covers the transition from the judges to the monarchy — from the birth of Samuel through the death of Saul (approximately 1105-1010 BC). Key themes: God's sovereignty in raising and removing leaders, the danger of rejecting God's rule, the contrast between Saul (man's choice) and David (God's choice), and the principle that 'man looketh on the outward appearance, but the LORD looketh on the heart' (16:7).
  • Historical Context: Chapter 22 contains two contrasting scenes: the formation of David's band at Adullam (vv.1-5) and Saul's massacre of the priests of Nob (vv.6-23). At Adullam, David becomes captain over 400 men who are "in distress, in debt, and discontented" (v.2) — the rejected and marginalized of Israel gather to the rejected king. This is a powerful type of Christ, who gathers to Himself the broken, the burdened, and the outcast (Matthew 11:28). Meanwhile, Saul descends into paranoid tyranny. Sitting under a tree with his spear (the symbol of his violent kingship), he accuses his own servants of conspiracy and self-pity: "there is none of you that is sorry for me" (v.8). When Doeg reports David's visit to Nob, Saul summons the priests and condemns them without fair hearing. His own soldiers refuse to execute the priests — even Saul's guard recognizes the horror of killing God's servants. But Doeg the Edomite (a foreigner with no covenant loyalty to Israel's God) carries out the slaughter: 85 priests killed, and the entire city of Nob destroyed — men, women, children, infants, and livestock (v.19). The irony is devastating: Saul was commanded to destroy the Amalekites completely and refused (ch.15); now he destroys an Israelite priestly city completely. What he would not do to God's enemies, he does to God's servants. Only Abiathar escapes, fleeing to David — bringing the priestly ephod and the ability to inquire of God. David accepts responsibility: "I have occasioned the death of all the persons of thy father's house" (v.22).
  • David at Adullam (vv.1-5): David escapes to the cave of Adullam — a large cave in the Judean foothills, about 10 miles southeast of Gath. His family joins him (v.1) — they are now targets of Saul's rage. Then others gather: "every one that was in distress, and every one that was in debt, and every one that was discontented" (v.2). Four hundred men — society's rejects become David's army. He becomes their "captain" (sar = commander, leader). David sends his parents to Moab for safety (v.3) — his great-grandmother Ruth was a Moabitess (Ruth 4:17), giving him a family connection there. The prophet Gad instructs David to return to Judah (v.5) — God directs David's movements even in exile.
  • Saul's Paranoia (vv.6-10): Saul sits at Gibeah under a tree (KJV) / tamarisk tree (ESV), spear in hand, surrounded by his Benjamite servants (v.6). He appeals to tribal loyalty: "will the son of Jesse give every one of you fields and vineyards?" (v.7). His argument is purely political — David is from Judah, not Benjamin; he will not reward Benjamites. Saul accuses everyone of conspiracy (v.8) and self-pity dominates: "none of you is sorry for me." Doeg seizes the opportunity and reports David's visit to Nob (vv.9-10), adding that Ahimelech "enquired of the LORD for him" — which may or may not be accurate (Ahimelech denies initiating it, v.15).
  • The Massacre of Nob (vv.11-19): Saul summons all the priests of Nob (v.11). He accuses Ahimelech of conspiracy (v.13). Ahimelech's defense is reasonable and truthful: David is the king's faithful servant, his son-in-law, his bodyguard captain — why would helping him be treason? (vv.14-15). Saul ignores the defense: "Thou shalt surely die, Ahimelech, thou, and all thy father's house" (v.16). He commands his guard to kill the priests — they refuse (v.17). This is significant: even Saul's own soldiers recognize that killing God's priests is unconscionable. Doeg alone obeys: 85 priests slaughtered, then the entire city of Nob — total destruction (vv.18-19). This is the "cherem" (ban of total destruction) that Saul was supposed to execute against Amalek but refused. Now he executes it against God's own priests.
  • Abiathar Escapes (vv.20-23): One son of Ahimelech survives — Abiathar. He flees to David and reports the massacre (vv.20-21). David's response shows genuine remorse: "I knew it that day, when Doeg the Edomite was there, that he would surely tell Saul: I have occasioned the death of all the persons of thy father's house" (v.22). David accepts responsibility for the consequences of his deception at Nob. He offers Abiathar protection: "with me thou shalt be in safeguard" (v.23). Abiathar will serve as David's priest throughout his fugitive years and into his reign.

Map & Geography

  • Cave of Adullam: In the western Judean foothills (Shephelah) — David's hideout where 400 outcasts gather to him
  • Nob: Where Saul massacres 85 priests — Doeg the Edomite carries out the slaughter
  • Mizpeh of Moab: Where David sends his parents for safety — across the Dead Sea in Moab (his great-grandmother Ruth's homeland)

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): enduringword.com Guzik emphasizes the typological significance of David at Adullam: as David gathered the distressed, indebted, and discontented, so Christ gathers sinners to Himself and transforms them. He also notes the devastating irony of Saul executing the "ban" against priests that he refused to execute against Amalek.
  • Charles Spurgeon: "To the cave of Adullam came the distressed, the debtors, and the discontented — and David became captain over them. What a picture of our Lord Jesus! To Him come the spiritually bankrupt, the soul- distressed, the heart-discontented with this world's emptiness. And He receives them all, makes them His own, and transforms them into soldiers of the cross. Are you in distress? Go to David's greater Son. Are you in debt to God's law? Go to Him who paid it all. Are you discontented with sin's wages? Go to Him who gives eternal life."

Translations

  • v.2 → KJV: "every one that was discontented" → ESV: "everyone who was bitter in soul" — Hebrew "mar nephesh" = bitter of soul. KJV "discontented" captures the social dimension; ESV "bitter in soul" captures the emotional/spiritual dimension. These men were embittered by life under Saul's unjust rule.
  • v.6 → KJV: "Saul abode in Gibeah under a tree in Ramah" → ESV: "Saul was sitting at Gibeah under the tamarisk tree on the height" — Hebrew "tachat ha-eshel ba-ramah." "Eshel" = tamarisk tree (ESV) or simply "tree" (KJV). "Ba-ramah" can mean "in Ramah" (a place name) or "on the height" (a common noun). ESV reads it as a description of location (on the hill); KJV reads it as a place name. The ESV reading is more likely given the context of Gibeah.
  • v.8 → KJV: "my son hath made a league with the son of Jesse" → ESV: "my son makes a covenant with the son of Jesse" — Hebrew "karath berith" = cut a covenant. Both translations are accurate. Saul knows about Jonathan's covenant with David and sees it as betrayal.
  • v.18 → KJV: "fourscore and five persons that did wear a linen ephod" → ESV: "eighty-five persons who wore the linen ephod" — Both agree: 85 priests killed. The "linen ephod" (ephod bad) identifies them as serving priests — not the high priest's ornate ephod but the simple linen garment of priestly service.
  • v.19 → KJV: "children and sucklings" → ESV: "child and infant" — Hebrew "olel ve-yoneq" = child and nursing infant. The total destruction of Nob includes the most innocent — a horror that echoes the destruction Saul was supposed to bring on Amalek (15:3) but now brings on God's own people.

The Bible Project — 1 Samuel Overview

Reflection

  • 1. God gathers the broken and transforms them (v.2). David's 400 rejects became Israel's mighty men. Christ's church is built from the distressed, the indebted, and the discontented — people the world rejects. If you feel broken, you are exactly who God is looking for. He does not need the qualified; He qualifies the called.
  • 2. Our sins have victims beyond ourselves (v.22). David's lie at Nob cost 85 priests their lives. Sin is never private — it always has collateral damage. When we deceive, cut corners, or act in fear rather than faith, others may pay the price. Consider the ripple effects of your choices.
  • 3. There are orders that must be disobeyed (v.17). Saul's soldiers refused to kill the priests. When human authority commands what God forbids, faithful disobedience is the only righteous option. Not all obedience is virtue — obedience to evil is complicity.
  • 4. Self-pity blinds us to our own wickedness (v.8). Saul sees himself as the victim: "none of you is sorry for me." He is about to murder 85 innocent priests, yet he feels sorry for himself. Self- pity is one of sin's most dangerous disguises — it makes the perpetrator feel like the victim and justifies any retaliation.
  • 5. Accept responsibility for consequences (v.22). David does not deflect blame. He says plainly: "I have occasioned the death." This is the mark of a man after God's heart — not sinlessness but honest accountability. When your actions contribute to harm, own it. Confession is the beginning of restoration.
