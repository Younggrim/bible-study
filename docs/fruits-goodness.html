Goodness — Agathosune (ἀγαθωσύνη)

Understanding Goodness

The Greek word agathosune (ἀγαθωσύνη) is a uniquely biblical term — it appears almost exclusively in the New Testament and the Septuagint (Greek Old Testament), with very little usage in secular Greek literature. This suggests it describes a quality so distinctly God-like that the pagan world had no word for it. Agathosune denotes an active, energetic goodness — not just moral purity or innocence, but a robust virtue that vigorously pursues what is right and beneficial for others.

While kindness (chrestotes) is gentle and gracious, goodness (agathosune) can be firm and confrontational when necessary. Jesus displayed agathosune when He overturned the money changers' tables in the temple — it was an act of moral goodness that opposed evil and defended the oppressed. Goodness, in the biblical sense, is not passive. It actively opposes wrong, champions justice, and does what is right regardless of personal cost or popular opinion.

God is the source and standard of all goodness. Jesus Himself said, "No one is good except God alone" (Mark 10:18). Yet through the indwelling Spirit, God's own goodness begins to flow through believers. Psalm 23:6 promises that "surely goodness and mercy shall follow me all the days of my life." When the Spirit produces agathosune in us, we become agents of God's goodness in the world — people who not only refrain from evil but actively generate good wherever they go.

Key Scriptures

  • Romans 15:14 — Paul is confident that believers are full of goodness, filled with knowledge, and able to instruct one another.
  • Galatians 6:9-10 — Do not grow weary of doing good, for in due season you will reap if you do not give up. Do good to everyone.
  • Ephesians 5:8-9 — Walk as children of light, for the fruit of light is found in all that is good and right and true.
  • Psalm 23:6 — Surely goodness and mercy shall follow me all the days of my life, and I shall dwell in the house of the LORD forever.
  • 3 John 1:11 — Do not imitate evil but imitate good. Whoever does good is from God; whoever does evil has not seen God.

Living It Out

Living out goodness means being intentionally proactive about doing what is right. It is not enough to simply avoid sin — agathosune calls us to actively pursue righteousness and create good in the world around us. This might look like volunteering your time for a cause that serves the vulnerable, speaking up for someone who is being treated unfairly, or making a decision that costs you convenience but benefits others. Ask yourself daily: "Where can I generate good today — not just avoid evil?"

Goodness also requires moral courage. There will be times when doing what is right means standing alone, confronting injustice, or saying the hard truth that others are unwilling to say. Jesus' goodness was not always popular — it made religious leaders uncomfortable and exposed hypocrisy. Similarly, the Spirit-produced goodness in your life may sometimes create friction with a culture that celebrates comfort over conviction. But take heart: the good you do in God's power is never wasted. Galatians 6:9 promises a harvest for those who persist in doing good without giving up.

Reflection Questions

  • Am I content with merely avoiding evil, or am I actively pursuing and creating good in my spheres of influence?
  • Is there a situation where I know the right thing to do but have been avoiding it because it's costly or uncomfortable? What is holding me back?
  • How has God's goodness been evident in my life this past month? How can I reflect that same goodness to others?
  • Where do I see injustice or need around me that I have the ability and opportunity to address with active goodness?
