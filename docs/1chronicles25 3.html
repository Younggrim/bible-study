1 Chronicles — Chapter 25

1David and the chiefs of the service also set apart for the service the sons of Asaph, and of Heman, and of Jeduthun, who prophesied with lyres, with harps, and with cymbals. The list of those who did the work and of their duties was:

2Of the sons of Asaph: Zaccur, Joseph, Nethaniah, and Asharelah, sons of Asaph, under the direction of Asaph, who prophesied under the direction of the king.

3Of Jeduthun, the sons of Jeduthun: Gedaliah, Zeri, Jeshaiah, Shimei, Hashabiah, and Mattithiah, six, under the direction of their father Jeduthun, who prophesied with the lyre in thanksgiving and praise to the LORD.

4Of Heman, the sons of Heman: Bukkiah, Mattaniah, Uzziel, Shebuel and Jerimoth, Hananiah, Hanani, Eliathah, Giddalti, and Romamti-ezer, Joshbekashah, Mallothi, Hothir, Mahazioth.

5All these were the sons of Heman the king’s seer, according to the promise of God to exalt him, for God had given Heman fourteen sons and three daughters.

6They were all under the direction of their father in the music in the house of the LORD with cymbals, harps, and lyres for the service of the house of God. Asaph, Jeduthun, and Heman were under the order of the king.

7The number of them along with their brothers, who were trained in singing to the LORD, all who were skillful, was 288.

8And they cast lots for their duties, small and great, teacher and pupil alike.

Summary

David and the military commanders set apart the sons of Asaph, Heman, and Jeduthun for prophetic ministry through music, organizing them into twenty-four divisions by lot. These musicians serve as worship leaders who prophesy with lyres, harps, and cymbals.

Key Verses

v.1
"Moreover David and the captains of the host separated to the service of the sons of Asaph, and of Heman, and of Jeduthun, who should prophesy with harps, with psalteries, and with cymbals." (KJV) — This verse revolutionizes the understanding of worship music. The musicians "prophesied" — their playing was not background music but Spirit- inspired proclamation. Instrumental worship is placed on the same level as prophetic speech. The "captains of the host" participated in this selection, showing that worship preparation was a national priority, not merely a religious afterthought.
v.3
"Of Jeduthun: the sons of Jeduthun; Gedaliah, and Zeri, and Jeshaiah, Hashabiah, and Mattithiah, six, under the hands of their father Jeduthun, who prophesied with a harp, to give thanks and to praise the LORD." (KJV) — The purpose of prophetic worship is specified: thanksgiving and praise. Jeduthun's harp was a vehicle for divine communication directed upward (to God in praise) and outward (to the congregation in proclamation). True worship music is simultaneously God-directed and congregation-edifying.
v.5
"All these were the sons of Heman the king's seer in the words of God, to lift up the horn. And God gave to Heman fourteen sons and three daughters." (KJV) — Heman is identified as a "seer" — a prophet who sees by divine revelation. His ministry was "in the words of God," confirming that worship music conveyed divine speech. "To lift up the horn" indicates exaltation and strength. God gave him many children specifically for this ministry — divine providence multiplied the worshipers.
v.7
"So the number of them, with their brethren that were instructed in the songs of the LORD, even all that were cunning, was two hundred fourscore and eight." (KJV) — 288 trained musicians — a substantial investment in worship excellence. "Instructed" and "cunning" (skilled) indicate rigorous training. Prophetic worship required both spiritual gifting and developed skill. Natural talent submitted to discipline produces the excellence God's worship deserves.
v.8
"And they cast lots, ward against ward, as well the small as the great, the teacher as the scholar." (KJV) — The lot made no distinction between experienced masters and new students. Before God, the teacher is not above the pupil in dignity of service. This radical equality in sacred assignment challenges every hierarchy of spiritual privilege based on experience or reputation.

Cross-References

  • v.1 — 2 Chronicles 29:25-26 — "And he [Hezekiah] set the Levites in the house of the LORD with cymbals, with psalteries, and with harps, according to the commandment of David... for so was the commandment of the LORD by his prophets." David's musical arrangements were not his own idea but divinely commanded through prophets — giving them the same authority as Mosaic legislation.
  • v.1 — 1 Samuel 10:5 — "Thou shalt meet a company of prophets coming down from the high place with a psaltery, and a tabret, and a pipe, and a harp, before them; and they shall prophesy." Musical instruments accompanied prophetic activity from Samuel's time — David formalized what already existed in Israel's prophetic tradition.
  • v.3 — Psalm 39 (superscription) — "To the chief Musician, even to Jeduthun." Jeduthun's name appears in Psalm titles (also Psalms 62, 77), confirming his ongoing role in Israel's worship music tradition. His descendants carried forward this prophetic musical ministry.
  • v.5 — Psalm 88 (superscription) — "A Song of the sons of Korah... Maschil of Heman the Ezrahite." Heman composed at least one psalm — the darkest in the Psalter — demonstrating that prophetic worship encompasses lament as well as praise. Honest grief before God is also prophetic ministry.
  • v.6 — Ephesians 5:18-19 — "Be filled with the Spirit; speaking to yourselves in psalms and hymns and spiritual songs, singing and making melody in your heart to the Lord." The New Testament connects Spirit-filling directly to musical worship — the same link 1 Chronicles 25 establishes between prophecy and instrumentation.
  • v.7 — Colossians 3:16 — "Teaching and admonishing one another in psalms and hymns and spiritual songs, singing with grace in your hearts to the Lord." Music teaches and admonishes — it is didactic, not merely aesthetic. This echoes the prophetic function of Temple musicians who proclaimed God's truth through song.
  • v.8 — 1 Corinthians 12:4-7 — "There are diversities of gifts, but the same Spirit... the manifestation of the Spirit is given to every man to profit withal." As the lot assigned equal dignity regardless of skill level, so the Spirit distributes gifts without creating hierarchies of worth.
  • v.5 — 1 Chronicles 6:33 — "Heman a singer, the son of Joel, the son of Shemuel [Samuel]." Heman was a grandson of Samuel the prophet — his prophetic musical gift carried a prophetic heritage. Godly lineage and divine calling converged in his ministry.

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/1-chronicles-25/ Guzik emphasizes the revolutionary concept of prophesying with instruments — that worship music was considered a vehicle for divine revelation, not merely human artistic expression. He notes that the 288 trained musicians represent an enormous national investment in worship, and that the casting of lots for service order prevented any musician from claiming precedence based on talent or seniority.
  • Charles Spurgeon: "Here is a word that should silence forever those who treat music in worship as a thing indifferent or merely ornamental. These men 'prophesied with harps' — the instrument was the vehicle of the Holy Spirit's utterance as truly as the prophet's tongue. God speaks through melody as surely as through discourse. The harpist who plays under divine anointing does no less service than the preacher who declares God's Word from the pulpit. Let us never separate the musician from the prophet, nor treat the choir as lesser ministers. They are 'seers' — those who see God's glory and translate it into sound that the congregation might behold what words alone cannot express."

Translations

  • v.1 — KJV: "captains of the host" / ESV: "chiefs of the service" — Hebrew "sarey ha-tsaba." KJV interprets "tsaba" as military host (the word's primary meaning); ESV interprets it as service/duty (a secondary meaning). The Hebrew is ambiguous — military commanders may have participated in organizing worship personnel, or "host" may refer to the Levitical service corps. Both readings have scholarly support.
  • v.1 — KJV: "psalteries" / ESV: "harps" — Hebrew "nevalim." The "nevel" was a larger stringed instrument (possibly a lyre or harp) distinct from the "kinnor" (smaller lyre, also translated "harp" in both versions). KJV's archaic "psalteries" (from Greek "psalterion") distinguishes the instruments; ESV loses this distinction by rendering both as "harps" or "lyres" without consistent differentiation.
  • v.5 — KJV: "the king's seer in the words of God, to lift up the horn" / ESV: "the king's seer, according to the promise of God to exalt him" — Hebrew "bidvarey ha-elohim leharim qaren." KJV translates literally: "in the words of God, to lift up the horn." ESV interprets "words of God" as "promise of God" and "lift up the horn" as "exalt him." The KJV preserves the Hebrew idiom; ESV provides the interpretive meaning. "Lift up the horn" (qeren) is a common biblical metaphor for strength and honor.
  • v.7 — KJV: "all that were cunning" / ESV: "all who were skillful" — Hebrew "mevin" (from "bin," to understand/discern). KJV's archaic "cunning" meant "skilled/knowledgeable" in 1611 English (not deceptive). ESV's "skillful" is the modern equivalent. The Hebrew root emphasizes understanding and discernment — these musicians comprehended what they performed, not merely executing notes mechanically.
  • v.8 — KJV: "ward against ward, as well the small as the great, the teacher as the scholar" / ESV: "for their duties, small and great, teacher and pupil alike" — Hebrew "mishmereth le'ummath mishmereth" (watch corresponding to watch). KJV's "ward against ward" preserves the Hebrew parallel structure; ESV simplifies. "Scholar" (KJV) vs. "pupil" (ESV) both translate "talmid" — one who learns. The KJV/ESV pair shows master-apprentice equality before God's lot.
  • v.3 — KJV: "six, under the hands of their father Jeduthun" / ESV: "six, under the direction of their father Jeduthun" — Hebrew "al-yedey" literally "upon the hands of." KJV's "under the hands" is more literal; ESV's "under the direction" captures the meaning idiomatically. The metaphor of being "in someone's hands" indicates authority and supervisory responsibility.

Reflection

  • 1. Worship music is prophetic ministry, not performance (v.1). These musicians "prophesied with harps" — their playing was a vehicle for God's Spirit to communicate truth. Music in worship should never be reduced to entertainment, atmosphere-setting, or artistic self-expression. It is ministry of the Word through melody and rhythm. Whether you lead worship, sing in a congregation, or play an instrument, ask: Is my music proclaiming God's truth, or merely producing pleasant sounds?
  • 2. Skill and spirituality belong together (v.7). The 288 musicians were both "instructed" (trained) and "cunning" (skilled). God's work deserves excellence, and excellence requires disciplined preparation. Spiritual passion without developed skill produces well-meaning mediocrity; skill without spiritual passion produces empty performance. Invest in both your spiritual life and your craft — whatever form of service God has called you to.
  • 3. God multiplies resources for worship (v.5). God "gave to Heman fourteen sons and three daughters" specifically for worship ministry. When God calls a work into being, He provides the personnel and resources to sustain it. If God has called your church, ministry, or family to a particular service, trust Him to provide what is needed. Do not limit God's provision by assuming scarcity.
  • 4. Equality in service before God (v.8). The lot made no distinction between "the teacher" and "the scholar" — master and apprentice received assignments with equal dignity. In God's economy, the newest believer serving faithfully is as valued as the seasoned veteran. Do not despise beginners or elevate experts beyond measure. God assigns each person their place, and every place matters equally.
  • 5. Worship preparation is a national/community priority (vv.1-6). David and the military commanders devoted resources and attention to organizing worship — it was not an afterthought but a central concern of national leadership. Communities that prioritize worship in their planning, budgeting, and leadership attention reflect this biblical pattern. Ask whether worship receives the same careful preparation and resource investment in your church as other programs and ministries do.
