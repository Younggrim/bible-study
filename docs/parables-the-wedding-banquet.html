Parables of Jesus

The Wedding Banquet

The Parable

A king prepared a wedding feast for his son and sent his servants to call those who were invited. But they refused to come. He sent other servants saying, "Tell those who are invited, 'See, I have prepared my dinner, my oxen and my fat calves have been slaughtered, and everything is ready. Come to the wedding feast.'" But they paid no attention — one went to his farm, another to his business — and some even seized the servants, treated them shamefully, and killed them.

The king was angry. He destroyed those murderers and burned their city. Then he said to his servants, "The wedding feast is ready, but those invited were not worthy. Go therefore to the main roads and invite to the wedding feast as many as you find." The servants went out into the streets and gathered all whom they found, both bad and good, and the wedding hall was filled with guests. The invitation that the privileged refused was extended to everyone without distinction.

But there is a final scene: the king came in to look at the guests and saw a man with no wedding garment. "Friend, how did you get in here without a wedding garment?" The man was speechless. The king had him cast out. In ancient custom, the host often provided garments for guests — this man had refused the king's provision, attempting to come on his own terms. Entry to the feast requires accepting what the king provides.

Key Scriptures

  • Matthew 22:1-14 — The full parable: rejected invitation, open call, and the necessity of the wedding garment.
  • Luke 14:15-24 — Luke's parallel: "Go out to the highways and hedges and compel people to come in, that my house may be filled."
  • Revelation 19:7-9 — "Blessed are those who are invited to the marriage supper of the Lamb" — the ultimate wedding feast.
  • Isaiah 25:6-8 — "On this mountain the LORD of hosts will make for all peoples a feast of rich food" — God's banquet promise.
  • Matthew 7:21-23 — "Not everyone who says to me, 'Lord, Lord,' will enter the kingdom" — presence without transformation is not enough.

What Jesus Was Teaching

Jesus was warning the religious leaders of Israel that their privileged position as God's covenant people did not guarantee entrance into the Kingdom. The original guests represent Israel's leaders who rejected the prophets and would soon reject the Messiah Himself. The open invitation to the streets represents the gospel going to Gentiles, sinners, and outsiders — anyone willing to come. God's grace is not exclusive to the religiously pedigreed; it is available to all who respond.

The wedding garment adds a crucial nuance: the invitation is free and open, but it is not without expectation. Coming to the feast requires accepting the garment the King provides — which represents the righteousness of Christ given to those who believe (Isaiah 61:10, Galatians 3:27). We cannot come to God on our own terms, in our own righteousness. Salvation is free but not cheap; it requires surrender to the King's provision rather than insistence on our own adequacy. Many are called, but few are chosen — because few are willing to come on God's terms rather than their own.

Reflection Questions

  • Have you ever been "too busy" for God's invitation — letting work, comfort, or distraction take priority over His call?
  • How does it change your evangelism to know that God's invitation extends to "both bad and good" on the highways and hedges?
  • Are you wearing the King's garment — resting in Christ's righteousness — or trying to come to God in your own self-made covering?
  • Is there someone in your life who has repeatedly refused God's invitation? How can you continue to extend it with grace and urgency?
