Job — Chapter 32

1So these three men ceased to answer Job, because he was righteous in his own eyes.

2Then Elihu the son of Barachel the Buzite, of the family of Ram, burned with anger. He burned with anger at Job because he justified himself rather than God.

3He burned with anger also at Job’s three friends because they had found no answer, although they had declared Job to be in the wrong.

4Now Elihu had waited to speak to Job because they were older than he.

5And when Elihu saw that there was no answer in the mouth of these three men, he burned with anger.

6And Elihu the son of Barachel the Buzite answered and said: “I am young in years, and you are aged; therefore I was timid and afraid to declare my opinion to you.

7I said, ‘Let days speak, and many years teach wisdom.’

8But it is the spirit in man, the breath of the Almighty, that makes him understand.

9It is not the old who are wise, nor the aged who understand what is right.

10Therefore I say, ‘Listen to me; let me also declare my opinion.’

11“Behold, I waited for your words, I listened for your wise sayings, while you searched out what to say.

12I gave you my attention, and, behold, there was none among you who refuted Job or who answered his words.

13Beware lest you say, ‘We have found wisdom; God may vanquish him, not a man.’

14He has not directed his words against me, and I will not answer him with your speeches.

15“They are dismayed; they answer no more; they have not a word to say.

16And shall I wait, because they do not speak, because they stand there, and answer no more?

17I also will answer with my share; I also will declare my opinion.

18For I am full of words; the spirit within me constrains me.

19Behold, my belly is like wine that has no vent; like new wineskins ready to burst.

20I must speak, that I may find relief; I must open my lips and answer.

21I will not show partiality to any man or use flattery toward any person.

22For I do not know how to flatter, else my Maker would soon take me away.

Summary

Elihu's introduction — a young man angry at both Job and the three friends breaks his silence, claiming the Spirit of God (not age) gives understanding, and announces he will speak where the elders have failed.

Key Verses

v.2
"Then Elihu the son of Barachel the Buzite, of the family of Ram, burned with anger. He burned with anger at Job because he justified himself rather than God." (ESV) — Elihu's core complaint against Job: Job was so focused on defending his own righteousness that he implicitly accused God of injustice.
v.3
"He burned with anger also at Job's three friends because they had found no answer, although they had declared Job to be in the wrong." (ESV) — Elihu's complaint against the friends: they declared Job guilty without proving it. They condemned without convincing.
v.8
"But it is the spirit in man, the breath of the Almighty, that makes him understand." (ESV) — Elihu's theological basis for speaking: wisdom does not come from age or experience alone but from God's Spirit. This is both his justification and his limitation — does he truly have divine insight, or does he merely claim it?
v.9
"It is not the old who are wise, nor the aged who understand what is right." (ESV) — A true observation that challenges the assumption that seniority equals wisdom. Age brings experience; only the Spirit brings understanding.

Cross-References

  • v.2 — Job 40:8 — God will raise the same concern: "Will you even put me in the wrong? Will you condemn me that you may be in the right?" (ESV) Elihu's critique anticipates God's. v.8 — Genesis 2:7 — "Breathed into his nostrils the breath of life." The same divine breath that gives life gives understanding. v.8 — 1 Corinthians 2:10-12 — "The Spirit searches everything ...we have received the Spirit who is from God." (ESV) v.9 — 1 Corinthians 1:26-27 — "Not many of you were wise according to worldly standards...God chose what is foolish in the world." (ESV) v.18 — Jeremiah 20:9 — "There is in my heart as it were a burning fire shut up in my bones." (ESV) Like Jeremiah, Elihu cannot contain the word within him. v.19 — Matthew 9:17 — "Neither is new wine put into old wineskins" (ESV) — Elihu uses the same wineskin metaphor. v.22 — James 2:1-4 — Warning against partiality and flattery in the assembly.

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/job-32/ Guzik notes that Elihu is a controversial figure: some see him as genuinely inspired, others as a pompous young man who adds nothing new. Guzik observes that God's silence about Elihu in chapter 42 (neither rebuking nor praising him) leaves his status ambiguous. His main contribution will be the idea of suffering as divine discipline — not punishment for past sin but training for future righteousness. This partially correct insight prepares for God's speeches.
  • Charles Spurgeon: "Elihu arrives like a thunderstorm — much wind and noise before the rain. He is young, hot, and full of words. Whether he is full of wisdom remains to be seen. He is right that the friends failed: they condemned without convincing. He is right that Job erred: he defended himself at God's expense. But being right about others' failures does not make you right about everything. Elihu will say some true things and some proud things — and the reader must discern the difference."

Translations

  • v.2 — KJV: "justified himself rather than God" / ESV: "justified himself rather than God" — Both translations capture the issue: Job's self-defense implicitly made God the unjust party. This is a legitimate critique — though Job's innocence was real, his rhetoric sometimes crossed into accusing God.
  • v.8 — KJV: "the inspiration of the Almighty" / ESV: "the breath of the Almighty" — Hebrew "neshmat Shaddai" = the breath/ inspiration of the Almighty. The same word used in Genesis 2:7 for the divine breath of life. Elihu claims the same Spirit that gives life also gives understanding.
  • v.19 — KJV: "new bottles" / ESV: "new wineskins" — Hebrew "obot" = wineskins (animal skins used to hold wine). New wine in a sealed skin produces gas that can burst it. Elihu feels ready to burst with words.
  • v.22 — KJV: "flattering titles" / ESV: "flattery" — Hebrew "kinnah" = to give an honorary name, to flatter. Elihu commits to calling things as they are, regardless of social status.

Reflection

  • 1. Youth is not a disqualification for speaking truth (vv.6-9). Elihu's initial deference to age was appropriate, but his recognition that age does not guarantee wisdom is equally appropriate. If God's Spirit gives understanding, it can come through anyone — young or old, credentialed or not.
  • 2. The inability to contain truth is a mark of calling (vv.18-20). Elihu cannot keep silent because the word within him demands release. This parallels Jeremiah's experience (Jer 20:9). When God gives you something to say, silence becomes unbearable.
  • 3. Correct diagnosis of others' failures does not guarantee correct solutions (vv.2-3). Elihu rightly identifies that the friends failed and that Job went too far. But seeing what went wrong is easier than providing what is right. Critique is cheap; wisdom is costly.
  • 4. Claiming divine inspiration carries divine accountability (v.8). Elihu appeals to the Spirit as his authority. This is either genuinely courageous or dangerously presumptuous — the content of his speeches will determine which. Anyone who claims to speak for God must be prepared for God to evaluate the claim.
