Ten Commandments

Remember the Sabbath

The Commandment

The fourth commandment calls God's people to "Remember the Sabbath day, to keep it holy" (Exodus 20:8-11). Unlike the other commandments that primarily prohibit harmful actions, this one commands a positive practice: stopping. God embedded a rhythm of rest into the very fabric of creation, working six days and resting on the seventh — not because He was weary, but to establish a pattern of trust, worship, and delight for His image-bearers to follow.

In the context of Israel's history, the Sabbath was a radical declaration of freedom. For a people who had been slaves in Egypt — forced to produce without ceasing under the lash of Pharaoh — the command to rest was revolutionary. It declared that their value was not determined by their productivity. They belonged to a God who provided, who could sustain them even when they stopped striving. Every Sabbath was a weekly Exodus — a liberation from the tyranny of endless work.

Jesus declared Himself "Lord of the Sabbath" (Mark 2:28) and taught that the Sabbath was made for humanity, not humanity for the Sabbath. He healed on the Sabbath, demonstrating that rest was never meant to be a legalistic burden but a gift of restoration. The New Testament presents Jesus Himself as our Sabbath rest — the One in whom we cease striving to earn God's favor and enter into the finished work of grace.

Key Scriptures

  • Exodus 20:8-11 — "Remember the Sabbath day, to keep it holy. Six days you shall labor, and do all your work, but the seventh day is a Sabbath to the LORD your God."
  • Mark 2:27-28 — "The Sabbath was made for man, not man for the Sabbath. So the Son of Man is lord even of the Sabbath."
  • Hebrews 4:9-10 — "So then, there remains a Sabbath rest for the people of God, for whoever has entered God's rest has also rested from his works."
  • Matthew 11:28-30 — "Come to me, all who labor and are heavy laden, and I will give you rest."
  • Colossians 2:16-17 — "Let no one pass judgment on you in questions of... a Sabbath. These are a shadow of the things to come, but the substance belongs to Christ."

Heart of the Law

The Sabbath principle speaks to one of our deepest spiritual struggles: the belief that our worth depends on our output. In a culture of constant productivity, hustle, and achievement, the fourth commandment is countercultural in the extreme. It says: stop. Trust. Remember that God is God and you are not. The world will not fall apart if you cease your striving for a day. Rest is not laziness; it is an act of faith.

For the Christian, Sabbath rest points ultimately to the gospel itself. Just as Israel rested from physical labor, believers rest from spiritual labor — from the impossible task of earning God's acceptance through performance. We enter God's rest by faith in Christ's finished work. Practicing regular rhythms of rest, worship, and delight becomes a weekly rehearsal of the gospel: I am loved not for what I produce, but for who I am in Christ.

Reflection Questions

  • What does your relationship with rest reveal about your trust in God's provision and sovereignty?
  • How might establishing a regular rhythm of Sabbath rest reshape your identity and sense of worth?
  • In what ways do you find it difficult to stop working — and what fears drive that difficulty?
  • How does Jesus as our ultimate "Sabbath rest" free you from performance-based spirituality?
