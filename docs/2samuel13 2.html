2 Samuel — Chapter 13

1Now Absalom, David’s son, had a beautiful sister, whose name was Tamar. And after a time Amnon, David’s son, loved her.

2And Amnon was so tormented that he made himself ill because of his sister Tamar, for she was a virgin, and it seemed impossible to Amnon to do anything to her.

3But Amnon had a friend, whose name was Jonadab, the son of Shimeah, David’s brother. And Jonadab was a very crafty man.

4And he said to him, “O son of the king, why are you so haggard morning after morning? Will you not tell me?” Amnon said to him, “I love Tamar, my brother Absalom’s sister.”

5Jonadab said to him, “Lie down on your bed and pretend to be ill. And when your father comes to see you, say to him, ‘Let my sister Tamar come and give me bread to eat, and prepare the food in my sight, that I may see it and eat it from her hand.’”

6So Amnon lay down and pretended to be ill. And when the king came to see him, Amnon said to the king, “Please let my sister Tamar come and make a couple of cakes in my sight, that I may eat from her hand.”

7Then David sent home to Tamar, saying, “Go to your brother Amnon’s house and prepare food for him.”

8So Tamar went to her brother Amnon’s house, where he was lying down. And she took dough and kneaded it and made cakes in his sight and baked the cakes.

9And she took the pan and emptied it out before him, but he refused to eat. And Amnon said, “Send out everyone from me.” So everyone went out from him.

10Then Amnon said to Tamar, “Bring the food into the chamber, that I may eat from your hand.” And Tamar took the cakes she had made and brought them into the chamber to Amnon her brother.

11But when she brought them near him to eat, he took hold of her and said to her, “Come, lie with me, my sister.”

12She answered him, “No, my brother, do not violate me, for such a thing is not done in Israel; do not do this outrageous thing.

13As for me, where could I carry my shame? And as for you, you would be as one of the outrageous fools in Israel. Now therefore, please speak to the king, for he will not withhold me from you.”

14But he would not listen to her, and being stronger than she, he violated her and lay with her.

15Then Amnon hated her with very great hatred, so that the hatred with which he hated her was greater than the love with which he had loved her. And Amnon said to her, “Get up! Go!”

16But she said to him, “No, my brother, for this wrong in sending me away is greater than the other that you did to me.” But he would not listen to her.

17He called the young man who served him and said, “Put this woman out of my presence and bolt the door after her.”

18Now she was wearing a long robe with sleeves, for thus were the virgin daughters of the king dressed. So his servant put her out and bolted the door after her.

19And Tamar put ashes on her head and tore the long robe that she wore. And she laid her hand on her head and went away, crying aloud as she went.

20And her brother Absalom said to her, “Has Amnon your brother been with you? Now hold your peace, my sister. He is your brother; do not take this to heart.” So Tamar lived, a desolate woman, in her brother Absalom’s house.

21When King David heard of all these things, he was very angry.

22But Absalom spoke to Amnon neither good nor bad, for Absalom hated Amnon, because he had violated his sister Tamar.

23After two full years Absalom had sheepshearers at Baal-hazor, which is near Ephraim, and Absalom invited all the king’s sons.

24And Absalom came to the king and said, “Behold, your servant has sheepshearers. Please let the king and his servants go with your servant.”

25But the king said to Absalom, “No, my son, let us not all go, lest we be burdensome to you.” He pressed him, but he would not go but gave him his blessing.

26Then Absalom said, “If not, please let my brother Amnon go with us.” And the king said to him, “Why should he go with you?”

27But Absalom pressed him until he let Amnon and all the king’s sons go with him.

28Then Absalom commanded his servants, “Mark when Amnon’s heart is merry with wine, and when I say to you, ‘Strike Amnon,’ then kill him. Do not fear; have I not commanded you? Be courageous and be valiant.”

29So the servants of Absalom did to Amnon as Absalom had commanded. Then all the king’s sons arose, and each mounted his mule and fled.

30While they were on the way, news came to David, “Absalom has struck down all the king’s sons, and not one of them is left.”

31Then the king arose and tore his garments and lay on the earth. And all his servants who were standing by tore their garments.

32But Jonadab the son of Shimeah, David’s brother, said, “Let not my lord suppose that they have killed all the young men, the king’s sons, for Amnon alone is dead. For by the command of Absalom this has been determined from the day he violated his sister Tamar.

33Now therefore let not my lord the king so take it to heart as to suppose that all the king’s sons are dead, for Amnon alone is dead.”

34But Absalom fled. And the young man who kept the watch lifted up his eyes and looked, and behold, many people were coming from the road behind him by the side of the mountain.

35And Jonadab said to the king, “Behold, the king’s sons have come; as your servant said, so it has come about.”

36And as soon as he had finished speaking, behold, the king’s sons came and lifted up their voice and wept. And the king also and all his servants wept very bitterly.

37But Absalom fled and went to Talmai the son of Ammihud, king of Geshur. And David mourned for his son day after day.

38So Absalom fled and went to Geshur, and was there three years.

39And the spirit of the king longed to go out to Absalom, because he was comforted about Amnon, since he was dead.

Summary

Amnon rapes his half-sister Tamar and then despises her. Absalom nurses hatred for two years, then murders Amnon at a sheep-shearing feast and flees to Geshur—the sword has come to David's house.

Key Verses

v.12
"Nay, my brother, do not force me; for no such thing ought to be done in Israel: do not you this folly." — Tamar's plea appeals to Israel's moral identity. "Such a thing is not done in Israel" — this is beneath who we are as God's people. She calls it "folly" (nebalah) — the same word used for the worst moral outrages in Israel (Judges 19:23, 20:6). Her wisdom and courage in this moment are remarkable.
v.15
"Then Amnon hated her exceedingly; so that the hatred wherewith he hated her was greater than the love wherewith he had loved her." — The nature of lust exposed. What Amnon called "love" was actually self-centered desire that, once gratified, turned to contempt. True love cherishes and protects; lust uses and discards. The reversal from "love" to hatred reveals that there was never love at all.
v.21
"But when king David heard of all these things, he was very wroth." — David's anger without action. He is furious but does nothing — no punishment for Amnon, no justice for Tamar. This failure of leadership creates the vacuum that Absalom fills with murder. A father who will not discipline creates children who take justice into their own hands.
v.22
"Absalom spake to his brother Amnon neither good nor bad: for Absalom hated Amnon." — Silence more dangerous than anger. Absalom's refusal to speak reveals not forgiveness but calculation. He is waiting — planning — for two years. Cold hatred is more dangerous than hot anger.

Cross-References

  • v.1 — 2 Samuel 3:2-3 — Amnon (firstborn, by Ahinoam) and Absalom (third son, by Maacah) — the family structure that creates this tragedy. v.12 — Genesis 34:7 — "Folly in Israel" — the same language used for Shechem's rape of Dinah. The parallel is deliberate. v.14 — 2 Samuel 12:11 — "I will raise up evil against you out of your own house" — Nathan's prophecy being fulfilled. v.15 — Proverbs 5:3-5 — The path of lust: sweet at first, bitter as wormwood in the end. v.21 — 2 Samuel 12:9-10 — David's own sin disables his moral authority to punish his son's sin. v.28 — 2 Samuel 11:15 — Absalom's command to kill Amnon echoes David's letter ordering Uriah's death — the son mirrors the father. v.37 — 2 Samuel 3:3 — Maacah was daughter of Talmai king of Geshur — Absalom flees to his maternal grandfather. v.39 — 2 Samuel 14:1 — David's longing for Absalom sets up Joab's scheme in the next chapter.

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/2-samuel-13/ Guzik emphasizes the parallel between David's sin and Amnon's: both saw, desired, and took. But David's failure to punish Amnon is the chapter's most devastating element — his own guilt silenced his authority. Guzik notes that Absalom's two-year silence was not healing but hardening — cold premeditation replacing hot anger.
  • Charles Spurgeon: "The sword entered David's house and would not depart. His own sin with Bathsheba was now reproduced in his son — but without even the pretense of love. Amnon's lust, David's inaction, Absalom's murder — all flow from that night on the rooftop. Let every man who toys with sin consider: you are not sinning for yourself alone. Your sin will bear fruit in your children and your children's children."

Translations

  • v.3 — KJV: "a very subtil man" / ESV: "a very crafty man" — Hebrew "chakam" = wise, shrewd, crafty. The word is neutral — it can mean genuinely wise or cunningly shrewd. Context determines: Jonadab uses his intelligence for evil. KJV "subtil" (subtle) and ESV "crafty" both capture the negative application.
  • v.13 — KJV: "one of the fools in Israel" / ESV: "one of the outrageous fools in Israel" — Hebrew "nebalim" = fools in the moral sense (not intellectual). A "nabal" is one who acts with moral outrage against God and community (cf. Nabal in 1 Samuel 25). ESV adds "outrageous" to clarify this is not mere stupidity but moral depravity.
  • v.18 — KJV: "a garment of divers colours" / ESV: "a long robe with sleeves" — Hebrew "ketonet passim" — the same phrase used for Joseph's coat (Genesis 37:3). Its exact meaning is debated: "many colors," "long sleeves," or "reaching to the extremities." It marked royal virgin daughters — its tearing symbolized Tamar's destroyed status.
  • v.20 — KJV: "regard not this thing" / ESV: "do not take this to heart" — Absalom's words to Tamar seem dismissive but may be an attempt to calm her while he plans his own response. His silence toward Amnon (v.22) shows he is not dismissing the crime but internalizing it for future action.
  • v.39 — KJV: "the soul of king David longed to go forth unto Absalom" / ESV: "the spirit of the king longed to go out to Absalom" — Hebrew "vatekhal" = to be consumed with longing, to pine. Both translations capture David's aching desire to see his exiled son. David is torn between justice (Absalom murdered Amnon) and love (Absalom is still his son).

Reflection

  • 1. Sin reproduces itself in the next generation (vv.1-14). David's adultery is echoed in Amnon's rape — the son mirrors the father's pattern of seeing, desiring, and taking. Our sins do not stay contained — they teach our children patterns of behavior. The most powerful sermon we preach is the life we live before those who watch us.
  • 2. Lust is not love (v.15). Amnon's "love" turned instantly to hatred once gratified. True love seeks the other's good; lust seeks self-gratification at the other's expense. The test is simple: does this desire lead me to protect and honor the other person, or to use them? If it uses, it is lust — regardless of what we call it.
  • 3. Failure to act as a leader creates worse problems (v.21). David's anger without action left Tamar without justice and Absalom without recourse. When those in authority refuse to address wrong, they do not prevent conflict — they guarantee it will escalate. Absalom's murder was the direct result of David's inaction.
  • 4. Silence can be more dangerous than anger (v.22). Absalom's two years of silence were not healing — they were hardening. Unresolved anger that goes underground does not disappear; it calcifies into something far more dangerous. Address conflict openly rather than letting it fester in silence.
  • 5. The consequences of sin extend far beyond the sinner (vv.19-20). Tamar — completely innocent — lived "desolate" for the rest of her life. David's sin with Bathsheba set in motion a chain of events that destroyed his daughter's life. Sin always has collateral damage. The innocent suffer alongside the guilty.
