Job — Chapter 27

1And Job again took up his discourse, and said:

2“As God lives, who has taken away my right, and the Almighty, who has made my soul bitter,

3as long as my breath is in me, and the spirit of God is in my nostrils,

4my lips will not speak falsehood, and my tongue will not utter deceit.

5Far be it from me to say that you are right; till I die I will not put away my integrity from me.

6I hold fast my righteousness and will not let it go; my heart does not reproach me for any of my days.

7“Let my enemy be as the wicked, and let him who rises up against me be as the unrighteous.

8For what is the hope of the godless when God cuts him off, when God takes away his life?

9Will God hear his cry when distress comes upon him?

10Will he take delight in the Almighty? Will he call upon God at all times?

11I will teach you concerning the hand of God; what is with the Almighty I will not conceal.

12Behold, all of you have seen it yourselves; why then have you become altogether vain?

13“This is the portion of a wicked man with God, and the heritage that oppressors receive from the Almighty:

14If his children are multiplied, it is for the sword, and his descendants have not enough bread.

15Those who survive him the pestilence buries, and his widows do not weep.

16Though he heap up silver like dust, and pile up clothing like clay,

17he may pile it up, but the righteous will wear it, and the innocent will divide the silver.

18He builds his house like a moth’s, like a booth that a watchman makes.

19He goes to bed rich, but will do so no more; he opens his eyes, and his wealth is gone.

20Terrors overtake him like a flood; in the night a whirlwind carries him off.

21The east wind lifts him up and he is gone; it sweeps him out of his place.

22It hurls at him without pity; he flees from its power in headlong flight.

23It claps its hands at him and hisses at him from its place.

Summary

Job's final oath of integrity — swears by God that he will never admit his friends are right or relinquish his righteousness, then describes the ultimate fate of the wicked and the hypocrite.

Key Verses

v.2
"As God lives, who has taken away my right, and the Almighty, who has made my soul bitter" (ESV) — Job swears by God while simultaneously accusing God of injustice. This is not hypocrisy — it is the complexity of faith. Job's relationship with God is strained but unbroken.
v.5
"Far be it from me to say that you are right; till I die I will not put away my integrity from me." (ESV) — Job refuses to confess sins he did not commit simply to validate his friends' theology. False confession is not humility — it is bearing false witness against yourself.
v.6
"I hold fast my righteousness and will not let it go; my heart does not reproach me for any of my days." (ESV) — Job's conscience is clear. He will not manufacture guilt to satisfy others' expectations. A clean conscience before God is worth defending.
v.8
"For what is the hope of the godless when God cuts him off, when God takes away his life?" (ESV) — Job knows the fate of the false. This is why he refuses to become one — false confession would make him the very hypocrite whose doom he here describes.

Cross-References

  • v.2 — Job 34:5-6 — Elihu will later quote Job's claim that God has denied him justice. v.3 — Genesis 2:7 — "The LORD God...breathed into his nostrils the breath of life." (ESV) Job's life depends on God's breath even while he disputes with God. v.5 — Job 2:3 — "He still holds fast his integrity" (ESV) — God's own description of Job. Job maintains what God Himself affirmed. v.6 — Acts 24:16 — Paul's parallel: "I always take pains to have a clear conscience toward both God and man." (ESV) v.8 — Luke 12:20 — "Fool! This night your soul is required of you" (ESV) — the hypocrite's hope is cut off. v.16 — Proverbs 13:22 — "The wealth of the sinner is laid up for the just." Job echoes this proverb in v.17. v.20 — Psalm 73:19 — "How they are destroyed in a moment, swept away utterly by terrors!" (ESV) — the sudden destruction of the wicked.

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/job-27/ Guzik emphasizes that Job's oath in vv.2-6 is one of the most remarkable declarations of personal integrity in Scripture. Job swears by God — acknowledging God's reality and sovereignty — while simultaneously charging God with injustice. This is not contradiction but honest faith. Guzik also notes that vv.13-23 deliberately echo the friends' language, turning their own arguments into evidence for Job's innocence.
  • Charles Spurgeon: "Job will not lie to please God, and this pleases God more than a thousand false confessions. The friends would have Job say 'I have sinned' when he has not — but a false confession dishonors God as much as a false denial. God does not want manufactured guilt; He wants truth in the inward parts. Job's stubbornness here is not pride — it is honesty. And God will vindicate that honesty in chapter 42: 'My servant Job hath spoken of me the thing that is right.'"

Translations

  • v.1 — KJV: "continued his parable" / ESV: "took up his discourse" — Hebrew "mashal" can mean proverb, parable, discourse, or oracle. Here it simply means Job continued his extended speech. The third cycle is incomplete, and Job fills the gap.
  • v.5 — KJV: "God forbid that I should justify you" / ESV: "Far be it from me to say that you are right" — Hebrew "chalilah" = "far be it!" or "profane be the thought!" A strong expression of rejection. Job considers agreeing with his friends to be a moral impossibility.
  • v.8 — KJV: "hypocrite" / ESV: "godless" — Hebrew "chaneph" means profane, godless, or hypocritical. The ESV broadens the term; the KJV emphasizes the pretense aspect. Job's point: if he confessed sins he didn't commit, he would be the hypocrite.
  • v.18 — KJV: "as a moth" / ESV: "like a moth's" — The wicked man's house is as fragile and temporary as a moth's cocoon — or as a watchman's temporary shelter (booth). Both images emphasize impermanence.

Reflection

  • 1. Integrity is worth more than comfort (v.5). Job could end his suffering immediately by confessing sins he did not commit — his friends would be satisfied, and their theology would be vindicated. But Job refuses. He would rather suffer with integrity than find relief through falsehood. Your character is not for sale — not even for peace.
  • 2. You can dispute with God without departing from God (v.2). Job swears by the God he accuses. He argues with God but does not abandon God. Wrestling with God is still holding onto God. Honest wrestling is more faithful than dishonest peace.
  • 3. A clean conscience is its own defense (v.6). "My heart shall not reproach me" — Job does not need others' approval because his own conscience is clear. When you know you are right before God, human judgment loses its power to define you. Let your conscience answer your accusers.
  • 4. The wicked eventually fall — but not on our timeline (vv.13-23). Job agrees with his friends that the wicked are ultimately destroyed. His disagreement is not about the destination but about the timing and the logic. He refuses to accept that his suffering means he is wicked, even while affirming that wickedness does lead to destruction.
