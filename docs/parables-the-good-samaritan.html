Parables of Jesus

The Good Samaritan

The Parable

A lawyer stood up to test Jesus, asking, "Teacher, what shall I do to inherit eternal life?" Jesus turned the question back: "What is written in the Law?" The man answered perfectly — love God with all your heart, and love your neighbor as yourself. But wanting to justify himself, he pressed further: "And who is my neighbor?" This question was not innocent; it was an attempt to draw boundaries around obligation, to define who could be safely excluded from love.

Jesus responded with a story. A man traveling from Jerusalem to Jericho — a notoriously dangerous seventeen-mile descent through barren wilderness — fell among robbers who stripped him, beat him, and left him half dead. A priest came down that road, saw him, and passed by on the other side. A Levite did the same. Both had religious excuses — touching a possibly dead body would render them ceremonially unclean. They chose ritual purity over compassion.

Then a Samaritan came — a man despised by the Jewish audience, considered a half-breed heretic. Yet he was moved with compassion, bandaged the man's wounds with oil and wine, placed him on his own animal, brought him to an inn, and paid for his care with his own money. Jesus asked, "Which of these three proved to be a neighbor?" The lawyer could barely bring himself to say it: "The one who showed him mercy." Jesus said, "Go and do likewise."

Key Scriptures

  • Luke 10:25-37 — The full parable: a question about eternal life becomes a lesson about boundary-breaking love.
  • Micah 6:8 — "What does the LORD require of you but to do justice, love kindness, and walk humbly with your God?"
  • James 2:15-17 — "If a brother or sister is poorly clothed and lacking daily food... faith by itself, if it does not have works, is dead."
  • Matthew 25:40 — "As you did it to one of the least of these my brothers, you did it to me."
  • Galatians 6:2 — "Bear one another's burdens, and so fulfill the law of Christ."

What Jesus Was Teaching

Jesus reversed the lawyer's question entirely. The man asked "Who is my neighbor?" — seeking to limit his responsibility. Jesus reframed it as "To whom can I be a neighbor?" — expanding responsibility to anyone in need, regardless of ethnicity, religion, or social status. The command to love has no boundary conditions. The priest and Levite represent religion that knows the right answers but fails to act; the Samaritan represents love that crosses every human-made barrier to meet real need with real sacrifice.

There is also a deeper layer: many early church fathers saw the Good Samaritan as a picture of Christ Himself. We are the man left half-dead by sin. The religious law passes by — it diagnoses but cannot heal. Christ, the ultimate outsider who "had no form or majesty" (Isaiah 53:2), comes to us in our brokenness, binds our wounds, bears our weight, and pays a debt we cannot repay. The parable is both an ethical command and a gospel portrait — we love others this way because we have first been loved this way.

Reflection Questions

  • Who are the "Samaritans" in your world — people you might be inclined to avoid or dismiss? How is God calling you to cross that boundary?
  • Have you ever been the man in the ditch, and someone unexpected showed you mercy? How did that change you?
  • Are there ways you resemble the priest or Levite — knowing the right thing but choosing comfort or reputation over compassion?
  • What would it look like this week to actively seek out someone in need and show up with tangible help?
