Psalms — Chapter 36

1Transgression speaks to the wicked deep in his heart; there is no fear of God before his eyes.

2For he flatters himself in his own eyes that his iniquity cannot be found out and hated.

3The words of his mouth are trouble and deceit; he has ceased to act wisely and do good.

4He plots trouble while on his bed; he sets himself in a way that is not good; he does not reject evil.

5Your steadfast love, O LORD, extends to the heavens, your faithfulness to the clouds.

6Your righteousness is like the mountains of God; your judgments are like the great deep; man and beast you save, O LORD.

7How precious is your steadfast love, O God! The children of mankind take refuge in the shadow of your wings.

8They feast on the abundance of your house, and you give them drink from the river of your delights.

9For with you is the fountain of life; in your light do we see light.

10Oh, continue your steadfast love to those who know you, and your righteousness to the upright of heart!

11Let not the foot of arrogance come upon me, nor the hand of the wicked drive me away.

12There the evildoers lie fallen; they are thrust down, unable to rise.

Summary

A psalm contrasting wickedness and God's love — the wicked have no fear of God, but God's steadfast love reaches the heavens, His faithfulness the clouds; in His light we see light.

Key Verses

Cross-References

  • v.1 — Romans 3:18: Paul quotes this verse as the culmination of his indictment of universal human sinfulness — "There is no fear of God before their eyes."
  • v.5 — Psalm 57:10; Psalm 108:4: God's mercy/love reaching to the heavens — repeated imagery of divine love exceeding all created measurement.
  • v.7 — Psalm 17:8; Psalm 57:1; Ruth 2:12: The shadow of God's wings — protective imagery combining the mother bird (maternal care) with the wings of the cherubim over the ark (divine presence).
  • v.8 — Psalm 46:4; Revelation 22:1-2: The river of God — from Eden's river (Gen. 2:10), to the river in God's city (Ps. 46:4), to the river of life flowing from God's throne (Rev. 22:1).
  • v.9 — John 1:4-9; John 8:12: "In him was life; and the life was the light of men." Jesus identifies Himself as this fountain of life and light of the world — the New Testament fulfillment of Psalm 36:9.
  • v.9 — Jeremiah 2:13; John 4:14: God as the fountain of living waters — contrasted with the broken cisterns of human-made religion and the living water Christ offers.
  • v.9 — 1 John 1:5: "God is light, and in him is no darkness at all." The epistle's theology of God as light flows directly from this Old Testament foundation.
  • v.12 — Psalm 1:6; Psalm 37:36: The fall of the wicked is certain and final — "unable to rise."

Translations

  • v.1 — "The transgression of the wicked saith within my heart" (KJV) / "Transgression speaks to the wicked deep in his heart" (ESV): A significant textual issue. The Hebrew reads "in my heart" (libbi), but many manuscripts, the Septuagint, and the Syriac read "in his heart" (libbo). KJV follows the Hebrew Masoretic text; ESV follows the emended reading. The difference changes whether David is reporting what sin reveals to him about the wicked, or what sin speaks directly to the wicked person's own heart.
  • v.5 — "Thy mercy" (KJV) / "Your steadfast love" (ESV): Hebrew chesed — God's covenant loyalty and love. KJV's "mercy" emphasizes compassion; ESV's "steadfast love" emphasizes commitment and constancy. Both aspects are present in chesed.
  • v.7 — "How excellent is thy lovingkindness" (KJV) / "How precious is your steadfast love" (ESV): Hebrew mah-yaqar — literally "how precious, costly, valuable." ESV's "precious" captures the sense of rare value better than KJV's "excellent," which in modern English suggests mere quality rather than costly preciousness.
  • v.8 — "the fatness of thy house" (KJV) / "the abundance of your house" (ESV): Hebrew deshen means fatness, richness — the choicest portions of sacrificial meals. KJV preserves the vivid culinary metaphor; ESV generalizes to "abundance." In ancient Near Eastern culture, fat was the richest, most prized part of food.

Reflection

  • 1. The portrait of wickedness begins with "no fear of God before his eyes" (v.1). The fear of the LORD is not merely one virtue among many — it is the foundation. When fear of God erodes, everything else follows: self-deception (v.2), corrupt speech (v.3), abandonment of wisdom (v.3), and deliberate evil (v.4). How actively do you cultivate the fear of God?
  • 2. Verses 5-6 measure God's attributes by creation's scale. His love is not small, not limited, not conditional — it reaches to the heavens. His faithfulness extends to the clouds. When you feel God's love is inadequate for your situation, remember: you are trying to outrun something as vast as the sky.
  • 3. "The river of thy pleasures" (v.8) — the word for "pleasures" shares its root with "Eden." God Himself is paradise. The deepest human longing for joy, beauty, and satisfaction is ultimately a longing for God. Are you seeking Eden in created things, or drinking from the Source directly?
  • 4. "In thy light shall we see light" (v.9) has profound epistemological implications: God is the necessary precondition for all true knowledge. Apart from His revelation, we cannot rightly interpret reality. How does this shape your approach to wisdom, education, and decision-making?
  • 5. The psalm contrasts two worlds: the cramped, dark world of the wicked (vv.1-4) who see only themselves, and the vast, luminous world of those who know God (vv.5-9) who see infinite love, boundless faithfulness, and eternal light. Which world do you inhabit moment by moment?
