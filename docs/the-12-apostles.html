The 12 Apostles

"And he appointed twelve (whom he also named apostles) so that they might be with him and he might send them out to preach and have authority to cast out demons."

— Mark 3:14-15 (ESV)

1. Simon Peter

Name meaning: "Rock" (Greek: Petros / Aramaic: Cephas)
A fisherman from Bethsaida who became the leader of the apostles. Known for his boldness, impulsiveness, and powerful preaching at Pentecost. Denied Jesus three times but was restored. Authored 1 & 2 Peter.
Context & Background Simon was a working-class fisherman on the Sea of Galilee when Jesus called him. He was married (Mark 1:30) and partnered with James and John in the fishing trade. Jesus renamed him "Peter" (Rock), signifying his foundational role. He became the spokesman for the Twelve and preached the first gospel sermon at Pentecost (Acts 2), leading to about 3,000 conversions.
Key Scriptures:
  • Matthew 16:16-18
  • John 21:15-17
  • Acts 2:14-41
  • Luke 5:1-11
Simon Peter - Wikipedia

2. Andrew

Name meaning: "Manly" or "Courageous" (Greek: Andreas)
Brother of Simon Peter and a former disciple of John the Baptist. Known as the first called and for bringing others to Jesus — he brought Peter, the boy with loaves and fish, and the Greeks seeking Jesus.
Context & Background Andrew was originally a follower of John the Baptist and was present when John declared Jesus to be the "Lamb of God" (John 1:36). He immediately sought out his brother Simon to tell him they had found the Messiah. Andrew consistently appears as a bridge — introducing people to Jesus — earning him the title "the first-called" in Eastern Christian tradition.
Key Scriptures:
  • John 1:40-42
  • John 6:8-9
  • John 12:20-22
  • Mark 1:16-18
Andrew - Wikipedia

3. James (son of Zebedee)

Name meaning: "Supplanter" (Hebrew: Ya'akov)
Elder brother of John, part of Jesus' inner circle (with Peter and John). A fisherman nicknamed "Son of Thunder." First apostle to be martyred — executed by Herod Agrippa I around AD 44.
Context & Background James was part of a prosperous fishing family — his father Zebedee employed hired servants (Mark 1:20). Along with Peter and John, he witnessed the Transfiguration (Mark 9:2), the raising of Jairus's daughter (Mark 5:37), and Jesus' agony in Gethsemane (Mark 14:33). His martyrdom in Acts 12:2 is the only apostolic death recorded in Scripture.
Key Scriptures:
  • Mark 3:17
  • Acts 12:1-2
  • Mark 9:2-3
  • Mark 14:33
James (son of Zebedee) - Wikipedia

4. John

Name meaning: "God is gracious" (Hebrew: Yohanan)
Younger brother of James, the "beloved disciple." Part of Jesus' inner circle. Entrusted with the care of Mary at the cross. Authored the Gospel of John, 1-3 John, and Revelation. Lived the longest of the apostles.
Context & Background John was likely the youngest apostle and outlived all the others, dying of natural causes around AD 100. Despite the fiery nickname "Son of Thunder," his later writings emphasize love above all else. He was exiled to the island of Patmos under Emperor Domitian, where he received the visions recorded in Revelation. He is traditionally identified as "the disciple whom Jesus loved."
Key Scriptures:
  • John 13:23
  • John 19:26-27
  • Revelation 1:1-2
  • Mark 10:35-40
John - Wikipedia

5. Philip

Name meaning: "Lover of horses" (Greek: Philippos)
From Bethsaida, the city of Andrew and Peter. Known for his practical, calculating nature. Brought Nathanael to Jesus. Asked Jesus to "show us the Father." Approached by Greeks wanting to see Jesus.
Context & Background Philip is notable as the first apostle Jesus directly called with the words "Follow me" (John 1:43). He had a Greek name, suggesting Hellenistic cultural influence, which may explain why Greek-speaking visitors approached him (John 12:20-21). His practical, analytical nature is seen when Jesus tested him about feeding the 5,000 and Philip calculated the cost (John 6:7).
Key Scriptures:
  • John 1:43-46
  • John 6:5-7
  • John 14:8-9
  • John 12:20-22
Philip - Wikipedia

6. Bartholomew (Nathanael)

Name meaning: "Son of Tolmai" (Aramaic: Bar-Talmay)
Likely the Nathanael brought to Jesus by Philip. Jesus declared him "an Israelite indeed, in whom there is no deceit." Initially skeptical ("Can anything good come from Nazareth?") but quickly believed.
Context & Background Bartholomew is always paired with Philip in the Synoptic apostle lists, supporting the identification with Nathanael, who Philip brought to Jesus in John's Gospel. He was from Cana of Galilee (John 21:2). Jesus' supernatural knowledge of him sitting "under the fig tree" before Philip called him led to his immediate confession of faith. Tradition holds he preached in India and Armenia.
Key Scriptures:
  • John 1:45-51
  • John 21:2
  • Matthew 10:3
  • Mark 3:18
Bartholomew - Wikipedia

7. Matthew (Levi)

Name meaning: "Gift of God" (Hebrew: Mattityahu)
A tax collector from Capernaum, considered a traitor by fellow Jews for working with Rome. Left everything to follow Jesus. Hosted a great banquet for Jesus. Authored the Gospel of Matthew.
Context & Background As a tax collector (publican), Matthew was despised by fellow Jews for collaborating with Rome and was considered ceremonially unclean. His inclusion among the Twelve was shocking and demonstrated Jesus' ministry to sinners. His Gospel, written primarily for a Jewish audience, emphasizes Jesus as the fulfillment of Old Testament prophecy and contains more OT quotations than any other Gospel.
Key Scriptures:
  • Matthew 9:9-13
  • Luke 5:27-32
  • Mark 2:14
  • Matthew 10:3
Matthew - Wikipedia

8. Thomas

Name meaning: "Twin" (Aramaic: T'oma / Greek: Didymus)
Known as "Doubting Thomas" for wanting physical proof of the resurrection, but also showed great courage (John 11:16) and made one of Scripture's greatest confessions: "My Lord and my God!"
Context & Background Thomas is often unfairly reduced to his doubt. When Jesus proposed returning to Judea where people sought to kill Him, Thomas said, "Let us also go, that we may die with him" (John 11:16) — showing remarkable loyalty and courage. His demand for proof (John 20:25) ultimately led to one of the greatest Christological confessions in Scripture. Tradition holds he took the gospel to India, where the Mar Thoma Christians trace their heritage to him.
Key Scriptures:
  • John 20:24-29
  • John 11:16
  • John 14:5
  • Mark 3:18
Thomas - Wikipedia

9. James (son of Alphaeus)

Name meaning: "Supplanter" (Hebrew: Ya'akov)
Often called "James the Less" (possibly due to stature or age). Little is known of his specific ministry, but he faithfully followed Jesus and was present in the upper room after the Ascension.
Context & Background Distinguished from James son of Zebedee, this James is called "the Less" (or "the Younger") in Mark 15:40, possibly indicating shorter stature or younger age. His father Alphaeus may be the same as Clopas (John 19:25), making his mother "Mary of Clopas" who stood at the cross. Despite limited biblical record, his faithful presence among the Twelve through the crucifixion, resurrection, and Pentecost speaks to quiet, steadfast discipleship.
Key Scriptures:
  • Mark 3:18
  • Matthew 10:3
  • Luke 6:15
  • Acts 1:13
James (son of Alphaeus) - Wikipedia

10. Thaddaeus (Judas son of James)

Name meaning: "Courageous heart" (possibly from Aramaic)
Also called Judas (not Iscariot) or Lebbaeus. Asked Jesus at the Last Supper why He would reveal Himself to the disciples but not the world. A faithful but quiet apostle.
Context & Background Known by multiple names — Thaddaeus (Matthew/Mark), Judas son of James (Luke/Acts), and Lebbaeus in some manuscripts — likely to distinguish him from Judas Iscariot. His single recorded question in John 14:22 reveals genuine theological curiosity about why Jesus chose a private rather than public revelation. Tradition credits him with evangelizing Mesopotamia and Persia, often alongside Simon the Zealot.
Key Scriptures:
  • John 14:22
  • Matthew 10:3
  • Mark 3:18
  • Luke 6:16
Thaddaeus - Wikipedia

11. Simon the Zealot

Name meaning: "He has heard" + "Zealous one" (Hebrew/Greek)
Called "the Zealot" — likely a former member of the Zealot political party that sought to overthrow Roman rule by force. His inclusion alongside Matthew (a Roman collaborator) shows Christ's power to unite opposites.
Context & Background The Zealots were a Jewish political movement seeking to expel Rome from Israel by armed revolt. Simon's designation as "the Zealot" (Luke 6:15; Acts 1:13) or "the Cananaean" (an Aramaic equivalent in Matthew/Mark) indicates his political background. His presence alongside Matthew the tax collector — who profited from Roman rule — is a powerful testimony that the gospel reconciles even the most deeply divided people under one Lord.
Key Scriptures:
  • Luke 6:15
  • Acts 1:13
  • Matthew 10:4
  • Mark 3:18
Simon the Zealot - Wikipedia

12. Judas Iscariot → Matthias

Judas: "Praised" / Iscariot: possibly "man of Kerioth"
Judas served as treasurer but betrayed Jesus for 30 silver pieces, then hanged himself. He was replaced by Matthias, chosen by lot from among those who had accompanied Jesus from His baptism through His ascension.
Context & Background Judas was entrusted as the group's treasurer (John 12:6; 13:29), yet John records he "was a thief" who helped himself to the money bag. His betrayal for 30 pieces of silver fulfilled Zechariah 11:12-13. After Judas's death, the remaining eleven required his replacement to have been a witness of Jesus' ministry from John's baptism through the Ascension (Acts 1:21-22). Matthias was chosen by lot — the last recorded instance of this Old Testament method of discerning God's will before Pentecost.
Key Scriptures:
  • Matthew 26:14-16
  • Matthew 27:3-5
  • Acts 1:15-26
  • John 12:6
