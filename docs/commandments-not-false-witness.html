Ten Commandments

You Shall Not Bear False Witness

The Commandment

The ninth commandment, "You shall not bear false witness against your neighbor" (Exodus 20:16), protects truth as the foundation of justice and community. Originally situated in the context of legal testimony — where false witness could lead to an innocent person's condemnation or death — this commandment extends to all forms of dishonesty: lying, slander, gossip, half-truths, misleading silence, and the manipulation of facts to harm others.

In Israelite law, a false witness who was discovered bore the punishment that would have fallen on the accused (Deuteronomy 19:18-19). This severe consequence demonstrated how seriously God takes truthfulness. The tongue has immense power — to build or destroy, to protect or condemn, to heal or wound. When we bear false witness, we weaponize our words against the very people God has called us to love.

The New Testament consistently calls believers to radical truthfulness. Paul commands, "Having put away falsehood, let each one of you speak the truth with his neighbor" (Ephesians 4:25). James warns extensively about the destructive power of the tongue, calling it "a restless evil, full of deadly poison" (James 3:8). Yet Christian truthfulness is always to be spoken in love — truth without love becomes cruelty, while love without truth becomes sentimentality.

Key Scriptures

  • Exodus 20:16 — "You shall not bear false witness against your neighbor."
  • Ephesians 4:25 — "Therefore, having put away falsehood, let each one of you speak the truth with his neighbor, for we are members one of another."
  • Colossians 3:9 — "Do not lie to one another, seeing that you have put off the old self with its practices."
  • Proverbs 12:22 — "Lying lips are an abomination to the LORD, but those who act faithfully are his delight."
  • James 3:1-12 — "No human being can tame the tongue. It is a restless evil, full of deadly poison."

Heart of the Law

The heart of this commandment is that God is a God of truth, and His people are called to reflect His character. Satan is identified as "the father of lies" (John 8:44), and every act of deception aligns us with his character rather than God's. Truthfulness is not merely a moral preference but a reflection of reality itself — God created a world of truth, and falsehood is always a distortion of what He has made.

Living truthfully requires courage. We lie because we fear consequences, crave approval, or want to control how others perceive us. The ninth commandment calls us to trust God with the results of truth-telling — even when truth is costly, uncomfortable, or socially unpopular. It also calls us to be truthful about ourselves, refusing the false witness we often bear about our own lives through hypocrisy, image management, and spiritual pretense.

Reflection Questions

  • In what situations are you most tempted to shade the truth, exaggerate, or remain misleadingly silent?
  • How does gossip — speaking about others in ways that damage their reputation — violate this commandment?
  • What fears drive your dishonesty, and how might trusting God's sovereignty free you to be more truthful?
  • Are there relationships where you need to speak truth you have been avoiding, or confess falsehood you have spoken?
