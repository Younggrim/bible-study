Women of the Bible

Rahab

Her Story

Rahab was a prostitute in Jericho — a woman at the very bottom of the social order in a city marked for divine judgment. When Joshua's spies entered Jericho, it was Rahab who hid them, lied to the king's soldiers, and helped them escape over the city wall. Her actions were born not of mere opportunism but of genuine faith: "I know that the LORD has given you this land... for the LORD your God is God in heaven above and on earth below."

In exchange for her protection of the spies, Rahab secured a promise of safety for herself and her family when Jericho fell. The scarlet cord she hung from her window — the sign of deliverance — has become one of Scripture's most powerful symbols of redemption. When the walls of Jericho collapsed, Rahab's house alone stood firm, and she and her family were brought safely out of destruction.

Rahab's story did not end with survival. She was grafted into the nation of Israel, married Salmon, and became the mother of Boaz — who would marry Ruth and become great-grandfather of King David. She appears in the genealogy of Jesus Christ, forever honored as an ancestor of the Messiah. The prostitute of Jericho became royalty in God's Kingdom — a transformation so complete that only grace can explain it.

Key Scriptures

  • Joshua 2:11 — "The LORD your God is God in heaven above and on the earth below."
  • Hebrews 11:31 — "By faith the prostitute Rahab, because she welcomed the spies, was not killed with those who were disobedient."
  • James 2:25 — "Was not even Rahab the prostitute considered righteous for what she did when she gave lodging to the spies?"
  • Matthew 1:5 — Rahab listed in the genealogy of Jesus Christ.
  • Joshua 6:25 — "Joshua spared Rahab the prostitute, with her family... because she hid the men Joshua had sent."

Lessons from Her Life

Rahab demolishes every notion that our past disqualifies us from God's future. She was a pagan prostitute in a city under judgment, yet her faith — imperfect and mixed with deception — was counted as righteousness. God does not require a cleaned-up life before He accepts us; He accepts us in the midst of our mess and then transforms us from within. No past is too dark, no reputation too damaged, for God's redeeming grace.

Rahab also teaches us that faith is demonstrated through action. James uses her as his primary example that "faith without works is dead." Her belief in God's power was proven by her willingness to risk everything — her safety, her home, her standing in Jericho — to align herself with God's people. True faith always moves us to action, even when that action is costly and the outcome uncertain.

Reflection Questions

  • Do you believe your past disqualifies you from God's purposes? How does Rahab's story challenge that belief?
  • What bold action might God be calling you to take as evidence of your faith?
  • How does Rahab's inclusion in Jesus' genealogy shape your understanding of grace?
