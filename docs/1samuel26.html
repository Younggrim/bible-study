1 Samuel — Chapter 26

1Then the Ziphites came to Saul at Gibeah, saying, “Is not David hiding himself on the hill of Hachilah, which is on the east of Jeshimon?”

2So Saul arose and went down to the wilderness of Ziph with three thousand chosen men of Israel to seek David in the wilderness of Ziph.

3And Saul encamped on the hill of Hachilah, which is beside the road on the east of Jeshimon. But David remained in the wilderness. When he saw that Saul came after him into the wilderness,

4David sent out spies and learned that Saul had indeed come.

5Then David rose and came to the place where Saul had encamped. And David saw the place where Saul lay, with Abner the son of Ner, the commander of his army. Saul was lying within the encampment, while the army was encamped around him.

6Then David said to Ahimelech the Hittite, and to Joab’s brother Abishai the son of Zeruiah, “Who will go down with me into the camp to Saul?” And Abishai said, “I will go down with you.”

7So David and Abishai went to the army by night. And there lay Saul sleeping within the encampment, with his spear stuck in the ground at his head, and Abner and the army lay around him.

8Then Abishai said to David, “God has given your enemy into your hand this day. Now please let me pin him to the earth with one stroke of the spear, and I will not strike him twice.”

9But David said to Abishai, “Do not destroy him, for who can put out his hand against the LORD’s anointed and be guiltless?”

10And David said, “As the LORD lives, the LORD will strike him, or his day will come to die, or he will go down into battle and perish.

11The LORD forbid that I should put out my hand against the LORD’s anointed. But take now the spear that is at his head and the jar of water, and let us go.”

12So David took the spear and the jar of water from Saul’s head, and they went away. No man saw it or knew it, nor did any awake, for they were all asleep, because a deep sleep from the LORD had fallen upon them.

13Then David went over to the other side and stood far off on the top of the hill, with a great space between them.

14And David called to the army, and to Abner the son of Ner, saying, “Will you not answer, Abner?” Then Abner answered, “Who are you who calls to the king?”

15And David said to Abner, “Are you not a man? Who is like you in Israel? Why then have you not kept watch over your lord the king? For one of the people came in to destroy the king your lord.

16This thing that you have done is not good. As the LORD lives, you deserve to die, because you have not kept watch over your lord, the LORD’s anointed. And now see where the king’s spear is and the jar of water that was at his head.”

17Saul recognized David’s voice and said, “Is this your voice, my son David?” And David said, “It is my voice, my lord, O king.”

18And he said, “Why does my lord pursue after his servant? For what have I done? What evil is on my hands?

19Now therefore let my lord the king hear the words of his servant. If it is the LORD who has stirred you up against me, may he accept an offering, but if it is men, may they be cursed before the LORD, for they have driven me out this day that I should have no share in the heritage of the LORD, saying, ‘Go, serve other gods.’

20Now therefore, let not my blood fall to the earth away from the presence of the LORD, for the king of Israel has come out to seek a single flea like one who hunts a partridge in the mountains.”

21Then Saul said, “I have sinned. Return, my son David, for I will no more do you harm, because my life was precious in your eyes this day. Behold, I have acted foolishly, and have made a great mistake.”

22And David answered and said, “Here is the spear, O king! Let one of the young men come over and take it.

23The LORD rewards every man for his righteousness and his faithfulness, for the LORD gave you into my hand today, and I would not put out my hand against the LORD’s anointed.

24Behold, as your life was precious this day in my sight, so may my life be precious in the sight of the LORD, and may he deliver me out of all tribulation.”

25Then Saul said to David, “Blessed be you, my son David! You will do many things and will succeed in them.” So David went his way, and Saul returned to his place.

8Then said Abishai to David, God hath delivered thine enemy into thine hand this day: now therefore let me smite him, I pray thee, with the spear even to the earth at once, and I will not smite him the second time.

9And David said to Abishai, Destroy him not: for who can stretch forth his hand against the LORD’S anointed, and be guiltless?

14And David cried to the people, and to Abner the son of Ner, saying, Answerest thou not, Abner? Then Abner answered and said, Who art thou that criest to the king?

15And David said to Abner, Art not thou a valiant man? and who is like to thee in Israel? wherefore then hast thou not kept thy lord the king? for there came one of the people in to destroy the king thy lord.

17And Saul knew David’s voice, and said, Is this thy voice, my son David? And David said, It is my voice, my lord, O king.

If the LORD have stirred thee up against me, let him accept an offering: but if they be the children of men, cursed be they before the LORD; for they have driven me out this day from abiding in the inheritance of the LORD, saying, Go, serve other gods.

20Now therefore, let not my blood fall to the earth before the face of the LORD: for the king of Israel is come out to seek a flea, as when one doth hunt a partridge in the mountains.

21Then said Saul, I have sinned: return, my son David: for I will no more do thee harm, because my soul was precious in thine eyes this day: behold, I have played the fool, and have erred exceedingly.

22And David answered and said, Behold the king’s spear! and let one of the young men come over and fetch it.

23The LORD render to every man his righteousness and his faithfulness: for the LORD delivered thee into my hand to day, but I would not stretch forth mine hand against the LORD’S anointed.

24And, behold, as thy life was much set by this day in mine eyes, so let my life be much set by in the eyes of the LORD, and let him deliver me out of all tribulation.

1The Ziphites came to Saul at Gibeah and said, “Isn’t David hiding on the hill of Hakilah near Jeshimon?”

2So Saul arose and went down to the wilderness of Ziph, accompanied by 3,000 select men of Israel, to look for David in the wilderness of Ziph.

3Saul camped by the road on the hill of Hakilah near Jeshimon, but David was staying in the wilderness. When he realized that Saul had come to the wilderness to find him,

4David sent scouts and verified that Saul had indeed arrived.

5So David set out and went to the place where Saul was camped. David saw the place where Saul and Abner son of Ner, the general in command of his army, were sleeping. Now Saul was lying in the entrenchment, and the army was camped all around him.

6David said to Ahimelech the Hittite and Abishai son of Zeruiah, Joab’s brother, “Who will go down with me to Saul in the camp?” Abishai replied, “I will go down with you.”

7So David and Abishai approached the army at night and found Saul lying asleep in the entrenchment with his spear stuck in the ground by his head. Abner and the army were lying all around him.

8Abishai said to David, “Today God has delivered your enemy into your hands. Now let me drive the spear right through him into the ground with one swift jab! A second jab won’t be necessary!”

9But David said to Abishai, “Don’t kill him! Who can extend his hand against the Lord’s chosen one and remain guiltless?”

10David went on to say, “As the Lord lives, the Lord himself will strike him down. Either his day will come and he will die, or he will go down into battle and be swept away.

11But may the Lord prevent me from extending my hand against the Lord’s chosen one! Now take the spear by Saul’s head and the jug of water, and let’s get out of here!”

12So David took the spear and the jug of water by Saul’s head, and they got out of there. No one saw them or was aware of their presence or woke up. All of them were asleep, for the Lord had caused a deep sleep to fall on them.

13Then David crossed to the other side and stood on the top of the hill some distance away; there was a considerable distance between them.

14David called to the army and to Abner son of Ner, “Won’t you answer, Abner?” Abner replied, “Who are you, that you have called to the king?”

15David said to Abner, “Aren’t you a man? After all, who is like you in Israel? Why then haven’t you protected your lord the king? One of the soldiers came to kill your lord the king.

16This failure on your part isn’t good! As surely as the Lord lives, you people who have not protected your lord, the Lord’s chosen one, are as good as dead! Now look where the king’s spear and the jug of water that was by his head are!”

17When Saul recognized David’s voice, he said, “Is that your voice, my son David?” David replied, “Yes, it’s my voice, my lord the king.”

18He went on to say, “Why is my lord chasing his servant? What have I done? What wrong have I done?

19So let my lord the king now listen to the words of his servant. If the Lord has incited you against me, may he take delight in an offering. But if men have instigated this, may they be cursed before the Lord! For they have driven me away this day from being united with the Lord’s inheritance, saying, ‘Go on, serve other gods!’

20Now don’t let my blood fall to the ground away from the Lord’s presence, for the king of Israel has gone out to look for a flea the way one looks for a partridge in the hill country.”

21Saul replied, “I have sinned. Come back, my son David. I won’t harm you anymore, for you treated my life with value this day. I have behaved foolishly and have made a very terrible mistake!”

22David replied, “Here is the king’s spear! Let one of your servants cross over and get it.

23The Lord rewards each man for his integrity and loyalty. Even though today the Lord delivered you into my hand, I was not willing to extend my hand against the Lord’s chosen one.

24In the same way that I valued your life this day, may the Lord value my life and deliver me from all danger.”

25Saul replied to David, “May you be rewarded, my son David! You will without question be successful!” So David went on his way, and Saul returned to his place.

Reflection

Summary

David again spares Saul's life, this time stealing his spear and water jug while Saul sleeps in his camp. Saul again confesses his sin, but David wisely retreats rather than trust Saul's unstable repentance.

Key Verses

  • v.9 — "Who can stretch forth his hand against the LORD's anointed, and be guiltless?" — David's consistent principle, now stated for the second time. No one can harm God's chosen and escape judgment. This protects Saul now — and will protect David later. The principle is universal: God defends those He has appointed.
  • v.10 — "The LORD shall smite him; or his day shall come to die; or he shall descend into battle, and perish." — David's theology of divine timing. God has three options for removing Saul: direct judgment, natural death, or death in battle. David does not need to be the instrument. This is prophetic — Saul will indeed "descend into battle, and perish" (ch.31).
  • v.12 — "A deep sleep from the LORD was fallen upon them." — God orchestrates the entire scene. The deep sleep (tardemah — the same word used of Adam in Genesis 2:21 and Abraham in Genesis 15:12) is supernatural. God puts Saul's army to sleep so that David can demonstrate his mercy — and so that Saul can have no excuse.
  • v.21 — "I have sinned... I have played the fool, and have erred exceedingly." — Saul's most complete confession. He uses three terms: sinned (chata'ti), played the fool (niskalti), erred exceedingly (shagiti harbeh). Yet this confession, like his tears in chapter 24, produces no lasting change. Words of repentance without works of repentance are empty.
  • v.23 — "The LORD render to every man his righteousness and his faithfulness." — David's final word to Saul is a commitment to divine justice. He trusts God to evaluate and reward both his own faithfulness and Saul's unfaithfulness. This is the ultimate expression of faith: leaving the final verdict to God.

Authorship & Background

  • Author: Unknown, traditionally attributed to Samuel (for the early chapters), Nathan, and Gad (1 Chronicles 29:29). Originally one book with 2 Samuel in the Hebrew Bible. Written approximately 1050-950 BC. The book covers the transition from the judges to the monarchy — from the birth of Samuel through the death of Saul (approximately 1105-1010 BC). Key themes: God's sovereignty in raising and removing leaders, the danger of rejecting God's rule, the contrast between Saul (man's choice) and David (God's choice), and the principle that 'man looketh on the outward appearance, but the LORD looketh on the heart' (16:7).
  • Historical Context: Chapter 26 parallels chapter 24 — David spares Saul a second time. The Ziphites again betray David's location (as in 23:19). Saul again pursues with 3,000 men. David again has the opportunity to kill Saul and again refuses. But the circumstances differ: in chapter 24, Saul entered David's cave unknowingly; here, David deliberately infiltrates Saul's camp at night. God causes a "deep sleep" to fall on Saul's army (v.12) — divine intervention that places Saul completely in David's power. Abishai urges David to let him kill Saul with one spear-thrust (v.8), but David refuses with the same principle: "who can stretch forth his hand against the LORD's anointed, and be guiltless?" (v.9). Instead of killing Saul, David takes his spear and water jug as proof. He then calls out from a safe distance, rebukes Abner for failing to guard the king, and confronts Saul with the evidence of his mercy. David's theology is explicit: "the LORD shall smite him; or his day shall come to die; or he shall descend into battle, and perish" (v.10) — God will remove Saul in His own time and way. David will not preempt God's judgment. Saul's response — "I have sinned... I have played the fool" (v.21) — is his last recorded words to David. They part permanently. David concludes that Saul will never stop pursuing him and decides to flee to Philistia (ch.27).
  • David Infiltrates Saul's Camp (vv.1-12): The Ziphites betray David again (v.1). Saul pursues with 3,000 men and camps at Hachilah (v.3). David scouts the camp and sees Saul sleeping in the center with Abner nearby and the army around him (v.5). He asks for a volunteer: Abishai (Joab's brother, David's nephew) agrees to go (v.6). They enter the camp at night — Saul sleeps with his spear stuck in the ground at his head and a water jug beside him (v.7). Abishai sees divine providence: "God hath delivered thine enemy into thine hand this day" (v.8) — the same interpretation David's men gave in the cave (24:4). He asks permission to kill Saul with one stroke. David refuses absolutely: "Destroy him not: for who can stretch forth his hand against the LORD's anointed, and be guiltless?" (v.9). David's theology of God's timing is explicit: God will strike Saul, or he will die naturally, or he will fall in battle (v.10). David takes only the spear and water jug (v.11). No one wakes — "a deep sleep from the LORD was fallen upon them" (v.12). God Himself arranged this test and this protection.
  • David Confronts Abner and Saul (vv.13-20): From a safe distance, David calls out to Abner (v.14). He rebukes Abner for failing to guard the king: "ye are worthy to die, because ye have not kept your master, the LORD's anointed" (v.16). The missing spear and water jug prove David's point. Saul recognizes David's voice: "Is this thy voice, my son David?" (v.17). David's speech raises two possibilities: either God has stirred Saul against him (in which case, let God accept an offering to end it) or men have stirred Saul (in which case, they are cursed for driving David from "the inheritance of the LORD" — the promised land — effectively saying "Go, serve other gods" (v.19). This is a profound theological complaint: being driven from Israel means being driven from God's presence and worship.
  • Saul's Final Words to David (vv.21-25): Saul confesses: "I have sinned... I have played the fool, and have erred exceedingly" (v.21). He invites David to return. David does not return — he has heard these promises before (24:16-22). Instead, he sends for the spear (v.22) and declares his principle: "The LORD render to every man his righteousness and his faithfulness" (v.23). He trusts God to reward his restraint. Saul's final words to David are a blessing: "Blessed be thou, my son David: thou shalt both do great things, and also shalt still prevail" (v.25). They part — permanently. David goes his way; Saul returns to his place. They will never speak again.

Map & Geography

  • Wilderness of Ziph: The hill of Hachilah, south of Jeshimon — Saul camps here with 3,000 men pursuing David
  • David enters Saul's camp at night: The trench/barricade of wagons surrounds the sleeping army
  • This is the second time David spares Saul (first was En-gedi, ch.24) — both in the Judean wilderness

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): enduringword.com Guzik notes that David's second refusal to kill Saul is even more remarkable than the first — he deliberately enters the camp, has complete control, and still refuses. He emphasizes David's three options for Saul's death (v.10) as showing David's trust that God has multiple ways to accomplish His purposes without David's help.
  • Charles Spurgeon: "'The LORD shall smite him; or his day shall come to die; or he shall descend into battle, and perish.' Here is David's creed concerning his enemy: God will deal with him. I need not. The Lord has a thousand ways to remove the wicked — by His own hand, by natural death, by the sword of battle. Why should I stain my hands when God has so many instruments at His disposal? Let us learn this holy patience. Our enemies are in God's hands, not ours. He will deal with them in His time and way — and His way is always better than ours."

Translations

  • v.5 → KJV: "Saul lay in the trench" → ESV: "Saul was lying within the encampment" — Hebrew "ba-ma'gal." The same word as 17:20. It refers to the circular arrangement of the camp (wagons or soldiers in a circle with the commander in the center). KJV "trench" and ESV "encampment" reflect different interpretations of the same word.
  • v.7 → KJV: "at his bolster" → ESV: "at his head" — Hebrew "me-ra'ashotav" = at his head-place. KJV "bolster" (pillow/ headrest) is an interpretation; ESV "at his head" is more literal. The spear was stuck in the ground near where Saul's head lay.
  • v.11 → KJV: "the cruse of water" → ESV: "the jar of water" — Hebrew "tsappachat ha-mayim." "Tsappachat" = a flask, jug, or jar. KJV "cruse" is archaic for a small container; ESV "jar" is clearer.
  • v.19 → KJV: "from abiding in the inheritance of the LORD" → ESV: "that I should have no share in the heritage of the LORD" — Hebrew "me-histappeach be-nachalat Yahweh." David's complaint is theological: being driven from Israel means being driven from God's land, God's worship, God's presence. Both translations capture this, though ESV's "no share in the heritage" emphasizes the disinheritance more strongly.
  • v.21 → KJV: "I have played the fool" → ESV: "I have acted foolishly" — Hebrew "niskalti" from "sakal" = to be foolish, to act stupidly. KJV's "played the fool" is more vivid and memorable; ESV's "acted foolishly" is more literal. Both capture Saul's self-assessment — he knows his pursuit of David is irrational.

Reflection

  • 1. Consistency in principle proves character (vv.9-11). David refuses to kill Saul a second time — same principle, same restraint, same trust in God. Character is not proven by a single decision but by consistent decisions over time. Are you as faithful to your principles the second time as the first?
  • 2. God has many ways to accomplish His purposes (v.10). David lists three ways God might remove Saul — and refuses to add a fourth (his own hand). God does not need our help to execute His plans. When we try to "help God" through sinful means, we only complicate His work and stain our own hands.
  • 3. Confession without change is not repentance (v.21). Saul says "I have sinned" — again. But nothing changes. True repentance is not words but transformation. If you find yourself confessing the same sin repeatedly without change, the problem is not your confession but your will. Repentance means turning, not just talking.
  • 4. Being driven from God's presence is the deepest pain (v.19). David's greatest grief is not physical danger but spiritual exile — being cut off from "the inheritance of the LORD." When circumstances separate us from worship, community, and God's felt presence, that is the real suffering. Pursue God's presence above all else.
  • 5. Trust God's timing even when it seems slow (v.23). David has now spared Saul twice. He could have ended his suffering years ago. But he trusts: "The LORD render to every man his righteousness." God's timing may seem slow, but it is never wrong. What He promises, He will perform — in His time, not ours.
