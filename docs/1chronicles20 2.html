1 Chronicles — Chapter 20

1In the spring of the year, the time when kings go out to battle, Joab led out the army and ravaged the country of the Ammonites and came and besieged Rabbah. But David remained at Jerusalem. And Joab struck down Rabbah and overthrew it.

2And David took the crown of their king from his head. He found that it weighed a talent of gold, and in it was a precious stone. And it was placed on David’s head. And he brought out the spoil of the city, a very great amount.

3And he brought out the people who were in it and set them to labor with saws and iron picks and axes. And thus David did to all the cities of the Ammonites. Then David and all the people returned to Jerusalem.

4And after this there arose war with the Philistines at Gezer. Then Sibbecai the Hushathite struck down Sippai, who was one of the descendants of the giants, and the Philistines were subdued.

5And there was again war with the Philistines, and Elhanan the son of Jair struck down Lahmi the brother of Goliath the Gittite, the shaft of whose spear was like a weaver’s beam.

6And there was again war at Gath, where there was a man of great stature, who had six fingers on each hand and six toes on each foot, twenty-four in number, and he also was descended from the giants.

7And when he taunted Israel, Jonathan the son of Shimea, David’s brother, struck him down.

8These were descended from the giants in Gath, and they fell by the hand of David and by the hand of his servants.

Summary

Joab besieges and captures Rabbah, the Ammonite capital, and David takes the king's crown. Additional Philistine wars are recorded, including the slaying of giants by David's warriors, echoing David's own victory over Goliath.

Key Verses

v.1
"And it came to pass, that after the year was expired, at the time that kings go out to battle, Joab led forth the power of the army, and wasted the country of the children of Ammon, and came and besieged Rabbah. But David tarried at Jerusalem." (KJV) — For readers of Samuel, "David tarried at Jerusalem" is loaded with tragic irony — this is when the Bathsheba incident occurred. The Chronicler includes the phrase but moves immediately past it. Theologically, the verse shows that Joab faithfully executed David's military campaigns while David remained in the capital. The work of the kingdom continued even when the king was not personally present.
v.2
"And David took the crown of their king from off his head, and found it to weigh a talent of gold, and there were precious stones in it; and it was set upon David's head: and he brought also exceeding much spoil out of the city." (KJV) — The crown weighing a talent of gold (approximately 75 pounds) was likely a ceremonial piece suspended above the throne rather than worn constantly. David takes the enemy's crown — a symbol of transferred sovereignty. The "exceeding much spoil" would later be designated for Temple construction (cf. 1 Chronicles 22:14), connecting military victory to worship preparation.
v.5
"And there was war again with the Philistines; and Elhanan the son of Jair slew Lahmi the brother of Goliath the Gittite, whose spear staff was like a weaver's beam." (KJV) — This verse resolves a famous textual difficulty in 2 Samuel 21:19, which in some manuscripts appears to say Elhanan killed Goliath himself. Chronicles clarifies: he killed Lahmi, the brother of Goliath. The "weaver's beam" comparison for the spear shaft indicates enormous size — the same description given of Goliath's spear in 1 Samuel 17:7. The giant tradition continued in Goliath's family.
v.8
"These were born unto the giant in Gath; and they fell by the hand of David, and by the hand of his servants." (KJV) — The summary statement attributes all giant-killing to both David and his servants. Though David did not personally slay these three, his leadership enabled his men's victories. This principle applies to spiritual leadership: a faithful leader raises up warriors who can defeat giants the leader himself does not personally face. David's legacy includes the victories of those he trained.
v.3
"And he brought out the people that were in it, and cut them with saws, and with harrows of iron, and with axes. Even so dealt David with all the cities of the children of Ammon." (KJV) — This difficult verse describes the subjugation of the Ammonite population. Some scholars read this as forced labor (the ESV renders "set them to labor with saws") rather than execution. The Hebrew admits both readings. In the ancient Near East, conquered peoples were commonly pressed into labor gangs for building projects — consistent with David's massive material preparations for the Temple.

Cross-References

  • v.1 — 2 Samuel 11:1 — "At the time when kings go out to battle... David tarried still at Jerusalem." Samuel uses this identical phrase to introduce the Bathsheba narrative. The Chronicler uses it and moves on — the most significant editorial silence in the Old Testament.
  • v.1 — 2 Samuel 12:26-31 — The parallel account of Rabbah's capture, where Joab calls David to come take the city personally so that the victory would be credited to the king. Chronicles compresses this into a single verse.
  • v.2 — 1 Chronicles 22:14 — David's preparation of "an hundred thousand talents of gold" for the Temple. The "exceeding much spoil" from Rabbah and other conquests became the material foundation for Temple construction.
  • v.4 — 2 Samuel 21:18-22 — The parallel account of the Philistine giant encounters. Samuel records four incidents; Chronicles records three (omitting the one involving David personally being rescued by Abishai, since it showed David's weakness).
  • v.5 — 1 Samuel 17:7 — "The staff of his [Goliath's] spear was like a weaver's beam." The same description applied to Goliath's brother Lahmi demonstrates that enormous physical power ran in this family. What Goliath was, his brother was also.
  • v.6 — Genesis 6:4 — "There were giants (Nephilim) in the earth in those days." The Rephaim/giants of Gath represent the continuation of the oversized warrior tradition. David's kingdom systematically eliminates this ancient threat.
  • v.8 — Numbers 13:33 — "We saw the giants, the sons of Anak... and we were in our own sight as grasshoppers." Israel's original failure at Kadesh-Barnea was caused by fear of giants. Under David, giants are routinely defeated — faith has replaced fear.
  • v.8 — Romans 8:37 — "In all these things we are more than conquerors through him that loved us." David's servants conquered giants not by their own superior size but through the anointed king's authority. Believers conquer through Christ's authority likewise.

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/1-chronicles-20/ Guzik highlights the Chronicler's deliberate omission of the Bathsheba-Uriah narrative as a key interpretive feature. He notes that this is not dishonesty but a different purpose: Chronicles presents David as the covenant king whose reign establishes the pattern for Temple worship. Guzik also observes that the giant- killing passages show David raising up a generation of warriors who could do what only he had done before.
  • Charles Spurgeon: "David's mighty men slew giants because David had shown them how. When the shepherd boy walked into the Valley of Elah with a sling and five smooth stones, he was not only winning a battle — he was training a generation. Sibbechai, Elhanan, Jonathan — these men learned giant-killing from their king. So it is with Christ and His church: our Lord has conquered every principality and power, and now His servants go forth in His name to slay the giants that remain. You face Goliaths of fear, of temptation, of despair? Remember that your King has already shown the way. Take up His weapons — faith, prayer, the Word — and strike. The giant will fall, for he falls 'by the hand of David, and by the hand of his servants.'"

Translations

  • v.1 — KJV: "after the year was expired" / ESV: "In the spring of the year" — Hebrew "litshuvat hashanah" literally means "at the return/turning of the year." KJV interprets this as a completed cycle; ESV follows the understanding that this refers to spring — when rains ceased, roads dried, and military campaigns became feasible. The ESV rendering is widely accepted as the intended sense: ancient Near Eastern armies campaigned in spring and summer.
  • v.1 — KJV: "wasted the country" / ESV: "ravaged the country" — Hebrew "yashcheth" means to destroy, ruin, lay waste. Both translations convey systematic devastation of Ammonite territory. ESV's "ravaged" is slightly more vivid for modern readers; KJV's "wasted" carries the same force in its 17th-century usage.
  • v.3 — KJV: "cut them with saws, and with harrows of iron, and with axes" / ESV: "set them to labor with saws and iron picks and axes" — This is a significant translation difference. Hebrew "vayyasar bamgérah" can mean either "he cut with the saw" or "he set [them to work] with the saw." KJV reads execution/torture; ESV reads forced labor. The ESV interpretation is supported by the parallel in 2 Samuel 12:31 and the context of David's massive building preparations. The Hebrew consonants allow both readings.
  • v.4 — KJV: "the children of the giant" / ESV: "the descendants of the giants" — Hebrew "ylidé harapha." "Rapha" (or Rephaim) refers to an ancient race of giant warriors. KJV's singular "the giant" could suggest one specific ancestor; ESV's plural "the giants" treats this as a clan designation. The Hebrew is ambiguous — "harapha" with the article could be either a personal name or a group identifier.
  • v.5 — KJV: "Elhanan the son of Jair slew Lahmi the brother of Goliath" / ESV: "Elhanan the son of Jair struck down Lahmi the brother of Goliath" — Both translations include "Lahmi the brother of" — clarifying the textual confusion in 2 Samuel 21:19. The Hebrew here explicitly names the victim as "Lahmi" (lachmi) and identifies him as Goliath's brother, resolving the apparent contradiction with David killing Goliath in 1 Samuel 17.
  • v.6 — KJV: "whose fingers and toes were four and twenty, six on each hand, and six on each foot" / ESV: "who had six fingers on each hand and six toes on each foot, twenty- four in number" — Both translations convey the same information with different word order. The Hebrew describes polydactyly — a genetic condition associated with the Rephaim/giant lineage. The detail authenticates the account with specificity that a fabricator would not include.

Reflection

  • 1. God's editorial silence teaches as powerfully as His speech (v.1). The Chronicler's deliberate omission of Bathsheba-Uriah reminds us that God is not obligated to rehearse our forgiven sins perpetually. When God forgives, He removes transgressions "as far as the east is from the west" (Psalm 103:12). While Samuel records the sin for instructive purposes, Chronicles shows that God's purposes for David continued unbroken despite the failure. Your worst chapter does not define your entire story in God's economy. Grace covers, restores, and moves forward.
  • 2. Leaders multiply their victories through those they train (v.8). "They fell by the hand of David, and by the hand of his servants." David's greatest legacy was not the giants he killed personally but the giant-killers he raised up. True leadership is not measured by personal accomplishments but by the accomplishments of those you equip. Are you raising up others who can fight battles you will not always be present to fight? Invest in people, train them in faith, show them how to face giants — and your influence will outlast your own strength.
  • 3. Giants come in families (vv.4-7). Three separate giants from the same lineage appear — defeating one did not eliminate the others. Temptation works similarly: related sins cluster together. Defeat one form of pride, and another manifestation appears. Overcome one expression of lust, and a cousin temptation emerges. Do not be discouraged when new giants appear after old victories. Stay vigilant, stay armed, and recognize that the same faith that conquered the first will conquer the others.
  • 4. Every giant has a weakness (vv.4-7). Despite their terrifying size, every giant fell to a normal-sized warrior empowered by God. No enemy is invincible. The six-fingered giant, Goliath's brother, Sippai — each was formidable, each was mortal. Whatever oversized problem towers over your life, it has a point of vulnerability. Faith does not deny the giant's size; faith affirms God's greater power. "Greater is he that is in you, than he that is in the world" (1 John 4:4).
  • 5. Faithful service continues even when leadership fails (v.1). Joab led the army, besieged Rabbah, and completed the campaign while David remained in Jerusalem during his greatest moral failure. The work of God does not stop when leaders stumble. If you are serving under imperfect leadership (and all earthly leadership is imperfect), your faithfulness is to God's mission, not to human perfection. Continue your work, do your duty, serve the kingdom — God's purposes advance through faithful servants regardless of what the leader is doing privately.
