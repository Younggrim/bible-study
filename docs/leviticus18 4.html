Summary

God forbids sexual immorality in all its forms — incest, adultery, homosexuality, and bestiality — distinguishing Israel from the surrounding nations.

Key Verses

  • v.2 — "I am the LORD your God." — The foundation of all ethics. God's identity grounds His commands.
  • v.3 — "You shall not do as they do in the land of Egypt, where you lived, and you shall not do as they do in the land of Canaan, to which I am bringing you." (ESV) — God's people are distinct from the cultures they leave and the cultures they enter.
  • v.5 — "You shall therefore keep my statutes and my rules; if a person does them, he shall live by them: I am the LORD." (ESV) — God's commands are life-giving. Obedience leads to life.
  • v.22 — "You shall not lie with a male as with a woman; it is an abomination." (ESV) — Clear, unambiguous prohibition.
  • v.25 — "And the land became unclean, so that I punished its iniquity, and the land vomited out its inhabitants." (ESV) — Sin defiles the land itself. The land responds to moral corruption.
  • v.28 — "lest the land vomit you out when you make it unclean, as it vomited out the nation that was before you." (ESV) — Israel is not exempt. The same sins bring the same consequences.

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): enduringword.com Guzik emphasizes the framing: Israel must be different from both Egypt and Canaan. He notes that the inclusion of Molech worship among sexual sins shows the connection between sexual immorality and idolatry. He highlights the personification of the land — creation itself responds to moral corruption.
  • Charles Spurgeon: Sermon: "Separation from the World" "God says to His people: you shall not do as Egypt does, and you shall not do as Canaan does. You came FROM the world, and you live IN the world, but you are not OF the world. The world behind you practiced these things — do not look back. The world before you practices these things — do not conform. You are a peculiar people, a holy nation, set apart for God. The standard is not what the culture permits but what God commands. And His commands are not burdensome — they are LIFE. 'Which if a man do, he shall live in them.' God's boundaries are not prison walls; they are garden walls — keeping death out and life in."

Translations

  • v.3 → KJV: "After the doings of the land of Egypt" / ESV: "You shall not do as they do in the land of Egypt" — Hebrew "k'ma'aseh eretz mitzrayim... lo ta'asu" — according to the deeds of the land of Egypt you shall not do. KJV preserves the Hebrew word order; ESV restructures for clarity. Both convey the same prohibition.
  • v.5 → KJV: "he shall live in them" / ESV: "he shall live by them" — Hebrew "va-chai ba-hem" — and he shall live in/by them. The preposition "ba" can mean "in" or "by." Paul quotes this in Romans 10:5 and Galatians 3:12 to contrast law-righteousness with faith-righteousness.
  • v.6 → KJV: "to uncover their nakedness" / ESV: "to uncover nakedness" — Hebrew "l'galot ervah" — to uncover nakedness. A euphemism for sexual relations. "Ervah" (nakedness/exposure) becomes the key term throughout the chapter. The phrase occurs over 20 times in this chapter alone.
  • v.17 → KJV: "it is wickedness" / ESV: "it is depravity" — Hebrew "zimmah" — a plan, scheme, or heinous sin. Implies deliberate, calculated wickedness. ESV's "depravity" captures the intentional nature.
  • v.21 → KJV: "let any of thy seed pass through the fire to Molech" / ESV: "give any of your children to offer them to Molech" — Hebrew "l'ha'avir la-Molekh" — to cause to pass over to Molech. KJV adds "the fire" (interpretive addition based on other passages like 2 Kings 23:10). ESV is more literal to this verse. Molech worship involved child sacrifice by fire.
  • v.22 → KJV: "it is abomination" / ESV: "it is an abomination" — Hebrew "to'evah" — an abomination, something detestable. A strong term of moral revulsion. Used for idolatry (Deuteronomy 7:25), dishonest business (Proverbs 11:1), and sexual perversion. Not merely a ritual category but a moral one.
  • v.23 → KJV: "it is confusion" / ESV: "it is perversion" — Hebrew "tevel" — confusion, mixing, perversion. The word implies a violation of created order — a mixing of what God has separated. KJV's "confusion" captures the disorder; ESV's "perversion" captures the moral weight.
  • v.25 → KJV: "the land itself vomiteth out her inhabitants" / ESV: "the land vomited out its inhabitants" — Hebrew "va-taqi ha-aretz et yosh'veha" — the land vomited out its inhabitants. Vivid personification: the land is nauseated by moral corruption and expels those who practice it. The same verb is used for physical vomiting.

Reflection

  • 1. "After the doings of Egypt... after the doings of Canaan... shall ye not do" (v.3). You came FROM a culture and you live IN a culture — but you are not defined by either. The standard for God's people is not "what everyone else does" but "what God says." Where are you conforming to the culture you came from or the culture you live in rather than to God's Word?
  • 2. "Which if a man do, he shall live in them" (v.5). God's commands are not arbitrary restrictions — they are the path to LIFE. Every boundary God sets is a protection. The fence is not a prison wall; it is a guardrail on a cliff. Obedience leads to flourishing; transgression leads to destruction.
  • 3. "I am the LORD" — repeated throughout. God does not negotiate His standards. He does not adjust them to cultural trends. He does not explain Himself to satisfy human curiosity. He declares: "I am the LORD." His character is the unchanging standard. Are you adjusting God's Word to fit your culture, or adjusting your life to fit God's Word?
  • 4. "The land itself vomiteth out her inhabitants" (v.25). Sin has consequences that extend beyond the individual. Moral corruption affects families, communities, nations, and even the physical land. The Canaanites were expelled for these very sins. Israel was later exiled for the same. No nation is exempt from moral accountability.
  • 5. The Canaanites' sin was "not yet full" in Abraham's day (Genesis 15:16). God waited 400 years before judging them. He is patient — but patience has a limit. The same sins that filled the Canaanites' cup will fill any nation's cup. God's patience is not permission.
