Parables of Jesus

The Lost Sheep

The Parable

Jesus asked the Pharisees and scribes a question that seemed absurd on the surface: "What man of you, having a hundred sheep, if he has lost one of them, does not leave the ninety-nine in the open country, and go after the one that is lost, until he finds it?" In purely economic terms, risking ninety-nine sheep for one stray makes no business sense. A rational shepherd would cut his losses. But this shepherd is not driven by economics — he is driven by love for the individual.

The shepherd searches relentlessly — not casually glancing around, but actively pursuing "until he finds it." When he does, he doesn't scold the sheep or drag it home begrudgingly. He lays it on his shoulders, rejoicing. He comes home and calls together his friends and neighbors saying, "Rejoice with me, for I have found my sheep that was lost." The image is one of celebration, not obligation — of joy, not mere duty.

Jesus delivered the punchline: "Just so, I tell you, there will be more joy in heaven over one sinner who repents than over ninety-nine righteous persons who need no repentance." This was a direct challenge to the Pharisees, who saw sinners as contamination to avoid rather than people to pursue. Heaven's priorities are radically different from religious bookkeeping.

Key Scriptures

  • Luke 15:3-7 — The shepherd leaves the ninety-nine to find the one — heaven rejoices over one sinner who repents.
  • Matthew 18:12-14 — "It is not the will of my Father who is in heaven that one of these little ones should perish."
  • John 10:11-14 — "I am the good shepherd. The good shepherd lays down his life for the sheep." Jesus identifies Himself as this shepherd.
  • Ezekiel 34:11-12 — "I myself will search for my sheep and will seek them out... in a day of clouds and thick darkness."
  • Isaiah 53:6 — "All we like sheep have gone astray; we have turned — every one — to his own way; and the LORD has laid on him the iniquity of us all."

What Jesus Was Teaching

Jesus was revealing God's heart for the individual. In a world that values efficiency and percentages, God counts by ones. He doesn't manage populations — He pursues persons. The lost sheep didn't find its own way home; the shepherd went after it. This is the gospel in miniature: salvation is not primarily about human seeking but about divine pursuit. "We love because he first loved us" (1 John 4:19). Before we ever turned toward God, He was already coming for us through hill and valley.

Jesus was also rebuking the Pharisees' attitude toward sinners. They grumbled that He received tax collectors and ate with them. In their theology, holiness meant separation from unclean people. But Jesus demonstrated that true holiness moves toward brokenness, not away from it. A doctor goes to the sick, not the healthy. The shepherd goes to the wilderness, not the pen. The Kingdom of God advances through pursuit, compassion, and joy — not through walls, exclusion, and contempt.

Reflection Questions

  • Have you experienced being the "lost sheep" — wandering from God and being pursued by His relentless grace? What did that look like?
  • Is there someone in your life who has wandered, and God might be calling you to go after them with patience and love?
  • How does the shepherd's joy at finding the lost sheep challenge any idea that God is reluctant or annoyed when we return to Him?
  • Do you tend to focus on the ninety-nine (those already "in") or the one (those still outside)? How might God be shifting your perspective?
