1 Chronicles — Chapter 13

1David consulted with the commanders of thousands and of hundreds, with every leader.

2And David said to all the assembly of Israel, “If it seems good to you and from the LORD our God, let us send abroad to our brothers who remain in all the lands of Israel, as well as to the priests and Levites in the cities that have pasturelands, that they may be gathered to us.

3Then let us bring again the ark of our God to us, for we did not seek it in the days of Saul.”

4All the assembly agreed to do so, for the thing was right in the eyes of all the people.

5So David assembled all Israel from the Nile of Egypt to Lebo-hamath, to bring the ark of God from Kiriath-jearim.

6And David and all Israel went up to Baalah, that is, to Kiriath-jearim that belongs to Judah, to bring up from there the ark of God, which is called by the name of the LORD who sits enthroned above the cherubim.

7And they carried the ark of God on a new cart, from the house of Abinadab, and Uzzah and Ahio were driving the cart.

8And David and all Israel were celebrating before God with all their might, with song and lyres and harps and tambourines and cymbals and trumpets.

9And when they came to the threshing floor of Chidon, Uzzah put out his hand to take hold of the ark, for the oxen stumbled.

10And the anger of the LORD was kindled against Uzzah, and he struck him down because he put out his hand to the ark, and he died there before God.

11And David was angry because the LORD had broken out against Uzzah. And that place is called Perez-uzza to this day.

12And David was afraid of God that day, and he said, “How can I bring the ark of God home to me?”

13So David did not take the ark home into the city of David, but took it aside to the house of Obed-edom the Gittite.

14And the ark of God remained with the household of Obed-edom in his house three months. And the LORD blessed the household of Obed-edom and all that he had.

9When they arrived at the threshing floor of Kidon, Uzzah reached out his hand to take hold of the ark because the oxen stumbled.

Summary

David's first attempt to bring the ark of God from Kiriath-jearim to Jerusalem ends in tragedy when Uzzah touches the ark and is struck dead. David, fearful, leaves the ark at the house of Obed-edom where it brings blessing for three months.

Key Verses

v.3
"Then let us bring again the ark of our God to us, for we did not seek it in the days of Saul." (ESV) — David identifies the problem of Saul's reign: God's presence was neglected. The ark sat forgotten in Kiriath-jearim for approximately 70 years. David's first act as king is to restore what Saul ignored. The contrast with chapter 10 is explicit: Saul "enquired not of the LORD" (10:14); David wants to "enquire at" the ark.
v.7
"And they carried the ark of God on a new cart, from the house of Abinadab" (ESV) — The critical error. A "new cart" seems respectful — fresh, dedicated, clean. But God had specified that the ark be carried on Levitical shoulders with poles (Exodus 25:14-15; Numbers 7:9). Innovation in worship, however well-meaning, is not the same as obedience. They copied Philistine methodology (1 Samuel 6:7) instead of consulting God's word.
v.10
"And the anger of the LORD was kindled against Uzzah, and he struck him down because he put out his hand to the ark, and he died there before God." (ESV) — A terrifying moment. Uzzah's instinct was natural — steady the falling ark. But God's holiness is not governed by human instinct. The ark was not falling because God couldn't hold it; the whole situation was wrong. Uzzah's death was the consequence of systematic disobedience (the cart), not merely his individual reflex.
v.11
"And David was angry because the LORD had broken out against Uzzah." (ESV) — David's honest emotional response: anger, displeasure, frustration. Even godly people can initially respond to God's actions with confusion and anger. But David's anger gives way to fear (v.12), and fear eventually gives way to obedience (ch.15).
v.14
"And the ark of God remained with the household of Obed-edom in his house three months. And the LORD blessed the household of Obed-edom and all that he had." (ESV) — God's presence blesses those who host it properly. While the ark was dangerous when mishandled, it was a source of abundant blessing when received with respect. The same God who struck Uzzah blessed Obed-edom.

Cross-References

  • v.5 — 1 Samuel 7:1-2 — The ark at Kiriath-jearim in the house of Abinadab "and all the house of Israel lamented after the LORD." (ESV) The ark had been there since the Philistines returned it — approximately 70 years of neglect.
  • v.7 — Numbers 4:15; 7:9 — God's explicit command: "but they shall not touch the holy things, lest they die." (ESV) The method was prescribed; the cart violated it directly.
  • v.7 — 1 Samuel 6:7-12 — The Philistines returned the ark on a new cart pulled by cows. God tolerated this from pagans who knew no better. But Israel had God's written instructions and was held to a higher standard.
  • v.9 — 2 Samuel 6:6-7 — The parallel account. 2 Samuel names the threshing floor "Nachon"; Chronicles calls it "Chidon." Both names are otherwise unknown — likely the same place with variant traditions of its name.
  • v.11 — 1 Chronicles 15:13 — David later acknowledges the real problem: "the LORD our God broke out against us, because we did not seek him according to the rule." (ESV) The issue was method, not motive.
  • v.14 — 2 Samuel 6:11 — The parallel account confirms the blessing on Obed-edom's house. This blessing encouraged David to complete the transfer of the ark (chapter 15).

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/1-chronicles-13/ Guzik emphasizes that Uzzah's death was not arbitrary divine anger but the consequence of systemic disobedience. David's good intentions did not sanctify wrong methods. God's holiness demands that worship be conducted according to His revealed will, not human innovation or convenience.
  • Charles Spurgeon: "The road to hell is paved with good intentions — and the road to Perez-uzzah was paved with the same material. David meant well. The cart was new. The music was enthusiastic. The people were united. Everything seemed right — except it was wrong by God's Word. A new cart is no substitute for Levitical shoulders. Human innovation in worship, however sincere, however popular, however joyful, is no substitute for obedience to God's prescribed order. Learn this, churches of God: enthusiasm without obedience is dangerous. Zeal without knowledge kills."

Translations

  • v.5 — KJV: "from Shihor of Egypt even unto the entering of Hemath" / ESV: "from the Nile of Egypt to Lebo-hamath" — "Shihor" is the eastern branch of the Nile (or a border brook near Egypt). "Hemath/Lebo-hamath" is the northern boundary of Israel's ideal territory. KJV transliterates; ESV translates. The phrase means "from the southern to the northern border" — all Israel.
  • v.9 — KJV: "the threshingfloor of Chidon" / ESV: "the threshing floor of Chidon" — 2 Samuel 6:6 calls this "Nachon." The discrepancy may reflect variant names for the same place, or a textual corruption in one account. "Chidon" may mean "javelin/destruction"; "Nachon" may mean "fixed/prepared."
  • v.10 — KJV: "he smote him" / ESV: "he struck him down" — Hebrew "vayakkehu" (and He struck him). Simple, devastating. The same verb used for God striking enemies in battle is used for God striking Uzzah. God's holiness makes no distinction between well-meaning Israelites and hostile enemies when His commands are violated.
  • v.11 — KJV: "David was displeased" / ESV: "David was angry" — Hebrew "vayichar" (to burn, to be angry/displeased). KJV's "displeased" is softer; ESV's "angry" is more accurate to the Hebrew heat of the word. David's response was genuine anger — the same word used for God's anger in v.10. David was angry at God — an honest but dangerous emotion.
  • v.11 — KJV: "Perez-uzza" / ESV: "Perez-uzza" — "Perez" means "breach/breaking out" (same root as Perez son of Judah). "Uzzah" is the man's name. The place is named "the breaking-out against Uzzah" — a permanent memorial to the cost of disobedience.

Reflection

  • 1. Good intentions do not justify wrong methods (v.7). David's desire was right; his method was wrong. The church is full of well-intentioned practices that substitute human innovation for biblical obedience. Zeal without knowledge is dangerous. Always ask: "Does Scripture prescribe how this should be done?" before asking "Does this feel right?"
  • 2. Familiarity can breed presumption (v.10). Uzzah grew up with the ark in his house. He may have walked past it daily for decades. Familiarity made him casual about what was supremely holy. Are you casual about sacred things? Prayer, Scripture, communion, worship — these are not routine activities but encounters with the holy God. Never let familiarity replace reverence.
  • 3. Anger at God is honest but must lead somewhere (vv.11-12). David was angry, then afraid, then (in chapter 15) obedient. The progression matters: anger acknowledged → fear of God restored → obedience embraced. If you're angry at God, don't stay there. Let anger become reverence, and reverence become submission.
  • 4. God's presence blesses those who receive it properly (v.14). The same ark that killed Uzzah blessed Obed-edom. God's presence is both dangerous and delightful — the difference is how you approach Him. Come with reverence, on His terms, and His presence transforms everything it touches.
  • 5. Neglecting God's presence is a failure of leadership (v.3). David identifies Saul's failure: "We enquired not at it in the days of Saul." Leaders who neglect God's presence doom their people. David's first priority as king was restoring what Saul had ignored. What are you neglecting that needs restoration?
