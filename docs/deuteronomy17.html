Deuteronomy — Chapter 17

1“You shall not sacrifice to the LORD your God an ox or a sheep in which is a blemish, any defect whatever, for that is an abomination to the LORD your God.

2“If there is found among you, within any of your towns that the LORD your God is giving you, a man or woman who does what is evil in the sight of the LORD your God, in transgressing his covenant,

3and has gone and served other gods and worshiped them, or the sun or the moon or any of the host of heaven, which I have forbidden,

4and it is told you and you hear of it, then you shall inquire diligently, and if it is true and certain that such an abomination has been done in Israel,

5then you shall bring out to your gates that man or woman who has done this evil thing, and you shall stone that man or woman to death with stones.

6On the evidence of two witnesses or of three witnesses the one who is to die shall be put to death; a person shall not be put to death on the evidence of one witness.

7The hand of the witnesses shall be first against him to put him to death, and afterward the hand of all the people. So you shall purge the evil from your midst.

8“If any case arises requiring decision between one kind of homicide and another, one kind of legal right and another, or one kind of assault and another, any case within your towns that is too difficult for you, then you shall arise and go up to the place that the LORD your God will choose.

9And you shall come to the Levitical priests and to the judge who is in office in those days, and you shall consult them, and they shall declare to you the decision.

10Then you shall do according to what they declare to you from that place that the LORD will choose. And you shall be careful to do according to all that they direct you.

11According to the instructions that they give you, and according to the decision which they pronounce to you, you shall do. You shall not turn aside from the verdict that they declare to you, either to the right hand or to the left.

12The man who acts presumptuously by not obeying the priest who stands to minister there before the LORD your God, or the judge, that man shall die. So you shall purge the evil from Israel.

13And all the people shall hear and fear and not act presumptuously again.

14“When you come to the land that the LORD your God is giving you, and you possess it and dwell in it and then say, ‘I will set a king over me, like all the nations that are around me,’

15you may indeed set a king over you whom the LORD your God will choose. One from among your brothers you shall set as king over you. You may not put a foreigner over you, who is not your brother.

16Only he must not acquire many horses for himself or cause the people to return to Egypt in order to acquire many horses, since the LORD has said to you, ‘You shall never return that way again.’

17And he shall not acquire many wives for himself, lest his heart turn away, nor shall he acquire for himself excessive silver and gold.

18“And when he sits on the throne of his kingdom, he shall write for himself in a book a copy of this law, approved by the Levitical priests.

19And it shall be with him, and he shall read in it all the days of his life, that he may learn to fear the LORD his God by keeping all the words of this law and these statutes, and doing them,

20that his heart may not be lifted up above his brothers, and that he may not turn aside from the commandment, either to the right hand or to the left, so that he may continue long in his kingdom, he and his children, in Israel.

Summary

God gives laws for judges, forbids idolatry, and prescribes rules for Israel's future king — he must write a copy of the law and read it daily.

Authorship & Background

Author: Moses. Deuteronomy is the fifth and final book of the Pentateuch, written at the end of the 40 years of wilderness wandering (approximately 1406 BC). Hebrew title: "Devarim" — "Words/Things." Greek title: "Deuteronomion" — "Second Law" (a repetition/expansion of the Law for the new generation). The book consists of Moses' farewell speeches to Israel on the plains of Moab, just before they enter the Promised Land. Moses will not enter with them — these are his final words to the nation he has led for 40 years.
Historical Context: Chapter 17 addresses three critical areas of governance: (1) the prosecution of idolaters with proper judicial safeguards (vv.1-7), (2) the supreme court for difficult cases (vv.8-13), and (3) the law of the king (vv.14-20). The chapter opens with a prohibition against blemished sacrifices (v.1) — connecting worship integrity to judicial integrity. The idolatry laws (vv.2-7) establish the crucial requirement of multiple witnesses: no one may be executed on the testimony of a single witness (v.6). The witnesses must cast the first stones (v.7) — ensuring personal accountability for accusations. The supreme court (vv.8-13) provides for cases too difficult for local judges: they go to the central sanctuary where Levitical priests and the presiding judge render binding decisions. The law of the king (vv.14-20) is remarkable: Moses anticipates Israel's future request for a king (fulfilled in 1 Samuel 8) and provides constitutional limits. The king must be God's choice, an Israelite (v.15). He must NOT multiply horses (military power), wives (political alliances), or silver and gold (wealth) (vv.16-17). Most remarkably, he must write his own copy of the law and read it daily (vv.18-19). The king is not above the law — he is under it. His heart must not be "lifted up above his brethren" (v.20). Israel's king is a brother under God's law, not an absolute monarch. Solomon violated every prohibition in vv.16-17 (1 Kings 10-11), and his kingdom was torn apart.
Blemished Sacrifices Forbidden (v.1): "Thou shalt not sacrifice unto the LORD thy God any bullock, or sheep, wherein is blemish, or any evilfavouredness" (v.1). This is "an abomination unto the LORD thy God" (v.1). God deserves the best, not the defective. A blemished sacrifice insults God — it says He is not worth your best.
Prosecution of Idolaters (vv.2-7): "If there be found among you... man or woman, that hath wrought wickedness... in transgressing his covenant" (v.2). The specific crime: serving "other gods... either the sun, or moon, or any of the host of heaven" (v.3) — astral worship, common in the ancient Near East. Process: "enquired diligently" (v.4) — thorough investigation. If confirmed: "stone them with stones, till they die" (v.5). Critical safeguard: "At the mouth of two witnesses, or three witnesses, shall he that is worthy of death be put to death; but at the mouth of one witness he shall not be put to death" (v.6). The witnesses must throw first (v.7) — they bear personal responsibility for the execution. "So thou shalt put the evil away from among you" (v.7).
The Supreme Court for Difficult Cases (vv.8-13): "If there arise a matter too hard for thee in judgment" (v.8) — cases involving homicide, legal rights, or assault that are too complex for local courts. Solution: "arise, and get thee up into the place which the LORD thy God shall choose" (v.8). Consult "the priests the Levites, and unto the judge that shall be in those days" (v.9). Their decision is binding: "thou shalt do according to the sentence" (v.10). "Thou shalt not decline from the sentence... to the right hand, nor to the left" (v.11). Penalty for contempt of court: "the man that will do presumptuously, and will not hearken unto the priest... or unto the judge, even that man shall die" (v.12). Purpose: "all the people shall hear, and fear, and do no more presumptuously" (v.13).
The Law of the King (vv.14-20): "When thou art come unto the land... and shalt say, I will set a king over me, like as all the nations" (v.14). Moses anticipates the request. God permits it with conditions: "Thou shalt in any wise set him king over thee, whom the LORD thy God shall choose" (v.15). He must be an Israelite — "thou mayest not set a stranger over thee" (v.15). Three prohibitions: (1) "he shall not multiply horses to himself, nor cause the people to return to Egypt" (v.16) — no military buildup through Egyptian alliances; (2) "Neither shall he multiply wives to himself, that his heart turn not away" (v.17) — no political marriages that introduce foreign gods; (3) "neither shall he greatly multiply to himself silver and gold" (v.17) — no excessive wealth accumulation. The king's primary duty: "he shall write him a copy of this law in a book" (v.18). "He shall read therein all the days of his life" (v.19). Purpose: "that he may learn to fear the LORD his God" (v.19), "that his heart be not lifted up above his brethren" (v.20), and "that he turn not aside from the commandment" (v.20). Promise: "he may prolong his days in his kingdom, he, and his children" (v.20).

Map & Geography

  • Moses speaks from the plains of Moab. The future king will rule "in the land which the LORD thy God giveth thee" (v.14) — laws for Canaan's governance.

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): enduringword.com Guzik emphasizes that the law of the king is a constitutional monarchy — the king is under God's law, not above it. He notes that Solomon violated all three prohibitions (horses, wives, gold) and suffered the consequences. The requirement to copy and read the law daily made the king a student of Scripture first and a ruler second.
  • Charles Spurgeon: "The king must write out the law with his own hand and read it every day of his life. What a prescription for leadership! Not military strategy, not political cunning, not economic theory — but the daily reading of God's word. The leader who neglects Scripture will inevitably multiply what God forbids. Solomon is the proof: the wisest man who ever lived destroyed his kingdom because he stopped listening to God's word."

Reflection

  • 1. No blemished sacrifice (v.1). God deserves our best — not our leftovers, not our defective offerings. This applies to time, energy, attention, and resources given to God. Offering God what costs us nothing is an abomination (2 Samuel 24:24).
  • 2. The two-witness rule (v.6). God builds safeguards into justice. No person's life depends on a single accusation. This protects against false testimony, personal vendettas, and hasty judgment. Justice requires patience and corroboration.
  • 3. The king under the law (vv.18-20). The highest human authority is still under God's authority. No leader is above Scripture. The king's primary qualification is not charisma or competence but submission to God's word. Leaders who neglect Scripture inevitably abuse power.
  • 4. "He shall read therein all the days of his life" (v.19). Daily Scripture reading is not optional for leaders — or for any believer. The king who stops reading becomes the king who starts multiplying what God forbids. Neglect of the word precedes every other failure.
  • 5. "That his heart be not lifted up above his brethren" (v.20). Power tends toward pride. The antidote is daily immersion in God's word, which reminds the leader that he is a brother, not a lord. Humility is maintained by the word, not by willpower.
  • 6. Solomon's warning. Every prohibition in vv.16-17 was violated by Solomon: horses from Egypt (1 Kings 10:28), 700 wives and 300 concubines (1 Kings 11:3), silver and gold beyond measure (1 Kings 10:14-23). The result: his heart turned away (1 Kings 11:4) and his kingdom was torn (1 Kings 11:11). The law of the king is not theoretical — it is prophetically precise.
