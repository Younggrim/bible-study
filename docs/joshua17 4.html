Joshua — Chapter 17

1Then allotment was made to the people of Manasseh, for he was the firstborn of Joseph. To Machir the firstborn of Manasseh, the father of Gilead, were allotted Gilead and Bashan, because he was a man of war.

2And allotments were made to the rest of the people of Manasseh by their clans, Abiezer, Helek, Asriel, Shechem, Hepher, and Shemida. These were the male descendants of Manasseh the son of Joseph, by their clans.

3Now Zelophehad the son of Hepher, son of Gilead, son of Machir, son of Manasseh, had no sons, but only daughters, and these are the names of his daughters: Mahlah, Noah, Hoglah, Milcah, and Tirzah.

4They approached Eleazar the priest and Joshua the son of Nun and the leaders and said, “The LORD commanded Moses to give us an inheritance along with our brothers.” So according to the mouth of the LORD he gave them an inheritance among the brothers of their father.

5Thus there fell to Manasseh ten portions, besides the land of Gilead and Bashan, which is on the other side of the Jordan,

6because the daughters of Manasseh received an inheritance along with his sons. The land of Gilead was allotted to the rest of the people of Manasseh.

7The territory of Manasseh reached from Asher to Michmethath, which is east of Shechem. Then the boundary goes along southward to the inhabitants of En-tappuah.

8The land of Tappuah belonged to Manasseh, but the town of Tappuah on the boundary of Manasseh belonged to the people of Ephraim.

9Then the boundary went down to the brook Kanah. These cities, to the south of the brook, among the cities of Manasseh, belong to Ephraim. Then the boundary of Manasseh goes on the north side of the brook and ends at the sea,

10the land to the south being Ephraim’s and that to the north being Manasseh’s, with the sea forming its boundary. On the north Asher is reached, and on the east Issachar.

11Also in Issachar and in Asher Manasseh had Beth-shean and its villages, and Ibleam and its villages, and the inhabitants of Dor and its villages, and the inhabitants of En-dor and its villages, and the inhabitants of Taanach and its villages, and the inhabitants of Megiddo and its villages; the third is Naphath.

12Yet the people of Manasseh could not take possession of those cities, but the Canaanites persisted in dwelling in that land.

13Now when the people of Israel grew strong, they put the Canaanites to forced labor, but did not utterly drive them out.

14Then the people of Joseph spoke to Joshua, saying, “Why have you given me but one lot and one portion as an inheritance, although I am a numerous people, since all along the LORD has blessed me?”

15And Joshua said to them, “If you are a numerous people, go up by yourselves to the forest, and there clear ground for yourselves in the land of the Perizzites and the Rephaim, since the hill country of Ephraim is too narrow for you.”

16The people of Joseph said, “The hill country is not enough for us. Yet all the Canaanites who dwell in the plain have chariots of iron, both those in Beth-shean and its villages and those in the Valley of Jezreel.”

17Then Joshua said to the house of Joseph, to Ephraim and Manasseh, “You are a numerous people and have great power. You shall not have one allotment only,

18but the hill country shall be yours, for though it is a forest, you shall clear it and possess it to its farthest borders. For you shall drive out the Canaanites, though they have chariots of iron, and though they are strong.”

Summary

The rest of Manasseh receives its territory, but complains their allotment is too small. Joshua challenges them to clear the forested hill country rather than shrink from the Canaanites with iron chariots.

Key Verses

v.4
"The LORD commanded Moses to give us an inheritance among our brethren. Therefore according to the commandment of the LORD he gave them an inheritance among the brethren of their father." (ESV) — The daughters of Zelophehad claim God's promise boldly. They know the Word and stand on it. Their faith is rewarded.
v.12
"Yet the children of Manasseh could not drive out the inhabitants of those cities; but the Canaanites would dwell in that land." (ESV) — Another failure of incomplete conquest. Beth-shean, Megiddo, Dor, Taanach — major cities that remained Canaanite. "Could not" again likely means "would not" — lack of faith, not lack of divine power.
v.13
"Yet it came to pass, when the children of Israel were waxen strong, that they put the Canaanites to tribute; but did not utterly drive them out." (ESV) — When Israel grew strong enough to conquer, they chose profit over obedience. Strength without faithfulness leads to compromise.
v.14
"Why hast you given me but one lot and one portion to inherit, seeing I am a great people, forasmuch as the LORD has blessed me hitherto?" (ESV) — The Joseph tribes complain. Note the contradiction: they claim to be "great" but are unwilling to do the work of greatness. They want more territory handed to them without effort.
v.15
"If you be a great people, then get you up to the wood country, and cut down for thyself there in the land of the Perizzites and of the giants, if mount Ephraim be too narrow for you." (ESV) — Joshua's brilliant response: You say you're great? Prove it. Clear the forest. Fight the giants. Your greatness is your solution — use it.
v.18
"But the mountain shall be your; for it is a wood, and you shall cut it down: and the outgoings of it shall be your: for you shall drive out the Canaanites, though they have iron chariots, and though they be strong." (ESV) — Joshua's final word: the iron chariots are not an excuse. God's people can overcome any obstacle if they will act in faith. Stop fearing and start clearing.

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/joshua-17/ Guzik contrasts the Joseph tribes with Caleb: Caleb asked for the mountain with the giants; Joseph's tribes complained about the difficulty of their territory. Joshua's response is essentially: "Don't come to me with complaints — go conquer." Guzik notes that Joshua refused to enable their laziness. He affirmed their strength but directed it toward action, not entitlement.
  • Charles Spurgeon: "The children of Joseph said, 'The hill is not enough for us.' And Joshua said, 'Then clear the forest and take the valley.' They wanted more room but less work. They wanted a larger inheritance without a larger faith. But God does not give territory to those who will not fight for it. If you want more of God's blessing, you must be willing to do more of God's work."

Translations

  • v.1 — KJV: "for he was the firstborn of Joseph" / ESV: "for he was the firstborn of Joseph" — Both identical. Though Manasseh was firstborn, Jacob gave Ephraim the greater blessing (Genesis 48:19-20). Yet Manasseh still receives recognition as firstborn here — the birthright was not entirely revoked.
  • v.2 — KJV: "the male children of Manasseh" / ESV: "the male descendants of Manasseh" — Hebrew "b'nei Menasheh ha-z'kharim" = the sons/descendants of Manasseh, the males. The specification "male" sets up the contrast with Zelophehad's daughters in v.3.
  • v.3 — KJV: "Zelophehad... had no sons, but daughters" / ESV: "Zelophehad... had no sons, but only daughters" — Hebrew "lo hayu lo banim ki im banot." The case of Zelophehad established that daughters could inherit when there were no sons — a remarkable provision in the ancient Near East.
  • v.11 — KJV: "even three countries" / ESV: "the third is Naphath" — Hebrew "sh'loshet ha-napheth." This is a difficult phrase. KJV reads it as "three regions/districts." ESV transliterates "Naphath" as a place name. The Hebrew is obscure; it may refer to three highland districts or to the region of Naphoth-dor (Joshua 12:23).
  • v.14 — KJV: "seeing I am a great people" / ESV: "although I am a numerous people" — Hebrew "am rav." "Rav" = many, numerous, great. ESV's "numerous" focuses on population size; KJV's "great" could imply both size and importance. The Joseph tribes were indeed large (Numbers 26:34,37 — combined ~85,000 men).
  • v.15 — KJV: "the wood country" / ESV: "the forest" — Hebrew "ya'ar" = forest, woodland. The central hill country was heavily forested in this period. Joshua tells them to clear it — create your own space through labor.
  • v.15 — KJV: "the giants" / ESV: "the Rephaim" — As elsewhere, KJV translates "Rephaim" generically as "giants"; ESV preserves the proper name.
  • v.16 — KJV: "chariots of iron" / ESV: "chariots of iron" — Hebrew "rekhev barzel." Iron-fitted chariots were the advanced military technology of the day — the ancient equivalent of tanks. Israel had no chariots and no iron-working capability at this time. Humanly speaking, the Canaanites had overwhelming military superiority in the valleys.
  • v.18 — KJV: "thou shalt cut it down" / ESV: "you shall clear it" — Hebrew "u-verata" from "bara" = to cut down, clear (trees). This is a different "bara" from the creation verb in Genesis 1:1. Joshua commands them to transform wilderness into habitable land through hard work.

Reflection

  • 1. Zelophehad's daughters: bold faith (vv.3-4). They knew God's promise and claimed it. They did not passively accept exclusion — they approached the leaders and stated their case based on God's Word. Do we know God's promises well enough to claim them? Do we approach Him boldly (Hebrews 4:16)?
  • 2. Complaint vs. conquest (vv.14-18). The Joseph tribes wanted more territory but less effort. They complained to Joshua rather than conquering their land. How often do we complain about our circumstances rather than doing the work God has set before us?
  • 3. Joshua's challenge: "If you are great, act great" (v.15). Joshua does not coddle them. He turns their own claim back on them. True greatness is demonstrated in action, not in complaint. If God has blessed you with strength, use it — don't demand that others make your life easier.
  • 4. Iron chariots are no excuse (v.18). The Canaanites had superior technology. But Joshua says: "You shall drive them out, though they have iron chariots." God's power is not limited by human technology. What "iron chariots" do we use as excuses for inaction? No obstacle is too great when God has commanded us to advance.
  • 5. The contrast with Caleb. Caleb: "Give me this mountain — where the giants are." Joseph's tribes: "The hill is not enough — and the valley has iron chariots." Same God, same promises, opposite responses. Faith embraces difficulty; unbelief shrinks from it.
  • 6. Strength without obedience (v.13). When Israel grew strong, they enslaved the Canaanites rather than driving them out. Increased capacity did not produce increased obedience. Spiritual maturity should lead to greater holiness, not greater compromise.
