1 Corinthians — Chapter 5

1It is actually reported that there is sexual immorality among you, and of a kind that is not tolerated even among pagans, for a man has his father’s wife.

2And you are arrogant! Ought you not rather to mourn? Let him who has done this be removed from among you.

3For though absent in body, I am present in spirit; and as if present, I have already pronounced judgment on the one who did such a thing.

4When you are assembled in the name of the Lord Jesus and my spirit is present, with the power of our Lord Jesus,

5you are to deliver this man to Satan for the destruction of the flesh, so that his spirit may be saved in the day of the Lord.

6Your boasting is not good. Do you not know that a little leaven leavens the whole lump?

7Cleanse out the old leaven that you may be a new lump, as you really are unleavened. For Christ, our Passover lamb, has been sacrificed.

8Let us therefore celebrate the festival, not with the old leaven, the leaven of malice and evil, but with the unleavened bread of sincerity and truth.

9I wrote to you in my letter not to associate with sexually immoral people—

10not at all meaning the sexually immoral of this world, or the greedy and swindlers, or idolaters, since then you would need to go out of the world.

11But now I am writing to you not to associate with anyone who bears the name of brother if he is guilty of sexual immorality or greed, or is an idolater, reviler, drunkard, or swindler—not even to eat with such a one.

12For what have I to do with judging outsiders? Is it not those inside the church whom you are to judge?

13God judges those outside. “Purge the evil person from among you.”

Summary

Paul commands the church to expel an unrepentant man living in sexual immorality, warning that "a little leaven leavens the whole lump" — the church must judge those inside while leaving outsiders to God.

Key Verses

v.2
"And you are arrogant! Ought you not rather to mourn?" (ESV) — The wrong response to sin in the church: pride instead of grief.
v.5
"You are to deliver this man to Satan for the destruction of the flesh, so that his spirit may be saved." (ESV) — Redemptive discipline.
v.6
"A little leaven leavens the whole lump." (ESV) — Tolerated sin spreads.
v.7
"For Christ, our Passover lamb, has been sacrificed." (ESV) — Christ as the Passover Lamb — the basis for holy living.
v.11
"If anyone bearing the name of brother is guilty of sexual immorality... not even to eat with such a one." (ESV) — The standard for church discipline.
v.12
"Is it not those inside the church whom you are to judge?" (ESV) — The church's responsibility to judge its own members.

Commentary

  • Enduring Word (David Guzik): https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/1-corinthians-5/ Guzik examines the nature of the sin, the church's wrong response, the purpose of discipline, and the leaven principle.
  • Charles Spurgeon: Sermon: "A Little Leaven" on v.6 (MTP Vol. 10, No. 647) "A little leaven leaveneth the whole lump — tolerated sin never stays small. It spreads. It infects. It corrupts everything it touches. The church that tolerates open sin in its members will soon find that sin has become normal. Purge it out — for the sake of the whole body." Sermon: "Christ Our Passover" on v.7 (MTP Vol. 22, No. 1380) "Christ our passover is sacrificed for us — therefore let us keep the feast. Because Christ died for sin, we must not live in sin. The Passover lamb was killed so Israel could leave Egypt — Christ was killed so you could leave sin. Don't go back to Egypt."

Translations

  • v.1 — KJV: "his father's wife" / ESV: "father’s wife" — Not his biological mother but his stepmother. Still a violation of Leviticus 18:8 and shocking even by pagan standards.
  • v.5 — KJV: "deliver such an one unto Satan" / ESV: "you are to deliver this man to Satan" — Excommunication — removing the person from the church's spiritual protection into the world (Satan's domain). The purpose: "that the spirit may be saved" — redemptive, not punitive.
  • v.7 — KJV: "Christ our passover" / ESV: "For Christ, our Passover lamb, has" — Greek "pascha" — Passover. Christ fulfills the Passover lamb — sacrificed so that judgment passes over believers. This is the theological basis for holy living.
  • v.11 — KJV: "no not to eat" / ESV: "to eat with such a one" — Not even sharing a meal. In the ancient world, eating together signified fellowship and acceptance. Refusing to eat with someone was a powerful statement of separation.
  • ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB) v.5 — NET: "hand this man over to Satan for the destruction of the flesh, so that his spirit may be saved in the day of the Lord" — "destruction of the flesh" (olethron tEs sarkos) — debated: physical suffering? death? removal of fleshly desires? WEB: "for the destruction of the flesh." The goal is redemptive, not merely punitive.
  • v.6 — NET: "Your boasting is not good. Don't you know that a little yeast affects the whole batch of dough?" — "yeast" (zymE = leaven). WEB: "a little leaven leavens the whole lump." KJV: "a little leaven leaveneth the whole lump." Tolerated sin spreads — no contamination stays contained.
  • v.7 — NET: "Clean out the old yeast so that you may be a new batch of dough — you are, in fact, without yeast. For Christ, our Passover lamb, has been sacrificed" — "Christ our Passover" (to pascha hEmOn Christos). WEB: "our Passover, Christ, has been sacrificed." The Exodus imagery applied to the cross.
  • v.11 — NET: "not to associate with anyone who calls himself a Christian who is sexually immoral, or greedy, or an idolater" — "calls himself a Christian" (onomazomenos adelphos = named a brother). WEB: "anyone who is called a brother." The distinction: professing believers held to higher standard than outsiders (v.12).
  • v.13 — NET: "Remove the evil person from among you" — quoting Deuteronomy 17:7. WEB: "Put away the wicked man from among yourselves." KJV: "put away from among yourselves that wicked person." Church discipline is a biblical command, not optional.

Reflection

  • 1. Church discipline is rarely practiced today. Why do you think that is? What are the consequences of a church that never addresses sin in its members?
  • 2. The purpose of discipline is "that the spirit may be saved" (v.5) — redemptive, not punitive. How does knowing the goal is restoration change how you view church discipline?
  • 3. Paul says "do not ye judge them that are within?" (v.12). The church IS supposed to judge its members — not harshly but redemptively. How do you balance grace and accountability in your relationships with other believers?
  • 4. The Corinthians confused tolerance with love. True love confronts sin for the person's good. Where might you be confusing tolerance with love — allowing someone to continue in destructive behavior because confrontation is uncomfortable?
  • 5. "Such were some of you" (6:11) — the next chapter reminds them they WERE these things but have been washed. Discipline is for those who claim Christ but live in unrepentant sin — not for those who are struggling and repenting. How do you distinguish between the two?
  • 1. The Corinthians were "puffed up" about sin in their midst (v.2). They should have mourned. When you become aware of serious sin in your church community, is your response grief or indifference? Pride in "tolerance" or sorrow over brokenness?
  • 2. "A little leaven leaveneth the whole lump" (v.6). Tolerated sin spreads. Is there sin in your life that you're tolerating — thinking it's "small" or "manageable"? It will spread if not addressed.
  • 3. "Christ our passover is sacrificed for us" (v.7). Because Christ died for sin, we must not live in sin. His sacrifice is not a license to continue sinning — it is the power to stop. How does the cross motivate your pursuit of holiness?
  • 4. Paul distinguishes between judging those "within" and those "without" (vv.12-13). The church is responsible for its own members' behavior — not for policing the world. Are you holding fellow believers accountable while showing grace to outsiders?
