Galatians - Chapter 3
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Paul argues that justification has always been by faith (as Abraham believed and it was counted to him as righteousness), the Law was a temporary guardian until Christ came, and in Christ "there is neither Jew nor Greek, slave nor free, male nor female."

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Paul (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 3 is Paul's theological argument for justification
by faith — using Abraham, the Law, and the promise to prove that faith has
ALWAYS been God's way of salvation. The Law came 430 years AFTER the promise
to Abraham — it cannot cancel or modify the promise.

The Argument from Experience (vv.1-5): Paul asks: "Did you receive the Spirit
by works of the law, or by hearing with faith?" (v.2). Their own experience
proves the point — they received the Spirit through faith, not law-keeping.
Why go back to works now?

The Argument from Abraham (vv.6-9): Abraham "believed God, and it was accounted
to him for righteousness" (v.6, quoting Genesis 15:6). Abraham was justified
by faith — 430 years before the Law existed. Therefore, faith is the original
and permanent way of salvation.

The Argument from the Law's Purpose (vv.19-25): "Wherefore then serveth the
law?" (v.19). The Law was added "because of transgressions" — to reveal sin,
restrain evil, and drive people to Christ. It was a "schoolmaster to bring us
unto Christ" (v.24) — a temporary guardian until faith came.

"There is neither Jew nor Greek" (v.28): In Christ, all human distinctions —
ethnic, social, gender — are transcended. Not erased (you're still who you are)
but transcended (your identity in Christ is primary).

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "Did you receive the Spirit by works of the law or by hearing with
        faith?" (ESV) — Experience proves faith, not works.

v.10 — "All who rely on works of the law are under a curse." (ESV) — The Law
        curses everyone who doesn't keep it perfectly.

v.11 — "The righteous shall live by faith." (ESV) — Habakkuk 2:4 — the thesis
        of the Reformation.

v.13 — "Christ redeemed us from the curse of the law by becoming a curse for
        us." (ESV) — Christ bore the Law's curse in our place.

v.24 — "So then, the law was our guardian until Christ came, in order that we
        might be justified by faith." (ESV) — The Law's purpose: to lead to
        Christ.

v.26 — "For in Christ Jesus you are all sons of God, through faith." (ESV) —
        Sonship through faith alone.

v.28 — "There is neither Jew nor Greek, there is neither slave nor free, there
        is no male and female, for you are all one in Christ Jesus." (ESV) —
        Unity and equality in Christ.

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1  — KJV: "O foolish Galatians" / ESV: Both equivalent. — Greek "anoētoi" — senseless, thoughtless,
       not using their minds. Paul is not calling them stupid — he's saying
       they're not thinking clearly. They've been "bewitched" (fascinated,
       deceived).
       NET: "You foolish Galatians! Who has cast a spell on
       you?" — "foolish" (anoEtoi = without understanding/senseless).
       "Cast a spell" (ebaskanen = bewitched/given the evil eye).
       WEB: "who has bewitched you?" KJV: "who hath bewitched you?"
       Paul uses magical language — they've been enchanted away
       from truth.

v.6  — NET: "Just as Abraham believed God, and it was credited to
       him as righteousness" — quoting Genesis 15:6. WEB: "Abraham
       believed God, and it was accounted to him for righteousness."
       The same verb (logizesthai = reckon/credit) as Romans 4.
       Faith has always been the means of righteousness.

v.10 — KJV: "under the curse" / ESV: "are under a curse" — Anyone who relies on law-keeping for
       righteousness is cursed — because no one keeps the Law perfectly.
       The Law demands 100% compliance; anything less = curse.

v.13 — KJV: "made a curse for us" / ESV: "becoming a curse for us" — Not "cursed" (adjective) but "a curse"
       (noun). Christ became the curse itself — fully identified with it,
       absorbing it completely.
       NET: "Christ redeemed us from the curse of the law by
       becoming a curse for us" — "becoming a curse" (genomenos
       hyper hEmOn katara). WEB: "having become a curse for us."
       KJV: "being made a curse for us." Quoting Deuteronomy 21:23
       — "cursed is everyone who hangs on a tree." Substitution.

v.24 — KJV: "schoolmaster" / ESV: "was our guardian" — Greek "paidagōgos" — a household slave who
       supervised children and escorted them to school. Not the teacher but
       the guardian who brings you TO the teacher (Christ).
       NET: "the law had become our guardian until Christ came,
       so that we could be declared righteous by faith" — "guardian"
       (paidagOgos = child-conductor/tutor-slave). WEB: "the law
       has become our tutor to bring us to Christ." KJV: "the law
       was our schoolmaster to bring us unto Christ." Not a teacher
       but a slave who escorted children — temporary authority.

v.28 — KJV: "neither Jew nor Greek... bond nor free... male nor female" / ESV: "neither Jew nor Greek, there is neither slave nor free" —
       Not that these distinctions cease to exist but that they no longer
       determine spiritual status. In Christ, all are equally children of God.
       NET: "There is neither Jew nor Greek, there is neither
       slave nor free, there is neither male nor female — for all
       of you are one in Christ Jesus" — three social divisions
       abolished in Christ. WEB: "There is neither Jew nor Greek...
       male nor female." KJV: "There is neither Jew nor Greek."
       Not elimination of distinction but equality of access.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Bewitched     — Fascinated, deceived, put under a spell. The Galatians were
               charmed by the Judaizers' message away from the simplicity of
               grace.

Curse of the Law — The condemnation that falls on everyone who fails to keep
               the Law perfectly (Deuteronomy 27:26). Christ bore this curse
               for us.

Schoolmaster  — Greek "paidagōgos" — a guardian/tutor who supervised children
               until maturity. The Law's role: to supervise until Christ came.

Seed of Abraham — Christ (singular, v.16) — and all who are in Christ (v.29).
               Believers are Abraham's spiritual descendants through faith.

Children of God — Status received through faith in Christ (v.26) — not
               through law-keeping, circumcision, or ethnic heritage.

One in Christ — Unity that transcends all human divisions — ethnic, social,
               and gender. Not uniformity but equality of status before God.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Having begun in the Spirit, are ye now made perfect by the flesh?" (v.3).
   You started by faith — why are you trying to finish by works? Where have
   you drifted from grace back to performance?

2. "Christ hath redeemed us from the curse of the law, being made a curse for
   us" (v.13). Christ bore the curse you deserved. You are free from
   condemnation. Do you live in that freedom, or do you still feel cursed?

3. "The law was our schoolmaster to bring us unto Christ" (v.24). The Law's
   job was to show you your need — not to save you. Have you graduated from
   the schoolmaster to the Savior? Or are you still trying to please the
   schoolmaster?

4. "Ye are all one in Christ Jesus" (v.28). In Christ, every human division
   is transcended. How does this truth shape how you view people who are
   different from you — different race, different class, different background?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. Paul asks "Did you receive the Spirit by works or by faith?" (v.2). The
   answer is obvious: faith. So why go back to works? What makes legalism
   so attractive even after experiencing grace?

2. "As many as are of the works of the law are under the curse" (v.10). If
   you rely on law-keeping, you must keep ALL of it perfectly — or you're
   cursed. How does this impossibility drive you to Christ?

3. The Law was a "schoolmaster" (v.24) — temporary, preparatory, leading to
   Christ. Once you've come to Christ, you're no longer under the schoolmaster.
   Are you living as a graduate (in freedom) or as a student still under the
   guardian (in bondage)?

4. "If ye be Christ's, then are ye Abraham's seed, and heirs according to the
   promise" (v.29). You are an heir — not by blood but by faith. What does
   it mean to live as an heir of God's promises?

5. "There is neither Jew nor Greek" (v.28). The Gospel demolishes every
   barrier between people. Where do barriers still exist in your church or
   community that the Gospel should be breaking down?
