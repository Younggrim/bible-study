2 Corinthians - Chapter 1
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Paul opens with praise to the God of all comfort who comforts us in affliction so we can comfort others, defends his sincerity in changing travel plans, and affirms that all God's promises find their "Yes" in Christ.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: The Apostle Paul, with Timothy (v.1). Written from Macedonia,
approximately AD 55-56 — shortly after 1 Corinthians.

Audience: The church at Corinth and "all the saints which are in all Achaia"
(the broader region of southern Greece).

Purpose: 2 Corinthians is Paul's most personal, emotional letter. He defends
his apostleship against false teachers, explains his changed travel plans,
encourages generous giving, and reveals his own suffering and weakness. It is
the most autobiographical of Paul's letters.

Chapter 1 Content: The God of all comfort (vv.3-11), Paul's integrity (vv.12-14),
his changed travel plans (vv.15-22), and his reason for not coming (vv.23-24).

The God of All Comfort (vv.3-7): Paul opens with praise for God who "comforteth
us in all our tribulation" (v.4). The purpose of comfort: "that we may be able
to comfort them which are in any trouble, by the comfort wherewith we ourselves
are comforted of God" (v.4). Your suffering qualifies you to comfort others.

"All the Promises of God in Him Are Yea" (v.20): Every promise God has ever
made finds its "yes" in Christ. Not one promise fails. Christ is the guarantee
of every divine commitment.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.3-4 — "The Father of mercies and God of all comfort, who comforts us in all
        our affliction, so that we may be able to comfort those who are in any
        affliction." (ESV) — Comfort received becomes comfort given.

v.9  — "We felt that we had received the sentence of death. But that was to
        make us rely not on ourselves but on God who raises the dead." (ESV) —
        The purpose of extreme suffering: to destroy self-reliance.

v.20 — "All the promises of God find their Yes in him." (ESV) — Every promise
        guaranteed in Christ.

v.21-22 — "It is God who establishes us... and has anointed us, and who has
        also put his seal on us and given us his Spirit in our hearts as a
        guarantee." (ESV) — Four securities: established, anointed, sealed,
        given the Spirit.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------

v.3  — KJV: "the God of all comfort" / ESV: "mercies and God of all comfort" — Greek "paraklēseōs" — comfort,
       encouragement, consolation. The same root as "Paraclete" (the Holy
       Spirit). God is the source of all genuine comfort.

v.4  — KJV: "comforteth us in all our tribulation" / ESV: "who comforts us in all our affliction, so that we may be" — The comfort is IN the
       tribulation, not FROM it. God doesn't always remove suffering — He
       comforts us through it.

v.9  — KJV: "the sentence of death in ourselves" / ESV: "we felt that we had received the sentence of death" — Paul felt he was going to
       die. The purpose: "that we should not trust in ourselves." Extreme
       suffering destroys self-reliance and produces God-reliance.

v.20 — KJV: "yea, and in him Amen" / ESV: "him. That is why it is through him" — "Yea" = yes (God's affirmation).
       "Amen" = so be it (our response). Every promise is God's "yes" and
       our "amen" — confirmed in Christ.

v.22 — KJV: "earnest of the Spirit" / ESV: "given us his Spirit in our hearts as" — Greek "arrabōn" — a down payment,
       a deposit guaranteeing the full amount. The Spirit in you is God's
       deposit guaranteeing your full inheritance.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
v.3  — NET: "Blessed is the God and Father of our Lord Jesus
       Christ, the Father of mercies and God of all comfort" —
       "Father of mercies" (patEr tOn oiktirmOn). WEB: "the Father
       of mercies and God of all comfort." KJV: "the Father of
       mercies, and the God of all comfort." Mercy defines God's
       paternal character.

v.5  — NET: "For just as the sufferings of Christ overflow toward
       us, so also our comfort through Christ overflows to you" —
       "overflow" (perisseuei = abounds/exceeds). WEB: "as the
       sufferings of Christ abound to us." Suffering and comfort
       both come in abundance — the ratio is maintained.

v.20 — NET: "For every one of God's promises are 'Yes' in him" —
       "Yes" (nai = affirmative). WEB: "For however many are the
       promises of God, in him is the 'Yes.'" KJV: "all the
       promises of God in him are yea, and in him Amen." Christ is
       the fulfillment of every divine promise.

v.22 — NET: "who also sealed us and gave us the Spirit in our
       hearts as a down payment" — "down payment" (arrabOna =
       deposit/guarantee/earnest). KJV: "the earnest of the Spirit."
       WEB: "the down payment of the Spirit." A commercial term —
       the Spirit now guarantees the full inheritance later.

v.24 — NET: "Not that we lord it over your faith, but we are
       workers with you for your joy" — "workers with you"
       (synergoi = co-workers). WEB: "we are fellow workers with
       you for your joy." Apostolic authority serves joy, not
       domination.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
God of all comfort — God as the source of every genuine consolation. He
               comforts us so we can comfort others.

Tribulation   — Greek "thlipsis" — pressure, affliction. Paul experienced
               extreme suffering that brought him to the point of despair.

Sentence of death — Paul's near-death experience in Asia — so severe he
               expected to die. God used it to destroy self-reliance.

Earnest/Deposit — The Holy Spirit as God's down payment — guaranteeing the
               full inheritance to come. A legal/financial term for a deposit
               that secures a purchase.

Sealed        — Marked with God's ownership — like a seal on a document
               proving authenticity and ownership. The Spirit is God's seal
               on believers.

Yea and Amen  — God's "yes" to every promise, confirmed in Christ. Not one
               promise will fail.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Who comforteth us in all our tribulation, that we may be able to comfort
   them which are in any trouble" (v.4). Your suffering has a purpose: it
   qualifies you to comfort others. What pain in your past has equipped you
   to minister to someone else? Are you using it?

2. "We should not trust in ourselves, but in God which raiseth the dead" (v.9).
   God sometimes allows extreme suffering to destroy self-reliance. Where are
   you still trusting in yourself? What would it take for you to fully depend
   on God?

3. "All the promises of God in him are yea" (v.20). Every promise — forgiveness,
   presence, provision, resurrection, eternal life — is guaranteed in Christ.
   Which promise do you most need to claim today?

4. "We have dominion over your faith, but are helpers of your joy" (v.24).
   Paul didn't lord it over the Corinthians — he served their joy. How do you
   use whatever authority you have — to control or to serve?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. Paul was "pressed out of measure, above strength" and "despaired even of
   life" (v.8). Even apostles experience despair. How does knowing that Paul
   went through this encourage you in your own dark seasons?

2. The purpose of comfort is to give it away (v.4). Are you hoarding God's
   comfort or sharing it? Who in your life needs the comfort you've received?

3. Paul asked for prayer (v.11) — even the greatest apostle needed others
   praying for him. Who is praying for you? Who are you praying for?

4. God "stablisheth... anointed... sealed... given the earnest of the Spirit"
   (vv.21-22). Four securities for the believer. Which of these do you most
   need to remember today?

5. Paul's travel plans changed (vv.15-23) — and he was accused of being
   unreliable. Sometimes God changes our plans. How do you handle it when
   your plans change? Do you trust God's sovereignty over your schedule?
