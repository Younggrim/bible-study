John - Chapter 2
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Jesus performs His first sign by turning water into wine at the wedding in Cana, then cleanses the temple in Jerusalem and speaks of raising "this temple" in three days — pointing to His resurrection.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: The Apostle John (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 2 contains two pivotal events — the first miracle at
the wedding in Cana, and the first cleansing of the Temple in Jerusalem. Both
events reveal Jesus' authority: over nature (water to wine) and over worship
(purifying the Temple). John places these early in his Gospel to establish
immediately that Jesus is no ordinary teacher.

Wedding at Cana: Weddings in first-century Jewish culture were major community
events lasting 7 days. Running out of wine was a serious social disgrace for the
host family. Mary's request to Jesus shows her confidence in His ability to act.

Temple Cleansing: The outer courts of the Temple (Court of the Gentiles) had
become a marketplace where money changers and animal sellers operated, often
exploiting worshippers. Jesus' response was not passive — He made a whip and
drove them out, demonstrating righteous anger at the corruption of His Father's
house.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Cana of Galilee (v.1): A village in Galilee (exact location debated — possibly Kefr Kenna, 4 miles northeast of Nazareth). Jesus performed His first miracle here.
- Jerusalem / the Temple (v.13): Jesus went up to Jerusalem for Passover and cleansed the Temple. John places this early in Jesus' ministry (the Synoptics record a later cleansing).

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.5  — "Do whatever he tells you." (ESV) — Mary's instruction to the
        servants. One of the most practical statements of faith in Scripture.

v.11 — "This, the first of his signs, Jesus did at Cana in Galilee, and
        manifested his glory. And his disciples believed in him." (ESV) — The
        purpose of miracles: to reveal Christ's glory and produce faith.

v.16 — "Do not make my Father's house a house of trade." (ESV) — Jesus' zeal for
        pure worship and His claim of unique relationship with God ("my Father").

v.19 — "Destroy this temple, and in three days I will raise it up." (ESV) — A veiled
        prophecy of His death and resurrection, misunderstood by His hearers.

v.25 — "He himself knew what was in man." (ESV) — Jesus' omniscience — He sees through
        superficial belief to the heart.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.4  — KJV: "Woman, what have I to do with thee?" / ESV: "Woman, what does this have to do with me?" — Greek "ti emoi kai soi" — A Hebrew idiom meaning "what is that to us?" or "that is not our concern." It is not disrespectful — "Woman" (gune) was a respectful address in Greek. Jesus is gently indicating His actions are governed by the Father's timing, not family requests.
  [ASV/NET/WEB]: NET: "Woman, why are you saying this to me? My time has
       not yet come" — "Woman" (gynai) is respectful address in
       Greek (not rude). KJV: "Woman, what have I to do with thee?"
       WEB: "Woman, what does that have to do with you and me?"
       ASV: "Woman, what have I to do with thee?"

v.6  — KJV: "two or three firkins apiece" / ESV: "twenty or thirty gallons" — Greek "metretas" — KJV uses the archaic "firkins"; ESV converts to modern gallons. Each pot held 20-30 gallons, meaning Jesus created 120-180 gallons of wine — an extravagant abundance, a picture of God's generosity.
  [ASV/NET/WEB]: NET: "six stone water jars were sitting there, meant for
       Jewish ceremonial washing" — "meant for" explains their
       purpose. WEB: "for the Jews' purification." KJV: "after
       the manner of the purifying of the Jews." Jesus transforms
       vessels of ritual law into vessels of celebratory wine.

v.10 — KJV: "when men have well drunk" / ESV: "when people have drunk freely" — Greek "methusthosin" — Can mean drunk or simply having drunk freely. The point is that the best wine was saved for last — opposite of human custom.

v.11 — KJV: "This beginning of miracles" / ESV: "This, the first of his signs" — Greek "semeion" — KJV uses "miracles"; ESV uses "signs." John consistently uses "signs" to indicate miracles that point to deeper truth about Jesus' identity.
  [ASV/NET/WEB]: NET: "Jesus did this as the first of his miraculous signs"
       — John calls miracles "signs" (sēmeia) — they point beyond
       themselves to who Jesus is. WEB: "the first of his signs."
       KJV: "This beginning of miracles." John's theology of signs
       is distinctive.

v.15 — KJV: "a scourge of small cords" / ESV: "a whip of cords" — Both equivalent. Jesus deliberately made the whip — this was not an impulsive act but a deliberate, righteous response.

v.16 — KJV: "make not my Father's house an house of merchandise" / ESV: "do not make my Father's house a house of trade" — Both equivalent. Jesus claims unique relationship with God ("my Father") and zeal for pure worship.

v.19 — KJV: "Destroy this temple, and in three days I will raise it up" / ESV: "Destroy this temple, and in three days I will raise it up" — Both identical.
  [ASV/NET/WEB]: NET: "Destroy this temple and in three days I will raise
       it up" — "I will raise it" — Jesus claims to raise Himself.
       WEB: "Destroy this temple, and in three days I will raise
       it up." All versions preserve the ambiguity that confused
       His hearers but was clarified by His resurrection (v.22).

v.24 — KJV: "Jesus did not commit himself unto them" / ESV: "Jesus on his part did not entrust himself to them" — Greek "episteusen" — Same root as "believe" (pisteuo). The wordplay is intentional: they "believed" in Him (v.23), but He did not "believe in/entrust" Himself to them.
  [ASV/NET/WEB]: NET: "Jesus would not entrust himself to them, because he
       knew all people" — "entrust himself" (episteusen) — same
       Greek word as "believe." They believed in Him (v.23) but He
       didn't believe/entrust Himself to them. WEB: "didn't trust
       himself to them." The wordplay is deliberate.


GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Cana          — A small village in Galilee, about 9 miles north of Nazareth.
               Site of Jesus' first miracle.

Firkins       — An old English unit of liquid measure, approximately 9 gallons.
               Each stone pot held 2-3 firkins (18-27 gallons).

Passover      — The annual Jewish feast commemorating Israel's deliverance from
               Egypt (Exodus 12). One of three feasts requiring pilgrimage to
               Jerusalem. Jesus attended multiple Passovers during His ministry.

Scourge       — A whip made of twisted cords or leather strips, used for driving
               animals. Jesus fashioned one to drive out the Temple merchants.

Money changers — Men who exchanged foreign currency for the Temple shekel required
               for the Temple tax. They often charged excessive exchange rates,
               exploiting worshippers.

Manifested    — To make visible or reveal. John uses this word to describe how
               miracles reveal the true nature and glory of Christ.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Mary's instruction — "Whatever He says to you, do it" (v.5) — is the simplest
   and most complete description of the Christian life. Where are you currently
   hearing something from Christ that you haven't fully acted on yet?

2. Jesus saved the best wine for last (v.10). God's provision often exceeds our
   expectations and comes at the right time, not our time. Where in your life
   are you struggling to trust God's timing?

3. Jesus' zeal for His Father's house (v.17) shows that righteous anger has a
   place — when God's honor is at stake. As a man, are there things in your home,
   workplace, or community that should stir righteous anger in you? What would
   it look like to act on that?

4. "He knew what was in man" (v.25). Jesus doesn't need your resume or your
   reputation — He sees exactly who you are. That can be humbling or freeing
   depending on where you stand with Him. How does being fully known by Christ
   affect how you approach Him?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. The servants obeyed Jesus' instruction to fill the pots with water before
   they saw any miracle. They acted in faith before they saw results. What step
   of obedience is God asking you to take right now, before you see the outcome?

2. Jesus' first miracle was at a wedding — an ordinary social event. What does
   it say about God that He cares about the everyday details and potential
   embarrassments of our lives?

3. Jesus drove out the money changers with a whip He made Himself. This was
   deliberate, not impulsive. What is the difference between righteous anger and
   sinful anger? How do you tell the difference in your own life?

4. Many believed in Jesus because of His miracles (v.23), but Jesus didn't
   entrust Himself to them because He knew their hearts (v.24-25). What is the
   difference between being impressed by Jesus and truly trusting Him?

5. The disciples didn't fully understand Jesus' words about the Temple until
   after the resurrection (v.22). Is there something in Scripture you don't
   fully understand yet that you're trusting God to make clear in time?
