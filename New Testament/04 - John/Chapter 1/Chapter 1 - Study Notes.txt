John - Chapter 1
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
The eternal Word who was with God and was God becomes flesh — John the Baptist bears witness, and Jesus calls His first disciples, who begin to recognize Him as Messiah, Son of God, and King of Israel.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: The Apostle John, son of Zebedee, one of the twelve disciples and part
of Jesus' inner circle (with Peter and James). Early church tradition unanimously
attributes this Gospel to John.

Date Written: Approximately AD 85–95, making it the last of the four Gospels written.

Audience: Written for both Jewish and Gentile believers, with a strong theological
purpose stated in John 20:31 — "that you may believe that Jesus is the Christ,
the Son of God."

Historical Context: John wrote during a time when early heresies (particularly
Gnosticism) were beginning to challenge the full humanity and divinity of Christ.
His opening prologue directly counters these false teachings by establishing Jesus
as the eternal Word (Logos) who became flesh.

Purpose of Chapter 1: This chapter serves as the theological foundation of the
entire Gospel. It establishes who Jesus is (the eternal Word, the Son of God, the
Lamb of God), introduces John the Baptist as His forerunner, and records the
calling of the first disciples.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Bethany beyond the Jordan (v.28): The location where John baptized, east of the Jordan River near Jericho. Not the Bethany near Jerusalem.
- Nazareth (v.46): Philip tells Nathanael about Jesus of Nazareth. Nathanael's skeptical response ("Can any good thing come out of Nazareth?") reflects the town's insignificance.
- Galilee (v.43): Jesus called Philip and Nathanael in Galilee.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word
        was God." (ESV) — Establishes the pre-existence and deity of Christ.

v.14 — "And the Word became flesh and dwelt among us, and we have seen his
        glory, glory as of the only Son from the Father, full of grace and
        truth." (ESV) — The Incarnation — God becoming man.

v.12 — "But to all who did receive him, who believed in his name, he gave the
        right to become children of God." (ESV) — The offer of salvation
        to all who believe.

v.29 — "Behold, the Lamb of God, who takes away the sin of the world!" (ESV) —
        John the Baptist's declaration of Jesus as the atoning sacrifice.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.1   — Genesis 1:1 ("In the beginning...") — John intentionally echoes Genesis,
        showing Christ was present at creation.
v.1   — Colossians 1:16-17 — "All things were created by him and for him."
v.3   — Hebrews 1:2 — God made the universe through His Son.
v.4   — John 8:12 — "I am the light of the world."
v.12  — Romans 8:15-17 — Adoption as sons of God through faith.
v.14  — Philippians 2:6-8 — Christ emptying Himself, taking on human form.
v.17  — Romans 6:14 — Grace vs. the law.
v.23  — Isaiah 40:3 — The prophecy John the Baptist fulfills.
v.29  — Isaiah 53:7 — "He was led as a lamb to the slaughter."
v.51  — Genesis 28:12 — Jacob's ladder, now fulfilled in Christ as the connection
        between heaven and earth.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1  — Greek "Logos" (Word)
  KJV: "the Word" — identical across translations
  ESV: "the Word"
  ASV: "the Word"
  NET: "the Word was fully God" — uniquely adds "fully" to emphasize complete deity, countering any subordinationist reading
  WEB: "the Word was God"
  Note: The Logos concept carries rich meaning — in Greek philosophy it meant reason/logic; in Jewish thought it connected to God's creative speech in Genesis 1.

v.3  — KJV/ESV: "without him was not any thing made that was made" — Both identical.

v.5  — Greek "katelaben" — Can mean both "to understand/comprehend" AND "to overcome/seize"
  KJV: "the darkness comprehended it not" — chose the intellectual meaning
  ESV: "the darkness has not overcome it" — chose the conquering meaning
  ASV: "the darkness apprehended it not" — similar to KJV (grasped/understood)
  NET: "the darkness has not mastered it" — emphasizes the conquering aspect
  WEB: "the darkness hasn't overcome it" — aligns with ESV
  Note: Both meanings are theologically valid — the darkness neither understood nor defeated the Light.

v.9  — KJV: "which lighteth every man that cometh into the world"
  ESV: "which gives light to everyone, was coming into the world"
  Note: ESV clarifies that it is the Light (Christ) who was coming into the world, not every man.

v.11 — KJV: "his own received him not"
  ESV: "his own people did not receive him"
  Note: Both equivalent. ESV adds "people" for clarity.

v.12 — Greek "exousian"
  KJV: "gave he power to become the sons of God" — uses "power" (authority)
  ESV: "he gave the right to become children of God" — uses "right"
  Note: Both convey the idea of granted authority/privilege.

v.14 — Greek "monogenes" and "eskenosen"
  KJV: "the only begotten of the Father" — reflects older theological tradition of eternal generation
  ESV: "the only Son from the Father" — conveys uniqueness without implying a time of origin
  ASV: "the only begotten from the Father"
  NET: "took up residence among us" — uniquely vivid rendering of "eskenosen" (tabernacled), capturing tent imagery connecting to God's dwelling with Israel in the wilderness
  WEB: "lived among us"

v.16 — Greek "charin anti charitos"
  KJV: "grace for grace"
  ESV: "grace upon grace"
  Note: Both equivalent. The idea is grace replacing grace in continuous abundance.

v.18 — Greek "monogenes theos" vs. "monogenes huios" — significant textual variant
  KJV: "the only begotten Son, which is in the bosom of the Father"
  ESV: "God the only Son, who is at the Father's side" — follows the "God" (theos) reading based on older manuscripts
  ASV: "the only begotten Son, who is in the bosom of the Father"
  NET: "The only one, himself God, who is in closest fellowship with the Father" — follows "monogenes theos" AND adds "closest fellowship" for "eis ton kolpon." Most theologically explicit rendering.
  WEB: "The one and only Son, who is in the bosom of the Father"

v.34 — Significant textual variant
  KJV: "the Son of God" (Greek "huios")
  ESV: "the Son of God"
  ASV: "the Son of God"
  NET: "the Chosen One of God" (Greek "eklektos") — follows a different manuscript reading
  WEB: "the Son of God"

v.39 — Time reckoning
  KJV: "about the tenth hour"
  ESV: "about the tenth hour"
  ASV: "about the tenth hour"
  NET: "about four o'clock in the afternoon" — converts to modern time, assuming Jewish counting from 6 AM
  WEB: "about the tenth hour"

v.42 — KJV: "Cephas, which is by interpretation, A stone"
  ESV: "You shall be called Cephas (which means Peter)"
  ASV: "thou shalt be called Cephas (which is by interpretation, Peter)"
  NET: "You will be called Cephas (which is translated Peter)"
  WEB: "You are Simon the son of Jonah" — reads "Jonah" where KJV/ESV/NET read "John" (textual variant reflecting WEB's Majority Text base)
  Note: Cephas is Aramaic for Peter (Greek: Petros = rock/stone).

v.51 — Greek "Amen, amen"
  KJV: "Verily, verily, I say unto you"
  ESV: "Truly, truly, I say to you"
  ASV: "Verily, verily, I say unto you"
  NET: "I tell all of you the solemn truth" — captures the weight of Jesus' double-amen formula
  WEB: "Most certainly, I tell you all"


GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Logos       — Greek word translated "Word." Refers to the divine reason, the
               creative and revelatory expression of God. John uses it to identify
               Jesus as the eternal, personal expression of God.

Incarnation — The theological term for God becoming human in the person of Jesus
               Christ. From Latin "in carne" meaning "in flesh." See v.14.

Messias     — Aramaic form of "Messiah," meaning "Anointed One." Greek equivalent
               is "Christ." See v.41.

Cephas      — Aramaic name meaning "rock" or "stone." The name Jesus gave Simon
               Peter. See v.42.

Bethabara   — (also Bethany beyond the Jordan) — The location where John was
               baptizing, east of the Jordan River. See v.28.

Levites     — Members of the tribe of Levi, assistants to the priests in the
               Temple. They were sent to question John the Baptist. See v.19.

Pharisees   — A prominent Jewish religious sect known for strict observance of
               the Law and oral traditions. They sent the delegation to John. v.24.

Rabbi       — Hebrew/Aramaic title of respect meaning "my teacher" or "master."
               Used by disciples addressing Jesus. See v.38.

Guile       — Deceit, cunning, or craftiness. Jesus says Nathanael has none in
               him — a man of complete honesty. See v.47.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. The Word became flesh (v.14) — Jesus did not stay at a distance. He entered
   our world, our pain, our humanity. In your own life, where do you need to
   experience God not as a distant concept but as Emmanuel — God with us?

2. John the Baptist's entire purpose was to point others to Jesus (v.29). He
   had crowds following him, influence, and a platform — and he used it all to
   say "look at Him, not me." As a man, consider where your life is pointing
   people. Are your words, your work, your reputation pointing toward Christ?

3. "He came unto his own, and his own received him not" (v.11). Rejection is
   one of the deepest human pains. Jesus experienced it fully. How does knowing
   Christ understands rejection change how you handle it in your own life?

4. Andrew's first response after meeting Jesus was to find his brother Simon and
   bring him to Christ (vv.40-42). Who in your life — a brother, a friend, a
   coworker — needs you to say "We have found the Messiah"?

5. "What seek ye?" (v.38) — The first words Jesus speaks in John's Gospel are a
   question directed at those following Him. What are you truly seeking right now?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. John 1:1 declares Jesus was "in the beginning" with God and was God. What does
   the eternal nature of Christ mean to you personally? How does it change how you
   pray or relate to Him?

2. Verse 12 says that to all who received Him and believed in His name, He gave
   the right to become children of God. Have you personally received Him? What
   does it mean in your daily life to live as a son of God?

3. John the Baptist said "I am not the Christ" (v.20) and consistently deflected
   attention away from himself. In what areas of your life do you struggle with
   wanting credit or recognition? How can John's example challenge you?

4. Jesus saw Nathanael under the fig tree before Philip called him (v.48). Jesus
   sees you fully — your private moments, your doubts, your struggles. How does
   being fully known by Christ bring you comfort or conviction?

5. The chapter ends with Jesus promising Nathanael he will see "heaven open, and
   the angels of God ascending and descending upon the Son of man" (v.51). Jesus
   is the ladder between heaven and earth. What does it mean to you that access
   to God is only through Him?
