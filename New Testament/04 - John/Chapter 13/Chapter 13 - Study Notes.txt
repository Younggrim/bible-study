John - Chapter 13
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
At the Last Supper, Jesus washes His disciples' feet as a model of servant love, identifies Judas as His betrayer, and gives the new commandment: "Love one another as I have loved you."

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: The Apostle John (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 13 opens John's "Upper Room Discourse" — chapters
13-17, the most intimate and extended teaching Jesus gave His disciples. It takes
place on the night of His arrest, at the Last Supper. John does not record the
institution of the Lord's Supper (covered in the synoptic Gospels) but instead
focuses on the foot washing and the new commandment.

Foot Washing: In first-century Palestine, roads were dusty and people wore
sandals. Foot washing was the task of the lowest household servant — so menial
that Jewish law exempted Jewish slaves from doing it for their masters. For Jesus
— knowing He was Lord of all, knowing He was about to be glorified — to wash
His disciples' feet was an act of breathtaking humility.

Judas: Jesus knew from the beginning who would betray Him (v.11). He washed
Judas' feet. He gave him the place of honor at the table (the sop was given to
the guest of honor). He gave him every opportunity to turn back. Judas chose
betrayal anyway.

The New Commandment (v.34): Jesus calls love for one another "new" — not because
love was absent from the Old Testament, but because the standard is new: "as I
have loved you." The measure of Christian love is the cross.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- The Upper Room in Jerusalem: The foot washing and Last Supper discourse. Jesus and the disciples are in a large furnished room in Jerusalem on the night before His crucifixion.
- Jesus tells the disciples He is going to the Father — He is "going away" from this earthly place to prepare a place for them.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "Having loved his own who were in the world, he loved them to the
        end." (ESV) — The scope and persistence of Christ's love — to the
        uttermost.

v.14-15 — "If I then, your Lord and Teacher, have washed your feet, you also
        ought to wash one another's feet. For I have given you an example,
        that you also should do just as I have done to you." (ESV) — Servant
        leadership defined.

v.17 — "If you know these things, blessed are you if you do them." (ESV) —
        Knowledge without obedience produces no blessing.

v.34-35 — "A new commandment I give to you, that you love one another: just
        as I have loved you, you also are to love one another. By this all
        people will know that you are my disciples, if you have love for one
        another." (ESV) — The mark of a true disciple.

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1  — KJV: "he loved them unto the end" / ESV: "he loved them to the end" — Greek "eis telos" — Both equivalent. Can mean "to the end" (until death) or "to the uttermost/completely." Both meanings are true and likely intended.
  [ASV/NET/WEB]: NET: "he loved them to the end" — "to the end" (eis
       telos) — both temporal (until death) and qualitative (to
       the uttermost/completely). WEB: "loved them to the end."
       KJV: "he loved them unto the end." The washing that follows
       demonstrates the nature of this love.

v.2  — KJV: "the devil having now put into the heart of Judas" / ESV: "when the devil had already put it into the heart of Judas" — Both equivalent. The devil planted the seed; Judas cultivated it.

v.5  — NET: "he poured water into the washbasin and began to wash
       the disciples' feet" — the Lord of glory performing the
       slave's task. WEB: "began to wash the disciples' feet."
       KJV: "began to wash the disciples' feet." All versions
       preserve the shocking reversal of status.

v.10 — KJV: "He that is washed needeth not save to wash his feet, but is clean every whit" / ESV: "The one who has bathed does not need to wash, except for his feet, but is completely clean" — Greek "leloumenos" (bathed) vs. "nipsasthai" (wash) — Two different Greek words for washing. The picture: a person who has bathed (positional cleansing at salvation) only needs foot washing (ongoing cleansing from daily sin).

v.17 — KJV: "happy are ye if ye do them" / ESV: "blessed are you if you do them" — Greek "makarioi" — KJV uses "happy"; ESV uses "blessed." "Blessed" carries deeper meaning than mere happiness — it is the state of divine favor and spiritual well-being.

v.18 — KJV: "He that eateth bread with me hath lifted up his heel against me" / ESV: "He who ate my bread has lifted his heel against me" — Both equivalent. An idiom for treachery — like a horse kicking the person who feeds it. From Psalm 41:9.

v.23 — NET: "One of his disciples, the one Jesus loved, was at
       the table to the right of Jesus in a place of honor" — "the
       one Jesus loved" (hon ēgapa ho Iēsous). WEB: "One of his
       disciples, whom Jesus loved." KJV: "leaning on Jesus'
       bosom." The unnamed beloved disciple — traditionally John.

v.26 — KJV: "He it is, to whom I shall give a sop, when I have dipped it" / ESV: "It is he to whom I will give this morsel of bread when I have dipped it" — Greek "psomion" — KJV uses "sop" (archaic); ESV uses "morsel of bread." A piece of bread dipped in the common dish and given to a guest as a sign of honor.

v.27 — NET: "What you are about to do, do quickly" — spoken to
       Judas. WEB: "What you do, do quickly." KJV: "That thou
       doest, do quickly." Jesus does not prevent the betrayal —
       He commands its completion. Sovereign over His own death.

v.34 — KJV: "A new commandment I give unto you, That ye love one another; as I have loved you" / ESV: "A new commandment I give to you, that you love one another: just as I have loved you" — Both equivalent. The "newness" is not the command to love (Leviticus 19:18) but the standard: "as I have loved you." The cross sets a new benchmark.
  [ASV/NET/WEB]: NET: "I give you a new commandment — to love one another.
       Just as I have loved you, you also are to love one another"
       — "new" (kainēn) = new in quality, not just chronologically.
       WEB: "A new commandment I give to you." The standard is
       "as I have loved you" — self-sacrificial, unconditional.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Bason         — KJV spelling of "basin" — a bowl used for washing. Jesus
               poured water into a basin to wash the disciples' feet.

Girded        — Wrapped around the waist. Jesus wrapped a towel around Himself
               as a servant's apron before washing feet.

Sop           — A piece of bread dipped in the common dish and given to a guest.
               Giving the sop to a guest was a sign of honor and friendship.
               Jesus gave it to Judas — a final act of grace toward His betrayer.

Supper        — The Passover meal (Last Supper). Eaten in the evening, reclining
               at a low table — hence "leaning on Jesus' bosom" (v.23).

Disciple whom Jesus loved — John's self-reference throughout the Gospel. He
               never names himself but identifies himself by his relationship
               to Jesus — a model of identity rooted in Christ's love.

New commandment — Not new in the sense of never before given, but new in its
               standard: love as Christ loved — sacrificially, unconditionally,
               to the point of death.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Jesus washed the feet of the man He knew would betray Him (v.11). He served
   Judas with the same hands that would be nailed to the cross. Is there someone
   in your life who has betrayed or hurt you? What would it look like to serve
   them anyway — not because they deserve it, but because Christ is your model?

2. Peter's first response was refusal — "Thou shalt never wash my feet" (v.8).
   Pride often disguises itself as humility or reverence. Is there an area where
   your pride is preventing you from receiving what Christ wants to give you —
   forgiveness, help, correction, grace?

3. "If ye know these things, happy are ye if ye do them" (v.17). Knowledge of
   servant leadership is not the same as practicing it. Where in your life —
   at home, at work, in your church — are you called to serve in a way that
   feels beneath you? What is stopping you?

4. "By this shall all men know that ye are my disciples, if ye have love one
   to another" (v.35). The primary evidence of genuine Christianity is not
   doctrine, not church attendance, not moral behavior — it is love for other
   believers. How is your love for other Christians? Is it visible enough that
   outsiders would notice?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. Jesus knew He was Lord of all, that all things were in His hands, that He
   came from God and was going to God (v.3) — and from that position of total
   security, He washed feet. True humility flows from security, not insecurity.
   How does knowing who you are in Christ free you to serve others without
   needing recognition?

2. Peter swung from "You'll never wash my feet" to "Not just my feet but my
   hands and head too!" (vv.8-9). He was impulsive and extreme. Do you tend
   toward stubbornness or impulsiveness in your relationship with God? How
   does Jesus' patient response to Peter encourage you?

3. Jesus gave Judas the sop — a gesture of honor and friendship — even knowing
   what Judas was about to do. What does this tell you about how Jesus treats
   people who are in the process of rejecting Him? How should that shape how
   you treat people who are hostile to the Gospel?

4. "A new commandment I give unto you, That ye love one another; as I have
   loved you" (v.34). The standard is the cross. Is there a relationship in
   your life where you are loving at a comfortable level but not at a cross-level?
   What would cross-level love look like in that relationship?

5. Peter confidently declared he would lay down his life for Jesus (v.37) —
   and denied Him three times within hours. How do you guard against overconfidence
   in your own spiritual strength? What does healthy self-awareness look like
   for a man of faith?
