John - Chapter 4
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Jesus breaks social barriers by speaking with a Samaritan woman at Jacob's well, offers her living water, reveals Himself as the Messiah, and teaches that true worshippers worship the Father in spirit and in truth.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: The Apostle John (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 4 records two encounters that demonstrate Jesus
crossing major cultural boundaries — the Samaritan woman at the well, and the
healing of a Roman official's son.

Jews and Samaritans: The hostility between Jews and Samaritans ran deep. When
Assyria conquered the northern kingdom of Israel in 722 BC, they resettled the
land with foreign peoples who intermarried with remaining Israelites, producing
the Samaritans — a mixed race with a mixed religion. Jews considered them
unclean and avoided all contact. Jesus deliberately went through Samaria (v.4)
and spoke to a Samaritan woman — breaking two cultural taboos at once (race and
gender).

Jacob's Well: Still exists today near the city of Nablus (ancient Shechem/Sychar).
It is approximately 100 feet deep. Jesus sat at this well around noon (the sixth
hour) — the hottest part of the day, when respectable women did not draw water.
The woman's presence at that hour hints at her social isolation.

The Nobleman's Son: The second miracle in Galilee (v.54) demonstrates faith that
acts on Jesus' word alone, without requiring His physical presence.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Samaria / Sychar (v.5): Jesus passed through Samaria (the region between Judea and Galilee) and stopped at Jacob's well near the town of Sychar (modern Askar), at the foot of Mount Gerizim.
- Mount Gerizim (v.20): The Samaritan holy mountain where they built a rival temple. The woman references this in her question about worship.
- Cana of Galilee (v.46): Jesus returned to Cana where He healed the nobleman's son at a distance — the boy was in Capernaum, about 16 miles away.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.14 — "But whoever drinks of the water that I will give him will never be
        thirsty again. The water that I will give him will become in him a spring
        of water welling up to eternal life." (ESV) — Jesus as the source of
        lasting spiritual satisfaction.

v.23 — "But the hour is coming, and is now here, when the true worshipers will
        worship the Father in spirit and truth, for the Father is seeking such
        people to worship him." (ESV) — The nature of true worship.

v.24 — "God is spirit, and those who worship him must worship in spirit and
        truth." (ESV) — One of the clearest statements about God's nature and
        the requirement of authentic worship.

v.34 — "My food is to do the will of him who sent me and to accomplish his
        work." (ESV) — Jesus' consuming purpose — a model for every man's life
        mission.

v.50 — "Jesus said to him, 'Go; your son will live.' The man believed the word
        that Jesus spoke to him and went on his way." (ESV) — Faith that acts on
        Christ's word before seeing the result.

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.6  — KJV: "the sixth hour" / ESV: "the sixth hour" — Both identical. In Jewish reckoning, noon. In Roman reckoning, 6 AM. Most scholars favor noon here — explaining Jesus' weariness from travel and the woman's unusual timing (avoiding others).

v.9  — KJV: "the Jews have no dealings with the Samaritans" / ESV: "Jews have no dealings with Samaritans" — Both equivalent. The Greek could also mean Jews don't use the same vessels as Samaritans (ritual purity concern). The broader social separation is the main point.

v.10 — NET: "If you had known the gift of God and who it is who
       said to you, 'Give me some water to drink,' you would have
       asked him, and he would have given you living water" —
       "living water" (hydōr zōn) = flowing/spring water (literal)
       AND eternal life (spiritual). WEB: "living water." The
       double meaning drives the conversation.

v.14 — KJV: "a well of water springing up into everlasting life" / ESV: "a spring of water welling up to eternal life" — Greek "pege" — Both equivalent. ESV uses "spring" and "welling up" for more natural English; the image is of an artesian spring — water that flows from within without external effort.

v.23 — NET: "the true worshipers will worship the Father in
       spirit and truth" — "in spirit and truth" (en pneumati kai
       alētheia). WEB: "in spirit and truth." KJV: "in spirit and
       in truth." Not a physical location (Jerusalem or Gerizim)
       but an inward orientation and genuine reality.

v.24 — KJV: "God is a Spirit" / ESV: "God is spirit" — Greek "pneuma ho theos" — ESV drops the article "a" — God is not one spirit among many but is spirit in His very nature. The KJV's "a Spirit" can be slightly misleading; the Greek has no article here.
  [ASV/NET/WEB]: NET: "God is spirit, and the people who worship him must
       worship in spirit and truth" — "God is spirit" (pneuma ho
       theos) — a definitive statement about God's nature. WEB:
       "God is spirit." KJV: "God is a Spirit." The absence of the
       article in Greek argues against "a spirit" — God IS spirit
       by nature.

v.26 — NET: "I, the one speaking to you, am he" — Jesus' direct
       self-identification as Messiah to a Samaritan woman (He
       rarely stated this so explicitly). WEB: "I am he, the one
       who speaks to you." KJV: "I that speak unto thee am he."
       The Greek echoes "I AM" (egō eimi).

v.34 — KJV: "My meat is to do the will" / ESV: "My food is to do the will" — Greek "broma" — KJV's "meat" means food generally (archaic usage), not specifically flesh. Both convey the same meaning — Jesus' sustenance is obedience to the Father.

v.42 — KJV: "this is indeed the Christ, the Saviour of the world" / ESV: "this is indeed the Savior of the world" — Greek "soter tou kosmou" — ESV omits "the Christ" following older manuscripts. Both affirm the universal scope of Jesus' saving work.
  [ASV/NET/WEB]: NET: "we know that this one is truly the Savior of the
       world" — "Savior of the world" (sōtēr tou kosmou) — a title
       used in the Roman world for Caesar. The Samaritans apply it
       to Jesus. WEB: "the Savior of the world." A universal claim
       from outsiders who spent only two days with Him.

v.50 — KJV: "thy son liveth" / ESV: "your son will live" — Greek "ho huios sou ze" (present tense) — Both equivalent. The present tense "lives" indicates the healing is already accomplished at the moment Jesus speaks.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Samaria       — The region between Judea and Galilee, home of the Samaritans.
               Jews typically avoided it by crossing the Jordan River to travel
               around it. Jesus deliberately went through it.

Samaritans    — A mixed-race people descended from Israelites who intermarried
               with Assyrian settlers after 722 BC. They accepted only the
               Pentateuch (first 5 books of Moses) and worshipped on Mount
               Gerizim rather than in Jerusalem.

Living water  — In the literal sense, flowing spring water (as opposed to
               stagnant cistern water). Jesus uses it metaphorically for the
               Holy Spirit and eternal life — water that truly satisfies.

Jacob's well  — A well dug by the patriarch Jacob, located near Shechem (modern
               Nablus). Still exists today, approximately 100 feet deep.

Spirit and truth — True worship involves the inner person (spirit) genuinely
               engaging with God, and truth — worship aligned with who God
               actually is, not who we imagine Him to be.

Nobleman      — Greek "basilikos" — a royal official, likely in the service of
               Herod Antipas. A man of status who humbled himself before Jesus.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Jesus was physically exhausted (v.6) but still engaged the woman at the well.
   As men, we often use busyness or tiredness as reasons to disengage from the
   people around us. Who in your life needs your attention even when you're tired?

2. The woman had five failed marriages and was living with a man outside of
   marriage. Jesus didn't lead with condemnation — He led with an offer of living
   water. How does Jesus' approach to broken people challenge how you approach
   people in your life who are living in ways you disagree with?

3. Jesus said His food was to do the will of the Father and finish His work (v.34).
   What is the driving purpose of your life? What gets you up in the morning?
   How does it compare to Jesus' singular focus?

4. The nobleman believed Jesus' word and went home before he saw his son healed
   (v.50). He acted on the word alone. Where in your life is God asking you to
   trust His word before you see the evidence?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. Jesus crossed racial, cultural, and gender barriers to reach one woman. What
   barriers — social, racial, cultural — do you need to cross to reach people
   around you with the Gospel?

2. The woman tried to redirect the conversation from her personal life to a
   theological debate about worship locations (v.20). Do you ever use theological
   discussion to avoid personal conviction? What is God putting His finger on in
   your life right now?

3. Jesus said true worshippers worship in spirit and in truth (v.23-24). Evaluate
   your own worship — is it genuine (spirit) and is it grounded in who God
   actually is (truth)? Or has it become routine and performance-based?

4. The disciples were surprised Jesus was talking to the woman, but none of them
   asked why (v.27). They had assumptions about who Jesus should talk to. What
   assumptions do you carry about who deserves to hear the Gospel?

5. The Samaritans believed because they heard Jesus themselves (v.42) — not just
   because of the woman's testimony. Are you helping people get to a place where
   they can encounter Jesus personally, or are you keeping them at arm's length
   from Him?
