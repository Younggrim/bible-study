John - Chapter 12
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Mary anoints Jesus' feet with costly perfume, He enters Jerusalem to palm branches and "Hosanna," Greeks seek Him, He speaks of His death as a grain of wheat falling into the ground, and the Father's voice thunders from heaven.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: The Apostle John (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 12 marks the transition from Jesus' public ministry
to His final week. It contains four significant events: Mary's anointing of Jesus
(vv.1-8), the Triumphal Entry into Jerusalem (vv.12-19), the arrival of Greeks
seeking Jesus (vv.20-36), and John's theological summary of Israel's unbelief
(vv.37-50).

The Triumphal Entry: Jesus entered Jerusalem on a donkey, fulfilling Zechariah
9:9. The crowd waved palm branches and shouted "Hosanna" — a Hebrew word meaning
"Save now!" (Psalm 118:25-26). They expected a political deliverer; Jesus came
as a suffering servant. The same crowd would cry "Crucify Him" within the week.

The Greeks Seeking Jesus (vv.20-22): The arrival of Gentiles seeking Jesus
triggers His declaration "The hour is come" — the moment He had been pointing
toward throughout John's Gospel. Their arrival signals that the Gospel is about
to break beyond Israel to the whole world.

The Grain of Wheat (v.24): Jesus uses this agricultural image to explain His
death — a seed must die to produce a harvest. His death is not defeat but the
necessary path to multiplication.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Bethany (v.1): Six days before Passover, Jesus was honored with a dinner in Bethany. Mary anointed His feet with costly ointment.
- Jerusalem (v.12): The triumphal entry — Jesus rode from the Mount of Olives down into Jerusalem while crowds waved palm branches.
- The Temple (implied): Greeks came to Jerusalem for Passover and sought to see Jesus.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.24 — "Truly, truly, I say to you, unless a grain of wheat falls into the
        earth and dies, it remains alone; but if it dies, it bears much
        fruit." (ESV) — The principle of death producing life — the pattern
        of the cross.

v.25 — "Whoever loves his life loses it, and whoever hates his life in this
        world will keep it for eternal life." (ESV) — The paradox of
        discipleship — surrender is the path to true life.

v.32 — "And I, when I am lifted up from the earth, will draw all people to
        myself." (ESV) — The cross as the great magnet of the Gospel.

v.43 — "For they loved the glory that comes from man more than the glory that
        comes from God." (ESV) — One of the most convicting diagnoses of the
        human heart in Scripture.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.3  — KJV: "a pound of ointment of spikenard, very costly" / ESV: "a pound of expensive ointment made from pure nard" — Both equivalent. Spikenard (nard) was an aromatic oil imported from India, extremely expensive. 300 denarii (v.5) = approximately one year's wages for a laborer.
  [ASV/NET/WEB]: NET: "Then Mary took three quarters of a pound of
       expensive aromatic oil — pure nard — and anointed the feet
       of Jesus" — "three quarters of a pound" (litran = Roman
       pound, ~12 oz). KJV: "a pound." WEB: "a pound." NET
       converts to modern measurement. Worth a year's wages (v.5).

v.13 — KJV: "Hosanna: Blessed is the King of Israel that cometh in the name of the Lord" / ESV: "Hosanna! Blessed is he who comes in the name of the Lord, even the King of Israel!" — Both equivalent. Hebrew/Aramaic "hoshia na" — "Save now!" Originally a cry for help; by Jesus' time it had become a shout of praise and acclamation.

v.24 — KJV: "Except a corn of wheat fall into the ground and die" / ESV: "unless a grain of wheat falls into the earth and dies" — Both equivalent. KJV uses archaic "corn of wheat"; ESV uses modern "grain of wheat." The agricultural principle is the same.
  [ASV/NET/WEB]: NET: "unless a kernel of wheat falls into the ground and
       dies, it remains by itself alone. But if it dies, it
       produces much grain" — "kernel of wheat" vs. KJV "corn of
       wheat." WEB: "a grain of wheat." Jesus' own death as the
       paradigm for all fruitfulness — death precedes life.

v.25 — KJV: "He that loveth his life shall lose it; and he that hateth his life" / ESV: "Whoever loves his life loses it, and whoever hates his life" — Greek "miseo" — Both equivalent. "Hates" is a Semitic idiom for "loves less" or "puts in second place" — not literal hatred. The meaning: hold your life loosely, not tightly.

v.27 — NET: "Now my soul is in turmoil. What should I say —
       'Father, deliver me from this hour'? No, but for this very
       reason I have come to this hour" — "in turmoil" (tetaraktai
       = deeply disturbed). WEB: "Now my soul is troubled." KJV:
       "Now is my soul troubled." John's equivalent of Gethsemane.

v.32 — KJV: "will draw all men unto me" / ESV: "will draw all people to myself" — Greek "pantas" — Both equivalent. "All men/people" does not mean every individual without exception but people from all nations — Jew and Gentile alike. The arrival of the Greeks (v.20) is the immediate context.
  [ASV/NET/WEB]: NET: "And I, when I am lifted up from the earth, will draw
       all people to myself" — "lifted up" (hypsōthō) — double
       meaning: physically lifted on the cross AND exalted in glory.
       WEB: "will draw all people to myself." KJV: "will draw all
       men unto me." The cross as magnet.

v.40 — NET: "He has blinded their eyes and hardened their heart"
       — quoting Isaiah 6:10. The theological tension: divine
       sovereignty and human responsibility. WEB: "He has blinded
       their eyes and he hardened their heart." NET/ESV attribute
       the blinding to God; the context shows it follows their
       choice to reject.

v.43 — KJV: "they loved the praise of men more than the praise of God" / ESV: "they loved the glory that comes from man more than the glory that comes from God" — Greek "doxan" — KJV uses "praise"; ESV uses "glory." Both convey the same devastating diagnosis — preferring human approval over divine approval.


GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Spikenard     — An aromatic oil extracted from the nard plant, native to the
               Himalayas. Extremely expensive — imported from India. Used for
               anointing and perfuming. Mary's pound was worth a year's wages.

Hosanna       — Hebrew/Aramaic "hoshia na" — "Save now!" A cry from Psalm 118:25
               used as a shout of praise and messianic acclamation.

Triumphal Entry — Jesus' entry into Jerusalem on a donkey, five days before
               Passover, fulfilling Zechariah 9:9. Celebrated as Palm Sunday.

Lifted up     — A double meaning in John: (1) physically lifted up on the cross,
               (2) exalted/glorified. Jesus uses it to describe both His
               crucifixion and His glorification.

Prince of this world — A title for Satan — the one who currently exercises
               influence over the fallen world system. The cross would cast
               him out (v.31) — his power broken, his accusation silenced.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Mary poured out an entire year's wages on Jesus' feet — an act of extravagant,
   wasteful-looking worship. Judas called it waste. Jesus called it beautiful.
   What does extravagant devotion to Christ look like in your life? Is there
   anything you're holding back from Him?

2. "He that loveth his life shall lose it" (v.25). The man who grips his life
   tightly — his comfort, his reputation, his plans — loses it. The man who
   holds it loosely gains eternal life. What are you gripping too tightly right
   now?

3. "They loved the praise of men more than the praise of God" (v.43). This is
   the diagnosis of men who believed but wouldn't confess. Is there an area of
   your faith you're keeping private because of what others would think? What
   would it cost you to be more open?

4. The grain of wheat must die to produce fruit (v.24). What in your life needs
   to die — a habit, an ambition, a relationship pattern, a version of yourself
   — so that something fruitful can grow?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. Judas objected to Mary's "waste" of expensive ointment, claiming concern for
   the poor — but John tells us he was a thief (v.6). How do you discern when
   someone's objection to generosity or worship is genuine concern vs. a cover
   for selfish motives? How do you check your own motives?

2. The crowd shouted "Hosanna" and waved palms — and within days cried "Crucify
   Him." What does this tell you about the danger of crowd-driven faith? How do
   you guard against a faith that rises and falls with the emotional temperature
   of those around you?

3. The Greeks said "Sir, we would see Jesus" (v.21). That is still the deepest
   need of every person. When people encounter you, do they get closer to seeing
   Jesus, or further away? What would need to change for your life to be a
   clearer window to Christ?

4. Jesus said "Now is my soul troubled" (v.27) — and then immediately said
   "Father, glorify thy name." He moved from honest acknowledgment of anguish
   to surrender to God's purpose. How do you move from honest prayer about your
   pain to surrender? What does that look like practically?

5. Isaiah saw Christ's glory and spoke of Him (v.41). The entire Old Testament
   points to Jesus. How does knowing that the whole Bible is about Christ change
   how you read the Old Testament?
