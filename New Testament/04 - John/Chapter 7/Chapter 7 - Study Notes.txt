John - Chapter 7
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
At the Feast of Tabernacles, Jesus teaches in the temple amid growing division — some call Him a prophet, others the Christ — and on the last day He cries out, "If anyone thirsts, let him come to me and drink."

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: The Apostle John (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 7 takes place at the Feast of Tabernacles
(Sukkot) in Jerusalem — one of the three great pilgrimage feasts of Israel.
Tabernacles was a week-long celebration commemorating Israel's 40 years of
wilderness wandering, during which the people lived in temporary shelters
(booths/sukkot). It was also associated with prayers for rain and the
pouring out of water at the Temple altar.

Jesus' brothers (v.3-5) — likely sons of Joseph and Mary born after Jesus —
did not believe in Him at this point. Their challenge to "show yourself to
the world" echoes Satan's temptation in the wilderness (Matthew 4:6).

The Feast of Tabernacles Context: On the last and greatest day of the feast
(v.37), the high priest would pour water from the Pool of Siloam over the
altar — a ceremony praying for rain and recalling the water from the rock in
the wilderness. It is against this dramatic backdrop that Jesus stood and
cried out "If any man thirst, let him come unto me and drink."

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Jerusalem / the Temple (v.14): Jesus went up to Jerusalem for the Feast of Tabernacles (a major pilgrimage feast) and taught in the Temple courts.
- Galilee (v.1): Jesus had been staying in Galilee, avoiding Judea because the Jews there sought to kill Him.
- The Pool of Siloam (implied, cf. 9:7): The water-pouring ceremony during Tabernacles drew water from the Pool of Siloam — the backdrop for Jesus' "rivers of living water" declaration.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.17 — "If anyone's will is to do God's will, he will know whether the teaching
        is from God or whether I am speaking on my own authority." (ESV) —
        Obedience is the pathway to spiritual understanding.

v.24 — "Do not judge by appearances, but judge with right judgment." (ESV)
        — A call to look beneath the surface and evaluate rightly.

v.37-38 — "If anyone thirsts, let him come to me and drink. Whoever believes in
        me, as the Scripture has said, 'Out of his heart will flow rivers of
        living water.'" (ESV) — The open invitation of the Gospel.

v.46 — "No one ever spoke like this man!" (ESV) — The temple officers sent to
        arrest Jesus return empty-handed, undone by His words.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.2  — KJV: "the Jews' feast of tabernacles" / ESV: "the Jews' Feast of Booths" — Greek "skenopegia" — KJV uses "tabernacles" (Latin-derived); ESV uses "Booths" (more literal translation of the Hebrew "sukkot"). Both refer to the same feast.

v.5  — NET: "For not even his own brothers believed in him" —
       a striking detail. WEB: "For even his brothers didn't
       believe in him." KJV: "For neither did his brethren believe
       in him." Jesus' own family doubted until after the
       resurrection (cf. Acts 1:14, James as leader).

v.8  — KJV: "I go not up yet unto this feast" / ESV: "I am not going up to this feast, for my time has not yet fully come" — Some manuscripts omit "yet" (ESV footnote). If "yet" is original, Jesus is saying His time hasn't fully come; if omitted, it creates an apparent contradiction with v.10. Most scholars favor including "yet" as original.

v.17 — NET: "If anyone wants to do God's will, he will know about
       my teaching, whether it is from God or whether I speak from
       my own authority" — obedience precedes understanding. WEB:
       "if anyone desires to do his will, he will know about the
       teaching." KJV: "If any man will do his will, he shall know
       of the doctrine."

v.24 — KJV: "Judge not according to the appearance, but judge righteous judgment" / ESV: "Do not judge by appearances, but judge with right judgment" — Both equivalent. Jesus calls for discernment based on substance, not surface.

v.37 — KJV: "If any man thirst, let him come unto me, and drink" / ESV: "If anyone thirsts, let him come to me and drink" — Both equivalent. The open invitation of the Gospel, spoken against the backdrop of the water-pouring ceremony.
  [ASV/NET/WEB]: NET: "If anyone is thirsty, let him come to me, and let
       the one who believes in me drink" — punctuation debated:
       does "rivers of living water" flow from Christ or from the
       believer? WEB: "If anyone is thirsty, let him come to me
       and drink." NET's punctuation links drinking to believing.

v.38 — KJV: "out of his belly shall flow rivers of living water" / ESV: "Out of his heart will flow rivers of living water" — Greek "koilia" — Literally means belly/stomach but is used idiomatically for the innermost being. ESV's "heart" captures the meaning better for modern readers.

v.39 — KJV: "the Holy Ghost was not yet given" / ESV: "the Spirit had not been given" — Both equivalent. The Spirit existed eternally but had not yet been poured out in the new covenant fullness that came at Pentecost (Acts 2).

v.46 — NET: "No one ever spoke like this man!" — the officers'
       report when they return empty-handed. WEB: "No man ever
       spoke like this man!" KJV: "Never man spake like this man."
       Even those sent to arrest Him were disarmed by His words.

v.53–8:11 — KJV: includes the story of the woman caught in adultery as part of the text / ESV: brackets the passage with a note that the earliest manuscripts do not include it — The passage is absent from the oldest manuscripts. Most modern translations include it but note its uncertain textual history. It is consistent with Jesus' character.
  [ASV/NET/WEB]: NET: "So each one departed to his own home" — this verse
       and 7:53-8:11 (the woman caught in adultery) are absent
       from the earliest manuscripts. NET includes them with
       brackets. WEB includes them. The passage is likely authentic
       tradition, though not originally part of John's Gospel.


GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Feast of Tabernacles — Hebrew "Sukkot." A 7-day harvest festival in the fall
               commemorating Israel's wilderness wandering. One of three
               annual pilgrimage feasts requiring attendance in Jerusalem.

Sukkot        — Hebrew plural of "sukkah" (booth/shelter). Temporary structures
               built during the feast to recall Israel's wilderness dwellings.

Jewry         — KJV term for Judea, the southern region of Israel centered on
               Jerusalem. Jesus avoided it because the leaders there sought
               to kill Him.

Dispersed     — The Diaspora — Jews living outside of Israel scattered throughout
               the Roman Empire and beyond.

Tabernacles   — The temporary shelters (booths) built during the feast. Also
               the name of the feast itself.

Rivers of living water — A metaphor for the Holy Spirit's work in and through
               a believer — not a trickle but an overflowing abundance.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Jesus' own brothers didn't believe in Him (v.5). Even those closest to us
   may not understand or support our faith. How do you handle skepticism or
   lack of support from family members regarding your faith?

2. Jesus said "If any man will do his will, he shall know" (v.17). Spiritual
   understanding comes through obedience, not just study. Is there something
   God has already shown you that you haven't acted on? Could that be blocking
   deeper understanding?

3. The officers sent to arrest Jesus came back saying "Never man spake like
   this man" (v.46). They were undone by His words. When did the words of
   Jesus last stop you in your tracks? Are you reading His words with that
   kind of openness?

4. Nicodemus — a member of the Sanhedrin — spoke up for Jesus at personal risk
   (vv.50-51). He didn't fully commit publicly yet, but he took a step. What
   step of courage is God asking you to take in standing for what is right,
   even when it costs you socially or professionally?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. Jesus' brothers challenged Him to "show yourself to the world" (v.4) — to
   use His power for public recognition. Jesus refused. How do you handle the
   pressure to prove yourself, to perform, or to seek public validation?

2. Jesus said the world hates Him because He testifies that its works are evil
   (v.7). If you are living like Christ, you should expect some level of the
   world's hostility. Are you experiencing any friction with the world because
   of your faith? Or has your faith become too comfortable and accommodating?

3. Verse 17 says that willingness to do God's will leads to knowing whether
   Jesus' teaching is from God. Is your approach to Scripture "show me and
   I'll obey" or "I'll obey and you'll show me"? What's the difference?

4. The crowd was divided about Jesus (v.43) — some said prophet, some said
   Christ, some said He was deceiving people. The same divisions exist today.
   How do you respond when people around you have wrong views of Jesus?

5. "Never man spake like this man" (v.46). The officers couldn't arrest Jesus
   because His words arrested them first. When you read the Gospels, do you
   read them with that kind of expectation — that His words will stop you,
   challenge you, change you?
