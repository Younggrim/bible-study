John - Chapter 21
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
The risen Jesus appears at the Sea of Galilee, provides a miraculous catch of fish, restores Peter with three questions ("Do you love me? Feed my sheep"), and prophesies Peter's death — the disciple whom Jesus loved bears witness to these things.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: The Apostle John — the "disciple whom Jesus loved" (v.20,24).

Historical Context: Chapter 21 is an epilogue to John's Gospel, recording a
post-resurrection appearance of Jesus by the Sea of Galilee (also called the
Sea of Tiberias). It focuses on two things: the miraculous catch of fish and
the restoration of Peter.

Peter's Restoration: Peter had denied Jesus three times around a charcoal fire
(John 18:18,25-27). Now Jesus meets him at another charcoal fire (v.9) and asks
him three times "Do you love me?" — one question for each denial. This is not
punishment but restoration — Jesus publicly reinstating Peter to his calling
before the other disciples.

The Greek Words for Love: In vv.15-17, Jesus uses "agapao" (unconditional,
sacrificial love) in His first two questions. Peter responds with "phileo"
(brotherly affection, friendship love). In the third question, Jesus uses
"phileo" — meeting Peter where he is. This is not Peter lowering the standard;
it is Jesus graciously accepting Peter's honest answer and working with it.

153 Fish (v.11): The exact number has fascinated scholars for centuries. Various
explanations: (1) the number of known species of fish at the time, (2) a
symbolic number, (3) simply an eyewitness detail. The net did not break — a
picture of the church holding all who come to Christ.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- The Sea of Tiberias (Sea of Galilee, v.1): The post-resurrection appearance occurred on the shore of the Sea of Galilee. The disciples had returned to fishing in Galilee.
- Galilee: Jesus had told the disciples to meet Him in Galilee (Matt. 28:10). This chapter records that meeting — back where their journey with Jesus first began.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.6  — "Cast the net on the right side of the boat, and you will find
        some." (ESV) — Obedience to Christ's specific direction produces
        abundance.

v.15 — "Simon, son of John, do you love me more than these?" (ESV) — The
        question that restores Peter — and the question Jesus asks every man.

v.17 — "Lord, you know everything; you know that I love you." (ESV) —
        Peter's appeal to Christ's omniscience — the most honest prayer a
        man can pray.

v.19 — "Follow me." (ESV) — The same two words Jesus spoke when He first
        called Peter (Matthew 4:19). After failure, the call is the same.

v.22 — "What is that to you? You follow me!" (ESV) — The answer to every
        comparison and distraction from our own calling.

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.6  — NET: "Throw your net on the right side of the boat, and
       you will find some" — the catch of 153 fish (v.11). WEB:
       "Cast the net on the right side of the boat." KJV: "Cast
       the net on the right side." The specific number 153 has
       generated much speculation; Jerome said it represented every
       known species of fish — universal mission.

v.7  — NET: "It is the Lord!" — the beloved disciple recognizes
       Him first (perception); Peter jumps in immediately (action).
       WEB: "It's the Lord!" KJV: "It is the Lord." The two
       disciples represent two responses to the risen Christ —
       contemplation and impulsive devotion.

vv.15-17 — KJV: "lovest thou me" (all three questions) / ESV: "do you love me" (all three questions) — Both obscure the Greek distinction in English. In vv.15-16, Jesus uses "agapaō" (unconditional love); Peter responds with "phileō" (friendship/affection). In v.17, Jesus switches to "phileō" — meeting Peter at his level. ESV/NET footnotes note this distinction. The shift is significant — Jesus accepts Peter's honest answer rather than demanding what Peter cannot yet give.
  KJV: "more than these" / ESV: "more than these" — Both identical. Could mean: (1) more than the other disciples love Jesus, (2) more than Peter loves these other disciples, or (3) more than Peter loves these things (fishing, his former life). Most scholars favor (1) — recalling Peter's boast "Even if all fall away, I will not" (Matthew 26:33).
  KJV: "Feed my lambs... Feed my sheep... Feed my sheep" / ESV: "Feed my lambs... Tend my sheep... Feed my sheep" — Greek uses different words: "boske" (feed/nourish) and "poimaine" (shepherd/tend). ESV distinguishes them; KJV uses "feed" for all three. The commission includes both nourishing and leading.
  [ASV/NET/WEB]: NET: "Simon son of John, do you love me more than these?"
       — "love" — the famous interchange between agapaō and phileō.
       Jesus asks agapas (self-sacrificial love); Peter answers
       philō (friendship love). WEB: "do you love me?" The
       significance of the word shift is debated but noticeable.

v.18 — KJV: "thou shalt stretch forth thy hands, and another shall gird thee" / ESV: "you will stretch out your hands, and another will dress you and carry you where you do not want to go" — Both equivalent. A clear reference to crucifixion. John confirms this in v.19: "This he said to show by what kind of death he was to glorify God." Peter was crucified in Rome, tradition says upside down, around AD 64-68.
  [ASV/NET/WEB]: NET: "when you are old, you will stretch out your hands,
       and others will tie you up and bring you where you do not
       want to go" — "stretch out your hands" = crucifixion. WEB:
       "you will stretch out your hands." KJV: "thou shalt stretch
       forth thy hands." John notes this signified Peter's death
       (v.19) — tradition says crucified upside-down.

v.19 — KJV: "Follow me" / ESV: "Follow me" — Both identical. The same two words Jesus spoke when He first called Peter (Matthew 4:19). After failure, the call is the same.

v.22 — KJV: "If I will that he tarry till I come, what is that to thee? follow thou me" / ESV: "If it is my will that he remain until I come, what is that to you? You follow me!" — Both equivalent. KJV uses archaic "tarry"; ESV uses "remain." The answer to every comparison and distraction from our own calling.

v.25 — KJV: "the world itself could not contain the books that should be written" / ESV: "the world itself could not contain the books that would be written" — Both equivalent. A hyperbolic statement emphasizing the inexhaustible nature of Christ's works and words.
  [ASV/NET/WEB]: NET: "There are many other things that Jesus did. If every
       one of them were written down, I suppose the whole world
       would not have room for the books that would be written" —
       hyperbole expressing the inexhaustible richness of Christ.
       WEB: "I suppose that even the world itself wouldn't have
       room for the books." The Gospel closes with overflow.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Sea of Tiberias — Another name for the Sea of Galilee, named after the Roman
               city of Tiberias on its western shore. A freshwater lake in
               northern Israel, about 13 miles long and 8 miles wide.

Charcoal fire — Greek "anthrakia" — the same word used in John 18:18 for the
               fire where Peter warmed himself when he denied Jesus. The
               deliberate echo connects the restoration to the failure.

Agapao        — Greek for unconditional, sacrificial, self-giving love. The
               love of God. Jesus uses this word in His first two questions
               to Peter.

Phileo        — Greek for brotherly affection, friendship love, warm personal
               attachment. Peter uses this word in all three of his responses.

Feed my sheep — Jesus' commission to Peter — pastoral care of God's people.
               "Feed" (Greek: boske/poimaine) means to provide nourishment
               and to shepherd/lead. Peter's calling was to care for Christ's
               flock.

153           — The exact number of fish caught. John records it as an
               eyewitness detail. The net did not break despite the number —
               a picture of the church's capacity to hold all who come.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Peter went back to fishing after the resurrection — back to what he knew,
   back to his old life. When you've failed or feel disqualified, do you go
   back to your old life? Jesus came to find Peter there — and He comes to
   find you where you've retreated to as well.

2. Jesus asked Peter "Do you love me?" three times — once for each denial.
   He didn't ignore the failure or pretend it didn't happen. He addressed it
   directly and specifically. Is there a failure in your past that you need
   Jesus to address directly — not to condemn you but to restore you?

3. "Lord, thou knowest all things; thou knowest that I love thee" (v.17).
   Peter stopped defending himself and appealed to Christ's knowledge of his
   heart. That is the most honest prayer a man can pray — "Lord, you know."
   What do you need to bring to Jesus with that kind of honesty?

4. "Follow me" — after all the failure, the same call. Jesus doesn't give
   Peter a new, reduced calling. He gives him the same one. What calling has
   God placed on your life that failure or shame has caused you to step back
   from? Is it time to hear "Follow me" again?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. The disciples fished all night and caught nothing — then obeyed Jesus'
   specific instruction and caught 153 fish. Where in your life are you
   working hard in your own strength and coming up empty? What specific
   instruction from Christ are you not following?

2. Jesus prepared breakfast for His disciples (v.9-13). The risen Lord of
   the universe made them breakfast. What does this tell you about the kind
   of God you serve — His attention to ordinary needs, His care for the
   practical details of your life?

3. Jesus asked Peter "Do you love me more than these?" (v.15). What are the
   "these" in your life — the things that compete with your love for Christ?
   Career, comfort, reputation, relationships? How would you honestly answer
   Jesus' question right now?

4. Peter asked about John — "Lord, and what shall this man do?" Jesus said
   "What is that to thee? Follow thou me" (vv.21-22). We often compare our
   calling, our suffering, our path to others'. How does Jesus' answer to
   Peter speak to your tendency to compare yourself to others?

5. John closes by saying the world itself could not contain the books that
   could be written about Jesus (v.25). The Gospel accounts are a selection —
   the most important things, chosen to produce faith. What does it mean to
   you that the infinite God has been made knowable through the finite words
   of Scripture?
