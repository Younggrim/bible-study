John - Chapter 3
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Jesus tells Nicodemus that no one can see the kingdom of God unless born again of water and the Spirit, declares "God so loved the world that He gave His only begotten Son," and John the Baptist testifies "He must increase, but I must decrease."

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: The Apostle John (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 3 contains one of the most famous conversations in
Scripture — Jesus and Nicodemus — and one of the most quoted verses in the Bible
(John 3:16). Nicodemus was a Pharisee and a member of the Sanhedrin, the Jewish
ruling council of 70 men. He came to Jesus at night, likely to avoid public
scrutiny from his peers.

The chapter also records John the Baptist's final testimony about Jesus, including
his famous statement "He must increase, but I must decrease" (v.30) — a model of
humility and God-centered purpose.

Theological Significance: This chapter introduces the doctrine of regeneration
(being "born again") — the supernatural work of the Holy Spirit that transforms
a person from the inside. Jesus makes clear that religious position, education,
and moral effort cannot earn entrance into God's kingdom. New birth is required.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Jerusalem (v.1): Nicodemus came to Jesus in Jerusalem — he was a Pharisee and member of the Sanhedrin.
- Aenon near Salim (v.23): John was baptizing at Aenon ("springs") near Salim, because "there was much water there." The exact location is debated — possibly in Samaria or the Jordan Valley.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.3  — "Truly, truly, I say to you, unless one is born again he cannot see the
        kingdom of God." (ESV) — The necessity of spiritual rebirth — Jesus'
        non-negotiable requirement.

v.16 — "For God so loved the world, that he gave his only Son, that whoever
        believes in him should not perish but have eternal life." (ESV)
        — The Gospel in one verse.

v.17 — "For God did not send his Son into the world to condemn the world, but
        in order that the world might be saved through him." (ESV) — God's
        motive: salvation, not condemnation.

v.30 — "He must increase, but I must decrease." (ESV) — John the Baptist's
        defining statement of God-centered humility.

v.36 — "Whoever believes in the Son has eternal life; whoever does not obey the
        Son shall not see life, but the wrath of God remains on him." (ESV) —
        The two eternal destinies plainly stated.

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.3  — KJV: "born again" / ESV: "born again" — Greek "anothen" — Both identical in rendering, though "anothen" carries both meanings simultaneously — born again (a second time) AND born from above (from God). Nicodemus understood it as "again" (v.4); Jesus meant "from above." The double meaning is intentional.
  [ASV/NET/WEB]: NET: "unless a person is born from above, he cannot see
       the kingdom of God" — "from above" (anōthen) — a word with
       double meaning: "again" (KJV/WEB) AND "from above." NET
       chooses "from above"; Nicodemus hears "again" (v.4). The
       ambiguity is intentional in the Greek.

v.5  — KJV: "born of water and of the Spirit" / ESV: "born of water and the Spirit" — Both equivalent. "Water" is debated — interpretations include: (1) physical birth (amniotic fluid), (2) baptism, (3) the Word of God (Ephesians 5:26), (4) John's baptism of repentance. Most commentators favor either physical birth or the cleansing work of the Spirit (Ezekiel 36:25-27).

v.8  — KJV: "The wind bloweth where it listeth" / ESV: "The wind blows where it wishes" — Greek "pneuma" — Both equivalent. The same Greek word means both "wind" and "spirit" — Jesus uses the wordplay intentionally.
  [ASV/NET/WEB]: NET: "The wind blows wherever it will, and you hear the
       sound it makes, but do not know where it comes from and
       where it is going" — "wind" and "Spirit" are the same Greek
       word (pneuma). WEB: "The wind blows where it wants to."
       The wordplay is untranslatable — the Spirit/wind is
       sovereign and mysterious.

v.14 — KJV: "even so must the Son of man be lifted up" / ESV: "so must the Son of Man be lifted up" — Greek "hupsothenai" — Both equivalent. "Lifted up" carries double meaning: physically on the cross and exalted in glory.

v.16 — KJV: "only begotten Son" / ESV: "only Son" — Greek "monogenes" — KJV's "only begotten" emphasizes eternal generation; ESV's "only" emphasizes uniqueness. "Monogenes" means one-of-a-kind — Jesus is the unique Son of God, unlike any other.
  [ASV/NET/WEB]: NET: "For this is the way God loved the world: He gave his
       one and only Son" — "one and only" (monogenē) vs. KJV "only
       begotten." WEB: "his one and only Son." ASV: "his only
       begotten Son." NET avoids "begotten" since monogenēs means
       "unique/one of a kind" rather than "generated/begotten."

v.18 — KJV: "he that believeth not is condemned already" / ESV: "whoever does not believe is condemned already" — Both equivalent. Greek "kekritai" (perfect tense) — condemnation is a present state, not merely a future event.

v.30 — NET: "He must become more important while I become less
       important" — "more important/less important" vs. KJV's
       elegant "He must increase, but I must decrease." WEB: "He
       must increase, but I must decrease." ASV: "He must increase,
       but I must decrease." KJV's phrasing remains superior here.

v.36 — KJV: "he that believeth not the Son" / ESV: "whoever does not obey the Son" — Greek "apeithon" — Means both "disbelieve" and "disobey." KJV chose the belief aspect; ESV chose the obedience aspect. True belief and obedience are inseparable in John's theology.
  [ASV/NET/WEB]: NET: "the one who does not obey the Son will not see life,
       but God's wrath remains on him" — "does not obey" (ho
       apeithōn) vs. KJV "believeth not." WEB: "doesn't obey the
       Son." The Greek word means both "disbelieve" and "disobey"
       — unbelief IS disobedience.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Pharisee      — Member of a strict Jewish religious sect emphasizing exact
               observance of the Law and oral traditions. Nicodemus was both
               a Pharisee and a member of the Sanhedrin.

Sanhedrin     — The supreme Jewish council of 70 elders plus the high priest,
               responsible for religious and civil matters. Nicodemus was a
               "ruler of the Jews" — a member of this body.

Born again    — Greek "anothen" — to be born from above, by the Spirit of God.
               A supernatural transformation of the inner person, not a
               physical or moral reformation.

Regeneration  — The theological term for the new birth — God's act of giving
               spiritual life to a person who was spiritually dead.

Aenon         — A location near Salim where John was baptizing, noted for
               having "much water." Likely in the Jordan Valley.

Condemnation  — Greek "krisis" — judgment. Those who reject Christ are already
               under judgment because they have rejected the only remedy for sin.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Nicodemus came to Jesus at night — perhaps out of fear of what others would
   think. Many men live their faith privately, afraid of peer judgment. Is there
   an area of your faith you're keeping in the dark? What would it look like to
   bring it into the light?

2. Jesus told a highly educated, religiously accomplished man that he needed to
   start over — be born again. Religious activity, moral effort, and good
   reputation cannot substitute for genuine spiritual transformation. Have you
   experienced the new birth, or are you relying on your own goodness?

3. John the Baptist said "He must increase, but I must decrease" (v.30). This
   is the posture of a man who knows his purpose. As a man — in your career,
   your family, your ministry — are you building your own name or pointing to
   Christ? What would it look like to decrease in a specific area this week?

4. John 3:16 is the most memorized verse in the Bible, but familiarity can dull
   its impact. Read it slowly: God SO loved — the world — that He GAVE. What
   does it mean to you personally that God gave His Son for you specifically?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. Nicodemus was a religious expert who didn't understand the most basic truth
   about entering God's kingdom. What does this say about the danger of
   substituting religious knowledge for genuine relationship with God?

2. Jesus compared the new birth to the wind — you can't see it, but you see its
   effects (v.8). What evidence of the Spirit's work do you see in your own life?
   What evidence would others see?

3. The bronze serpent in Numbers 21 — those who were bitten by snakes were healed
   by looking at the bronze serpent Moses lifted up. Jesus says He must be "lifted
   up" the same way (v.14). What does it mean to "look to" Christ for salvation?
   Is that a one-time act or ongoing?

4. Verse 19 says men loved darkness rather than light because their deeds were
   evil. Honesty check: Is there an area of your life you're keeping in the dark
   because you don't want it exposed? What would it take to bring it to the light?

5. John the Baptist's disciples were upset that people were leaving them to follow
   Jesus (v.26). John's response was joy, not jealousy (v.29). How do you respond
   when someone else gets the credit or the following you feel you deserve?
