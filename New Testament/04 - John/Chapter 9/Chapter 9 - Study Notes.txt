John - Chapter 9
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Jesus heals a man born blind, sparking an investigation by the Pharisees who expel the man from the synagogue — while the healed man's faith grows from "a man called Jesus" to worshipping Him as Lord, Jesus declares the Pharisees' spiritual blindness.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: The Apostle John (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 9 records the healing of a man born blind — the
sixth sign miracle in John's Gospel. It is one of the most carefully structured
narratives in the New Testament, moving from physical blindness to spiritual
sight, while the religious leaders move in the opposite direction — from physical
sight to spiritual blindness.

The chapter unfolds in seven scenes: (1) the disciples' question, (2) the
healing, (3) the neighbors' reaction, (4) the first Pharisee interrogation,
(5) the parents' testimony, (6) the second Pharisee interrogation, (7) Jesus
finding the man and revealing Himself.

Blindness from Birth: In first-century Jewish thought, disability was often
assumed to be divine punishment for sin. The disciples' question (v.2) reflects
this assumption. Jesus directly corrects it — this man's blindness was not
punishment but preparation for a display of God's glory.

Excommunication (v.22): The threat of being "put out of the synagogue" was
serious — it meant social, religious, and economic exclusion from the community.
The parents' fear of this explains their evasive answers.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Jerusalem: The healing of the man born blind occurred in Jerusalem, still during the period around the Feast of Tabernacles.
- The Pool of Siloam (v.7): Located at the southern end of the City of David in Jerusalem. Fed by the Gihon Spring through Hezekiah's tunnel. Jesus sent the blind man to wash here.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.3  — "It was not that this man sinned, or his parents, but that the works of
        God might be displayed in him." (ESV) — Suffering is not always
        punishment — sometimes it is the stage for God's glory.

v.4  — "We must work the works of him who sent me while it is day; night is
        coming, when no one can work." (ESV) — The urgency of purposeful living.

v.25 — "Whether he is a sinner I do not know. One thing I do know, that though
        I was blind, now I see." (ESV) — The power of personal testimony.

v.35-38 — Jesus seeks out the man after his excommunication and reveals Himself.
        The man's response: "Lord, I believe." And he worshiped him. (ESV)

v.41 — "If you were blind, you would have no guilt; but now that you say, 'We
        see,' your guilt remains." (ESV) — The danger of spiritual
        self-sufficiency.

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.3  — KJV: "that the works of God should be made manifest in him" / ESV: "that the works of God might be displayed in him" — Greek "phanerōthē" — Both equivalent. KJV uses "manifest"; ESV uses "displayed." Both convey that suffering can be the stage for God's glory.
  [ASV/NET/WEB]: NET: "Neither this man nor his parents sinned, but he
       was born blind so that the acts of God may be revealed
       through him" — Jesus rejects the sin-causation framework
       for suffering. WEB: "that the works of God might be revealed
       in him." Not punishment but platform for glory.

v.4  — KJV: "I must work the works of him that sent me" / ESV: "We must work the works of him who sent me" — Some manuscripts read "I" (KJV); others read "we" (ESV). If "we," Jesus is including His disciples in the urgency of the mission. Both readings are theologically consistent.

v.5  — NET: "As long as I am in the world, I am the light of the
       world" — "as long as I am" implies a limited time. WEB:
       "While I am in the world, I am the light of the world."
       Connects to the works He "must" do while it is day (v.4).

v.6  — KJV: "he spat on the ground, and made clay of the spittle" / ESV: "he spit on the ground and made mud with the saliva" — Greek "pelon" — KJV uses "clay"; ESV uses "mud." Both refer to the same substance — dirt mixed with saliva.

v.7  — KJV: "the pool of Siloam, (which is by interpretation, Sent)" / ESV: "the pool of Siloam (which means Sent)" — Both equivalent. John translates the Hebrew name for his Greek-speaking audience. "Siloam" = "sent" — a pointer to Jesus, who was also "sent" by the Father.

v.22 — KJV: "put out of the synagogue" / ESV: "put out of the synagogue" — Greek "aposunagōgos" — Both identical. A technical term for formal excommunication — a severe social and religious penalty.

v.25 — KJV: "one thing I know, that, whereas I was blind, now I see" / ESV: "One thing I do know, that though I was blind, now I see" — Both equivalent. The power of personal testimony — simple, undeniable, and unanswerable.
  [ASV/NET/WEB]: NET: "One thing I do know, that although I was blind, now
       I can see" — the healed man's testimony: simple, undeniable,
       personal experience. WEB: "One thing I know: that though I
       was blind, now I see." KJV: "one thing I know, that,
       whereas I was blind, now I see." Theology can be debated;
       experience cannot.

v.35 — KJV: "Dost thou believe on the Son of God?" / ESV: "Do you believe in the Son of Man?" — Greek manuscripts vary between "Son of God" (huiou tou theou) and "Son of Man" (huiou tou anthrōpou). ESV follows older manuscripts reading "Son of Man." Either way, the man's worship in v.38 confirms he understood Jesus' divine identity.
  [ASV/NET/WEB]: NET: "Do you believe in the Son of Man?" — textual variant:
       "Son of Man" (NET/ESV, earliest manuscripts) vs. "Son of
       God" (KJV/WEB, later manuscripts). Both titles apply to
       Jesus, but the original likely read "Son of Man" — Jesus'
       preferred self-designation.

v.39 — NET: "For judgment I have come into this world, so that
       those who do not see may gain their sight, and those who
       see may become blind" — the great reversal: physical
       blindness cured, spiritual blindness exposed. WEB: "that
       those who don't see may see; and that those who see may
       become blind." Irony as theology.

v.41 — KJV: "ye should have no sin: but now ye say, We see; therefore your sin remaineth" / ESV: "you would have no guilt; but now that you say, 'We see,' your guilt remains" — Greek "hamartia" — KJV uses "sin"; ESV uses "guilt." Both convey that claiming spiritual sight while rejecting Christ leaves one without excuse.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Born blind    — Congenital blindness — present from birth, not acquired through
               illness or injury. No medical cure existed in the ancient world.

Pool of Siloam — A reservoir in Jerusalem fed by the Gihon Spring through
               Hezekiah's tunnel (2 Kings 20:20). Still exists today. The name
               means "sent."

Excommunication — Formal exclusion from the synagogue community. In first-century
               Judaism this meant loss of religious standing, social relationships,
               and often economic livelihood.

Synagogue     — The local Jewish assembly for worship, prayer, and Torah reading.
               The center of Jewish community life outside the Temple.

Clay          — Jesus mixed dirt with saliva to make clay and applied it to the
               man's eyes. The method was unusual — perhaps to test obedience,
               perhaps to echo God forming man from dust (Genesis 2:7).

Manifest      — To make visible, to reveal, to display openly. God's works are
               "manifested" — made visible — through human circumstances.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. The disciples assumed the man's blindness was punishment for sin (v.2). We
   often make the same assumption about people's hardships. How does Jesus'
   answer — "that the works of God should be made manifest" — change how you
   view suffering in your own life or in the lives of people around you?

2. The man's testimony was simple: "I was blind, now I see" (v.25). He didn't
   have all the theology figured out — he just knew what had happened to him.
   What is your personal testimony? Can you state it simply and clearly?

3. The man's parents were afraid of excommunication and threw their son under
   the bus (vv.21-23). Fear of social consequences silenced them. Where in your
   life does fear of what others think silence your testimony or compromise your
   integrity?

4. After the man was cast out, Jesus went looking for him (v.35). When you are
   rejected, excluded, or alone because of your faith, Jesus seeks you out. How
   does that truth speak to where you are right now?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. Jesus said the man's blindness was "that the works of God should be made
   manifest in him" (v.3). Is there a difficulty, limitation, or painful
   circumstance in your life that God might be using as a stage for His glory?
   What would it look like to embrace that perspective?

2. The healed man's understanding of Jesus grew through the chapter: first "a
   man called Jesus" (v.11), then "a prophet" (v.17), then "Son of God" (v.35-38).
   How has your understanding of Jesus grown over time? Where are you in that
   progression?

3. The Pharisees were so committed to their position that they refused to accept
   an undeniable miracle. What would it take for you to change your mind about
   something you believe strongly? Are you open to being wrong?

4. The man stood alone before the religious authorities and gave a courageous,
   even sarcastic defense of Jesus (vv.27-33). He had everything to lose. What
   does courage in the face of opposition look like for you in your specific
   context — at work, in your family, in your community?

5. Jesus said those who claim to see but are spiritually blind have no excuse
   (v.41). Religious knowledge without genuine faith is more dangerous than
   ignorance. How do you guard against the kind of spiritual pride that blinds
   you to your own need?
