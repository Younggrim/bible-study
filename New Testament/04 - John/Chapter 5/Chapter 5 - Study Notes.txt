John - Chapter 5
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Jesus heals a man at the Pool of Bethesda on the Sabbath, provoking persecution, and delivers a discourse on His divine authority — "the Son can do nothing by Himself" yet has authority to give life and execute judgment.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: The Apostle John (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 5 marks a turning point in John's Gospel — from this
point forward, the Jewish religious leaders actively seek to kill Jesus. The
chapter opens with a healing at the Pool of Bethesda in Jerusalem, which triggers
a Sabbath controversy, which leads to Jesus' most extensive discourse on His
relationship with the Father and the witnesses that testify to His identity.

Pool of Bethesda: Archaeological excavations in Jerusalem have confirmed the
existence of this pool near the Sheep Gate (modern St. Anne's Church area). It
had five covered colonnades (porches) and was believed to have healing properties
when the water was stirred. The pool was used by the sick, blind, lame, and
paralyzed who waited for the water's movement.

The Sabbath Controversy: Jewish law prohibited carrying objects on the Sabbath.
When Jesus healed the man and told him to carry his mat, the religious leaders
focused on the law violation rather than the miracle. Jesus' response — "My
Father is working until now, and I am working" (v.17) — was understood as a
claim to equality with God, intensifying their desire to kill Him.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Jerusalem / the Pool of Bethesda (v.2): Located near the Sheep Gate in northeast Jerusalem. The pool had five covered porticoes where the sick gathered. Archaeological remains confirm this five-portico structure.
- The Temple (implied): Jesus healed on the Sabbath in Jerusalem, provoking the Jewish leaders.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.6  — "Do you want to be healed?" (ESV) — Jesus' penetrating question to a man
        who had been ill 38 years. A question He still asks today.

v.17 — "My Father is working until now, and I am working." (ESV) — Jesus' claim
        of co-equal activity with God the Father — understood by His hearers as
        a claim to deity.

v.24 — "Truly, truly, I say to you, whoever hears my word and believes him who
        sent me has eternal life. He does not come into judgment, but has passed
        from death to life." (ESV) — The assurance of salvation for the believer.

v.39 — "You search the Scriptures because you think that in them you have eternal
        life; and it is they that bear witness about me." (ESV) — The entire Old
        Testament points to Christ.

v.44 — "How can you believe, when you receive glory from one another and do not
        seek the glory that comes from the only God?" (ESV) — The deadly trap of
        living for human approval.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.2  — KJV: "by the sheep market a pool" / ESV: "by the Sheep Gate a pool" — Greek "probatike" — KJV supplies "market"; ESV supplies "Gate." Most modern translations favor "Sheep Gate" (a gate in Jerusalem's wall, Nehemiah 3:1) rather than a sheep market.
  [ASV/NET/WEB]: NET: "Now there is in Jerusalem by the Sheep Gate a
       pool called Bethzatha in Aramaic" — "Bethzatha" (NET) vs.
       "Bethesda" (KJV/WEB/ASV). The name varies in manuscripts:
       Bethesda, Bethzatha, Bethsaida. Archaeological discoveries
       confirmed a five-porticoed pool north of the temple.

v.3-4 — KJV: includes v.4 about the angel troubling the water / ESV: omits v.4 (placed in footnote) — The phrase about the angel is absent from many older manuscripts. ESV and most modern translations place it in brackets or footnotes. Most scholars consider it a later scribal addition explaining the popular belief about the pool.

v.4  — NET omits v.4 entirely (the angel stirring the water) as
       not in the earliest manuscripts. KJV includes it. WEB
       includes it in brackets. ASV omits it. This is one of the
       most significant textual variants in John — the later
       addition likely explains the popular belief mentioned in v.7.

v.6  — KJV: "Wilt thou be made whole?" / ESV: "Do you want to be healed?" — Greek "hugiēs" — Both equivalent. KJV's "whole" captures the broader sense of complete restoration; ESV's "healed" is more specific to physical healing.

v.17 — KJV: "My Father worketh hitherto, and I work" / ESV: "My Father is working until now, and I am working" — Both equivalent. Jesus claims continuous, uninterrupted co-activity with the Father — including on the Sabbath.
  [ASV/NET/WEB]: NET: "My Father is working until now, and I too am working"
       — "My Father" (not "our Father") — Jesus claims a unique
       relationship. WEB: "My Father is still working, so I am
       working, too." The Jews understood this as a claim to
       equality with God (v.18).

v.24 — KJV: "shall not come into condemnation; but is passed from death unto life" / ESV: "does not come into judgment, but has passed from death to life" — Greek "krisin" — KJV uses "condemnation"; ESV uses "judgment." Both convey that the believer is exempt from the final verdict.
  [ASV/NET/WEB]: NET: "the one who hears my word and believes the one who
       sent me has eternal life" — "has" (echei) — present tense.
       Eternal life is a present possession, not just future hope.
       WEB: "has eternal life." KJV: "hath everlasting life." The
       present tense is emphatic in all translations.

v.39 — KJV: "Search the scriptures" / ESV: "You search the Scriptures" — Greek "eraunate" — Can be read as a command (imperative) or a statement (indicative). ESV favors the indicative: Jesus is not commanding them to search but pointing out that they already do — yet miss the point.
  [ASV/NET/WEB]: NET: "You study the scriptures thoroughly because you think
       in them you possess eternal life, and it is these same
       scriptures that testify about me" — "study thoroughly"
       (eraunate) can be indicative ("you do search") or imperative
       ("search!"). NET/ESV take it as indicative (a statement);
       KJV takes it as imperative (a command).

v.44 — KJV: "How can ye believe, which receive honour one of another" / ESV: "How can you believe, when you receive glory from one another" — Greek "doxan" — KJV uses "honour"; ESV uses "glory." Both identify the desire for human approval as a barrier to genuine faith.


GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Bethesda      — Hebrew/Aramaic name meaning "house of mercy" or "house of grace."
               The pool in Jerusalem where Jesus healed the paralyzed man.

Impotent      — KJV term meaning powerless, unable — referring to those who were
               physically disabled or chronically ill.

Sabbath       — The seventh day of the Jewish week (Saturday), set apart for rest
               and worship per the fourth commandment (Exodus 20:8-11). Jewish
               oral law had expanded Sabbath restrictions far beyond Scripture.

Quickeneth    — KJV term meaning "gives life to" or "makes alive." Used of both
               physical resurrection and spiritual regeneration.

Condemnation  — Greek "krisis" — judgment, being found guilty. Believers in
               Christ have passed out of this state permanently (v.24).

Dispersed     — Jews living outside of Israel, scattered throughout the Roman
               Empire and beyond. Also called the Diaspora.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Jesus asked the man "Do you want to be made whole?" (v.6). The man's answer
   was full of excuses — no one to help him, others get there first. Jesus healed
   him anyway. Are there areas of your life where you've given up wanting to
   change? What excuses are you hiding behind?

2. After healing the man, Jesus found him in the Temple and said "sin no more,
   lest a worse thing come unto thee" (v.14). Physical healing and spiritual
   wholeness are connected. Is there a sin pattern in your life that is
   producing consequences you keep experiencing?

3. Jesus said the religious leaders searched the Scriptures but missed the One
   the Scriptures pointed to (v.39-40). It's possible to be very familiar with
   the Bible and still not know Christ personally. How is your Bible reading
   leading you to a deeper relationship with Jesus, not just more knowledge?

4. "How can ye believe, which receive honour one of another?" (v.44). Living for
   human approval is one of the greatest obstacles to genuine faith. Where in
   your life are you making decisions based on what others will think rather than
   what God says?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. The man at the pool had been ill for 38 years. That's a long time to wait.
   Is there something in your life you've been waiting on God for so long that
   you've stopped expecting Him to act? How does this story speak to that?

2. Jesus healed the man on the Sabbath, knowing it would cause controversy. He
   didn't ask permission from the religious establishment. Are there times when
   doing the right thing will cost you socially or professionally? How do you
   navigate that?

3. Jesus presents four witnesses to His identity: John the Baptist, His miracles,
   the Father's voice, and the Scriptures (vv.33-47). Which of these is most
   compelling to you personally, and why?

4. Verse 44 identifies the desire for human approval as a barrier to faith. In
   what specific relationships or settings do you find yourself most tempted to
   seek man's approval over God's? What would it look like to break that pattern?

5. Jesus told the religious leaders that Moses — the very one they trusted —
   would accuse them (v.45). What is the danger of trusting in religious heritage
   or tradition rather than in Christ Himself?
