John - Chapter 6
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Jesus feeds five thousand, walks on water, and delivers the Bread of Life discourse — "I am the bread of life; he that cometh to me shall never hunger" — causing many disciples to turn back while Peter confesses "Thou hast the words of eternal life."

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: The Apostle John (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 6 is the longest chapter in John's Gospel and
contains two of Jesus' most dramatic miracles — feeding 5,000 men (plus women
and children) and walking on water — followed by His most controversial teaching:
the Bread of Life discourse. The chapter ends with mass defection from His
followers and Peter's great confession.

Feeding the 5,000: The only miracle (besides the resurrection) recorded in all
four Gospels. It took place near the Sea of Galilee (also called the Sea of
Tiberias) shortly before Passover. The crowd's response — wanting to make Jesus
king by force (v.15) — reveals they wanted a political messiah who would feed
them and overthrow Rome, not a spiritual Savior who would die for their sins.

The Bread of Life Discourse: Delivered in the synagogue at Capernaum (v.59),
this teaching was so hard for many followers that they left (v.66). Jesus
deliberately did not soften it to keep the crowd. He was separating those who
followed for bread from those who followed in genuine faith.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- The Sea of Galilee / Sea of Tiberias (v.1): The feeding of the 5,000 occurred on the northeast shore near Bethsaida. Jesus walked on the water crossing to Capernaum.
- Capernaum / the synagogue (v.59): The Bread of Life discourse was delivered in the synagogue at Capernaum.
- Tiberias (v.23): A city on the western shore of the Sea of Galilee, built by Herod Antipas. Boats from Tiberias came near where the miracle occurred.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.35 — "I am the bread of life; whoever comes to me shall not hunger, and
        whoever believes in me shall never thirst." (ESV) — The first of seven
        "I AM" statements in John's Gospel.

v.37 — "All that the Father gives me will come to me, and whoever comes to me
        I will never cast out." (ESV) — The security of the believer —
        Jesus will never reject anyone who comes to Him.

v.44 — "No one can come to me unless the Father who sent me draws him." (ESV)
        — The sovereignty of God in salvation.

v.63 — "It is the Spirit who gives life; the flesh is no help at all. The words
        that I have spoken to you are spirit and life." (ESV)

v.68 — "Lord, to whom shall we go? You have the words of eternal life." (ESV) —
        Peter's confession — the only reasonable response to Christ.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.7  — KJV: "Two hundred pennyworth of bread" / ESV: "Two hundred denarii worth of bread" — Greek "denarion" — KJV uses archaic "pennyworth"; ESV uses the actual coin name "denarii." A denarius was one day's wage — 200 denarii = approximately 8 months' wages.
  [ASV/NET/WEB]: NET: "Two hundred silver coins worth of bread would not
       be enough for each one to get a little" — "silver coins"
       (dēnariōn = denarii, about 8 months' wages). KJV: "two
       hundred pennyworth." WEB: "Two hundred denarii worth."
       Philip calculates impossibility; Jesus has already decided
       what He will do (v.6).

v.10 — KJV: "Make the men sit down" / ESV: "Have the people sit down" — Greek "anthropous" — Can mean people generally. John notes "the men sat down, in number about five thousand" — the Greek "andres" (men specifically) is used for the count. Matthew 14:21 adds "besides women and children" — total crowd likely 15,000-20,000.

v.19 — KJV: "five and twenty or thirty furlongs" / ESV: "about three or four miles" — Greek "stadious" — KJV uses the literal Greek measurement; ESV converts to modern miles for clarity.

v.20 — KJV: "It is I; be not afraid" / ESV: "It is I; do not be afraid" — Greek "ego eimi" — Both equivalent. The phrase "It is I" is literally "I AM" — an echo of the divine name (Exodus 3:14).
  [ASV/NET/WEB]: NET: "It is I. Do not be afraid" — "It is I" (egō eimi =
       I AM). WEB: "It is I. Don't be afraid." KJV: "It is I; be
       not afraid." The phrase functions both as identification and
       as a divine name claim — "I AM" walks on water.

v.27 — KJV: "Labour not for the meat which perisheth" / ESV: "Do not work for the food that perishes" — Both equivalent. KJV's "meat" means food generally (archaic); "labour" becomes "work." Jesus is not condemning honest work but redirecting ultimate priorities.

v.35 — NET: "I am the bread of life. The one who comes to me will
       never go hungry, and the one who believes in me will never
       be thirsty" — the first of John's seven "I AM" statements
       with predicates. WEB: "I am the bread of life." All
       versions — the offer is absolute and unconditional.

v.44 — KJV: "except the Father which hath sent me draw him" / ESV: "unless the Father who sent me draws him" — Greek "helkuo" — Both identical in meaning. "Draw" means to drag or pull with force — the same word used for hauling in a fishing net (John 21:6). This is not a gentle invitation but an irresistible drawing.

v.53 — NET: "unless you eat the flesh of the Son of Man and drink
       his blood, you have no life in yourselves" — the most
       debated verse in John. WEB: "Unless you eat the flesh of
       the Son of Man and drink his blood, you don't have life in
       yourselves." Eucharistic? Metaphorical? All translations
       preserve the shocking language without softening it.

v.54 — KJV: "Whoso eateth my flesh, and drinketh my blood" / ESV: "Whoever feeds on my flesh and drinks my blood" — Greek "trogon" — KJV uses "eateth"; ESV uses "feeds on." The Greek word means to munch or gnaw — a vivid, physical word used to emphasize the reality of spiritual participation in Christ.

v.63 — KJV: "the flesh profiteth nothing" / ESV: "the flesh is no help at all" — Both equivalent. Jesus is not denigrating His physical body but clarifying that a literal, physical eating of His flesh is not what He means. The spiritual reality is what gives life.

v.68 — NET: "Lord, to whom would we go? You have the words of
       eternal life" — Peter's confession when many leave. WEB:
       "Lord, to whom would we go? You have the words of eternal
       life." KJV: "Lord, to whom shall we go? thou hast the words
       of eternal life." Not "we understand" but "where else could
       we possibly go?

v.69 — KJV: "thou art that Christ, the Son of the living God" / ESV: "you are the Holy One of God" — Greek "ho hagios tou theou" — ESV follows older manuscripts reading "Holy One of God"; KJV follows manuscripts reading "Christ, the Son of the living God." Both affirm Peter's confession of Jesus' divine identity.


GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Pennyworth    — A denarius, a Roman silver coin representing one day's wage
               for a laborer. 200 denarii = approximately 8 months' wages.

Barley loaves — Barley bread was the food of the poor. Five small loaves and
               two fish — a child's lunch — became the raw material for a
               miracle feeding thousands.

Furlongs      — A unit of distance, approximately 1/8 of a mile. 25-30 furlongs
               = about 3-4 miles across the Sea of Galilee.

Manna         — Hebrew for "what is it?" — the miraculous bread God provided
               for Israel in the wilderness for 40 years (Exodus 16). Jesus
               presents Himself as the true manna from heaven.

I AM          — The divine name God revealed to Moses (Exodus 3:14). Jesus'
               "I am the bread of life" is the first of seven "I AM" statements
               in John, each claiming divine identity.

Murmured      — To grumble or complain under one's breath. The same word used
               of Israel's complaints against God in the wilderness — John
               draws the parallel deliberately.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. The crowd followed Jesus because He fed them (v.26), not because of who He
   was. It's easy to follow Jesus when He's giving you what you want. What
   happens to your faith when He doesn't? What are you really following Him for?

2. Jesus said "him that cometh to me I will in no wise cast out" (v.37). No
   matter what you've done, no matter how far you've wandered — if you come to
   Him, He will not turn you away. Do you believe that for yourself?

3. Many disciples walked away when the teaching got hard (v.66). Jesus didn't
   chase them or soften the message. He turned to the twelve and asked "Will
   you also go away?" As a man, when following Christ costs you something —
   relationships, reputation, comfort — what is your answer to that question?

4. Peter said "Lord, to whom shall we go? You have the words of eternal life"
   (v.68). This is not blind loyalty — it's reasoned conviction. Peter had
   looked at every alternative and concluded there was none. Have you reached
   that same conclusion? What would it take?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. Jesus tested Philip by asking where they would buy bread, "for he himself
   knew what he would do" (v.6). God often allows us to face impossible
   situations He already has a plan for. How does knowing God tests us with
   problems He already has answers to change how you face difficulties?

2. The boy gave his five loaves and two fish — everything he had — and Jesus
   multiplied it. What small, seemingly insufficient thing do you have that
   God might want you to place in His hands?

3. After the feeding, the crowd wanted to make Jesus king by force (v.15).
   They wanted a Jesus who served their agenda. In what ways are you tempted
   to shape Jesus into the kind of Savior you want rather than accepting Him
   as He is?

4. Jesus said "No man can come to me, except the Father draw him" (v.44).
   If you are a believer, God drew you to Christ. How does that truth affect
   your gratitude, your humility, and your confidence in your salvation?

5. When many disciples left, Jesus asked the twelve "Will you also go away?"
   Peter answered for himself — not for the group. Your faith is personal.
   If Jesus asked you that question today, what would your honest answer be?
