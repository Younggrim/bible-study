1 Timothy - Chapter 1
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Paul charges Timothy to combat false teachers in Ephesus, recounts his own conversion as the "chief of sinners" saved by grace, and urges Timothy to wage the good warfare — holding faith and a good conscience.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Paul the Apostle (v.1). Written approximately AD 62-64, after Paul's
release from his first Roman imprisonment (Acts 28). First Timothy is one of
the three "Pastoral Epistles" (with 2 Timothy and Titus) — letters written to
individual pastors about church leadership and ministry.

Recipient: Timothy — Paul's "own son in the faith" (v.2). Timothy was from
Lystra (Acts 16:1), had a Jewish mother (Eunice) and Greek father, and became
Paul's closest ministry partner. He was young (4:12), somewhat timid (2 Tim
1:7), and had stomach problems (5:23). Paul left him in Ephesus to deal with
false teachers and organize the church.

Purpose: To instruct Timothy on how to lead the church at Ephesus — dealing
with false teaching, organizing worship, establishing leadership qualifications,
and maintaining personal integrity. Paul writes "that thou mayest know how thou
oughtest to behave thyself in the house of God" (3:15).

Chapter 1 Overview: Paul charges Timothy to confront false teachers (vv.3-11),
shares his own testimony of grace (vv.12-17), and recommits the charge to
Timothy (vv.18-20).

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.5 — "The aim of our charge is love that issues from a pure heart and a
 good conscience and a sincere faith." (ESV) — The goal of all teaching:
 love from a pure heart.

v.15 — "The saying is trustworthy and deserving of full acceptance, that
 Christ Jesus came into the world to save sinners, of whom I am the
 foremost." (ESV) — The gospel summarized; Paul's humility.

v.16 — "In me, as the foremost, Jesus Christ might display his perfect
 patience as an example to those who were to believe in him for
 eternal life." (ESV) — Paul as exhibit A of God's patience.

v.17 — "To the King of the ages, immortal, invisible, the only God, be
 honor and glory forever and ever." (ESV) — Spontaneous doxology.

v.18-19 — "Wage the good warfare, holding faith and a good conscience." (ESV)
 — Ministry as warfare requiring faith and integrity.

 Sermon: "Chief of Sinners" on v.15 (MTP Vol. 15, No. 849)
 "Of whom I am chief. Not 'was' — am. Present tense. Paul never got over

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.2 — KJV: "my own son in the faith" / ESV: "my true child in the faith" — Greek "gnēsiō teknō" — genuine
 child, true-born son. Timothy was Paul's spiritual son — converted
 and mentored by Paul.

v.4 — KJV: "fables and endless genealogies" / ESV: "endless genealogies" — Likely Jewish speculative
 traditions about OT genealogies, combined with Gnostic myths. They
 produced "questions" (debates) rather than "godly edifying" (God's
 work of building up faith).

v.5 — KJV: "the end of the commandment" / ESV: "the aim of our charge" — Greek "telos" — the goal, aim,
 purpose. The goal of all instruction is love — not knowledge, not
 debate, not speculation.

v.8 — KJV: "the law is good, if a man use it lawfully" / ESV: "good, if one uses it lawfully" — The Law has a
 proper use: to expose sin and drive sinners to Christ (Gal 3:24). It
 was not designed for the righteous but for the lawless (v.9).

v.15 — KJV: "a faithful saying" / ESV: "saying" — Greek "pistos ho logos" — a trustworthy
 word. A formula Paul uses 5 times in the Pastorals to introduce
 important doctrinal statements.

v.17 — KJV: "King eternal, immortal, invisible" / ESV: "King of the ages, immortal, invisible" — Three attributes: eternal
 (no beginning or end), immortal (incorruptible, undying), invisible
 (unseen by human eyes). A burst of worship.

v.19 — KJV: "shipwreck" / ESV: Both equivalent. — Greek "enauagēsan" — to suffer shipwreck. Faith
 is like a ship; a good conscience is the hull. Damage the conscience
 and the ship of faith sinks.

v.20 — KJV: "delivered unto Satan" / ESV: "Satan" — Excommunication — removing someone from
 the church's protection into Satan's domain. Purpose: discipline that
 leads to repentance ("that they may learn").

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
v.15 — NET: "Christ Jesus came into the world to save sinners
       — and I am the worst of them!" — "worst" (prOtos = first/
       chief). WEB: "of whom I am chief." KJV: "of whom I am
       chief." Paul doesn't say "was" but "am" — present tense
       humility, not just past-tense testimony.

v.17 — NET: "Now to the eternal king, immortal, invisible, the
       only God, be honor and glory forever and ever!" — "eternal
       king" (basilei tOn aiOnOn = king of the ages). WEB: "the
       King eternal." KJV: "the King eternal." A spontaneous
       doxology prompted by remembering his own salvation.

v.18 — NET: "fight the good fight" — "fight" (strateuE = wage
       warfare/campaign). WEB: "that you may wage the good warfare."
       KJV: "war a good warfare." Military language — Timothy is a
       soldier, not just a pastor.

v.19 — NET: "holding to faith and a good conscience, which some
       have rejected and so have suffered shipwreck in regard to
       the faith" — "shipwreck" (enauagEsan = suffered a wreck at
       sea). WEB: "made a shipwreck concerning the faith." Faith
       can be wrecked by abandoning conscience.

v.20 — NET: "Among these are Hymenaeus and Alexander, whom I
       handed over to Satan to be taught not to blaspheme" —
       "handed over to Satan" (paredOka tO satana) — excommunication
       with redemptive purpose (cf. 1 Cor 5:5). WEB: "delivered to
       Satan, that they might be taught not to blaspheme." Discipline
       as education.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Son in the faith — A spiritual child — someone converted and mentored by a
 spiritual father. Timothy was Paul's closest disciple.

Fables and genealogies — Speculative Jewish traditions and myths that
 produced debate rather than faith. Useless for spiritual growth.

End of the commandment — The goal/purpose of all Christian instruction: love
 from a pure heart, good conscience, and sincere faith.

Faithful saying — A formula in the Pastoral Epistles introducing key
 doctrinal truths. "You can trust this."

Chief of sinners — Paul's self-assessment. Not false humility but genuine
 awareness of his past persecution of Christ's church.

Shipwreck of faith — The destruction of faith caused by abandoning a good
 conscience. Moral failure leads to doctrinal failure.

Delivered unto Satan — Excommunication from the church as discipline.
 Removing the protective covering of fellowship so the person
 experiences the consequences of sin and repents.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "The end of the commandment is charity" (v.5). Is love the goal of your
 Bible study, your theology, your church involvement? Or has knowledge
 become an end in itself? Does your doctrine produce love?

2. "Christ Jesus came into the world to save sinners; of whom I am chief"
 (v.15). Do you see yourself as a great sinner saved by great grace? Or
 have you forgotten how much you've been forgiven? The more you see your
 sin, the more you'll marvel at grace.

3. "In me first Jesus Christ might shew forth all longsuffering, for a
 pattern" (v.16). Your testimony of grace is meant to encourage others.
 If God saved you, He can save anyone. Are you sharing your story?

4. "War a good warfare" (v.18). Ministry and the Christian life are warfare.
 Are you fighting or coasting? What battles are you currently engaged in?

5. "Holding faith, and a good conscience" (v.19). Faith and conscience go
 together. When you violate your conscience, your faith is damaged. Are
 you protecting your conscience? Where might you be compromising?
