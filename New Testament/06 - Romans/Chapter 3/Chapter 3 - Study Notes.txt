Romans - Chapter 3
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
All humanity — Jew and Gentile alike — stands guilty before God with no one righteous, but now God's righteousness is revealed apart from the Law through faith in Jesus Christ, who is set forth as a propitiation by His blood.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Paul (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 3 completes Paul's argument that ALL humanity is
guilty before God — Gentile (ch.1), moralist (ch.2:1-16), and Jew (ch.2:17-29).
Then comes the great turning point: "But now" (v.21) — the righteousness of God
apart from the Law has been revealed. This is the heart of the Gospel: justification
by grace through faith.

The Universal Indictment (vv.9-20): Paul strings together Old Testament quotations
(Psalms, Isaiah, Ecclesiastes) to prove that "there is none righteous, no, not
one" (v.10). Every mouth is stopped. The whole world is guilty before God. The
Law cannot save — it can only reveal sin.

"But Now" (vv.21-26): The most important two words in Romans. After establishing
universal guilt, Paul reveals God's solution: "the righteousness of God which is
by faith of Jesus Christ unto all and upon all them that believe" (v.22). This
righteousness is: (1) apart from the Law, (2) witnessed by the Law and Prophets,
(3) through faith in Christ, (4) for all who believe, (5) freely given by grace,
(6) through the redemption in Christ, (7) based on Christ's propitiation.

Romans 3:23-26 is arguably the most theologically dense passage in the entire
Bible — containing the doctrines of universal sin, justification, grace,
redemption, propitiation, and God's righteousness in one paragraph.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.10 — "None is righteous, no, not one." (ESV) — The universal verdict:
        all guilty.

v.20 — "By works of the law no human being will be justified in his sight,
        since through the law comes knowledge of sin." (ESV) — The Law's
        purpose: not to save but to reveal sin.

v.23 — "For all have sinned and fall short of the glory of God." (ESV) — The
        universal problem stated simply.

v.24 — "And are justified by his grace as a gift, through the redemption that
        is in Christ Jesus." (ESV) — The solution: free justification by grace
        through Christ's redemption.

v.25 — "Whom God put forward as a propitiation by his blood, to be received
        by faith." (ESV) — Christ as the satisfaction of God's wrath.

v.26 — "So that he might be just and the justifier of the one who has faith
        in Jesus." (ESV) — The great problem solved: how can God be just AND
        justify sinners? Through Christ.

v.28 — "One is justified by faith apart from works of the law." (ESV) — The
        Reformation principle: justification by faith alone.

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.10 — NET: "There is no one righteous, not even one" — Paul
       begins a catena of OT quotations (Psalms, Isaiah, Ecclesiastes).
       WEB: "There is no one righteous; no, not one." KJV: "There
       is none righteous, no, not one." The universal indictment.

v.20 — KJV: "by the deeds of the law there shall no flesh be justified" / ESV: "For by works of the law no human being will be justified" —
       The Law was never designed to save — only to reveal sin. It is a
       mirror, not a soap. It shows you the dirt; it cannot wash it off.

v.21 — NET: "But now apart from the law the righteousness of God
       has been disclosed" — "disclosed" (pephanerOtai = made
       manifest/revealed). WEB: "a righteousness of God has been
       revealed." KJV: "the righteousness of God without the law
       is manifested." A new era: righteousness available without
       law-keeping.

v.22 — KJV: "the righteousness of God which is by faith of Jesus Christ" —
       ESV: "through faith in Jesus Christ" — The Greek can mean "faith in
       Christ" (objective genitive) or "the faithfulness of Christ" (subjective
       genitive). Most translations favor "faith in Christ."

v.24 — KJV: "justified freely" / ESV: "and are justified by his grace as" — Greek "dorean" — as a gift, without cost,
       gratis. Justification costs the believer nothing — it cost Christ
       everything.
       NET: "justified freely by his grace through the redemption
       that is in Christ Jesus" — "freely" (dOrean = as a gift/
       without cause). WEB: "being justified freely by his grace."
       KJV: "justified freely by his grace." We contribute nothing
       to our justification.

v.25 — KJV: "propitiation" / ESV: Both equivalent. — Greek "hilastērion" — the mercy seat (the lid
       of the Ark of the Covenant where blood was sprinkled on the Day of
       Atonement). Christ IS the mercy seat — the place where God's wrath
       is satisfied and mercy is dispensed.
       NET: "God publicly displayed him at his death as the mercy
       seat accessible through faith" — "mercy seat" (hilastErion)
       — the lid of the Ark where blood was sprinkled on Atonement
       Day. KJV: "propitiation." WEB: "a sacrifice of atonement."
       NET's rendering connects Christ directly to the Day of
       Atonement imagery.

v.26 — KJV: "just, and the justifier" / ESV: "he might be just and the justifier" — The great theological problem: how
       can a just God forgive guilty sinners without compromising His justice?
       Answer: Christ bore the penalty, satisfying justice, so God can justify
       believers without being unjust.

v.28 — NET: "For we consider that a person is declared righteous
       by faith apart from the works of the law" — "declared
       righteous" (dikaiousthai) — forensic/legal declaration.
       WEB: "justified by faith apart from the works of the law."
       KJV: "justified by faith without the deeds of the law."
       Luther's sola fide finds its primary text here.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Justified     — Declared righteous before God — acquitted of guilt. A legal
               term: the judge declares the defendant "not guilty" — not
               because they're innocent but because the penalty has been paid.

Freely        — Greek "dorean" — as a gift, without cost. Justification is
               free to the recipient — paid for entirely by Christ.

Grace         — Unmerited favor — God giving what we don't deserve (salvation)
               and not giving what we do deserve (judgment).

Redemption    — The payment of a price to free a captive. Christ's death is
               the ransom price that frees sinners from bondage to sin and
               death.

Propitiation  — The satisfaction of God's wrath through sacrifice. Christ
               absorbed God's righteous anger against sin so that believers
               are no longer under wrath.

Just and Justifier — God's dual achievement at the cross: He remains perfectly
               just (sin is punished) while also justifying sinners (the
               penalty is borne by Christ, not by them).

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "There is none righteous, no, not one" (v.10). This includes you. No
   matter how moral, religious, or good you think you are — you are not
   righteous enough. Have you accepted this verdict about yourself? Until
   you do, you cannot receive the solution.

2. "All have sinned, and come short of the glory of God" (v.23). "Come short"
   — like an arrow that falls short of the target. God's standard is His own
   glory — and every human falls short. How does this level playing field
   affect how you view yourself and others?

3. "Justified freely by his grace" (v.24). Freely — as a gift. You cannot
   earn it, buy it, or deserve it. Are you resting in free grace, or are you
   still trying to earn God's acceptance through performance?

4. "Where is boasting then? It is excluded" (v.27). If salvation is by faith
   alone, there is nothing to boast about. You contributed nothing to your
   salvation. How does this truth produce humility rather than pride in your
   spiritual life?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. Paul says the Law's purpose is "the knowledge of sin" (v.20) — not the
   cure for sin. The Law is a diagnostic tool, not a treatment. How do you
   use the Law — as a mirror to reveal your need for Christ, or as a ladder
   to climb toward self-righteousness?

2. "But now" (v.21) — the great turning point. After the bad news (all guilty),
   the good news arrives. How does understanding the depth of the bad news
   (chapters 1-3:20) make the good news (3:21-26) more glorious?

3. God is "just, and the justifier" (v.26). At the cross, God's justice and
   His mercy meet perfectly. He doesn't compromise either one. How does
   understanding this solve the problem of "How can a good God forgive bad
   people?"

4. "A man is justified by faith without the deeds of the law" (v.28). This
   verse launched the Reformation. Do you truly believe it — that your
   standing before God is based entirely on faith in Christ, not on your
   performance? How does that change how you live?

5. Paul asks "Where is boasting?" (v.27). If you were saved by works, you
   could boast. But since you're saved by faith — by receiving a gift — there
   is nothing to boast about. Does your spiritual life reflect humility or
   subtle pride?
