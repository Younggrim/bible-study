Romans - Chapter 3
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
All humanity — Jew and Gentile alike — stands guilty before God with no one righteous, but now God's righteousness is revealed apart from the Law through faith in Jesus Christ, who is set forth as a propitiation by His blood.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Paul (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 3 completes Paul's argument that ALL humanity is
guilty before God — Gentile (ch.1), moralist (ch.2:1-16), and Jew (ch.2:17-29).
Then comes the great turning point: "But now" (v.21) — the righteousness of God
apart from the Law has been revealed. This is the heart of the Gospel: justification
by grace through faith.

The Universal Indictment (vv.9-20): Paul strings together Old Testament quotations
(Psalms, Isaiah, Ecclesiastes) to prove that "there is none righteous, no, not
one" (v.10). Every mouth is stopped. The whole world is guilty before God. The
Law cannot save — it can only reveal sin.

"But Now" (vv.21-26): The most important two words in Romans. After establishing
universal guilt, Paul reveals God's solution: "the righteousness of God which is
by faith of Jesus Christ unto all and upon all them that believe" (v.22). This
righteousness is: (1) apart from the Law, (2) witnessed by the Law and Prophets,
(3) through faith in Christ, (4) for all who believe, (5) freely given by grace,
(6) through the redemption in Christ, (7) based on Christ's propitiation.

Romans 3:23-26 is arguably the most theologically dense passage in the entire
Bible — containing the doctrines of universal sin, justification, grace,
redemption, propitiation, and God's righteousness in one paragraph.

REFLECTION
------------------------------------
1. "There is none righteous, no, not one" (v.10). This includes you. No
   matter how moral, religious, or good you think you are — you are not
   righteous enough. Have you accepted this verdict about yourself? Until
   you do, you cannot receive the solution.

2. "All have sinned, and come short of the glory of God" (v.23). "Come short"
   — like an arrow that falls short of the target. God's standard is His own
   glory — and every human falls short. How does this level playing field
   affect how you view yourself and others?

3. "Justified freely by his grace" (v.24). Freely — as a gift. You cannot
   earn it, buy it, or deserve it. Are you resting in free grace, or are you
   still trying to earn God's acceptance through performance?

4. "Where is boasting then? It is excluded" (v.27). If salvation is by faith
   alone, there is nothing to boast about. You contributed nothing to your
   salvation. How does this truth produce humility rather than pride in your
   spiritual life?

1. Paul says the Law's purpose is "the knowledge of sin" (v.20) — not the
   cure for sin. The Law is a diagnostic tool, not a treatment. How do you
   use the Law — as a mirror to reveal your need for Christ, or as a ladder
   to climb toward self-righteousness?

2. "But now" (v.21) — the great turning point. After the bad news (all guilty),
   the good news arrives. How does understanding the depth of the bad news
   (chapters 1-3:20) make the good news (3:21-26) more glorious?

3. God is "just, and the justifier" (v.26). At the cross, God's justice and
   His mercy meet perfectly. He doesn't compromise either one. How does
   understanding this solve the problem of "How can a good God forgive bad
   people?"

4. "A man is justified by faith without the deeds of the law" (v.28). This
   verse launched the Reformation. Do you truly believe it — that your
   standing before God is based entirely on faith in Christ, not on your
   performance? How does that change how you live?

5. Paul asks "Where is boasting?" (v.27). If you were saved by works, you
   could boast. But since you're saved by faith — by receiving a gift — there
   is nothing to boast about. Does your spiritual life reflect humility or
   subtle pride?
