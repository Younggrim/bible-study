Romans - Chapter 4
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Abraham is the proof that justification has always been by faith, not works — his faith was counted as righteousness before circumcision and apart from the Law, making him the father of all who believe, both Jew and Gentile.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Paul (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 4 proves justification by faith from the Old
Testament — using Abraham and David as examples. Paul's argument: if the
greatest figures in Jewish history were justified by faith (not works), then
justification by faith is not a new doctrine — it has always been God's way.

Abraham's Faith (vv.1-5): Abraham "believed God, and it was counted unto him
for righteousness" (v.3, quoting Genesis 15:6). This happened BEFORE
circumcision (v.10) and BEFORE the Law (v.13). Abraham was justified by faith
alone — not by works, not by ritual, not by law-keeping.

David's Testimony (vv.6-8): David describes "the blessedness of the man, unto
whom God imputeth righteousness without works" (v.6, quoting Psalm 32:1-2).
David — a man guilty of adultery and murder — was forgiven and declared
righteous without works. If David can be justified by faith, anyone can.

"Fully Persuaded" (vv.18-21): Abraham's faith was not blind — it was informed.
He considered the impossibility (his body "now dead," Sarah's womb "dead") and
chose to believe God's promise anyway. He was "fully persuaded that, what he
had promised, he was able also to perform" (v.21). Faith is not ignoring reality
— it is trusting God's power over reality.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.3  — "Abraham believed God, and it was counted to him as righteousness."
        (ESV) — The foundational text for justification by faith (Genesis 15:6).

v.5  — "To the one who does not work but believes in him who justifies the
        ungodly, his faith is counted as righteousness." (ESV) — The clearest
        statement of justification by faith alone in the Bible.

v.16 — "That is why it depends on faith, in order that the promise may rest
        on grace and be guaranteed to all his offspring." (ESV) — Faith ensures
        grace; grace ensures certainty.

v.21 — "Fully convinced that God was able to do what he had promised." (ESV)
        — The nature of Abraham's faith: full persuasion in God's ability.

v.25 — "Who was delivered up for our trespasses and raised for our
        justification." (ESV) — Christ's death for our sins; His resurrection
        for our justification.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------

v.3  — KJV: "counted unto him for righteousness" / ESV: "and it was counted to him as righteousness.”" — Greek "elogisthē" — to
       reckon, to credit, to impute. An accounting term: righteousness was
       credited to Abraham's account — not earned but deposited.

v.5  — KJV: "him that justifieth the ungodly" / ESV: "believes in him who justifies the ungodly, his" — A shocking statement. God
       justifies the UNGODLY — not the righteous. This is the scandal of
       grace: God declares guilty sinners righteous through faith.

v.11 — KJV: "a seal of the righteousness of the faith" / ESV: "as a seal of the righteousness that he had by faith" — Circumcision was
       not the cause of Abraham's righteousness but the SEAL (confirmation)
       of a righteousness he already had by faith. The sign came after the
       reality.

v.17 — KJV: "calleth those things which be not as though they were" / ESV: "to the dead and calls into existence the things that do not" — God
       speaks of non-existent things as if they already exist. He called
       Abraham "father of many nations" before he had a single child.

v.25 — KJV: "raised again for our justification" / ESV: "and raised for our justification" — Christ's resurrection
       is not just proof of His deity — it is the basis of our justification.
       His resurrection declares that His sacrifice was accepted and our
       debt is paid.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
v.3  — NET: "Abraham believed God, and it was credited to him
       as righteousness" — "credited" (elogisthE = counted/reckoned
       to his account). WEB: "it was accounted to him for
       righteousness." KJV: "it was counted unto him for
       righteousness." Accounting language — deposited to his
       ledger by God.

v.5  — NET: "his faith is credited as righteousness" — the one
       who does NOT work but BELIEVES. WEB: "his faith is accounted
       for righteousness." The paradox: the non-worker receives
       the worker's reward. Faith receives what it cannot earn.

v.17 — NET: "the God who makes the dead alive and summons the
       things that do not yet exist as though they already do" —
       "summons things that do not yet exist" (kalountos ta mE
       onta hOs onta). WEB: "calls the things that are not, as
       though they were." KJV: "calleth those things which be not
       as though they were." God's creative word.

v.19 — NET: "he did not weaken in faith, contemplating his own
       body which was already dead" — "already dead" (nenekrOmenon
       = having been made dead/impotent). WEB: "his own body, now
       as good as dead." KJV: "his own body now dead." Abraham was
       ~100 years old — biologically hopeless.

v.25 — NET: "He was given over because of our transgressions and
       was raised for the sake of our justification" — "for the
       sake of our justification" (dia tEn dikaiOsin hEmOn). WEB:
       "raised for our justification." KJV: "raised again for our
       justification." The resurrection proves the sacrifice was
       accepted — justification confirmed.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Imputation    — The crediting of righteousness to a person's account. Not
               infused (put inside) but imputed (credited to). Abraham's
               faith was "counted" as righteousness.

Counted/Reckoned — Greek "logizomai" — an accounting term meaning to credit,
               to reckon to one's account. Used 11 times in this chapter.

Ungodly       — Those without godliness — sinners. God justifies the ungodly
               (v.5) — not those who have earned it but those who believe.

Seal          — A mark of authentication and ownership. Circumcision was the
               seal (not the cause) of Abraham's faith-righteousness.

Fully persuaded — Complete confidence in God's ability to fulfill His promises
               — despite contrary evidence. The essence of Abraham's faith.

Delivered/Raised — Christ was "delivered" (handed over to death) for our sins
               and "raised" for our justification. His death pays; His
               resurrection proves the payment was accepted.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "To him that worketh not, but believeth" (v.5). Salvation is for the person
   who STOPS working and STARTS believing. Are you still trying to earn God's
   acceptance through performance? What would it look like to simply believe?

2. Abraham was justified BEFORE circumcision (v.10) — before any religious
   ritual. Your standing before God is not based on baptism, church membership,
   or any external act — it is based on faith. Are you trusting in rituals or
   in Christ?

3. Abraham was "fully persuaded that, what he had promised, he was able also
   to perform" (v.21). He looked at impossibility and chose to trust God's
   word over his circumstances. What impossible situation are you facing? Can
   you be "fully persuaded" that God is able?

4. Christ "was delivered for our offences, and was raised again for our
   justification" (v.25). His death dealt with your sin; His resurrection
   guarantees your righteous standing. Do you live in the confidence of a
   justified person?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. Paul says God "justifieth the ungodly" (v.5). This is counterintuitive —
   we expect God to justify the godly. How does knowing that God justifies
   the UNGODLY change how you approach Him? Do you come as you are or try
   to clean up first?

2. Abraham's faith was "counted" as righteousness (v.3) — not his obedience,
   not his sacrifice of Isaac, not his moral life. Faith alone. What are you
   trusting in for your standing before God — your faith in Christ or your
   own track record?

3. "Against hope believed in hope" (v.18). Abraham believed when there was no
   human reason to hope. Where in your life do you need to believe "against
   hope" — trusting God's promise when circumstances say it's impossible?

4. "It was not written for his sake alone" (v.23). Abraham's story was
   recorded for YOU — to show you how faith works. How does Abraham's example
   encourage your own faith journey?

5. Faith is being "fully persuaded" (v.21) — not that you understand everything
   but that God is able. What promise of God do you need to be more fully
   persuaded about today?
