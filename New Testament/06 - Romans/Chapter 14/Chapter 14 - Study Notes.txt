Romans - Chapter 14
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Paul addresses disputable matters (food, holy days) — the strong must not despise the weak, the weak must not judge the strong, for each person stands before God's judgment seat, and the kingdom of God is righteousness, peace, and joy in the Holy Spirit.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Paul (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 14 addresses disputes between "strong" and "weak"
believers over matters of conscience — specifically food and holy days. In Rome,
Jewish Christians still observed dietary laws and Sabbaths; Gentile Christians
felt free from these. Paul's solution: neither judge nor despise each other.
Both are serving the Lord. Don't let secondary issues destroy unity.

The "Weak" and "Strong" (vv.1-4): The "weak" are those whose conscience
restricts them in areas where Scripture gives freedom (eating certain foods,
observing certain days). The "strong" are those who understand their freedom
in Christ. Paul identifies with the strong (15:1) but commands both groups:
the strong must not despise the weak; the weak must not judge the strong.

The Principle of Accountability (vv.7-12): "None of us liveth to himself" (v.7).
Every believer will stand before Christ's judgment seat (v.10) and give account
of themselves (v.12). Since each person answers to God — not to you — stop
judging your brother's servant (v.4).

The Principle of Love (vv.13-23): Even if you have freedom, don't use it in a
way that causes a weaker brother to stumble (v.13). "The kingdom of God is not
meat and drink; but righteousness, and peace, and joy in the Holy Ghost" (v.17).
Love limits liberty.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.4  — "Who are you to pass judgment on the servant of another? It is before
        his own master that he stands or falls." (ESV) — You are not your
        brother's judge.

v.7-8 — "None of us lives to himself... whether we live or whether we die, we
        are the Lord's." (ESV) — We belong to Christ in life and death.

v.10 — "We will all stand before the judgment seat of God." (ESV) — Universal
        accountability to Christ.

v.13 — "Let us not pass judgment on one another any longer, but rather decide
        never to put a stumbling block or hindrance in the way of a brother."
        (ESV) — Stop judging people; start judging your own behavior's impact.

v.17 — "For the kingdom of God is not a matter of eating and drinking but of
        righteousness and peace and joy in the Holy Spirit." (ESV) — The
        kingdom's priorities: not external rules but internal realities.

v.23 — "Whatever does not proceed from faith is sin." (ESV) — The principle:
        if you can't do it in faith, don't do it.

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1  — KJV: "doubtful disputations" — ESV: "quarreling over opinions" —
       Don't receive weak believers just to argue with them about their
       scruples. Welcome them without making their convictions a debate topic.
       NET: "Now accept the one who is weak in faith, and do
       not have disputes over differing opinions" — "weak in faith"
       — not morally weak but with a sensitive/immature conscience.
       WEB: "accept him who is weak in faith." KJV: "Him that is
       weak in the faith receive ye." Welcome without argument.

v.5  — KJV: "Let every man be fully persuaded in his own mind" / ESV: "days alike. Each one should be fully convinced in his own mind" — In matters
       of conscience (not clear commands), each person must be convinced in
       their own mind. Don't violate your conscience — and don't impose yours
       on others.
       NET: "One person regards one day holier than other days,
       and another regards them all alike. Each must be fully
       convinced in his own mind" — "fully convinced" (plErophoreisthO
       = carry to full assurance). WEB: "Let each man be fully
       assured in his own mind." Conviction, not conformity.

v.10 — KJV: "the judgment seat of Christ" / ESV: "before the judgment seat of God" — Greek "bēma" — the raised
       platform where a judge sat. For believers, this is not about salvation
       (settled at the cross) but about rewards and accountability for how
       we lived.
       NET: "For we will all stand before the judgment seat of
       God" — "judgment seat" (bEmati = tribunal/platform). WEB:
       "we will all stand before the judgment seat of Christ." Some
       manuscripts: "of God" (NET) vs. "of Christ" (WEB/KJV).
       Either way — divine evaluation of believers.

v.17 — KJV: "righteousness, and peace, and joy in the Holy Ghost" / ESV: "and drinking but of righteousness and peace and joy in the Holy" — The three
       marks of the kingdom: right relationship with God, harmony with others,
       and Spirit-produced gladness. These matter more than any external rule.
       NET: "For the kingdom of God does not consist of food and
       drink, but righteousness, peace, and joy in the Holy Spirit"
       — the kingdom's essence defined. WEB: "the Kingdom of God
       is not eating and drinking, but righteousness, peace, and
       joy in the Holy Spirit." Not dietary rules but Spirit-fruit.

v.23 — KJV: "whatsoever is not of faith is sin" / ESV: "For whatever does not proceed from faith is sin" — The broadest definition of
       sin in the Bible: anything done without faith — without confidence that
       it pleases God — is sin.
       NET: "everything that does not come from faith is sin" —
       the chapter's conclusion and one of the most sweeping
       ethical principles in Scripture. WEB: "whatever is not of
       faith is sin." KJV: "whatsoever is not of faith is sin."
       If you can't do it in good conscience before God, don't.

       NOTE ON VERSE COUNT DIFFERENCES: The WEB translation includes 26 verses in
       Romans 14, while ESV, KJV, ASV, and NET include only 23. This is because
       some ancient manuscripts place the doxology of Romans 16:25-27 ("Now to
       him who is able to strengthen you...") after 14:23, reflecting an early
       manuscript tradition where Romans circulated in a shorter form ending at
       chapter 14. The WEB follows this alternative arrangement, placing the
       doxology at both locations. Most modern translations (ESV, NET, KJV)
       place it only at the end of chapter 16. Neither is "wrong" — they
       reflect different manuscript traditions.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Weak in faith — Believers whose conscience restricts them in areas where
               Scripture gives freedom. Not morally weak but scrupulously
               cautious about disputable matters.

Strong in faith — Believers who understand their freedom in Christ regarding
               disputable matters (food, days, etc.). They are not bound by
               extra-biblical restrictions.

Judgment seat of Christ — The "bēma" — where believers give account for their
               lives. Not about salvation (already secured) but about
               faithfulness and rewards.

Stumblingblock — Anything that causes a weaker believer to violate their
               conscience or fall into sin. The strong must avoid placing
               these in others' paths.

Disputable matters — Issues where Scripture does not give a clear command —
               matters of conscience and personal conviction (food, drink,
               days, etc.).

Kingdom of God — Not external rules but internal realities: righteousness
               (right standing), peace (harmony), and joy (Spirit-produced
               gladness).

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Who art thou that judgest another man's servant?" (v.4). Other believers
   answer to God, not to you. Where are you judging brothers over matters of
   conscience — things Scripture doesn't clearly command or forbid?

2. "The kingdom of God is not meat and drink; but righteousness, and peace,
   and joy" (v.17). Are you majoring on minors? Are you fighting over
   secondary issues while neglecting the kingdom's real priorities?

3. "Let us not therefore judge one another any more: but judge this rather,
   that no man put a stumblingblock in his brother's way" (v.13). Stop
   judging people; start evaluating your own behavior's impact on others.
   Is your freedom causing someone else to stumble?

4. "Whatsoever is not of faith is sin" (v.23). If you can't do it with a
   clear conscience before God, don't do it — even if others can. What are
   you doing that your conscience is uneasy about?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. Paul says both the one who eats and the one who doesn't eat are "doing it
   unto the Lord" (v.6). Two opposite practices — both honoring God. How does
   this challenge your tendency to think YOUR way is the only right way?

2. "We shall all stand before the judgment seat of Christ" (v.10). You will
   give account for YOUR life — not your brother's. How does knowing you'll
   answer to Christ (not to other Christians) change how you live?

3. "Destroy not him with thy meat, for whom Christ died" (v.15). Christ died
   for your weaker brother — will you give up a freedom for someone Christ
   gave His life for? What freedom might you need to limit for love's sake?

4. "None of us liveth to himself" (v.7). Your choices affect others. You are
   not an island. How does this interconnectedness shape your decisions about
   how you use your freedom?

5. "Happy is he that condemneth not himself in that thing which he alloweth"
   (v.22). The happiest person is the one whose actions align with their
   conscience. Is there a gap between what you do and what your conscience
   approves? Close it.
