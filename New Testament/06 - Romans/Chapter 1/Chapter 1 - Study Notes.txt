Romans - Chapter 1
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Paul introduces himself and the gospel's theme — the righteousness of God revealed by faith — then exposes humanity's universal guilt: God gave sinners over to their desires because they suppressed the truth and exchanged the Creator for the creature.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: The Apostle Paul, writing from Corinth during his third missionary
journey (approximately AD 57). Romans is Paul's longest, most systematic letter
— his theological masterpiece.

Audience: The church in Rome — a church Paul did not found and had not yet
visited. He writes to introduce himself and his Gospel before arriving.

Purpose: To present a comprehensive, systematic explanation of the Gospel —
from humanity's condemnation (chs.1-3) through justification (chs.3-5),
sanctification (chs.6-8), Israel's place in God's plan (chs.9-11), and
practical Christian living (chs.12-16).

Chapter 1 Structure: Introduction and greeting (vv.1-7), Paul's desire to
visit Rome (vv.8-15), the thesis statement (vv.16-17), and the indictment of
pagan humanity (vv.18-32).

The Thesis (vv.16-17): "The gospel... is the power of God unto salvation to
every one that believeth... For therein is the righteousness of God revealed
from faith to faith: as it is written, The just shall live by faith." This is
the thesis of the entire letter — and arguably of the entire New Testament.

The Downward Spiral (vv.18-32): Paul traces humanity's descent: (1) they knew
God but didn't glorify Him (v.21), (2) they exchanged God's glory for idols
(v.23), (3) God "gave them up" three times (vv.24,26,28) to the consequences
of their choices. Sin is both rebellion against God and God's judgment on
rebellion.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.16 — "For I am not ashamed of the gospel, for it is the power of God for
        salvation to everyone who believes, to the Jew first and also to the
        Greek." (ESV) — The Gospel as God's power for salvation.

v.17 — "The righteous shall live by faith." (ESV) — The thesis of Romans —
        quoting Habakkuk 2:4. The verse that ignited the Reformation.

v.20 — "His invisible attributes, namely, his eternal power and divine nature,
        have been clearly perceived, ever since the creation of the world...
        So they are without excuse." (ESV) — General revelation leaves all
        humanity accountable.

v.21 — "For although they knew God, they did not honor him as God or give
        thanks to him." (ESV) — The root of all sin: refusing to glorify God
        and be grateful.

v.28 — "God gave them up to a debased mind." (ESV) — The most terrifying
        judgment: God letting people have what they want.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------

v.1  — KJV: "separated unto the gospel" / ESV: "set apart for the gospel of God" — Greek "aphōrismenos" — set apart,
       designated. Paul's entire life was devoted to one thing: the Gospel.

v.16 — KJV: "the power of God" / ESV: Both equivalent. — Greek "dunamis theou" — dynamite, dynamic
       power. The Gospel is not information about power — it IS power.

v.17 — KJV: "from faith to faith" / ESV: "from faith for faith" — Debated: (1) from God's faithfulness to
       our faith, (2) from one degree of faith to another, (3) by faith from
       start to finish. The point: faith is the sole instrument throughout.

v.18 — KJV: "hold the truth in unrighteousness" — ESV: "suppress the truth"
       — Greek "katechontōn" — to hold down, to suppress. Humanity doesn't
       lack truth — they actively suppress it.

v.24,26,28 — KJV: "God gave them up/over" / ESV: "gave them up in the lusts" — Three times God "hands over"
       humanity to the consequences of their choices. This is not active
       punishment but judicial abandonment — letting people have what they
       insist on.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
v.1  — NET: "called to be an apostle, set apart for the gospel
       of God" — "set apart" (aphorismenos) echoes Paul's Pharisee
       identity (pharisaios = separated one). WEB: "set apart for
       the Good News of God." He was separated FOR something, not
       just FROM something.

v.16 — NET: "For I am not ashamed of the gospel, for it is God's
       power for salvation to everyone who believes, to the Jew
       first and also to the Greek" — "God's power" (dynamis theou).
       WEB: "the power of God for salvation." KJV: "the power of
       God unto salvation." The gospel IS power, not just describes it.

v.17 — NET: "the righteousness of God is revealed in the gospel
       from faith to faith" — "from faith to faith" (ek pisteOs
       eis pistin) is debated. WEB: "from faith to faith." ASV:
       "from faith unto faith." NET preserves the ambiguity — faith
       as starting point AND destination.

v.25 — NET: "They exchanged the truth of God for a lie and
       worshiped and served the creation rather than the Creator"
       — "the creation rather than the Creator" makes the absurdity
       explicit. WEB: "served the creature rather than the Creator."
       KJV: "served the creature more than the Creator."

v.28 — NET: "God gave them over to a depraved mind, to do what
       should not be done" — "depraved mind" (adokimon noun = a
       mind that fails the test). WEB: "a reprobate mind." KJV:
       "a reprobate mind." There is wordplay in Greek: they did not
       approve (edokimasan) God, so God gave them an unapproved
       (adokimon) mind.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Gospel        — Greek "euangelion" — good news. The announcement of what God
               has done in Christ for the salvation of sinners.

Righteousness of God — God's own righteousness, given to believers through
               faith. Not human righteousness earned by works but divine
               righteousness received by faith.

General revelation — God's self-disclosure through creation (v.20). Enough
               to leave all humanity "without excuse" but not enough to save
               — special revelation (the Gospel) is needed.

Gave them up  — God's judicial abandonment — removing His restraining grace
               and allowing people to experience the full consequences of
               their rebellion.

Propitiation  — The satisfaction of God's wrath through sacrifice. Christ is
               the propitiation — He absorbed God's wrath so believers don't
               have to.

Reprobate mind — A mind God has given over to its own corruption — unable to
               think rightly about God, morality, or reality.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "I am not ashamed of the gospel" (v.16). Are you? In your workplace, your
   social circle, your family — are you ashamed of the Gospel? What would
   "unashamed" look like in your specific context?

2. "The just shall live by faith" (v.17). Your entire Christian life — from
   beginning to end — is lived by faith, not by performance. Are you trying
   to live by works or by faith? What's the difference in daily practice?

3. "When they knew God, they glorified him not as God, neither were thankful"
   (v.21). The root of all sin: refusing to glorify God and be grateful. How
   is your gratitude? How is your worship? These two things are the antidote
   to the downward spiral.

4. "God gave them up" (vv.24,26,28). The most terrifying judgment is not fire
   from heaven — it's God letting you have what you want. Is there anything
   you're insisting on that God might be "giving you over" to? What would
   repentance look like?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. Paul says the Gospel is "the power of God unto salvation" (v.16). Not a
   suggestion, not advice, not a philosophy — power. Have you experienced the
   Gospel as power in your life? Where do you need its power right now?

2. Creation reveals God's "eternal power and Godhead" (v.20) — leaving all
   people "without excuse." How does the natural world point you to God? When
   did you last see God's attributes in creation?

3. The downward spiral begins with ingratitude and ends with a "reprobate mind"
   (vv.21-28). Gratitude is the guardrail against spiritual decline. How
   intentional are you about cultivating gratitude daily?

4. Paul says people "suppress the truth in unrighteousness" (v.18). Truth is
   not absent — it is suppressed. Where might you be suppressing truth that
   you don't want to face?

5. The chapter ends with people who "have pleasure in them that do" evil (v.32)
   — not just sinning but celebrating sin in others. How does the culture
   around you celebrate what God condemns? How do you navigate that without
   either participating or becoming self-righteous?
