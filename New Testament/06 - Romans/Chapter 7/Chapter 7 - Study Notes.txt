Romans - Chapter 7
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Paul explains the believer's release from the Law through death with Christ, acknowledges the Law's holiness, and describes the intense inner struggle — "the good that I would I do not, but the evil which I would not, that I do" — crying out for deliverance.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Paul (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 7 addresses the believer's relationship to the Law
and the ongoing struggle with sin. It is one of the most debated chapters in
Romans — particularly vv.14-25: is Paul describing his pre-conversion experience,
his current Christian struggle, or a hypothetical person under the Law?

The Marriage Analogy (vv.1-6): Paul uses marriage law to illustrate freedom from
the Law. A wife is bound to her husband while he lives; if he dies, she is free
to remarry. Similarly, believers have "died to the law" through Christ's death
and are now "married" to Christ — free to bear fruit for God.

The Law's Purpose (vv.7-13): The Law is not sin — it is "holy, and just, and
good" (v.12). But sin uses the Law as a weapon — the commandment "Thou shalt
not covet" actually aroused covetousness (v.8). The Law reveals sin and even
provokes it — but the fault is sin's, not the Law's.

The Inner Struggle (vv.14-25): Paul describes a war between wanting to do good
and actually doing evil. "The good that I would I do not: but the evil which I
would not, that I do" (v.19). This is the experience of every honest believer
— the tension between the new nature and the remaining flesh. The chapter ends
with a cry of desperation: "O wretched man that I am! who shall deliver me?"
(v.24) — answered in chapter 8.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.12 — "So the law is holy, and the commandment is holy and righteous and
        good." (ESV) — The Law is not the problem; sin is.

v.15 — "For I do not do what I want, but I do the very thing I hate." (ESV)
        — The universal experience of moral failure.

v.18 — "For I know that nothing good dwells in me, that is, in my flesh."
        (ESV) — The honest assessment of the flesh.

v.24 — "Wretched man that I am! Who will deliver me from this body of death?"
        (ESV) — The cry of desperation that leads to chapter 8.

v.25 — "Thanks be to God through Jesus Christ our Lord!" (ESV) — The answer
        to the cry: deliverance through Christ.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------

v.4  — KJV: "ye also are become dead to the law" / ESV: "you also have died to the law through" — Not that the Law died but
       that YOU died to it — through union with Christ's death. The Law still
       exists; your obligation to it as a means of righteousness is ended.

v.14 — KJV: "I am carnal, sold under sin" / ESV: "but I am of the flesh, sold under sin" — The shift to present tense
       (from past tense in vv.7-13) is significant. Many scholars see this
       as Paul's current experience as a believer — the ongoing struggle
       with remaining sin.

v.18 — KJV: "in me (that is, in my flesh)" / ESV: "that nothing good dwells in me, that is" — Paul qualifies: the problem is
       not his entire being but his "flesh" — the remaining sinful nature
       that coexists with his new nature in Christ.

v.24 — KJV: "the body of this death" / ESV: "me from this body of death?" — Possibly an allusion to a Roman
       punishment where a corpse was strapped to a living person until the
       decay killed them. The flesh is like a dead body strapped to the
       believer.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
v.7  — NET: "Is the law sin? Absolutely not!" — "Absolutely
       not!" (mE genoito = may it never be!). KJV: "God forbid."
       WEB: "May it never be!" ASV: "God forbid." Paul's strongest
       denial — the law reveals sin but is not itself sinful.

v.14 — NET: "For we know that the law is spiritual — but I am
       unspiritual, sold into slavery to sin" — "unspiritual"
       (sarkinos = fleshly/made of flesh). WEB: "but I am fleshly,
       sold under sin." KJV: "I am carnal, sold under sin." The
       law is spiritual; the problem is in US, not in the law.

v.15 — NET: "For I don't understand what I am doing. For I do not
       do what I want — instead, I do what I hate" — "don't
       understand" (ou ginOskO = don't comprehend/acknowledge). WEB:
       "For I don't know what I am doing." KJV: "that which I do I
       allow not." The divided self — wanting good but doing evil.

v.18 — NET: "For I know that nothing good lives in me, that is,
       in my flesh" — "in my flesh" is Paul's qualifier. WEB:
       "that is, in my flesh." KJV: "that is, in my flesh." The
       indwelling Spirit IS good; the flesh is the problem domain.

v.24 — NET: "Wretched man that I am! Who will rescue me from this
       body of death?" — "wretched" (talaipOros = miserable/
       exhausted from the struggle). WEB: "What a wretched man I
       am!" KJV: "O wretched man that I am!" The cry of genuine
       desperation that leads to the answer: "Jesus Christ our
       Lord" (v.25).

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Dead to the Law — Freedom from the Law as a means of righteousness. Not
               lawlessness but a new relationship — married to Christ instead
               of bound to the Law.

Flesh          — The remaining sinful nature in the believer — the old
               patterns, desires, and tendencies that war against the new
               nature. Not the physical body itself but the sin principle
               operating through it.

Concupiscence — KJV for "covetousness" or "evil desire" — the sinful craving
               that the Law's prohibition actually aroused.

Inward man    — The new nature — the regenerated self that "delights in the
               law of God" (v.22). The real "you" in Christ.

Wretched man  — Paul's honest self-assessment in the struggle against sin.
               Not despair but honesty — leading to dependence on Christ.

Body of death — The flesh — the remaining sin nature that clings to the
               believer like a corpse. Deliverance comes through Christ,
               not through self-effort.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "The good that I would I do not: but the evil which I would not, that I do"
   (v.19). Every honest man knows this struggle. You want to be better — but
   you keep failing. This is not a sign that you're not saved; it's a sign
   that you're in the fight. Are you fighting or have you given up?

2. "In me (that is, in my flesh,) dwelleth no good thing" (v.18). Brutal
   honesty about the flesh. You cannot improve the flesh — you can only put
   it to death (chapter 8). Are you trying to reform your flesh or crucify it?

3. "O wretched man that I am!" (v.24). Paul — the greatest apostle — felt
   wretched about his sin struggle. If you never feel this, you may not be
   fighting hard enough. Wretchedness about sin is healthy — it drives you
   to Christ.

4. "I thank God through Jesus Christ our Lord" (v.25). The answer to the
   struggle is not trying harder — it is Christ. Deliverance comes through
   Him, not through willpower. Are you depending on Christ or on yourself?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. Paul says the Law is "holy, and just, and good" (v.12) — but sin uses it
   as a weapon. How does knowing that the problem is sin (not the Law) change
   how you view God's commands?

2. The inner struggle (vv.14-25) is the experience of every honest believer.
   Does knowing that Paul experienced this same war encourage you in your own
   struggle? How?

3. Paul "delights in the law of God after the inward man" (v.22) — his new
   nature loves God's law. Do you delight in God's Word? Is there a genuine
   desire for holiness in you, even when you fail?

4. The chapter ends with a cry for deliverance (v.24) — not a resolution
   through willpower. The answer comes in chapter 8: the Spirit. Are you
   trying to win the war in your own strength, or are you depending on the
   Spirit?

5. "Who shall deliver me?" (v.24). The question assumes you CANNOT deliver
   yourself. Have you reached that conclusion — that self-effort cannot
   produce holiness? That only Christ can deliver?
