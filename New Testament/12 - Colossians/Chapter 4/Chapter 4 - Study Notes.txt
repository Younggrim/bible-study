Colossians - Chapter 4
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Paul instructs masters to treat slaves justly, urges persistent prayer, wise conduct toward outsiders, gracious speech "seasoned with salt," and sends personal greetings — including a reference to his letter to the Laodiceans.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Paul the Apostle (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 4 concludes Colossians with final instructions
on prayer and witness (vv.2-6), then personal greetings (vv.7-18). The
greetings reveal Paul's network of co-workers and provide fascinating
historical details about the early church. Notable: Mark is restored to
fellowship (v.10, cf. Acts 15:37-39), Luke is identified as "the beloved
physician" (v.14), and Demas — who will later desert Paul (2 Tim 4:10) — is
still present (v.14).

Masters' Responsibility (v.1): Completing the household code from 3:18-25.
Masters must treat servants justly, knowing they too have a Master in heaven.

Prayer and Witness (vv.2-6): Pray persistently and thankfully (v.2). Pray for
open doors for the gospel (v.3). Walk wisely toward outsiders, redeeming the
time (v.5). Let speech be gracious and "seasoned with salt" (v.6).

Personal Greetings (vv.7-18): Tychicus and Onesimus carry the letter (vv.7-9).
Aristarchus, Mark, and Justus send greetings (vv.10-11). Epaphras prays
fervently for the Colossians (vv.12-13). Luke and Demas greet them (v.14).
Paul gives instructions about sharing letters between churches (v.16) and
exhorts Archippus to fulfill his ministry (v.17).

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "Continue steadfastly in prayer, being watchful in it with
        thanksgiving." (ESV) — Persistent, alert, thankful prayer.

v.3  — "At the same time, pray also for us, that God may open to us a door
        for the word, to declare the mystery of Christ." (ESV) — Prayer for
        gospel opportunity.

v.5  — "Walk in wisdom toward outsiders, making the best use of the time."
        (ESV) — Wise conduct toward unbelievers; making the most of
        opportunities.

v.6  — "Let your speech always be gracious, seasoned with salt, so that you
        may know how you ought to answer each person." (ESV) — Gracious,
        flavorful, appropriate speech.

v.12 — "Epaphras... always struggling on your behalf in his prayers, that
        you may stand mature and fully assured in all the will of God." (ESV)
        — Fervent intercessory prayer.

v.17 — "See that you fulfill the ministry that you have received in the
        Lord." (ESV) — Fulfill your calling.

Charles Spurgeon:
  Sermon: "Speech Seasoned with Salt" on v.6 (MTP Vol. 31, No. 1866)
  "Seasoned with salt — not sugar. Salt preserves, purifies, and gives
  flavor. Your speech should preserve truth, purify conversation, and add
  flavor to every interaction. Bland speech is useless; harsh speech is
  harmful. But gracious speech seasoned with salt — that is the speech of
  wisdom. It knows how to answer every man — the skeptic, the seeker, the
  scoffer, the saint — each with the right word at the right time."
  Sermon: "Redeeming the Time" on v.5 (MTP Vol. 25, No. 1468)
  "Redeeming the time — buying up the opportunity. Time is a market and
  opportunities are the goods for sale. The wise man buys them up; the fool
  lets them pass. Every conversation with an unbeliever is an opportunity.
  Every moment of influence is a commodity. Buy it up — you cannot get it
  back once it's gone."

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------

v.2  — KJV: "continue" / ESV: Both equivalent. — Greek "proskartereite" — to persist, be devoted to,
       give constant attention to. Same word used of the early church's
       devotion to prayer (Acts 1:14, 2:42).

v.2  — KJV: "watch" / ESV: Both equivalent. — Greek "grēgorountes" — be alert, stay awake. Prayer
       requires mental alertness, not drowsy repetition.

v.3  — KJV: "door of utterance" / ESV: "to us a door for the word" — Greek "thura tou logou" — a door for the
       word. An open opportunity to speak the gospel. Even in prison, Paul
       asks for more opportunities.

v.5  — KJV: "them that are without" / ESV: "toward outsiders, making the best use" — Greek "tous exō" — those outside the
       faith community. Paul is conscious of the church's witness to
       outsiders.

v.6  — KJV: "seasoned with salt" / ESV: Both equivalent. — Salt preserves, purifies, and flavors.
       Speech should be: preserving (truthful), purifying (clean), and
       flavorful (interesting, engaging). Not bland or corrupt.

v.10 — KJV: "Marcus, sister's son to Barnabas" / ESV: "Barnabas (concerning whom" — Mark (John Mark), author
       of the Gospel of Mark. He had deserted Paul on the first missionary
       journey (Acts 13:13, 15:38). Now fully restored — a testimony to
       grace and second chances.

v.11 — KJV: "Jesus, which is called Justus" / ESV: "and Jesus who is called Justus. These are" — A Jewish Christian with the
       common name "Jesus" (Joshua) and the Latin surname "Justus." One of
       only three Jewish believers working with Paul at this time.

v.14 — KJV: "Luke, the beloved physician" / ESV: "Luke the beloved physician greets you, as does" — The only NT identification of
       Luke's profession. Author of Luke-Acts. His medical background shows
       in his precise vocabulary.

v.16 — KJV: "the epistle from Laodicea" / ESV: "also read the letter from Laodicea" — A lost letter. Either a letter
       Paul wrote to Laodicea (now lost) or possibly our Ephesians (if it
       was a circular letter). We don't have it — a reminder that not
       everything the apostles wrote was preserved.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
v.2  — NET: "Be devoted to prayer, keeping alert in it with
       thanksgiving" — "be devoted" (proskartereite = persist/
       continue steadfastly). WEB: "Continue steadfastly in prayer."
       KJV: "Continue in prayer." The same word used of the early
       church's devotion (Acts 2:42).

v.5  — NET: "Conduct yourselves with wisdom toward outsiders,
       making the most of the opportunities" — "making the most"
       (ton kairon exagorazomenoi = buying up the time/redeeming
       the moment). WEB: "redeeming the time." KJV: "redeeming the
       time." Time with unbelievers is precious — use it wisely.

v.6  — NET: "Let your speech always be gracious, seasoned with
       salt" — "seasoned with salt" (halati Ertymenos). WEB:
       "seasoned with salt." KJV: "seasoned with salt." Salt
       preserves and flavors — speech should be both pure and
       interesting/memorable. Not bland but not caustic.

v.14 — NET: "Luke, the beloved physician, and Demas greet you" —
       "beloved physician" (ho iatros ho agapEtos). WEB: "Luke, the
       beloved physician." Luke is the only Gentile author of
       Scripture. Later, Demas deserts Paul "having loved this
       present world" (2 Tim 4:10) — a tragic contrast.

v.16 — NET: "also have it read in the church of the Laodiceans"
       — a lost letter to Laodicea (or possibly Ephesians, which
       may have circulated without a specific address). WEB: "cause
       it to be read also in the assembly of the Laodiceans." The
       early church shared apostolic letters between congregations.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Continue in prayer — Persistent, devoted, constant prayer. Not occasional
               but habitual; not casual but intentional.

Door of utterance — An open opportunity to speak the gospel. Something God
               opens that human effort cannot force.

Redeeming the time — Buying up opportunities. Making the most of every
               moment, especially for witness to unbelievers.

Seasoned with salt — Speech that is preserving (truthful), purifying (clean),
               and flavorful (engaging). The opposite of bland or corrupt.

Them that are without — Unbelievers, those outside the faith community.
               Christians are to be conscious of their witness to outsiders.

Labouring fervently in prayer — Greek "agōnizomenos" — agonizing, wrestling,
               striving. Prayer as intense spiritual labor, not passive
               recitation.

Fulfil your ministry — Complete the calling God has given you. Don't abandon
               it, neglect it, or leave it half-done.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Continue in prayer" (v.2). Is prayer a consistent discipline in your life
   or an occasional emergency measure? What would "continuing" in prayer look
   like for you this week?

2. "Walk in wisdom toward them that are without" (v.5). How do unbelievers
   perceive you? Are you wise in your interactions with them — or careless,
   judgmental, or invisible? How can you "redeem the time" with them?

3. "Let your speech be alway with grace, seasoned with salt" (v.6). Evaluate
   your speech: Is it gracious? Does it have flavor and substance? Do you
   know how to answer different people appropriately? Where does your speech
   need improvement?

4. Mark was restored after failure (v.10). Have you written off someone who
   failed? Is there a "Mark" in your life who deserves a second chance? Have
   YOU received a second chance that you need to extend to others?

5. "Take heed to the ministry which thou hast received" (v.17). What ministry
   has God given you? Are you fulfilling it or neglecting it? What would it
   look like to "take heed" — to pay careful attention to your calling?
