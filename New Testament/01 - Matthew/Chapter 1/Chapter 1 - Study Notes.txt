Matthew - Chapter 1
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Matthew traces Jesus' genealogy from Abraham through David to establish His messianic credentials, then recounts His miraculous virgin birth as the fulfillment of Isaiah's Emmanuel prophecy.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Matthew (also called Levi), a tax collector who became one of the twelve
apostles (Matthew 9:9). He was an eyewitness to Jesus' ministry. Tax collectors
were despised in Jewish society — they worked for Rome and were considered
traitors and extortioners. Matthew's inclusion in the twelve is itself a
demonstration of grace.

Date Written: Approximately AD 50-70, making it one of the earliest Gospels.

Audience: Written primarily for a Jewish audience. Matthew quotes the Old
Testament more than any other Gospel (approximately 60 direct quotes and 100
allusions), constantly showing how Jesus fulfills Jewish Scripture and prophecy.

Purpose of Matthew's Gospel: To present Jesus as the promised Messiah — the
King of the Jews, the fulfillment of the Law and the Prophets. Matthew begins
with a genealogy to establish Jesus' legal right to David's throne.

Purpose of Chapter 1: The genealogy establishes Jesus' legal credentials as
the Messiah — descended from Abraham (the covenant) and David (the throne).
The birth narrative establishes His divine origin — conceived by the Holy Spirit,
fulfilling Isaiah 7:14.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Bethlehem: The birthplace of Jesus, located about 5 miles south of Jerusalem in Judea. The city of David, fulfilling Micah 5:2.
- Nazareth: A small village in Galilee (northern Israel) where Joseph and Mary lived. Jesus would grow up here.
- The genealogy spans the geography of Israel's history — from Abraham in Ur/Canaan, through David in Bethlehem/Jerusalem, through the Babylonian exile, and back to the land.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "The book of the genealogy of Jesus Christ, the son of David, the son
        of Abraham." (ESV) — The opening line establishes the two most important
        messianic credentials: Davidic kingship and Abrahamic covenant.

v.21 — "She will bear a son, and you shall call his name Jesus, for he will
        save his people from their sins." (ESV) — The name and mission of
        Christ declared before His birth.

v.23 — "Behold, the virgin shall conceive and bear a son, and they shall call
        his name Immanuel (which means, God with us)." (ESV) — The fulfillment
        of Isaiah 7:14 — the Incarnation.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1  — KJV: "The book of the generation" — ESV: "The book of the genealogy"
       — Greek "biblos geneseos" — literally "book of genesis/origin." Matthew
       deliberately echoes Genesis 2:4 and 5:1 — Jesus is the new beginning,
       the new Adam.

v.16 — KJV: "Jacob begat Joseph the husband of Mary, of whom was born Jesus"
       — The Greek "of whom" (ex hes) is feminine singular — "of her" — making
       clear that Jesus was born of Mary, not begotten by Joseph. The genealogy
       shifts from "begat" to "was born of" at this crucial point.

v.19 — KJV: "a just man" — ESV: "a just man" — NET: "a righteous man" —
       Greek "dikaios" — righteous, upright, law-abiding. Joseph's righteousness
       was shown both in his desire to protect Mary and in his obedience to God.

v.23 — KJV: "a virgin" — ESV/NET: "a virgin" — Hebrew "almah" in Isaiah 7:14
       means "young woman" (which in context implies virginity); the Greek
       Septuagint translated it "parthenos" (virgin). Matthew uses "parthenos"
       — the specific word for a woman who has never had sexual relations.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
v.1  — NET: "This is the record of the genealogy" — uniquely
       renders "biblos geneseos" as "record of the genealogy"
       rather than "book of the generation/genealogy," emphasizing
       the document nature of what follows.

v.18 — WEB: "she was found pregnant by the Holy Spirit" — more
       direct modern phrasing than KJV's "with child of the Holy
       Ghost" or ESV's "to be with child from the Holy Spirit."
       NET similarly: "found to be pregnant through the Holy Spirit."

v.19 — NET: "because he did not want to disgrace her, he intended
       to divorce her privately" — clarifies Joseph's two motives
       (righteousness + compassion) more explicitly than KJV's
       condensed "not willing to make her a publick example."

v.20 — NET: "the child conceived in her is from the Holy Spirit"
       — renders the angel's explanation with "conceived in" rather
       than ASV/KJV "that which is conceived in her is of the Holy
       Spirit," making the active divine agency clearer.

v.25 — WEB: "didn't know her sexually until she had given birth
       to her firstborn son" — the most explicit modern rendering.
       ASV: "knew her not till she had brought forth a son." NET:
       "did not have marital relations with her until she gave
       birth to a son." All three clarify the euphemism of KJV's
       "knew her not."

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Genealogy     — A record of ancestry and descent. Matthew's genealogy establishes
               Jesus' legal right to David's throne through Joseph (His legal
               father) and His covenant connection to Abraham.

Espoused      — Betrothed, engaged. In Jewish culture, betrothal was legally
               binding — more than modern engagement. Breaking it required a
               formal divorce. The betrothed couple did not yet live together.

Emmanuel      — Hebrew "Immanuel" — "God with us." A name/title for Jesus
               declaring His divine nature and His presence among humanity.

Publick example — KJV phrase meaning to make a public spectacle. Joseph could
               have had Mary publicly shamed or stoned; instead he planned to
               divorce her quietly.

Privily       — KJV for "secretly" or "quietly." Joseph's plan to protect
               Mary from public shame, even at personal cost to himself.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. The genealogy includes four women — Tamar (deception), Rahab (prostitute),
   Ruth (foreigner), Bathsheba (adultery). God included broken, scandalous
   stories in the lineage of His Son. What does this say about how God views
   your past? Is there anything in your history you think disqualifies you
   from being used by God?

2. Joseph was a "just man" who chose mercy over his legal rights (v.19). He
   could have publicly shamed Mary. Instead he planned to protect her quietly.
   As a man, how do you handle situations where you have the right to expose
   or punish someone but mercy would be the better path?

3. Joseph obeyed the angel's instruction immediately — "he did as the angel
   of the Lord had bidden him" (v.24). No argument, no delay. When God speaks
   clearly to you through Scripture or the Spirit, how quickly do you obey?

4. "He shall save his people from their sins" (v.21). Not from their
   circumstances, not from their enemies — from their sins. What sin do you
   most need Jesus to save you from right now?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. Matthew's genealogy includes Gentiles (Rahab, Ruth) and notorious sinners
   (Tamar, Bathsheba's situation). What does the inclusion of these people in
   the Messiah's lineage tell you about the scope of God's grace and His
   willingness to work through imperfect people?

2. Joseph received his guidance through a dream (v.20). He had no Bible verse
   that directly addressed his situation. He had to trust God's word through
   an unusual channel. How do you discern God's guidance in situations where
   Scripture doesn't give a direct answer?

3. "Emmanuel — God with us" (v.23). The entire Old Testament is the story of
   God seeking to dwell with His people. The Incarnation is the ultimate
   fulfillment of that desire. What does it mean to you personally that God
   wanted to be with you so much that He became one of us?

4. Joseph was asked to take on a responsibility that wasn't his — raising a
   child he didn't father, protecting a woman whose pregnancy he didn't cause.
   He did it without complaint. What responsibilities has God placed on you
   that feel unfair or unearned? How do you respond to them?

5. The three sets of 14 generations (v.17) show God's sovereign hand in
   history — working through centuries of human choices, failures, and
   faithfulness to bring His Son into the world at exactly the right time.
   How does seeing God's long-term sovereignty in history affect your trust
   in His plan for your life?
