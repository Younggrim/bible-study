Matthew - Chapter 11
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
John the Baptist sends messengers asking if Jesus is truly the Messiah, Jesus pronounces woe on unrepentant cities, and offers His great invitation: "Come unto me, all ye that labour and are heavy laden, and I will give you rest."

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Matthew (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 11 marks a turning point in Matthew's Gospel. The
initial excitement about Jesus is fading. John the Baptist doubts from prison.
The cities where Jesus performed the most miracles refuse to repent. The chapter
moves from doubt (vv.1-6), to praise of John (vv.7-15), to condemnation of
unbelief (vv.16-24), to one of the most beautiful invitations in Scripture
(vv.28-30).

John's Doubt (vv.2-6): John the Baptist — the bold prophet who declared Jesus
the Lamb of God — now sits in Herod's prison and wonders if he got it wrong.
He expected a conquering Messiah who would bring judgment; instead Jesus was
healing the sick and preaching to the poor. John's doubt was not about Jesus'
power but about His method.

The Unrepentant Cities (vv.20-24): Chorazin, Bethsaida, and Capernaum saw more
miracles than any cities in history — and refused to repent. Jesus says their
judgment will be worse than Tyre, Sidon, and Sodom. Greater privilege brings
greater accountability.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Capernaum and the cities of Galilee: John's disciples came to Jesus in Galilee. Jesus pronounced woes on Chorazin, Bethsaida, and Capernaum (vv.20-24) — cities around the northern shore of the Sea of Galilee where He performed most of His miracles.
- Tyre and Sidon (v.21-22): Ancient Phoenician coastal cities (modern Lebanon), used as a comparison — even pagan cities would have repented at Jesus' works.
- Sodom (v.24): The destroyed city of the Dead Sea plain, referenced as a benchmark of judgment.

REFLECTION
------------------------------------
1. John the Baptist doubted from prison (v.3). Even the greatest men of faith
   have seasons of doubt — especially in suffering. If you're in a season of
   doubt or confusion, you're in good company. What would it look like to
   bring your honest questions to Jesus rather than hiding them?

2. "Blessed is he, whosoever shall not be offended in me" (v.6). Jesus doesn't
   always do what we expect. He doesn't always answer the way we want. Are you
   offended at Jesus for something He hasn't done or hasn't given you? Can you
   trust Him even when He doesn't meet your expectations?

3. "Come unto me, all ye that labour and are heavy laden" (v.28). What burden
   are you carrying right now — work pressure, family stress, guilt, anxiety,
   exhaustion? Jesus says come. Not "fix yourself first" — come as you are,
   heavy and tired.

4. "Take my yoke upon you" (v.29). Rest doesn't come from doing nothing — it
   comes from being yoked to Christ. His yoke fits. His burden is light. Are
   you carrying burdens Jesus never asked you to carry? What would it look
   like to exchange your heavy yoke for His?

5. Jesus answered John's doubt not with a theological argument but with evidence
   — "the blind see, the lame walk" (v.5). When you doubt, what evidence of
   God's work in your life can you point to? How does remembering what God has
   done strengthen your faith in what He will do?

6. The cities that saw the most miracles were judged most severely (vv.20-24).
   Greater privilege = greater accountability. You have access to the Bible,
   teaching, community, and the Spirit. What are you doing with that privilege?

7. Jesus said God hides truth from the "wise and prudent" and reveals it to
   "babes" (v.25). Intellectual pride can be a barrier to spiritual understanding.
   Are you approaching God with the humility of a child, or the self-sufficiency
   of an expert?

8. "Learn of me; for I am meek and lowly in heart" (v.29). Jesus — the Lord
   of the universe — describes Himself as meek and lowly. What does it mean
   for a man to be meek? How is meekness different from weakness?

9. "My yoke is easy, and my burden is light" (v.30). If your Christian life
   feels crushing and exhausting, you may be carrying a yoke Jesus didn't give
   you — legalism, performance, others' expectations. What burdens do you need
   to lay down that aren't from Christ?
