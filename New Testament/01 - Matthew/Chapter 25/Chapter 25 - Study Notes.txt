Matthew - Chapter 25
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Jesus teaches three parables of readiness and accountability — the Ten Virgins, the Talents, and the Sheep and Goats — revealing that the kingdom demands faithful stewardship and genuine compassion toward "the least of these."

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Matthew (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 25 continues the Olivet Discourse with three
parables about readiness for Christ's return: the Ten Virgins (vv.1-13), the
Talents (vv.14-30), and the Sheep and Goats (vv.31-46). Together they answer
the question: "What does it look like to be ready when Jesus returns?"

The Ten Virgins: Readiness through spiritual preparedness (you cannot borrow
someone else's relationship with God).

The Talents: Readiness through faithful stewardship (use what God has given you).

The Sheep and Goats: Readiness through compassionate action (serve "the least
of these" as if serving Christ Himself).

These three parables form a complete picture: genuine faith is prepared (virgins),
productive (talents), and compassionate (sheep/goats). All three are necessary.

The Parable of the Talents: A talent was an enormous sum — approximately 20
years' wages. The master entrusted his servants with staggering resources. The
issue was not the amount given but what was done with it. The five-talent and
two-talent servants both received the same commendation: "Well done, good and
faithful servant."

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- The Mount of Olives: Jesus continues His Olivet Discourse (begun in ch.24), still seated on the Mount of Olives overlooking Jerusalem and the Temple.
- "All nations" (v.32): The final judgment encompasses all peoples from every geographic location — the scope is universal.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.13 — "Watch therefore, for you know neither the day nor the hour." (ESV) —
        The conclusion of the virgins parable: be ready always.

v.21 — "Well done, good and faithful servant. You have been faithful over a
        little; I will set you over much. Enter into the joy of your master."
        (ESV) — The words every believer longs to hear.

v.40 — "Truly, I say to you, as you did it to one of the least of these my
        brothers, you did it to me." (ESV) — Service to the vulnerable is
        service to Christ Himself.

v.46 — "And these will go away into eternal punishment, but the righteous into
        eternal life." (ESV) — The two eternal destinies — no middle ground.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1  — KJV: "ten virgins" — ESV: "ten bridesmaids" — Greek "parthenoi" —
       virgins/maidens. In the wedding custom, they waited with lamps to
       escort the bridegroom to the feast. The oil represents genuine
       spiritual life — it cannot be borrowed or shared.

v.15 — KJV: "talents" / ESV: "talents" — Both identical. A talent was a unit
       of weight (about 75 pounds of silver), worth approximately 6,000
       denarii or 20 years' wages. Five talents = 100 years' wages. The
       master entrusted enormous resources.

v.30 — KJV: "outer darkness" — ESV/NET: "outer darkness" — A metaphor for
       exclusion from God's presence and the joy of His kingdom. Used three
       times in Matthew (8:12, 22:13, 25:30).

v.46 — KJV: "everlasting punishment... life eternal" / ESV: "eternal
       punishment... eternal life" — Both identical in meaning. The same
       Greek word "aiōnios" (eternal) is used for both punishment and life.
       If one is eternal, so is the other. They stand or fall together.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
v.1  — NET: "the kingdom of heaven will be like ten virgins
       who took their lamps and went out to meet the bridegroom" —
       "lamps" (lampadas = torches). WEB: "ten virgins who took
       their lamps." Not small oil lamps but torches for a
       nighttime procession — they need oil reserves.

v.14 — NET: "it is like a man going on a journey, who summoned
       his slaves and entrusted his property to them" — "entrusted"
       (paredOken = handed over). WEB: "entrusted his goods to
       them." "Talent" (talanton = ~20 years' wages) — enormous
       sums, not small coins.

v.21 — NET: "Well done, good and faithful slave!" — "faithful"
       (piste = trustworthy/reliable). WEB: "Well done, good and
       faithful servant." KJV: "Well done, thou good and faithful
       servant." The commendation is for FAITHFULNESS, not the size
       of the return — both 5-talent and 2-talent servants receive
       identical praise.

v.40 — NET: "whatever you did for one of the least of these
       brothers or sisters of mine, you did for me" — "the least"
       (tOn elachistOn = the most insignificant). WEB: "inasmuch
       as you did it to one of the least of these my brothers."
       Christ identifies with the hungry, naked, sick, imprisoned.

v.46 — NET: "And these will depart into eternal punishment, but
       the righteous into eternal life" — "eternal" (aiOnion) —
       the same adjective modifies both punishment and life. WEB:
       "these will go away into eternal punishment." If one is
       temporary, both must be — the parallel demands equal duration.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Virgins       — Young unmarried women serving as bridesmaids in the wedding
               procession. They represent professing believers — some genuine
               (wise), some superficial (foolish).

Oil           — In the parable, represents genuine spiritual life — the Holy
               Spirit's indwelling, personal relationship with God. It cannot
               be borrowed from others.

Talent        — A unit of weight/money worth approximately 20 years' wages.
               In the parable, represents the resources, abilities, and
               opportunities God entrusts to each person.

Sheep/Goats   — In Palestine, sheep and goats grazed together but were
               separated at night. Jesus uses this familiar image for the
               final separation of the righteous and the wicked.

Least of these — Debated: (1) all people in need, (2) persecuted Christians
               specifically ("my brethren"), (3) any vulnerable person. The
               principle applies broadly: how you treat the powerless reveals
               your heart.

Outer darkness — A metaphor for eternal separation from God — the absence of
               His light, joy, and presence. The opposite of "enter into the
               joy of thy lord."

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. The foolish virgins had lamps but no oil — the appearance of readiness
   without the reality. Is your faith genuine and personal, or are you
   coasting on someone else's faith — your parents', your spouse's, your
   church's? Oil cannot be borrowed.

2. "Well done, good and faithful servant" (v.21). These are the words you
   want to hear. What has God entrusted to you — abilities, resources, time,
   influence, relationships? Are you investing them for His kingdom, or
   burying them in fear?

3. The one-talent servant's problem was fear (v.25) — "I was afraid." Fear
   of failure, fear of risk, fear of the master's expectations. Is fear
   keeping you from stepping out in faith? What would you do for God if you
   weren't afraid?

4. "Inasmuch as ye have done it unto one of the least of these" (v.40). Jesus
   identifies with the hungry, thirsty, stranger, naked, sick, and imprisoned.
   Who are "the least of these" in your world? What are you doing for them?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. The wise and foolish virgins looked identical until the moment of testing
   (v.5 — "they all slumbered and slept"). The difference was internal, not
   external. How do you know your faith is genuine and not just external
   conformity? What is the "oil" in your life?

2. The master gave "to every man according to his several ability" (v.15) —
   different amounts to different people. God doesn't compare you to others;
   He evaluates your faithfulness with what He gave you. Are you faithful
   with YOUR talents, or are you envying someone else's?

3. The one-talent servant said "I knew thee that thou art an hard man" (v.24)
   — he had a wrong view of the master. His theology was wrong, and it
   produced fear and inaction. How does your view of God affect your
   willingness to take risks for Him?

4. The sheep were surprised — "Lord, when saw we thee hungry?" (v.37). They
   served without realizing they were serving Christ. Genuine compassion
   doesn't calculate — it just acts. Is your service motivated by reward or
   by genuine love?

5. Jesus says the eternal fire was "prepared for the devil and his angels"
   (v.41) — not originally for humans. Hell was not God's desire for people.
   How does this truth affect your urgency in sharing the Gospel with those
   who don't know Christ?
