Matthew - Chapter 23
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Jesus pronounces seven devastating "woes" against the scribes and Pharisees for their hypocrisy, then weeps over Jerusalem: "How often would I have gathered thy children together, even as a hen gathereth her chickens under her wings, and ye would not!"

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Matthew (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 23 contains Jesus' most devastating public
denunciation of the religious leaders — the Seven Woes against the scribes and
Pharisees. This is not a private rebuke but a public indictment, delivered in
the Temple during Passion Week. It is the last public discourse Jesus gives
before the Olivet Discourse (chapters 24-25).

The Seven Woes: "Woe" (Greek: ouai) is a pronouncement of grief and judgment —
not anger for its own sake but righteous sorrow over corruption that destroys
people. Each woe exposes a specific form of religious hypocrisy:
  (1) Shutting the kingdom (v.13)
  (2) Devouring widows (v.14)
  (3) Making converts worse (v.15)
  (4) Blind oath-making (vv.16-22)
  (5) Neglecting justice, mercy, faith (vv.23-24)
  (6) External cleanliness, internal corruption (vv.25-26)
  (7) Whitewashed tombs (vv.27-28)

The chapter ends with Jesus' lament over Jerusalem (vv.37-39) — one of the
most emotionally raw moments in the Gospels. The same Jesus who pronounces
judgment weeps over the city that rejects Him.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- The Temple in Jerusalem: Jesus delivers the seven woes against the Pharisees in the Temple courts — His final public teaching.
- Jerusalem (v.37): The city itself is addressed — "O Jerusalem, Jerusalem." The city that killed the prophets and would soon kill the Messiah.
- "Your house is left unto you desolate" (v.38): A reference to the Temple — Jesus departs, and with Him goes God's presence.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.11 — "The greatest among you shall be your servant." (ESV) — Kingdom
        greatness defined — again — as service.

v.12 — "Whoever exalts himself will be humbled, and whoever humbles himself
        will be exalted." (ESV) — The universal principle of the kingdom:
        humility leads to exaltation; pride leads to humiliation.

v.23 — "You tithe mint and dill and cumin, and have neglected the weightier
        matters of the law: justice and mercy and faithfulness." (ESV) — The
        danger of religious precision without moral substance.

v.27 — "You are like whitewashed tombs, which outwardly appear beautiful, but
        within are full of dead people's bones." (ESV) — The most vivid image
        of hypocrisy in Scripture.

v.37 — "O Jerusalem, Jerusalem... How often would I have gathered your
        children together as a hen gathers her brood under her wings, and you
        were not willing!" (ESV) — Christ's grief over those who reject Him.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.5  — KJV: "phylacteries" — Small leather boxes containing Scripture verses
       (Exodus 13:9,16; Deuteronomy 6:8, 11:18), worn on the forehead and
       left arm during prayer. The Pharisees made theirs extra large to
       appear more devout. ESV/NET: same term.

v.5  — KJV: "borders of their garments" — ESV: "fringes" — NET: "tassels"
       — The tzitzit (Numbers 15:38-40) — tassels on the corners of garments
       as reminders of God's commands. The Pharisees enlarged them for show.

v.23 — KJV: "mint and anise and cummin" / ESV: "mint and dill and cumin" —
       Tiny garden herbs. The Pharisees tithed even these insignificant plants
       while neglecting justice, mercy, and faith. Jesus says "these ought ye
       to have done, and not to leave the other undone" — both matter, but
       priorities must be right.

v.24 — KJV: "strain at a gnat, and swallow a camel" — ESV: "strain out a
       gnat but swallow a camel" — A vivid hyperbole. They filtered their
       wine to avoid accidentally swallowing an unclean insect (Leviticus
       11:23) while "swallowing" massive moral failures.

v.27 — KJV: "whited sepulchres" / ESV: "whitewashed tombs" — Tombs were
       whitewashed before Passover so people wouldn't accidentally touch them
       and become ritually unclean (Numbers 19:16). They looked clean on the
       outside but contained death.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
v.3  — NET: "do whatever they tell you and follow it, but do
       not do what they do, for they do not practice what they
       teach" — "do not do what they do" (kata de ta erga autOn
       mE poieite). WEB: "don't do their works." Authority is
       valid even when the authority-holder is hypocritical.

v.13 — NET: "you shut the door of the kingdom of heaven in
       people's faces" — "shut the door" (kleiete = lock/close).
       WEB: "you shut up the Kingdom of Heaven against men." KJV:
       "ye shut up the kingdom of heaven against men." They neither
       enter nor allow others to enter.

v.23 — NET: "you neglect what is more important in the law —
       justice, mercy, and faithfulness!" — "more important" (ta
       barytera = the weightier things). WEB: "the weightier
       matters of the law: justice, mercy, and faith." KJV: "the
       weightier matters of the law." Not all commandments are
       equal — some are weightier.

v.27 — NET: "you are like whitewashed tombs that look beautiful
       on the outside but inside are full of dead men's bones" —
       "whitewashed tombs" (taphois kekoniamenois). WEB: "like
       whitewashed tombs." Beautiful exterior concealing interior
       corruption — the most vivid image of religious hypocrisy.

v.37 — NET: "how often I have longed to gather your children
       together as a hen gathers her chicks under her wings" —
       "longed" (EthelEsa = wanted/desired repeatedly). WEB: "how
       often I would have gathered your children together." KJV:
       "how often would I have gathered." Maternal imagery for God
       — tender, protective, rejected.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Woe           — Greek "ouai" — a pronouncement of grief and coming judgment.
               Not a curse but a lament — expressing both sorrow and warning.

Phylacteries  — Small leather boxes (Hebrew: tefillin) containing Scripture
               passages, worn during prayer on the forehead and left arm.
               Based on Deuteronomy 6:8.

Hypocrite     — Greek "hupokritēs" — originally an actor on a stage, one who
               wears a mask. In Jesus' usage: one whose outward appearance
               contradicts their inward reality.

Proselyte     — A Gentile convert to Judaism who underwent circumcision,
               baptism, and sacrifice. The Pharisees made converts "twofold
               more the child of hell" by teaching them legalism rather than
               genuine faith.

Whitewashed   — Tombs were coated with white lime before festivals to make
               them visible (preventing accidental contact). Beautiful outside,
               full of death inside — Jesus' metaphor for hypocrisy.

Desolate      — Empty, abandoned, forsaken. Jesus declares Jerusalem's "house"
               (the Temple) will be left desolate — fulfilled in AD 70 when
               Rome destroyed it.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Jesus said the Pharisees "say, and do not" (v.3). They taught truth but
   didn't live it. Is there a gap between what you teach/profess and how you
   actually live? Where is your walk not matching your talk?

2. "All their works they do for to be seen of men" (v.5). The Pharisees'
   motivation was human approval, not God's glory. Honest check: when you
   pray, give, serve, or post about your faith — who is your audience? God
   or people?

3. "Ye have omitted the weightier matters of the law, judgment, mercy, and
   faith" (v.23). It's possible to be precise about small religious details
   while missing the big things — justice, mercy, faithfulness. Which
   "weightier matters" are you neglecting while being careful about lesser
   things?

4. "Ye are like unto whited sepulchres" (v.27). Beautiful outside, dead
   inside. Every man has an outer life and an inner life. How different are
   yours? What would people see if your inner life was suddenly visible?

5. Jesus wept over Jerusalem (v.37) — even as He pronounced judgment. He
   wanted to gather them, but they refused. How does Jesus' grief over those
   who reject Him shape how you view people who reject the Gospel?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. The Pharisees loved titles, positions, and public recognition (vv.6-7).
   Jesus says "be not ye called Rabbi" — don't seek titles for status. How
   do you handle titles, positions, and recognition? Do they serve your
   ministry or feed your ego?

2. Jesus said the Pharisees "shut up the kingdom of heaven against men" (v.13)
   — they made it harder for people to come to God. Is there anything about
   your faith, your church, or your behavior that makes it harder for people
   to come to Christ? What would it look like to open doors rather than close
   them?

3. "Strain at a gnat, and swallow a camel" (v.24). Where in your life are you
   being meticulous about small things while ignoring something much larger?
   What "camel" are you swallowing while straining out gnats?

4. Jesus' lament (v.37) shows that judgment and love coexist in God. He
   pronounces woe AND weeps. How do you hold together truth (calling sin what
   it is) and love (grieving over those trapped in it)?

5. "How often would I have gathered thy children together... and ye would not"
   (v.37). God's desire is to gather, protect, and save. The barrier is human
   refusal. Is there an area of your life where you are saying "I will not"
   to God's gathering, protecting love?
