Matthew - Chapter 6
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Jesus teaches His disciples to practice righteousness in secret rather than for show — covering prayer (including the Lord's Prayer), fasting, and freedom from anxiety through trusting the Father's provision.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Matthew (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 6 is the heart of the Sermon on the Mount, dealing
with the inner life of the believer — how we give, pray, fast, and relate to
money and anxiety. While chapter 5 addressed our relationships with others,
chapter 6 addresses our relationship with God and our inner motivations.

The central theme: audience. Jesus repeatedly contrasts doing things "to be seen
of men" (human audience) with doing things "in secret" before the Father (divine
audience). The question is not whether you pray, give, or fast — but who you're
performing for.

The Lord's Prayer (vv.9-13): Not a prayer to be recited mechanically but a
model — a pattern for how to pray. It covers worship (hallowed be thy name),
submission (thy will be done), dependence (daily bread), forgiveness (forgive
us our debts), and protection (deliver us from evil).

Anxiety (vv.25-34): Jesus addresses worry — one of the most common struggles
for men. His argument: if God feeds the birds and clothes the flowers, He will
certainly care for you. Worry is a faith issue, not a circumstance issue.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- The Mount (near Capernaum): The Sermon on the Mount continues. Same hillside location near the Sea of Galilee in northern Galilee.
- Jesus references "Solomon in all his glory" (v.29) — connecting to the Temple in Jerusalem, the center of Jewish worship and wealth.
- The setting remains Galilee, where Jesus' audience consisted largely of working-class fishermen, farmers, and tradespeople who would understand references to birds, lilies, and daily bread.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.6  — "But when you pray, go into your room and shut the door and pray to
        your Father who is in secret." (ESV) — The priority of private prayer
        over public performance.

v.21 — "For where your treasure is, there your heart will be also." (ESV) —
        Your spending reveals your true priorities.

v.24 — "No one can serve two masters... You cannot serve God and money." (ESV)
        — The impossibility of divided loyalty.

v.33 — "But seek first the kingdom of God and his righteousness, and all these
        things will be added to you." (ESV) — The one priority that orders
        all others.

v.34 — "Therefore do not be anxious about tomorrow, for tomorrow will be
        anxious for itself." (ESV) — Live one day at a time.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.11 — KJV: "Give us this day our daily bread" / ESV: "Give us this day our
       daily bread" — Both identical. The Greek "epiousios" (translated
       "daily") is one of the rarest words in the NT — found only here and
       in Luke 11:3. Its exact meaning is debated: "for today," "for the
       coming day," or "necessary for existence." All translations use "daily"
       but the word is unique.

v.13 — KJV: "For thine is the kingdom, and the power, and the glory, for ever.
       Amen." / ESV: [omitted from main text; footnoted] — This doxology is
       absent from the oldest manuscripts. ESV/NET place it in a footnote. It
       was likely added by the early church for liturgical use. It is
       theologically sound but probably not original.

v.22 — KJV: "if thine eye be single" — ESV: "if your eye is healthy" — NET:
       "if your eye is good" — Greek "haplous" means single, simple, generous.
       A "single eye" is one focused on God alone — not divided between God
       and money.

v.24 — KJV: "mammon" — Aramaic word for wealth/money personified as a rival
       god. ESV/NET: "money." KJV preserves the Aramaic to emphasize that
       money can become an idol — a master demanding worship.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
v.6  — NET: "go into your inner room, close the door, and
       pray to your Father in secret" — "inner room" (tameion =
       storeroom/private chamber). WEB: "enter into your inner
       room." KJV: "enter into thy closet." Privacy not for
       isolation but for sincerity — God sees in secret.

v.9  — NET: "So pray this way: Our Father in heaven, may your
       name be honored" — "may your name be honored" (hagiasthEtO
       to onoma sou). WEB: "may your name be kept holy." KJV:
       "Hallowed be thy name." NET interprets the passive
       imperative; KJV preserves the traditional form.

v.11 — NET: "Give us today our daily bread" — "daily" (epiousion
       = a unique word, meaning debated: for today? for tomorrow?
       necessary? supernatural?). WEB: "Give us today our daily
       bread." The word appears nowhere else in Greek literature —
       its exact meaning remains uncertain.

v.22 — NET: "The eye is the lamp of the body" — "lamp" (lychnos
       = oil lamp). WEB: "The lamp of the body is the eye." KJV:
       "The light of the body is the eye." How you see determines
       what enters you — healthy eyes let in light; bad eyes fill
       you with darkness.

v.34 — NET: "So then, do not worry about tomorrow, for tomorrow
       will worry about itself" — "worry" (merimnEsEte = be anxious/
       divided in mind). WEB: "Don't be anxious for tomorrow." KJV:
       "Take therefore no thought for the morrow." Each day has
       enough trouble of its own (v.34b).

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Alms          — Charitable giving to the poor. One of the three "acts of
               righteousness" in Jewish piety (along with prayer and fasting).

Closet        — KJV for a private inner room — a storeroom or small chamber.
               The point: pray where no one sees you but God.

Vain repetitions — Greek "battalogeo" — to babble, to pile up empty words.
               Not about length of prayer but about mindless repetition
               without genuine engagement.

Mammon        — Aramaic word for wealth, riches, material possessions. Used
               as a personification — money as a rival god demanding loyalty.

Single eye    — An eye focused on one thing — God. The opposite of a divided
               heart trying to serve two masters.

Cubit         — A unit of measurement (approximately 18 inches). Jesus' point:
               worry cannot add even a small amount to your life.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Jesus says the hypocrites "have their reward" (vv.2,5,16) — human applause.
   That's all they get. When you give, pray, or serve — who is your audience?
   Are you performing for people or living before God? What would change if
   no one ever saw your good deeds?

2. "Where your treasure is, there will your heart be also" (v.21). Look at
   your bank statement and your calendar — they reveal your true priorities.
   What do they say about where your heart actually is?

3. "Ye cannot serve God and mammon" (v.24). Jesus doesn't say "should not" —
   He says "cannot." It's impossible. Which master are you actually serving
   with your financial decisions? Is money a tool in your hand or a master
   over your life?

4. "Seek ye first the kingdom of God" (v.33). First — before career, before
   comfort, before security. What does seeking God's kingdom first look like
   in your actual daily schedule? Is it first, or is it what gets the leftovers?

5. "Take no thought for the morrow" (v.34). Anxiety is a faith issue. What
   are you worried about right now? Can you name it specifically? Now — what
   would it look like to hand that to the Father who feeds the birds?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. The Lord's Prayer begins with "Our Father" — establishing relationship
   before making requests. How does your prayer life reflect that order? Do
   you rush to requests, or do you begin with worship and submission?

2. "Forgive us our debts, as we forgive our debtors" (v.12). Jesus doubles
   down in vv.14-15 — if you don't forgive others, the Father won't forgive
   you. Is there someone you are refusing to forgive? What is that unforgiveness
   costing you spiritually?

3. Jesus says to fast without looking miserable (vv.16-18). Fasting is assumed
   as a normal practice, not optional. Do you fast? If not, why not? What
   might God do in you through the discipline of fasting?

4. "Consider the lilies" (v.28) — Jesus points to nature as evidence of God's
   care. When did you last slow down enough to see God's provision in the
   ordinary details of your life? What evidence of His care are you overlooking?

5. "Sufficient unto the day is the evil thereof" (v.34). Today has enough
   challenges without borrowing tomorrow's. Are you living in today, or are
   you mentally living in a future that hasn't happened yet?
