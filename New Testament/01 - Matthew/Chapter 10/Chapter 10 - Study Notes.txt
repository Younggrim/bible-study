Matthew - Chapter 10
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Jesus commissions the twelve apostles with authority over demons and disease, sends them to the lost sheep of Israel, and warns them of persecution — promising that whoever confesses Him before men, He will confess before the Father.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Matthew (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 10 is the second of Matthew's five major discourses
— the Mission Discourse. Jesus commissions the twelve apostles and sends them
out with authority, instructions, and warnings. The chapter begins with immediate
instructions for their first mission (vv.5-15) but expands into broader teaching
about persecution and the cost of discipleship that applies to all believers
throughout history (vv.16-42).

The Twelve Apostles: Jesus chose twelve — matching the twelve tribes of Israel.
They represent the foundation of the new Israel, the church. The list includes
a tax collector (Matthew), a zealot (Simon), fishermen, and a betrayer (Judas).
Jesus chose ordinary, flawed men — not the religious elite.

Persecution Warnings: Jesus does not promise His followers safety or popularity.
He promises opposition — from religious authorities (v.17), governments (v.18),
and even family members (v.21). But He also promises the Spirit's help (v.20),
the Father's care (vv.29-31), and ultimate vindication (v.32).

"I came not to send peace, but a sword" (v.34): Jesus is not promoting violence
— He is acknowledging that following Him will divide relationships. The Gospel
creates division because it demands ultimate allegiance.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- The mission is confined to Israel: "Go not into the way of the Gentiles, and into any city of the Samaritans enter ye not" (v.5). The disciples were sent only to "the lost sheep of the house of Israel."
- Cities and villages of Galilee and Judea: The twelve were sent throughout the towns of Israel, preaching the kingdom.
- The geographic restriction would later be lifted in the Great Commission (Matt. 28:19) — "all nations."

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.8  — "You received without paying; give without pay." (ESV) — The principle
        of grace-based ministry — what you received as a gift, give as a gift.

v.16 — "So be wise as serpents and innocent as doves." (ESV) — The balance
        of shrewdness and innocence in a hostile world.

v.28 — "And do not fear those who kill the body but cannot kill the soul.
        Rather fear him who can destroy both soul and body in hell." (ESV)
        — The proper object of fear — God alone.

v.29-31 — "Are not two sparrows sold for a penny? And not one of them will
        fall to the ground apart from your Father... you are of more value
        than many sparrows." (ESV) — God's detailed, personal care for each
        individual.

v.38 — "And whoever does not take his cross and follow me is not worthy of
        me." (ESV) — The first mention of the cross in Matthew — before the
        crucifixion. Discipleship means death to self.

v.39 — "Whoever finds his life will lose it, and whoever loses his life for
        my sake will find it." (ESV) — The paradox of the kingdom.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.4  — KJV: "Simon the Canaanite" — ESV/NET: "Simon the Zealot" — Not from
       Canaan but from the Aramaic "qan'an" meaning zealous. He was likely
       a former member of the Zealot party — Jewish nationalists who advocated
       violent overthrow of Rome. Jesus put a zealot and a tax collector
       (Roman collaborator) in the same group of twelve.

v.10 — KJV: "the workman is worthy of his meat" — ESV: "the laborer deserves
       his food" — The principle that those who minister deserve support.
       Paul quotes this in 1 Timothy 5:18.

v.25 — KJV: "Beelzebub" / ESV: "Beelzebul" — A name for Satan. KJV uses
       "Beelzebub" (lord of the flies); ESV uses "Beelzebul" (lord of the
       house), following older manuscripts. The Pharisees accused Jesus of
       casting out demons by Satan's power (Matthew 12:24).

v.34 — KJV: "I came not to send peace, but a sword" / ESV: "I have not come
       to bring peace, but a sword" — Both equivalent. The "sword" is not
       military violence but the division that the Gospel inevitably creates.
       Following Jesus will divide families and relationships because it
       demands ultimate allegiance above all other loyalties.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
v.8  — NET: "Heal the sick, raise the dead, cleanse lepers,
       cast out demons. Freely you received, freely give" —
       "freely" (dOrean = as a gift/without payment). WEB: "Freely
       you received, freely give." KJV: "freely ye have received,
       freely give." Grace received must become grace distributed.

v.16 — NET: "I am sending you out like sheep among wolves, so be
       wise as serpents and innocent as doves" — "wise" (phronimoi
       = shrewd/prudent) and "innocent" (akeraioi = pure/unmixed).
       WEB: "wise as serpents, and harmless as doves." Both
       qualities together — shrewdness without corruption.

v.28 — NET: "Do not be afraid of those who kill the body but
       cannot kill the soul" — "cannot kill the soul" (tEn psychEn
       mE dynamenOn apokteinai). WEB: "Don't be afraid of those
       who kill the body." Human power is limited to the physical
       — fear the One who governs both body and soul.

v.29 — NET: "Aren't two sparrows sold for a penny? Yet not one of
       them falls to the ground apart from your Father's will" —
       "penny" (assariou = smallest Roman coin). WEB: "Aren't two
       sparrows sold for an assarion coin?" God's providence
       extends to the cheapest, most insignificant creatures.

v.34 — NET: "Do not think that I have come to bring peace on the
       earth. I have not come to bring peace but a sword" — "sword"
       (machairan = short sword/dagger). WEB: "I came not to send
       peace, but a sword." KJV: "I came not to send peace, but a
       sword." The gospel divides — even families (vv.35-36).

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Apostle       — Greek "apostolos" — one sent with authority. The twelve were
               sent by Jesus with His authority to preach, heal, and cast out
               demons.

Farthing      — A small Roman coin of very little value. Two sparrows were
               sold for one farthing — yet God notices each one.

Scrip         — KJV for a traveler's bag or knapsack. Jesus told them to
               travel light — depending on God's provision through hospitality.

Beelzebub     — A name for Satan. Originally "Baal-zebub" (lord of the flies)
               — a mocking corruption of "Baal-zebul" (lord of the house).

Cross         — Before the crucifixion, this word meant one thing: a Roman
               instrument of execution. To "take up your cross" meant to
               walk toward your own death. Jesus uses it as a metaphor for
               total self-denial.

Confess       — Greek "homologeo" — to say the same thing, to agree publicly.
               To confess Christ is to publicly identify with Him regardless
               of the cost.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Freely ye have received, freely give" (v.8). Everything you have — your
   salvation, your gifts, your opportunities — was given freely by God. How
   freely are you giving to others? Where are you hoarding what was meant
   to be shared?

2. "Be wise as serpents, and harmless as doves" (v.16). Jesus doesn't call
   us to be naive or reckless — but He also doesn't call us to be manipulative
   or aggressive. How do you balance shrewdness and innocence in your workplace,
   your relationships, your witness?

3. "Fear not them which kill the body" (v.28). The worst any human can do is
   kill your body — they cannot touch your soul. What human opinion or threat
   are you fearing more than God? What would change if you feared God alone?

4. "The very hairs of your head are all numbered" (v.30). God's knowledge of
   you is that detailed. He is not distant or distracted. How does knowing
   you are that specifically known and cared for change how you face today?

5. "He that taketh not his cross, and followeth after me, is not worthy of me"
   (v.38). What is your cross right now — the specific thing that costs you
   to follow Jesus? Are you carrying it or avoiding it?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. Jesus sent the twelve with authority but also with vulnerability — no money,
   no extra supplies, dependent on others' hospitality (vv.9-10). Why does God
   often send us into mission in a posture of dependence rather than self-
   sufficiency? What does that teach us?

2. Jesus warned that following Him would divide families (vv.34-36). Have you
   experienced family tension because of your faith? How do you navigate loving
   your family while maintaining ultimate allegiance to Christ?

3. "He that loveth father or mother more than me is not worthy of me" (v.37).
   Jesus demands first place — above every human relationship. Is there any
   relationship in your life that competes with your loyalty to Christ?

4. "He that findeth his life shall lose it: and he that loseth his life for
   my sake shall find it" (v.39). What does "losing your life" look like
   practically — not martyrdom, but daily dying to self? Where is God asking
   you to let go of something you're clinging to?

5. Jesus promised that even a cup of cold water given in His name would be
   rewarded (v.42). Small acts of service matter. What small, seemingly
   insignificant act of kindness or service is God calling you to today?
