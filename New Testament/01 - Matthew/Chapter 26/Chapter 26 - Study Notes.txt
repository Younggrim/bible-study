Matthew - Chapter 26
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
The Passion begins: Jesus is anointed at Bethany, institutes the Lord's Supper, agonizes in Gethsemane, is betrayed by Judas' kiss, arrested, and tried before the Sanhedrin — while Peter denies Him three times.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Matthew (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 26 is the longest chapter in Matthew and covers the
events of Thursday night/Friday morning of Passion Week: the plot to kill Jesus
(vv.1-5), the anointing at Bethany (vv.6-13), Judas' betrayal agreement (vv.14-16),
the Last Supper (vv.17-30), the prediction of Peter's denial (vv.31-35),
Gethsemane (vv.36-46), the arrest (vv.47-56), the trial before Caiaphas (vv.57-68),
and Peter's three denials (vv.69-75).

The Last Supper: Jesus transforms the Passover meal into the Lord's Supper —
the bread representing His body, the cup representing His blood of the new
covenant. Every Passover pointed forward to this moment — the true Lamb of God
sacrificed for the sins of the world.

Gethsemane: The most intimate glimpse into Jesus' humanity in the Gospels. He
was "sorrowful, even unto death" (v.38). He asked the Father to remove the cup
if possible — but submitted: "not as I will, but as thou wilt." This is not
weakness but the deepest strength — choosing obedience when every fiber of His
being recoiled from what lay ahead.

Peter's Denial: The man who said "I will never be offended" (v.33) denied Jesus
three times within hours — the last time with cursing and swearing. Then the
rooster crowed, and Peter "wept bitterly" (v.75). This is the anatomy of
spiritual failure: overconfidence → distance → compromise → denial → brokenness.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Bethany (v.6): A village on the eastern slope of the Mount of Olives, about 2 miles from Jerusalem. The anointing at Simon the leper's house occurred here.
- The Upper Room (vv.17-30): A large furnished room in Jerusalem where Jesus celebrated the Last Supper with His disciples.
- Gethsemane (v.36): A garden/olive grove at the foot of the Mount of Olives, across the Kidron Valley from the Temple. Jesus prayed here and was arrested.
- The High Priest's house (v.57): Caiaphas's palace in Jerusalem where Jesus was tried by the Sanhedrin.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.28 — "For this is my blood of the covenant, which is poured out for many
        for the forgiveness of sins." (ESV) — The institution of the new
        covenant in Christ's blood.

v.39 — "My Father, if it be possible, let this cup pass from me; nevertheless,
        not as I will, but as you will." (ESV) — The model prayer of
        submission — honest desire yielded to the Father's will.

v.41 — "Watch and pray that you may not enter into temptation. The spirit
        indeed is willing, but the flesh is weak." (ESV) — The prescription
        for spiritual vigilance.

v.52 — "For all who take the sword will perish by the sword." (ESV) — The
        kingdom is not advanced by violence.

v.64 — "From now on you will see the Son of Man seated at the right hand of
        Power and coming on the clouds of heaven." (ESV) — Jesus' claim before
        the Sanhedrin — the statement that sealed His death sentence.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.26 — KJV: "This is my body" / ESV: "This is my body" — Both identical. The
       meaning of "is" (Greek: estin) is debated: (1) Catholic/Orthodox:
       literal — transubstantiation; (2) Lutheran: "in, with, and under" —
       consubstantiation; (3) Reformed: "represents" or "signifies" —
       memorial. Jesus was physically present when He said it, making a
       purely literal reading difficult.

v.28 — KJV: "new testament" — ESV: "covenant" — Greek "diathēkē" — a covenant,
       not a "testament" in the modern sense. The new covenant replaces the
       old Mosaic covenant (Jeremiah 31:31-34).

v.39 — KJV: "this cup" / ESV: "this cup" — Both identical. The cup of God's
       wrath against sin (Isaiah 51:17, Psalm 75:8). Jesus was not merely
       afraid of physical death but of bearing the full weight of divine
       judgment against all human sin.

v.64 — KJV: "Thou hast said" / ESV: "You have said so" — Both identical in
       meaning. A Jewish idiom for "yes, you are correct." Jesus affirms He
       is the Christ, then adds the Daniel 7:13 claim — making His deity
       explicit. This is what the high priest calls "blasphemy."

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
v.15 — NET: "What are you willing to give me to betray him to
       you?" — "willing to give" (thelete moi dounai). WEB: "What
       are you willing to give me?" KJV: "What will ye give me?"
       Judas initiates. "They set out for him thirty pieces of
       silver" — the price of a slave (Exodus 21:32).

v.26 — NET: "Take, eat, this is my body" — "this is" (touto estin)
       — the words of institution. WEB: "Take, eat; this is my
       body." Debated across church history: literal? spiritual?
       memorial? The simplicity of the words conceals centuries of
       theological debate.

v.28 — NET: "this is my blood of the covenant, that is poured out
       for many for the forgiveness of sins" — "blood of the
       covenant" (to haima mou tEs diathEkEs). WEB: "my blood of
       the new covenant." KJV: "my blood of the new testament."
       Some manuscripts omit "new" — NET/ESV omit; KJV includes.

v.39 — NET: "My Father, if possible, let this cup pass from me!
       Yet not what I will, but what you will" — "if possible"
       (ei dynaton estin). WEB: "if it is possible." KJV: "if it
       be possible." Honest prayer + total submission — the model
       for all prayer in suffering.

v.64 — NET: "You have said it yourself. But I tell you, from now
       on you will see the Son of Man sitting at the right hand of
       the Power" — "You have said it" (sy eipas = affirmative
       answer in rabbinic idiom). WEB: "You have said so." KJV:
       "Thou hast said." Jesus affirms He is the Christ (v.63)
       then escalates: He will sit at God's right hand (Psalm 110)
       and come on clouds (Daniel 7:13).

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Passover      — The annual Jewish feast commemorating the Exodus from Egypt
               (Exodus 12). The lamb was slain and its blood applied to the
               doorposts. Jesus is the ultimate Passover Lamb.

New covenant  — The covenant promised in Jeremiah 31:31-34, established by
               Christ's blood. It replaces the Mosaic covenant with one based
               on grace, forgiveness, and the indwelling Spirit.

Gethsemane    — "Oil press" — a garden on the Mount of Olives where Jesus
               prayed before His arrest. The place where He was "pressed"
               under the weight of coming judgment.

Thirty pieces of silver — The price of a slave (Exodus 21:32). The amount
               Judas received for betraying Jesus — fulfilling Zechariah 11:12.

Sanhedrin     — The Jewish supreme court of 71 members. They tried Jesus at
               night (illegal), with predetermined verdict (illegal), and
               without proper witnesses (illegal).

Cup           — A metaphor for God's wrath poured out against sin. Jesus
               drank this cup at the cross so that believers would never
               have to.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. The woman poured expensive ointment on Jesus' head — an act of extravagant
   devotion that the disciples called "waste" (v.8). Jesus called it "a good
   work" (v.10). What does extravagant devotion to Christ look like in your
   life? Are you holding back what He deserves?

2. "Not as I will, but as thou wilt" (v.39). This is the hardest prayer a man
   can pray — surrendering his own desires to God's plan, especially when God's
   plan involves suffering. What is the "cup" in your life right now that you
   wish would pass? Can you pray this prayer honestly?

3. "Watch and pray, that ye enter not into temptation" (v.41). The disciples
   slept when they should have prayed — and within hours they all fled. What
   is the connection between prayerlessness and spiritual failure in your own
   experience?

4. Peter "followed him afar off" (v.58) — close enough to see but far enough
   to deny. Are you following Christ closely or at a distance? What happens
   to your faith when you create distance between yourself and Jesus?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. Judas betrayed Jesus with a kiss (v.49) — the most intimate gesture used
   for the most treacherous act. Is there anything in your life that looks
   like devotion on the surface but is actually betrayal — using religious
   language or behavior to cover unfaithfulness?

2. Jesus could have called twelve legions of angels (v.53) — 72,000 angels —
   but chose not to. He had the power to escape and chose to stay. What does
   it mean to you that Jesus chose the cross when He could have avoided it?

3. Peter's denial escalated: first a simple "I know not what thou sayest"
   (v.70), then an oath (v.72), then cursing and swearing (v.74). Sin always
   escalates. Where in your life is a "small" compromise in danger of
   escalating into something worse?

4. "He went out, and wept bitterly" (v.75). Peter's tears were the beginning
   of restoration. When was the last time you wept over your sin — not just
   felt bad about consequences, but genuinely grieved that you had dishonored
   Christ?

5. The disciples "all forsook him, and fled" (v.56). Every one of them. In
   the moment of greatest testing, they all failed. How does knowing that
   even the apostles failed catastrophically give you hope after your own
   failures?
