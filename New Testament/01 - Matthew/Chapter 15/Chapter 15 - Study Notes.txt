Matthew - Chapter 15
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Jesus rebukes the Pharisees for elevating human tradition over God's commandments, honors a Canaanite woman's persistent faith by healing her daughter, and feeds four thousand in another miraculous provision.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Matthew (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 15 contains two major sections: Jesus' confrontation
with the Pharisees over tradition vs. God's commands (vv.1-20), and His encounter
with the Canaanite woman (vv.21-28), followed by the feeding of the 4,000
(vv.29-39).

Tradition of the Elders: The Pharisees had developed an extensive oral tradition
(later codified as the Mishnah) that added hundreds of rules to the written Law.
Hand washing before meals was not a hygiene issue but a ritual purity requirement
they had elevated to the level of God's command. Jesus exposes how their
traditions actually violated God's actual commands.

Corban (vv.5-6): A practice where a person could declare their money or property
"devoted to God" (corban), making it unavailable to support aging parents — while
still using it themselves. This loophole allowed people to avoid the fifth
commandment (honor your father and mother) under the guise of religious devotion.

The Canaanite Woman: Jesus traveled to the region of Tyre and Sidon — Gentile
territory. A Canaanite woman (a descendant of Israel's ancient enemies) came to
Him. His initial silence and seemingly harsh response ("It is not meet to take
the children's bread, and to cast it to dogs") was not cruelty but a test of
faith — and she passed magnificently.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Tyre and Sidon (v.21): Phoenician coastal cities (modern Lebanon), about 35-50 miles northwest of the Sea of Galilee. Jesus traveled into Gentile territory — a significant geographic move.
- Sea of Galilee / mountain (v.29): Jesus returned to the Sea of Galilee and went up on a mountain, where He healed multitudes and fed 4,000.
- Magadan/Magdala (v.39): A town on the western shore of the Sea of Galilee, where Jesus sailed after the feeding.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.8-9 — "This people honors me with their lips, but their heart is far from
        me; in vain do they worship me, teaching as doctrines the commandments
        of men." (ESV) — The danger of external religion without internal
        reality.

v.11 — "It is not what goes into the mouth that defiles a person, but what
        comes out of the mouth; this defiles a person." (ESV) — Defilement is
        a heart issue, not a dietary one.

v.18-19 — "But what comes out of the mouth proceeds from the heart, and this
        defiles a person. For out of the heart come evil thoughts, murder,
        adultery, sexual immorality, theft, false witness, slander." (ESV) —
        The heart is the source of all sin.

v.28 — "O woman, great is your faith! Be it done for you as you desire." (ESV)
        — One of only two times Jesus commends someone for "great faith" (the
        other is the centurion in Matthew 8:10) — both Gentiles.

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.3  — NET: "Why do you also break the commandment of God
       because of your tradition?" — "tradition" (paradosin =
       handed-down teaching). WEB: "Why do you also disobey the
       commandment of God because of your tradition?" Human
       traditions elevated above divine commands — the perennial
       temptation of religion.

v.5  — KJV: "It is a gift" — ESV: "What you would have gained from me is
       given to God" — The Hebrew/Aramaic word is "corban" (Mark 7:11) — a
       vow dedicating something to God, making it unavailable for other uses.

v.11 — NET: "it is not what goes into the mouth that defiles a
       person, but what comes out of the mouth" — "defiles" (koinoi
       = makes common/ritually unclean). WEB: "that which proceeds
       out of the mouth, this defiles the man." A revolutionary
       principle overturning the entire Jewish purity system.

v.22 — KJV: "a woman of Canaan" — ESV: "a Canaanite woman" — Mark calls her
       "Syrophoenician" (Mark 7:26). Both identify her as a Gentile from the
       ancient enemy territory of Israel. Her faith is all the more remarkable
       given the historical hostility between these peoples.
       NET: "Have mercy on me, Lord, Son of David! My daughter
       is horribly demon-possessed" — "have mercy" (eleEson =
       show compassion). WEB: "Have mercy on me." KJV: "Have mercy
       on me." A Canaanite woman uses the messianic title "Son of
       David" — greater faith from a pagan than from Israel.

v.26 — KJV: "dogs" / ESV: "dogs" — Both identical. Greek "kunarion" —
       diminutive form meaning "little dogs" or "household pets," not the wild
       scavenger dogs of the streets. Jesus uses the softer term — referring to
       pets under the table, not unclean animals. The woman picks up on this
       distinction in her reply.

v.28 — NET: "Woman, your faith is great! Let what you want be
       done for you" — "great" (megalE = large). WEB: "great is
       your faith!" KJV: "O woman, great is thy faith." Only two
       people receive this commendation in Matthew: this Canaanite
       and the centurion (8:10) — both Gentiles.

v.32 — NET: "I have compassion on the crowd, because they have
       already been here with me three days" — "compassion"
       (splanchnizomai) again — the same visceral emotion as 14:14.
       WEB: "I have compassion on the multitude." Jesus feeds 4,000
       — a second feeding miracle.

v.38 — KJV: "four thousand men, beside women and children" / ESV: "four
       thousand men, besides women and children" — Both identical in meaning.
       A different miracle from the feeding of the 5,000 (ch.14). Different
       location, different numbers, different baskets (Greek "spuris" here vs.
       "kophinos" in ch.14).

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Tradition of the elders — The oral law developed by rabbis to interpret and
               apply the written Torah. Later codified as the Mishnah (~AD 200).
               Jesus distinguished between God's commands and human additions.

Corban        — A Hebrew/Aramaic term meaning "devoted to God." A vow that
               could be used to avoid financial obligations to family while
               keeping the resources for personal use.

Defile        — To make unclean, impure, or unfit for worship. Jesus redefines
               defilement as a heart condition, not a ritual one.

Tyre and Sidon — Phoenician coastal cities north of Israel (modern Lebanon).
               Gentile territory. Jesus' journey there signals the Gospel's
               reach beyond Israel.

Canaanite     — A descendant of the ancient inhabitants of the Promised Land
               — Israel's historical enemies. This woman's faith transcends
               ethnic and religious barriers.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Jesus exposed how religious tradition can actually contradict God's commands
   (vv.3-6). Are there religious habits or traditions in your life that you
   follow without examining whether they align with Scripture? What would it
   look like to evaluate your practices against God's actual Word?

2. "Out of the heart proceed evil thoughts" (v.19). Jesus lists the sins that
   flow from the heart — and every man recognizes himself in that list. Which
   of these is your greatest struggle? What are you doing to address the heart
   issue, not just the behavior?

3. The Canaanite woman was met with silence, apparent rejection, and a
   seemingly insulting comparison — and she persisted. Her faith was not
   deterred by difficulty. How persistent is your prayer life? Do you give
   up when God seems silent, or do you press in?

4. Jesus commended "great faith" in two Gentiles — people outside the
   religious establishment. Sometimes the people with the deepest faith are
   those with the least religious background. How does that challenge your
   assumptions about who has real faith?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. The Pharisees honored God with their lips but their hearts were far from
   Him (v.8). Honest check: is your worship genuine or performative? Is there
   a gap between what you say about God and how you actually live?

2. Jesus said defilement comes from within, not from external sources (v.11).
   We often blame our environment for our sin — "If my circumstances were
   different, I wouldn't struggle with this." How does Jesus' teaching
   challenge that excuse?

3. The Canaanite woman called Jesus "Lord" and "Son of David" — she had more
   theological clarity than many in Israel. Where did her faith come from?
   What does her example teach you about faith that persists through apparent
   rejection?

4. The disciples had already seen Jesus feed 5,000 (ch.14) and still asked
   "Where would we get enough bread?" (v.33). How quickly we forget God's
   past faithfulness. What has God done for you in the past that you need to
   remember right now?

5. Jesus had compassion on the crowd because they had been with Him three days
   without food (v.32). He noticed their physical need. How attentive are you
   to the practical needs of people around you — not just their spiritual needs?
