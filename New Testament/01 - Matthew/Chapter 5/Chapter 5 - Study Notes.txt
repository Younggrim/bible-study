Matthew - Chapter 5
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Jesus opens the Sermon on the Mount with the Beatitudes and raises the standard of the Law from outward behavior to the condition of the heart — teaching that righteousness must exceed that of the scribes and Pharisees.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Matthew (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapters 5-7 contain the Sermon on the Mount — the most
famous and comprehensive teaching of Jesus. It was delivered on a hillside near
Capernaum, likely early in His Galilean ministry. Matthew presents it as the
first of five major discourses in his Gospel (echoing the five books of Moses),
establishing Jesus as the new Moses giving a new law from a new mountain.

The Sermon on the Mount is not a plan of salvation — it is a description of
kingdom character. It describes what citizens of God's kingdom look like from
the inside out. It is not a new law to earn God's favor but a portrait of the
life that flows from genuine transformation.

Chapter 5 contains three sections:
  (1) The Beatitudes (vv.3-12) — the character of kingdom citizens
  (2) Salt and Light (vv.13-16) — the influence of kingdom citizens
  (3) The Law Fulfilled (vv.17-48) — the righteousness of kingdom citizens

The Six Antitheses ("You have heard... but I say"): Jesus does not contradict
the Law — He deepens it, going from external behavior to internal motivation.
Murder begins with anger; adultery begins with lust. The kingdom requires heart
transformation, not just behavioral compliance.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- The Mount (near Capernaum): The Sermon on the Mount was delivered on a hillside near Capernaum in Galilee. Tradition identifies it with the hill above the northwest shore of the Sea of Galilee.
- Galilee: Jesus' primary ministry region in northern Israel. The sermon was given early in His Galilean ministry.
- Jerusalem/Temple references (vv.23-24, 35): Jesus references the altar and Jerusalem as "the city of the great King," showing the audience's connection to the worship center in Judea.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.3  — "Blessed are the poor in spirit, for theirs is the kingdom of heaven."
        (ESV) — The first beatitude — the foundation of all the others.
        Spiritual poverty (recognizing our need) is the entry point to the
        kingdom.

v.16 — "In the same way, let your light shine before others, so that they may
        see your good works and give glory to your Father who is in heaven."
        (ESV) — The purpose of visible Christian living: not self-promotion
        but God's glory.

v.20 — "For I tell you, unless your righteousness exceeds that of the scribes
        and Pharisees, you will never enter the kingdom of heaven." (ESV)
        — The standard is not external compliance but internal transformation.

v.44 — "But I say to you, Love your enemies and pray for those who persecute
        you." (ESV) — The most radical command in the Sermon.

v.48 — "You therefore must be perfect, as your heavenly Father is perfect."
        (ESV) — The ultimate standard — not perfectionism but wholeness,
        completeness, maturity in love.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.3  — KJV: "poor in spirit" / ESV: "poor in spirit" — Both identical. Greek
       "ptōchos tō pneumati" — utterly destitute in spirit, spiritually
       bankrupt. Not materially poor but those who recognize their complete
       spiritual poverty before God.

v.5  — KJV: "the meek" / ESV: "the meek" — Both identical. Greek "praus" —
       not weakness but controlled strength. The same word used for a trained
       warhorse — power under control. Moses was called the meekest man on
       earth (Numbers 12:3) — yet he confronted Pharaoh.

v.22 — KJV: "Raca" / ESV: "insults his brother" — The KJV retains the Aramaic
       "Raca," a term of contempt meaning "empty-headed" or "worthless." The
       ESV translates the concept rather than transliterating. A term of utter
       dismissal and dehumanization.

v.28 — KJV: "looketh on a woman to lust after her" / ESV: "looks at a woman
       with lustful intent" — Both equivalent in meaning. The Greek "pros to
       epithumēsai" — looking with the purpose of lusting, the deliberate
       cultivation of lust. Not an involuntary glance but an intentional gaze.

v.48 — KJV: "perfect" / ESV: "perfect" — Both identical. Greek "teleios" —
       complete, mature, whole. Not sinless perfection but wholeness of
       character, especially in love. The context (vv.43-47) is about loving
       completely — as God loves.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
v.3  — NET: "Blessed are the poor in spirit, for the kingdom
       of heaven belongs to them" — "poor in spirit" (hoi ptOchoi
       tO pneumati = the spiritually destitute). WEB: "Blessed are
       the poor in spirit." KJV: "Blessed are the poor in spirit."
       Not financially poor (Luke's version) but spiritually
       bankrupt — aware of their need for God.

v.17 — NET: "Do not think that I have come to abolish the law or
       the prophets. I have not come to abolish these things but
       to fulfill them" — "fulfill" (plErOsai = fill up/complete/
       bring to full meaning). WEB: "not to destroy, but to
       fulfill." Jesus completes what the Law pointed toward.

v.22 — NET: "everyone who is angry with a brother will be
       subjected to judgment" — some manuscripts add "without
       cause" (eikE). KJV includes "without cause"; NET/ESV omit
       it following earlier manuscripts. WEB: "everyone who is
       angry with his brother without a cause." A significant
       textual variant affecting the teaching.

v.44 — NET: "But I say to you, love your enemies and pray for
       those who persecute you" — "love" (agapate = active,
       willed love). WEB: "Love your enemies." KJV: "Love your
       enemies." Some manuscripts add "bless those who curse you,
       do good to those who hate you" (KJV includes; NET omits).

v.48 — NET: "So then, be perfect, as your heavenly Father is
       perfect" — "perfect" (teleioi = complete/mature/whole). WEB:
       "Therefore you shall be perfect." KJV: "Be ye therefore
       perfect." Not sinless perfection but wholeness of character
       — complete in love (the context: love for enemies).

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Beatitudes    — From Latin "beatus" — blessed, happy. The eight declarations
               of blessing that open the Sermon on the Mount, describing the
               character of kingdom citizens.

Blessed       — Greek "makarios" — deeply happy, fortunate, to be envied.
               Not a surface happiness but a deep well-being rooted in
               relationship with God.

Meek          — Controlled strength, power under authority. Not weakness or
               passivity but strength submitted to God's will.

Raca          — Aramaic term of contempt — "empty-headed," "worthless." Used
               to dismiss and dehumanize another person.

Jot           — The smallest letter in the Hebrew alphabet (yod) — like an
               apostrophe. Jesus says even this will not pass from the Law.

Tittle        — A small decorative stroke on a Hebrew letter that distinguishes
               one letter from another. Even the smallest detail of Scripture
               is permanent.

Mammon        — Aramaic word for wealth, money, material possessions. Used
               as a personification of wealth as a rival god.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. The Beatitudes describe a man who is spiritually poor, mourning over sin,
   meek, hungry for righteousness, merciful, pure in heart, a peacemaker, and
   willing to be persecuted. Which of these describes you most? Which describes
   you least? What does the gap reveal?

2. "Ye are the salt of the earth" (v.13). Salt preserves and flavors. Your
   presence in your workplace, your neighborhood, your family should make a
   difference — preserving what is good, adding flavor to life. Are you
   functioning as salt, or have you lost your saltiness by blending in?

3. Jesus says anger toward a brother is the heart of murder (v.22). Is there
   someone you are angry with — a slow burn of resentment, contempt, or
   bitterness? Jesus says deal with it before you bring your worship (v.23-24).
   Who do you need to be reconciled with?

4. "Whosoever looketh on a woman to lust after her hath committed adultery
   with her already in his heart" (v.28). Jesus goes to the heart. What
   disciplines do you have in place to guard your eyes and your mind? Are
   they working?

5. "Love your enemies" (v.44). Name one person who has wronged you, hurt you,
   or is hostile to you. What would it look like to bless them, do good to
   them, and pray for them this week?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. The Beatitudes begin with "poor in spirit" — recognizing our spiritual
   bankruptcy. This is the opposite of the self-made man mentality. How does
   the culture of masculinity you grew up in conflict with the posture of
   spiritual poverty? What does it cost a man to admit he has nothing before God?

2. Jesus says our righteousness must exceed that of the Pharisees (v.20) —
   men who were externally impeccable. The standard is internal. How do you
   evaluate your own spiritual condition — by external behavior or by the
   state of your heart?

3. "Be ye therefore perfect, even as your Father which is in heaven is perfect"
   (v.48). In context, this is about love — loving completely, without
   partiality. How complete is your love? Do you love only those who love you
   back, or do you love across lines of hostility and difference?

4. Jesus says to go a second mile (v.41) — Roman soldiers could compel Jews
   to carry their gear one mile. Jesus says go two. Where in your life is God
   calling you to go beyond what is required — in your marriage, your parenting,
   your work, your service?

5. The six antitheses ("You have heard... but I say") show that Jesus has
   authority over the Law — He doesn't just interpret it, He deepens it. What
   does it mean to you that Jesus speaks with that kind of authority? How does
   it change how you read His commands?
