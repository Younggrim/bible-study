Matthew - Chapter 18
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Jesus teaches on humility, the seriousness of causing others to stumble, the parable of the lost sheep, church discipline, and the parable of the unforgiving servant — commanding forgiveness "seventy times seven."

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Matthew (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 18 is Matthew's fourth major discourse — the
"Community Discourse" or "Church Discourse." It addresses how citizens of the
kingdom relate to one another: humility (vv.1-5), protecting the vulnerable
(vv.6-14), church discipline (vv.15-20), and radical forgiveness (vv.21-35).

The disciples had been arguing about who was greatest (v.1) — a question rooted
in pride and ambition. Jesus' answer overturns every human system of greatness:
the greatest in the kingdom is the one who humbles himself like a child.

The Parable of the Unforgiving Servant (vv.23-35): Ten thousand talents was an
astronomical sum — roughly 200,000 years of wages. It represents an unpayable
debt. One hundred pence (denarii) was about 100 days' wages — a real but
manageable debt. The contrast is the point: God has forgiven us an infinite debt;
how can we refuse to forgive the small debts others owe us?

Church Discipline (vv.15-17): Jesus gives the only explicit process for
addressing sin within the community of believers — private confrontation first,
then witnesses, then the church. The goal at every stage is restoration, not
punishment.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Capernaum (v.1): Jesus was still teaching in Capernaum, His home base in Galilee. This discourse on humility and forgiveness was given in the house.
- The setting is domestic and intimate — a private teaching to the disciples, not a public sermon.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.3  — "Truly, I say to you, unless you turn and become like children, you
        will never enter the kingdom of heaven." (ESV) — Childlike humility
        is the entry requirement for the kingdom.

v.6  — "But whoever causes one of these little ones who believe in me to sin,
        it would be better for him to have a great millstone fastened around
        his neck." (ESV) — The severity of leading others into sin.

v.15 — "If your brother sins against you, go and tell him his fault, between
        you and him alone." (ESV) — The first step of reconciliation: private,
        direct, and personal.

v.20 — "For where two or three are gathered in my name, there am I among
        them." (ESV) — Christ's presence in community.

v.22 — "I do not say to you seven times, but seventy-seven times." (ESV) —
        Unlimited forgiveness — not a number but a posture.

v.35 — "So also my heavenly Father will do to every one of you, if you do not
        forgive your brother from your heart." (ESV) — The forgiven must
        forgive.

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.3  — NET: "unless you turn around and become like little
       children, you will never enter the kingdom of heaven" —
       "turn around" (straphEte = convert/change direction). WEB:
       "unless you turn and become as little children." Not
       childish but child-LIKE: dependent, trusting, humble.

v.6  — KJV: "a millstone" / ESV: "a great millstone" — Greek "mulos onikos"
       — literally "a donkey millstone" — the large upper millstone turned
       by a donkey, not the small hand-mill. A massive stone. The ESV makes
       the size explicit. The image is of certain, violent death — and Jesus
       says that would be preferable to causing a child to stumble.

v.8  — NET: "If your hand or your foot causes you to sin, cut it
       off" — "causes you to sin" (skandalizei = ensnares/trips
       you up). WEB: "if your hand or your foot causes you to
       stumble." Radical self-surgery — amputation metaphor for
       eliminating sin's sources.

v.10 — NET: "their angels in heaven always see the face of my
       Father in heaven" — "their angels" — guardian angels with
       direct access to God. WEB: "their angels in heaven always
       see the face of my Father." KJV: "their angels do always
       behold the face of my Father." Children have heavenly
       representatives.

v.17 — KJV: "let him be unto thee as an heathen man and a publican" — ESV:
       "let him be to you as a Gentile and a tax collector" — This does not
       mean hatred but a recognition that the person is living outside the
       community of faith. Notably, Jesus loved Gentiles and tax collectors
       — the goal is still redemption.

v.20 — NET: "where two or three are assembled in my name, I am
       there among them" — "assembled in my name" (synagmenoi eis
       to emon onoma). WEB: "where two or three are gathered
       together in my name." Christ's presence in the smallest
       gathering — not dependent on numbers.

v.22 — KJV: "seventy times seven" / ESV: "seventy-seven times" — Could be
       490 (KJV) or 77 (ESV) — the Greek is ambiguous. Either way, the point
       is not a number but a posture — unlimited, unconditional forgiveness.
       Peter thought 7 was generous; Jesus multiplied it beyond counting.
       NET: "Not seven times, I tell you, but seventy-seven
       times!" — "seventy-seven" (hebdomEkontakis hepta) — or
       "seventy times seven" (490). KJV: "seventy times seven."
       WEB: "seventy times seven." Either way: unlimited forgiveness,
       not mathematical calculation.

v.24 — KJV: "ten thousand talents" / ESV: "ten thousand talents" — Both
       identical. One talent = approximately 6,000 denarii (20 years' wages).
       Ten thousand talents = 200,000 years' wages. An absurdly unpayable
       sum — representing the infinite debt of sin before God.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Converted     — Greek "straphēte" — to turn, to change direction. Not just
               intellectual belief but a fundamental reorientation of life.

Millstone     — A large circular stone used for grinding grain. The "donkey
               millstone" was the largest type — too heavy for a person to
               lift alone.

Offend        — Greek "skandalizō" — to cause to stumble, to trip up, to
               lead into sin. The source of our word "scandalize."

Church discipline — The process Jesus outlines for addressing sin within the
               believing community: private confrontation → witnesses → church
               → separation. The goal is always restoration.

Talent        — A unit of weight/money. One talent of silver = approximately
               6,000 denarii = 20 years' wages for a laborer.

Pence/Denarii — A Roman silver coin worth one day's wage for a laborer.
               100 denarii = about 3-4 months' wages.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. The disciples asked "Who is the greatest?" (v.1). Every man has this
   question somewhere in his heart — the desire to be recognized, to be
   important, to be above others. Jesus says the greatest is the one who
   humbles himself. Where is your ambition for greatness competing with
   your call to humility?

2. Jesus warns severely against causing "little ones" to stumble (v.6). As
   a man — as a father, a leader, a mentor — you have influence over people
   who look up to you. Are you using that influence to lead them toward Christ
   or away from Him? What would it look like to take that responsibility more
   seriously?

3. "Go and tell him his fault between thee and him alone" (v.15). Most men
   either avoid conflict entirely or handle it publicly/aggressively. Jesus'
   way is direct, private, and aimed at restoration. Is there a conflict in
   your life you need to address directly rather than avoiding or gossiping
   about?

4. The unforgiving servant was forgiven an unpayable debt and then refused to
   forgive a small one (vv.28-30). Is there someone you are refusing to forgive
   — while you yourself have been forgiven infinitely more by God? What is
   holding you back from releasing that debt?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. Jesus said "become as little children" (v.3). What specific qualities of
   children is He pointing to — trust, humility, dependence, lack of pretense?
   Which of these is hardest for you as a man? Why?

2. The parable of the lost sheep (vv.12-14) shows God's heart for the one who
   wanders. He leaves the 99 to find the 1. How does this shape how you view
   people who have wandered from faith — with judgment or with pursuit?

3. Church discipline (vv.15-17) is rarely practiced well. Most churches either
   ignore sin or handle it harshly. What would it look like for your community
   of faith to practice this process with both truth and love?

4. "Seventy times seven" (v.22) — unlimited forgiveness. Is there a limit to
   your forgiveness? Is there someone who has sinned against you so many times
   that you've mentally cut them off? What does Jesus' standard say to that?

5. The unforgiving servant's problem was not that he didn't know he was forgiven
   — it was that he didn't let that forgiveness change how he treated others.
   How is the forgiveness you've received from God actively shaping how you
   treat the people who wrong you?
