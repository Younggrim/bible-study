Matthew - Chapter 2
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Wise men from the East worship the newborn King, Herod massacres Bethlehem's infants in jealous rage, and Joseph flees with his family to Egypt — fulfilling prophecy at every turn.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Matthew (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 2 records the visit of the Magi, the flight to
Egypt, the massacre of the innocents, and the return to Nazareth. Four Old
Testament prophecies are fulfilled in this chapter — more than any other single
chapter in Matthew.

Herod the Great: One of history's most brutal rulers. He was an Idumean (Edomite)
appointed king of Judea by Rome in 37 BC. He was known for his massive building
projects (including the Temple expansion) and his paranoid cruelty — he executed
his own wife and sons when he suspected them of plotting against him. The massacre
of the innocents (v.16) is entirely consistent with his character.

The Magi: Not kings, not three in number (the Bible doesn't specify). They were
likely Zoroastrian astrologer-priests from Persia or Babylon, part of a priestly
caste that studied the stars. They may have known of the Jewish prophecies through
the influence of Daniel (who was chief of the Babylonian wise men, Daniel 2:48).
Their visit likely occurred when Jesus was up to 2 years old (v.16) — not at
the manger.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Bethlehem: Located 5 miles south of Jerusalem in Judea. Birthplace of Jesus and King David. Fulfills Micah 5:2.
- Jerusalem: Herod's capital, where the Magi first inquired about the newborn King. About 5 miles north of Bethlehem.
- Egypt: The holy family fled south into Egypt to escape Herod's massacre, fulfilling Hosea 11:1 ("Out of Egypt I called my son").
- Nazareth: A small village in Galilee (northern Israel), where the family settled upon return, fulfilling "He shall be called a Nazarene."

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "Where is he who has been born king of the Jews? For we saw his star
        when it rose and have come to worship him." (ESV) — Gentiles from afar
        seeking to worship the Jewish Messiah — a preview of the Gospel's
        global reach.

v.11 — "They fell down and worshiped him. Then, opening their treasures, they
        offered him gifts, gold and frankincense and myrrh." (ESV) — The
        appropriate response to encountering Christ: worship and giving.

v.13 — "Rise, take the child and his mother, and flee to Egypt." (ESV)
        — Joseph's immediate obedience to God's warning — no hesitation.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1  — KJV: "wise men from the east" — ESV/NET: "wise men from the east" —
       Greek "magoi" — Magi, a priestly caste of scholars and astrologers.
       The tradition of "three kings" comes from the three gifts, not from
       Scripture. The number of Magi is not stated.

v.11 — KJV: "young child" / ESV: "the child" — Greek "paidion" — a young
       child, not an infant (Greek "brephos"). Both equivalent in meaning.
       This, combined with Herod killing children up to 2 years old (v.16),
       suggests the Magi arrived months or up to 2 years after the birth —
       not at the manger.

v.15 — KJV: "Out of Egypt have I called my son" / ESV: "Out of Egypt I called
       my son" — Both identical in meaning. Matthew applies Hosea 11:1
       (originally about Israel) to Jesus. This is typological fulfillment —
       Jesus recapitulates Israel's history, succeeding where Israel failed.

v.23 — KJV: "He shall be called a Nazarene" / ESV: "he would be called a
       Nazarene" — Both equivalent. This is not a direct quote from any single
       OT text. Matthew says "spoken by the prophets" (plural). Most scholars
       connect it to Isaiah 11:1 ("netzer" = branch/shoot) or to the general
       OT theme of the Messiah being despised (Nazareth was looked down upon —
       John 1:46).

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
v.1  — NET: "After Jesus was born in Bethlehem in Judea, in
       the time of King Herod, wise men from the East came to
       Jerusalem" — "wise men" (magoi = Magi/astrologers). WEB:
       "wise men from the east." KJV: "wise men from the east."
       Not kings, not three (the number is unspecified) — they
       were Persian/Babylonian astrologer-priests.

v.2  — NET: "Where is the one who is born king of the Jews?" —
       "born king" — not made king but BORN king. WEB: "Where is
       he who is born King of the Jews?" KJV: "Where is he that is
       born King of the Jews?" The question threatens Herod who was
       MADE king by Rome, not born to it.

v.6  — NET: "a ruler who will shepherd my people Israel" —
       "shepherd" (poimanei = tend/feed/rule). WEB: "who will
       govern my people, Israel." KJV: "that shall rule my people
       Israel." Quoting Micah 5:2 — the Messiah is a shepherd-
       king, not merely a political ruler.

v.11 — NET: "they offered him gifts: gold, frankincense, and
       myrrh" — "offered" (prosEnenkan = presented formally). WEB:
       "they offered him gifts." Traditional interpretation: gold
       (kingship), frankincense (deity), myrrh (death/burial).

v.16 — NET: "he sent men to kill all the children in Bethlehem
       and throughout the surrounding region who were two years old
       and under" — "two years old and under" (apo dietous kai
       katOterO) — Herod calculated from when the star first
       appeared. WEB: "from two years old and under." The massacre
       of innocents — not recorded outside Matthew but consistent
       with Herod's documented paranoia.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Magi          — Greek "magoi" — a priestly caste of scholars, astrologers,
               and interpreters of dreams from Persia/Babylon. Not kings;
               number not specified in Scripture.

Frankincense  — A fragrant resin burned as incense in worship. Associated
               with the priestly office and prayer (Revelation 8:3-4).

Myrrh         — A fragrant resin used in embalming and burial preparations.
               Foreshadowing Jesus' death.

Herod         — Herod the Great (73-4 BC), Roman-appointed king of Judea.
               Known for massive building projects and extreme cruelty.

Innocents     — The male children 2 years old and under killed by Herod in
               Bethlehem. The "Massacre of the Innocents" — an act of
               political paranoia.

Archelaus     — Son of Herod the Great who ruled Judea after his father's
               death. Known for cruelty — eventually removed by Rome in AD 6.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. The Magi traveled hundreds of miles, following a star, to worship a child
   they had never met. They gave their best gifts. What does your worship cost
   you? Is it convenient worship or costly worship?

2. The three gifts — gold (king), frankincense (priest), myrrh (death) — told
   the whole story of Christ. What gifts has God given you that, when offered
   to Him, tell His story through your life?

3. Joseph received a warning in a dream and "arose... by night" (v.14) —
   immediate obedience, no delay. When God warns you about something — through
   Scripture, through the Spirit, through wise counsel — how quickly do you act?

4. Herod was threatened by a baby. Power always feels threatened by Christ.
   Where in your life does the lordship of Christ threaten something you're
   holding onto — control, comfort, reputation, a relationship?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. The chief priests and scribes knew exactly where the Messiah would be born
   (v.5-6) — they quoted the prophecy accurately — but they didn't go to
   Bethlehem. Knowledge without response is worse than ignorance. Where do
   you have accurate knowledge of what God wants that you're not acting on?

2. The Magi "fell down and worshipped him" (v.11) — before He had done any
   miracles, before He had preached any sermons. They worshipped based on who
   He was, not what He had done for them. Is your worship of Christ based on
   His identity or on what He does for you?

3. God warned the Magi in a dream not to return to Herod (v.12). They obeyed
   a dream from God over the command of a king. When God's direction conflicts
   with human authority, how do you navigate that? What principles guide you?

4. The massacre of the innocents (v.16) — innocent children died because of
   Herod's fear of Jesus. The coming of Christ into the world brought both
   salvation and suffering. How do you hold together the reality that following
   Christ can bring both blessing and cost?

5. Jesus spent His early years as a refugee in Egypt — a foreign land, fleeing
   political violence. How does knowing that Jesus experienced displacement,
   danger, and being an outsider affect how you view people in similar
   circumstances today?
