Matthew - Chapter 7
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Jesus concludes the Sermon on the Mount with warnings against judging hypocritically, instructions on prayer, the narrow gate, false prophets, and the parable of the wise and foolish builders.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Matthew (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 7 concludes the Sermon on the Mount with some of
Jesus' most famous and most misunderstood teachings — on judging, prayer, the
narrow gate, false prophets, and the two foundations. The sermon ends with the
crowd "astonished at his doctrine" because He taught "as one having authority"
— not quoting other rabbis but speaking with His own divine authority.

The chapter moves from relationships (vv.1-6), to prayer (vv.7-11), to the
Golden Rule (v.12), to warnings about the two paths (vv.13-14), false prophets
(vv.15-23), and the two foundations (vv.24-27). The overall message: hearing
Jesus' words is not enough — doing them is what matters.

"I Never Knew You" (vv.21-23): One of the most sobering passages in Scripture.
People who prophesied, cast out demons, and did miracles in Jesus' name are
told "depart from me." The issue was not their activity but their relationship
— Jesus says "I never knew you." Religious activity without genuine relationship
is worthless.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- The Mount (near Capernaum): The concluding portion of the Sermon on the Mount, still on the hillside in Galilee near Capernaum.
- The "rock" and "sand" imagery (vv.24-27) reflects the landscape around the Sea of Galilee — sudden rains cause flash floods in the wadis (dry riverbeds) that destroy poorly-founded structures.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "Judge not, that you be not judged." (ESV) — The most quoted and most
        misunderstood verse in the Sermon. Not a prohibition of all moral
        evaluation but of hypocritical, self-righteous condemnation.

v.7  — "Ask, and it will be given to you; seek, and you will find; knock, and
        it will be opened to you." (ESV) — The progressive intensity of prayer.

v.12 — "So whatever you wish that others would do to you, do also to them, for
        this is the Law and the Prophets." (ESV) — The Golden Rule — the
        summary of all ethical teaching.

v.14 — "For the gate is narrow and the way is hard that leads to life, and
        those who find it are few." (ESV) — The exclusivity of the Gospel.

v.23 — "And then will I declare to them, 'I never knew you; depart from me,
        you workers of lawlessness.'" (ESV) — The most terrifying words in
        Scripture.

v.24 — "Everyone then who hears these words of mine and does them will be like
        a wise man who built his house on the rock." (ESV) — Obedience is the
        foundation that survives the storm.

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1  — KJV: "Judge not" / ESV: "Judge not" — Both identical. Greek "mē
       krinete" — stop judging (present imperative with negative). The context
       (vv.2-5) makes clear: Jesus forbids hypocritical, self-righteous
       judgment — not all moral discernment. Verse 5 says "THEN shalt thou
       see clearly to cast out the mote" — after dealing with your own sin,
       you CAN help others.
       NET: "Do not judge so that you will not be judged" —
       "judge" (krinete = pronounce condemning verdict). WEB:
       "Don't judge, so that you won't be judged." KJV: "Judge not,
       that ye be not judged." Not forbidding discernment (v.6:
       "don't give what is holy to dogs") but condemning
       hypocritical, self-righteous judgment.

v.6  — KJV: "Give not that which is holy unto the dogs" / ESV: "Do not give
       dogs what is holy" — Both equivalent. "Dogs" and "swine" were Jewish
       terms for those who were hostile to sacred things. Jesus is saying: use
       discernment about who is ready to receive truth. Not everyone is open —
       some will trample it and attack you.
       NET: "Do not give what is holy to dogs or throw your
       pearls before pigs" — "dogs... pigs" — animals considered
       unclean. WEB: "Don't give that which is holy to the dogs."
       Discernment IS required — some people will trample truth
       and turn to attack you.

v.11 — NET: "how much more will your Father in heaven give good
       gifts to those who ask him!" — "good gifts" (agatha = good
       things). Luke 11:13 has "Holy Spirit" instead of "good
       gifts." WEB: "give good things to those who ask him." The
       parallel shows the Holy Spirit IS the supreme good gift.

v.13 — KJV: "strait gate" — ESV: "narrow gate" — "Strait" means narrow or
       constricted (not "straight"). The entrance to life is narrow — it
       requires specific faith in Christ, not a broad tolerance of all paths.
       NET: "Enter through the narrow gate, because the gate is
       wide and the way is spacious that leads to destruction" —
       "spacious" (eurychOros = broad/roomy). WEB: "wide is the
       gate and broad is the way." KJV: "wide is the gate, and
       broad is the way." The easy path accommodates many; the
       narrow requires effort and finds few.

v.23 — KJV: "I never knew you" / ESV: "I never knew you" — Both identical.
       Greek "ouk egnōn" — "I never had intimate, relational knowledge of
       you." The same word for deep personal knowing. These people had
       activity without intimacy.
       NET: "I never knew you. Go away from me, you lawbreakers!"
       — "knew" (egnOn = knew intimately/acknowledged). WEB:
       "I never knew you. Depart from me." KJV: "I never knew you:
       depart from me." Not ignorance but non-recognition —
       miracles without relationship count for nothing.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Mote          — KJV for a speck, splinter, or small particle. A minor fault
               in someone else's life — contrasted with the "beam" (log) in
               your own eye.

Beam          — A large piece of timber — a log. Represents a major fault or
               sin in your own life that you're blind to while criticizing
               others for smaller issues.

Strait        — Narrow, constricted, difficult to pass through. Not "straight"
               (direct) but "strait" (tight). The gate to life is narrow.

False prophets — Teachers who claim to speak for God but teach error. They
               look like sheep (appear harmless/godly) but are inwardly
               wolves (destructive).

Iniquity      — Greek "anomia" — lawlessness, living without regard for God's
               law. The workers of iniquity in v.23 did religious works but
               lived lawlessly.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Judge not" (v.1) is not a command to never evaluate anything — it's a
   command to deal with your own sin first (v.5). Are you more aware of
   other people's faults than your own? What "beam" in your own eye needs
   attention before you address the "mote" in someone else's?

2. Ask, seek, knock (v.7) — there's a progression of intensity. How persistent
   is your prayer life? Do you ask once and give up, or do you seek and knock
   with increasing urgency?

3. The Golden Rule (v.12) — "do unto others as you would have them do unto
   you." This is not passive (don't harm others) but active (do good to
   others). What specific good would you want done to you that you're not
   doing for someone else?

4. "Few there be that find it" (v.14). The narrow way is not popular. Following
   Christ authentically will put you in the minority. Are you OK with that?
   Where are you tempted to take the broad, easy path because it's what
   everyone else is doing?

5. "I never knew you" (v.23). Religious activity without genuine relationship
   with Christ is worthless. How is your actual relationship with Jesus — not
   your church attendance, not your Bible knowledge, not your ministry — but
   your personal, daily, honest relationship with Him?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. Jesus says to deal with your own sin before addressing others' (v.5). In
   your closest relationships — marriage, friendship, mentoring — do you lead
   with self-examination or with criticism of the other person? What would
   change if you consistently dealt with yourself first?

2. The wise man and the foolish man both heard the same words (vv.24-26). The
   difference was obedience. What teaching of Jesus have you heard repeatedly
   but not yet acted on? What is the cost of continued inaction?

3. False prophets are known by their fruit, not their words (vv.15-20). How
   do you evaluate teachers and leaders in your life? What fruit do you look
   for? What red flags should you watch for?

4. The people in vv.21-23 did impressive things — prophecy, exorcism, miracles
   — but Jesus said "I never knew you." What does this tell you about the
   danger of substituting religious performance for genuine relationship?
   How do you cultivate knowing Christ, not just working for Him?

5. The storm came to both houses (vv.25,27). Trials are not optional — they
   come to everyone. The question is not whether the storm will come but
   whether your foundation will hold. What is your life built on? How do
   you know?
