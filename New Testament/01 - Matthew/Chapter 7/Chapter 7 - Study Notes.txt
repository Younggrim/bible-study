Matthew - Chapter 7
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Jesus concludes the Sermon on the Mount with warnings against judging hypocritically, instructions on prayer, the narrow gate, false prophets, and the parable of the wise and foolish builders.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Matthew (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 7 concludes the Sermon on the Mount with some of
Jesus' most famous and most misunderstood teachings — on judging, prayer, the
narrow gate, false prophets, and the two foundations. The sermon ends with the
crowd "astonished at his doctrine" because He taught "as one having authority"
— not quoting other rabbis but speaking with His own divine authority.

The chapter moves from relationships (vv.1-6), to prayer (vv.7-11), to the
Golden Rule (v.12), to warnings about the two paths (vv.13-14), false prophets
(vv.15-23), and the two foundations (vv.24-27). The overall message: hearing
Jesus' words is not enough — doing them is what matters.

"I Never Knew You" (vv.21-23): One of the most sobering passages in Scripture.
People who prophesied, cast out demons, and did miracles in Jesus' name are
told "depart from me." The issue was not their activity but their relationship
— Jesus says "I never knew you." Religious activity without genuine relationship
is worthless.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- The Mount (near Capernaum): The concluding portion of the Sermon on the Mount, still on the hillside in Galilee near Capernaum.
- The "rock" and "sand" imagery (vv.24-27) reflects the landscape around the Sea of Galilee — sudden rains cause flash floods in the wadis (dry riverbeds) that destroy poorly-founded structures.

REFLECTION
------------------------------------
1. "Judge not" (v.1) is not a command to never evaluate anything — it's a
   command to deal with your own sin first (v.5). Are you more aware of
   other people's faults than your own? What "beam" in your own eye needs
   attention before you address the "mote" in someone else's?

2. Ask, seek, knock (v.7) — there's a progression of intensity. How persistent
   is your prayer life? Do you ask once and give up, or do you seek and knock
   with increasing urgency?

3. The Golden Rule (v.12) — "do unto others as you would have them do unto
   you." This is not passive (don't harm others) but active (do good to
   others). What specific good would you want done to you that you're not
   doing for someone else?

4. "Few there be that find it" (v.14). The narrow way is not popular. Following
   Christ authentically will put you in the minority. Are you OK with that?
   Where are you tempted to take the broad, easy path because it's what
   everyone else is doing?

5. "I never knew you" (v.23). Religious activity without genuine relationship
   with Christ is worthless. How is your actual relationship with Jesus — not
   your church attendance, not your Bible knowledge, not your ministry — but
   your personal, daily, honest relationship with Him?

1. Jesus says to deal with your own sin before addressing others' (v.5). In
   your closest relationships — marriage, friendship, mentoring — do you lead
   with self-examination or with criticism of the other person? What would
   change if you consistently dealt with yourself first?

2. The wise man and the foolish man both heard the same words (vv.24-26). The
   difference was obedience. What teaching of Jesus have you heard repeatedly
   but not yet acted on? What is the cost of continued inaction?

3. False prophets are known by their fruit, not their words (vv.15-20). How
   do you evaluate teachers and leaders in your life? What fruit do you look
   for? What red flags should you watch for?

4. The people in vv.21-23 did impressive things — prophecy, exorcism, miracles
   — but Jesus said "I never knew you." What does this tell you about the
   danger of substituting religious performance for genuine relationship?
   How do you cultivate knowing Christ, not just working for Him?

5. The storm came to both houses (vv.25,27). Trials are not optional — they
   come to everyone. The question is not whether the storm will come but
   whether your foundation will hold. What is your life built on? How do
   you know?
