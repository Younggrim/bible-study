Matthew - Chapter 14
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Herod executes John the Baptist, Jesus feeds five thousand with five loaves and two fish, and then walks on the sea to His terrified disciples — with Peter briefly joining Him on the water before sinking in doubt.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Matthew (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 14 records three major events: the death of John
the Baptist (vv.1-12), the feeding of the 5,000 (vv.13-21), and Jesus walking
on water (vv.22-33). The chapter opens with grief (John's execution) and moves
through compassion (feeding the hungry) to revelation (Jesus' deity displayed
on the water).

Herod Antipas: Son of Herod the Great. He ruled Galilee and Perea as tetrarch
(ruler of a quarter). He divorced his wife to marry Herodias, his brother
Philip's wife. John the Baptist publicly condemned this as unlawful (Leviticus
18:16, 20:21). Herod imprisoned John but feared killing him because of his
popularity. A rash oath at a birthday party sealed John's fate.

Peter Walking on Water (vv.28-31): Only recorded in Matthew. Peter's request
to walk on water was bold faith — he got out of the boat. His failure came when
he shifted his focus from Jesus to the wind. Jesus' response was not anger but
rescue — "immediately Jesus stretched forth his hand, and caught him."

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Machaerus: The fortress east of the Dead Sea (in Perea) where Herod imprisoned and beheaded John the Baptist.
- A desert place (v.13): A remote area on the northeast shore of the Sea of Galilee near Bethsaida, where the feeding of the 5,000 occurred.
- Sea of Galilee: Jesus walked on the water crossing from the eastern shore back toward Capernaum/Gennesaret on the western shore (v.34).

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.14 — "And Jesus went out, and saw a great multitude, and he had compassion
        on them and healed their sick." (ESV) — Compassion is the motivation
        behind Jesus' miracles.

v.16 — "They need not go away; you give them something to eat." (ESV) — Jesus
        involves His disciples in the miracle — they distribute what He
        multiplies.

v.27 — "Take heart; it is I. Do not be afraid." (ESV) — Three commands in
        the storm: courage, recognition, fearlessness.

v.29 — "Come." (ESV) — One word from Jesus was enough for Peter to step onto
        the water.

v.31 — "O you of little faith, why did you doubt?" (ESV) — Little faith is
        still faith — Jesus rescued Peter, not rebuked him into the water.

Charles Spurgeon:
  Sermon: "Peter's Walk on the Water" on vv.28-31 (MTP Vol. 17, No. 1005)
  "Peter walked on the water — and then he sank. But notice: he sank while
  looking at the wind. He walked while looking at Jesus. That is the whole
  Christian life in one picture. Eyes on Christ — you walk. Eyes on
  circumstances — you sink. And when you sink, He catches you."
  Sermon: "Five Loaves and Two Fishes" on vv.15-21 (MTP Vol. 37, No. 2208)
  "Bring them hither to Me. That is all. Bring what you have — however
  small, however insufficient — bring it to Jesus. He does not ask you to
  have enough. He asks you to bring what you have."

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.14 — NET: "he had compassion on them and healed their sick"
       — "compassion" (esplanchnisthE = visceral, gut-level
       emotion). WEB: "he had compassion on them." KJV: "was moved
       with compassion toward them." Jesus' motivation for miracles
       is never display but compassion.

v.19 — NET: "he gave thanks, and breaking the loaves he gave them
       to the disciples" — "gave thanks" (eulogEsen = blessed).
       WEB: "looking up to heaven, he blessed." KJV: "blessed."
       Jewish blessing formula: blessing God for providing the food,
       not blessing the food itself.

v.25 — KJV: "the fourth watch of the night" / ESV: "in the fourth watch of
       the night" — Both identical in meaning. Between 3:00 and 6:00 AM. The
       Romans divided the night into four watches. The disciples had been
       struggling against the wind for hours — Jesus came at the darkest hour.
       NET: "he came to them walking on the sea" — "on the sea"
       (epi tEn thalassan = upon the sea). WEB: "walking on the
       sea." KJV: "walking on the sea." In the OT, walking on
       water is God's exclusive prerogative (Job 9:8; Psalm 77:19).
       Jesus does what only God does.

v.27 — KJV: "it is I" / ESV: "it is I" — Both identical. Greek "ego eimi" —
       "I AM." The same divine name from Exodus 3:14 and John 8:58. Jesus is
       not just identifying Himself — He is declaring His deity. Walking on
       water + "I AM" = theophany.
       NET: "Have courage! It is I. Do not be afraid" — "It is
       I" (egO eimi = I AM). WEB: "Cheer up! It is I! Don't be
       afraid." The divine name spoken on the water — theophany
       in action. "I AM" calms the storm and the fear.

v.30 — KJV: "when he saw the wind boisterous" — ESV: "when he saw the wind"
       — You can't literally see wind — you see its effects. Peter shifted
       his attention from Jesus to the evidence of danger around him.

v.31 — NET: "You of little faith, why did you doubt?" — "little
       faith" (oligopiste) and "doubt" (edistasas = hesitated/
       wavered). WEB: "You of little faith, why did you doubt?"
       Peter's faith was genuine (he walked!) but insufficient
       (he sank when he looked at waves instead of Jesus).

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Tetrarch      — Ruler of a quarter of a territory. Herod Antipas ruled Galilee
               and Perea under Roman authority. Less than a king.

Charger       — KJV for a large platter or serving dish. John's head was
               brought on a platter — a gruesome detail.

Fourth watch  — 3:00-6:00 AM. The darkest, coldest part of the night. Jesus
               came to the disciples at their most exhausted and afraid.

Gennesaret    — A fertile plain on the northwest shore of the Sea of Galilee,
               near Capernaum.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Herod killed John because of a rash oath and peer pressure (v.9). He was
   "sorry" but did it anyway. How often do you make decisions you regret
   because of what others will think? Where is peer pressure overriding your
   conscience?

2. Jesus said "Give ye them to eat" (v.16). He involves us in His work — even
   when we feel inadequate. What has God asked you to do that feels impossible?
   What do you have in your hands right now that He might want to multiply?

3. Peter walked on water when he looked at Jesus and sank when he looked at
   the wind (vv.29-30). What are you looking at right now — Jesus or your
   circumstances? What would it take to fix your eyes back on Him?

4. When Peter sank, he cried "Lord, save me" — and Jesus immediately caught
   him (v.31). The shortest, most desperate prayer in Scripture — and it was
   answered instantly. When you're sinking, do you cry out to Jesus or try
   to save yourself?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. After hearing of John's death, Jesus withdrew to a solitary place (v.13).
   Even Jesus needed time to grieve. How do you process grief and loss? Do
   you give yourself permission to grieve, or do you push through without
   stopping?

2. The disciples said "Send the multitude away" (v.15) — Jesus said "Give ye
   them to eat" (v.16). The disciples saw a problem to dismiss; Jesus saw
   people to feed. When you see need around you, is your instinct to avoid
   it or to engage it?

3. Peter got out of the boat — the other eleven didn't. He failed, but he
   also walked on water. Would you rather stay safe in the boat or risk
   failure by stepping out in faith? What boat is God calling you out of?

4. "O thou of little faith, wherefore didst thou doubt?" (v.31). Jesus didn't
   say "no faith" — He said "little faith." Even little faith gets rescued.
   How does knowing that Jesus works with imperfect, small faith encourage
   you to step out?

5. After the storm, the disciples worshipped and said "Of a truth thou art
   the Son of God" (v.33). Storms often produce deeper worship and clearer
   understanding of who Jesus is. What has a recent storm in your life
   revealed to you about Christ?
