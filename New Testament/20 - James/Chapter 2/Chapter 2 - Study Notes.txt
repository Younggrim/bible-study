James - Chapter 2
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
James condemns showing favoritism to the rich over the poor, declares that faith without works is dead — "as the body without the spirit is dead, so faith without works is dead also" — using Abraham and Rahab as examples of active faith.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: James, the brother of Jesus (see Chapter 1 notes for full details).

Historical Context: Chapter 2 addresses two related issues: favoritism in the
church (vv.1-13) and the relationship between faith and works (vv.14-26). The
early church included both rich and poor, and the temptation to favor the
wealthy was real. James condemns this as incompatible with faith in Christ.
He then tackles the most debated question in James: Can faith without works
save? His answer: No. Genuine faith always produces works. Faith and works are
not opposed — they are inseparable.

Favoritism Condemned (vv.1-13): Don't show partiality based on wealth (vv.1-4).
God chose the poor to be rich in faith (v.5). The rich often oppress believers
(vv.6-7). The "royal law" — love your neighbor — condemns favoritism (v.8).
Partiality is sin (v.9). Breaking one point of the Law makes you a lawbreaker
(vv.10-11). Live as those judged by "the law of liberty" (v.12). "Mercy
rejoiceth against judgment" (v.13).

Faith and Works (vv.14-26): Can faith without works save? (v.14). Faith that
doesn't help the needy is useless (vv.15-16). "Faith, if it hath not works,
is dead" (v.17). Show me your faith BY your works (v.18). Even demons believe
— and tremble (v.19). Abraham was "justified by works" when he offered Isaac
(vv.21-23). Rahab was justified by works (v.25). "Faith without works is dead"
(v.26).

Note: James does not contradict Paul. Paul speaks of justification BEFORE GOD
(declared righteous by faith alone, Rom 3:28). James speaks of justification
BEFORE MEN (demonstrated righteous by works, v.18). Paul addresses how we are
saved; James addresses how we know we are saved.

Charles Spurgeon:
 Sermon: "Faith and Works" on vv.17-18 (MTP Vol. 18, No. 1078)
 "Faith without works is dead — not weak, not sick, not sleeping — DEAD.
 A dead faith is no faith at all. It is a corpse of faith — it has the form
 but not the life. How do you know faith is alive? By its works. As a tree
 is known by its fruit, faith is known by its works. James does not say
 works save you — he says works PROVE you are saved. Show me your faith BY
 your works. Faith that doesn't work doesn't exist."
 Sermon: "Mercy Rejoiceth Against Judgment" on v.13 (MTP Vol. 11, No. 624)
 "Mercy rejoiceth — it triumphs, it exults, it overcomes. Against judgment —
 when mercy and judgment meet, mercy wins. Not that judgment is unjust but
 that mercy is greater. The merciful man will receive mercy. The unmerciful
 man will receive strict judgment. Show mercy — and mercy will triumph over
 the judgment you deserve."

REFLECTION
------------------------------------
1. "Have not the faith... with respect of persons" (v.1). Do you treat people
 differently based on their wealth, appearance, or status? How would Jesus
 treat the poorly dressed person who walks into your church?

2. "Mercy rejoiceth against judgment" (v.13). Are you merciful toward others?
 Or do you demand strict justice for everyone except yourself? The measure
 of mercy you give is the measure you'll receive.

3. "Faith, if it hath not works, is dead" (v.17). Examine your faith: what
 visible evidence does it produce? If someone looked at your life, would
 they see living faith or dead religion?

4. "The devils also believe, and tremble" (v.19). Is your faith merely
 intellectual agreement with facts about God? Or does it produce obedience,
 love, and transformation? What's the difference between your faith and a
 demon's?

5. "Shew me thy faith by thy works" (v.18). If someone asked you to prove
 your faith, what would you point to? What works demonstrate that your
 faith is alive?
