Revelation - Chapter 9
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
The fifth trumpet opens the bottomless pit releasing tormenting locusts, and the sixth trumpet unleashes four angels who kill a third of mankind — yet those who survive still refuse to repent of their idolatry and murders.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: The Apostle John (see Chapter 1 notes for full details).

Historical Context: Chapter 9 contains the fifth and sixth trumpet judgments
— the first two "woes" announced in 8:13. These are the most terrifying
judgments yet: demonic locusts from the abyss (5th trumpet) and a demonic
army of 200 million that kills a third of humanity (6th trumpet). Despite
this unprecedented devastation, the chapter ends with humanity's stubborn
refusal to repent (vv.20-21).

Fifth Trumpet / First Woe (vv.1-12): A star (fallen angel) opens the
bottomless pit (v.1). Smoke darkens the sky (v.2). Demonic locusts emerge —
not to kill but to torment for five months (vv.3-5). Men seek death but
cannot find it (v.6). The locusts are described in terrifying detail: horse-
like, crowned, human-faced, lion-toothed, armored, with scorpion tails
(vv.7-10). Their king: Abaddon/Apollyon — "Destroyer" (v.11).

Sixth Trumpet / Second Woe (vv.13-21): A voice from the golden altar commands
the release of four angels bound at the Euphrates (vv.13-14). They were
prepared for this exact moment — to kill a third of mankind (v.15). An army
of 200 million with lion-headed horses breathing fire, smoke, and brimstone
(vv.16-19). A third of humanity killed (v.18).

Humanity's Response (vv.20-21): Despite all this, "the rest of the men...
repented not" (v.20). They continued worshipping demons and idols, and
refused to repent of murders, sorceries, fornication, and thefts (vv.20-21).

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1 — "I saw a star fallen from heaven to earth, and he was given the key to
 the shaft of the bottomless pit." (ESV) — A fallen angel opens the
 abyss.

v.6 — "In those days people will seek death and will not find it. They will
 long to die, but death will flee from them." (ESV) — Torment so severe
 death is preferred but unavailable.

v.11 — "They have as king over them the angel of the bottomless pit. His
 name in Hebrew is Abaddon, and in Greek he is called Apollyon." (ESV)
 — The Destroyer.

v.15 — "So the four angels, who had been prepared for the hour, the day, the
 month, and the year, were released to kill a third of mankind." (ESV)
 — Precise divine timing.

v.20 — "The rest of mankind, who were not killed by these plagues, did not
 repent." (ESV) — Stubborn unrepentance despite devastating judgment.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
 https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/revelation-9/
 Guzik covers the abyss, the demonic locusts, Abaddon/Apollyon, the

Charles Spurgeon:
 Sermon: "Impenitence" on vv.20-21 (MTP Vol. 20, No. 1192)
 "They repented NOT. After all this — after a third of humanity killed,
 after demonic torment, after cosmic devastation — they still refused to
 repent. This is the hardness of the human heart. Judgment alone does not
 produce repentance. Only grace produces repentance. You can terrify a man
 into compliance but not into love. Only the goodness of God leads to
 repentance (Romans 2:4)."

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1 — KJV: "a star fall from heaven" / ESV: "star fallen from heaven" — Greek "peptokata" — perfect tense:
 having fallen (already fallen). This is a fallen angel — likely Satan
 or a high-ranking demon. "Bottomless pit" — Greek "abussos" — the
 abyss, the deep. The prison of demons.

v.5 — KJV: "five months" / ESV: Both equivalent. — The natural lifespan of locusts (May-September).
 A limited, defined period of torment.

v.6 — KJV: "death shall flee from them" / ESV: "death will flee from them" — An unprecedented condition: the
 inability to die. Torment without the escape of death.

v.11 — KJV: "Abaddon... Apollyon" / ESV: "Abaddon, and in Greek he is called Apollyon" — Hebrew and Greek names both meaning
 "Destroyer." The king of the demonic locusts — a powerful fallen angel.

v.15 — KJV: "prepared for an hour, and a day, and a month, and a year" / ESV: "prepared for the hour, the day, the month, and the year" —
 God's precise timing. These angels were prepared for this exact moment
 in history. Nothing is random in God's plan.

v.16 — KJV: "two hundred thousand thousand" / ESV: "twice ten thousand times
 ten thousand" — Both render the Greek "dismyriades myriadōn" (two
 myriads of myriads) = 200 million. KJV uses English multiplication;
 ESV follows the Greek phrasing more literally. Whether literal or
 symbolic of an incomprehensibly large force, it represents overwhelming
 destructive power.

v.20 — KJV: "repented not of the works of their hands" / ESV: "did not
 repent of the works of their hands nor give up worshiping demons and
 idols" — Greek "ou metenoesan" — did not change their minds, did not
 turn. ESV adds "nor give up worshiping" for clarity. Despite
 unprecedented judgment, the human heart remains stubbornly resistant
 without grace.

v.21 — KJV: "sorceries" / ESV: "sorceries" — Both equivalent. Greek
 "pharmakōn" — drugs, potions, sorcery. Root of English "pharmacy."
 Drug use connected with occult practices. KJV: "fornication" / ESV:
 "sexual immorality" — Greek "porneias." ESV uses the broader, more
 accurate modern term.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
v.1  — NET: "a star that had fallen from heaven to earth, and
       he was given the key to the shaft of the abyss" — "a star
       fallen" (astera peptOkota = a star having fallen — perfect
       participle). WEB: "a star from the sky which had fallen."
       Not a meteorite but a personal being (given a key) —
       likely a fallen angel.

v.3  — NET: "locusts came out of the smoke onto the earth" —
       "locusts" (akrides) — but not ordinary locusts (v.4: they
       don't harm vegetation). WEB: "out of the smoke came
       locusts." Demonic beings in locust form — tormenting but
       not killing.

v.6  — NET: "In those days people will seek death, but will not
       be able to find it; they will long to die, but death will
       flee from them" — "death will flee" (pheuxetai ho thanatos).
       WEB: "death will flee from them." A terrifying inversion —
       death itself becomes unattainable. Suffering without the
       escape of dying.

v.11 — NET: "whose name in Hebrew is Abaddon, and in Greek,
       Apollyon" — both names mean "Destroyer." WEB: "In Hebrew,
       Abaddon, but in Greek, Apollyon." KJV: "Abaddon... Apollyon."
       Named in two languages for both audiences — the king over
       the abyss army.

v.20 — NET: "The rest of humanity, who had not been killed by
       these plagues, did not repent" — "did not repent" (ou
       metenoEsan = refused to change their minds). WEB: "didn't
       repent." The purpose of judgment is repentance (cf. 16:9,
       11) — but humanity refuses. Hardened beyond response.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Bottomless pit/Abyss — The prison of demons. A place of confinement for
 evil spirits, opened only by divine permission.

Demonic locusts — Supernatural beings from the abyss. Not natural insects
 but demonic entities with power to torment.

Abaddon/Apollyon — "Destroyer." The king of the abyss locusts. A powerful
 fallen angel commanding demonic forces.

Five months — The limited duration of the locust torment. God sets
 boundaries even on demonic activity.

200 million — The vast demonic army released at the sixth trumpet.
 Overwhelming destructive force.

Repented not — Humanity's stubborn refusal to turn to God despite
 devastating judgment. The hardness of the unrepentant heart.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Men seek death, and shall not find it" (v.6). There are fates worse than
 death. This should produce urgency about the gospel — people need to be
 rescued before judgment falls.

2. "Repented not" (v.20). Judgment alone doesn't produce repentance — only
 grace does. Are you relying on God's grace for transformation, or trying
 to scare yourself into obedience?

3. God's timing is precise (v.15) — "an hour, and a day, and a month, and a
 year." Nothing in God's plan is random or accidental. Can you trust His
 timing in your life?

4. The sealed (7:3) are protected from the locusts (v.4). God distinguishes
 between His people and the world. Are you sealed — marked as His?

5. "Neither repented they of their murders, nor of their sorceries, nor of
 their fornication, nor of their thefts" (v.21). Four categories of sin
 that persist despite judgment. Which ones characterize our culture today?
 Are you standing against them?
