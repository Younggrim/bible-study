Revelation - Chapter 15
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Seven angels receive seven bowls of God's final wrath, victorious saints sing the song of Moses and the Lamb, and the temple in heaven is filled with smoke from God's glory — no one can enter until the seven plagues are completed.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: The Apostle John (see Chapter 1 notes for full details).

Historical Context: Chapter 15 is the shortest chapter in Revelation (8
verses) and serves as the prelude to the seven bowl judgments (ch.16). It
presents a scene of worship in heaven before the final, most severe judgments
are poured out. The victorious saints sing, and seven angels receive seven
bowls of God's wrath. This chapter balances terror (final plagues) with
triumph (the overcomers' song).

The Seven Last Plagues (v.1): Seven angels with seven plagues — "in them is
filled up the wrath of God." These are the final, most intense judgments.
God's wrath reaches its completion.

The Overcomers' Song (vv.2-4): Those who conquered the beast stand on a sea
of glass mixed with fire (v.2). They sing "the song of Moses... and the song
of the Lamb" (v.3) — celebrating God's works as "great and marvellous," His
ways as "just and true" (v.3). "Who shall not fear thee, O Lord?" (v.4). All
nations will worship because His judgments are revealed (v.4).

The Angels with Bowls (vv.5-8): The heavenly temple opens (v.5). Seven angels
emerge in pure white with golden sashes (v.6). One of the living creatures
gives them seven golden bowls "full of the wrath of God" (v.7). The temple
fills with smoke from God's glory — no one can enter until the plagues are
complete (v.8).

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1 — "Seven angels with seven plagues, which are the last, for with them
 the wrath of God is finished." (ESV) — The final judgments.

v.3 — "Great and amazing are your deeds, O Lord God the Almighty! Just and
 true are your ways, O King of the nations!" (ESV) — The song of the
 overcomers.

v.4 — "Who will not fear, O Lord, and glorify your name? For you alone are
 holy." (ESV) — God's holiness demands universal reverence.

v.8 — "The temple was filled with smoke from the glory of God... and no one
 could enter the temple until the seven plagues... were ended." (ESV)
 — God's glory fills the temple during judgment.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1 — KJV: "in them is filled up the wrath of God" / ESV: "with them the
 wrath of God is finished" — Greek "etelesthē" — completed, finished,
 brought to an end. ESV's "finished" captures the finality more clearly.
 God's wrath reaches its full expression in these final plagues.

v.2 — KJV: "them that had gotten the victory over the beast" / ESV: "those
 who had conquered the beast" — ESV's "conquered" (Greek "nikōntas") is
 more forceful than KJV's "gotten the victory." KJV: "sea of glass
 mingled with fire" / ESV: "a sea of glass mingled with fire" — Both
 equivalent. In ch.4 it was calm crystal; now it's mixed with fire —
 reflecting the judgment about to be poured out.

v.3 — KJV: "Great and marvellous are thy works, Lord God Almighty; just and
 true are thy ways, thou King of saints" / ESV: "Great and amazing are
 your deeds, O Lord God the Almighty! Just and true are your ways, O King
 of the nations!" — Key difference: KJV has "King of saints" while ESV
 has "King of the nations" (Greek "ethnōn" — nations; some manuscripts
 read "hagiōn" — saints). ESV's "amazing" for Greek "thaumasta" vs.
 KJV's "marvellous." One song with two themes: deliverance (Moses at the
 Red Sea) and redemption (the Lamb at the cross).

v.4 — KJV: "Who shall not fear thee, O Lord, and glorify thy name? for thou
 only art holy" / ESV: "Who will not fear, O Lord, and glorify your name?
 For you alone are holy" — Both equivalent. Greek "monos hosios" — You
 alone are holy. God's unique, unshared holiness.

v.5 — KJV: "the temple of the tabernacle of the testimony in heaven was
 opened" / ESV: "the sanctuary of the tent of witness in heaven was
 opened" — ESV uses "sanctuary" and "tent of witness" for Greek "naos"
 and "skēnē tou marturiou." KJV's "temple of the tabernacle of the
 testimony" is more literal but less clear.

v.6 — KJV: "clothed in pure and white linen, and having their breasts
 girded with golden girdles" / ESV: "clothed in pure, bright linen, with
 golden sashes around their chests" — ESV modernizes "girdles" to
 "sashes." Both describe priestly garments reflecting holiness and
 authority.

v.7 — KJV: "seven golden vials full of the wrath of God" / ESV: "seven
 golden bowls full of the wrath of God" — ESV's "bowls" (Greek "phialas"
 — broad, shallow bowls) is more accurate than KJV's "vials" (which
 suggests narrow containers). These are libation bowls — designed to pour
 out quickly.

v.8 — KJV: "no man was able to enter into the temple" / ESV: "no one could
 enter the sanctuary" — Both equivalent. During the outpouring of final
 wrath, even heaven's temple is inaccessible. God's glory and wrath fill
 the space completely. Intercession is suspended — judgment must run its
 course.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
v.2  — NET: "those who had conquered the beast and his image...
       standing beside the sea of glass, holding harps of God" —
       "conquered" (nikOntas = those overcoming/victorious). WEB:
       "those who overcame the beast." Victory through faithfulness
       (not military power) — they stood firm and now they stand
       glorified.

v.3  — NET: "They sang the song of Moses the servant of God and
       the song of the Lamb" — "song of Moses AND the Lamb" — old
       and new covenants united in one worship. WEB: "the song of
       Moses, the servant of God, and the song of the Lamb."
       Exodus 15 (Red Sea deliverance) meets the Lamb's redemption.

v.3  — NET: "Great and astounding are your deeds, Lord God, the
       All-Powerful!" — "astounding" (thaumasta = marvelous/
       wonderful). WEB: "Great and marvelous are your works." KJV:
       "Great and marvellous are thy works." The content echoes
       multiple OT psalms (Psalms 111:2, 139:14, 145:17).

v.4  — NET: "Who will not fear you, O Lord, and glorify your
       name? Because you alone are holy" — "you alone are holy"
       (monos hosios = uniquely set apart). WEB: "for you only are
       holy." After all the judgments, the conclusion: God is
       righteous, God alone is holy, all nations will worship.

v.8  — NET: "no one could enter the temple until the seven
       plagues from the seven angels were completed" — "no one
       could enter" (oudeis edynato eiselthein). WEB: "No one was
       able to enter into the temple." Access blocked during wrath
       — intercession is suspended while justice runs its full
       course.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Seven last plagues — The final, most severe judgments. God's wrath completed
 and finished in these bowls.

Song of Moses and the Lamb — A victory song celebrating God's deliverance
 (like the Exodus) and redemption (through Christ).

Sea of glass with fire — The heavenly expanse now reflecting judgment's fire.
 The overcomers stand on it — victorious above the chaos.

Just and true — God's character in judgment. His wrath is fair (just) and
 right (true). Never excessive, never unjust.

Temple filled with smoke — God's glory so intense that the temple is
 inaccessible. Judgment must be completed before access resumes.

Overcomers — Those who conquered the beast by refusing his mark. They
 stand victorious, singing God's praise.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Great and marvellous are thy works... just and true are thy ways" (v.3).
 Can you say this about God's ways — even the difficult ones? Even His
 judgments are just and true. Do you trust His character?

2. "Who shall not fear thee?" (v.4). God's holiness demands reverence. Do you
 approach God with appropriate awe? Or has familiarity bred casualness?

3. The overcomers conquered the beast (v.2). They refused to compromise even
 at the cost of their lives. What would you refuse to compromise on, even
 if it cost you everything?

4. "In them is filled up the wrath of God" (v.1). God's wrath has a limit —
 it will be "filled up" and finished. Even judgment has an end. How does
 this shape your understanding of God's character?

5. Before judgment falls, heaven worships (vv.2-4). Worship precedes and
 frames everything God does. Is worship the framework of your life?
