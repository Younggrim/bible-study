1 Peter - Chapter 2
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Believers are living stones built into a spiritual house, a royal priesthood and holy nation — Peter urges them to live honorably among pagans, submit to authorities, and follow Christ's example of suffering without retaliation.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Peter the Apostle (see Chapter 1 notes for full details).

Historical Context: Chapter 2 moves from the believer's identity (vv.1-10)
to the believer's conduct in a hostile world (vv.11-25). Peter establishes
who believers ARE (living stones, royal priesthood, holy nation) and then
shows how they should LIVE (as pilgrims, as citizens, as servants). The
chapter climaxes with Christ as the supreme example of suffering unjustly
(vv.21-25).

Spiritual Growth (vv.1-3): Put away sin; crave the Word like newborns crave
milk (vv.1-2). You've tasted God's goodness — now grow (v.3).

Living Stones and Royal Priesthood (vv.4-10): Christ is the living stone —
rejected by men, chosen by God (v.4). Believers are living stones built into
a spiritual house, a holy priesthood (v.5). Christ is the cornerstone —
precious to believers, a stumbling stone to unbelievers (vv.6-8). Believers
are "a chosen generation, a royal priesthood, an holy nation, a peculiar
people" (v.9) — called out of darkness into light (v.9).

Conduct as Pilgrims (vv.11-12): Abstain from fleshly lusts (v.11). Live
honorably among unbelievers so they glorify God (v.12).

Submission to Authority (vv.13-17): Submit to government for the Lord's sake
(vv.13-14). Silence critics through good conduct (v.15). Use freedom
responsibly (v.16). Honor all, love believers, fear God, honor the king (v.17).

Christ's Example of Suffering (vv.18-25): Servants endure unjust suffering
(vv.18-20). Christ suffered unjustly as our example (v.21). He did no sin,
didn't retaliate, committed Himself to God (vv.22-23). He bore our sins on
the cross so we could live to righteousness (v.24). "By whose stripes ye were
healed" (v.24).

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.5 — "you yourselves like living stones are being built up as a spiritual
 house, to be a holy priesthood." (ESV) — Believers as living stones
 and priests.

v.9 — "But you are a chosen race, a royal priesthood, a holy nation, a
 people for his own possession, that you may proclaim the excellencies
 of him who called you out of darkness into his marvelous light." (ESV)
 — The believer's identity.

v.21 — "Christ also suffered for you, leaving you an example, so that you
 might follow in his steps." (ESV) — Christ's suffering as our model.

v.24 — "He himself bore our sins in his body on the tree, that we might die
 to sin and live to righteousness. By his wounds you have been healed."
 (ESV) — Substitutionary atonement.

v.25 — "For you were straying like sheep, but have now returned to the
 Shepherd and Overseer of your souls." (ESV) — From lost to found.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.2 — KJV: "sincere milk of the word" / ESV: "pure spiritual milk" — Greek
 "logikon adolon gala" — pure/unadulterated spiritual milk. "Logikon"
 = rational, spiritual (same word as Romans 12:1). "Adolon" = without
 deceit, pure.

v.5 — KJV: "lively stones" / ESV: "living stones" — Greek "lithoi zōntes"
 — living stones. A paradox: stones are dead, but these are alive.
 Built into a "spiritual house" — the church as God's temple.

v.9 — KJV: "a chosen generation, a royal priesthood, an holy nation, a
 peculiar people" / ESV: "a chosen race, a royal priesthood, a holy
 nation, a people for his own possession" — Four OT titles applied to
 the church: chosen generation/race (Isa 43:20), royal priesthood
 (Exod 19:6), holy nation (Exod 19:6), peculiar people/God's own
 possession (Deut 7:6). Israel's titles now belong to all believers.

v.11 — KJV: "strangers and pilgrims" / ESV: "sojourners and exiles" — Greek
 "paroikous kai parepidēmous" — resident aliens and temporary
 sojourners. Two words for the same idea: this world is not home.

v.18 — KJV: "servants" / ESV: "servants" — Greek "oiketai" — household
 servants (not "doulos" = slave). Specifically domestic workers in a
 household. Both translations equivalent.

v.21 — KJV: "example" / ESV: Both equivalent. — Greek "hupogrammon" — a
 writing copy, a pattern to trace over. Like a child learning to write
 by tracing letters. Christ left a pattern for us to follow.

v.24 — KJV: "bare our sins" / ESV: "bore our sins" — Greek "anēnenken" —
 carried up, bore. Same word used of offering sacrifice on an altar.
 Christ carried our sins up onto the cross as a sacrifice. KJV:
 "stripes" / ESV: "wounds" — Greek "mōlōpi" — singular: wound, welt.
 By His one wound (the totality of His suffering) we are healed.

v.25 — KJV: "Bishop of your souls" / ESV: "Overseer of your souls" — Greek
 "episkopon" — overseer, guardian. Christ as the one who watches over
 and cares for our souls. Same root as "bishop/episcopal."

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
v.2  — NET: "Like newborn babies, crave pure spiritual milk" —
       "crave" (epipothEsate = long intensely for/desire eagerly).
       WEB: "as newborn babies, long for the pure milk of the Word."
       KJV: "desire the sincere milk of the word." The analogy: a
       baby's instinctive, urgent desire for milk.

v.5  — NET: "you yourselves, as living stones, are built up as a
       spiritual house" — "living stones" (lithoi zOntes). WEB:
       "you also, as living stones, are built up as a spiritual
       house." Each believer is a living stone in God's temple —
       corporate, not individualistic.

v.9  — NET: "But you are a chosen race, a royal priesthood, a
       holy nation, a people of his own" — four OT Israel titles
       now applied to the church. WEB: "a royal priesthood." KJV:
       "a royal priesthood." All believers are priests — no special
       class mediates between laity and God.

v.21 — NET: "Christ also suffered for you, leaving an example for
       you to follow in his steps" — "example" (hypogrammon = a
       writing pattern for children to trace). WEB: "leaving you
       an example, that you should follow his steps." KJV: "leaving
       us an example." Like tracing paper — we follow His pattern.

v.24 — NET: "He himself bore our sins in his body on the tree" —
       "bore" (anEnegken = carried up/offered up). WEB: "who his
       own self bore our sins in his body on the tree." KJV: "Who
       his own self bare our sins." Sacrificial language — He
       carried sins as a priest carries an offering to the altar.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Living stones — Believers as alive, active building blocks in God's
 spiritual temple. Not dead religion but living worship.

Royal priesthood — Believers as both kings (royal authority) and priests
 (direct access to God). Every believer is a priest.

Peculiar people — God's own special possession. Not strange but specially
 valued and owned.

Strangers and pilgrims — Believers as temporary residents on earth. Not
 permanent settlers but travelers heading home.

Example/Pattern — Christ's suffering as a template for believers to trace.
 He showed us how to suffer unjustly.

Bare our sins on the tree — Christ carrying our sins as a sacrifice on the
 cross. Substitutionary atonement.

By whose stripes ye were healed — Christ's suffering as the source of our
 spiritual healing. His wounds cure ours.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "As newborn babes, desire the sincere milk of the word" (v.2). Do you
 crave Scripture like a baby craves milk? Is your appetite for God's Word
 growing or diminishing?

2. "Ye are a chosen generation, a royal priesthood" (v.9). Do you live
 according to your identity — chosen, royal, holy, specially owned? Or do
 you live beneath your calling?

3. "Abstain from fleshly lusts, which war against the soul" (v.11). What
 fleshly desires are warring against your soul? Are you abstaining or
 indulging?

4. "Christ also suffered for us, leaving us an example" (v.21). When you
 suffer unjustly, do you follow Christ's pattern — no retaliation, no
 threats, committing yourself to God? Or do you fight back?

5. "By whose stripes ye were healed" (v.24). Christ's wounds healed yours.
 His suffering purchased your wholeness. Have you received this healing —
 spiritually, relationally, emotionally?
