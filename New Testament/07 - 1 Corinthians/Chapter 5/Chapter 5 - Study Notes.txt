1 Corinthians - Chapter 5
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Paul commands the church to expel an unrepentant man living in sexual immorality, warning that "a little leaven leavens the whole lump" — the church must judge those inside while leaving outsiders to God.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Paul (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 5 addresses a shocking case of sexual immorality
in the Corinthian church — a man living with his father's wife (stepmother).
The church was not only tolerating it but was "puffed up" about it (v.2) —
perhaps boasting about their "freedom" and "tolerance." Paul commands immediate
church discipline.

The Sin (v.1): "Such fornication as is not so much as named among the Gentiles"
— even pagans wouldn't tolerate this. A man was in a sexual relationship with
his father's wife (likely his stepmother). The church's response: pride instead
of mourning.

Church Discipline (vv.3-5): Paul commands the church to "deliver such an one
unto Satan for the destruction of the flesh, that the spirit may be saved"
(v.5). This means excommunication — removing the person from the protective
community of the church into Satan's domain. The purpose is redemptive — "that
the spirit may be saved." Discipline aims at restoration, not destruction.

The Leaven Principle (vv.6-8): "A little leaven leaveneth the whole lump" (v.6).
Tolerated sin spreads. One person's unaddressed sin affects the entire community.
"Purge out the old leaven" — remove the corrupting influence.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "And you are arrogant! Ought you not rather to mourn?" (ESV) — The
        wrong response to sin in the church: pride instead of grief.

v.5  — "You are to deliver this man to Satan for the destruction of the flesh,
        so that his spirit may be saved." (ESV) — Redemptive discipline.

v.6  — "A little leaven leavens the whole lump." (ESV) — Tolerated sin
        spreads.

v.7  — "For Christ, our Passover lamb, has been sacrificed." (ESV) — Christ
        as the Passover Lamb — the basis for holy living.

v.11 — "If anyone bearing the name of brother is guilty of sexual immorality...
        not even to eat with such a one." (ESV) — The standard for church
        discipline.

v.12 — "Is it not those inside the church whom you are to judge?" (ESV) — The
        church's responsibility to judge its own members.

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1  — KJV: "his father's wife" / ESV: "father’s wife" — Not his biological mother but his
       stepmother. Still a violation of Leviticus 18:8 and shocking even
       by pagan standards.

v.5  — KJV: "deliver such an one unto Satan" / ESV: "you are to deliver this man to Satan" — Excommunication — removing
       the person from the church's spiritual protection into the world
       (Satan's domain). The purpose: "that the spirit may be saved" —
       redemptive, not punitive.
       NET: "hand this man over to Satan for the destruction
       of the flesh, so that his spirit may be saved in the day of
       the Lord" — "destruction of the flesh" (olethron tEs sarkos)
       — debated: physical suffering? death? removal of fleshly
       desires? WEB: "for the destruction of the flesh." The goal
       is redemptive, not merely punitive.

v.6  — NET: "Your boasting is not good. Don't you know that a
       little yeast affects the whole batch of dough?" — "yeast"
       (zymE = leaven). WEB: "a little leaven leavens the whole
       lump." KJV: "a little leaven leaveneth the whole lump."
       Tolerated sin spreads — no contamination stays contained.

v.7  — KJV: "Christ our passover" / ESV: "For Christ, our Passover lamb, has" — Greek "pascha" — Passover. Christ
       fulfills the Passover lamb — sacrificed so that judgment passes over
       believers. This is the theological basis for holy living.
       NET: "Clean out the old yeast so that you may be a new
       batch of dough — you are, in fact, without yeast. For Christ,
       our Passover lamb, has been sacrificed" — "Christ our
       Passover" (to pascha hEmOn Christos). WEB: "our Passover,
       Christ, has been sacrificed." The Exodus imagery applied to
       the cross.

v.11 — KJV: "no not to eat" / ESV: "to eat with such a one" — Not even sharing a meal. In the ancient world,
       eating together signified fellowship and acceptance. Refusing to eat
       with someone was a powerful statement of separation.
       NET: "not to associate with anyone who calls himself a
       Christian who is sexually immoral, or greedy, or an idolater"
       — "calls himself a Christian" (onomazomenos adelphos = named
       a brother). WEB: "anyone who is called a brother." The
       distinction: professing believers held to higher standard
       than outsiders (v.12).

v.13 — NET: "Remove the evil person from among you" — quoting
       Deuteronomy 17:7. WEB: "Put away the wicked man from among
       yourselves." KJV: "put away from among yourselves that
       wicked person." Church discipline is a biblical command,
       not optional.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Fornication   — Greek "porneia" — sexual immorality of any kind. Here
               specifically: a man living with his father's wife.

Puffed up     — Inflated with pride. The Corinthians were proud of their
               "tolerance" — which was actually moral failure.

Deliver to Satan — Excommunication — removing a person from the church's
               protective community. The purpose is redemptive: shock them
               into repentance.

Leaven        — Yeast — used metaphorically for sin's spreading influence.
               A small amount affects the entire batch.

Christ our Passover — Jesus as the fulfillment of the Passover lamb (Exodus 12).
               His sacrifice is the basis for the church's holiness.

Judge within  — The church's responsibility to hold its own members accountable.
               Not judging outsiders — but maintaining standards within.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. The Corinthians were "puffed up" about sin in their midst (v.2). They
   should have mourned. When you become aware of serious sin in your church
   community, is your response grief or indifference? Pride in "tolerance"
   or sorrow over brokenness?

2. "A little leaven leaveneth the whole lump" (v.6). Tolerated sin spreads.
   Is there sin in your life that you're tolerating — thinking it's "small"
   or "manageable"? It will spread if not addressed.

3. "Christ our passover is sacrificed for us" (v.7). Because Christ died for
   sin, we must not live in sin. His sacrifice is not a license to continue
   sinning — it is the power to stop. How does the cross motivate your
   pursuit of holiness?

4. Paul distinguishes between judging those "within" and those "without"
   (vv.12-13). The church is responsible for its own members' behavior — not
   for policing the world. Are you holding fellow believers accountable while
   showing grace to outsiders?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. Church discipline is rarely practiced today. Why do you think that is? What
   are the consequences of a church that never addresses sin in its members?

2. The purpose of discipline is "that the spirit may be saved" (v.5) —
   redemptive, not punitive. How does knowing the goal is restoration change
   how you view church discipline?

3. Paul says "do not ye judge them that are within?" (v.12). The church IS
   supposed to judge its members — not harshly but redemptively. How do you
   balance grace and accountability in your relationships with other believers?

4. The Corinthians confused tolerance with love. True love confronts sin for
   the person's good. Where might you be confusing tolerance with love —
   allowing someone to continue in destructive behavior because confrontation
   is uncomfortable?

5. "Such were some of you" (6:11) — the next chapter reminds them they WERE
   these things but have been washed. Discipline is for those who claim Christ
   but live in unrepentant sin — not for those who are struggling and repenting.
   How do you distinguish between the two?
