1 Corinthians - Chapter 3
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Paul rebukes the Corinthians as spiritual infants, insists that ministers are merely servants — one plants, another waters, but God gives the growth — and warns that each person's work will be tested by fire on the day of judgment.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Paul (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 3 continues addressing the divisions in Corinth.
Paul diagnoses the problem: they are "carnal" — spiritual infants (vv.1-4).
He then uses two metaphors — a garden (vv.5-9) and a building (vv.10-17) — to
explain the proper view of Christian leaders and the seriousness of building
on the right foundation.

Spiritual Infancy (vv.1-4): The Corinthians were "babes in Christ" (v.1) —
still on milk, unable to handle solid food. The evidence of their immaturity:
"envying, and strife, and divisions" (v.3). Factionalism is a sign of spiritual
infancy, not sophistication.

The Garden (vv.5-9): Paul planted, Apollos watered — but "God gave the increase"
(v.6). Human leaders are merely servants; God is the one who produces growth.
"Neither is he that planteth any thing, neither he that watereth; but God that
giveth the increase" (v.7).

The Building (vv.10-17): Christ is the only foundation (v.11). What you build
on that foundation matters — gold, silver, precious stones (lasting work) vs.
wood, hay, stubble (worthless work). Fire will test every man's work (v.13).
If it survives, you receive a reward; if it burns, you suffer loss — "but he
himself shall be saved; yet so as by fire" (v.15).

"Ye are the temple of God" (v.16): The church — collectively — is God's temple.
The Spirit dwells in the community of believers. To destroy the church through
division is to defile God's temple — a serious offense (v.17).

REFLECTION
------------------------------------
1. Paul called the Corinthians "babes" because of their divisions (v.3).
   Factionalism is a sign of immaturity, not sophistication. How mature are
   you? Is there envy, strife, or division in your relationships with other
   believers?

2. "God gave the increase" (v.6). You can plant and water — but only God
   produces growth. Are you trying to force spiritual results through human
   effort? Can you trust God with the outcomes?

3. "Other foundation can no man lay than that is laid, which is Jesus Christ"
   (v.11). What is your life built on? If Christ is removed, does everything
   collapse? Or is He truly the foundation of everything?

4. "Every man's work shall be... revealed by fire" (v.13). Your work will be
   tested. Not your salvation — your work. What are you building — gold or
   hay? Will your life's work survive the fire?

1. The Corinthians were "carnal" — believers living like unbelievers (v.3).
   Is there an area of your life where you are living more like the world
   than like Christ? What would spiritual maturity look like there?

2. "Neither is he that planteth any thing, neither he that watereth" (v.7).
   Leaders are nothing — God is everything. How does this truth prevent both
   hero-worship of leaders and discouragement in your own ministry?

3. The fire will test "of what sort it is" (v.13) — not how much but what
   kind. Quality matters more than quantity. Are you focused on doing much
   or doing well? What would "gold" quality work look like in your life?

4. "All things are yours" (v.21) — Paul, Apollos, Cephas, the world, life,
   death, present, future — ALL yours in Christ. You don't need to fight over
   leaders or resources. Everything belongs to you because you belong to Christ.
   How does this abundance mentality change how you relate to other believers?

5. "Ye are the temple of God" (v.16). The church — your community of believers
   — is God's dwelling place. How seriously do you take the health and unity
   of your church? Are you building it up or tearing it down?
