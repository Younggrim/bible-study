1 Corinthians - Chapter 10
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Israel's wilderness failures serve as warnings against idolatry and testing God, Paul teaches that God provides a way of escape from every temptation, and commands that whether eating or drinking, "do all to the glory of God."

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Paul (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 10 continues the idol-meat discussion (chapters 8-10)
with a warning from Israel's history (vv.1-13), a command to flee idolatry
(vv.14-22), and practical guidelines for eating (vv.23-33). Paul uses Israel's
wilderness failures as a warning: privilege doesn't guarantee perseverance.

Israel's Warning (vv.1-13): All Israel experienced God's blessings — the cloud,
the sea, the manna, the water from the rock. Yet "with many of them God was not
well pleased" (v.5). They fell through lust, idolatry, fornication, testing God,
and grumbling. Paul's point: spiritual privilege doesn't make you immune to
falling. "Let him that thinketh he standeth take heed lest he fall" (v.12).

The Faithfulness of God (v.13): One of the most quoted and comforting verses in
the Bible. God is faithful — He won't allow temptation beyond your ability, and
He always provides "a way to escape." Not escape FROM temptation but escape
THROUGH it — the ability to bear it.

"Do All to the Glory of God" (v.31): The chapter's summary principle — and one
of the most comprehensive commands in Scripture. Every action — eating, drinking,
whatever you do — should aim at God's glory.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.12 — "Therefore let anyone who thinks that he stands take heed lest he
        fall." (ESV) — The danger of overconfidence.

v.13 — "No temptation has overtaken you that is not common to man. God is
        faithful, and he will not let you be tempted beyond your ability, but
        with the temptation he will also provide the way of escape, that you
        may be able to endure it." (ESV) — God's faithfulness in temptation.

v.14 — "Therefore, my beloved, flee from idolatry." (ESV) — The command:
        don't negotiate — flee.

v.31 — "So, whether you eat or drink, or whatever you do, do all to the glory
        of God." (ESV) — The comprehensive life principle.

v.33 — "Not seeking my own advantage, but that of many, that they may be
        saved." (ESV) — Paul's motivation: others' salvation over personal
        benefit.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------

v.4  — KJV: "that Rock was Christ" / ESV: "and the Rock was Christ" — Paul identifies the rock that gave water
       in the wilderness as Christ — a pre-incarnate appearance/type. Christ
       was present with Israel, providing for them.

v.11 — KJV: "upon whom the ends of the world are come" — ESV: "on whom the
       end of the ages has come." Believers live in the last days — the final
       era of history before Christ's return.

v.13 — KJV: "a way to escape" / ESV: "the way of escape" — Greek "ekbasin" — a way out, an exit. God
       always provides an exit from temptation — but you must look for it
       and take it.

v.16 — KJV: "communion" / ESV: "not a participation" — Greek "koinōnia" — participation, sharing,
       fellowship. The Lord's Supper is genuine participation in Christ's
       body and blood — not mere symbolism.

v.31 — KJV: "do all to the glory of God" / ESV: Both equivalent. — The most comprehensive ethical
       principle in Scripture. Every action — sacred or secular — should aim
       at displaying God's worth.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
v.4  — NET: "they all drank the same spiritual drink. For they
       were all drinking from the spiritual rock that followed
       them, and the rock was Christ" — "the rock was Christ" —
       Paul identifies the wilderness rock with Christ's pre-
       incarnate presence. WEB: "the rock was Christ." Christological
       reading of the OT.

v.12 — NET: "So let the one who thinks he is standing be careful
       that he does not fall" — "thinks he is standing" (ho dokOn
       hestanai) — the danger of overconfidence. WEB: "let him who
       thinks he stands be careful that he doesn't fall." Self-
       assurance is the prelude to collapse.

v.13 — NET: "God is faithful, and he will not let you be tempted
       beyond what you can bear. But with the temptation he will
       also provide a way out" — "a way out" (tEn ekbasin = the
       exit/escape). WEB: "will with the temptation also make the
       way of escape." God engineers escapes for every test.

v.16 — NET: "Is not the cup of blessing that we bless a sharing
       in the blood of Christ?" — "sharing" (koinOnia = communion/
       participation/fellowship). WEB: "a communion of the blood
       of Christ." KJV: "the communion of the blood of Christ."
       The Lord's Supper as genuine participation in Christ.

v.31 — NET: "So whether you eat or drink, or whatever you do,
       do everything for the glory of God" — the simplest and most
       comprehensive ethical principle. WEB: "do all to the glory
       of God." KJV: "do all to the glory of God." Every action —
       even the mundane — can glorify God.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Examples/Types — Israel's experiences were "types" — patterns that foreshadow
               and warn later believers. Their failures are our warnings.

Common to man — Every temptation you face is human — not unique, not
               unprecedented. Others have faced it and overcome.

Way of escape — God's provision in every temptation — an exit, a way through.
               Not removal of temptation but ability to endure it.

Communion     — Greek "koinōnia" — genuine participation and fellowship.
               The Lord's Supper is real participation in Christ.

Table of the Lord vs. table of demons — You cannot participate in both.
               Worship of Christ and participation in idolatry are mutually
               exclusive.

Glory of God  — The display of God's worth, beauty, and excellence. The
               ultimate purpose of every human action.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Let him that thinketh he standeth take heed lest he fall" (v.12). Israel
   had every spiritual privilege — and still fell. Overconfidence is dangerous.
   Where are you most confident in your spiritual strength? That may be where
   you're most vulnerable.

2. "God is faithful, who will not suffer you to be tempted above that ye are
   able" (v.13). Every temptation has a God-set limit and a God-provided exit.
   When you're tempted, are you looking for the way of escape? It's always
   there.

3. "Flee from idolatry" (v.14). Don't negotiate with idols — flee. What are
   the idols in your life — things that compete with God for your worship,
   trust, or affection? Career? Money? Approval? Pleasure?

4. "Do all to the glory of God" (v.31). Every action — eating, working,
   resting, relating — can glorify God. How would your day change if you
   evaluated every activity by this standard?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. Israel had the cloud, the sea, the manna, the water — and still fell (vv.1-5).
   Spiritual experiences don't guarantee spiritual perseverance. What are you
   relying on besides ongoing faith and obedience?

2. Paul lists five sins that destroyed Israel: lust, idolatry, fornication,
   testing God, and grumbling (vv.6-10). Which of these is most relevant to
   your life right now?

3. "God is faithful" (v.13). In the middle of temptation, do you believe that?
   Do you look for the way of escape, or do you assume there isn't one?

4. Paul says you "cannot drink the cup of the Lord, and the cup of devils"
   (v.21). You cannot participate in Christ AND in things that oppose Him.
   Where might you be trying to have both?

5. "Not seeking mine own profit, but the profit of many" (v.33). Paul's
   motivation was others' salvation — not his own comfort. What motivates
   your daily decisions — your own benefit or others' good?
