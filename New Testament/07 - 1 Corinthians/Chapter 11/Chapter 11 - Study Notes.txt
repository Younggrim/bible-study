1 Corinthians - Chapter 11
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Paul addresses head coverings in worship and corrects abuses of the Lord's Supper — warning that whoever eats and drinks in an unworthy manner "eats and drinks judgment on himself," and that many are sick because they fail to discern the body.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Paul (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 11 addresses two issues in Corinthian worship:
head coverings (vv.2-16) and abuses of the Lord's Supper (vv.17-34). Both
reflect the Corinthians' tendency to import cultural values (status, independence)
into worship rather than submitting to God's order.

Head Coverings (vv.2-16): A culturally complex passage. In Corinth, a woman's
head covering signified her married status and respectability. Removing it in
public was associated with prostitution or rebellion. Paul's principle: worship
should reflect God's created order (headship) and cultural propriety — not
independence or rebellion.

The Lord's Supper (vv.17-34): The Corinthians had turned communion into a
divisive, selfish meal. The wealthy ate lavishly while the poor went hungry
(v.21). Paul corrects this by recounting the institution of the Supper
(vv.23-26) and warning about "eating and drinking unworthily" (v.27). The
solution: "let a man examine himself" (v.28).

"This Do in Remembrance of Me" (vv.24-25): The Lord's Supper is a memorial —
"ye do shew the Lord's death till he come" (v.26). It looks back (His death),
looks forward (till He come), and looks inward (examine yourself).

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "Be imitators of me, as I am of Christ." (ESV) — Imitate Paul as he
        imitates Christ.

v.24-25 — "This is my body, which is for you. Do this in remembrance of me...
        This cup is the new covenant in my blood." (ESV) — The institution
        of the Lord's Supper.

v.26 — "For as often as you eat this bread and drink the cup, you proclaim
        the Lord's death until he comes." (ESV) — Communion: proclaiming His
        death until His return.

v.28 — "Let a person examine himself, then, and so eat of the bread and drink
        of the cup." (ESV) — Self-examination before communion.

v.31 — "But if we judged ourselves truly, we would not be judged." (ESV) —
        Self-judgment prevents God's judgment.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------

v.3  — KJV: "the head of every man is Christ; and the head of the woman is
       the man; and the head of Christ is God" — "Head" (kephalē) is debated:
       (1) authority/leadership, (2) source/origin. Most scholars favor
       "authority" — a chain of functional (not ontological) order.

v.10 — KJV: "power on her head because of the angels" / ESV: "symbol of authority on her head, because of the angels" — One of the most
       difficult verses in the NT. "Power" likely means "a sign of authority"
       (the covering). "Because of the angels" — possibly because angels
       observe worship and value proper order.

v.24 — KJV: "broken for you" — Some manuscripts omit "broken" (ESV: "which
       is for you"). Either way, Christ's body was given sacrificially for
       believers.

v.27 — KJV: "unworthily" / ESV: "unworthy manner will be" — An adverb (manner) not an adjective (person).
       It's not about being WORTHY (no one is) but about eating in an
       UNWORTHY MANNER — selfishly, divisively, without discernment.

v.29 — KJV: "not discerning the Lord's body" / ESV: "discerning the body eats and drinks" — Failing to recognize the
       significance of what the bread and cup represent — and failing to
       recognize the church as Christ's body (treating fellow believers with
       contempt).

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
v.3  — NET: "But I want you to know that Christ is the head of
       every man, and the man is the head of a woman, and God is
       the head of Christ" — "head" (kephalE) — debated: authority?
       source? preeminence? WEB: "the head of every man is Christ."
       The meaning of kephalE drives the entire head covering debate.

v.24 — NET: "This is my body that is for you. Do this in
       remembrance of me" — "for you" (to hyper hymOn). WEB: "This
       is my body, which is broken for you." KJV: "this is my
       body, which is broken for you." Some manuscripts omit
       "broken" — NET/ESV omit; KJV/WEB include.

v.26 — NET: "For every time you eat this bread and drink the cup,
       you proclaim the Lord's death until he comes" — "until he
       comes" (achri hou elthE) — the Supper is temporary,
       pointing forward to the Marriage Supper of the Lamb. WEB:
       "until he comes." An eschatological ordinance.

v.29 — NET: "For the one who eats and drinks without careful
       regard for the body eats and drinks judgment against himself"
       — "without careful regard for the body" (mE diakrinOn to
       sOma = not discerning the body). WEB: "not discerning the
       Lord's body." Which body — Christ's physical body or the
       church body? Both are possible.

v.30 — NET: "That is why many of you are weak and sick, and quite
       a few are dead" — "dead" (koimOntai = sleeping/have fallen
       asleep). WEB: "not a few sleep." KJV: "many sleep." Physical
       consequences of spiritual carelessness — God disciplines
       His own (v.32).

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Head covering — A cultural symbol of a woman's married status and
               respectability in first-century Corinth. Its removal signified
               rebellion or immorality.

Headship      — Functional authority/leadership within God's created order.
               Not superiority of nature but difference of role.

Lord's Supper — The memorial meal instituted by Jesus at the Last Supper.
               Bread = His body; Cup = His blood of the new covenant.

Unworthily    — In an unworthy manner — selfishly, divisively, without
               reverence. Not about personal worthiness (no one is worthy)
               but about the manner of participation.

Examine himself — Self-assessment before communion — confessing sin, resolving
               conflicts, recognizing the significance of Christ's sacrifice.

Discerning the body — Recognizing both the significance of Christ's sacrifice
               (the bread/cup) and the unity of Christ's body (the church).

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Be ye followers of me, even as I also am of Christ" (v.1). Paul could say
   "imitate me" because he imitated Christ. Could you say that? Is your life
   worth imitating?

2. "This do in remembrance of me" (v.24). When you take communion, what are
   you remembering? His body broken for you. His blood shed for you. Is the
   Lord's Supper meaningful to you, or has it become routine?

3. "Let a man examine himself" (v.28). Before communion — examine. Is there
   unconfessed sin? Unresolved conflict? Contempt for a brother? Deal with
   it before you eat.

4. "If we would judge ourselves, we should not be judged" (v.31). Self-judgment
   prevents God's discipline. Are you honest with yourself about your sin?
   Or does God have to discipline you because you won't discipline yourself?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. The Corinthians turned the Lord's Supper into a selfish meal — the rich
   ate while the poor went hungry (v.21). How does your church practice
   communion? Does it reflect unity or division?

2. Paul says eating "unworthily" brings judgment (v.29). This is not about
   being perfect — it's about being reverent and self-aware. How do you
   prepare your heart before taking communion?

3. "Ye do shew the Lord's death till he come" (v.26). Communion looks back
   (His death) and forward (His return). How does the Lord's Supper connect
   you to both the past and the future?

4. "For this cause many are weak and sickly among you" (v.30). Paul connects
   unworthy communion to physical consequences. How seriously do you take the
   Lord's Supper? Is it possible you're experiencing consequences of
   irreverent participation?

5. The head covering issue (vv.2-16) is culturally complex. The underlying
   principle: worship should reflect God's order, not cultural rebellion. How
   do you distinguish between timeless principles and cultural expressions
   in Scripture?
