1 Corinthians - Chapter 1
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Paul addresses a divided church, pleading for unity around Christ crucified — "the foolishness of God is wiser than men" — rejecting worldly wisdom and boasting only in the Lord.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: The Apostle Paul, writing from Ephesus (16:8) during his third
missionary journey, approximately AD 55.

Audience: The church at Corinth — a church Paul founded (Acts 18) but which
had developed serious problems: divisions, immorality, lawsuits, abuse of
spiritual gifts, and doctrinal confusion.

Corinth: A wealthy, cosmopolitan, morally corrupt city. "To live like a
Corinthian" was a Greek idiom for sexual immorality. The church reflected its
culture — gifted but immature, free but undisciplined.

Purpose: To address specific problems reported to Paul (by Chloe's household,
v.11, and by letter, 7:1) and to call the church back to the centrality of
the cross, unity in Christ, and holy living.

Chapter 1 Content: After greeting (vv.1-9), Paul addresses the first problem:
divisions (vv.10-17). The church was splitting into factions around human
leaders. Paul's response: the cross makes all human wisdom and boasting
irrelevant (vv.18-31).

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.10 — "That all of you agree, and that there be no divisions among you."
        (ESV) — The call to unity.

v.18 — "For the word of the cross is folly to those who are perishing, but to
        us who are being saved it is the power of God." (ESV) — The cross:
        foolish to the world, powerful to believers.

v.23 — "But we preach Christ crucified, a stumbling block to Jews and folly to
        Gentiles." (ESV) — The offense of the cross.

v.27 — "God chose what is foolish in the world to shame the wise." (ESV) —
        God's upside-down selection process.

v.30 — "Christ Jesus, whom God made our wisdom and our righteousness and
        sanctification and redemption." (ESV) — Christ is everything we need.

v.31 — "Let the one who boasts, boast in the Lord." (ESV) — The only
        legitimate boast: the Lord.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------

v.10 — KJV: "perfectly joined together" / ESV: "but that you be united in the same" — Greek "katērtismenoi" — to mend,
       to restore, to put in order. The same word used for mending fishing
       nets (Matthew 4:21). Unity requires active repair work.

v.17 — KJV: "not with wisdom of words" / ESV: "and not with words of eloquent" — Paul deliberately avoided rhetorical
       sophistication — not because he couldn't (he was highly educated) but
       because human eloquence can empty the cross of its power.

v.18 — KJV: "the preaching of the cross" / ESV: "For the word of the cross is folly to those" — Greek "ho logos tou staurou" —
       the word/message of the cross. Not just the event but the proclamation
       of its meaning.

v.23 — KJV: "stumblingblock" / ESV: "a stumbling block to Jews" — Greek "skandalon" — a trap, an offense. The
       cross offends Jewish expectations (they wanted power) and Greek
       expectations (they wanted wisdom).

v.30 — KJV: "wisdom, and righteousness, and sanctification, and redemption" / ESV: "to us wisdom from God"
       — Christ is ALL of these for us. Not just one — all four. He is our
       complete provision.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
v.10 — NET: "I urge you, brothers and sisters, by the name of
       our Lord Jesus Christ, to agree together" — "brothers and
       sisters" (adelphoi = siblings, inclusive). WEB: "brothers."
       KJV: "brethren." NET uses gender-inclusive language for the
       Greek masculine plural that includes women.

v.18 — NET: "For the message about the cross is foolishness to
       those who are perishing, but to us who are being saved it
       is the power of God" — "being saved" (sOzomenois = present
       participle — ongoing process). WEB: "to us who are being
       saved." KJV: "unto us which are saved." The present tense
       suggests salvation as ongoing, not just past event.

v.23 — NET: "but we preach about a crucified Christ, a stumbling
       block to Jews and foolishness to Gentiles" — "stumbling
       block" (skandalon). WEB: "a stumbling block to Jews." KJV:
       "unto the Jews a stumblingblock." The cross offends Jewish
       expectation of a conquering Messiah.

v.27 — NET: "God chose what is foolish in the world to shame the
       wise" — "chose" (exelexato = deliberately selected). WEB:
       "God chose the foolish things of the world." God's selection
       criteria invert human hierarchies — weakness shames strength.

v.30 — NET: "He is the reason you have a relationship with Christ
       Jesus, who became for us wisdom from God, and righteousness,
       and sanctification, and redemption" — Christ as four things
       from God. WEB: "who was made to us wisdom from God, and
       righteousness and sanctification, and redemption." All
       sufficiency in Christ.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Divisions     — Greek "schismata" — tears, splits, factions. The Corinthians
               were dividing around human leaders instead of uniting around
               Christ.

Foolishness of preaching — Not that preaching is foolish but that the MESSAGE
               (a crucified Messiah) seems foolish to the world's wisdom.

Stumblingblock — Something that causes offense or tripping. The cross offends
               because it demolishes human pride and self-sufficiency.

Called        — God's effective summons — those whom God calls experience the
               cross as "the power of God, and the wisdom of God" (v.24).

Wisdom/Righteousness/Sanctification/Redemption — The four things Christ IS
               for believers: our wisdom (understanding), righteousness
               (standing), sanctification (holiness), and redemption (freedom).

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Is Christ divided?" (v.13). The Corinthians were dividing around human
   leaders. Do you identify more with a denomination, a pastor, or a movement
   than with Christ Himself? Where is your loyalty — to a person or to Christ?

2. "The preaching of the cross is... the power of God" (v.18). The cross is
   not just history — it is POWER. Present-tense power for your life today.
   Are you experiencing the cross as power, or just as doctrine?

3. "God hath chosen the foolish things" (v.27). God doesn't choose the
   impressive — He chooses the unlikely so that He gets the glory. How does
   knowing that God uses "nobodies" encourage you about your own usefulness
   to Him?

4. "He that glorieth, let him glory in the Lord" (v.31). The only legitimate
   boast is Christ. What are you boasting in — your accomplishments, your
   church, your knowledge? Or in the Lord alone?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. The Corinthians said "I am of Paul... I am of Apollos" (v.12). They
   elevated human leaders to the point of division. How do you honor spiritual
   leaders without making them the center of your faith?

2. Paul says the cross is "foolishness" to the perishing (v.18). The Gospel
   will always seem foolish to the world. Are you tempted to make the Gospel
   more "acceptable" by removing the offense of the cross?

3. "Not many wise... not many mighty... not many noble, are called" (v.26).
   God's kingdom is not built on human credentials. How does this challenge
   the world's (and sometimes the church's) obsession with status and success?

4. Christ is made unto us "wisdom, and righteousness, and sanctification, and
   redemption" (v.30). He is EVERYTHING you need. Where are you looking for
   wisdom, righteousness, holiness, or freedom outside of Christ?

5. Paul came "not with excellency of speech" (2:1) — he deliberately avoided
   impressive rhetoric. Why? "Lest the cross of Christ should be made of none
   effect" (v.17). How do you balance excellence in communication with
   dependence on the Spirit's power?
