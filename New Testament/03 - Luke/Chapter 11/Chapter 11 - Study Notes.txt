Luke - Chapter 11
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Jesus teaches on prayer (including the Lord's Prayer and the persistent friend), casts out a demon and refutes the Beelzebul accusation, warns against seeking signs, and pronounces woes against the Pharisees and lawyers for their hypocrisy.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Luke (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 11 contains Jesus' teaching on prayer (vv.1-13),
His response to the accusation of casting out demons by Beelzebub (vv.14-26),
and His pronouncement of woes against the Pharisees and lawyers (vv.37-54).

The Lord's Prayer (vv.1-4): Luke's version is shorter than Matthew's (Matthew
6:9-13). A disciple asked "Lord, teach us to pray" — prompted by watching Jesus
pray. The prayer Jesus gives is a model — not a formula to recite but a pattern
to follow: address God as Father, honor His name, seek His kingdom, ask for
daily provision, seek forgiveness, and ask for protection from temptation.

The Persistent Friend (vv.5-8): A parable about prayer — not that God is
reluctant like the sleeping friend, but that persistence in prayer is appropriate
and rewarded. The Greek word "anaideia" (v.8) means shameless persistence or
boldness. God invites bold, persistent asking.

The Beelzebub Controversy (vv.14-26): Jesus' enemies accused Him of casting out
demons by Satan's power. Jesus demolishes this logic: a kingdom divided against
itself cannot stand. He then introduces the "stronger man" — Himself — who
overcomes the "strong man" (Satan) and plunders his house.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- On the journey to Jerusalem: Jesus is traveling south toward Jerusalem (set in motion in 9:51). This teaching occurs "on the way" — the exact location is unspecified but is between Galilee and Jerusalem.
- Jesus teaches His disciples privately amid growing crowds during this travel period through Galilee, Samaria, or Perea.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2-4 — "When you pray, say: 'Father, hallowed be your name. Your kingdom
        come...'" (ESV) — The model prayer — a pattern for all prayer.

v.9  — "Ask, and it will be given to you; seek, and you will find; knock,
        and it will be opened to you." (ESV) — The progressive intensity of
        prayer: ask, seek, knock.

v.13 — "How much more will the heavenly Father give the Holy Spirit to those
        who ask him!" (ESV) — The greatest gift God gives in response to
        prayer: Himself (the Spirit).

v.28 — "Blessed rather are those who hear the word of God and keep it!" (ESV)
        — True blessedness: hearing and obeying God's Word.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.2  — KJV includes "which art in heaven" and "Thy will be done, as in heaven,
       so in earth" / ESV: "Father, hallowed be your name. Your kingdom come."
       — These phrases are absent from many manuscripts of Luke (ESV/NET omit
       them). They may have been added from Matthew's version. Luke's original
       prayer may have been shorter.

v.8  — KJV: "because of his importunity" / ESV: "because of his impudence"
       — Greek "anaideia" — shamelessness, boldness, persistence without
       embarrassment. The point: pray boldly, without shame, without giving up.

v.13 — KJV: "give the Holy Spirit" / ESV: "give the Holy Spirit" — Both
       identical. Matthew 7:11: "give good things." Luke specifies the
       greatest "good thing" God gives: the Holy Spirit Himself. This is the
       ultimate answer to prayer — God giving us Himself.

v.20 — KJV: "the finger of God" / ESV: "the finger of God" — Both identical.
       Matthew 12:28: "the Spirit of God." Both refer to God's direct power.
       "Finger of God" echoes Exodus 8:19 — connecting Jesus' work to God's
       work in the Exodus.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
v.2  — NET: "Father, may your name be honored; may your
       kingdom come" — Luke's Lord's Prayer is shorter than
       Matthew's version. No "Our" before Father, no "who art in
       heaven," no "Thy will be done." WEB: "Our Father in heaven,
       may your name be kept holy." WEB follows the longer reading.

v.8  — NET: "because of his sheer persistence he will get up and
       give him whatever he needs" — "sheer persistence" (anaideian
       = shamelessness/boldness). KJV: "because of his importunity."
       WEB: "because of his persistence." Not about friendship but
       relentless asking.

v.13 — NET: "how much more will the heavenly Father give the Holy
       Spirit to those who ask him!" — Luke has "Holy Spirit" where
       Matthew has "good things" (7:11). This is distinctly Lukan
       — the supreme gift of prayer is the Spirit Himself. WEB:
       "give the Holy Spirit to those who ask him."

v.20 — NET: "But if I cast out demons by the finger of God, then
       the kingdom of God has already overtaken you" — "finger of
       God" (Luke) vs. "Spirit of God" (Matthew). WEB: "by the
       finger of God." The allusion is to Exodus 8:19 — the
       Egyptian magicians recognizing God's power.

v.41 — NET: "give from your heart to those in need, and then
       everything will be clean for you" — "give from your heart"
       (ta enonta dote eleēmosynēn = give alms from what is within).
       KJV: "give alms of such things as ye have." WEB: "give for
       gifts to the needy those things which are within." Internal
       generosity cleanses externally.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Importunity   — KJV for shameless persistence (Greek: anaideia). Bold,
               unashamed asking that refuses to give up.

Beelzebub     — "Lord of the flies" — a mocking name for Satan, the prince
               of demons. The Pharisees accused Jesus of operating by Satan's
               power.

Finger of God — A metaphor for God's direct, effortless power. Used in
               Exodus 8:19 and 31:18 (the tablets of the Law). Jesus uses
               it to describe His authority over demons.

Strong man    — In Jesus' parable, Satan — who holds people captive. The
               "stronger man" is Christ, who overcomes Satan and frees
               the captives.

Lawyers       — Experts in the Mosaic Law (Torah scholars). Not modern
               attorneys but religious legal experts who interpreted and
               applied Scripture.

Key of knowledge — The ability to understand and enter God's truth. The
               lawyers had "taken away" this key — preventing both themselves
               and others from entering the kingdom.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. The disciples asked "Lord, teach us to pray" (v.1) — prompted by watching
   Jesus pray. They didn't ask Him to teach them to preach or heal — to pray.
   How is your prayer life? Would watching you pray make anyone want to learn?

2. "Ask, seek, knock" (v.9) — progressive intensity. Are you asking casually
   or seeking earnestly? Are you knocking persistently or giving up after one
   attempt? What would bold, shameless persistence in prayer look like for
   your current needs?

3. "How much more shall your heavenly Father give the Holy Spirit to them that
   ask him?" (v.13). The greatest gift God gives is Himself. Are you asking
   for things from God or for God Himself? What would it look like to pray
   primarily for more of the Spirit's presence and power?

4. The Pharisees cleaned the outside of the cup but were full of wickedness
   inside (v.39). External religion without internal transformation is
   hypocrisy. How clean is the inside of your cup — your thought life, your
   motives, your private behavior?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. The Lord's Prayer begins with "Our Father" — establishing relationship
   before requests. How does approaching God as Father (not judge, not boss,
   not distant deity) change how you pray?

2. The persistent friend got what he needed not because of friendship but
   because of boldness (v.8). God is not reluctant — but He rewards persistence.
   What prayer have you given up on that you need to return to with boldness?

3. Jesus said a house swept clean but left empty will be filled with worse
   spirits (vv.24-26). Moral reformation without being filled with God's Spirit
   leaves you vulnerable. Are you trying to clean up your life without being
   filled? What's the difference?

4. The lawyers "took away the key of knowledge" (v.52) — they made God's truth
   inaccessible. Are there ways you make the Gospel harder for people to
   understand or access? How can you be a key-giver rather than a key-taker?

5. "Blessed are they that hear the word of God, and keep it" (v.28). Not just
   hear — keep. What specific word from God have you heard recently that you
   need to act on?
