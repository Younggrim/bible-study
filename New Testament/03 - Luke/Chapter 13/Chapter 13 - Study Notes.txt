Luke - Chapter 13
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Jesus calls for repentance in light of sudden calamity, tells the parable of the barren fig tree, heals a crippled woman on the Sabbath, teaches that the kingdom starts small like a mustard seed, and laments over Jerusalem's rejection.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Luke (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 13 addresses the urgent need for repentance (vv.1-9),
records a Sabbath healing (vv.10-17), presents two kingdom parables (vv.18-21),
teaches about the narrow door (vv.22-30), and ends with Jesus' lament over
Jerusalem (vv.34-35).

The Galileans and the Tower of Siloam (vv.1-5): Two recent tragedies — Pilate
killing Galilean worshippers and a tower collapse killing 18 people. The crowd
assumed these victims were worse sinners. Jesus corrects this: tragedy is not
proof of greater guilt. But He uses both events to press the urgency of
repentance — "except ye repent, ye shall all likewise perish."

The Barren Fig Tree (vv.6-9): A parable of God's patience and coming judgment.
The tree has had three years to produce fruit (paralleling Jesus' three-year
ministry to Israel). The vinedresser (Christ) intercedes for one more year of
grace. But patience has a limit — if no fruit comes, judgment follows.

The Narrow Door (vv.23-30): Someone asks "Are there few that be saved?" Jesus
doesn't answer the theoretical question — He makes it personal: "Strive to
enter in at the strait gate." The door is narrow, and it will be shut. Casual
association with Jesus ("we ate and drank in your presence") is not enough.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Siloam (v.4): A tower near the Pool of Siloam in southeast Jerusalem that fell and killed 18 people. Jesus uses this local tragedy as a lesson on repentance.
- A synagogue (v.10): Jesus healed the crippled woman in a synagogue — likely on the route through Perea or Judea.
- Jerusalem (v.34): Jesus laments over Jerusalem, the city that kills the prophets — the destination He is heading toward.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.3  — "Unless you repent, you will all likewise perish." (ESV) — The universal
        urgency of repentance — repeated twice for emphasis.

v.16 — "Ought not this woman, a daughter of Abraham whom Satan bound for
        eighteen years, be loosed from this bond on the Sabbath day?" (ESV)
        — Compassion trumps religious regulation.

v.24 — "Strive to enter through the narrow door. For many, I tell you, will
        seek to enter and will not be able." (ESV) — The narrow way
        requires earnest effort, not casual interest.

v.34 — "O Jerusalem, Jerusalem, the city that kills the prophets and stones
        those who are sent to it! How often would I have gathered your
        children together as a hen gathers her brood under her wings, and
        you were not willing!" (ESV) — Christ's grief over those who reject
        His love.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.3  — KJV: "likewise perish" / ESV: "likewise perish" — Both identical.
       Not that they would die the same way (by Pilate's sword or a falling
       tower) but that they would perish eternally without repentance. The
       physical deaths illustrate the spiritual reality.

v.11 — KJV: "a spirit of infirmity" / ESV: "a disabling spirit" — Both
       equivalent. Luke the physician notes this was not merely a physical
       condition but had a spiritual cause — Satan had bound her (v.16).
       Physical and spiritual realities intersect.

v.24 — KJV: "Strive" / ESV: "Strive" — Both identical. Greek "agōnizesthe"
       — from which we get "agonize." It means to struggle, to fight, to
       exert maximum effort. Entering the kingdom is not passive — it
       requires earnest pursuit.

v.32 — KJV: "tell that fox" / ESV: "tell that fox" — Both identical. Jesus
       calls Herod Antipas a "fox" — cunning, sly, destructive. This is one
       of the few times Jesus uses a derogatory term for a specific individual.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
v.1  — NET: "some people who told him about the Galileans
       whose blood Pilate had mixed with their sacrifices" — a
       historical incident not recorded elsewhere. WEB: "some who
       told him about the Galileans, whose blood Pilate had mixed
       with their sacrifices." Murdered while worshipping.

v.4  — NET: "Or those eighteen who were killed when the tower in
       Siloam fell on them" — another unrecorded disaster. Jesus
       uses both events to teach: suffering ≠ greater sinfulness.
       WEB: "those eighteen, on whom the tower in Siloam fell."
       Tragedy is not divine targeting.

v.16 — NET: "Should not this woman, a daughter of Abraham whom
       Satan bound for eighteen long years, be released from this
       imprisonment on the Sabbath day?" — "daughter of Abraham"
       — a phrase unique to Luke. WEB: "daughter of Abraham." She
       has covenant rights. Her healing is liberation.

v.24 — NET: "Exert every effort to enter through the narrow door,
       because many, I tell you, will try to enter and will not be
       able to" — "exert every effort" (agōnizesthe = agonize/
       struggle). KJV: "Strive to enter." WEB: "Strive to enter."
       The narrow door requires genuine effort, not casual interest.

v.34 — NET: "O Jerusalem, Jerusalem, you who kill the prophets
       and stone those who are sent to you! How often I have longed
       to gather your children together as a hen gathers her chicks
       under her wings, but you would have none of it!" — "but you
       would have none of it" is vivid. WEB: "you wouldn't." The
       maternal image of God — tender, protective, rejected.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Repent        — Greek "metanoeō" — to change one's mind completely, resulting
               in changed behavior. Not merely feeling sorry but turning
               from sin to God.

Barren fig tree — A tree that takes up space and resources but produces no
               fruit. Represents Israel (or any person) who receives God's
               blessings but produces no righteousness.

Strait/Narrow gate — The entrance to the kingdom of God — narrow because it
               requires repentance, faith, and surrender. Not many paths but
               one way.

Mustard seed  — One of the smallest seeds, growing into a large tree. The
               kingdom starts small but grows to encompass all nations.

Leaven        — Yeast that permeates dough. Here used positively — the kingdom
               works invisibly but transforms everything it touches.

Daughter of Abraham — A Jewish woman — part of the covenant community. Jesus
               uses this title to shame the synagogue ruler who valued rules
               over people.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Except ye repent, ye shall all likewise perish" (v.3). Jesus doesn't
   answer the question about others' guilt — He redirects to personal
   responsibility. Stop comparing yourself to others and ask: have I repented?
   Is there unrepented sin in my life right now?

2. The barren fig tree was given one more year of grace (v.8). God is patient
   — but patience has a limit. If you've been fruitless — spiritually
   unproductive, going through the motions — this is your "one more year."
   What fruit is God looking for in your life?

3. Jesus healed the bent woman on the Sabbath and called out the hypocrisy
   of those who cared more about rules than people (v.15). Where might you
   be prioritizing religious correctness over compassion for hurting people?

4. "Strive to enter in at the strait gate" (v.24). Following Christ requires
   effort, intentionality, and urgency. Are you striving or coasting? What
   would "agonizing" to enter look like in your daily life?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. The crowd assumed the Galileans died because they were worse sinners (v.2).
   We still make this assumption — "bad things happen to bad people." How does
   Jesus' correction change how you view suffering and tragedy?

2. The vinedresser intercedes for the barren tree — "let it alone this year
   also" (v.8). Christ intercedes for you. How does knowing that Jesus
   advocates for you before the Father affect your confidence and gratitude?

3. Many will say "Lord, Lord" and claim association with Jesus but be turned
   away (vv.25-27). What is the difference between knowing about Jesus and
   being known by Him? Which describes your relationship?

4. "There are last which shall be first, and there are first which shall be
   last" (v.30). The kingdom inverts human expectations. Who do you expect to
   see in heaven that might surprise you? Who might be surprised to see you?

5. Jesus wept over Jerusalem's rejection (v.34) — "I would have gathered you...
   and ye would not." God's desire is to gather and protect. The barrier is
   human refusal. Where are you saying "I will not" to God's gathering love?
