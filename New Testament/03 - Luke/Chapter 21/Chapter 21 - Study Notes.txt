Luke - Chapter 21
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Jesus commends the poor widow's sacrificial giving, prophesies the destruction of Jerusalem and the temple, describes signs of the end, and calls His disciples to watchfulness — "your redemption is drawing near."

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Luke (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 21 is Luke's version of the Olivet Discourse —
Jesus' prophetic teaching about the destruction of Jerusalem and His second
coming. It begins with the widow's offering (vv.1-4) and then moves to
prophecy about the Temple's destruction (vv.5-6), signs of the end (vv.7-19),
the fall of Jerusalem (vv.20-24), the coming of the Son of Man (vv.25-28),
and the call to watchfulness (vv.29-38).

The Widow's Mites (vv.1-4): A brief but powerful observation. Rich people gave
large amounts from their abundance; a poor widow gave two tiny coins — all she
had. Jesus says she gave more than all of them. God measures giving not by
amount but by sacrifice.

The Fall of Jerusalem (vv.20-24): Luke's version is more specific than Matthew's
about the destruction of Jerusalem — "when ye shall see Jerusalem compassed with
armies" (v.20). This was fulfilled literally in AD 70 when Roman armies under
Titus besieged and destroyed the city. Early Christians, remembering Jesus'
words, fled to Pella across the Jordan and were saved.

"In your patience possess ye your souls" (v.19): The key to enduring persecution
and tribulation — patient endurance. Not escape but perseverance.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- The Temple in Jerusalem: The widow's offering and the Olivet Discourse (vv.1-36). Jesus observed the widow in the Temple treasury and then prophesied the Temple's destruction.
- Jerusalem (v.20): "When ye shall see Jerusalem compassed with armies" — Jesus prophesied the Roman siege of AD 70.
- The mountains (v.21): Jesus warned that those in Judea should flee to the mountains when they see the siege coming.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.3-4 — "Truly, I tell you, this poor widow has put in more than all of them.
        For they all contributed out of their abundance, but she out of her
        poverty put in all she had to live on." (ESV) — God measures
        sacrifice, not amount.

v.19 — "By your endurance you will gain your lives." (ESV) — Endurance as
        the mark of genuine faith.

v.28 — "Now when these things begin to take place, straighten up and raise
        your heads, because your redemption is drawing near." (ESV) — Hope
        in the midst of tribulation.

v.33 — "Heaven and earth will pass away, but my words will not pass away."
        (ESV) — The eternal permanence of Christ's words.

v.36 — "But stay awake at all times, praying that you may have strength to
        escape all these things that are going to take place, and to stand
        before the Son of Man." (ESV) — The practical application:
        watchfulness and prayer.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.2  — KJV: "two mites" / ESV: "two small copper coins" — Greek "lepta duo"
       — the smallest coins in circulation, worth about 1/64 of a denarius.
       Together worth less than a penny. Yet Jesus said she gave more than
       all the rich combined.

v.19 — KJV: "In your patience possess ye your souls" / ESV: "By your
       endurance you will gain your lives" — Greek "hupomonē" — patient
       endurance under pressure. Not passive waiting but active perseverance.

v.24 — KJV: "the times of the Gentiles" / ESV: "the times of the Gentiles"
       — Both identical. A period during which Gentile nations dominate
       Jerusalem. Its end signals the approach of Christ's return. Debated
       whether this ended in 1967 (Six-Day War) or awaits future fulfillment.

v.34 — KJV: "surfeiting" / ESV: "dissipation" — Both translate Greek
       "kraipale" — excess, overindulgence, the hangover of self-indulgent
       living. Jesus warns that careless living will leave you unprepared for
       His return.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
v.2  — NET: "he also saw a poor widow put in two small copper
       coins" — same event as Mark 12:42. WEB: "two small brass
       coins." KJV: "two mites." The smallest possible offering
       from the poorest possible giver — yet the greatest in God's
       economy.

v.19 — NET: "By your endurance you will gain your lives" — 
       "endurance" (hypomonē = patient steadfastness under
       pressure). KJV: "In your patience possess ye your souls."
       WEB: "By your endurance you will win your lives." Not
       passive waiting but active perseverance.

v.24 — NET: "Jerusalem will be trampled down by the Gentiles until
       the times of the Gentiles are fulfilled" — "times of the
       Gentiles" — a period of Gentile domination over Jerusalem
       with a definite end. WEB: "until the times of the Gentiles
       are fulfilled." All versions mark this as temporary.

v.28 — NET: "straighten up and raise your heads, because your
       redemption is drawing near" — "straighten up" (anakupsate =
       stand erect). WEB: "look up, and lift up your heads." KJV:
       "look up, and lift up your heads." While the world cowers
       in fear, believers stand tall — rescue is arriving.

v.34 — NET: "But be on your guard so that your hearts are not
       weighed down with dissipation and drunkenness and the worries
       of this life" — "weighed down" (barēthōsin = made heavy).
       WEB: "that your hearts aren't loaded down." KJV: "overcharged."
       Spiritual heaviness from indulgence or anxiety both dull
       watchfulness.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Mites         — Greek "lepta" — the smallest copper coins in circulation.
               Two mites = one quadrans = 1/64 of a denarius. The widow's
               entire livelihood.

Penury        — Extreme poverty, destitution. The widow gave from her poverty,
               not her surplus.

Times of the Gentiles — The period during which Gentile nations exercise
               dominion over Jerusalem. Its duration and end are debated
               among interpreters.

Surfeiting    — Overindulgence, excess, dissipation. The dulling of spiritual
               alertness through careless, self-indulgent living.

Redemption    — Deliverance, liberation, rescue. When believers see the signs
               of the end, they should look up — their final redemption
               (resurrection/rapture) is approaching.

Snare         — A trap that catches suddenly and unexpectedly. The day of the
               Lord will come like a snare on those who are unprepared.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. The widow gave "all the living that she had" (v.4). God measures generosity
   by sacrifice, not amount. How sacrificial is your giving? Are you giving
   from your surplus or from your substance?

2. "In your patience possess ye your souls" (v.19). Endurance — not escape —
   is the mark of genuine faith. What are you enduring right now that requires
   patient perseverance? How are you holding up?

3. "Look up, and lift up your heads; for your redemption draweth nigh" (v.28).
   When the world gets darker, believers should look up — not down in despair.
   Where is your gaze right now — on the problems around you or on the Christ
   above you?

4. "Take heed to yourselves, lest at any time your hearts be overcharged with
   surfeiting, and drunkenness, and cares of this life" (v.34). Three things
   that dull spiritual alertness: overindulgence, substance abuse, and worry.
   Which of these is most threatening to your spiritual readiness?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. Jesus watched people give (v.1) — He notices how you handle money. If Jesus
   evaluated your giving the way He evaluated the widow's, what would He say?
   Are you giving sacrificially or comfortably?

2. Jesus predicted the Temple's destruction (v.6) — the most impressive
   building in the Jewish world. Nothing man builds is permanent. What are you
   putting your confidence in that could be "thrown down"?

3. Jesus promised "I will give you a mouth and wisdom" (v.15) when facing
   persecution. You don't need to prepare a defense in advance — the Spirit
   will provide. How does this promise affect your willingness to speak up
   for Christ in hostile environments?

4. "Heaven and earth shall pass away: but my words shall not pass away" (v.33).
   Everything physical is temporary; only God's Word is eternal. How much of
   your life is invested in temporary things vs. eternal things?

5. "Watch ye therefore, and pray always" (v.36). Watchfulness and prayer are
   the two disciplines that keep you ready for Christ's return. How is your
   watchfulness? How is your prayer life? Are you ready if He came today?
