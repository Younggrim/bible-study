Luke - Chapter 23
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Jesus is tried before Pilate and Herod, the crowd demands Barabbas, He speaks to the weeping women on the road to Calvary, is crucified between two thieves (one repents and receives paradise), and is buried in Joseph's tomb.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Luke (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 23 records the trials, crucifixion, and burial of
Jesus. Luke includes unique details: the trial before Herod Antipas (vv.6-12),
Jesus' words to the weeping women (vv.27-31), the repentant thief (vv.39-43),
and Jesus' final words "Father, into thy hands I commend my spirit" (v.46).

The Trial Before Herod (vv.6-12): Only in Luke. Herod Antipas — the man who
beheaded John the Baptist — had long wanted to see Jesus perform a miracle.
Jesus refused to speak to him or perform for him. Herod mocked Him and sent
Him back to Pilate. Ironically, this shared injustice made Pilate and Herod
friends (v.12).

The Repentant Thief (vv.39-43): One of the most powerful conversion stories in
Scripture. A criminal, dying on a cross, with nothing to offer — no good works,
no baptism, no church membership — simply said "Lord, remember me when thou
comest into thy kingdom." Jesus' response: "To day shalt thou be with me in
paradise." Salvation by grace through faith alone, demonstrated in the most
extreme circumstances.

"Father, forgive them" (v.34): Jesus' first words from the cross — a prayer
for His executioners. Only in Luke. Even while being crucified, He interceded
for those killing Him.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Pilate's judgment hall (v.1): The Roman governor's headquarters in Jerusalem (the Praetorium).
- Herod's palace (v.7): Jesus was sent to Herod Antipas, who was also in Jerusalem for Passover. Herod's palace was in the upper city.
- Golgotha / Calvary (v.33): "The place which is called Calvary" — the execution site outside Jerusalem's walls.
- Cyrene (v.26): Simon was from Cyrene in North Africa. He carried Jesus' cross.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.34 — "Father, forgive them, for they know not what they do." (ESV) — Grace
        at its most extreme — forgiving those who are actively destroying you.

v.43 — "Truly, I say to you, today you will be with me in paradise." (ESV) —
        Salvation in the final moment — grace that reaches the very end.

v.46 — "Father, into your hands I commit my spirit!" (ESV) — Jesus' final
        words in Luke — a prayer of trust and surrender (Psalm 31:5).

v.47 — "Certainly this man was innocent!" (ESV) — The centurion's confession —
        a Gentile recognizing what Israel's leaders refused to see.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.34 — KJV: "Father, forgive them; for they know not what they do" / ESV:
       "Father, forgive them, for they know not what they do" — Both
       equivalent. This verse is absent from some manuscripts (ESV footnote).
       Most scholars consider it authentic — it is consistent with Jesus'
       character and with Luke's emphasis on forgiveness throughout his Gospel.

v.43 — KJV: "To day shalt thou be with me in paradise" / ESV: "today you
       will be with me in paradise" — Both equivalent. Greek "paradeisos" —
       a Persian word for a walled garden/park. Used for the place of the
       righteous dead (Abraham's bosom) and for heaven itself. The thief
       would be with Christ that very day.

v.45 — KJV: "the sun was darkened" / ESV: "the sun's light failed" — Some
       manuscripts differ. Either way, supernatural darkness covered the land
       from noon to 3 PM — creation itself responding to the Creator's death.

v.46 — KJV: "Father, into thy hands I commend my spirit" / ESV: "Father,
       into your hands I commit my spirit!" — Both equivalent. Quoting
       Psalm 31:5. Jesus' final words in Luke are a prayer of trust — He
       actively gave up His spirit; it was not taken from Him.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
v.4  — NET: "I find no basis for an accusation against this
       man" — Pilate's first declaration of Jesus' innocence (he
       will repeat it three times). WEB: "I find no basis for a
       charge against this man." KJV: "I find no fault in this man."

v.34 — NET: "Father, forgive them, for they don't know what they
       are doing" — some manuscripts omit this verse, but it is
       included in NET/ESV/WEB/KJV. WEB: "Father, forgive them,
       for they don't know what they are doing." The first words
       from the cross — intercession for His executioners.

v.43 — NET: "I tell you the truth, today you will be with me in
       paradise" — "paradise" (paradeisō) — originally a Persian
       word for a walled garden/royal park. WEB: "Today you will
       be with me in Paradise." KJV: "To day shalt thou be with
       me in paradise." Immediate presence with Christ at death.

v.45 — NET: "the sun's light failed" — "failed" (tou hēliou
       eklipontos). Some manuscripts: "the sun was darkened" (ho
       hēlios eskotisthē). WEB: "the sun was darkened." KJV: "the
       sun was darkened." NET's "failed" suggests an eclipse-like
       event, though a solar eclipse is impossible during Passover
       (full moon). This was supernatural.

v.46 — NET: "Father, into your hands I commit my spirit!" — the
       final word from the cross in Luke (quoting Psalm 31:5). WEB:
       "Father, into your hands I commit my spirit!" KJV: "into
       thy hands I commend my spirit." Jesus dies with a prayer —
       voluntarily entrusting Himself to the Father.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Calvary       — Latin "calvaria" = skull. Greek "kranion." The place of
               crucifixion outside Jerusalem's walls. Also called Golgotha
               (Aramaic for skull).

Paradise      — A place of blessedness for the righteous dead. Jesus promised
               the thief immediate presence with Him there — "today."

Barabbas      — A convicted murderer and insurrectionist released instead of
               Jesus. The guilty goes free; the innocent is condemned — a
               picture of substitutionary atonement.

Green tree/Dry tree — Jesus' metaphor (v.31): if Rome does this to an innocent
               man (green tree), what will they do to a guilty nation (dry
               tree)? Fulfilled in AD 70.

Centurion     — A Roman officer who witnessed the crucifixion and confessed
               Jesus as righteous/Son of God. A Gentile recognizing what
               Israel's leaders rejected.

Preparation   — The day before the Sabbath (Friday). Jesus was buried before
               sunset when the Sabbath began.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Father, forgive them; for they know not what they do" (v.34). While being
   crucified, Jesus prayed for His executioners. Is there anyone you are
   refusing to forgive? If Jesus could forgive those who nailed Him to a cross,
   what excuse do you have for holding onto bitterness?

2. The repentant thief had nothing to offer — no works, no time, no resources.
   He simply asked to be remembered. And Jesus gave him paradise (v.43). This
   is the Gospel: salvation is not earned; it is received by faith. Are you
   resting in grace or still trying to earn your way?

3. "Father, into thy hands I commend my spirit" (v.46). Jesus' final act was
   trust — placing Himself in the Father's hands. Can you pray that prayer
   today — committing your life, your future, your fears into God's hands?

4. The centurion said "Certainly this was a righteous man" (v.47). Even a
   pagan soldier could see what the religious leaders refused to acknowledge.
   Sometimes outsiders see the truth more clearly than insiders. Are you
   open to truth from unexpected sources?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. Herod wanted Jesus to perform a miracle for his entertainment (v.8). Jesus
   refused to speak to him. God is not a performer. He doesn't respond to
   curiosity or entertainment-seeking. Are you approaching God with genuine
   faith or with curiosity? What's the difference?

2. Pilate declared Jesus innocent three times (vv.4,14,22) and still condemned
   Him. He knew the truth but chose political safety. Where in your life are
   you choosing safety over truth?

3. The two thieves represent all of humanity — both guilty, both dying, both
   in the presence of Christ. One mocked; one believed. The difference was
   not their crime but their response to Jesus. What is your response?

4. Jesus told the weeping women "weep not for me, but weep for yourselves"
   (v.28). He was more concerned about their spiritual condition than His own
   physical suffering. How does this challenge your tendency to focus on your
   own problems rather than the spiritual needs of others?

5. Joseph of Arimathea "had not consented to the counsel and deed of them"
   (v.51). He was a dissenting voice in the Sanhedrin — a man who stood
   against the majority. When have you stood against the crowd for what was
   right? What did it cost you?
