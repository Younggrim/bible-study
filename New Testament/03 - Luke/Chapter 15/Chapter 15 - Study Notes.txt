Luke - Chapter 15
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
The heart of Luke's Gospel — Jesus tells three parables of God's relentless joy in recovering the lost: the Lost Sheep, the Lost Coin, and the Prodigal Son, revealing the Father who runs to embrace returning sinners.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Luke (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 15 is one of the most beloved chapters in the Bible
— containing three parables about lost things: the Lost Sheep (vv.3-7), the
Lost Coin (vv.8-10), and the Prodigal Son (vv.11-32). All three are told in
response to the Pharisees' complaint: "This man receiveth sinners, and eateth
with them" (v.2).

The Context: Tax collectors and sinners were drawing near to Jesus (v.1). The
Pharisees were grumbling about it (v.2). Jesus tells these three parables to
reveal God's heart — He is not reluctant to receive sinners; He actively seeks
them and rejoices when they return.

The Three Parables share a common structure:
  (1) Something valuable is lost
  (2) A diligent search is conducted
  (3) The lost is found
  (4) There is great rejoicing

The Prodigal Son (vv.11-32): The most famous parable in the world. It reveals
three characters: the younger son (the rebel who returns), the father (God's
extravagant grace), and the elder son (the religious person who resents grace).
The parable is really about the father — his patience, his watching, his running,
his restoration, and his pursuit of both sons.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- On the journey to Jerusalem: These three parables (lost sheep, lost coin, lost son) are told while Jesus travels toward Jerusalem, likely in Perea.
- "A far country" (v.13): The prodigal went to a distant land — representing spiritual distance from the Father. He returned home, representing repentance.
- The setting is domestic and agricultural — sheep on hillsides, coins in a house, pigs in fields — all reflecting the landscape of rural Palestine.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.7  — "There will be more joy in heaven over one sinner who repents than
        over ninety-nine righteous persons who need no repentance." (ESV) —
        Heaven's value system: one repentant sinner brings more joy than 99
        who don't think they need it.

v.10 — "There is joy before the angels of God over one sinner who repents."
        (ESV) — Angels rejoice when you repent.

v.20 — "And he arose and came to his father. But while he was still a long
        way off, his father saw him and felt compassion, and ran and embraced
        him and kissed him." (ESV) — The most beautiful picture of God's
        grace in all of Scripture.

v.24 — "For this my son was dead, and is alive again; he was lost, and is
        found." (ESV) — The father's declaration — death to life, lost to
        found.

v.31 — "Son, you are always with me, and all that is mine is yours." (ESV) —
        The father's assurance to the elder son — grace for the rebel doesn't
        diminish what belongs to the faithful.

  watching. He had been watching every day since the boy left. And when he

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.2  — NET: "This man welcomes sinners and eats with them" —
       the Pharisees' accusation that becomes Jesus' highest
       compliment. WEB: "This man welcomes sinners, and eats with
       them." All three parables respond to this one critique.

v.7  — NET: "there will be more joy in heaven over one sinner who
       repents than over ninety-nine righteous people who have no
       need to repent" — "more joy" — heaven's value system differs
       radically from earth's. WEB: "more joy in heaven over one
       sinner who repents." The party in heaven exceeds anything
       on earth.

v.12 — KJV: "give me the portion of goods that falleth to me" / ESV: "Father,
       give me the share of property that is coming to me" — Both equivalent.
       In Jewish culture, asking for your inheritance while your father was
       alive was equivalent to saying "I wish you were dead." It was the
       ultimate dishonor. The father's compliance shows extraordinary grace.

v.15 — KJV: "to feed swine" / ESV: "to feed pigs" — Both equivalent. For a
       Jewish man, this was the absolute bottom — pigs were unclean animals
       (Leviticus 11:7). Feeding them meant complete degradation and
       separation from everything Jewish.

v.17 — NET: "when he came to his senses he said, 'How many of my
       father's hired workers have food enough to spare'" — "came
       to his senses" (eis heauton elthōn = came to himself). KJV:
       "when he came to himself." WEB: "when he came to himself."
       Repentance begins as self-awareness — realizing where you
       actually are.

v.20 — KJV: "his father... ran" / ESV: "his father... ran" — Both identical.
       In ancient Middle Eastern culture, a dignified man never ran — it
       required lifting his robes and exposing his legs, which was shameful.
       The father's running is a deliberate act of absorbing the shame his
       son deserved.
       NET: "But while he was still a long way from home his
       father saw him, and was filled with compassion for him, and
       ran and embraced him and kissed him" — the father RUNS
       (unusual for a dignified elder in that culture). WEB: "ran,
       fell on his neck, and kissed him." ASV: "ran, and fell on
       his neck, and kissed him." The father's undignified love.

v.22 — KJV: "the best robe... a ring... shoes" / ESV: "the best robe... a
       ring... shoes" — Both identical. The robe = honor and restoration to
       family status. The ring = authority (a signet ring for conducting
       family business). Shoes = sonship (slaves went barefoot; sons wore
       shoes). Full restoration, not probation.

v.31 — NET: "Son, you are always with me, and everything that
       belongs to me is yours" — the father's gentle correction
       to the elder brother. WEB: "Son, you are always with me,
       and all that is mine is yours." The elder brother's problem
       isn't denied access — it's a servant mentality in a son's
       position.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Prodigal      — From Latin "prodigus" — wasteful, extravagant. The younger
               son wasted his inheritance in riotous living. But the word
               also applies to the father — whose grace is prodigally generous.

Husks         — The pods of the carob tree — used as animal feed. The prodigal
               was so desperate he envied the pigs' food.

Far country   — A place of distance from the father — both geographically and
               spiritually. Represents life lived apart from God.

Came to himself — The moment of clarity when the prodigal recognized his
               condition and remembered his father's house. The beginning of
               repentance.

Elder brother — The older son who stayed home, obeyed the rules, but resented
               the father's grace toward the rebel. Represents the Pharisees
               — religious, dutiful, but lacking the father's heart.

Fatted calf   — Reserved for the most special occasions. Killing it meant a
               community celebration — the father was publicly declaring his
               son's restoration.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. The father was watching for his son — "when he was yet a great way off,
   his father saw him" (v.20). God is watching for you. No matter how far
   you've wandered, He sees you the moment you turn toward home. Is there
   an area of your life where you need to "come to yourself" and turn back?

2. The father ran to his son — absorbing the shame, covering the disgrace,
   restoring fully before the son could finish his rehearsed speech. That is
   grace. Do you believe God receives you like that — with running, embracing,
   restoring love? Or do you think you need to earn your way back?

3. The elder brother was angry at the father's generosity (v.28). He had
   served faithfully but with a servant's heart, not a son's heart (v.29 —
   "these many years do I serve thee"). Are you serving God out of duty and
   resentment, or out of love and gratitude? Do you resent God's grace toward
   people you think don't deserve it?

4. "There is joy in heaven over one sinner that repenteth" (v.10). Your
   repentance causes celebration in heaven. When did you last repent of
   something specific? When did you last cause heaven to rejoice?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. The lost sheep wandered away. The lost coin was dropped. The lost son chose
   to leave. People are "lost" in different ways — some drift, some are
   neglected, some rebel. Which describes your story? Which describes people
   you know?

2. In all three parables, the seeker doesn't wait for the lost to come back
   — they go looking. God is a seeking God. How does knowing that God actively
   pursues the lost change how you view evangelism? Are you seeking, or just
   waiting?

3. The prodigal "came to himself" (v.17) in the pig pen. Sometimes God allows
   us to hit bottom so we'll look up. Has God ever used a "pig pen" moment
   in your life to bring you back? What was it?

4. The elder brother said "thou never gavest me a kid" (v.29) — but the father
   said "all that I have is thine" (v.31). The elder brother had everything
   but didn't enjoy it because he was focused on what his brother got. Where
   is comparison and resentment robbing you of the joy of what God has already
   given you?

5. The parable ends without telling us if the elder brother went in to the
   party (v.32). It's an open ending — directed at the Pharisees. The question
   hangs: will you join the celebration of grace, or will you stand outside
   in resentment? Which are you doing?
