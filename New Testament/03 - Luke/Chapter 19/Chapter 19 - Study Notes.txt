Luke - Chapter 19
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Jesus transforms Zacchaeus the tax collector ("salvation has come to this house"), tells the parable of the Ten Minas about faithful stewardship, enters Jerusalem weeping over the city, and cleanses the temple.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Luke (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 19 marks the end of Jesus' journey to Jerusalem
(begun in 9:51) and the beginning of Passion Week. It contains the story of
Zacchaeus (vv.1-10), the Parable of the Ten Minas (vv.11-27), the Triumphal
Entry (vv.28-40), Jesus weeping over Jerusalem (vv.41-44), and the Temple
cleansing (vv.45-48).

Zacchaeus (vv.1-10): A chief tax collector — wealthy from exploitation, hated
by his community. He climbed a tree to see Jesus — a humiliating act for a
wealthy man. Jesus called him by name and invited Himself to his house. The
result: immediate, radical repentance — giving half his goods to the poor and
restoring fourfold to anyone he had cheated. This is what genuine conversion
looks like.

Jesus Weeping Over Jerusalem (vv.41-44): Only recorded in Luke. As Jesus
approached Jerusalem in triumph, He wept — not tears of joy but of grief. He
saw what the crowd could not: the city's coming destruction (fulfilled in AD 70).
"If thou hadst known... the things which belong unto thy peace!" (v.42).

"The Son of man is come to seek and to save that which was lost" (v.10): The
mission statement of Luke's Gospel — and the summary of the Zacchaeus story.
Jesus doesn't wait for sinners to come to Him; He seeks them out.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Jericho (v.1): Jesus entered and passed through Jericho — about 15 miles east of Jerusalem. Zacchaeus climbed a sycamore tree here.
- Jerusalem / the Mount of Olives (vv.29-44): Jesus approached Jerusalem from the east, via Bethphage and Bethany on the Mount of Olives. He wept over Jerusalem as it came into view.
- The Temple (v.45): Jesus entered the Temple and drove out those buying and selling.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.5  — "Zacchaeus, hurry and come down, for I must stay at your house today."
        (ESV) — Jesus invites Himself into the life of a sinner.

v.8  — "Behold, Lord, the half of my goods I give to the poor. And if I have
        defrauded anyone of anything, I restore it fourfold." (ESV) — The
        fruit of genuine repentance: radical generosity.

v.10 — "For the Son of Man came to seek and to save the lost." (ESV) — The
        mission of Christ summarized.

v.40 — "I tell you, if these were silent, the very stones would cry out."
        (ESV) — Creation itself demands that Christ be praised.

v.42 — "Would that you, even you, had known on this day the things that make
        for peace!" (ESV) — Jesus' grief over missed opportunity.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.2  — KJV: "chief among the publicans" / ESV: "a chief tax collector" —
       Both equivalent. Greek "architelōnēs" — a chief tax collector,
       overseeing other collectors. A position of great wealth and great
       corruption. Only mentioned here in the NT.

v.8  — KJV: "I restore him fourfold" / ESV: "I restore it fourfold" — Both
       equivalent. Exceeding the Law's requirement (Exodus 22:1 required
       fourfold for stolen sheep; Numbers 5:7 required restitution plus
       one-fifth). Zacchaeus went beyond what was required.

v.13 — KJV: "ten pounds" / ESV: "ten minas" — KJV uses "pounds"; ESV
       transliterates the Greek "mnas" (minas). One mina = about 3 months'
       wages. Much less than a talent (Matthew 25). The point: even small
       resources must be invested faithfully.

v.40 — KJV: "the stones would immediately cry out" / ESV: "the very stones
       would cry out" — Both equivalent. If human voices are silenced,
       creation itself will testify to Christ's kingship. Praise of Christ is
       inevitable — the only question is who will offer it.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
v.5  — NET: "Zacchaeus, come down quickly, because I must stay
       at your house today" — "I must" (dei) — divine necessity.
       Jesus didn't wait to be invited. WEB: "I must stay at your
       house today." KJV: "I must abide at thy house." The Son of
       Man seeks — He doesn't wait to be sought.

v.8  — NET: "But Zacchaeus stopped and said to the Lord, 'Look,
       Lord, half of my possessions I now give to the poor, and if
       I have cheated anyone of anything, I am paying back four
       times as much!'" — present tense "I now give" — immediate
       action. WEB: "I give." KJV: "I give." Restitution as
       evidence of genuine repentance.

v.10 — NET: "For the Son of Man came to seek and to save the
       lost" — Luke's thesis statement for the entire Gospel. WEB:
       "For the Son of Man came to seek and to save that which was
       lost." KJV: "to seek and to save that which was lost." All
       versions — the mission in one sentence.

v.38 — NET: "Blessed is the king who comes in the name of the
       Lord!" — Luke adds "the king" to Psalm 118:26. WEB:
       "Blessed is the King who comes in the name of the Lord!"
       KJV: "Blessed be the King." Luke makes the royal claim
       explicit — this is a coronation procession.

v.41 — NET: "Now when Jesus approached and saw the city, he wept
       over it" — "wept" (eklausen) — not quiet tears but audible
       weeping/wailing. Only Luke records this. WEB: "he wept over
       it." The conquering King weeps for the city that will
       reject Him. Unique to Luke.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Zacchaeus     — A chief tax collector in Jericho — wealthy, short in stature,
               despised by his community. His name means "pure" or "righteous"
               — ironic given his profession.

Sycamore tree — A fig-mulberry tree with low branches, easy to climb. Common
               in Jericho. Zacchaeus climbed one to see over the crowd.

Mina/Pound    — A unit of money worth about 3 months' wages (100 drachmas).
               Much less than a talent. Each servant received the same amount
               — unlike the Talents parable.

Occupy        — KJV for "do business" or "trade" (Greek: pragmateusasthe).
               The master's instruction: put the money to work until I return.

Triumphal Entry — Jesus' entry into Jerusalem on a donkey, fulfilling
               Zechariah 9:9. The crowd acclaimed Him as King — but within
               days would cry "Crucify Him."

Visitation    — God's coming to His people — either in mercy or judgment.
               Jerusalem did not recognize "the time of thy visitation" (v.44)
               — and would be destroyed in AD 70.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Jesus sought out Zacchaeus — a man everyone else despised (v.5). He didn't
   wait for Zacchaeus to clean up his life first. He came to him in his sin.
   Do you believe Jesus seeks you out the same way — not waiting for you to
   be worthy but coming to you as you are?

2. Zacchaeus' repentance was immediate and radical — half his goods to the
   poor, fourfold restoration (v.8). Genuine conversion produces visible,
   costly change. What visible change has your faith produced in how you
   handle money, relationships, and integrity?

3. Jesus wept over Jerusalem (v.41) — a city that rejected Him. He didn't
   respond with anger but with grief. How do you respond to people who reject
   Christ — with frustration or with compassion?

4. "The Son of man is come to seek and to save that which was lost" (v.10).
   Jesus is a seeking Savior. Who in your life is "lost" that you need to
   actively seek out rather than passively wait for?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. Zacchaeus climbed a tree — a humiliating act for a wealthy man. He was
   willing to look foolish to see Jesus. What are you willing to do that
   might look foolish in order to get closer to Christ?

2. The crowd murmured that Jesus went to a sinner's house (v.7). They wanted
   Jesus to associate only with the respectable. Who do you think Jesus
   "shouldn't" associate with? How does His example challenge your categories?

3. The servant who hid his mina said "I feared thee" (v.21). Fear of the
   master produced inaction. Is your view of God producing bold action or
   fearful paralysis? What would change if you saw God as generous rather
   than harsh?

4. "If thou hadst known... the things which belong unto thy peace" (v.42).
   Jesus grieved over missed opportunity. Is there an opportunity for peace
   with God, peace with others, or peace in your circumstances that you're
   missing right now?

5. The stones would cry out if the people were silent (v.40). Praise of
   Christ is inevitable — the question is whether you'll be part of it.
   Is your life a testimony of praise, or are you silent while the stones
   are ready to speak?
