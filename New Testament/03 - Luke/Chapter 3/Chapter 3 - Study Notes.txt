Luke - Chapter 3
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
John the Baptist preaches repentance with specific ethical demands for each class of people, baptizes Jesus as heaven opens with the Father's voice and the Spirit's descent, and Luke traces Jesus' genealogy back through David to Adam and God.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Luke (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 3 records the ministry of John the Baptist and the
baptism of Jesus, followed by Luke's genealogy of Christ. Luke the historian
anchors the narrative in specific historical time — naming the emperor (Tiberius),
the governor (Pilate), the tetrarchs (Herod, Philip, Lysanias), and the high
priests (Annas and Caiaphas). This is approximately AD 26-27.

Luke's Genealogy (vv.23-38): Unlike Matthew's genealogy (which goes from Abraham
forward to Jesus through Joseph's legal line), Luke traces Jesus' lineage
backward from Jesus all the way to Adam and to God. Most scholars believe Luke
traces the biological line through Mary (Heli being Mary's father), while
Matthew traces the legal line through Joseph. Luke's genealogy emphasizes Jesus'
connection to all humanity — He is the Son of Adam, the Son of God.

John's Practical Ethics (vv.10-14): Unique to Luke — John gives specific,
practical instructions to different groups: the crowds (share your possessions),
tax collectors (don't extort), soldiers (don't abuse power). Repentance produces
concrete behavioral change, not just religious feeling.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- The Jordan River: John baptized in the region around the Jordan, likely near Bethany beyond the Jordan (Bethabara), in the lower Jordan Valley near Jericho.
- Luke's genealogy traces from Nazareth (where Jesus lived) back to Adam — spanning the entire geography of human history from Eden to Galilee.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.3  — "Proclaiming a baptism of repentance for the forgiveness of sins." (ESV) —
        John's message: repentance that leads to forgiveness.

v.8  — "Bear fruits in keeping with repentance." (ESV) — True repentance
        is visible in changed behavior.

v.16 — "He will baptize you with the Holy Spirit and fire." (ESV) — John
        points to the One who is infinitely greater.

v.22 — "You are my beloved Son; with you I am well pleased." (ESV) — The Father's
        declaration over Jesus — identity affirmed before ministry begins.

v.38 — "The son of Adam, the son of God." (ESV) — Luke traces
        Jesus' lineage to Adam — connecting Him to all humanity.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1  — KJV: "the fifteenth year of the reign of Tiberius Caesar" / ESV:
       "In the fifteenth year of the reign of Tiberius Caesar" — Both
       equivalent. This dates John's ministry to approximately AD 26-29
       (depending on how Tiberius' reign is calculated — from his co-regency
       in AD 12 or his sole rule in AD 14).

v.14 — KJV: "Do violence to no man, neither accuse any falsely; and be
       content with your wages" — ESV: "Do not extort money from anyone by
       threats or by false accusation, and be content with your wages." —
       Soldiers often supplemented their pay through intimidation and false
       charges. John doesn't tell them to quit — he tells them to serve
       with integrity.

v.22 — KJV: "Thou art my beloved Son" / ESV: "You are my beloved Son; with
       you I am well pleased" — Both equivalent. Luke uses "Thou art" (second
       person — spoken to Jesus) while Matthew uses "This is" (third person —
       spoken about Jesus to others). Both are accurate — the Father spoke to
       Jesus and about Jesus simultaneously.

v.23 — KJV: "being (as was supposed) the son of Joseph" / ESV: "being the
       son (as was supposed) of Joseph, the son of Heli" — Both equivalent.
       The parenthetical "(as was supposed)" is Luke's way of noting that
       Joseph was not Jesus' biological father. The genealogy that follows is
       likely Mary's line.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
v.4  — NET: "a voice of one shouting in the wilderness:
       'Prepare the way for the Lord'" — all translations quote
       Isaiah 40:3. Luke uniquely extends the Isaiah quotation
       through v.6 ("all flesh shall see the salvation of God")
       — reflecting his universal emphasis.

v.8  — NET: "produce fruit that proves your repentance" — "fruit
       that proves" is interpretive. KJV: "fruits worthy of
       repentance." WEB: "fruits worthy of repentance." ASV:
       "fruits worthy of repentance." NET makes the evidentiary
       nature of fruit explicit.

v.14 — NET: "Some soldiers also asked him, 'And as for us—what
       should we do?' He told them, 'Take money from no one by
       violence'" — "take money from no one by violence" (mēdena
       diaseisēte). KJV: "Do violence to no man." WEB: "Extort
       from no one by violence." The specific military corruption
       of extortion through intimidation.

v.22 — NET: "the Holy Spirit descended on him in bodily form like
       a dove" — "in bodily form" is unique to Luke's account —
       the Spirit's descent was visible, not merely spiritual.
       WEB: "in bodily form like a dove." ASV: "in a bodily form,
       as a dove."

v.38 — NET: "the son of Enosh, the son of Seth, the son of Adam,
       the son of God" — Luke traces Jesus' genealogy to Adam and
       then to God — asserting Jesus' connection to all humanity,
       not just Israel. WEB: "the son of Adam, the son of God."
       This universalism is distinctly Lukan.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Tetrarch      — Literally "ruler of a fourth." A title for a subordinate
               ruler governing part of a larger territory under Roman authority.

Census/Taxing — A Roman registration for taxation purposes. Required people
               to return to their ancestral city.

Baptism of repentance — Immersion in water as a public declaration of turning
               from sin. John's baptism was preparatory — pointing forward to
               Christ's baptism with the Spirit.

Publicans     — Tax collectors who worked for Rome. They bid for the right to
               collect taxes and kept anything above the required amount —
               making them wealthy through exploitation.

Winnowing fork — A tool used to throw grain into the air so wind separates
               wheat (valuable) from chaff (worthless). A picture of judgment.

Son of Adam   — Luke's genealogy ends with "Adam, which was the son of God"
               — connecting Jesus to all humanity, not just Israel.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. John gave different instructions to different groups (vv.10-14) — practical,
   specific, tailored to their situation. Repentance looks different for
   different people. What does repentance look like specifically in YOUR life,
   in YOUR situation, with YOUR particular temptations?

2. John told the soldiers "be content with your wages" (v.14). Discontentment
   drives much of our sin — greed, envy, dishonesty. Where is discontentment
   driving sinful behavior in your life? What would contentment look like?

3. The Father said "Thou art my beloved Son; in thee I am well pleased" (v.22)
   — before Jesus had done anything publicly. Your identity in Christ is not
   based on your performance. Do you live from approval or for approval?

4. Luke traces Jesus' lineage to Adam (v.38) — connecting Him to all humanity.
   Jesus is not just the Jewish Messiah but the Savior of the world. How does
   the universal scope of the Gospel affect how you view people who are
   different from you?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. Luke anchors his narrative in specific historical facts (v.1-2). Christianity
   is not a myth or philosophy — it is rooted in real history. How does the
   historical grounding of the Gospel strengthen your confidence in its truth?

2. John told people with two coats to give one away (v.11). Simple, practical
   generosity. What do you have two of that someone else has none of? What
   would it look like to share it this week?

3. John rebuked Herod for his sin (v.19) — and it cost him his freedom and
   eventually his life. Speaking truth to power is costly. Is there a situation
   where you need to speak truth even though it will cost you something?

4. The genealogy goes all the way back to "Adam, which was the son of God"
   (v.38). Every human being is descended from Adam — made in God's image.
   How does this truth shape how you treat every person you encounter,
   regardless of their background?

5. Jesus was "about thirty years of age" (v.23) when He began His ministry —
   30 years of preparation for 3 years of public work. God often prepares us
   longer than we expect. Are you in a season of preparation? How are you
   stewarding it?
