Luke - Chapter 5
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Jesus calls Simon Peter after a miraculous catch of fish, cleanses a leper, heals a paralytic lowered through the roof (declaring his sins forgiven), calls Levi, and teaches that new wine requires new wineskins.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Luke (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 5 records the calling of the first disciples through
the miraculous catch of fish (vv.1-11), the healing of a leper (vv.12-16), the
healing of the paralytic lowered through the roof (vv.17-26), and the calling
of Levi/Matthew (vv.27-39). Each event reveals something about who Jesus calls
and how He transforms them.

The Miraculous Catch: Peter was an experienced fisherman who had fished all
night and caught nothing. Jesus — a carpenter — told him to try again. Peter's
response "at thy word I will let down the net" (v.5) is the essence of faith:
obeying God's word even when experience says otherwise. The result overwhelmed
him — and his response was not joy but conviction: "Depart from me; for I am
a sinful man" (v.8). Encountering Christ's power reveals our own unworthiness.

The Paralytic: Four friends carried a paralyzed man to Jesus, and when they
couldn't get through the crowd, they dug through the roof. Jesus saw "their
faith" (v.20) — the faith of the friends, not just the sick man. Then He said
something unexpected: "Thy sins are forgiven thee" — addressing the deeper need
before the physical one.

Levi's Calling: Matthew/Levi was a tax collector — despised, wealthy from
exploitation, and considered a traitor. Jesus said "Follow me" and Levi "left
all" (v.28). Then Levi threw a party for his sinner friends to meet Jesus.
That's evangelism — introducing your world to Christ.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- The Lake of Gennesaret (Sea of Galilee, v.1): Jesus taught from Simon's boat on the lake, then called Peter after the miraculous catch of fish.
- Capernaum (implied): Levi's tax booth was likely on the Via Maris trade route near Capernaum. The paralytic was also healed in a house here.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.5  — "Master, we toiled all night and took nothing! But at your word I will
        let down the nets." (ESV) — Faith that obeys God's word despite
        contrary experience.

v.8  — "Depart from me, for I am a sinful man, O Lord." (ESV) — The proper
        response to encountering Christ's holiness — awareness of our own
        sinfulness.

v.20 — "When he saw their faith, he said, 'Man, your sins are forgiven
        you.'" (ESV) — Jesus addresses the deeper need first.

v.31-32 — "Those who are well have no need of a physician, but those who are
        sick. I have not come to call the righteous but sinners to
        repentance." (ESV) — Jesus' mission defined: He came for the broken,
        not the self-righteous.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1  — KJV: "lake of Gennesaret" / ESV: "lake of Gennesaret" — Both
       identical. Another name for the Sea of Galilee (also called Sea of
       Tiberias in John). Gennesaret refers to the fertile plain on its
       northwest shore.

v.12 — KJV: "a man full of leprosy" / ESV: "a man full of leprosy" — Both
       identical. Luke the physician notes the severity — "full of leprosy"
       indicates advanced disease. Only Luke uses this medical precision.

v.19 — KJV: "through the tiling" / ESV: "through the tiles" — Both
       equivalent. Luke says tiles; Mark says they "uncovered the roof"
       (Mark 2:4). Palestinian houses had flat roofs made of beams, branches,
       and packed earth — sometimes with tiles. Both descriptions are accurate
       for different construction styles.

v.39 — KJV: "No man also having drunk old wine straightway desireth new: for
       he saith, The old is better." / ESV: "And no one after drinking old
       wine desires new, for he says, 'The old is good.'" — KJV: "better" /
       ESV: "good" — Greek "chrēstos" can mean either. Only in Luke. Jesus
       acknowledges that people prefer the familiar — the old religious system
       feels comfortable. But the new wine of the kingdom requires new
       containers.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
v.5  — NET: "Master, we worked hard all night and caught
       nothing! But at your word I will lower the nets" — "Master"
       (epistatēs) — a term of respect unique to Luke, different
       from "Rabbi" or "Lord." WEB: "Master." KJV: "Master." It
       implies authority over the team.

v.8  — NET: "Go away from me, Lord, for I am a sinful man" —
       Peter's instinctive response to holiness. WEB: "Depart from
       me, for I am a sinful man, Lord." The encounter with divine
       power reveals human unworthiness. ASV: "Depart from me;
       for I am a sinful man, O Lord."

v.20 — NET: "Friend, your sins are forgiven" — "Friend" (anthrōpe
       = "man/fellow") is Luke's distinctive address. KJV: "Man,
       thy sins are forgiven thee." WEB: "Man, your sins are
       forgiven you." More intimate than Matthew/Mark's parallel.

v.26 — NET: "Then astonishment seized them all, and they glorified
       God and were filled with fear, saying, 'We have seen
       incredible things today'" — "incredible things" (paradoxa)
       = paradoxes/things contrary to expectation. KJV: "strange
       things." WEB: "strange things."

v.39 — NET: "No one after drinking old wine wants the new, for he
       says, 'The old is good enough'" — unique to Luke. A warning:
       people comfortable with the old system resist Jesus' new
       wine. WEB: "The old is better." NET's "good enough" captures
       the complacency.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Gennesaret    — The fertile plain on the northwest shore of the Sea of Galilee.
               Also used as a name for the lake itself.

Leprosy       — In biblical usage, a broad term covering various skin diseases
               (not limited to Hansen's disease). Lepers were socially isolated,
               ritually unclean, and forbidden from community life.

Palsy         — KJV term for paralysis — inability to move part or all of the
               body.

Publican      — A tax collector working for Rome. Publicans bid for the right
               to collect taxes and kept anything above the required amount.
               Despised as traitors and extortioners.

Wineskins     — Animal skins (usually goat) used to store wine. New wine
               ferments and expands — requiring flexible new skins. Old,
               brittle skins would burst.

Physician     — Jesus uses this metaphor for Himself — He came as a doctor
               for the spiritually sick, not for those who think they're well.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Peter obeyed Jesus' word despite his professional expertise saying otherwise
   (v.5). When God's Word contradicts your experience or logic, which do you
   follow? Where is God asking you to "let down your net" in an area where
   you've already tried and failed?

2. Peter's response to the miracle was not celebration but conviction — "I am
   a sinful man" (v.8). Genuine encounters with Christ's power reveal our own
   unworthiness. When did you last feel the weight of your own sinfulness in
   God's presence? Has familiarity dulled your awareness?

3. The friends of the paralytic wouldn't let obstacles stop them — they dug
   through a roof (v.19). Jesus saw "their faith." Who in your life needs you
   to carry them to Jesus? What obstacles are you willing to overcome to get
   them there?

4. Jesus said "I came not to call the righteous, but sinners" (v.32). If you
   think you're too good for grace, you'll never receive it. If you know
   you're sick, the Physician is available. Which posture describes you today?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. After the miraculous catch, Peter, James, and John "forsook all, and
   followed him" (v.11). They left a boat full of fish — their biggest haul
   ever — to follow Jesus. What would you have to leave at the peak of its
   value to follow Christ more fully?

2. Jesus touched the leper (v.13) — something no one else would do. The leper
   was untouchable. Who are the "untouchables" in your world — people others
   avoid? What would it look like to reach out and touch their lives?

3. Jesus forgave the paralytic's sins before healing his body (v.20). He
   addressed the deeper need first. Are you more focused on God fixing your
   circumstances or transforming your heart? Which does He prioritize?

4. Levi threw a party to introduce his friends to Jesus (v.29). He used his
   existing relationships for evangelism. Who in your social circle doesn't
   know Christ? What would it look like to create an opportunity for them to
   encounter Him?

5. "New wine must be put into new bottles" (v.38). Jesus' kingdom doesn't fit
   into old religious structures. Where are you trying to fit the new work God
   is doing into old patterns, old habits, or old ways of thinking?
