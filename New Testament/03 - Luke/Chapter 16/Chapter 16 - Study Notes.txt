Luke - Chapter 16
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Jesus tells the parable of the Shrewd Manager (use earthly wealth for eternal purposes), rebukes the money-loving Pharisees, affirms the permanence of the Law, and tells of the Rich Man and Lazarus — revealing the eternal consequences of ignoring the poor.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Luke (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 16 contains two of Jesus' most challenging teachings:
the Parable of the Unjust Steward (vv.1-13) and the account of the Rich Man
and Lazarus (vv.19-31). Between them, Jesus addresses the Pharisees' love of
money (vv.14-18). Both stories deal with how we use wealth and its eternal
consequences.

The Unjust Steward (vv.1-13): One of the most puzzling parables in the Gospels.
A dishonest manager, about to be fired, reduces his master's debtors' bills to
secure future friendships. The master commends his shrewdness (not his dishonesty).
Jesus' point: worldly people are more strategic about their future than God's
people are about eternity. Use your resources wisely for eternal purposes.

The Rich Man and Lazarus (vv.19-31): Not called a parable — Jesus names the
beggar "Lazarus" (the only named character in any parable). It may be a true
account. It reveals the reality of conscious existence after death, the
irreversibility of eternal destiny, and the sufficiency of Scripture ("Moses
and the prophets") for producing faith.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- On the journey to Jerusalem: These teachings continue as Jesus travels toward Jerusalem, likely through Perea (east of the Jordan).
- "A far country" (v.12): The Parable of the Rich Man and Lazarus references no specific geography but the "great gulf fixed" (v.26) between the realms of the dead.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.10 — "One who is faithful in a very little is also faithful in much." (ESV)
        — Faithfulness in small things reveals character for larger things.

v.13 — "No servant can serve two masters... You cannot serve God and money."
        (ESV) — The impossibility of divided loyalty.

v.15 — "What is exalted among men is an abomination in the sight of God."
        (ESV) — God's values are often the inverse of the world's.

v.26 — "Between us and you a great chasm has been fixed." (ESV) — The finality
        of eternal destiny — no second chances after death.

v.31 — "If they do not hear Moses and the Prophets, neither will they be
        convinced if someone should rise from the dead." (ESV) — Scripture is
        sufficient for faith; miracles alone don't produce repentance.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.8  — KJV: "the lord commended the unjust steward" / ESV: "The master
       commended the dishonest manager" — KJV: "unjust steward" / ESV:
       "dishonest manager" — both equivalent. "The lord" here is the master
       in the parable, not Jesus. He commended the steward's shrewdness
       (strategic thinking), not his dishonesty.

v.9  — KJV: "mammon of unrighteousness" / ESV: "unrighteous wealth" — Both
       equivalent. Money is called "unrighteous" not because it is inherently
       evil but because it belongs to this fallen world system. Use it
       strategically for eternal purposes.

v.22 — KJV: "Abraham's bosom" / ESV: "Abraham's side" — Both equivalent.
       A Jewish expression for the place of comfort where the righteous dead
       await resurrection. Also called "paradise" (Luke 23:43).

v.23 — KJV: "in hell" / ESV: "in Hades, being in torment" — KJV renders
       "hadēs" as "hell"; ESV transliterates it. Greek "hadēs" — the realm
       of the dead. In this context, the place of torment for the unrighteous.
       Distinguished from "gehenna" (the final lake of fire) — hadēs is the
       intermediate state before final judgment.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
v.8  — NET: "the master commended the dishonest manager because
       he acted shrewdly" — the most puzzling parable in the
       Gospels. "Shrewdly" (phronimōs = prudently/wisely). WEB:
       "his lord commended the dishonest manager because he had
       done wisely." Jesus commends the strategy, not the ethics.

v.9  — NET: "make friends for yourselves by how you use worldly
       wealth, so that when it runs out you will be welcomed into
       the eternal homes" — "worldly wealth" (mamōna tēs adikias =
       mammon of unrighteousness). KJV: "mammon of unrighteousness."
       WEB: "unrighteous mammon." Use temporal resources for
       eternal purposes.

v.15 — NET: "what is highly prized among people is utterly
       detestable in God's sight" — strong language. KJV:
       "abomination in the sight of God." WEB: "what is exalted
       among men is an abomination in the sight of God." Human
       values inverted — God's economy operates on opposite
       principles.

v.22 — NET: "The poor man died and was carried by the angels to
       Abraham's side" — "Abraham's side" (kolpon Abraam = bosom
       of Abraham). KJV: "Abraham's bosom." WEB: "Abraham's bosom."
       NET modernizes the idiom. The image is of reclining at the
       heavenly banquet next to Abraham.

v.31 — NET: "If they do not respond to Moses and the prophets,
       they will not be convinced even if someone rises from the
       dead" — prophetic irony: Jesus WILL rise, and many still
       won't believe. WEB: "neither will they be persuaded if one
       rises from the dead." The sufficiency of Scripture.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Steward       — A manager entrusted with another's property. In the parable,
               he represents anyone who manages God's resources (which is
               everyone — we own nothing; we manage everything).

Mammon        — Aramaic word for wealth/money, personified as a rival god.
               You either serve God or serve money — never both.

Abraham's bosom — A Jewish expression for the place of comfort and rest
               where the righteous dead await the resurrection.

Hades         — Greek for the realm of the dead. In this passage, it includes
               a place of torment (for the rich man) separated by an
               impassable gulf from the place of comfort (for Lazarus).

Great gulf    — The fixed, impassable barrier between the righteous and
               unrighteous after death. Destiny is sealed at death.

Moses and the prophets — The Old Testament Scriptures. Abraham says these
               are sufficient to produce faith — if people won't believe
               Scripture, they won't believe even a resurrection.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "He that is faithful in that which is least is faithful also in much"
   (v.10). How are you handling the "small" things God has entrusted to you
   — your daily responsibilities, your finances, your time? Your faithfulness
   there determines what God will entrust to you next.

2. "Ye cannot serve God and mammon" (v.13). This is not about having money
   — it's about what has you. Is money a tool you use for God's purposes, or
   is it a master that drives your decisions? Which one are you serving?

3. The rich man ignored Lazarus at his gate every day (vv.19-21). He didn't
   abuse him — he just didn't see him. Who is at your "gate" — in your daily
   path — that you're walking past without seeing?

4. "If they hear not Moses and the prophets, neither will they be persuaded,
   though one rose from the dead" (v.31). Scripture is sufficient for faith.
   Are you waiting for some dramatic sign before you fully obey, or are you
   responding to what God has already revealed in His Word?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. The unjust steward was commended for being strategic about his future (v.8).
   Jesus says worldly people are more strategic about temporal things than
   believers are about eternal things. How strategic are you about eternity?
   Are you investing your resources for eternal return?

2. "That which is highly esteemed among men is abomination in the sight of
   God" (v.15). What does the world esteem that God considers abominable?
   Where are you pursuing things the world values that God doesn't?

3. The rich man in torment wanted to warn his brothers (v.28). He finally
   cared about others — but too late. Who in your life needs to be warned
   about eternity? Are you telling them now, while there's still time?

4. The great gulf is "fixed" (v.26) — no crossing after death. How does the
   finality of eternal destiny affect your urgency about your own faith and
   the faith of those you love?

5. Abraham said "They have Moses and the prophets; let them hear them" (v.29).
   God has given us His Word — it is enough. Are you hearing it? Are you
   letting it change you? Or are you waiting for something more dramatic?
