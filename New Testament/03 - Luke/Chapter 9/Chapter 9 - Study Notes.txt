Luke - Chapter 9
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Jesus sends the twelve on mission, feeds five thousand, draws Peter's confession, is transfigured in glory, predicts His death, and sets His face toward Jerusalem — teaching that following Him requires daily cross-bearing.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Luke (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 9 is a pivotal chapter in Luke — it marks the
transition from Jesus' Galilean ministry to His journey toward Jerusalem and
the cross. It contains the sending of the twelve (vv.1-6), the feeding of the
5,000 (vv.10-17), Peter's confession (vv.18-20), the first passion prediction
(vv.21-22), the cost of discipleship (vv.23-27), the Transfiguration (vv.28-36),
and the beginning of the travel narrative (v.51).

"He stedfastly set his face to go to Jerusalem" (v.51): This is the turning
point of Luke's Gospel. From here until chapter 19, Jesus is on the road to
Jerusalem — and everything He teaches is colored by the cross that awaits Him.
Luke's "travel narrative" (9:51-19:27) is the longest section unique to his
Gospel.

The Cost of Discipleship (vv.57-62): Three would-be followers approach Jesus.
Each has a condition: "wherever you go," "let me first bury my father," "let
me first say goodbye." Jesus' responses are startling in their directness —
following Him requires total, immediate, unconditional commitment.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Bethsaida (v.10): A fishing village on the northeast shore of the Sea of Galilee. The 5,000 were fed in a desert place nearby.
- The mountain of Transfiguration (v.28): A high mountain where Jesus was transfigured. Likely Mount Hermon near Caesarea Philippi.
- Jerusalem (v.51): "He stedfastly set his face to go to Jerusalem." This verse marks the turning point — Jesus begins His deliberate final journey south.
- A Samaritan village (v.52): Jesus was rejected while passing through Samaria on the way to Jerusalem.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.20 — "But who do you say that I am? And Peter answered, 'The Christ of
        God.'" (ESV) — The central confession of the Gospel.

v.23 — "If anyone would come after me, let him deny himself and take up his
        cross daily and follow me." (ESV) — Luke adds "daily" — the cross is
        not a one-time event but a daily posture.

v.24 — "For whoever would save his life will lose it, but whoever loses his
        life for my sake will save it." (ESV) — The paradox of the kingdom.

v.51 — "He set his face to go to Jerusalem." (ESV) — Resolute,
        determined, unwavering — Jesus chose the cross deliberately.

v.62 — "No one who puts his hand to the plow and looks back is fit for the
        kingdom of God." (ESV) — Total commitment without reservation.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.23 — KJV: "take up his cross daily" / ESV: "take up his cross daily" —
       Both identical. Only Luke includes "daily" (Greek: kath' hēmeran).
       Matthew and Mark have "take up his cross" without "daily." Luke
       emphasizes the ongoing, repeated nature of self-denial — it is not a
       one-time act but a daily choice.

v.31 — KJV: "spake of his decease which he should accomplish at Jerusalem"
       / ESV: "spoke of his departure, which he was about to accomplish at
       Jerusalem" — KJV: "decease" / ESV: "departure" — Greek "exodon" —
       literally "exodus." Luke uses the word for Israel's departure from
       Egypt to describe Jesus' death — His death is a new exodus, a
       liberation from bondage.

v.51 — KJV: "he stedfastly set his face" / ESV: "he set his face" — Both
       equivalent. Greek "to prosōpon estērisen" — He hardened/fixed His face.
       An echo of Isaiah 50:7 — "I set my face like flint." This is resolute,
       determined commitment to the Father's will despite knowing what awaits.

v.60 — KJV: "Let the dead bury their dead" / ESV: "Leave the dead to bury
       their own dead" — Both equivalent. A shocking statement. "The
       spiritually dead can handle earthly obligations; you are called to
       something higher." Jesus is not being callous but establishing the
       absolute priority of the kingdom.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
v.23 — NET: "If anyone wants to become my follower, he must
       deny himself, take up his cross daily, and follow me" —
       "daily" is unique to Luke's version (not in Matthew/Mark).
       WEB: "take up his cross daily." The cross is not a one-time
       decision but a daily discipline.

v.29 — NET: "As he was praying, the appearance of his face was
       transformed" — the transfiguration happens WHILE PRAYING
       (unique Lukan detail). WEB: "as he was praying, the
       appearance of his face was altered." Luke connects prayer
       to every major event in Jesus' life.

v.31 — NET: "they spoke of his departure that he was about to
       carry out at Jerusalem" — "departure" (exodon = exodus).
       WEB: "his departure, which he was about to accomplish at
       Jerusalem." Jesus' death is a new Exodus — liberation from
       slavery to sin, not Egypt.

v.51 — NET: "he determined to go to Jerusalem" — literally "he
       set his face" (to prosōpon estērisen). KJV: "he stedfastly
       set his face to go to Jerusalem." WEB: "set his face to go
       to Jerusalem." The travel narrative (9:51-19:27) — unique
       to Luke — begins here. Determination toward the cross.

v.58 — NET: "Foxes have dens and the birds in the sky have nests,
       but the Son of Man has no place to lay his head" — "birds
       in the sky" vs. KJV "birds of the air." WEB: "the birds of
       the sky." The homelessness of God incarnate.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Apostles      — "Sent ones" — the twelve chosen by Jesus and given authority
               to preach, heal, and cast out demons on His behalf.

Transfiguration — The event where Jesus' divine glory became visible — His
               face altered, His clothes dazzling white. Moses and Elijah
               appeared with Him, discussing His "exodus" at Jerusalem.

Decease/Exodus — Greek "exodos" — departure. Luke's word for Jesus' death,
               connecting it to Israel's liberation from Egypt. The cross is
               the ultimate exodus — liberation from sin and death.

Plough        — A farming implement pulled through soil to prepare it for
               planting. Requires looking forward to make straight furrows.
               Looking back = unfitness for the kingdom.

Travel narrative — Luke 9:51-19:27 — the longest section unique to Luke,
               recording Jesus' journey from Galilee to Jerusalem with
               extensive teaching along the way.

Sons of thunder — James and John's nickname (Mark 3:17). Their desire to
               call fire on the Samaritans (v.54) shows why Jesus gave them
               this name — and why He rebuked them.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Take up his cross daily" (v.23). Luke adds "daily" — this is not a
   one-time decision but a daily discipline. What does taking up your cross
   look like TODAY — this morning, in your specific circumstances?

2. "He stedfastly set his face to go to Jerusalem" (v.51). Jesus knew what
   awaited Him and went anyway — with resolve, not reluctance. What difficult
   thing is God calling you toward that you need to face with the same
   determination?

3. James and John wanted to call fire on the Samaritans who rejected Jesus
   (v.54). Jesus rebuked them — "Ye know not what manner of spirit ye are of"
   (v.55). When people reject you or your faith, is your instinct retaliation
   or grace? What spirit are you operating from?

4. The three would-be followers each had a "but first" (vv.57-62). What is
   your "but first" — the condition you've placed on following Jesus fully?
   What would unconditional commitment look like for you?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. Jesus sent the twelve with "nothing for your journey" (v.3) — no staff,
   no bag, no bread, no money. He wanted them to depend entirely on God's
   provision. How dependent are you on God, or have you built so many safety
   nets that you don't need faith?

2. Peter confessed "The Christ of God" (v.20) — and immediately Jesus
   predicted His suffering and death (v.22). The Messiah is a suffering
   Messiah. How does the reality of a suffering Savior challenge your
   expectations of what following Him should look like?

3. "Whosoever will save his life shall lose it" (v.24). The man who plays it
   safe, who protects himself at all costs, who avoids risk — loses everything.
   Where are you playing it too safe with your faith?

4. "The Son of man hath not where to lay his head" (v.58). Following Jesus
   may mean giving up comfort and security. Are you willing to follow Him
   even if it costs you material comfort? What would that look like?

5. "No man, having put his hand to the plough, and looking back, is fit for
   the kingdom" (v.62). What are you looking back at — a former life, a past
   relationship, a previous comfort — that is keeping you from moving forward
   with Christ?
