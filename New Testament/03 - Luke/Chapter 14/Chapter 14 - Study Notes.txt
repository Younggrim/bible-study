Luke - Chapter 14
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Jesus heals on the Sabbath at a Pharisee's dinner, teaches on humility through the parable of the wedding feast, tells the parable of the Great Banquet where the outcasts fill the table, and lays out the radical cost of discipleship.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Luke (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 14 takes place at a Sabbath dinner in a Pharisee's
home. Jesus uses the social setting to teach about humility (vv.7-11), generosity
(vv.12-14), the Great Banquet parable (vv.15-24), and the radical cost of
discipleship (vv.25-35). Every teaching subverts the social norms of the room.

The Great Banquet (vv.15-24): A man prepares a feast and invites guests. They
all make excuses — land, oxen, a new wife. The host then invites the poor,
maimed, lame, and blind — and finally sends servants to the highways and hedges
to "compel them to come in." The parable illustrates Israel's rejection of
God's invitation and the Gospel going to the marginalized and Gentiles.

Counting the Cost (vv.25-35): Jesus turns to the "great multitudes" following
Him and delivers His most demanding teaching on discipleship. Three times He
says "cannot be my disciple" (vv.26,27,33). He uses two illustrations — a
builder and a king — to make the point: don't start what you can't finish.
Count the cost before you commit.

"Hate" Father and Mother (v.26): A Semitic idiom for "love less by comparison."
Jesus is not commanding literal hatred but establishing that loyalty to Him must
surpass every other loyalty — including family.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- A Pharisee's house (v.1): Jesus dined at the house of a Pharisee ruler — likely in Perea or southern Galilee, on the journey to Jerusalem.
- The highways and hedges (v.23): The parable pictures God's invitation extending beyond the city (Israel) to the roads and countryside (Gentiles).

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.11 — "For everyone who exalts himself will be humbled, and he who humbles
        himself will be exalted." (ESV) — The universal kingdom principle.

v.23 — "Go out to the highways and hedges and compel people to come in, that
        my house may be filled." (ESV) — The urgency and scope of the Gospel
        invitation.

v.26 — "If anyone comes to me and does not hate his own father and mother and
        wife and children... he cannot be my disciple." (ESV) — The absolute
        priority of Christ over every relationship.

v.27 — "Whoever does not bear his own cross and come after me cannot be my
        disciple." (ESV) — No cross, no discipleship.

v.33 — "Any one of you who does not renounce all that he has cannot be my
        disciple." (ESV) — Total surrender is the entry requirement.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.26 — KJV: "hate not his father, and mother" — ESV: "does not hate his own
       father and mother" — Greek "miseō" — in Semitic usage, this means
       "love less" (compare Genesis 29:31 where Leah is "hated" = loved less
       than Rachel). Jesus is not commanding emotional hatred but establishing
       priority — He must come first, above all.

v.23 — KJV: "compel them to come in" / ESV: "compel people to come in" —
       Both equivalent. Greek "anankason" — to urge strongly, to constrain by
       persuasion. Not physical force but urgent, passionate invitation.
       Augustine unfortunately used this verse to justify forced conversion —
       a misuse of the text.

v.28 — KJV: "counteth the cost" / ESV: "count the cost" — Both equivalent.
       Greek "psēphizei" — to calculate with pebbles (the ancient calculator).
       Jesus wants deliberate, eyes-open commitment — not impulsive enthusiasm.

v.34 — KJV: "if the salt have lost his savour" / ESV: "if salt has lost its
       taste" — Both equivalent. Salt in Palestine was often impure (mixed
       with gypsum). It could lose its saltiness, becoming useless. A disciple
       who loses his distinctiveness is worthless.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
v.11 — NET: "For everyone who exalts himself will be humbled,
       and the one who humbles himself will be exalted" — a Lukan
       refrain (also 18:14). WEB: "whoever exalts himself will be
       humbled, and whoever humbles himself will be exalted." The
       kingdom inverts earthly status.

v.18 — NET: "But one after another they all began to make excuses"
       — "make excuses" (paraiteomai = to beg off). KJV: "with one
       consent began to make excuse." WEB: "they all as one began
       to make excuses." The unanimity of refusal — three excuses
       (land, oxen, wife) representing possessions, business, family.

v.26 — NET: "If anyone comes to me and does not hate his own
       father and mother" — "hate" (miseo) — Semitic hyperbole for
       "love less by comparison." WEB: "doesn't disregard his own
       father." NET/ESV preserve the shocking word; WEB softens it.
       The demand is for supreme loyalty, not emotional hatred.

v.28 — NET: "For which of you, wanting to build a tower, doesn't
       first sit down and compute the cost" — "compute the cost"
       (psēphizei tēn dapanēn). KJV: "counteth the cost." WEB:
       "counts the cost." Discipleship requires rational assessment
       before emotional commitment.

v.33 — NET: "any one of you who does not renounce all his own
       possessions cannot be my disciple" — "renounce" (apotassetai
       = say farewell to/take leave of). KJV: "forsaketh not all."
       WEB: "renounces all that he has." Total surrender is the
       entry fee — not optional generosity.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Dropsy        — An old medical term for edema — fluid retention causing
               swelling. The man Jesus healed at the Pharisee's dinner (v.2).

Chief rooms   — The places of honor at a banquet — closest to the host.
               The Pharisees competed for these positions.

Great Banquet — A parable representing God's invitation to the kingdom.
               The original guests (Israel) refuse; the invitation goes to
               the marginalized and outsiders.

Highways and hedges — The roads and field boundaries where the poorest and
               most marginalized people would be found — beggars, travelers,
               the homeless.

Count the cost — To calculate what following Christ will require before
               committing. Jesus wants informed, deliberate disciples —
               not impulsive followers who quit.

Forsake all   — To renounce ownership of everything — holding all possessions
               loosely, available for God's purposes. Not necessarily giving
               everything away but surrendering the right to keep it.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Jesus taught humility at a dinner where everyone was jockeying for position
   (vv.7-11). As a man, where do you compete for status — at work, in your
   church, in social settings? What would it look like to deliberately choose
   the lowest place?

2. "Call the poor, the maimed, the lame, the blind" (v.13) — invite people
   who can't repay you. Our natural tendency is to invest in relationships
   that benefit us. Who could you serve or invite this week who has nothing
   to offer you in return?

3. The invited guests all made excuses (vv.18-20) — land, oxen, a wife.
   Legitimate things that became barriers to the kingdom. What legitimate
   things in your life are functioning as excuses for not responding fully
   to God's invitation?

4. "Whosoever he be of you that forsaketh not all that he hath, he cannot be
   my disciple" (v.33). Have you counted the cost? Have you looked at what
   following Jesus requires — everything — and said yes? Or are you still
   negotiating terms?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. Jesus says "hate" father, mother, wife, children (v.26). In practice, what
   does it look like to love Christ more than family without neglecting family?
   How do you hold both loyalties?

2. The builder who doesn't count the cost becomes a laughingstock (vv.28-30).
   The king who doesn't assess his strength loses the war (vv.31-32). What
   does "counting the cost" look like practically for you right now? What
   will following Christ cost you?

3. The Great Banquet parable shows that God's invitation is rejected by the
   comfortable and accepted by the desperate. Which are you — comfortable
   enough to make excuses, or desperate enough to come running?

4. "Salt is good: but if the salt have lost his savour" (v.34). A disciple
   who loses his distinctiveness is useless. What makes you distinct as a
   follower of Christ? Is that distinctiveness growing or fading?

5. Three times Jesus says "cannot be my disciple" (vv.26,27,33). He's not
   trying to discourage people — He's being honest about what's required.
   Do you meet His conditions? Where do you fall short? What needs to change?
