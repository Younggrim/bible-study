Luke - Chapter 10
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Jesus sends seventy-two disciples ahead, pronounces woe on unrepentant cities, rejoices that truth is revealed to babes, tells the parable of the Good Samaritan, and visits Mary and Martha — affirming that sitting at His feet is the "one thing needful."

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Luke (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 10 contains three of the most beloved passages in
Luke's Gospel: the sending of the seventy (vv.1-24), the Parable of the Good
Samaritan (vv.25-37), and the story of Mary and Martha (vv.38-42). All three
are unique to Luke.

The Seventy: Jesus sends 70 (or 72, depending on manuscripts) disciples ahead
of Him in pairs. This expands the mission beyond the twelve — signaling that
the Gospel work is not limited to a small inner circle. The number 70 may echo
the 70 nations of Genesis 10 or the 70 elders of Israel (Numbers 11:16).

The Good Samaritan: One of the most famous parables in the world. A lawyer asks
"Who is my neighbour?" — trying to limit his obligation. Jesus' answer demolishes
every boundary: your neighbor is anyone in need, regardless of race, religion,
or social status. The hero of the story is a Samaritan — the most despised
outsider in Jewish eyes.

Mary and Martha: A contrast between busyness and devotion. Martha was "cumbered
about much serving" (v.40) — distracted by good things. Mary "sat at Jesus'
feet, and heard his word" (v.39) — choosing the "one thing needful." Jesus
doesn't condemn service but establishes priority: relationship with Him comes
before activity for Him.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- The road from Jerusalem to Jericho (v.30): A notoriously dangerous 17-mile road descending ~3,500 feet through rocky, barren terrain. Robbers hid among the rocks — the setting of the Good Samaritan parable.
- Samaria (v.33): The Samaritan was an outsider — from the despised region between Judea and Galilee. Jesus deliberately made the hero a Samaritan.
- Bethany (v.38): The village of Mary and Martha, about 2 miles east of Jerusalem on the Mount of Olives.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "The harvest is plentiful, but the laborers are few. Therefore pray
        earnestly to the Lord of the harvest to send out laborers into his
        harvest." (ESV) — The need for workers and the priority of prayer.

v.20 — "Nevertheless, do not rejoice in this, that the spirits are subject
        to you, but rejoice that your names are written in heaven." (ESV) —
        The ultimate source of joy: not spiritual power but eternal security.

v.27 — "You shall love the Lord your God with all your heart... and your
        neighbor as yourself." (ESV) — The summary of all God requires.

v.37 — "Go, and do likewise." (ESV) — The application of the Good Samaritan:
        stop defining who your neighbor is and start being a neighbor.

v.42 — "One thing is necessary. Mary has chosen the good portion." (ESV) —
        The priority of being with Jesus over doing for Jesus.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1  — KJV: "seventy" / ESV: "seventy-two" — Some manuscripts read
       "seventy-two" (ESV) while others read "seventy" (KJV). The difference
       is minor — both numbers have symbolic significance (70 nations in
       Genesis 10; 72 elders in some traditions).

v.25 — KJV: "a certain lawyer" / ESV: "a lawyer" — Both equivalent. Not a
       modern attorney but an expert in the Mosaic Law (Torah scholar). His
       question was a test, not a genuine inquiry.

v.33 — KJV: "a certain Samaritan" / ESV: "a Samaritan" — Both equivalent.
       The shock value of this cannot be overstated. Jews and Samaritans had
       centuries of mutual hatred. Making a Samaritan the hero was deliberately
       provocative — forcing the lawyer to rethink his categories.

v.42 — KJV: "one thing is needful" / ESV: "one thing is necessary" — Both
       equivalent. Some manuscripts read "few things are needful, or one"
       (ESV footnote). Either way, Jesus establishes that sitting at His feet
       is the priority — the "good part" that cannot be taken away.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
v.1  — NET: "the Lord appointed seventy-two others" — textual
       variant: "seventy" (KJV/ASV) vs. "seventy-two" (NET/ESV).
       WEB: "seventy others." The number may echo the 70 nations
       of Genesis 10 or the 72 elders of Numbers 11:24-25.

v.18 — NET: "I saw Satan fall like lightning from heaven" — all
       translations agree on this dramatic declaration. WEB: "I
       saw Satan having fallen like lightning from heaven." The
       tense suggests both a past event and ongoing reality.

v.21 — NET: "I praise you, Father, Lord of heaven and earth,
       because you have hidden these things from the wise and
       intelligent, and revealed them to little children" — "little
       children" (nēpiois = infants/toddlers). WEB: "to little
       children." God's economy inverts human hierarchies.

v.33 — NET: "But a Samaritan who was traveling came to where the
       injured man was, and when he saw him, he felt compassion for
       him" — "felt compassion" (esplanchnisthē) — same gut-level
       compassion used of Jesus. WEB: "was moved with compassion."
       The despised outsider embodies God's mercy.

v.42 — NET: "but one thing is needed. Mary has chosen the best
       part; it will not be taken away from her" — "the best part"
       (tēn agathēn merida). KJV: "that good part." WEB: "the
       good part." Sitting at Jesus' feet — the posture of a
       disciple — is the one essential thing.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Seventy       — The larger group of disciples sent out by Jesus (beyond the
               twelve). They went in pairs to prepare cities for Jesus' arrival.

Good Samaritan — The hero of Jesus' parable — a man from the despised
               Samaritan people who showed mercy to a wounded Jewish traveler
               when religious leaders would not.

Jericho Road  — The road from Jerusalem to Jericho descended about 3,400 feet
               over 17 miles through desolate, rocky terrain — notorious for
               bandits.

Innkeeper     — The host of the inn where the Samaritan brought the wounded
               man. He was paid to continue caring for the man — a picture
               of the church caring for those Christ brings to us.

Cumbered      — KJV for "distracted" or "pulled in many directions" (Greek:
               perispaomai). Martha was overwhelmed by the demands of hosting.

Good part     — Mary's choice to sit at Jesus' feet and listen. Jesus calls
               it "the good part" — the one thing that will not be taken away.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "The harvest truly is great, but the labourers are few" (v.2). The need
   is enormous; the workers are insufficient. Are you a laborer in the harvest,
   or are you sitting on the sidelines? What would it look like to step into
   the harvest field in your specific context?

2. "Rejoice, because your names are written in heaven" (v.20). Jesus redirects
   the disciples' joy from spiritual power to eternal security. What is the
   foundation of your joy — your accomplishments for God, or your relationship
   with God?

3. The Good Samaritan didn't ask "Is this person my neighbor?" — he simply
   acted with compassion. Who has God placed in your path who needs mercy?
   Are you walking past them like the priest and Levite, or stopping like
   the Samaritan?

4. Martha was serving Jesus but missing Jesus (v.40). Activity for Christ can
   become a substitute for intimacy with Christ. Is your busyness — even
   religious busyness — crowding out time at His feet?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. The priest and Levite "passed by on the other side" (vv.31-32). They had
   reasons — ritual purity, schedule, fear. What reasons do you use to justify
   passing by people in need?

2. Jesus asked "Which of these three was neighbour?" (v.36). He reframes the
   question from "Who deserves my help?" to "Am I being a neighbor?" How does
   this reframing change how you approach people who are different from you?

3. Mary sat at Jesus' feet — the posture of a disciple (v.39). In that culture,
   women were not typically taught by rabbis. Jesus welcomed her. What barriers
   — cultural, personal, or self-imposed — keep you from sitting at Jesus' feet?

4. Jesus said Martha was "careful and troubled about many things" (v.41). What
   are the "many things" that trouble and distract you? What would it look like
   to simplify your life around the "one thing needful"?

5. The seventy returned with joy because demons submitted to them (v.17). Jesus
   said "don't rejoice in that — rejoice that your names are written in heaven"
   (v.20). Where are you finding your identity in what you do for God rather
   than in who you are to God?
