Luke - Chapter 17
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Jesus teaches on forgiveness, faith as small as a mustard seed, the duty of servants, heals ten lepers (only one returns to give thanks), and warns that His coming will be sudden and inescapable — "as it was in the days of Noah."

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Luke (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 17 covers four topics: causing others to stumble
and the duty of forgiveness (vv.1-10), the healing of ten lepers (vv.11-19),
and teaching about the coming of the kingdom (vv.20-37). Jesus is still on
His journey toward Jerusalem.

The Ten Lepers (vv.11-19): Ten lepers cried out for mercy. Jesus told them to
go show themselves to the priests (the required step for being declared clean,
Leviticus 14). As they went, they were healed. Only one returned to give thanks
— and he was a Samaritan. Jesus' question "Where are the nine?" is one of the
most convicting questions in the Gospels.

Unprofitable Servants (vv.7-10): A challenging teaching on the nature of
obedience. After doing everything commanded, a servant should say "We are
unprofitable servants: we have done that which was our duty to do." Obedience
is not extra credit — it is the baseline. We don't earn God's favor by doing
what He commands; we simply fulfill our obligation.

The Coming of the Kingdom (vv.20-37): The Pharisees asked when the kingdom
would come. Jesus said it doesn't come "with observation" (v.20) — it's not
a visible political event you can point to. It is "within you" (or "among you").
But the Son of Man's return WILL be visible — like lightning across the sky (v.24).

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- The border of Samaria and Galilee (v.11): Jesus traveled along the border between Samaria and Galilee on His way south to Jerusalem. The ten lepers were healed in this region.
- Sodom (v.29): Referenced as a geographic and moral example — the city near the Dead Sea destroyed by fire from heaven.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.4  — "If he sins against you seven times in the day, and turns to you seven
        times, saying, 'I repent,' you must forgive him." (ESV) — Unlimited
        forgiveness for the repentant.

v.10 — "We are unworthy servants; we have only done what was our duty." (ESV)
        — The proper posture of obedience: duty, not merit.

v.17-18 — "Were not ten cleansed? Where are the nine? Was no one found to
        return and give praise to God except this foreigner?" (ESV) —
        The rarity and importance of gratitude.

v.21 — "The kingdom of God is in the midst of you." (ESV) — The kingdom is
        present now, not just future.

v.32 — "Remember Lot's wife." (ESV) — The shortest sermon in the Bible — a
        warning against looking back.

  you've left behind."

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.6  — KJV: "sycamine tree" — ESV: "mulberry tree" — Greek "sukaminon" —
       a tree with deep roots, difficult to uproot. Even small, genuine faith
       can accomplish what seems impossible.

v.10 — KJV: "unprofitable servants" — ESV: "unworthy servants" — Greek
       "achreioi" — useless, without merit. Not that our service is worthless
       to God, but that it doesn't earn us anything beyond what was already
       owed. Obedience is duty, not extra credit.

v.21 — KJV: "the kingdom of God is within you" — ESV: "the kingdom of God
       is in the midst of you" — Greek "entos humōn" — can mean "within you"
       (internal) or "among you" (in your midst). Since Jesus is speaking to
       Pharisees, "among you" (in the person of Jesus) may be more likely.

v.32 — KJV: "Remember Lot's wife" — Genesis 19:26. She looked back at Sodom
       while fleeing — and became a pillar of salt. The warning: don't cling
       to what God is calling you to leave behind.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
v.6  — NET: "If you had faith the size of a mustard seed, you
       could say to this black mulberry tree, 'Be pulled out by
       the roots and planted in the sea,' and it would obey you" —
       "black mulberry tree" (sykaminō) vs. KJV "sycamine tree."
       WEB: "sycamore tree." Different from Matthew's mountain.

v.10 — NET: "We are slaves undeserving of special praise; we have
       only done what was our duty" — "undeserving of special
       praise" (achreios = useless/unprofitable). KJV: "unprofitable
       servants." WEB: "unprofitable servants." Obedience doesn't
       create a debt God owes — it's simply duty.

v.16 — NET: "He fell with his face to the ground at Jesus' feet
       and thanked him. (Now he was a Samaritan.)" — the parenthetical
       heightens the irony. WEB: "He was a Samaritan." Ten healed,
       one returns — and he's the outsider. KJV: "and he was a
       Samaritan."

v.21 — NET: "the kingdom of God is in your midst" — "in your
       midst" (entos hymōn) is debated. KJV: "within you." WEB:
       "within you." ASV: "within you." Does it mean inside
       individuals (internal) or among them (in their midst because
       Jesus stands there)? NET favors the latter.

v.33 — NET: "Whoever tries to keep his life will lose it, but
       whoever loses his life will preserve it" — "preserve"
       (zōogonēsei = will give life to/will bring to life). WEB:
       "will preserve it." KJV: "shall preserve it." The paradox
       of losing to gain.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Offences/Stumbling blocks — Greek "skandala" — things that cause others to
               sin or fall away from faith. Jesus says they are inevitable
               but woe to the one who causes them.

Unprofitable  — Without merit or claim to reward. A servant who does his duty
               has not gone above and beyond — he has simply done what was
               required.

Lepers        — People with skin diseases that made them ritually unclean and
               socially isolated. They had to stand "afar off" (v.12) and
               cry out to warn others of their presence.

Lot's wife    — The woman who looked back at Sodom while fleeing God's judgment
               and was turned into a pillar of salt. A symbol of divided
               loyalty and attachment to the world.

Kingdom of God — God's sovereign rule — present now in the hearts of believers
               and in the person of Christ, but awaiting future consummation
               at His return.

Days of Noah/Lot — Periods of normalcy and complacency immediately before
               sudden, catastrophic judgment. Life went on as usual until
               destruction came without warning.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "If he trespass against thee seven times in a day... thou shalt forgive
   him" (v.4). Unlimited forgiveness for the repentant. Is there someone
   you've stopped forgiving because they keep failing? What does Jesus'
   standard say to that?

2. "We are unprofitable servants: we have done that which was our duty to do"
   (v.10). Obedience doesn't earn extra credit with God — it's the baseline.
   Where are you expecting God to reward you for simply doing what He commanded?
   How does this teaching humble your sense of spiritual accomplishment?

3. Only one of ten lepers returned to give thanks (v.15). Gratitude is rare.
   Are you among the nine who receive God's blessings and move on, or the one
   who returns to give thanks? What has God done for you recently that you
   haven't thanked Him for?

4. "Remember Lot's wife" (v.32). She was physically leaving Sodom but her
   heart was still there. What are you physically leaving behind but
   emotionally clinging to? What "Sodom" keeps pulling your gaze backward?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. The apostles said "Increase our faith" (v.5) after hearing about unlimited
   forgiveness. They felt they didn't have enough faith to forgive like that.
   Jesus said even mustard-seed faith is enough. Where do you feel your faith
   is insufficient? What would it look like to act on the small faith you have?

2. The grateful leper was a Samaritan — the outsider, the one you'd least
   expect. The nine who didn't return were presumably Jewish — the insiders.
   Does familiarity with God's blessings make you less grateful? How do you
   guard against taking grace for granted?

3. Jesus said the kingdom "cometh not with observation" (v.20) — it's not a
   visible political event. How does this challenge expectations of what God's
   kingdom looks like? Where are you looking for the kingdom in the wrong places?

4. "As it was in the days of Noah... eating, drinking, marrying" (vv.26-27).
   Normal life continued right up until judgment. How do you maintain spiritual
   urgency in the middle of ordinary, comfortable daily life?

5. "Whosoever shall seek to save his life shall lose it" (v.33). The instinct
   to self-preserve is strong. Where is self-preservation keeping you from
   following Christ fully? What would it look like to "lose your life" in
   your specific context?
