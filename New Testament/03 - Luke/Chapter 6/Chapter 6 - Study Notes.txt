Luke - Chapter 6
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Jesus declares Himself Lord of the Sabbath, chooses the twelve apostles, delivers the Sermon on the Plain with blessings and woes, commands love for enemies, and concludes with the parable of building on rock versus sand.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Luke (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 6 contains the Sabbath controversies (vv.1-11),
the choosing of the twelve apostles (vv.12-16), and Luke's version of the
Sermon on the Mount — often called the "Sermon on the Plain" because Luke says
Jesus "stood in the plain" (v.17). It is shorter than Matthew's version
(Matthew 5-7) but includes unique material — particularly the "woes" (vv.24-26)
that balance the blessings.

Sabbath Controversies: Two incidents — the disciples picking grain and Jesus
healing a withered hand — both on the Sabbath. Jesus declares Himself "Lord
of the Sabbath" (v.5) — a claim to divine authority over the very institution
God created. The Pharisees' response: "filled with madness" (v.11).

The Twelve Chosen: Luke notes that Jesus spent the entire night in prayer
before choosing the twelve (v.12). The most important leadership decision of
His ministry was preceded by the most intense prayer. This is a model for
every man facing significant decisions.

Luke's Beatitudes and Woes: Luke's version is more direct and concrete than
Matthew's. "Blessed are ye poor" (not "poor in spirit") — Luke emphasizes
literal poverty and suffering. The woes (vv.24-26) are unique to Luke —
warnings to the rich, full, laughing, and popular.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- A plain (v.17): Luke's "Sermon on the Plain" was delivered on a level place, likely near Capernaum in Galilee. Crowds came from Judea, Jerusalem, and the coast of Tyre and Sidon.
- A mountain (v.12): Before choosing the twelve apostles, Jesus went to a mountain to pray all night — likely a hill near the Sea of Galilee.

REFLECTION
------------------------------------
1. Jesus spent all night in prayer before choosing the twelve (v.12). How
   much prayer precedes your major decisions? What would it look like to
   bring that level of dependence on God to your next significant choice?

2. "Love your enemies, do good to them which hate you" (v.27). Name one
   person who is hostile to you. What specific act of love, blessing, or
   prayer could you direct toward them this week?

3. "Give, and it shall be given unto you" (v.38). God's economy is generous
   — you cannot out-give Him. Where are you holding back generosity out of
   fear of not having enough? What would radical generosity look like in
   your life?

4. "Why call ye me, Lord, Lord, and do not the things which I say?" (v.46).
   Calling Jesus "Lord" means nothing without obedience. What is Jesus saying
   to you right now that you are not doing?

5. The Pharisees were "filled with madness" (v.11) when Jesus healed on the
   Sabbath. They cared more about rules than people. Where might you be
   prioritizing religious correctness over compassion for people?

6. Luke's woes (vv.24-26) warn the rich, full, laughing, and popular. These
   are things our culture celebrates. How do you hold material comfort and
   social approval loosely when the world tells you to pursue them aggressively?

7. "Be ye therefore merciful, as your Father also is merciful" (v.36). The
   standard for mercy is God Himself — who is kind to the unthankful and evil
   (v.35). Who in your life is "unthankful and evil" toward you? How is God
   calling you to show them mercy?

8. The speck and plank teaching (vv.41-42) — you must deal with your own sin
   before addressing others'. What "plank" in your own eye are you ignoring
   while focusing on someone else's "speck"?

9. The two builders heard the same words — the difference was obedience (vv.47-49).
   What specific teaching of Jesus have you heard repeatedly but not yet acted
   on? What would it take to move from hearing to doing?
