Luke - Chapter 6
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Jesus declares Himself Lord of the Sabbath, chooses the twelve apostles, delivers the Sermon on the Plain with blessings and woes, commands love for enemies, and concludes with the parable of building on rock versus sand.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Luke (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 6 contains the Sabbath controversies (vv.1-11),
the choosing of the twelve apostles (vv.12-16), and Luke's version of the
Sermon on the Mount — often called the "Sermon on the Plain" because Luke says
Jesus "stood in the plain" (v.17). It is shorter than Matthew's version
(Matthew 5-7) but includes unique material — particularly the "woes" (vv.24-26)
that balance the blessings.

Sabbath Controversies: Two incidents — the disciples picking grain and Jesus
healing a withered hand — both on the Sabbath. Jesus declares Himself "Lord
of the Sabbath" (v.5) — a claim to divine authority over the very institution
God created. The Pharisees' response: "filled with madness" (v.11).

The Twelve Chosen: Luke notes that Jesus spent the entire night in prayer
before choosing the twelve (v.12). The most important leadership decision of
His ministry was preceded by the most intense prayer. This is a model for
every man facing significant decisions.

Luke's Beatitudes and Woes: Luke's version is more direct and concrete than
Matthew's. "Blessed are ye poor" (not "poor in spirit") — Luke emphasizes
literal poverty and suffering. The woes (vv.24-26) are unique to Luke —
warnings to the rich, full, laughing, and popular.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- A plain (v.17): Luke's "Sermon on the Plain" was delivered on a level place, likely near Capernaum in Galilee. Crowds came from Judea, Jerusalem, and the coast of Tyre and Sidon.
- A mountain (v.12): Before choosing the twelve apostles, Jesus went to a mountain to pray all night — likely a hill near the Sea of Galilee.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.5  — "The Son of Man is lord of the Sabbath." (ESV) — Jesus' claim to
        authority over God's own institution.

v.12 — "In these days he went out to the mountain to pray, and all night he
        continued in prayer to God." (ESV) — The foundation of Jesus'
        leadership decisions: extended, focused prayer.

v.27-28 — "Love your enemies, do good to those who hate you, bless those
        who curse you, pray for those who abuse you." (ESV) —
        The radical ethic of the kingdom.

v.38 — "Give, and it will be given to you. Good measure, pressed down,
        shaken together, running over." (ESV) — The principle of generous
        return.

v.46 — "Why do you call me 'Lord, Lord,' and not do what I tell you?" (ESV)
        — The test of genuine faith: obedience, not just words.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1  — KJV: "the second sabbath after the first" / ESV: "On a Sabbath" —
       This phrase is textually uncertain (absent from many manuscripts).
       ESV/NET simply say "on a Sabbath." The exact timing is unclear but
       the event is consistent across all accounts.

v.20 — KJV: "Blessed be ye poor" / ESV: "Blessed are you who are poor" —
       Both equivalent. Luke's version is more direct than Matthew's "poor
       in spirit." Luke likely preserves Jesus' original words to a specific
       audience of literally poor disciples, while Matthew expands the
       principle for a broader audience.

v.36 — KJV: "Be ye therefore merciful" / ESV: "Be merciful, even as your
       Father is merciful" — Both equivalent. Matthew 5:48: "Be ye therefore
       perfect." Luke emphasizes mercy; Matthew emphasizes completeness. Both
       are aspects of the same call — to reflect the Father's character.

v.38 — KJV: "good measure, pressed down, and shaken together, and running
       over" / ESV: "Good measure, pressed down, shaken together, running
       over" — Both identical. Imagery from the grain market. When measuring
       grain, a generous seller would press it down, shake it to settle, and
       let it overflow. God's generosity exceeds our giving.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
v.20 — NET: "Blessed are you who are poor, for the kingdom of
       God belongs to you" — Luke's version is "you who ARE poor"
       (direct address, second person) vs. Matthew's "the poor in
       spirit." The social dimension is more prominent in Luke.
       WEB: "Blessed are you who are poor."

v.24 — NET: "But woe to you who are rich, for you have received
       your comfort already" — Luke alone includes the WOES (absent
       from Matthew's Sermon on the Mount). WEB: "But woe to you
       who are rich! For you have received your consolation." Four
       blessings matched by four woes.

v.27 — NET: "Love your enemies, do good to those who hate you" —
       the most radical ethical command in human history. All
       translations agree. WEB: "Love your enemies, do good to
       those who hate you." No qualification, no exception.

v.35 — NET: "love your enemies, and do good, and lend, expecting
       nothing back" — "expecting nothing back" (mēden
       apelpizontes). KJV: "hoping for nothing again." WEB: "lend,
       expecting nothing back." Generosity without calculation.

v.38 — NET: "Give, and it will be given to you: A good measure,
       pressed down, shaken together, running over, will be poured
       into your lap" — "poured into your lap" refers to the fold
       of a robe used as a pouch. WEB: "pressed down, shaken
       together, and running over." Market imagery — God is
       generous beyond measure.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Lord of the Sabbath — A title Jesus claims for Himself (v.5) — asserting
               divine authority over the day God instituted at creation.

Apostles      — Greek "apostoloi" — "sent ones." The twelve men Jesus chose
               and commissioned with authority to represent Him.

Woes          — Pronouncements of grief and warning — the opposite of
               blessings. Luke's woes warn the rich, full, laughing, and
               popular that their comfort is temporary.

Golden Rule   — "As ye would that men should do to you, do ye also to them
               likewise" (v.31). The summary of ethical behavior — treating
               others as you want to be treated.

Mote/Beam     — A speck of sawdust vs. a plank of wood. Jesus' hyperbole
               for the absurdity of criticizing others' small faults while
               ignoring your own massive ones.

Foundation    — The base on which a house is built. In the parable, it
               represents obedience to Christ's words — the only foundation
               that survives the storm.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Jesus spent all night in prayer before choosing the twelve (v.12). How
   much prayer precedes your major decisions? What would it look like to
   bring that level of dependence on God to your next significant choice?

2. "Love your enemies, do good to them which hate you" (v.27). Name one
   person who is hostile to you. What specific act of love, blessing, or
   prayer could you direct toward them this week?

3. "Give, and it shall be given unto you" (v.38). God's economy is generous
   — you cannot out-give Him. Where are you holding back generosity out of
   fear of not having enough? What would radical generosity look like in
   your life?

4. "Why call ye me, Lord, Lord, and do not the things which I say?" (v.46).
   Calling Jesus "Lord" means nothing without obedience. What is Jesus saying
   to you right now that you are not doing?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. The Pharisees were "filled with madness" (v.11) when Jesus healed on the
   Sabbath. They cared more about rules than people. Where might you be
   prioritizing religious correctness over compassion for people?

2. Luke's woes (vv.24-26) warn the rich, full, laughing, and popular. These
   are things our culture celebrates. How do you hold material comfort and
   social approval loosely when the world tells you to pursue them aggressively?

3. "Be ye therefore merciful, as your Father also is merciful" (v.36). The
   standard for mercy is God Himself — who is kind to the unthankful and evil
   (v.35). Who in your life is "unthankful and evil" toward you? How is God
   calling you to show them mercy?

4. The speck and plank teaching (vv.41-42) — you must deal with your own sin
   before addressing others'. What "plank" in your own eye are you ignoring
   while focusing on someone else's "speck"?

5. The two builders heard the same words — the difference was obedience (vv.47-49).
   What specific teaching of Jesus have you heard repeatedly but not yet acted
   on? What would it take to move from hearing to doing?
