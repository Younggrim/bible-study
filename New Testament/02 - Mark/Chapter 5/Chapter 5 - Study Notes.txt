Mark - Chapter 5
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Three dramatic miracles display Jesus' power over every domain: He casts a legion of demons into swine, heals a woman with a twelve-year hemorrhage, and raises Jairus' daughter from the dead.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: John Mark (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 5 demonstrates Jesus' absolute authority over three
enemies of humanity: demons (the Gadarene demoniac, vv.1-20), disease (the woman
with the issue of blood, vv.25-34), and death (Jairus' daughter, vv.35-43).
Mark's account is the most detailed of the three synoptic versions — vivid,
eyewitness details that likely came from Peter.

The Gadarene Demoniac (vv.1-20): The most extreme case of demonic possession
in the Gospels. A man possessed by a "legion" of demons — naked, living among
tombs, supernaturally strong, self-harming, screaming day and night. No one
could help him. Jesus freed him completely with a word. The man was found
"sitting, clothed, and in his right mind" (v.15).

The Woman with the Issue of Blood (vv.25-34): Twelve years of hemorrhaging —
ritually unclean, socially isolated, financially ruined by doctors. She touched
Jesus' garment in faith and was instantly healed. Jesus stopped, found her, and
called her "Daughter" — restoring not just her body but her dignity and community.

Jairus' Daughter (vv.35-43): A synagogue ruler's 12-year-old daughter dies while
Jesus is delayed by the woman. Jesus says "Be not afraid, only believe" (v.36)
and raises the girl with the words "Talitha cumi" — Aramaic for "Little girl,
get up." Mark preserves Jesus' actual Aramaic words — a vivid eyewitness detail.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- The country of the Gadarenes/Gerasenes (v.1): The eastern shore of the Sea of Galilee, in the Decapolis region — Gentile territory. The demoniac lived among the tombs here.
- The Sea of Galilee (v.13): The pigs ran down a steep bank into the sea. The eastern shore has steep cliffs descending to the water.
- Capernaum (implied, v.21): Jesus crossed back to the western shore where Jairus and the woman with the issue of blood encountered Him.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.15 — "And they came to Jesus and saw the demon-possessed man, the one who
        had had the legion, sitting there, clothed and in his right mind." (ESV)
        — The transformation Christ brings: from chaos to peace, from bondage to freedom.

v.19 — "Go home to your friends and tell them how much the Lord has done for
        you." (ESV) — The freed man's commission: testify.

v.28 — "If I touch even his garments, I will be made well." (ESV) — Desperate faith
        that reaches out to Christ.

v.34 — "Daughter, your faith has made you well; go in peace." (ESV) — Faith that
        heals, and the peace that follows.

v.36 — "Do not fear, only believe." (ESV) — The antidote to fear: faith.

v.41 — "Talitha cumi," which means, "Little girl, I say to you, arise." (ESV)
        — The voice that commands death itself.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.9  — KJV: "My name is Legion: for we are many" / ESV: "My name is Legion,
       for we are many" — Both identical. A Roman legion = ~6,000 soldiers.
       The name indicates thousands of demons possessing one man. Mark notes
       ~2,000 pigs (v.13) — possibly one demon per pig.

v.30 — KJV: "virtue had gone out of him" / ESV: "power had gone out from him"
       — Greek "dunamis" — power, not moral virtue. The ESV is more accurate
       to the Greek meaning. Healing cost Jesus something — power flowed from
       Him to the woman.

v.34 — KJV: "Daughter, thy faith hath made thee whole" / ESV: "Daughter, your
       faith has made you well" — Both equivalent. The only time Jesus calls
       anyone "daughter" in the Gospels. After 12 years of isolation, she is
       given a family name — restored to relationship, not just health.

v.41 — KJV: "Talitha cumi; which is, being interpreted, Damsel, I say unto
       thee, arise" / ESV: "Talitha cumi," which means, "Little girl, I say
       to you, arise" — The ESV uses "Little girl" where KJV uses "Damsel."
       Mark preserves Jesus' actual Aramaic words — an eyewitness detail
       (likely from Peter, who was present). "Talitha" = little girl/lamb.
       "Cumi" = arise/get up. Tender and authoritative.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
v.1  — NET: "the region of the Gerasenes" — a significant
       textual variant. KJV: "Gadarenes." ASV: "Gerasenes." The
       manuscripts vary between Gerasenes, Gadarenes, and
       Gergesenes. NET/ESV follow the earliest manuscripts.

v.9  — NET: "'My name is Legion,' he said, 'for we are many'" —
       all versions agree on "Legion" (a Roman military unit of
       ~6,000 soldiers). The name communicates overwhelming
       multiplicity of demons possessing one man.

v.13 — WEB: "about two thousand" — specifies the number of pigs
       that rushed down the steep bank. All translations include
       this number. NET: "about two thousand in number."

v.30 — NET: "Jesus knew at once that power had gone out from him"
       — "at once" captures Mark's "immediately" (euthys). WEB:
       "Jesus, perceiving in himself that the power had gone out
       from him." The distinction is Jesus' immediate awareness.

v.41 — NET: "'Talitha koum,' which means, 'Little girl, I say to
       you, get up'" — preserves the Aramaic. WEB: "Talitha cumi"
       (different transliteration). KJV: "Talitha cumi." The
       intimate Aramaic — Peter's own memory — is preserved in all
       versions.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Legion        — A Roman military unit of ~6,000 soldiers. Used as the demon's
               name to indicate the vast number of evil spirits.

Gadarenes     — The region on the eastern shore of the Sea of Galilee. Gentile
               territory (hence the pigs — Jews didn't raise swine).

Issue of blood — A chronic hemorrhage making the woman perpetually ritually
               unclean (Leviticus 15:25-27), cutting her off from worship,
               community, and physical contact for 12 years.

Talitha cumi  — Aramaic for "Little girl, arise" or "Little lamb, get up."
               Jesus' tender, authoritative command to the dead girl.

Virtue/Power  — Greek "dunamis" — supernatural power. Jesus perceived that
               healing power had gone from Him when the woman touched His
               garment.

Jairus        — A ruler (president) of the synagogue — a respected community
               leader who humbled himself at Jesus' feet for his daughter.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. The demoniac was completely transformed — from naked, screaming, and
   self-harming to "sitting, clothed, and in his right mind" (v.15). No one
   is beyond Christ's power to restore. Who in your life have you given up
   on? What would it look like to believe Christ can transform them?

2. The townspeople asked Jesus to leave (v.17) — they preferred their pigs
   (economic stability) to His power. Where in your life might you be choosing
   comfort and the status quo over what Jesus wants to do?

3. The woman had tried everything for 12 years (v.26) — doctors, money, effort.
   Nothing worked. She was desperate enough to reach for Jesus as a last resort.
   Sometimes God allows us to exhaust every other option so we'll finally reach
   for Him. Where are you in that process?

4. "Be not afraid, only believe" (v.36). Jairus received the worst news — his
   daughter was dead. Jesus said: don't fear, just believe. What "worst news"
   are you facing? Can you hear Jesus saying the same thing to you?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. Jesus told the freed demoniac "Go home and tell" (v.19) — but told others
   to be silent. Why the difference? The demoniac was in Gentile territory
   where the messianic secret didn't apply. What is your specific commission
   — who has God told you to tell?

2. The woman touched Jesus' garment in a crowd — one touch among thousands of
   people pressing against Him. But Jesus knew the difference between the crowd's
   pressing and faith's touching (v.30). What is the difference between casual
   contact with Jesus and genuine faith?

3. When told his daughter was dead, Jairus was told "Why trouble the Master
   any further?" (v.35). People will tell you it's too late, it's hopeless,
   don't bother God. Jesus says "Only believe." Whose voice are you listening
   to — the crowd's or Christ's?

4. Jesus put the mourners out before raising the girl (v.40). He removed the
   unbelief from the room. Are there voices of unbelief in your life that you
   need to remove — people, media, influences that undermine your faith?

5. After raising the girl, Jesus "commanded that something should be given her
   to eat" (v.43). Even after a miracle, He attended to practical needs. How
   does Jesus' attention to ordinary details encourage you about His care for
   the mundane aspects of your life?
