Mark - Chapter 2
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Jesus demonstrates authority to forgive sins by healing a paralytic lowered through the roof, calls Levi the tax collector, and clashes with the Pharisees over eating with sinners, fasting, and Sabbath observance.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: John Mark (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 2 records five controversy stories that escalate
the conflict between Jesus and the religious leaders: the paralytic and
forgiveness (vv.1-12), eating with sinners (vv.13-17), the fasting question
(vv.18-22), and two Sabbath controversies (vv.23-28). Each reveals Jesus'
authority — over sin, over social boundaries, over religious tradition, and
over the Sabbath itself.

The Paralytic (vv.1-12): Four friends carry a paralyzed man to Jesus and dig
through the roof to lower him down. Jesus sees "their faith" (v.5) and says
"Son, thy sins be forgiven thee" — addressing the deeper need before the
physical one. The scribes correctly recognize that only God can forgive sins
(v.7) — but fail to draw the obvious conclusion about who Jesus is.

Levi/Matthew's Calling (vv.13-17): Jesus calls a tax collector and then eats
at his house with "publicans and sinners." The Pharisees are scandalized. Jesus'
response: "I came not to call the righteous, but sinners to repentance" (v.17).

"The Sabbath was made for man" (v.27): One of Jesus' most revolutionary
statements. The Sabbath was a gift for human benefit, not a burden for human
oppression. And the Son of Man is Lord even of the Sabbath (v.28).

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Capernaum (v.1): Jesus returned to Capernaum, His ministry base. The paralytic was lowered through the roof of a house here.
- The seaside (v.13): Jesus went to the shore of the Sea of Galilee to teach the crowds. Levi's tax booth was likely on the Via Maris trade route near the lakeshore.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.5  — "Son, your sins are forgiven." (ESV) — Jesus' authority to forgive sins
        — a claim to deity.

v.10 — "The Son of Man has authority on earth to forgive sins." (ESV) — The healing
        proves the authority to forgive.

v.17 — "Those who are well have no need of a physician, but those who are
        sick. I came not to call the righteous, but sinners." (ESV)
        — Jesus' mission defined.

v.22 — "New wine is for fresh wineskins." (ESV) — The kingdom cannot be
        contained in old religious structures.

v.27 — "The Sabbath was made for man, not man for the Sabbath." (ESV) — The
        purpose of God's commands: human flourishing, not human bondage.

v.28 — "So the Son of Man is lord even of the Sabbath." (ESV) — Jesus' authority
        extends over God's own institutions.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.4  — KJV: "uncovered the roof" / ESV: "they removed the roof above him" —
       Both equivalent. Palestinian houses had flat roofs made of beams,
       branches, and packed earth/clay. The friends literally dug through the
       roof material. Luke says "through the tiling" (Luke 5:19) — different
       construction styles in different regions.

v.14 — KJV: "Levi the son of Alphaeus" / ESV: "Levi the son of Alphaeus" —
       Both identical. This is Matthew the apostle (Matthew 9:9). "Levi" was
       his given name; "Matthew" (gift of God) was likely the name Jesus gave
       him.

v.22 — KJV: "bottles" / ESV: "wineskins" — The ESV modernizes the language.
       Not glass bottles but animal skins used to store wine. New wine
       ferments and expands — requiring flexible new skins. Old, brittle
       skins would burst.

v.26 — KJV: "in the days of Abiathar the high priest" / ESV: "in the time of
       Abiathar the high priest" — Both equivalent. A textual difficulty —
       1 Samuel 21 says Ahimelech was priest, not Abiathar (his son).
       Solutions: (1) Abiathar was present though not yet high priest, (2) the
       phrase means "in the section about Abiathar," (3) a scribal error.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
v.5  — NET: "When Jesus saw their faith, he said to the
       paralytic, 'Son, your sins are forgiven'" — clarifies the
       collective "their faith" (the friends who dug through the
       roof), not just the paralytic's own faith.

v.14 — NET: "he saw Levi, the son of Alphaeus, sitting at the tax
       booth" — identifies Levi with his patronymic; WEB same.
       KJV/ASV simply "Levi the son of Alphaeus."

v.17 — NET: "I have not come to call the righteous, but sinners"
       — all translations agree here but NET omits "to repentance"
       (found in some KJV manuscripts), following earlier Greek
       texts. The omission sharpens the statement.

v.22 — WEB: "new wine into fresh wineskins" — uses "fresh" rather
       than "new" (KJV/ASV) for the second adjective, distinguishing
       the wine (new in age) from the skins (fresh/unused).

v.27 — NET: "The Sabbath was made for people, not people for the
       Sabbath" — modern inclusive "people" vs. KJV's "man" and
       ESV's "man." The principle is universal, not gender-specific.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Palsy         — Paralysis — inability to move. The man was carried by four
               friends because he could not walk.

Blasphemy     — Speaking against God or claiming divine prerogatives. The
               scribes accused Jesus of blasphemy for forgiving sins — a
               right that belongs to God alone.

Publican      — A tax collector working for Rome. Publicans were despised as
               traitors and extortioners. Jesus called one as a disciple.

Bridegroom    — Jesus' metaphor for Himself. While the bridegroom is present,
               it is a time of celebration, not fasting. Fasting will come
               when He is "taken away" (the cross).

New wine/Old bottles — A metaphor for the incompatibility of Jesus' kingdom
               with old religious structures. The new cannot be contained in
               the old.

Lord of the Sabbath — Jesus' claim to authority over the Sabbath — the day
               God Himself instituted. Only God has authority over His own
               institutions.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. The four friends wouldn't let obstacles stop them from getting their friend
   to Jesus (v.4). They dug through a roof. Who in your life needs you to
   carry them to Christ? What obstacles are you willing to overcome?

2. Jesus forgave the paralytic's sins before healing his body (v.5). He
   addressed the deeper need first. Are you more focused on God fixing your
   circumstances or transforming your heart?

3. "I came not to call the righteous, but sinners" (v.17). If you think
   you're righteous, Jesus has nothing for you. If you know you're a sinner,
   He's exactly what you need. Which posture describes you today?

4. "The sabbath was made for man, and not man for the sabbath" (v.27). God's
   commands are for your benefit, not your bondage. Where have you turned
   God's gifts into burdens? Where has religion become oppressive rather
   than life-giving?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. Jesus saw "their faith" (v.5) — the faith of the friends. Your faith can
   benefit others. How is your faith actively helping the people around you
   get to Jesus?

2. The scribes reasoned "in their hearts" (v.6) — silently. Jesus perceived
   their thoughts (v.8). God knows your internal dialogue. What are you
   thinking about Jesus that you haven't said out loud?

3. Levi "left all, rose up, and followed him" (v.14). Then he threw a party
   for his sinner friends to meet Jesus (v.15). Genuine conversion leads to
   immediate evangelism. When you met Christ, did you introduce your friends
   to Him? Are you still doing that?

4. "New wine must be put into new bottles" (v.22). Jesus' kingdom doesn't fit
   into old religious structures. Where are you trying to fit the new work
   God is doing into old patterns? What needs to change?

5. Jesus is "Lord of the Sabbath" (v.28). He has authority over every
   institution, every tradition, every structure. Is there any area of your
   life where you've given tradition more authority than Christ?
