Mark - Chapter 14
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A woman anoints Jesus for burial, Judas agrees to betray Him, the Last Supper is shared, Jesus agonizes in Gethsemane, is arrested and tried before the Sanhedrin, and Peter denies Him three times.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: John Mark (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 14 is the longest chapter in Mark — recording the
anointing at Bethany (vv.1-11), the Last Supper (vv.12-26), the prediction of
Peter's denial (vv.27-31), Gethsemane (vv.32-42), the arrest (vv.43-52), the
trial before the Sanhedrin (vv.53-65), and Peter's denial (vv.66-72).

Mark includes a unique detail: the young man who fled naked (vv.51-52). Many
scholars believe this was Mark himself — his personal signature on the narrative,
placing himself at the scene as an eyewitness.

"Abba, Father" (v.36): Only Mark preserves Jesus' Aramaic address to God in
Gethsemane — "Abba" — the intimate family word for father (like "Dad" or "Papa").
Even in His deepest agony, Jesus addressed God with intimate trust.

"I AM" (v.62): When the high priest asks "Art thou the Christ, the Son of the
Blessed?" Jesus answers "I am" — the divine name (Exodus 3:14). In Mark, unlike
Matthew's "Thou hast said," Jesus gives a direct, unambiguous affirmation of
His deity. This is what the high priest calls "blasphemy."

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Bethany (v.3): The anointing occurred at Simon the leper's house in Bethany, on the eastern slope of the Mount of Olives.
- The Upper Room (v.15): A large furnished room in Jerusalem where Jesus celebrated the Last Supper.
- Gethsemane (v.32): A garden on the western slope of the Mount of Olives, across the Kidron Valley from the Temple. Jesus prayed and was arrested here.
- The High Priest's palace (v.53): Caiaphas's residence in Jerusalem where Jesus was tried by the Sanhedrin.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.8  — "She has done what she could." (ESV) — Jesus' commendation of the woman's
        extravagant worship.

v.22-24 — "Take; this is my body... This is my blood of the covenant, which
        is poured out for many." (ESV) — The institution of the Lord's
        Supper.

v.36 — "Abba, Father, all things are possible for you. Remove this cup from
        me. Yet not what I will, but what you will." (ESV) — The
        prayer of ultimate submission.

v.38 — "Watch and pray that you may not enter into temptation. The spirit
        indeed is willing, but the flesh is weak." (ESV) — The prescription
        for spiritual vigilance.

v.62 — "I am, and you will see the Son of Man seated at the right hand of
        Power, and coming with the clouds of heaven." (ESV) — Jesus' direct
        claim to deity before the Sanhedrin.

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.3  — NET: "a woman came with an alabaster jar of costly
       aromatic oil from pure nard. After breaking open the jar,
       she poured it on his head" — "costly aromatic oil from
       pure nard" is interpretive. KJV: "ointment of spikenard
       very precious." WEB: "pure nard perfume, very costly."

v.22 — NET: "he took bread, and after giving thanks he broke it,
       gave it to them, and said, 'Take it. This is my body'" —
       "Take it" is shorter than KJV's "Take, eat." NET/ESV omit
       "eat" following earlier manuscripts. WEB includes "Take,
       eat." A communion liturgy variant.

v.36 — KJV: "Abba, Father" / ESV: "Abba, Father" — Both identical. Only Mark
       preserves the Aramaic "Abba" — the intimate family word for father.
       Paul uses it in Romans 8:15 and Galatians 4:6 — believers share Jesus'
       intimate access to the Father.
       NET: "'Abba, Father, all things are possible for you. Take
       this cup away from me. Yet not what I will, but what you
       will'" — "Abba" (Aramaic for "Father/Daddy") is preserved
       untranslated in all versions. The intimate address in
       agony is uniquely powerful.

v.41 — KJV: "it is enough" / ESV: "It is enough" — Both identical. Greek
       "apechei" — a difficult word that could mean "it is settled," "the
       account is closed," or "enough [sleeping]." The hour of betrayal has
       arrived.

v.51-52 — KJV: "a certain young man... having a linen cloth cast about his
       naked body" / ESV: "a young man followed him, with nothing but a linen
       cloth about his body" — Both equivalent. Unique to Mark. Widely
       believed to be Mark himself — his personal "signature" on the
       narrative. If so, he was an eyewitness to the arrest.
       NET: "A young man was following him, wearing only a linen
       cloth. They tried to arrest him, but he ran away naked" —
       this mysterious detail is unique to Mark. Tradition
       identifies him as Mark himself — Peter's eyewitness source
       including this personal detail.

v.62 — KJV: "I am" / ESV: "I am" — Both identical. Greek "ego eimi" — the
       divine name. Mark's Jesus gives a direct, unambiguous answer (unlike
       Matthew's "You have said so" which is more indirect). This is the
       clearest self-identification as God in Mark's Gospel.

v.72 — KJV: "the cock crew" / ESV: "the rooster crowed" — Both equivalent.
       Mark alone mentions the cock crowing twice (v.30,72). A vivid detail
       from Peter's own memory — he remembered every painful detail of that
       night.
       NET: "he broke down and wept" — Peter's response to the
       rooster. KJV: "when he thought thereon, he wept." WEB:
       "When he thought about it, he wept." ASV: "And when he
       thought thereon, he wept." The Greek epibalōn is debated —
       NET renders it as emotional collapse.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Spikenard     — An expensive aromatic oil from India. Worth 300 denarii
               (nearly a year's wages). The woman broke the flask — a
               one-time, irreversible act of devotion.

Abba          — Aramaic for "father" — an intimate, familial address. Not
               casual or disrespectful but deeply personal. Jesus' word for
               God even in His darkest hour.

New testament/covenant — The covenant established by Christ's blood, replacing
               the Mosaic covenant. Based on grace, forgiveness, and the
               indwelling Spirit.

Gethsemane    — "Oil press" — a garden on the Mount of Olives. The place
               where Jesus was "pressed" under the weight of coming judgment.

I AM          — The divine name (Exodus 3:14). Jesus' direct claim to deity
               before the Sanhedrin — the statement that sealed His death
               sentence.

Blasphemy     — The high priest's charge against Jesus for claiming to be God.
               If Jesus was not God, it was blasphemy. If He was God, it was
               the truth.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "She hath done what she could" (v.8). Not what was expected, not what was
   practical, not what others approved of — what she could. Are you doing what
   you can for Christ with what you have? Or are you paralyzed by what you
   can't do?

2. "Abba, Father... not what I will, but what thou wilt" (v.36). Jesus prayed
   with intimate trust AND total submission. Can you pray like that — bringing
   your honest desires to God while surrendering to His plan?

3. "The spirit truly is ready, but the flesh is weak" (v.38). You want to be
   faithful — your spirit is willing. But your flesh is weak. What spiritual
   disciplines (prayer, accountability, Scripture) are you using to strengthen
   your flesh against temptation?

4. Peter denied Jesus three times — the man who said "I will never deny you."
   Overconfidence precedes failure. Where are you most confident in your own
   spiritual strength? That may be where you're most vulnerable.

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. The woman's anointing was called "waste" by the disciples (v.4). Extravagant
   devotion to Christ often looks wasteful to the world. What would extravagant,
   "wasteful" devotion look like in your life?

2. Jesus said "This is my blood... shed for many" (v.24). Every time you take
   communion, you remember His death. How meaningful is the Lord's Supper to
   you? Has it become routine, or does it still move you?

3. "All ye shall be offended because of me this night" (v.27). Jesus predicted
   total abandonment — and it happened. Every disciple fled (v.50). What does
   it tell you about human loyalty that even the closest followers failed?
   How does it affect your confidence in your own strength?

4. Jesus said "I am" (v.62) — knowing it would seal His death sentence. He
   chose truth over self-preservation. Where in your life are you tempted to
   hide the truth about who you are in Christ to avoid consequences?

5. Peter "wept" (v.72) — his failure broke him. But brokenness led to
   restoration (John 21). When did you last weep over your sin? Is your heart
   still soft enough to be broken by failure?
