Mark - Chapter 10
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Jesus teaches on divorce, blesses children, challenges the rich young ruler, predicts His death a third time, and teaches that the Son of Man came not to be served but to serve — then heals blind Bartimaeus outside Jericho.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: John Mark (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 10 records Jesus' journey from Galilee to Jerusalem
— the final approach to the cross. It contains teaching on divorce (vv.1-12),
blessing children (vv.13-16), the rich young ruler (vv.17-31), the third passion
prediction (vv.32-34), James and John's request (vv.35-45), and the healing of
blind Bartimaeus (vv.46-52).

Mark 10:45 is the key verse of the entire Gospel — the thesis statement that
summarizes Jesus' mission: "The Son of man came not to be ministered unto, but
to minister, and to give his life a ransom for many."

The Rich Young Ruler (vv.17-22): Mark alone notes that "Jesus beholding him
loved him" (v.21) — a detail showing Jesus' genuine affection for this man even
as He made the hardest demand. The man went away grieved — the only person in
the Gospels who came to Jesus and walked away sad.

Blind Bartimaeus (vv.46-52): The last healing miracle in Mark before the cross.
Bartimaeus cried out despite the crowd telling him to be quiet. He threw off
his cloak (his most valuable possession as a beggar) and came to Jesus. His
faith made him whole — and he "followed Jesus in the way" (v.52) — the way to
Jerusalem, the way to the cross.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Beyond the Jordan / Perea (v.1): Jesus departed Galilee and crossed to the region east of the Jordan (Perea), heading south toward Jerusalem. Crowds gathered again.
- Jericho (v.46): The last major city before the ascent to Jerusalem, about 15 miles east of the capital. Bartimaeus was healed on the road out of Jericho.
- The road to Jerusalem: The chapter marks the final journey toward the cross — Jesus is heading deliberately toward His death.

------------------------------------

        The permanence of marriage by divine design.

        enter it." (ESV) — Childlike faith as the entry requirement.

v.21 — "You lack one thing: go, sell all that you have and give to the poor...
        and come, follow me." (ESV) —
        The cost of following Jesus: everything.

v.27 — "With man it is impossible, but not with God. For all things are
        possible with God." (ESV) — Salvation is God's work.

v.43-44 — "Whoever would be great among you must be your servant, and whoever

        Kingdom leadership defined.

v.45 — "For even the Son of Man came not to be served but to serve, and to
        give his life as a ransom for many." (ESV) — The mission
        statement of Mark's Gospel.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.21 — KJV: "Jesus beholding him loved him" / ESV: "And Jesus, looking at him,
       loved him" — Both equivalent. Only Mark records this detail. Greek
       "ēgapēsen" — genuine love. Jesus loved the man even while making the
       hardest demand. Love and high standards are not contradictory.

v.24 — KJV: "them that trust in riches" / ESV: "Children, how difficult it is
       to enter the kingdom of God!" — The ESV omits "them that trust in
       riches" following better-attested manuscripts (noted in ESV footnote).
       If included, it clarifies: the problem is not wealth itself but
       trusting in it.

v.45 — KJV: "a ransom for many" / ESV: "a ransom for many" — Both identical.
       Greek "lutron anti pollōn" — "lutron" = the price paid to free a slave.
       "Anti" = in the place of, as a substitute. "Pollōn" = many.
       Substitutionary atonement in three words.

v.50 — KJV: "casting away his garment" / ESV: "throwing off his cloak" — Both
       equivalent. A blind beggar's cloak was his most valuable possession —
       his bed, his shelter, his identity. Bartimaeus threw it aside without
       hesitation to come to Jesus.

v.51 — KJV: "Lord, that I might receive my sight" / ESV: "Rabbi, let me
       recover my sight" — The ESV uses "Rabbi" where KJV uses "Lord" (Greek
       "Rabbouni"). Both convey respect and recognition of Jesus' authority.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
v.6  — NET: "from the beginning of creation he made them male
       and female" — Jesus grounds marriage in creation order.
       WEB: "from the beginning of the creation, God made them
       male and female." KJV: "from the beginning of the creation
       God made them male and female."

v.21 — NET: "Jesus, looking at him, loved him" — the emotional
       detail is preserved in all translations. This is unique to
       Mark's account of the rich young ruler. WEB: "Jesus looking
       at him loved him." The love precedes the hard demand.

v.25 — NET: "It is easier for a camel to go through the eye of a
       needle than for a rich person to enter the kingdom of God"
       — all versions agree. No translation supports the popular
       idea of a "small gate" called the Needle's Eye.

v.45 — NET: "For even the Son of Man did not come to be served
       but to serve, and to give his life as a ransom for many" —
       "ransom" (Greek lytron) = the price paid to release a slave
       or prisoner. WEB: "to give his life as a ransom for many."
       This is Mark's theological thesis statement.

v.52 — NET: "Jesus said to him, 'Go, your faith has healed you.'
       Immediately he regained his sight and followed Jesus on the
       road" — "healed" (sōzō) can also mean "saved." WEB: "your
       faith has made you whole." The double meaning is intentional.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Ransom        — Greek "lutron" — the price paid to free a slave or prisoner.
               Jesus' life is the payment that frees sinners from bondage to
               sin and death. The key theological term in Mark.

Bartimaeus    — "Son of Timaeus" — a blind beggar outside Jericho. His
               persistent faith and immediate following of Jesus make him a
               model disciple.

One flesh     — The union of husband and wife — physical, emotional, spiritual.
               A bond created by God, not merely a legal contract.

Camel/Needle  — Deliberate hyperbole — an impossibility. Wealth creates a
               barrier to dependence on God. Salvation requires God's power.

Minister/Servant — Greek "diakonos" (minister) and "doulos" (slave/servant).
               Kingdom greatness is measured by service, not authority.

Cup/Baptism   — Metaphors for suffering. The "cup" = God's wrath. The
               "baptism" = being overwhelmed by suffering. Jesus asks James
               and John if they can share in His suffering.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "What therefore God hath joined together, let not man put asunder" (v.9).
   Marriage is God's design — permanent, sacred. If you are married, how are
   you investing in the permanence and health of your marriage? What threatens
   it that you need to address?

2. "Jesus beholding him loved him" (v.21) — and then asked him to give up
   everything. Love and high demands go together. God's love for you doesn't
   mean He'll ask nothing of you — it means He'll ask everything. What is He
   asking you to surrender?

3. "The Son of man came not to be ministered unto, but to minister" (v.45).
   The most powerful being in the universe defined His mission as service.
   Is your life oriented toward being served or toward serving?

4. Bartimaeus threw off his cloak to come to Jesus (v.50). He let go of his
   most valuable possession without hesitation. What are you clinging to that
   you need to throw aside to follow Christ more fully?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. The rich ruler "went away grieved" (v.22) — the only person who came to
   Jesus and left sad. He chose his possessions over Christ. What would make
   you walk away from Jesus? What is your price point?

2. James and John asked for positions of glory (v.37). Jesus asked if they
   could drink His cup of suffering (v.38). They said "We can" without
   understanding what they were agreeing to. Are you willing to accept the
   suffering that comes with the glory of following Christ?

3. "With men it is impossible, but not with God" (v.27). Salvation is beyond
   human ability — it requires God's power. Are you resting in God's power
   to save, or still trying to earn your way?

4. The ten were "displeased" with James and John (v.41) — likely because they
   wanted the same thing. When you're angry at someone else's ambition, check
   your own motives. What does your reaction reveal about your own desires?

5. Bartimaeus cried out despite the crowd telling him to be quiet (v.48). He
   refused to let others silence his need for Jesus. What is silencing your
   cry to Christ — pride, social pressure, the noise of daily life?
