Mark - Chapter 6
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Jesus is rejected in His hometown Nazareth, sends the twelve out two by two, Herod executes John the Baptist, Jesus feeds five thousand with five loaves, and walks on the sea to His disciples.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: John Mark (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 6 is one of the longest and most eventful chapters
in Mark, containing: Jesus' rejection at Nazareth (vv.1-6), the sending of the
twelve (vv.7-13), the death of John the Baptist (vv.14-29), the feeding of the
5,000 (vv.30-44), and Jesus walking on water (vv.45-52).

Rejection at Nazareth (vv.1-6): Jesus' hometown rejected Him because of
familiarity — "Is not this the carpenter?" (v.3). They couldn't see past His
ordinary background. Mark notes that Jesus "could there do no mighty work" (v.5)
— not because He lacked power but because unbelief creates an environment where
God's power is not released. Jesus "marvelled because of their unbelief" (v.6).

The Death of John the Baptist (vv.14-29): A flashback explaining how Herod
Antipas beheaded John. Herod feared John, knew he was righteous, and "heard him
gladly" (v.20) — but was trapped by a rash oath made at a birthday party. He
chose his reputation over his conscience. A warning about the cost of people-
pleasing and impulsive promises.

The Feeding of the 5,000 (vv.30-44): The only miracle (besides the resurrection)
recorded in all four Gospels. Jesus was "moved with compassion" (v.34) — He saw
the crowd as "sheep not having a shepherd." He fed 5,000 men (plus women and
children) with five loaves and two fish — and had twelve baskets left over.

Walking on Water (vv.45-52): Jesus walks on the sea during the fourth watch
(3-6 AM). Mark adds a striking detail: "their heart was hardened" (v.52) — even
after the feeding miracle, they didn't understand who He was.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Nazareth ("His own country," v.1): Jesus' hometown in the hills of lower Galilee, about 20 miles southwest of Capernaum. He was rejected here.
- The villages (v.6): Jesus went through surrounding Galilean villages teaching.
- A desert place near Bethsaida (v.32): The remote area on the northeast shore of the Sea of Galilee where the 5,000 were fed.
- Sea of Galilee (v.47): Jesus walked on the water during the crossing back toward the western shore (Gennesaret, v.53).

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.4  — "A prophet is not without honor, except in his hometown." (ESV) — Familiarity
        breeds contempt — even toward the Son of God.

v.6  — "And he marveled because of their unbelief." (ESV) — One of only two times Jesus
        marveled — once at faith (the centurion), once at unbelief (Nazareth).

v.31 — "Come away by yourselves to a desolate place and rest a while." (ESV) —
        Jesus' invitation to rest — even in the middle of demanding ministry.

v.34 — "He had compassion on them, because they were like sheep without a
        shepherd." (ESV) — The heart of Christ toward the lost.

v.50 — "Take heart; it is I. Do not be afraid." (ESV) — Christ's presence in
        the storm — the antidote to fear.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.3  — KJV: "Is not this the carpenter?" / ESV: "Is not this the carpenter?"
       — Both identical. Mark identifies Jesus as "the carpenter" — the only
       Gospel that calls Jesus a carpenter directly (Matthew 13:55 says "the
       carpenter's son"). Jesus worked with His hands for approximately 18
       years before His public ministry.

v.5  — KJV: "he could there do no mighty work" / ESV: "he could do no mighty
       work there" — Both equivalent. Not inability but inappropriateness —
       God's power is not released where faith is absent. Jesus still healed
       "a few sick folk" (v.5) but the atmosphere of unbelief limited the
       scope of His ministry there.

v.48 — KJV: "would have passed by them" / ESV: "He meant to pass by them" —
       Both equivalent. An echo of Old Testament theophanies where God "passes
       by" (Exodus 33:19, 1 Kings 19:11). Jesus was revealing His divine glory
       — not ignoring them.

v.50 — KJV: "Be of good cheer: it is I; be not afraid" / ESV: "Take heart;
       it is I. Do not be afraid" — Both equivalent. Greek "ego eimi" — "I AM"
       — a divine self-identification echoing Exodus 3:14.

v.52 — KJV: "their heart was hardened" / ESV: "their hearts were hardened" —
       Both identical. Even the disciples had hard hearts at this point. They
       had seen the feeding miracle hours earlier but still didn't understand
       who Jesus was. Spiritual understanding is progressive, not instant.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
v.3  — NET: "Isn't this the carpenter, the son of Mary?" —
       makes explicit what KJV renders "Is not this the carpenter,
       the son of Mary?" The designation "son of Mary" (rather than
       "son of Joseph") may imply the townspeople's awareness of
       Jesus' unusual parentage.

v.14 — NET: "King Herod heard this, for Jesus' name had become
       well known" — Herod Antipas was technically a tetrarch,
       not a king. Mark uses the popular title; NET preserves it.

v.20 — NET: "Herod was afraid of John and protected him because
       he knew that John was a righteous and holy man" — "protected
       him" is distinctive. KJV/ASV: "observed him" or "kept him
       safe." WEB: "kept him safe." NET clarifies the paradox of
       Herod imprisoning yet shielding John.

v.41 — WEB: "he blessed and broke the loaves" — straightforward.
       NET: "he gave thanks for the loaves" — renders Greek
       "eulogēsen" as "gave thanks" rather than "blessed." The
       distinction matters: did Jesus bless God (Jewish berakah)
       or bless the food?

v.52 — NET: "they did not understand about the loaves, because
       their hearts were hardened" — "hardened" (pōroō) is strong
       language usually reserved for enemies of Jesus. Mark applies
       it to the disciples — a distinctive Markan theme.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Carpenter     — Greek "tektōn" — a craftsman who works with wood, stone, or
               metal. Jesus was a manual laborer for most of His life before
               beginning public ministry at age 30.

Herodias      — The wife of Herod Antipas, previously married to his brother
               Philip. John the Baptist condemned this marriage as unlawful.
               She orchestrated John's execution.

Compassion    — Greek "splagchnizomai" — to be moved in the bowels/gut. A
               deep, visceral emotional response — not mere pity but gut-level
               empathy that drives action.

Five loaves   — Barley bread — the food of the poor. A boy's lunch became
               the raw material for a miracle feeding thousands.

Fourth watch  — 3-6 AM — the last and darkest watch of the night. The hour
               when Jesus came walking on the water.

Hardened heart — A heart that has witnessed God's power but fails to understand
               or respond with faith. Even disciples can have hardened hearts.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Jesus "could do no mighty work" in Nazareth because of unbelief (v.5). Your
   faith (or lack of it) affects what God does in your life. Is there an area
   where your unbelief is limiting what God wants to do?

2. Herod "heard John gladly" (v.20) but never repented. He enjoyed the
   preaching without obeying it. Are you a hearer who enjoys sermons and Bible
   study but doesn't actually change? What's the difference between hearing
   gladly and obeying fully?

3. "Come ye yourselves apart... and rest a while" (v.31). Jesus invited His
   disciples to rest — even when the needs were overwhelming. Are you resting,
   or are you running on empty? What would it look like to accept Jesus'
   invitation to rest this week?

4. Jesus saw the crowd as "sheep without a shepherd" (v.34). How do you see
   the people around you — as interruptions, as problems, or as sheep who
   need a shepherd? What would compassion look like in your daily interactions?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. The people of Nazareth couldn't see past Jesus' ordinary background (v.3).
   Familiarity blinded them. Is there someone in your life — a spouse, a
   pastor, a friend — whose words you dismiss because you're too familiar
   with them? What might God be saying through them that you're missing?

2. Herod was trapped by a rash oath (v.26). He chose his reputation over his
   conscience. Where in your life are you making decisions based on what others
   will think rather than what is right?

3. Jesus told the disciples "Give ye them to eat" (v.37) — an impossible
   command with their resources. But He took what they had and multiplied it.
   What small, insufficient thing do you have that God might want to multiply?

4. The disciples' hearts were "hardened" even after witnessing the feeding
   miracle (v.52). Witnessing God's power doesn't automatically produce faith.
   What miracles or answers to prayer have you witnessed that you've already
   forgotten? How do you guard against a hardened heart?

5. Jesus came to the disciples in the fourth watch — the darkest hour (v.48).
   He comes when things are worst, not when they're easy. Are you in a "fourth
   watch" season? Can you trust that He sees you and is coming to you?
