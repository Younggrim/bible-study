Mark - Chapter 15
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Jesus is delivered to Pilate, the crowd chooses Barabbas, soldiers mock and crucify Him between two thieves, darkness covers the land, and at His death the temple veil is torn — prompting a centurion to declare, "Truly this man was the Son of God."

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: John Mark (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 15 records the crucifixion of Jesus — Mark's account
is the most concise and stark of the four Gospels. It covers the trial before
Pilate (vv.1-15), the mocking by soldiers (vv.16-20), the crucifixion (vv.21-41),
and the burial (vv.42-47).

Mark's account emphasizes the abandonment and suffering of Jesus. He records
only one word from the cross — the cry of dereliction: "My God, my God, why
hast thou forsaken me?" (v.34). This is the darkest moment in human history —
the Son of God experiencing separation from the Father as He bears the sins
of the world.

The Centurion's Confession (v.39): "Truly this man was the Son of God." This
is the climax of Mark's Gospel. The thesis stated in 1:1 ("Jesus Christ, the
Son of God") is finally confessed — not by a disciple, not by a Jew, but by
a Roman soldier at the foot of the cross. The cross reveals who Jesus is.

Simon of Cyrene (v.21): Mark identifies him as "the father of Alexander and
Rufus" — names known to Mark's readers. This suggests Simon became a believer
and his sons were part of the early church (Romans 16:13 may reference this
Rufus).

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- The Praetorium (v.16): The Roman governor's headquarters in Jerusalem where Jesus was mocked by the soldiers.
- Golgotha (v.22): "The place of a skull" — the execution site outside Jerusalem's walls.
- Cyrene (v.21): A city in North Africa (modern Libya). Simon was from there — showing the geographic diversity of Jerusalem's Passover crowds.
- The tomb (v.46): Joseph of Arimathea's rock-hewn tomb near Golgotha.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.34 — "Eloi, Eloi, lema sabachthani?" which means, "My God, my God, why
        have you forsaken me?" (ESV) — The cry of dereliction — Jesus
        experiencing the full weight of divine abandonment.

v.37 — "And Jesus uttered a loud cry and breathed his last." (ESV) — He died
        with strength — not fading away but actively giving up His spirit.

v.38 — "And the curtain of the temple was torn in two, from top to bottom." (ESV)
        — Access to God opened permanently.

v.39 — "Truly this man was the Son of God!" (ESV) — The centurion's confession —
        the climax of Mark's Gospel.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.25 — KJV: "the third hour" / ESV: "it was the third hour" — Both identical.
       9 AM by Jewish reckoning. Mark says Jesus was crucified at the third
       hour; John 19:14 says "about the sixth hour." This is one of the most
       discussed chronological questions. Solutions include different
       time-reckoning systems.

v.34 — KJV: "Eloi, Eloi, lama sabachthani?... My God, my God, why hast thou
       forsaken me?" / ESV: "Eloi, Eloi, lema sabachthani?... My God, my God,
       why have you forsaken me?" — Both equivalent. Mark preserves the Aramaic
       (Matthew has the Hebrew "Eli, Eli"). Both are quoting Psalm 22:1. The
       bystanders misheard it as a call for Elijah (v.35).

v.38 — KJV: "rent in twain from the top to the bottom" / ESV: "torn in two,
       from top to bottom" — Both equivalent. Torn from God's side, not man's.
       The veil was approximately 60 feet tall and a handbreadth thick. No
       human could tear it.

v.39 — KJV: "Truly this man was the Son of God" / ESV: "Truly this man was
       the Son of God!" — Both identical. Greek could also be "a son of God"
       (no definite article). But in Mark's theology, this is THE confession —
       the centurion recognizes what the religious leaders refused to see.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
v.1  — NET: "Early in the morning, after forming a plan, the
       chief priests with the elders and the experts in the law"
       — "forming a plan" (symboulion poiēsantes). KJV: "held a
       consultation." WEB: "held a consultation." NET emphasizes
       the deliberate, premeditated nature of the action.

v.21 — NET: "They forced a passerby to carry his cross, Simon of
       Cyrene, who was coming in from the country (he was the
       father of Alexander and Rufus)" — the parenthetical naming
       of Simon's sons suggests they were known to Mark's audience.
       All translations include this detail unique to Mark.

v.34 — NET: "'Eloi, Eloi, lema sabachthani?' which means, 'My
       God, my God, why have you forsaken me?'" — Aramaic preserved.
       KJV: "Eloi, Eloi, lama sabachthani?" WEB: "Eloi, Eloi,
       lama sabachthani?" The slight spelling differences reflect
       manuscript variations in transliterating the Aramaic.

v.39 — NET: "when the centurion, who was standing in front of him,
       saw how he died, he said, 'Truly this man was God's Son!'"
       — "how he died" captures what moved the centurion. WEB:
       "saw that he cried out like this and breathed his last."
       It was the manner of death, not just the death itself.

v.43 — NET: "Joseph of Arimathea, a highly regarded member of the
       council, who was himself looking forward to the kingdom of
       God" — "highly regarded" (euschēmōn). KJV: "an honourable
       counsellor." WEB: "a prominent council member." He risked
       his position by asking for the body.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Praetorium    — The Roman governor's official residence and judgment hall.
               The soldiers gathered the whole cohort (600 men) to mock Jesus.

Golgotha      — Aramaic for "place of a skull." The crucifixion site outside
               Jerusalem's walls.

Cry of dereliction — Jesus' cry "My God, my God, why hast thou forsaken me?"
               — the moment of substitutionary atonement when He bore the
               Father's wrath against sin.

Veil          — The massive curtain separating the Holy of Holies from the
               rest of the Temple. Torn at Jesus' death — access to God
               opened permanently.

Centurion     — A Roman officer commanding ~100 soldiers. He witnessed the
               crucifixion and confessed Jesus as the Son of God.

Joseph of Arimathea — A wealthy member of the Sanhedrin who "waited for the
               kingdom of God" (v.43). He boldly asked Pilate for Jesus' body
               and buried Him in his own new tomb.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "My God, my God, why hast thou forsaken me?" (v.34). Jesus was forsaken
   so that you would never be. He experienced the hell of separation from
   God so that you could experience eternal union with Him. Does the weight
   of what He endured for you move you?

2. The veil was torn "from the top to the bottom" (v.38). You now have direct
   access to God — no barrier, no mediator needed except Christ. Are you using
   that access? Are you coming boldly to God, or living as if there's still
   a wall between you?

3. The centurion — a pagan soldier — recognized Jesus as the Son of God (v.39).
   The religious leaders missed it; a Gentile outsider saw it. Sometimes those
   furthest from religion are closest to seeing the truth. How does that
   challenge your assumptions?

4. Joseph of Arimathea "went in boldly unto Pilate" (v.43). At the moment of
   greatest risk — after the crucifixion — he came forward publicly. What
   would bold faith look like for you right now?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. Pilate knew Jesus was innocent (v.10 — "delivered him for envy") and still
   condemned Him. He chose political safety over justice. Where in your life
   are you choosing safety over what you know is right?

2. The soldiers mocked Jesus as King (vv.17-19) — crown of thorns, purple
   robe, mock worship. They didn't know they were telling the truth. He IS
   the King. How does the irony deepen your understanding of the cross?

3. Simon of Cyrene was "compelled" to carry the cross (v.21). He didn't
   volunteer. Yet tradition says he became a believer. Has God ever forced
   you into something painful that later became a blessing?

4. Jesus died with a "loud voice" (v.37) — not fading away in weakness but
   actively giving up His spirit with strength. He was in control even in
   death. How does knowing that Jesus chose to die — that no one took His
   life — affect your understanding of the cross?

5. Mark's Gospel begins with "Jesus Christ, the Son of God" (1:1) and reaches
   its climax when the centurion confesses "Truly this man was the Son of God"
   (15:39). The cross is where Jesus' identity is most fully revealed. Why
   does it take the cross — not miracles, not teaching — to reveal who He is?
