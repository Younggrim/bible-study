Mark - Chapter 11
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Jesus enters Jerusalem triumphantly, curses the barren fig tree as a sign of judgment, cleanses the temple of its commercialism, and teaches on the power of faith and prayer.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: John Mark (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 11 begins Passion Week in Mark — the Triumphal
Entry (vv.1-11), the cursing of the fig tree (vv.12-14,20-26), the Temple
cleansing (vv.15-19), and the authority challenge (vv.27-33). Mark uniquely
"sandwiches" the fig tree around the Temple cleansing — a literary technique
where the outer story interprets the inner one. The fig tree (Israel) has
leaves (religious appearance) but no fruit (genuine righteousness).

The Triumphal Entry (vv.1-11): Jesus enters Jerusalem as King, fulfilling
Zechariah 9:9. Mark notes that Jesus "looked round about upon all things"
(v.11) in the Temple — surveying the corruption He would address the next day.
This is not impulsive anger but deliberate, planned action.

The Fig Tree and Temple (vv.12-19): The fig tree represents Israel — full of
leaves (outward religion) but bearing no fruit. The Temple cleansing is the
explanation: Israel's worship has become corrupt, exploitative, and fruitless.
The two stories interpret each other.

Faith and Prayer (vv.22-26): After the fig tree withers, Jesus teaches about
faith, prayer, and forgiveness. Mountain-moving faith is connected to prayer
that believes — and prayer that forgives. Unforgiveness blocks answered prayer.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Bethphage and Bethany (v.1): Villages on the eastern slope of the Mount of Olives. The triumphal entry into Jerusalem began here.
- The Mount of Olives: The ridge east of Jerusalem from which Jesus descended into the city.
- The Temple (v.15): Jesus entered the Temple in Jerusalem and cleansed it. The Court of the Gentiles had been turned into a marketplace.
- Bethany (v.11): Jesus returned to Bethany each evening during Passion Week — about 2 miles east of Jerusalem.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.9  — "Hosanna! Blessed is he who comes in the name of the Lord!" (ESV) — The
        messianic acclamation from Psalm 118:25-26.

v.17 — "Is it not written, 'My house shall be called a house of prayer for all
        the nations'? But you have made it a den of robbers." (ESV) — Jesus' zeal
        for pure, accessible worship.

v.22 — "Have faith in God." (ESV) — The foundation of everything that follows.

v.24 — "Whatever you ask in prayer, believe that you have received it, and it
        will be yours." (ESV) — The power of believing prayer.

v.25 — "Whenever you stand praying, forgive." (ESV) — Forgiveness as a prerequisite
        for effective prayer.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.13 — KJV: "for the time of figs was not yet" / ESV: "for it was not the
       season for figs" — Both equivalent. This puzzling detail explains why
       Jesus cursed the tree: fig trees in Palestine produce small early fruit
       (taqsh) before the main crop. Leaves without even this early fruit
       indicated the tree was barren — all show, no substance.

v.17 — KJV: "My house shall be called of all nations the house of prayer" /
       ESV: "My house shall be called a house of prayer for all the nations"
       — Both equivalent. Mark alone includes "of all nations" (from Isaiah
       56:7). The Court of the Gentiles — the only place non-Jews could
       worship — had been turned into a marketplace. Jesus was angry that
       Gentile worship was being obstructed.

v.22 — KJV: "Have faith in God" / ESV: "Have faith in God" — Both identical.
       Greek "echete pistin theou" — could also be translated "Have the faith
       of God" or "Have God's faith." Either way: trust God completely.

v.25 — KJV: "when ye stand praying, forgive" / ESV: "whenever you stand
       praying, forgive" — Both equivalent. Jews typically stood to pray (not
       kneeling). The posture is less important than the heart condition —
       specifically, a forgiving heart.

v.26 — KJV includes v.26: "But if ye do not forgive, neither will your Father
       which is in heaven forgive your trespasses." ESV omits this verse
       (noted in footnote) as it likely was added from Matthew 6:15. The
       principle remains taught in v.25.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
v.1  — NET: "Now as they approached Jerusalem, near Bethphage
       and Bethany, at the Mount of Olives" — geographic precision.
       WEB similarly names both villages. The approach from the
       east, over the Mount of Olives, was the expected route of
       the Messiah (Zechariah 14:4).

v.15 — NET: "he began to drive out those who were selling and
       buying in the temple courts" — "temple courts" specifies
       the Court of the Gentiles. KJV/ASV: simply "the temple."
       The commerce was displacing the only space where Gentiles
       could pray.

v.17 — NET: "My house will be called a house of prayer for all
       nations" — "all nations" (panta ta ethnē) is crucial — it's
       the Gentile access that the merchants were obstructing.
       WEB: "a house of prayer for all the nations." All versions
       preserve this phrase from Isaiah 56:7.

v.22 — NET: "Have faith in God" — straightforward. WEB: "Have
       faith in God." ASV: "Have faith in God." Some scholars
       suggest an alternate rendering: "Have the faith of God"
       (objective genitive). No major translation adopts this.

v.25 — NET: "Whenever you stand praying, if you have anything
       against anyone, forgive him" — links prayer's power (v.24)
       to the condition of forgiveness. WEB: "whenever you stand
       praying, forgive." KJV adds v.26 about the Father not
       forgiving; NET/ESV omit v.26 as not in earliest manuscripts.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Hosanna       — Hebrew "hoshia na" — "Save now!" A messianic acclamation
               from Psalm 118:25, shouted during the Triumphal Entry.

Fig tree      — A common tree in Israel, often symbolizing the nation. Leaves
               without fruit = religious appearance without genuine
               righteousness.

Den of thieves — Jeremiah's phrase (7:11) for the Temple when it becomes a
               cover for wickedness. The merchants exploited worshippers
               under the guise of facilitating worship.

Court of the Gentiles — The outer court of the Temple — the only area where
               non-Jews could worship. The merchants had set up shop here,
               making Gentile worship impossible.

Mountain-moving faith — Not literal mountain relocation but faith that
               accomplishes what seems impossible through prayer.

Forgiveness   — A prerequisite for effective prayer (v.25). Unforgiveness
               blocks your relationship with God and your prayers.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. The fig tree had leaves but no fruit — impressive appearance without
   substance. Is your spiritual life producing genuine fruit, or just the
   appearance of religion? What fruit would others see in your life?

2. Jesus cleansed the Temple because it had become a barrier to worship for
   outsiders (v.17 — "of all nations"). Is there anything about your faith
   or your church that creates barriers for people trying to come to God?

3. "Have faith in God" (v.22). Simple, direct, foundational. Where is your
   faith right now — in God, in yourself, in your circumstances, in other
   people? What would it look like to redirect your trust fully to God?

4. "When ye stand praying, forgive" (v.25). Unforgiveness blocks prayer. Is
   there someone you haven't forgiven? Your prayer life may be hindered until
   you release that debt. Who do you need to forgive today?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. Jesus "looked round about upon all things" (v.11) before cleansing the
   Temple the next day. His action was deliberate, not impulsive. How do you
   distinguish between righteous anger (deliberate, purposeful) and sinful
   anger (impulsive, self-serving)?

2. The fig tree was cursed for fruitlessness — not for bearing bad fruit but
   for bearing no fruit at all. Is spiritual passivity (doing nothing) as
   dangerous as active sin? Where are you passively fruitless?

3. Jesus said "believe that ye receive them, and ye shall have them" (v.24).
   This is not a blank check for selfish desires — it's connected to faith in
   God (v.22) and forgiveness (v.25). How do you pray with genuine faith
   while submitting to God's will?

4. The religious leaders couldn't answer Jesus' question about John's baptism
   (v.33) — not because they didn't know but because any honest answer would
   condemn them. Where are you avoiding an honest answer to God because the
   truth would require change?

5. The crowd shouted "Hosanna" (Save now!) — but within days would cry
   "Crucify Him." What does this tell you about the danger of emotional,
   crowd-driven faith? How do you ensure your commitment to Christ is deeper
   than emotion?
