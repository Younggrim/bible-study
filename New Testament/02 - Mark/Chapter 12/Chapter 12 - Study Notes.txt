Mark - Chapter 12
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Jesus tells the parable of the wicked tenants, silences questions about taxes, the resurrection, and the greatest commandment, then warns against the scribes' hypocrisy and commends a poor widow's sacrificial offering.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: John Mark (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 12 continues the Temple confrontations of Passion
Week: the Parable of the Wicked Tenants (vv.1-12), the tribute question
(vv.13-17), the resurrection question (vv.18-27), the greatest commandment
(vv.28-34), David's Lord (vv.35-37), warning against the scribes (vv.38-40),
and the widow's offering (vv.41-44).

The Greatest Commandment (vv.28-34): A scribe asks sincerely (unlike the others
who were trying to trap Jesus). Jesus answers with the Shema (Deuteronomy 6:4-5)
— love God with everything — and adds Leviticus 19:18 — love your neighbor as
yourself. The scribe agrees and Jesus says "Thou art not far from the kingdom
of God" (v.34). Close — but not yet in.

The Widow's Mites (vv.41-44): The chapter ends with Jesus watching people give.
Rich people gave large amounts from their surplus. A poor widow gave two tiny
coins — all she had. Jesus says she gave more than all of them combined. God
measures giving by sacrifice, not amount.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- The Temple in Jerusalem: Jesus continued teaching in the Temple courts during Passion Week. The religious leaders challenged Him here.
- The treasury (v.41): The area near the Court of Women where offering receptacles were located. Jesus observed the widow here.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.10 — "The stone that the builders rejected has become the cornerstone." (ESV)
        — Christ as the rejected cornerstone.

v.17 — "Render to Caesar the things that are Caesar's, and to God the things
        that are God's." (ESV) — Dual obligations: earthly and divine.

v.29-30 — "The Lord our God, the Lord is one. And you shall love the Lord your
        God with all your heart and with all your soul and with all your mind
        and with all your strength." (ESV) — The greatest commandment.

v.34 — "You are not far from the kingdom of God." (ESV) — Close to the kingdom
        but not yet in — intellectual agreement without full commitment.

v.43-44 — "This poor widow has put in more than all those who are contributing
        to the offering box... she out of her poverty has put in everything she
        had, all she had to live on." (ESV) — God measures sacrifice, not amount.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.29 — KJV: "Hear, O Israel; The Lord our God is one Lord" / ESV: "Hear, O
       Israel: The Lord our God, the Lord is one" — Both equivalent. The Shema
       (Deuteronomy 6:4) — the central confession of Jewish faith, recited
       twice daily. Jesus affirms monotheism as the foundation of all
       commandments.

v.30 — KJV: "heart... soul... mind... strength" / ESV: "heart... soul...
       mind... strength" — Both identical. Four dimensions of total love:
       emotional (heart), volitional (soul), intellectual (mind), and physical
       (strength). Nothing is held back.

v.34 — KJV: "Thou art not far from the kingdom" / ESV: "You are not far from
       the kingdom of God" — Both equivalent. The scribe understood
       intellectually but had not yet surrendered personally. Knowledge
       without commitment leaves you close but outside.

v.42 — KJV: "two mites, which make a farthing" / ESV: "two small copper
       coins, which make a penny" — The ESV modernizes the currency terms.
       Two lepta = one quadrans = 1/64 of a denarius. The smallest possible
       offering — yet Jesus said it was the largest.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
v.10 — NET: "The stone the builders rejected has become the
       cornerstone" — "cornerstone" vs. KJV "head of the corner."
       WEB: "the head of the corner." ASV: "the head of the
       corner." NET uses the modern architectural term that
       captures the meaning.

v.14 — NET: "Is it right to pay taxes to Caesar or not?" — "taxes"
       (Greek kēnsos, from Latin census) = the poll tax. WEB:
       "Should we pay, or should we not pay?" The trap: say yes
       and lose the crowd; say no and face Rome.

v.29 — NET: "The most important is: 'Listen, Israel, the Lord our
       God, the Lord is one'" — renders the Shema. WEB: "The first
       is, 'Hear, Israel, the Lord our God, the Lord is one.'"
       ASV: "Hear, O Israel; The Lord our God, the Lord is one."

v.42 — NET: "a poor widow came and put in two small copper coins,
       worth less than a penny" — specifies value. KJV: "two
       mites, which make a farthing." WEB: "two small brass coins,
       which equal a quadrans coin." NET gives the relative value
       to modern readers.

v.44 — NET: "she, out of her poverty, put in everything she had
       to live on" — "everything she had to live on" (holon ton
       bion) = her whole life/livelihood. WEB: "all her living."
       The Greek bios means not just money but LIFE itself.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Wicked tenants — The religious leaders of Israel who rejected God's messengers
               and killed His Son. The vineyard (kingdom) would be given to
               others.

Cornerstone   — The first stone laid in a building, determining alignment.
               Christ — rejected by the builders — is God's chosen foundation.

Shema         — Hebrew for "Hear!" — the opening word of Deuteronomy 6:4,
               the central Jewish confession of faith in one God.

Mites/Lepta   — The smallest copper coins in circulation. Two mites = one
               quadrans = less than a penny. The widow's entire livelihood.

Not far       — Intellectually close to the kingdom but not yet in it.
               Agreement without surrender. Understanding without commitment.

All her living — Greek "holon ton bion" — her whole life/livelihood. She
               gave everything she had to live on — total dependence on God.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Love the Lord thy God with ALL thy heart, soul, mind, and strength" (v.30).
   Not most — ALL. Which dimension is weakest in your love for God? Emotional
   engagement? Intellectual pursuit? Physical obedience? Willful commitment?

2. "Thou art not far from the kingdom of God" (v.34). It's possible to be
   close — to understand, to agree, to admire — and still be outside. Have
   you moved from intellectual agreement to personal surrender? What's the
   difference in your life?

3. The widow gave "all that she had, even all her living" (v.44). God measures
   sacrifice, not amount. How sacrificial is your giving — of money, time,
   energy? Are you giving from your surplus or from your substance?

4. "Render to Caesar... and to God the things that are God's" (v.17). Caesar's
   image is on the coin — give it to him. God's image is on you — give
   yourself to Him. Are you giving God what belongs to Him?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. The wicked tenants killed the servants and the son because they wanted the
   inheritance for themselves (v.7). They wanted God's blessings without God's
   authority. Where do you want God's blessings but resist His lordship?

2. The scribe said love is "more than all whole burnt offerings and sacrifices"
   (v.33). God values love over religious performance. Is your faith
   characterized more by love or by religious activity? What's the difference?

3. Jesus watched people give (v.41). He notices how you handle money. If Jesus
   evaluated your giving the way He evaluated the widow's, what would He say?

4. The scribes "devour widows' houses, and for a pretence make long prayers"
   (v.40). Religious exploitation of the vulnerable is the worst kind of
   hypocrisy. How do you ensure your faith serves the vulnerable rather than
   exploits them?

5. David called the Messiah "Lord" (v.37) — proving the Messiah is more than
   David's descendant. He is David's God. How does the full deity of Christ
   affect how you worship Him, pray to Him, and obey Him?
