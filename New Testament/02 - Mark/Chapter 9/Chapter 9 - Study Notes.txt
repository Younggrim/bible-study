Mark - Chapter 9
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Jesus is transfigured in glory before Peter, James, and John, then descends to cast out a demon the disciples could not, teaches that true greatness is servant-hearted, and warns severely against causing others to stumble.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: John Mark (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 9 contains the Transfiguration (vv.2-13), the
healing of a demon-possessed boy (vv.14-29), the second passion prediction
(vv.30-32), the dispute about greatness (vv.33-37), the unknown exorcist
(vv.38-41), and severe warnings about causing others to stumble (vv.42-50).

The Transfiguration (vv.2-8): Six days after Peter's confession, Jesus takes
Peter, James, and John up a high mountain. His divine glory breaks through —
His clothes become "exceeding white as snow; so as no fuller on earth can white
them" (v.3). Moses and Elijah appear. The Father speaks: "This is my beloved
Son: hear him." Then — "they saw no man any more, save Jesus only" (v.8).

The Demon-Possessed Boy (vv.14-29): A father brings his son — tormented since
childhood by a spirit that throws him into fire and water. The disciples failed
to cast it out. The father's cry is one of the most honest prayers in Scripture:
"Lord, I believe; help thou mine unbelief" (v.24). Jesus heals the boy and
tells the disciples this kind requires "prayer and fasting" (v.29).

"If any man desire to be first, the same shall be last of all, and servant of
all" (v.35): The disciples argued about who was greatest. Jesus' answer: true
greatness is found in being last and serving all — the complete inversion of
worldly ambition.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- "A high mountain" (v.2): The Transfiguration occurred on a high mountain, likely Mount Hermon near Caesarea Philippi (the location of ch.8). At ~9,200 feet, it dominates the northern landscape.
- Galilee (v.30): Jesus passed through Galilee privately, heading south. He did not want anyone to know — this was a time for private teaching of the disciples.
- Capernaum (v.33): Jesus returned to Capernaum where the disciples argued about greatness "in the house."

------------------------------------

        cloud: listen to Jesus above all others.

        — The power of faith — even imperfect faith.

        in the Bible — faith and doubt coexisting, brought to Christ.

        Some spiritual battles require deeper preparation.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.3  — KJV: "so as no fuller on earth can white them" / ESV: "as no one on
       earth could bleach them" — The ESV modernizes "fuller" to "one on
       earth" and "white" to "bleach." A "fuller" was a cloth bleacher/
       launderer. Mark's vivid comparison: Jesus' clothes were whiter than
       any human process could achieve — supernatural brilliance.

v.24 — KJV: "Lord, I believe; help thou mine unbelief" / ESV: "I believe;
       help my unbelief!" — Both equivalent. One of the most psychologically
       honest statements in Scripture. Faith and doubt coexist in the same
       person — and Jesus responds to the faith, not the doubt.

v.29 — KJV: "prayer and fasting" / ESV: "prayer" — The ESV omits "and
       fasting" following better-attested manuscripts (noted in ESV footnote).
       If original, it indicates that some spiritual battles require deeper
       preparation — extended prayer combined with fasting.

v.44,46 — KJV includes these verses (repeating v.48). ESV omits them as
       scribal additions. The content is the same as v.48 — "where their worm
       dieth not, and the fire is not quenched."

v.48 — KJV: "where their worm dieth not" / ESV: "where their worm does not
       die and the fire is not quenched" — Both equivalent. Quoting Isaiah
       66:24. The "worm" represents unending decay/corruption; the "fire"
       represents unending judgment. Together they describe the permanence
       of hell.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
v.3  — NET: "his clothes became radiantly white, more so than
       any launderer in the world could bleach them" — vivid
       domestic comparison. WEB: "exceedingly white, like snow,
       such as no launderer on earth can whiten them." KJV: "so
       as no fuller on earth can white them." Mark's homely detail
       — Peter remembering the practical impossibility.

v.24 — NET: "I believe; help my unbelief!" — all versions render
       this the same. The father's paradoxical cry is one of the
       most honest prayers in Scripture. WEB: "I believe. Help my
       unbelief!"

v.29 — NET: "He told them, 'This kind can come out only by
       prayer'" — some manuscripts add "and fasting" (KJV includes
       it). NET/ESV omit "and fasting" following earlier manuscripts.
       WEB includes "and fasting." A notable textual variant.

v.35 — NET: "If anyone wants to be first, he must be last of all
       and servant of all" — WEB: "If any man wants to be first,
       he shall be last of all, and servant of all." The paradox
       of kingdom greatness through servanthood.

v.48 — NET: "where their worm never dies and the fire is never
       quenched" — quoting Isaiah 66:24. Some manuscripts repeat
       this refrain three times (vv.44, 46, 48); NET/ESV include
       it only at v.48, following earlier texts. KJV includes all
       three repetitions.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Transfiguration — The event where Jesus' divine glory became visible — His
               clothes supernaturally white, His appearance transformed.
               A preview of His resurrection glory.

Fuller        — A person who bleaches and cleans cloth. Mark uses this trade
               as a comparison — no human process could produce the whiteness
               of Jesus' transfigured garments.

Unbelief      — Not the absence of all faith but the presence of doubt
               alongside faith. The father's prayer shows that imperfect
               faith is still real faith — and Jesus responds to it.

Millstone     — A large circular stone used for grinding grain. Having one
               hung around your neck and being thrown into the sea = certain,
               violent death. Jesus says this is preferable to causing a
               child to stumble.

Gehenna/Hell  — Greek "geenna" — the Valley of Hinnom outside Jerusalem,
               used as a garbage dump with perpetual fires. Jesus uses it as
               the name for the place of eternal punishment.

Salt          — Used for preservation and flavoring. "Have salt in yourselves"
               = maintain your distinctive character as disciples. Saltless
               salt is worthless.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Lord, I believe; help thou mine unbelief" (v.24). This is permission to
   be honest with God about your doubts. You don't need perfect faith — you
   need honest faith. What doubts are you carrying that you haven't brought
   to Jesus? Bring them. He's not offended.

2. The disciples argued about who was greatest (v.34) — and were ashamed to
   admit it (v.34). Every man has this argument internally. Jesus' answer:
   be last, serve all (v.35). Where is your ambition for greatness competing
   with your call to serve?

3. "This kind can come forth by nothing, but by prayer and fasting" (v.29).
   Some battles require deeper spiritual preparation. What battle in your life
   might require more than casual prayer — extended, focused, fasting prayer?

4. Jesus' warnings about sin are severe — cut off your hand, pluck out your
   eye (vv.43-47). He's not advocating self-mutilation but radical action
   against sin. What sin in your life requires radical, drastic measures?
   What would "cutting it off" look like practically?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. After the Transfiguration, "they saw no man any more, save Jesus only"
   (v.8). Moses and Elijah faded — only Jesus remained. What voices,
   traditions, or influences need to fade in your life so that Jesus alone
   has supremacy?

2. The father said "If thou canst do any thing" (v.22). Jesus turned it back:
   "If THOU canst believe" (v.23). The issue was never Jesus' ability but the
   father's faith. Where are you questioning God's ability when the real issue
   is your faith?

3. The disciples couldn't cast out the demon (v.18). Jesus attributed it to
   faithlessness and prayerlessness (vv.19,29). Where are you trying to fight
   spiritual battles without adequate spiritual preparation?

4. Jesus took a child in His arms and said receiving a child = receiving Him
   (vv.36-37). How you treat the small, the weak, the insignificant reveals
   how you treat Christ. Who are the "children" in your world that you need
   to receive?

5. "Have salt in yourselves, and have peace one with another" (v.50). Salt =
   distinctive character. Peace = right relationships. Both are needed. Is
   your distinctiveness creating conflict, or are you maintaining both
   saltiness and peace?
