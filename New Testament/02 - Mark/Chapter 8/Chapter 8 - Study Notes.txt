Mark - Chapter 8
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Jesus feeds four thousand, warns against the leaven of the Pharisees, heals a blind man in stages, and draws Peter's great confession — then stuns His disciples with the first prediction of His suffering, death, and resurrection.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: John Mark (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 8 is the turning point of Mark's Gospel. It contains
the feeding of the 4,000 (vv.1-9), the Pharisees demanding a sign (vv.10-13),
the leaven warning (vv.14-21), the two-stage healing of a blind man (vv.22-26),
Peter's confession (vv.27-30), the first passion prediction (vv.31-33), and the
cost of discipleship (vv.34-38). From this point forward, Jesus turns toward
Jerusalem and the cross.

The Two-Stage Healing (vv.22-26): Unique to Mark. Jesus heals a blind man in
two stages — first partial sight ("I see men as trees, walking"), then full
sight. This is likely a parable-in-action about the disciples: they have partial
understanding of who Jesus is (Peter's confession follows immediately), but full
understanding will come only after the cross and resurrection.

Peter's Confession (vv.27-30): The central confession of Mark's Gospel — "Thou
art the Christ" (v.29). But immediately after, Peter rebukes Jesus for predicting
His death. Jesus' response — "Get thee behind me, Satan" — shows that a correct
confession without a correct understanding of the cross is dangerous.

The Cost of Discipleship (vv.34-38): Jesus' most direct statement about what
following Him requires: deny yourself, take up your cross, follow Him. The cross
is not a metaphor for inconvenience — it is an instrument of death. Jesus is
saying: be willing to die.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Dalmanutha/Magadan (v.10): A town on the western shore of the Sea of Galilee.
- Bethsaida (v.22): A fishing village on the northeast shore of the Sea of Galilee. Jesus healed the blind man here in two stages.
- Caesarea Philippi (v.27): A pagan city at the base of Mount Hermon, 25 miles north of the Sea of Galilee. Peter's great confession occurred here — far from Jerusalem's religious establishment.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.29 — "You are the Christ." (ESV) — Peter's confession — the right answer to the
        most important question.

v.33 — "Get behind me, Satan! For you are not setting your mind on the things
        of God, but on the things of man." (ESV) — A correct confession without
        a correct understanding of the cross.

v.34 — "If anyone would come after me, let him deny himself and take up his
        cross and follow me." (ESV) — The non-negotiable cost of discipleship.

v.36 — "For what does it profit a man to gain the whole world and forfeit his
        soul?" (ESV) — The ultimate cost-benefit analysis.

v.38 — "For whoever is ashamed of me and of my words... of him will the Son
        of Man also be ashamed." (ESV) — The eternal consequence of
        being ashamed of Christ.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.12 — KJV: "he sighed deeply in his spirit" / ESV: "And he sighed deeply in
       his spirit" — Both identical. Greek "anastenaxas" — to groan deeply,
       to sigh with frustration. Jesus was emotionally affected by the
       Pharisees' demand for a sign — they had seen countless miracles and
       still demanded more.

v.24 — KJV: "I see men as trees, walking" / ESV: "I see people, but they look
       like trees, walking" — The ESV is slightly more explicit. Partial sight
       — a unique detail in the Gospels. The man could see shapes but not
       clearly. This mirrors the disciples' partial understanding of Jesus at
       this point.

v.34 — KJV: "take up his cross" / ESV: "take up his cross" — Both identical.
       In the first century, this was not a metaphor. It meant a literal
       instrument of execution. A condemned man carried his crossbeam to the
       place of death. Jesus is saying: follow me to death if necessary.

v.36 — KJV: "what shall it profit a man, if he shall gain the whole world,
       and lose his own soul?" / ESV: "what does it profit a man to gain the
       whole world and forfeit his soul?" — Both equivalent. The ESV uses
       "forfeit" where KJV uses "lose" — both translate Greek "zēmioō."

v.38 — KJV: "this adulterous and sinful generation" / ESV: "this adulterous
       and sinful generation" — Both identical. "Adulterous" is used
       metaphorically — spiritual unfaithfulness to God (as in the OT
       prophets). The generation that rejects Christ is spiritually unfaithful.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
v.1  — NET: "In those days there was another large crowd with
       nothing to eat" — "another" explicitly links this to the
       earlier feeding (ch.6). KJV/ASV don't include "another."

v.12 — NET: "Sighing deeply in his spirit, he said, 'Why does
       this generation look for a sign?'" — "sighing deeply"
       (anastenaxas) captures Jesus' emotional frustration. WEB:
       "He sighed deeply in his spirit." KJV: "he sighed deeply
       in his spirit."

v.24 — NET: "'I see people, but they look like trees walking'" —
       the partially-healed blind man's description. WEB: "I see
       men; for I see them like trees, walking." This unique
       two-stage miracle is only in Mark.

v.29 — NET: "He asked them, 'But who do you say that I am?' Peter
       answered him, 'You are the Christ'" — Mark's version is the
       shortest of the Synoptics (no "Son of the living God" as in
       Matthew). All translations reflect this brevity.

v.34 — NET: "If anyone wants to become my follower, he must deny
       himself, take up his cross, and follow me" — "follower"
       rather than KJV "come after me." WEB: "If anyone desires to
       come after me, let him deny himself." The cost of
       discipleship is starkly stated in all versions.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Leaven        — Yeast — used metaphorically for influence that spreads
               pervasively. The "leaven of the Pharisees" = hypocrisy and
               false teaching. The "leaven of Herod" = worldly compromise.

Dalmanutha    — A location on the western shore of the Sea of Galilee. Only
               mentioned here in the NT. Matthew calls it "Magadan" (15:39).

Caesarea Philippi — A city at the base of Mount Hermon, center of pagan
               worship. Jesus chose this location for Peter's confession —
               against the backdrop of false gods.

Christ        — Greek "Christos" = Hebrew "Mashiach" (Messiah) = "Anointed
               One." Peter's confession identifies Jesus as the long-awaited
               King and Deliverer.

Cross         — Not a religious symbol in the first century but an instrument
               of execution — the most shameful, painful death Rome could
               inflict. Jesus calls His followers to carry one.

Soul          — Greek "psuchē" — life, self, the whole person. "Losing your
               soul" = losing yourself eternally. No worldly gain compensates.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. The disciples had seen Jesus feed 5,000 and still worried about bread
   (vv.14-21). Jesus asked "Do ye not remember?" How quickly we forget God's
   past faithfulness. What has God done for you in the past that you need to
   remember right now?

2. Peter confessed "Thou art the Christ" (v.29) and then immediately rebuked
   Jesus for predicting the cross (v.32). Right theology without accepting
   God's methods is dangerous. Where are you confessing Christ as Lord but
   resisting His plan because it involves suffering?

3. "What shall it profit a man, if he shall gain the whole world, and lose his
   own soul?" (v.36). Every man is trading his life for something. What are
   you trading yours for? Is it worth your soul?

4. "Whosoever shall be ashamed of me" (v.38). In your workplace, your social
   circle, your family — are you ashamed of Jesus? Do people know you follow
   Him? What would it cost you to be more open?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. The blind man was healed in two stages (vv.23-25). Spiritual understanding
   often comes progressively, not instantly. Where is your understanding of
   Christ still partial? What "second touch" do you need from Him?

2. Jesus asked "Whom do men say that I am?" then "Whom say YE?" (vv.27,29).
   Public opinion is irrelevant — what matters is your personal answer. Who
   do YOU say Jesus is? Not what you've been taught — what do you believe?

3. Peter went from divine revelation to satanic influence in minutes (vv.29,33).
   How quickly we can move from spiritual insight to spiritual blindness. What
   guards do you have against pride after moments of clarity?

4. "Deny himself, take up his cross, and follow me" (v.34). Three commands:
   say no to self, accept suffering, follow Christ. Which of these three is
   hardest for you right now? Why?

5. "Whosoever will save his life shall lose it" (v.35). The man who plays it
   safe loses everything. The man who risks everything for Christ gains
   everything. Where are you playing it too safe?
