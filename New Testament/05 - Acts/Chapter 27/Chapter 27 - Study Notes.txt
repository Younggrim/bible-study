Acts - Chapter 27
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Paul sails for Rome as a prisoner, warns of danger but is ignored, a violent storm batters the ship for fourteen days, Paul encourages all 276 aboard that God has granted their lives, and the ship wrecks on Malta — but all survive.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Luke — an eyewitness ("we" throughout). This is one of the most
detailed accounts of ancient seafaring in all of literature.

Historical Context: Chapter 27 records Paul's voyage to Rome as a prisoner —
including the shipwreck on Malta. It is the longest "we" passage in Acts,
indicating Luke was present throughout. The chapter demonstrates God's
faithfulness to His promise (23:11, 27:24) — Paul WILL reach Rome, despite
storms, shipwreck, and human error.

The Storm (vv.13-20): A violent northeast wind (Euroclydon/Euraquilo) caught
the ship. For 14 days they saw neither sun nor stars. "All hope that we should
be saved was then taken away" (v.20). In the darkest moment, Paul stood and
declared God's promise: no lives would be lost.

Paul's Leadership (vv.21-36): Though a prisoner, Paul became the de facto
leader of 276 people. He encouraged them, gave practical instructions (eat!),
and modeled faith. His calm confidence in God's word ("I believe God, that it
shall be even as it was told me," v.25) sustained everyone through the crisis.

"God hath given thee all them that sail with thee" (v.24): Paul's presence on
the ship saved everyone. God's people are a blessing to those around them —
even in crisis.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Caesarea to Myra (vv.1-5): Paul sailed from Caesarea, stopped at Sidon (Phoenicia), passed south of Cyprus, and reached Myra in Lycia (southern coast of Asia Minor).
- Fair Havens, Crete (v.8): The ship sailed along the south coast of Crete, struggling against winds. They stopped at Fair Havens near the city of Lasea.
- The shipwreck at Malta (v.41): After 14 days adrift in the Adriatic Sea (the central Mediterranean), the ship ran aground on the island of Malta, south of Sicily.
- The voyage covers approximately 2,000 miles — one of the most detailed sea narratives in ancient literature.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.22 — "There will be no loss of life among you, but only of the ship." (ESV)
        — God's promise through Paul: lives saved, ship lost.

v.24 — "Do not be afraid, Paul; you must stand before Caesar. And behold, God
        has granted you all those who sail with you." (ESV) — God's guarantee
        and generosity.

v.25 — "I have faith in God that it will be exactly as I have been told."
        (ESV) — Faith defined: believing God's word despite contrary
        circumstances.

v.44 — "And so it was that all were brought safely to land." (ESV) — God's
        promise fulfilled completely.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.14 — KJV: "Euroclydon" / ESV: "the northeaster" — Greek "Eurakylōn." KJV
       preserves the Greek name; ESV translates it descriptively. The name
       combines "euros" (east wind) and "kludōn" (wave/surge). A hurricane-
       force storm.

v.27 — KJV: "driven up and down in Adria" / ESV: "driven across the Adriatic
       Sea" — Both equivalent. The Adriatic Sea in ancient usage included the
       central Mediterranean between Italy, Greece, and North Africa — not
       just the modern Adriatic.

v.37 — KJV: "two hundred threescore and sixteen souls" / ESV: "276 persons"
       — Both identical (276). A large grain ship from Alexandria carrying
       wheat to Rome.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
v.10 — NET: "Men, I can see that this voyage is going to end
       in disaster" — "I can see" (theOrO = I perceive/observe).
       WEB: "I perceive that the voyage will be with injury and
       much loss." Paul's practical wisdom — ignored by the captain
       and centurion.

v.20 — NET: "when neither sun nor stars appeared for many days
       and a violent storm continued to batter us, we finally
       abandoned all hope of being saved" — "abandoned all hope"
       (periEreito elpis pasa). WEB: "all hope that we would be
       saved was now taken away." Human hopelessness sets up
       divine intervention.

v.23 — NET: "an angel of the God to whom I belong and whom I
       serve came to me" — "to whom I belong" (hou eimi) — Paul's
       identity: God's possession. WEB: "An angel of God, whose I
       am and whom I serve." Ownership before service.

v.25 — NET: "I have faith in God that it will be just as I have
       been told" — "I have faith" (pisteuO tO theO). WEB: "I
       believe God." KJV: "I believe God." Simple, direct trust —
       the basis for Paul's calm amid 276 panicking people.

v.34 — NET: "not a hair of your head will be lost" — echoing
       Luke 21:18. WEB: "not a hair will perish from any of your
       heads." Sovereign protection — all 276 survive (v.44). God
       keeps His word to the letter.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Euroclydon    — A violent northeast wind — a Mediterranean hurricane. It
               drove the ship for 14 days without sun or stars.

Centurion Julius — The Roman officer responsible for transporting Paul and
               other prisoners to Rome. He treated Paul courteously.

Undergirding  — Passing ropes under the hull to hold the ship together in
               the storm — an ancient emergency technique.

Adria         — The central Mediterranean Sea (broader than the modern Adriatic).

Malta/Melita  — The island where they shipwrecked — modern Malta, south of
               Sicily.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "I believe God, that it shall be even as it was told me" (v.25). In the
   middle of a 14-day storm with no sun or stars, Paul believed God's word.
   What storm are you in right now? Can you say "I believe God" despite what
   you see?

2. Paul's presence saved everyone on the ship (v.24). God's people are a
   blessing to those around them — even in crisis. How is your presence a
   blessing to the people in your life?

3. Paul gave practical instructions — "eat" (v.34) — alongside spiritual
   encouragement. Faith doesn't ignore practical needs. How do you balance
   spiritual trust with practical action?

4. "They escaped all safe to land" (v.44). God's promise was fulfilled
   completely — every single person survived. God keeps His promises. What
   promise of God are you holding onto right now?
