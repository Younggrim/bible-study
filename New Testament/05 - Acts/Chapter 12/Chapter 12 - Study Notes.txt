Acts - Chapter 12
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Herod kills James the apostle and imprisons Peter, but the church prays earnestly and an angel delivers Peter from prison in the night — while Herod is struck dead by an angel for accepting worship, and the word of God continues to grow.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Luke (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 12 records the first state-sponsored persecution of
the church — Herod Agrippa I kills James and arrests Peter. It also records
Peter's miraculous deliverance from prison and Herod's dramatic death. This
chapter marks the end of the "Jerusalem section" of Acts — from chapter 13
onward, the focus shifts to Paul and the Gentile mission.

Herod Agrippa I (vv.1-4): Grandson of Herod the Great, nephew of Herod Antipas.
He ruled Judea from AD 41-44. He killed James (the brother of John) — the first
apostle martyred — and arrested Peter to gain favor with the Jewish leaders.
James was killed; Peter was delivered. God's sovereignty determines outcomes,
not human power.

Peter's Deliverance (vv.5-19): The church prayed "without ceasing" (v.5) for
Peter. An angel freed him from prison — chains fell off, gates opened, guards
didn't wake. When Peter arrived at the prayer meeting, they didn't believe it
was him (v.15). They prayed for his release but were shocked when God answered.

Herod's Death (vv.20-23): Herod accepted worship from the crowd — "It is the
voice of a god, and not of a man" (v.22). He "gave not God the glory" (v.23)
and was immediately struck by an angel and "eaten of worms." The contrast: the
man who killed an apostle and imprisoned another is himself destroyed by God.

"But the word of God grew and multiplied" (v.24): The chapter's conclusion —
despite persecution, despite martyrdom, despite political opposition, the Word
grows. Human power cannot stop God's purposes.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Jerusalem (v.2): Herod Agrippa I persecuted the church in Jerusalem — killing James (brother of John) and imprisoning Peter.
- The prison in Jerusalem (v.5): Peter was held in a Jerusalem prison, guarded by four squads of soldiers. An angel released him at night.
- Caesarea (v.19): Herod went from Jerusalem down to Caesarea (the Roman capital) where he was struck dead after accepting worship.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.5  — "Earnest prayer for him was made to God by the church." (ESV) —
        The church's weapon against persecution: persistent, united prayer.

v.7  — "And the chains fell off his hands." (ESV) — God's power over human
        imprisonment — no chain can hold what God wants free.

v.11 — "Now I am sure that the Lord has sent his angel and rescued me." (ESV)
        — Peter's realization of God's intervention.

v.23 — "He did not give God the glory, and he was eaten by worms and breathed
        his last." (ESV) — The fatal consequence of accepting worship that
        belongs to God alone.

v.24 — "But the word of God increased and multiplied." (ESV) — The
        unstoppable advance of the Gospel despite all opposition.

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.2  — NET: "He had James, the brother of John, executed with
       a sword" — "James the brother of John" — the first apostle
       martyred. WEB: "killed James, the brother of John, with the
       sword." KJV: "killed James the brother of John with the
       sword." One sentence — Luke's restraint regarding apostolic
       death.

v.4  — KJV: "after Easter" / ESV: "after the Passover" — Greek "pascha" =
       Passover, not Easter. The KJV's use of "Easter" is an anachronistic
       translation; ESV correctly renders it "Passover." Herod was waiting
       until after the Passover feast to execute Peter publicly.

v.5  — KJV: "prayer was made without ceasing" / ESV: "earnest prayer for him
       was made to God" — Greek "ektenōs" means "earnestly, fervently,
       stretched out." KJV emphasizes persistence; ESV emphasizes intensity.
       Both aspects are present in the Greek.

v.7  — NET: "an angel of the Lord appeared, and a light shone in
       the prison cell. He struck Peter on the side and woke him
       up" — "struck" (pataxas = hit/slapped). WEB: "struck Peter
       on the side." Peter sleeps so soundly between two soldiers
       the night before his likely execution that an angel must
       physically hit him.

v.12 — KJV: "Mary the mother of John, whose surname was Mark" / ESV: "Mary,
       the mother of John whose other name was Mark" — Both equivalent. This
       is John Mark, the author of the Gospel of Mark. His mother's house was
       a meeting place for the early church.

v.15 — KJV: "It is his angel" / ESV: "It is his angel!" — Both identical.
       The believers thought Peter's "angel" was at the door — reflecting a
       Jewish belief that guardian angels could appear in a person's likeness.
       They found it easier to believe in an angel than in answered prayer.
       NET: "You're out of your mind! But she kept insisting that
       it was Peter" — the praying church doesn't believe their
       prayer was answered. WEB: "You are crazy!" KJV: "Thou art
       mad." The irony: they prayed earnestly (v.5) then refused
       to believe.

v.23 — KJV: "eaten of worms, and gave up the ghost" / ESV: "eaten by worms
       and breathed his last" — Both equivalent. The historian Josephus
       confirms Herod Agrippa's death in AD 44, describing severe abdominal
       pain and death after five days. Luke attributes it to divine judgment
       for accepting divine honors.
       NET: "an angel of the Lord struck Herod down because he
       did not give the glory to God, and he was eaten by worms
       and died" — "eaten by worms" (genomenosSkOlEkobrOtos). WEB:
       "he was eaten by worms and died." KJV: "he was eaten of
       worms, and gave up the ghost." Josephus confirms this death.

v.24 — NET: "But the word of God kept on increasing and spreading"
       — contrast: Herod dies, the word grows. WEB: "the word of
       God grew and multiplied." KJV: "the word of God grew and
       multiplied." The king dies; the King's message thrives.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Herod Agrippa I — Grandson of Herod the Great. King of Judea AD 41-44.
               He persecuted the church to gain Jewish favor and was struck
               dead by God for accepting worship.

James         — The brother of John, son of Zebedee. One of Jesus' inner
               circle (with Peter and John). The first apostle martyred —
               killed by Herod's sword.

Quaternion    — A squad of four soldiers. Peter was guarded by four quaternions
               (16 soldiers total) in rotating shifts — maximum security.

Rhoda         — A servant girl in Mary's house who recognized Peter's voice
               but was too excited to open the door. A humorous, human detail.

John Mark     — Author of the Gospel of Mark. His mother's house was a church
               meeting place. He later traveled with Paul and Barnabas.

Word of God grew — Despite persecution, martyrdom, and political opposition,
               the Gospel continued to spread and the church continued to
               multiply.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. James was killed; Peter was delivered. Both were faithful apostles. God's
   sovereignty determines outcomes — not our faithfulness level. How do you
   handle the mystery of why God delivers some and allows others to suffer?
   Can you trust His sovereignty even when you don't understand His choices?

2. "Prayer was made without ceasing" (v.5). The church's response to crisis
   was not strategy or politics — it was prayer. When you face a crisis, is
   prayer your first response or your last resort?

3. They prayed for Peter's release but didn't believe it when he showed up
   (v.15). We often pray without expecting God to actually answer. How
   expectant is your prayer? Do you pray and then watch for the answer?

4. Herod "gave not God the glory" (v.23) — and was destroyed. Every good
   thing in your life is from God. Are you giving Him the glory, or are you
   accepting credit that belongs to Him?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. James was killed and Peter was saved — both by God's sovereign will. How
   do you reconcile God's goodness with the reality that faithful believers
   sometimes suffer and die? What does this tell you about the nature of
   faith?

2. Peter was sleeping between two soldiers the night before his scheduled
   execution (v.6). He was at peace — even facing death. What would it take
   for you to sleep peacefully in the face of your worst fear?

3. The church prayed "without ceasing" (v.5) — persistent, corporate,
   focused prayer. How does your church respond to crisis? Is prayer the
   first and primary response, or an afterthought?

4. "The word of God grew and multiplied" (v.24). Despite everything the enemy
   threw at the church — martyrdom, imprisonment, political persecution — the
   Word grew. How does this encourage you when the church seems weak or under
   attack in your context?

5. Herod's death came because he accepted glory that belonged to God (v.23).
   Pride — taking credit for what God has done — is one of the most dangerous
   sins. Where might you be accepting glory that belongs to God?
