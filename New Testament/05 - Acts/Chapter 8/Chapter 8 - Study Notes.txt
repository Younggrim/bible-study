Acts - Chapter 8
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Persecution scatters the church, Philip preaches in Samaria with great results, Simon the sorcerer believes but is rebuked for trying to buy the Spirit's power, and Philip leads the Ethiopian eunuch to Christ on the Gaza road.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Luke (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 8 marks a major transition in Acts — the Gospel
moves beyond Jerusalem for the first time, fulfilling Acts 1:8 ("Judaea and
Samaria"). Stephen's martyrdom triggers persecution that scatters believers —
and paradoxically, scattering becomes the mechanism for spreading the Gospel.
What the enemy meant to destroy the church, God used to expand it.

Philip in Samaria (vv.5-25): Philip (one of the seven deacons from chapter 6)
preaches in Samaria — enemy territory for Jews. The Samaritans respond with
faith and joy. Simon the sorcerer believes and is baptized but later reveals
his heart is wrong — he tries to buy the Spirit's power with money. Peter
rebukes him severely.

Philip and the Ethiopian (vv.26-40): One of the most beautiful conversion
stories in Acts. An Ethiopian official — a Gentile God-fearer — is reading
Isaiah 53 in his chariot. The Spirit directs Philip to him. Philip explains
that Isaiah's suffering servant is Jesus. The Ethiopian believes and is
baptized immediately. The Gospel reaches Africa.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Samaria (v.5): Philip went to the city of Samaria (between Judea and Galilee) and preached Christ there — fulfilling Acts 1:8's expansion pattern.
- The desert road from Jerusalem to Gaza (v.26): Philip met the Ethiopian eunuch on this road heading southwest from Jerusalem toward the coast and into Africa.
- Azotus (Ashdod, v.40): After baptizing the eunuch, Philip appeared at Azotus on the Mediterranean coast and preached through all the coastal cities to Caesarea.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.4  — "Now those who were scattered went about preaching the word." (ESV)
        — Persecution produces expansion. Scattering becomes sowing.

v.20 — "May your silver perish with you, because you thought you could obtain
        the gift of God with money!" (ESV) — God's gifts cannot be bought.

v.30-31 — "Do you understand what you are reading? And he said, How can I,
        unless someone guides me?" (ESV) — The need for someone to explain
        Scripture.

v.35 — "Then Philip opened his mouth, and beginning with this Scripture he
        told him the good news about Jesus." (ESV) — All Scripture points to
        Jesus.

v.37 — "I believe that Jesus Christ is the Son of God." (ESV) — The
        Ethiopian's confession of faith.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1  — KJV: "they were all scattered" / ESV: "they were all scattered" —
       Both identical. "All" is likely hyperbolic — the apostles stayed
       (v.1b). But the majority of believers fled Jerusalem, taking the
       Gospel with them wherever they went.

v.20 — KJV: "Thy money perish with thee" / ESV: "May your silver perish with
       you" — Both equivalent. Greek "to argurion sou sun soi eiē eis
       apōleian" — literally "may your silver go to destruction with you."
       A severe rebuke. The word "simony" (buying spiritual office) comes
       from Simon's name.

v.32 — KJV: "He was led as a sheep to the slaughter" / ESV: "Like a sheep
       he was led to the slaughter" — Both equivalent. Isaiah 53:7-8. The
       Ethiopian was reading the most explicitly messianic passage in the OT
       — and couldn't understand it without help. Philip showed him it was
       about Jesus.

v.37 — KJV includes: "I believe that Jesus Christ is the Son of God." ESV
       omits this verse (absent from many early manuscripts; ESV/NET place it
       in a footnote). It may be a later addition reflecting early baptismal
       practice. Its content is consistent with the rest of Acts.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
v.1  — NET: "a great persecution against the church in
       Jerusalem began, and all except the apostles were scattered"
       — "scattered" (diesparesan = scattered like seed). WEB: "All
       were scattered abroad." Persecution becomes the mechanism of
       mission — the scattered church plants everywhere it lands.

v.4  — NET: "those who had been scattered went around proclaiming
       the good news of the word" — "proclaiming" (euangelizomenoi
       = evangelizing). WEB: "went about preaching the word." KJV:
       "went every where preaching the word." Every believer a
       preacher — not just apostles.

v.20 — NET: "Give me this power too, so that everyone I place my
       hands on may receive the Holy Spirit" — Simon tries to BUY
       spiritual authority. WEB: "Give me also this power." KJV:
       "Give me also this power." Hence "simony" — the sin of
       purchasing spiritual office.

v.30 — NET: "Do you understand what you are reading?" — Philip's
       question to the Ethiopian. WEB: "Do you understand what you
       are reading?" KJV: "Understandest thou what thou readest?"
       Scripture requires explanation — the Spirit often uses
       people to illuminate the text.

v.36 — NET: "Look, there is water! What is to stop me from being
       baptized?" — the Ethiopian's eager response. WEB: "what is
       keeping me from being baptized?" KJV: "what doth hinder me
       to be baptized?" Faith leads immediately to obedience —
       no bureaucratic delay.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Scattered     — Greek "diaspeirō" — to scatter like seed. The same word used
               for sowing. Persecution scattered believers like seed — and
               the Gospel grew wherever they landed.

Samaria       — The region between Judea and Galilee, home of the Samaritans.
               Jews and Samaritans had centuries of hostility. The Gospel
               crossing this barrier was significant.

Simon/Simony  — Simon the sorcerer tried to buy spiritual power with money.
               "Simony" became the term for buying or selling spiritual
               offices or gifts.

Ethiopian eunuch — A high-ranking official under Candace (queen of Ethiopia).
               A God-fearer who had come to Jerusalem to worship. Likely the
               first African convert to Christianity.

Candace       — Not a personal name but a title for the queen mother of
               Ethiopia (modern Sudan). The eunuch served in her court.

Azotus        — The ancient Philistine city of Ashdod, on the Mediterranean
               coast. Philip appeared there after the Ethiopian's baptism.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "They that were scattered abroad went every where preaching the word" (v.4).
   Ordinary believers — not apostles — spread the Gospel through persecution.
   God uses disruption to expand His kingdom. What disruption in your life
   might God be using to spread the Gospel through you?

2. Simon tried to buy the Spirit's power with money (v.18). He wanted spiritual
   authority for personal gain. Where might you be approaching God's gifts
   with a consumer mentality — wanting what He offers for your own benefit
   rather than His glory?

3. Philip was directed by the Spirit to one man on a desert road (v.26). He
   left a successful city-wide revival to minister to one person. Are you
   willing to leave the crowd to reach the one? Who is the "one" God is
   directing you toward?

4. "Understandest thou what thou readest?" (v.30). The Ethiopian needed someone
   to explain Scripture to him. Who in your life needs you to open the Bible
   with them and point them to Jesus?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. Persecution scattered the church — and the Gospel spread (v.4). God turns
   the enemy's attacks into expansion. How have you seen God use difficult
   circumstances in your life to advance His purposes?

2. Simon "believed" and was "baptized" (v.13) but his heart was "not right"
   (v.21). External religious actions don't guarantee internal transformation.
   How do you evaluate whether your faith is genuine — by external actions or
   by the condition of your heart?

3. Philip "began at the same scripture, and preached unto him Jesus" (v.35).
   He started where the Ethiopian was and pointed him to Christ. How do you
   meet people where they are in spiritual conversations rather than forcing
   your agenda?

4. The Ethiopian "went on his way rejoicing" (v.39). Genuine conversion
   produces joy. Is joy a characteristic of your faith? If not, what's missing?

5. Philip was "caught away" by the Spirit (v.39) — moved supernaturally to
   his next assignment. Are you available for the Spirit to redirect you?
   How flexible are you when God changes your plans?
