Acts - Chapter 20
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Paul travels through Macedonia and Greece, raises Eutychus from death in Troas, and delivers his farewell address to the Ephesian elders at Miletus — warning of wolves and commending them to God's grace, declaring he is "innocent of the blood of all."

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Luke (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details). The "we"
passages resume in v.5, indicating Luke rejoined Paul's team.

Historical Context: Chapter 20 records Paul's journey from Ephesus through
Macedonia and Greece toward Jerusalem, the raising of Eutychus at Troas
(vv.7-12), and Paul's farewell address to the Ephesian elders at Miletus
(vv.17-38) — one of the most emotional and theologically rich passages in Acts.

Paul's Farewell to the Ephesian Elders (vv.17-38): Paul knows he will never
see them again (v.25). He reviews his ministry among them (vv.18-21), declares
his readiness to suffer (vv.22-24), warns of coming dangers (vv.28-31), and
commends them to God (v.32). This is the only speech in Acts addressed to
Christian leaders — making it a model for pastoral ministry.

"I count not my life dear unto myself" (v.24): Paul's defining statement about
his priorities. His life was not his own — it belonged to Christ and to the
mission. He was willing to lose everything to "finish my course with joy."

"Feed the church of God, which he hath purchased with his own blood" (v.28):
One of the most theologically dense verses in Acts — affirming that the church
belongs to God, was purchased by blood, and must be fed (taught/shepherded) by
its leaders.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Macedonia and Greece (v.2): Paul traveled through Macedonia (Philippi, Thessalonica, Berea) and spent three months in Greece (likely Corinth).
- Troas (v.6): Paul sailed from Philippi to Troas (on the Aegean coast of Asia Minor). The raising of Eutychus occurred here.
- Miletus (v.15): A coastal city about 30 miles south of Ephesus. Paul summoned the Ephesian elders here for his farewell address, unwilling to stop in Ephesus due to time constraints.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.24 — "But I do not account my life of any value nor as precious to myself,
        if only I may finish my course and the ministry that I received from
        the Lord Jesus, to testify to the gospel of the grace of God." (ESV)
        — Paul's life purpose.

v.27 — "I did not shrink from declaring to you the whole counsel of God."
        (ESV) — Faithful teaching: the whole counsel, not selective truth.

v.28 — "Pay careful attention to yourselves and to all the flock, in which
        the Holy Spirit has made you overseers, to care for the church of
        God, which he obtained with his own blood." (ESV) — The charge to
        elders: guard yourselves, guard the flock, feed the church.

v.32 — "And now I commend you to God and to the word of his grace, which is
        able to build you up and to give you the inheritance." (ESV) — God
        and His Word as the ultimate security.

v.35 — "It is more blessed to give than to receive." (ESV) — A saying of
        Jesus not recorded in the Gospels, preserved only here.

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.7  — KJV: "the first day of the week" / ESV: "the first day of the week"
       — Both identical. Sunday. The early church gathered on Sunday (the day
       of resurrection) rather than Saturday (the Jewish Sabbath). This is one
       of the earliest references to Sunday worship.
       NET: "On the first day of the week, when we gathered
       together to break bread, Paul began to speak" — "first day
       of the week" — early Christian worship on Sunday (resurrection
       day). WEB: "On the first day of the week." Evidence of
       Sunday gathering in the apostolic era.

v.9  — NET: "a young man named Eutychus...sank into a deep sleep
       while Paul continued to speak" — Paul preached until midnight
       (v.7). WEB: "overcome with deep sleep." KJV: "fallen into
       a deep sleep." The young man fell three stories — Luke notes
       "was picked up dead" (not "appeared dead").

v.24 — KJV: "finish my course" / ESV: "finish my course" — Both identical.
       Greek "dromos" — a race course. Paul sees his life as a race to be
       completed — not just survived but finished with joy.
       NET: "But I do not consider my life worth anything to
       myself, so that I may finish my task and the ministry that
       I received from the Lord Jesus" — "do not consider my life
       worth anything" (oudenos logou poioumai tEn psychEn). WEB:
       "I don't count my own life dear to myself." KJV: "neither
       count I my life dear unto myself."

v.28 — KJV: "the church of God, which he hath purchased with his own blood" /
       ESV: "the church of God, which he obtained with his own blood" — Both
       equivalent. KJV's "purchased" and ESV's "obtained" both translate Greek
       "periepoiēsato." "His own blood" refers to God's blood — a strong
       affirmation of Christ's deity. God purchased the church with His own
       blood through the incarnation and crucifixion.
       NET: "Watch out for yourselves and for all the flock of
       which the Holy Spirit has made you overseers" — "overseers"
       (episkopous = bishops/overseers). WEB: "which the Holy
       Spirit has made you overseers." Elders appointed by the
       Spirit, not just human selection.

v.28 — KJV: "overseers" / ESV: "overseers" — Both identical. Greek
       "episkopous" — bishops/overseers. The same men are called "elders"
       (presbuteroi) in v.17. Elder and overseer are the same office —
       different words for the same role.
       NET: "Watch out for yourselves and for all the flock of
       which the Holy Spirit has made you overseers" — "overseers"
       (episkopous = bishops/overseers). WEB: "which the Holy
       Spirit has made you overseers." Elders appointed by the
       Spirit, not just human selection.

v.35 — KJV: "It is more blessed to give than to receive" / ESV: "It is more
       blessed to give than to receive" — Both identical. An unrecorded saying
       of Jesus (an "agraphon") — not found in the four Gospels but preserved
       by Paul through oral tradition.
       NET: "by working in this way we must help the weak, and
       remember the words of the Lord Jesus that he himself said,
       'It is more blessed to give than to receive'" — an unwritten
       saying of Jesus (agraphon) preserved only here. WEB: "It is
       more blessed to give than to receive." Not in the Gospels.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Eutychus      — A young man who fell asleep during Paul's long sermon, fell
               from a third-story window, and was raised by Paul. His name
               means "fortunate" — fitting.

Miletus       — A coastal city about 30 miles south of Ephesus. Paul met the
               Ephesian elders here rather than stopping in Ephesus itself.

Overseers/Elders — Two terms for the same church leadership role. "Elder"
               (presbuteros) emphasizes maturity; "overseer" (episkopos)
               emphasizes function.

Course        — Greek "dromos" — a race, a course to be run. Paul viewed his
               life and ministry as a race with a finish line.

Wolves        — False teachers who would enter the church after Paul's
               departure — not attacking from outside but infiltrating from
               within.

All the counsel of God — The complete, unedited truth of Scripture — not
               selective teaching that avoids difficult topics.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "I count not my life dear unto myself" (v.24). Paul's life was not his own
   — it belonged to Christ and the mission. How tightly are you holding onto
   your life — your comfort, your safety, your plans? What would it look like
   to hold your life loosely for Christ's sake?

2. "I have not shunned to declare unto you all the counsel of God" (v.27).
   Paul taught everything — not just the comfortable parts. Are you receiving
   the WHOLE counsel of God, or only the parts you like? Are you teaching
   others the full truth?

3. "Take heed unto yourselves" (v.28). The first responsibility of a leader
   is self-care — guarding your own soul. Before you can feed others, you must
   feed yourself. How is your own spiritual health? Are you taking heed to
   yourself?

4. "It is more blessed to give than to receive" (v.35). A saying of Jesus
   preserved only here. Is your life oriented toward giving or receiving?
   What would it look like to live this principle more fully this week?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. Paul preached until midnight (v.7) — and Eutychus fell asleep and died.
   Paul raised him and kept preaching until dawn (v.11). His commitment to
   teaching was extraordinary. How committed are you to hearing and teaching
   God's Word?

2. Paul warned that "grievous wolves" would enter the church (v.29) and that
   false teachers would arise from within (v.30). How do you guard against
   false teaching in your own life and church? What are the warning signs?

3. Paul "commended them to God, and to the word of his grace" (v.32). When
   he left, he didn't leave them with a program — he left them with God and
   His Word. Are God and His Word sufficient for you, or do you need something
   more?

4. Paul worked with his own hands (v.34) and supported others. He modeled
   generosity and self-sufficiency. How does your work ethic reflect your
   faith? Are you a giver or a taker?

5. The elders "wept sore" at Paul's departure (v.37). Deep relationships
   marked the early church. How deep are your relationships with other
   believers? Do you have people who would weep at your departure?
