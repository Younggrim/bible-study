Acts - Chapter 11
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Peter defends his ministry to Gentiles before the Jerusalem church, they glorify God saying "Then to the Gentiles also God has granted repentance that leads to life," and the gospel reaches Antioch where believers are first called "Christians."

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Luke (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 11 records two significant developments: Peter's
defense of his ministry to Gentiles before the Jerusalem church (vv.1-18), and
the founding of the church at Antioch — which becomes the base for Gentile
missions (vv.19-30). The chapter also introduces the name "Christians" (v.26).

Peter's Defense (vv.1-18): The Jerusalem church criticized Peter for eating
with uncircumcised Gentiles (v.3). Peter recounts the entire Cornelius episode
— the vision, the Spirit's direction, and the Gentile Pentecost. His conclusion:
"What was I, that I could withstand God?" (v.17). The church's response: "Then
hath God also to the Gentiles granted repentance unto life" (v.18).

The Church at Antioch (vv.19-26): Believers scattered by Stephen's persecution
reached Antioch — the third-largest city in the Roman Empire. Some began
preaching to Greeks (Gentiles), not just Jews. A multi-ethnic church was born.
Barnabas was sent to investigate, saw God's grace, and went to find Saul in
Tarsus. Together they taught for a year. The disciples were first called
"Christians" in Antioch.

"Christians" (v.26): The name was likely given by outsiders — possibly as a
nickname or even a slur. It means "Christ's people" or "belonging to Christ."
The believers didn't choose the name — they were identified by it because their
lives were so centered on Christ that outsiders couldn't miss it.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Antioch in Syria (v.19-26): Believers scattered by persecution traveled as far as Phoenicia, Cyprus, and Antioch — about 300 miles north of Jerusalem. Antioch became the second major center of Christianity and the base for Gentile mission.
- Tarsus (v.25): Barnabas went to Tarsus (in Cilicia, modern southeast Turkey) to find Saul and bring him to Antioch.
- Jerusalem (v.2): Peter reported to the Jerusalem church about the Gentile inclusion.

REFLECTION
------------------------------------
1. "What was I, that I could withstand God?" (v.17). When God is clearly at
   work — even in ways that challenge your traditions or expectations — will
   you resist or submit? Where might God be doing something new that you're
   resisting because it's unfamiliar?

2. "The disciples were called Christians first in Antioch" (v.26). They were
   so identified with Christ that outsiders named them after Him. If people
   observed your life for a week, what would they name you after? Would
   "Christian" be obvious?

3. Barnabas "exhorted them all, that with purpose of heart they would cleave
   unto the Lord" (v.23). Deliberate, intentional commitment — not drifting
   but cleaving. How purposeful is your commitment to Christ? Is it deliberate
   or accidental?

4. Barnabas went to find Saul (v.25) — he saw potential in a man others were
   afraid of and invested a year teaching alongside him. Who has potential
   that you could invest in? Who needs a Barnabas in their life?

5. The Jerusalem church initially criticized Peter for eating with Gentiles
   (v.3). They were more concerned about tradition than about what God was
   doing. Where might your traditions be blinding you to God's current work?

6. "Then hath God also to the Gentiles granted repentance unto life" (v.18).
   Repentance is a gift from God — not just a human decision. How does seeing
   repentance as God's gift change how you pray for unbelieving friends?

7. The Antioch church was multi-ethnic from the beginning (v.20) — Jews and
   Greeks worshipping together. How diverse is your faith community? What
   barriers — racial, cultural, economic — still exist that the Gospel should
   be breaking down?

8. Barnabas saw "the grace of God" at Antioch and "was glad" (v.23). He
   recognized God's work and rejoiced in it — even though it looked different
   from Jerusalem. Can you rejoice when God works in ways that don't match
   your model?

9. The Antioch church responded to the famine prophecy by sending relief to
   Jerusalem (vv.29-30) — "every man according to his ability." Generosity
   was proportional and voluntary. How do you respond when you hear about
   needs in the body of Christ?
