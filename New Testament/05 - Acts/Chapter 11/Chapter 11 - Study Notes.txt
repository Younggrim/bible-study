Acts - Chapter 11
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Peter defends his ministry to Gentiles before the Jerusalem church, they glorify God saying "Then to the Gentiles also God has granted repentance that leads to life," and the gospel reaches Antioch where believers are first called "Christians."

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Luke (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 11 records two significant developments: Peter's
defense of his ministry to Gentiles before the Jerusalem church (vv.1-18), and
the founding of the church at Antioch — which becomes the base for Gentile
missions (vv.19-30). The chapter also introduces the name "Christians" (v.26).

Peter's Defense (vv.1-18): The Jerusalem church criticized Peter for eating
with uncircumcised Gentiles (v.3). Peter recounts the entire Cornelius episode
— the vision, the Spirit's direction, and the Gentile Pentecost. His conclusion:
"What was I, that I could withstand God?" (v.17). The church's response: "Then
hath God also to the Gentiles granted repentance unto life" (v.18).

The Church at Antioch (vv.19-26): Believers scattered by Stephen's persecution
reached Antioch — the third-largest city in the Roman Empire. Some began
preaching to Greeks (Gentiles), not just Jews. A multi-ethnic church was born.
Barnabas was sent to investigate, saw God's grace, and went to find Saul in
Tarsus. Together they taught for a year. The disciples were first called
"Christians" in Antioch.

"Christians" (v.26): The name was likely given by outsiders — possibly as a
nickname or even a slur. It means "Christ's people" or "belonging to Christ."
The believers didn't choose the name — they were identified by it because their
lives were so centered on Christ that outsiders couldn't miss it.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Antioch in Syria (v.19-26): Believers scattered by persecution traveled as far as Phoenicia, Cyprus, and Antioch — about 300 miles north of Jerusalem. Antioch became the second major center of Christianity and the base for Gentile mission.
- Tarsus (v.25): Barnabas went to Tarsus (in Cilicia, modern southeast Turkey) to find Saul and bring him to Antioch.
- Jerusalem (v.2): Peter reported to the Jerusalem church about the Gentile inclusion.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.17 — "Who was I that I could stand in God's way?" (ESV) — Peter's
        conclusion: when God acts, don't resist.

v.18 — "Then to the Gentiles also God has granted repentance that leads to
        life." (ESV) — The Jerusalem church's acceptance of Gentile inclusion.

v.21 — "And the hand of the Lord was with them, and a great number who
        believed turned to the Lord." (ESV) — God's power behind the Antioch
        church's growth.

v.23 — "He exhorted them all to remain faithful to the Lord with steadfast
        purpose." (ESV) — Barnabas' encouragement: deliberate, wholehearted
        commitment to Christ.

v.26 — "And in Antioch the disciples were first called Christians." (ESV) —
        The name that identifies Christ's people.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.3  — KJV: "they that were of the circumcision contended with him" / ESV:
       "the circumcision party criticized him" — Both equivalent. The
       "circumcision party" — Jewish believers who insisted Gentiles must
       become Jewish to be saved. They would remain a problem throughout Acts
       (chapter 15) and Paul's letters (Galatians).

v.18 — KJV: "granted repentance unto life" / ESV: "granted repentance that
       leads to life" — Both equivalent. Repentance is described as a gift
       God grants — not merely a human decision. God gives the ability to
       repent (see also Acts 5:31, 2 Timothy 2:25).

v.20 — KJV: "spake unto the Grecians" / ESV: "spoke to the Hellenists" —
       Some manuscripts read "Hellenists" (Greek-speaking Jews); others read
       "Greeks" (Hellēnas — Gentiles). If "Greeks," this is the first
       intentional Gentile evangelism by ordinary believers (not apostles).
       The textual variant is significant.

v.26 — KJV: "Christians" / ESV: "Christians" — Both identical. Greek
       "Christianoi" — "Christ-ones" or "belonging to Christ." The -iani
       suffix (Latin) indicates belonging to a party or household. They were
       identified as Christ's household.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
v.14 — NET: "a message by which you and your entire household
       will be saved" — "your entire household" (pas ho oikos sou)
       — household salvation is a pattern in Acts (16:15, 16:31,
       18:8). WEB: "you and all your house will be saved."

v.18 — NET: "God has granted the repentance that leads to life
       even to the Gentiles" — "granted" (edOken = gave as a gift).
       WEB: "God has also granted to the Gentiles repentance to
       life." KJV: "Then hath God also to the Gentiles granted
       repentance unto life." Repentance itself is a divine gift.

v.21 — NET: "The hand of the Lord was with them, and a great
       number who believed turned to the Lord" — "the hand of the
       Lord" — OT idiom for divine power/favor. WEB: "the hand of
       the Lord was with them." KJV: "the hand of the Lord was
       with them." Human preaching + divine power = conversions.

v.26 — NET: "the disciples were first called Christians at
       Antioch" — "Christians" (Christianous = Christ-followers).
       WEB: "The disciples were called Christians first in
       Antioch." KJV: "the disciples were called Christians first
       in Antioch." Originally likely a nickname/insult — they
       owned it.

v.28 — NET: "Agabus stood up and predicted by the Spirit that a
       severe famine was about to come over the whole inhabited
       world" — "predicted by the Spirit" (esEmanen dia tou
       pneumatos). WEB: "signified by the Spirit." Fulfilled under
       Claudius (AD 44-48). Prompted the relief collection.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Circumcision party — Jewish believers who insisted that Gentile converts must
               be circumcised and follow the Mosaic Law. A recurring source
               of conflict in the early church.

Antioch       — The capital of the Roman province of Syria, third-largest city
               in the empire (after Rome and Alexandria). It became the base
               for Gentile missions and the first multi-ethnic church.

Christians    — "Christ's people" — the name given to believers first at
               Antioch. Originally applied by outsiders, it became the
               defining identity of Christ's followers.

Barnabas      — "Son of encouragement" — a Levite from Cyprus who saw God's
               grace at Antioch and brought Saul from Tarsus to help teach.
               A bridge-builder and encourager.

Agabus        — A prophet from Jerusalem who predicted a famine under Claudius
               Caesar (AD 44-48). His prophecy prompted the Antioch church to
               send relief to Jerusalem.

Repentance unto life — Repentance that leads to eternal life — described as
               a gift God grants to the Gentiles (v.18).

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "What was I, that I could withstand God?" (v.17). When God is clearly at
   work — even in ways that challenge your traditions or expectations — will
   you resist or submit? Where might God be doing something new that you're
   resisting because it's unfamiliar?

2. "The disciples were called Christians first in Antioch" (v.26). They were
   so identified with Christ that outsiders named them after Him. If people
   observed your life for a week, what would they name you after? Would
   "Christian" be obvious?

3. Barnabas "exhorted them all, that with purpose of heart they would cleave
   unto the Lord" (v.23). Deliberate, intentional commitment — not drifting
   but cleaving. How purposeful is your commitment to Christ? Is it deliberate
   or accidental?

4. Barnabas went to find Saul (v.25) — he saw potential in a man others were
   afraid of and invested a year teaching alongside him. Who has potential
   that you could invest in? Who needs a Barnabas in their life?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. The Jerusalem church initially criticized Peter for eating with Gentiles
   (v.3). They were more concerned about tradition than about what God was
   doing. Where might your traditions be blinding you to God's current work?

2. "Then hath God also to the Gentiles granted repentance unto life" (v.18).
   Repentance is a gift from God — not just a human decision. How does seeing
   repentance as God's gift change how you pray for unbelieving friends?

3. The Antioch church was multi-ethnic from the beginning (v.20) — Jews and
   Greeks worshipping together. How diverse is your faith community? What
   barriers — racial, cultural, economic — still exist that the Gospel should
   be breaking down?

4. Barnabas saw "the grace of God" at Antioch and "was glad" (v.23). He
   recognized God's work and rejoiced in it — even though it looked different
   from Jerusalem. Can you rejoice when God works in ways that don't match
   your model?

5. The Antioch church responded to the famine prophecy by sending relief to
   Jerusalem (vv.29-30) — "every man according to his ability." Generosity
   was proportional and voluntary. How do you respond when you hear about
   needs in the body of Christ?
