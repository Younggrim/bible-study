Acts - Chapter 2
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
The Holy Spirit falls at Pentecost with rushing wind and tongues of fire, the apostles speak in languages they never learned, Peter preaches the first gospel sermon, three thousand are converted, and the first church is born.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Luke (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 2 records the Day of Pentecost — the birthday of
the church. Pentecost (Greek for "fiftieth") was a Jewish harvest festival
celebrated 50 days after Passover. Jews from every nation were in Jerusalem for
the feast. God chose this moment — when the world was gathered — to pour out
His Spirit and launch the church.

The Coming of the Spirit (vv.1-13): Three signs accompanied the Spirit's
arrival: sound (rushing wind), sight (tongues of fire), and speech (other
languages). The tongues were real languages (vv.6-11) — not ecstatic utterance
— enabling the Gospel to be heard by people from every nation in their own
language. The reversal of Babel (Genesis 11).

Peter's Sermon (vv.14-36): Peter — who denied Jesus seven weeks earlier — now
stands boldly before thousands and preaches the first Christian sermon. He
explains Pentecost from Joel 2, proves the resurrection from Psalm 16, and
declares Jesus as "both Lord and Christ" (v.36). The result: 3,000 saved.

The First Church (vv.42-47): The earliest description of church life — four
priorities: apostles' teaching, fellowship, breaking of bread, and prayer.
They shared possessions, met daily, and "the Lord added to the church daily
such as should be saved" (v.47).

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Jerusalem / the Upper Room (v.1): The Holy Spirit came upon the 120 disciples gathered in Jerusalem during the Feast of Pentecost (50 days after Passover).
- The list of nations (vv.9-11): Jews and proselytes from Parthia, Media, Mesopotamia, Cappadocia, Pontus, Asia, Phrygia, Pamphylia, Egypt, Libya, Rome, Crete, and Arabia — showing the geographic diversity of Jerusalem's Pentecost crowds.
- Jerusalem (v.5): "Devout men, out of every nation under heaven" were dwelling in Jerusalem — the hub where the Gospel first exploded outward.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.4  — "And they were all filled with the Holy Spirit and began to speak in
        other tongues as the Spirit gave them utterance." (ESV) — The Spirit's
        arrival and immediate effect: empowered speech.

v.21 — "And it shall come to pass that everyone who calls upon the name of
        the Lord shall be saved." (ESV) — The universal offer of salvation.

v.36 — "Let all the house of Israel therefore know for certain that God has
        made him both Lord and Christ, this Jesus whom you crucified." (ESV)
        — The conclusion of Peter's sermon: Jesus is Lord and Messiah.

v.38 — "Repent and be baptized every one of you in the name of Jesus Christ
        for the forgiveness of your sins, and you will receive the gift of
        the Holy Spirit." (ESV) — The response required: repentance and faith.

v.42 — "And they devoted themselves to the apostles' teaching and the
        fellowship, to the breaking of bread and the prayers." (ESV) — The
        four marks of the early church.

v.47 — "And the Lord added to their number day by day those who were being
        saved." (ESV) — Church growth is God's work, not human engineering.

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.2  — NET: "a sound like a violent wind blowing came from
       heaven and filled the entire house" — "violent wind" (pnoes
       biaias = a forceful breath/blast). WEB: "like a rushing
       mighty wind." KJV: "a rushing mighty wind." The word for
       "wind/breath" (pnoe) is related to pneuma (Spirit).

v.3  — NET: "tongues spreading out like a fire appeared to them
       and came to rest on each one of them" — "spreading out"
       (diamerizomenai = dividing/distributing). WEB: "tongues
       like fire appeared and were distributed to them." Individual
       flames on each person — personal, not corporate only.

v.4  — KJV: "other tongues" / ESV: "other tongues" — Both identical. Greek
       "heterais glōssais" — other languages. These were real, known languages
       (vv.6-11 list the nations represented). The miracle was in the speaking,
       not just the hearing.
       NET: "they were all filled with the Holy Spirit and began
       to speak in other languages" — "other languages" (heterais
       glOssais = different tongues). WEB: "other languages." KJV:
       "other tongues." These were real human languages (vv.8-11),
       not ecstatic speech.

v.21 — NET: "everyone who calls on the name of the Lord will be
       saved" — quoting Joel 2:32. WEB: "whoever will call on the
       name of the Lord will be saved." Peter applies Joel's "LORD"
       (Yahweh) to Jesus — a stunning Christological claim.

v.27 — KJV: "thou wilt not leave my soul in hell" / ESV: "you will not
       abandon my soul to Hades" — Greek "hadēs" — the realm of the dead,
       not the final place of punishment (gehenna). ESV uses the Greek term
       "Hades" directly; KJV uses "hell" (which modern readers may confuse
       with gehenna). Peter argues that David's prophecy (Psalm 16:10) was
       fulfilled in Christ's resurrection — His soul was not left in the grave.

v.38 — KJV: "for the remission of sins" / ESV: "for the forgiveness of your
       sins" — Both equivalent. Greek "eis aphesin" — "for" can mean "in
       order to obtain" or "because of" (like "wanted for murder" = wanted
       because of murder). Baptism is the public declaration of faith, not
       the cause of forgiveness.

v.42 — KJV: "continued stedfastly" / ESV: "devoted themselves" — Greek
       "proskarterountes" — to persist, to be devoted, to give constant
       attention to. ESV's "devoted themselves" captures the active commitment
       well. Not casual participation but committed, persistent devotion.
       NET: "They were devoting themselves to the apostles'
       teaching and to fellowship, to the breaking of bread and to
       prayer" — four marks of the early church. WEB: "continued
       steadfastly in the apostles' teaching and fellowship, in
       the breaking of bread, and prayer." KJV: "continued
       stedfastly." A pattern for all churches.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Pentecost     — Greek for "fiftieth" — the Jewish Feast of Weeks, celebrated
               50 days after Passover. A harvest festival that became the
               birthday of the church.

Tongues       — Real human languages spoken supernaturally by the Spirit's
               power. The crowd heard the Gospel in their own native languages
               — a reversal of Babel.

Pricked in heart — Convicted, pierced with guilt and awareness of sin. The
               Spirit's work through Peter's preaching produced deep conviction.

Apostles' doctrine — The teaching of the apostles — what would become the
               New Testament. The first priority of the early church.

Fellowship    — Greek "koinōnia" — sharing, partnership, community. Not just
               socializing but deep, sacrificial sharing of life and resources.

Breaking of bread — Both ordinary meals shared together and the Lord's Supper
               (communion). The early church ate together regularly.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. The Spirit came when they were "all with one accord in one place" (v.1).
   Unity preceded power. Is there disunity in your church, your family, or
   your relationships that might be hindering the Spirit's work?

2. Peter — who denied Jesus seven weeks earlier — now preaches boldly to
   thousands. The Spirit transforms cowards into courageous witnesses. Where
   do you need the Spirit's boldness? What fear is keeping you silent?

3. "Repent, and be baptized... and ye shall receive the gift of the Holy
   Ghost" (v.38). Have you repented? Have you been baptized? Are you living
   in the power of the Spirit? Which of these needs attention?

4. The early church had four priorities: teaching, fellowship, communion, and
   prayer (v.42). How does your church life compare? Which of these four is
   strongest in your experience? Which is weakest?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. The tongues at Pentecost were real languages — enabling people from every
   nation to hear the Gospel in their own tongue. God meets people where they
   are, in language they understand. How are you communicating the Gospel in
   ways people around you can actually understand?

2. Peter's sermon was simple: Jesus lived, died, rose, and is Lord (vv.22-36).
   The response: repent and believe (v.38). The Gospel is not complicated.
   Can you explain it that simply? Could you give a 3-minute summary of the
   Gospel right now?

3. "The Lord added to the church daily such as should be saved" (v.47). Church
   growth is God's work — but it happened in the context of devoted teaching,
   fellowship, communion, and prayer. Are you creating the conditions for God
   to add to your community?

4. The early believers "had all things common" and "sold their possessions"
   for those in need (vv.44-45). This was voluntary, not mandated. How
   generous are you with your resources toward other believers in need?

5. "Whosoever shall call on the name of the Lord shall be saved" (v.21).
   The offer is universal — whosoever. No one is excluded by background,
   ethnicity, or past sin. Who in your life needs to hear that "whosoever"
   includes them?
