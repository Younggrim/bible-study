Acts - Chapter 10
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
God gives Peter a vision of unclean animals declared clean, sends him to the Gentile centurion Cornelius, and the Holy Spirit falls on Gentiles for the first time — shattering the Jewish-Gentile barrier as Peter declares "God shows no partiality."

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Luke (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 10 is one of the most pivotal chapters in Acts —
the conversion of Cornelius, the first Gentile to receive the Gospel directly.
This event breaks the final barrier: the Gospel is no longer just for Jews and
Samaritans — it is for ALL nations. Peter's vision and Cornelius' conversion
establish that God "is no respecter of persons" (v.34).

Cornelius (vv.1-8): A Roman centurion — a Gentile "God-fearer" who prayed,
gave alms, and feared God, but had not converted to Judaism. He was devout but
not yet saved (11:14 — Peter would tell him "words, whereby thou and all thy
house shall be saved"). Morality and religious devotion are not enough — the
Gospel must be heard and believed.

Peter's Vision (vv.9-16): A sheet descending with unclean animals, and God's
command: "Kill, and eat." Peter refuses three times. God's response: "What God
hath cleansed, that call not thou common" (v.15). The vision is not primarily
about food — it's about people. God is declaring that Gentiles are no longer
"unclean" — the barrier between Jew and Gentile is removed in Christ.

The Gentile Pentecost (vv.44-48): While Peter is still preaching, the Holy
Spirit falls on the Gentiles — the same way He fell on the Jews at Pentecost.
The Jewish believers are "astonished" (v.45). God gave the Gentiles the same
gift — proving they are fully accepted without becoming Jewish first.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Caesarea (v.1): Cornelius was a centurion in Caesarea — the Roman administrative capital of Judea, a coastal city about 60 miles northwest of Jerusalem.
- Joppa (v.5): Peter was staying in Joppa (a port city 30 miles south of Caesarea) at the house of Simon the tanner. Cornelius sent men from Caesarea to Joppa to bring Peter.
- The geographic movement from Joppa to Caesarea represents the Gospel crossing from Jewish territory to the Gentile world.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.15 — "What God has made clean, do not call common." (ESV) — God removes
        the barriers humans create.

v.34-35 — "Truly I understand that God shows no partiality, but in every
        nation anyone who fears him and does what is right is acceptable to
        him." (ESV) — The universal scope of the Gospel.

v.36 — "Preaching good news of peace through Jesus Christ (he is Lord of
        all)." (ESV) — Jesus is Lord of ALL — not just Jews.

v.43 — "Everyone who believes in him receives forgiveness of sins through
        his name." (ESV) — Everyone — the universal offer.

v.44 — "The Holy Spirit fell on all who heard the word." (ESV) — The Gentile
        Pentecost — God's confirmation that Gentiles are fully accepted.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.2  — KJV: "one that feared God" / ESV: "a devout man who feared God" —
       Both equivalent. A technical term for Gentiles who worshipped the God
       of Israel but had not fully converted to Judaism (no circumcision).
       They attended synagogue and followed Jewish ethics but remained Gentiles.

v.14 — KJV: "common or unclean" / ESV: "common or unclean" — Both identical.
       Two categories of forbidden food: "common" (koinos) = ritually defiled;
       "unclean" (akathartos) = inherently forbidden by the Law. Peter had
       never eaten either.

v.34 — KJV: "no respecter of persons" / ESV: "God shows no partiality" —
       Greek "prosōpolēmptēs" — literally "face-taker" — one who judges by
       external appearance. ESV's "shows no partiality" is clearer to modern
       readers. God does not judge by race, nationality, or social status.

v.36 — KJV: "preaching peace by Jesus Christ: (he is Lord of all)" / ESV:
       "preaching good news of peace through Jesus Christ (he is Lord of
       all)" — ESV adds "good news of" to clarify the nature of the
       preaching. Both affirm Christ's universal lordship.

v.44 — KJV: "the Holy Ghost fell on all them" / ESV: "the Holy Spirit fell
       on all who heard the word" — Both equivalent. The same language as
       Pentecost (Acts 2:4). God gave the Gentiles the identical experience
       — proving they were fully accepted without becoming Jewish first.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
v.14 — NET: "I have never eaten anything defiled and ritually
       unclean!" — "defiled" (koinon = common/profane) and
       "unclean" (akatharton). WEB: "I have never eaten anything
       that is common or unclean." KJV: "I have never eaten any
       thing that is common or unclean." Peter's lifelong
       observance — about to be overturned.

v.15 — NET: "What God has made clean, you must not consider
       ritually unclean!" — "What God has made clean" (ha ho theos
       ekatharisen). WEB: "What God has cleansed, you must not
       call unclean." KJV: "What God hath cleansed, that call not
       thou common." Divine authority overrides human tradition.

v.34 — NET: "I now truly understand that God does not show
       favoritism in dealing with people" — "show favoritism"
       (prosOpolEmptEs = face-receiver). WEB: "God is no respecter
       of persons." KJV: "God is no respecter of persons." Peter's
       worldview shifts — the gospel is for ALL ethnicities.

v.44 — NET: "the Holy Spirit fell on all those who heard the
       message" — "fell on" (epepesen) — sudden, sovereign,
       uninvited. WEB: "the Holy Spirit fell on all those who
       heard the word." No laying on of hands, no prayer — the
       Spirit acts sovereignly on Gentiles.

v.47 — NET: "No one can withhold the water for these people to
       be baptized, who have received the Holy Spirit just as we
       did, can he?" — "just as we did" (hOs kai hEmeis) — full
       equality. WEB: "who have received the Holy Spirit just as
       we did." The Spirit's gift settles the Gentile question.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Cornelius     — A Roman centurion in Caesarea — a God-fearer, devout, generous,
               prayerful. The first Gentile to receive the Gospel directly
               from an apostle.

God-fearer    — A Gentile who worshipped Israel's God, attended synagogue,
               and followed Jewish ethics but had not fully converted through
               circumcision.

Common/Unclean — Ritually impure — forbidden under Jewish dietary and purity
               laws. God's vision declared these categories abolished in Christ.

Italian band  — A cohort of Roman soldiers from Italy stationed in Caesarea.
               Cornelius commanded about 100 men.

No respecter of persons — God does not show favoritism based on race,
               nationality, class, or any external distinction.

Gentile Pentecost — The outpouring of the Spirit on Cornelius' household —
               confirming that Gentiles receive the same gift as Jewish
               believers.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "What God hath cleansed, that call not thou common" (v.15). God removes
   barriers that humans create. Are there people you consider "unclean" or
   unworthy of the Gospel — because of their background, lifestyle, race, or
   social status? God says: don't call common what I have cleansed.

2. "God is no respecter of persons" (v.34). He doesn't favor one group over
   another. How does this truth challenge any prejudice, favoritism, or
   exclusivity in your own heart?

3. Cornelius was devout, generous, and prayerful (v.2) — but still needed to
   hear the Gospel to be saved (11:14). Morality is not enough. Religious
   devotion is not enough. Only faith in Christ saves. Do you know people who
   are "good" but don't know Christ? They still need the Gospel.

4. The Spirit fell on the Gentiles while Peter was still preaching (v.44) —
   God didn't wait for Peter to finish or for the Gentiles to be baptized
   first. God is sovereign over salvation — He moves when and how He chooses.
   Are you open to God working in unexpected ways and unexpected people?

REFLECTION QUESTIONS
------------------------------------
1. Peter had to be convinced three times (v.16) before he was willing to go
   to a Gentile's house. Even apostles have prejudices that God must overcome.
   What prejudices or assumptions might God be challenging in you right now?

2. Cornelius gathered "his kinsmen and near friends" (v.24) to hear Peter.
   When he encountered God's truth, he immediately shared it with his circle.
   Who would you gather if you had the opportunity to share the Gospel?

3. Peter said "I myself also am a man" (v.26) when Cornelius tried to worship
   him. He deflected worship immediately. How do you handle praise and
   recognition? Do you redirect it to God?

4. The Jewish believers were "astonished" that Gentiles received the Spirit
   (v.45). God's grace always goes further than we expect. Where has God's
   grace surprised you by reaching someone you didn't expect?

5. Peter asked "Can any man forbid water, that these should not be baptized?"
   (v.47). When God has clearly accepted someone, no human barrier should
   prevent their full inclusion. Are there barriers in your church that
   prevent people God has accepted from being fully included?
