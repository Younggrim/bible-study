Proverbs - Chapter 22
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Proverbs on reputation and the 'Sayings of the Wise' begin — a good name is better than riches, train up a child in the way he should go, and the opening of the thirty sayings section with warnings about boundaries and the poor.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Primarily Solomon (1:1, 10:1, 25:1), with sections by Agur
(ch.30) and King Lemuel (ch.31). Compiled over several centuries.
Proverbs is wisdom literature — practical instruction for godly
living. Hebrew title: 'Mishlei' — 'Proverbs/Comparisons.' Key
themes: the fear of the LORD as the beginning of wisdom (1:7, 9:10),
the two paths (wisdom vs. folly), the personification of Wisdom
(chs.1-9), and practical guidance for every area of life — speech,
money, work, relationships, leadership, and character.

Historical Context: Chapter 22 is transitional — vv.1-16 conclude
the first Solomonic collection (10:1-22:16), while vv.17-29 begin
the "Words of the Wise" section (22:17-24:34). This section has
notable parallels to the Egyptian "Instruction of Amenemope,"
suggesting shared ancient Near Eastern wisdom traditions. Key
proverbs include "train up a child" (v.6), "a good name" (v.1),
"the rich ruleth over the poor" (v.7), and "the diligent shall
stand before kings" (v.29).

Dominant Themes:
- Reputation and Character (v.1)
- Training Children (v.6)
- Wealth, Debt, and Servitude (vv.2, 7)
- Protection of the Poor (vv.16, 22-23)
- Diligence Brings Promotion (v.29)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "A good name is to be chosen rather than great riches, and
        favor is better than silver or gold." (ESV) — Reputation >
        wealth. Your name (your character's public testimony) is
        more valuable than any amount of money. This takes a
        lifetime to build and a moment to destroy.

v.4  — "The reward for humility and fear of the LORD is riches and
        honor and life." (ESV) — The path to riches, honor, and
        life is not ambition but humility and reverence. God's
        economy inverts the world's: the humble are exalted; the
        God-fearing are rewarded.

v.6  — "Train up a child in the way he should go; even when he is
        old he will not depart from it." (ESV) — One of the most
        quoted parenting proverbs. "Train up" (chanak) = dedicate,
        initiate, train the palate. "The way he should go" may mean
        (1) God's way, or (2) according to his individual nature/
        bent. The promise: early training produces lifelong patterns.

v.7  — "The rich rules over the poor, and the borrower is the slave
        of the lender." (ESV) — An observation (not endorsement)
        about economic power dynamics. Debt creates servitude. The
        borrower loses freedom. Financial wisdom includes avoiding
        unnecessary debt.

v.29 — "Do you see a man skillful in his work? He will stand before
        kings; he will not stand before obscure men." (ESV) —
        Diligence brings promotion. Excellence in work attracts
        attention from the powerful. If you want to "stand before
        kings," don't network — be excellent at what you do.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/proverbs-22/
  Guzik emphasizes v.6 with nuance: this is a general principle, not
  an absolute guarantee. Many godly parents have wayward children —
  the proverb states the norm, not the exception. On v.1, he notes:
  "A good name takes decades to build and seconds to lose. Guard it
  above your bank account." On v.29, Guzik states that excellence,
  not self-promotion, is the path to recognition.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "Train up a child in the way he should go. TRAIN — not merely
  teach. Training includes example, practice, discipline, and
  repetition. You train a vine by constantly directing its growth.
  You train a child by consistently shaping their habits, their
  loves, and their character. And note the promise: 'when he is
  old, he will not depart from it.' Early habits are nearly
  indestructible. Make them good ones."
TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1  — KJV: "A good name" / ESV: "A good name" — Hebrew "shem
       tov" = a good name/reputation. "Shem" is the same word for
       God's "name" — your name is your public identity, your
       reputation, the way others know you.

v.6  — KJV: "Train up" / ESV: "Train up" — Hebrew "chanak" = to
       dedicate, initiate, train. Used for dedicating a house
       (Deuteronomy 20:5) and the Temple (1 Kings 8:63). The word
       implies consecration — setting the child's direction from
       the start. "In the way he should go" = literally "according
       to his way" — possibly meaning according to his individual
       nature and capacity.

v.13 — KJV: "There is a lion without" / ESV: "There is a lion
       outside" — The sluggard's absurd excuse. He won't leave the
       house because there MIGHT be a lion in the street. Laziness
       always manufactures reasons for inaction — the more
       ridiculous the better.

v.29 — KJV: "mean men" / ESV: "obscure men" — Hebrew "chashukkim"
       = dark, obscure, undistinguished. The diligent person rises
       above obscurity to stand in the presence of nobility.
       Excellence elevates.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Good Name      — Hebrew "shem tov" = good name/reputation. The
              public testimony of your character — what people say
              about you when you're not in the room. Built slowly
              through consistent integrity; destroyed quickly
              through a single act of foolishness.

Train Up       — Hebrew "chanak" = to dedicate, initiate, narrow.
              The root idea is narrowing the path — training means
              limiting options so the child walks the right way.
              It includes instruction, example, discipline, and
              repetition until the way becomes habit.

Ancient Landmark — Hebrew "gebul 'olam" = eternal/ancient boundary.
              Property markers set by previous generations. Moving
              them was theft disguised as surveying. Respecting
              boundaries — literal and metaphorical — is a mark of
              integrity.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Guard your name above your wealth (v.1). Your reputation is your
   most valuable asset. What good is a full bank account with a
   ruined name? Make decisions that protect your character, even
   when they cost financially.

2. Train children early and consistently (v.6). Training is not a
   lecture — it's a lifestyle. It requires consistent example,
   correction, and practice over years. Start early. Stay
   consistent. The patterns set in childhood last a lifetime.

3. Avoid debt as slavery (v.7). "The borrower is servant to the
   lender." Debt costs more than interest — it costs freedom.
   Before borrowing, count the cost of servitude. Live within your
   means and preserve your liberty.

4. God defends the poor (vv.22-23). Don't exploit the vulnerable —
   God Himself will "plead their cause" and "spoil the soul of
   those that spoiled them." If you rob the poor, God will rob you.
   Be generous with the vulnerable; God is watching.

5. Diligence elevates (v.29). Want to be promoted? Be excellent.
   Not political — excellent. Do your work with such skill and
   consistency that it speaks for itself. The diligent person
   doesn't need to self-promote; their work promotes them.
