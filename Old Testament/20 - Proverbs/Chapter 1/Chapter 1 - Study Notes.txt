Proverbs - Chapter 1
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
The father-to-son prologue establishes the purpose and motto of the book — "the fear of the LORD is the beginning of knowledge" — then warns against joining sinners, and introduces personified Wisdom crying aloud in the streets, offering life to those who listen and judgment to those who refuse.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Primarily Solomon (1:1, 10:1, 25:1), with sections by Agur
(ch.30) and King Lemuel (ch.31). Compiled over several centuries.
Proverbs is wisdom literature — practical instruction for godly
living. Hebrew title: 'Mishlei' — 'Proverbs/Comparisons.' Key
themes: the fear of the LORD as the beginning of wisdom (1:7, 9:10),
the two paths (wisdom vs. folly), the personification of Wisdom
(chs.1-9), and practical guidance for every area of life — speech,
money, work, relationships, leadership, and character.

Historical Context: Chapter 1 serves as the grand introduction to
the entire book of Proverbs. Verses 1-7 form the prologue, stating
the purpose, audience, and motto of the book. The "fear of the LORD"
(v.7) is the theological foundation upon which every proverb rests.
Chapters 1-9 are distinct from the rest of Proverbs — they are
extended discourses (father-to-son speeches) rather than individual
two-line proverbs. This chapter introduces two voices that will
compete throughout the book: Wisdom (personified as a woman crying
in the streets, vv.20-33) and sinners (vv.10-19). The young man
must choose which voice to heed. Solomon likely wrote these
instructions for his son Rehoboam — tragically, Rehoboam rejected
wisdom and split the kingdom (1 Kings 12).

Structure:
- Prologue: Purpose and Motto (vv.1-7)
- A Father's Warning Against Sinners (vv.8-19)
- Wisdom's Public Appeal and Warning (vv.20-33)

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.7  — "The fear of the LORD is the beginning of knowledge; fools
        despise wisdom and instruction." (ESV) — The motto of the
        entire book. "Fear" (yir'ah) = reverence, awe, submission
        to God's authority. "Beginning" (re'shith) = first
        principle, foundation, chief part. Without God, knowledge
        has no proper starting point. Fools are not unintelligent
        — they are morally defiant.

v.10 — "My son, if sinners entice you, do not consent." (ESV) —
        The simplest and most direct command for resisting
        temptation: don't consent. Sin always requires our
        agreement. The power to say "no" is the first exercise
        of wisdom.

v.20 — "Wisdom cries aloud in the street, in the markets she
        raises her voice." (ESV) — Wisdom is not hidden or
        exclusive. She is public, accessible, loud. No one can
        claim ignorance — Wisdom makes herself available to all.
        The problem is never access but willingness.

v.32 — "For the simple are killed by their turning away, and the
        complacency of fools destroys them." (ESV) — Two dangers:
        (1) the simple turn away from wisdom and die for it;
        (2) fools prosper and their comfort destroys them. Ease
        without wisdom is fatal.

v.33 — "But whoever listens to me will dwell secure and will be
        at ease, without dread of disaster." (ESV) — The promise:
        listen to Wisdom and live in security. Not freedom from
        trouble but freedom from terror. Peace is the fruit of
        obedience.

         wisdom." (ESV) Identical principle in the Psalter.
v.7   — Proverbs 9:10 — "The fear of the LORD is the beginning of
         wisdom." (ESV) The motto repeated at the close of the
         discourse section.

         Lord." (ESV) NT continuation of the same principle.

         sinners.

         There is a deadline for responding to Wisdom.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/proverbs-1/
  Guzik notes that the prologue (vv.1-7) establishes Proverbs as
  practical, not merely theoretical. Wisdom here is skill for
  living — the Hebrew "chokmah" was used for craftsmen's skill
  (Exodus 31:3). Verse 7 is the "Alpha and Omega of all true
  knowledge." Guzik emphasizes that personified Wisdom in vv.20-33
  is no mere literary device — she represents God's own wisdom
  making itself available, yet terrifyingly, she can be refused
  until it is too late.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "The fear of the LORD is the beginning of knowledge. Not the
  middle, not the end — the BEGINNING. If your foundation is wrong,
  the whole building is unsafe. A man may be learned in ten thousand
  subjects and yet be a fool in God's estimation if he does not
  begin with the fear of the LORD. Better an unlettered peasant
  who fears God than a philosopher who does not."
TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.4  — KJV: "subtilty" / ESV: "prudence" — Hebrew "'ormah" can
       mean craftiness (negative) or prudence/shrewdness (positive).
       Same word describes the serpent in Genesis 3:1 ("subtil").
       Here it is positive — the ability to navigate complex
       situations wisely. ESV's "prudence" avoids the negative
       connotation.

v.7  — KJV: "beginning" / ESV: "beginning" — Both translate
       "re'shith" the same way. The word means first principle,
       starting point, and chief/most important part — not merely
       chronological beginning but foundational principle.

v.9  — KJV: "chains about thy neck" / ESV: "pendants for your
       neck" — Hebrew "anaqim" = necklaces/ornaments. KJV "chains"
       sounds negative to modern ears; ESV's "pendants" better
       conveys decorative jewelry. The image: wisdom adorns you
       with honor and beauty.

v.22 — KJV: "simplicity" / ESV: "being simple" — Hebrew "pethi"
       = open, naive, easily influenced. Not stupidity but
       gullibility — the one who believes everything (14:15).
       The simple person's fault is not malice but refusal to
       grow up.

v.32 — KJV: "prosperity" / ESV: "complacency" — Hebrew "shalvah"
       = ease, tranquility, carelessness. KJV "prosperity" focuses
       on the external condition; ESV "complacency" captures the
       inner attitude. The fool's comfort makes him feel no need
       for wisdom — and that ease destroys him.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Fear of the LORD — Not terror but reverence, awe, and willing
              submission to God's authority. It includes worship,
              obedience, and the recognition that God is holy and
              we are accountable to Him. It is the foundational
              disposition for all true knowledge and wisdom.

Simple (pethi) — The naive, open, gullible person who has not yet
              committed to wisdom or folly. The "simple" is at a
              crossroads — teachable but vulnerable. Proverbs
              addresses him repeatedly, urging him toward wisdom
              before folly claims him.

Wisdom (chokmah) — More than intellectual knowledge. In Proverbs,
              wisdom is skill for living — the ability to navigate
              life successfully under God's authority. It is moral,
              practical, relational, and spiritual. Personified as
              a woman in chs.1-9.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. The fear of the LORD is where everything starts (v.7). Before
   methods, before strategies, before education — reverence for
   God. If you skip this foundation, everything built above it is
   unstable. Check your foundation daily: is your life built on
   awe of God, or on self-confidence?

2. Sin always requires your consent (v.10). Temptation has no power
   without your agreement. "Consent thou not" — three words that
   can save your life. The moment you entertain the invitation, you
   have already taken the first step. Say no before the conversation
   continues.

3. Wisdom is public and available (vv.20-21). You cannot blame
   ignorance. Wisdom cries aloud in the streets — in Scripture, in
   creation, in conscience, in counsel. The issue is never "I
   couldn't find wisdom" but "I didn't want it." Are you listening?

4. There is a deadline for responding (vv.24-28). Wisdom will not
   wait forever. There comes a point when the one who has
   repeatedly refused will call and not be answered. This is not
   cruelty — it is consequence. Don't presume on tomorrow's
   availability of grace. Respond today.

5. Complacency kills (v.32). The greatest enemy of wisdom is not
   opposition but comfort. When life is easy, the fool sees no
   need for God. Guard against the prosperity that makes you feel
   self-sufficient. The comfortable fool is still a fool.
