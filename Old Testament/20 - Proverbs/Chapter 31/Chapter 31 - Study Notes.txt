Proverbs - Chapter 31
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
The words of King Lemuel (his mother's teaching on kingship) and the acrostic poem of the virtuous woman — 'who can find a virtuous woman? for her price is far above rubies'; a portrait of wisdom embodied in a godly wife.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Primarily Solomon (1:1, 10:1, 25:1), with sections by Agur
(ch.30) and King Lemuel (ch.31). Compiled over several centuries.
Proverbs is wisdom literature — practical instruction for godly
living. Hebrew title: 'Mishlei' — 'Proverbs/Comparisons.' Key
themes: the fear of the LORD as the beginning of wisdom (1:7, 9:10),
the two paths (wisdom vs. folly), the personification of Wisdom
(chs.1-9), and practical guidance for every area of life — speech,
money, work, relationships, leadership, and character.

Historical Context: Chapter 31 has two distinct sections by a unique
author. It opens with "the words of king Lemuel, the prophecy that
his mother taught him" (v.1). King Lemuel is otherwise unidentified
— some traditions equate him with Solomon (his mother being
Bathsheba), others with an Arabian or North Arabian king. The term
"massa" (oracle/prophecy) in v.1 may also indicate he was king of
Massa, an Ishmaelite territory (cf. Genesis 25:14). What is
remarkable is that this is a MOTHER's instruction to a KING — a
queen mother shaping her son's character and reign. Verses 1-9
contain her warnings against women, wine, and neglect of justice.
Verses 10-31 form the famous "Virtuous Woman" acrostic poem —
"eshet chayil" (woman of valor). This is an alphabetic acrostic:
each of the 22 verses (10-31) begins with a successive letter of
the Hebrew alphabet, from aleph to tav. The acrostic form indicates
this is a carefully composed literary masterpiece — a comprehensive,
A-to-Z portrait of feminine excellence. It is not a checklist of
duties but a celebration of character. The poem crowns the entire
book of Proverbs: wisdom, personified as a woman throughout
chapters 1-9, now takes flesh as a real woman who embodies
everything the book has taught.

Structure/Dominant Themes:
- A Mother's Oracle to a King (vv.1-9)
  * Warning Against Women (v.3)
  * Warning Against Wine (vv.4-7)
  * Command to Advocate for the Voiceless (vv.8-9)
- The Virtuous Woman — Eshet Chayil (vv.10-31)
  * Her Worth and Her Husband's Trust (vv.10-12)
  * Her Industry and Enterprise (vv.13-19, 24)
  * Her Generosity (v.20)
  * Her Preparation and Dignity (vv.21-22, 25)
  * Her Wisdom and Kindness (v.26)
  * Her Household Management (v.27)
  * Her Praise and Legacy (vv.28-31)
  * The Foundation: Fear of the LORD (v.30)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.10 — "An excellent wife who can find? She is far more precious
        than jewels." (ESV) — The opening of the acrostic poem. "Virtuous"
        (chayil) means far more than sexual purity — it means
        strength, valor, capability, excellence. It is the same word
        used of warriors (Judges 6:12 — "mighty man of valour") and
        of Ruth (Ruth 3:11 — "a virtuous woman"). The question "Who
        can find?" implies rarity and preciousness — not that such
        women do not exist, but that recognizing and valuing one
        requires wisdom. "Far above rubies" — her worth exceeds the
        most precious material treasure.

v.25 — "Strength and dignity are her clothing, and she laughs at
        the time to come." (ESV) — Her garments are metaphorical:
        strength (oz = might, force) and honour (hadar = splendor,
        dignity, majesty). These are character qualities worn as
        visibly as clothing. "She shall rejoice in time to come" —
        literally "she laughs at the future." She faces the future
        without anxiety because her preparation, character, and faith
        have made her secure. She is not worried about what will come
        because she has built her life on what endures.

v.26 — "She opens her mouth with wisdom, and the teaching of
        kindness is on her tongue." (ESV) — Her speech is characterized by two
        things: wisdom (chokmah — the skill for living that Proverbs
        has been teaching throughout) and "the law of kindness"
        (torath chesed — the Torah/instruction of loyal love). She is
        not merely intelligent; she is gracious. Her words build up,
        instruct, and comfort. This is the culmination of Proverbs'
        teaching on speech: wisdom and kindness united in one tongue.

v.30 — "Charm is deceitful, and beauty is vain, but a woman who
        fears the LORD is to be praised." (ESV) — The
        theological center of the poem and the key to the entire
        portrait. All the industry, wisdom, generosity, and strength
        described in vv.10-29 have ONE foundation: the fear of the
        LORD. Without it, charm is manipulation and beauty is vapor.
        With it, a woman's life becomes praiseworthy in the deepest
        sense. This verse connects the Virtuous Woman back to
        Proverbs 1:7 — "The fear of the LORD is the beginning of
        wisdom." The book ends where it began.

v.8-9 — "Open your mouth for the mute, for the rights of all who
        are destitute. Open your mouth, judge righteously, defend
        the rights of the poor and needy." (ESV) — The queen mother's positive command to her son:
        use your royal power for advocacy. "The dumb" = those who
        cannot speak for themselves. "Appointed to destruction" =
        those passing away, the perishing. The king's voice must
        speak for the voiceless; his power must protect the
        powerless. Righteous judgment and advocacy for the poor
        are not optional virtues for a king — they are his
        primary calling.

         husband." (ESV) Same word (chayil) — she is his glory.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/proverbs-31/
  Guzik emphasizes that the Proverbs 31 woman is not meant to
  produce guilt but to celebrate what godly womanhood looks like
  when the fear of the LORD is its foundation. He notes: "This is
  not a daily to-do list but a lifetime portrait. No woman does all
  these things in a single day — this is the accumulated testimony
  of a life lived in wisdom and faithfulness." On v.30, Guzik
  connects the acrostic to the book's central theme: "The entire
  book of Proverbs began with 'the fear of the LORD is the
  beginning of wisdom' (1:7). It ends with a woman who FEARS the
  LORD and receives praise. The book's theology is embodied in a
  person — wisdom incarnate in a real life." On the queen mother's
  instruction (vv.1-9), he notes the influence of a godly mother
  on a future king: "The most powerful man in the nation was shaped
  by his mother's words."

Charles Spurgeon:
  "Who can find a virtuous woman? — the question implies both
  rarity and supreme value. But note that the poem does not end
  with charm or beauty. 'Favour is deceitful, and beauty is vain.'
  The world worships at the altar of appearance; God looks upon the
  heart. A woman that feareth the LORD — there is the crown jewel.
  All her industry, all her wisdom, all her kindness flows from
  this one fountain: the fear of God. Take away that spring and
  the river dries. Preserve that spring and everything downstream
  flourishes. Young men — look for the fear of the LORD before you
  look for anything else. Everything else fades; that alone endures."
TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.10 — "virtuous" = Hebrew "chayil" — a word used over 240 times
       in the OT, primarily for military valor, strength, wealth,
       and ability. It is translated "army" (Exodus 14:4), "valour"
       (Judges 6:12), "might" (Deuteronomy 8:18), "able" (Exodus
       18:21). Applied to a woman, it means strength of character,
       capability, and moral excellence — far broader than the
       English "virtuous" suggests. "Eshet chayil" = woman of
       valor/strength/excellence.

v.1 — "Lemuel" = possibly "belonging to God" or "devoted to God."
      "The prophecy" = Hebrew "massa" — may mean "oracle" (a
      prophetic utterance) OR may be a place name ("king of Massa,"
      an Ishmaelite territory in Genesis 25:14; 1 Chronicles 1:30).
      If a place name, Lemuel was a non-Israelite king whose
      mother's wisdom was preserved in Israel's canon — showing
      that wisdom transcends ethnic boundaries.

v.30 — "Favour" = Hebrew "chen" = grace, charm, attractiveness.
       "Deceitful" = Hebrew "sheqer" = false, lying, deceptive.
       Charm LIES — it presents a false picture. "Beauty" = Hebrew
       "yophi" = physical loveliness. "Vain" = Hebrew "hebel" =
       vapor, breath, emptiness (the same word used 38 times in
       Ecclesiastes — "vanity of vanities"). Beauty is not sinful
       but it is VAPOR — transient, insubstantial, unreliable as a
       measure of worth.

v.26 — "the law of kindness" = Hebrew "torath chesed." "Torah" =
       instruction, teaching, law. "Chesed" = loyal love, covenant
       faithfulness, steadfast kindness. Her tongue carries the
       Torah of chesed — a consistent instruction characterized by
       covenant love. This is not mere pleasantness but a life-
       principle of gracious, faithful speech.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Eshet Chayil (v.10) — Hebrew phrase meaning "woman of valor" or
              "woman of strength/excellence." In Jewish tradition,
              husbands sing or recite Proverbs 31:10-31 to their
              wives at the Shabbat table on Friday evenings as a
              blessing and celebration. The phrase has become a
              term of highest honor in Jewish culture — spoken at
              weddings, funerals, and whenever a woman's life is
              being celebrated.

Acrostic Poem — A literary structure in which each line or verse
              begins with successive letters of the Hebrew alphabet
              (22 letters, aleph through tav). Used in Psalms 119,
              Psalm 25, Lamentations 1-4, and here. The form
              indicates careful, deliberate composition and often
              suggests completeness — the subject is covered "from
              A to Z" (or aleph to tav). It also aided memorization
              in oral cultures.

Chesed (v.26) — Hebrew word for loyal love, steadfast kindness,
              covenant faithfulness. One of the most important
              theological terms in the OT — used over 240 times
              to describe God's character and the love expected in
              covenant relationships. It combines loyalty, love,
              mercy, and faithfulness into a single concept that
              has no exact English equivalent. The Proverbs 31
              woman's speech is governed by this quality.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Character over charm (v.30). The world's system evaluates women
   (and men) by appearance, personality, and social appeal. God's
   system evaluates by the fear of the LORD. Charm lies — it
   presents a curated image that may not reflect reality. Beauty
   is vapor — it fades with time. But the fear of the LORD endures
   and produces genuine, lasting praise. Evaluate yourself and
   others by what endures, not by what impresses.

2. Use your voice for the voiceless (vv.8-9). The queen mother's
   command to her son applies to everyone with any measure of
   influence: speak up for those who cannot speak for themselves.
   Plead the cause of the poor and needy. Righteous judgment is
   not passive — it actively intervenes on behalf of the
   oppressed. Who are the voiceless in your world? What would it
   look like to open your mouth on their behalf?

3. The fear of the LORD is the foundation of everything (v.30).
   The Proverbs 31 woman's industry, wisdom, generosity, and
   strength are not the CAUSE of her praise — they are the FRUIT
   of her fear of God. Without that root, the tree produces
   nothing lasting. Whatever you build — career, family,
   ministry, character — build it on the fear of the LORD. Every
   other foundation is sand.

4. Preparation removes anxiety (v.25). She "laughs at the future"
   not because she is naive but because she has prepared. Faithful
   daily work — physical, spiritual, relational — builds a life
   that can face the unknown without fear. Anxiety often reveals
   neglected preparation. What are you doing TODAY that will allow
   you to laugh at TOMORROW? Preparation is faith expressed in
   action.

5. Wisdom and kindness together (v.26). Her speech has BOTH: the
   torah of wisdom and the torah of chesed. Truth without kindness
   is brutality. Kindness without truth is sentimentality. The
   godly life unites both — speaking what is true in a way that
   is gracious. Evaluate your words: Are they wise? Are they kind?
   The Proverbs 31 standard is both, never one without the other.
