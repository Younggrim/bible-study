Proverbs - Chapter 25
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Hezekiah's collection of Solomon's proverbs begins — proverbs on kings, self-control, neighborly conduct, and apt speech; 'a word fitly spoken is like apples of gold in settings of silver.'

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Primarily Solomon (1:1, 10:1, 25:1), with sections by Agur
(ch.30) and King Lemuel (ch.31). Compiled over several centuries.
Proverbs is wisdom literature — practical instruction for godly
living. Hebrew title: 'Mishlei' — 'Proverbs/Comparisons.' Key
themes: the fear of the LORD as the beginning of wisdom (1:7, 9:10),
the two paths (wisdom vs. folly), the personification of Wisdom
(chs.1-9), and practical guidance for every area of life — speech,
money, work, relationships, leadership, and character.

Historical Context: Chapter 25 begins the second Solomonic
collection (25:1-29:27), introduced with the editorial note:
"These are also proverbs of Solomon, which the men of Hezekiah
king of Judah copied out" (v.1). Hezekiah reigned c.715-686 BC —
roughly 250 years after Solomon. His scribes collected, edited, and
preserved Solomonic proverbs that had not been included in the
earlier collection (10:1-22:16). This chapter is rich in vivid
comparisons and similes, covering topics of kingship (vv.2-7),
speech (vv.11-15), self-control (vv.16-17, 28), responding to
enemies (vv.21-22), and the famous "apples of gold" image (v.11).

Dominant Themes:
- The Glory of Kings and the Glory of God (vv.2-7)
- The Power of Appropriate Speech (vv.11-15)
- Self-Control and Moderation (vv.16-17, 27-28)
- Kindness to Enemies (vv.21-22)
- Vivid Similes and Comparisons Throughout


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.11 — "A word fitly spoken is like apples of gold in a setting of
        silver." (ESV) — The right word at the right time in the
        right way is exquisite — as beautiful and valuable as golden
        apples displayed in silver settings. Timing, tone, and truth
        combine to make speech precious. This is the gold standard
        of communication.

v.21-22 — "If your enemy is hungry, give him bread to eat, and if
           he is thirsty, give him water to drink, for you will
           heap burning coals on his head, and the LORD will reward
           you." (ESV) — Feed your enemy. Meet his physical need.
           The "coals of fire" may refer to burning conviction
           (shame that leads to repentance) or the Egyptian practice
           of carrying coals on the head as a sign of contrition.
           Either way: respond to hatred with practical kindness.
           God Himself will reward you.

v.28 — "A man without self-control is like a city broken into and
        left without walls." (ESV) — Without self-control, you are
        defenseless. In the ancient world, a city without walls was
        utterly vulnerable to every enemy. Self-control is your
        wall. Without it, every temptation, every provocation,
        every impulse walks right in unopposed.

v.2  — "It is the glory of God to conceal things, but the glory of
        kings is to search things out." (ESV) — God's glory
        includes mystery — He conceals things because He is
        infinitely above us. But human rulers are honored when they
        investigate, research, and uncover truth. God hides; kings
        seek. Both activities are glorious in their proper sphere.

v.15 — "With patience a ruler may be persuaded, and a soft tongue
        will break a bone." (ESV) — Patience and gentleness are
        more powerful than force. A soft tongue can break bone — can
        overcome the hardest resistance. Persistence + gentleness =
        irresistible persuasion. Don't escalate — endure.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.6-7 — Luke 14:7-11 — Jesus' parable of taking the lowest seat.
         Direct allusion to this proverb: "better to be told 'come
         up higher' than to be humiliated."
v.11  — Colossians 4:6 — "Let your speech always be gracious,
         seasoned with salt." (ESV) Speech that is both gracious
         and apt.
v.15  — James 3:17 — Wisdom is "gentle, open to reason." (ESV)
         Soft persuasion over forceful argument.
v.21-22 — Romans 12:20-21 — Paul directly quotes this passage as
         the Christian ethic: "overcome evil with good." (ESV)
v.22  — Matthew 5:44 — "Love your enemies and pray for those who
         persecute you." (ESV) Jesus expands the principle.
v.25  — Proverbs 15:30 — "The light of the eyes rejoices the
         heart, and good news refreshes the bones." (ESV) Good
         news as refreshment.
v.28  — Galatians 5:22-23 — "The fruit of the Spirit is... self-
         control." (ESV) The Spirit produces what the flesh cannot.
v.28  — Proverbs 16:32 — "Whoever is slow to anger is better than
         the mighty." (ESV) Self-rule > military conquest.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/proverbs-25/
  Guzik notes the significance of v.1: Hezekiah's men "copied out"
  these proverbs — showing that Scripture preservation involved
  human effort guided by divine providence. On v.11, he explains:
  "'Apples of gold in settings of silver' — the right word is both
  inherently beautiful (gold) and perfectly framed (silver setting).
  Content and context both matter." On v.28, he emphasizes that
  ancient cities without walls were abandoned as indefensible — so
  the person without self-control is practically ruined.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "He that hath no rule over his own spirit is like a city that is
  broken down, and without walls. Mark well this picture! A city
  without walls in ancient days was no city at all — it was a ruin,
  an invitation to every marauder. So the man without self-control:
  every temptation enters freely, every provocation conquers him,
  every passion rules him. He is not a fortress — he is a ruin.
  Build your walls, Christian. Let the Spirit of God build what
  your flesh cannot maintain."
TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1  — KJV: "the men of Hezekiah... copied out" / ESV: "copied" —
       Hebrew "he'thiqu" = to move, transfer, transcribe. These
       scribes collected and transcribed Solomonic proverbs from
       various sources into a unified collection — an ancient
       editorial and scribal process of canonical formation.

v.11 — KJV: "apples of gold in pictures of silver" / ESV: "apples
       of gold in a setting of silver" — Hebrew "tappuche zahav
       b'maskiyyoth keseph." "Pictures" (KJV) is better rendered
       "settings" or "filigree work" — ornamental silver frames or
       baskets. "Apples" may refer to apricots, oranges, or
       ornamental golden fruit. The image: exquisite content in an
       equally exquisite frame.

v.20 — KJV: "As he that taketh away a garment in cold weather, and
       as vinegar upon nitre" / ESV: "Like one who takes off a
       garment on a cold day, and like vinegar on soda" — "Nitre"
       (KJV) = natron/soda (not modern "niter"/saltpeter). Vinegar
       on natron causes a fizzing reaction — dissolving it. Singing
       songs to a heavy heart is equally destructive and
       inappropriate.

v.22 — KJV: "heap coals of fire upon his head" / ESV: same — The
       metaphor's origin is debated. Egyptian parallels suggest a
       ritual of carrying burning coals on the head as a sign of
       repentance/shame. Others interpret it as the burning pain of
       conviction. Paul quotes this in Romans 12:20 without
       explaining the metaphor, treating it as self-evident.

v.1 — NET: "copied:" for KJV's "copied out."
v.11 — ASV: "Is [like]" for KJV's "is like"
       NET: "Like" for KJV's "A word fitly spoken is like"
v.20 — ASV: "[As] one" for KJV's "As he"
       NET: "Like one who takes off" for KJV's "As he that taketh away"
       WEB: "one who takes" for KJV's "he that taketh"
v.22 — ASV: uses "Jehovah" for KJV's "LORD" (Hebrew YHWH).
       NET: "for you will" for KJV's "For thou shalt"
       WEB: uses "Yahweh" for KJV's "LORD" (Hebrew YHWH).

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Apples of Gold — Hebrew "tappuche zahav" = possibly golden apples,
              apricots, or ornamental golden fruit. The precise
              fruit is uncertain; the point is beauty and value.
              Combined with silver "settings" (maskiyyoth), the
              image portrays speech that is both beautiful in
              content and perfectly timed in delivery.

Coals of Fire  — A metaphor for the burning effect of kindness on
              an enemy. Whether interpreted as conviction (shame
              leading to repentance) or an Egyptian penitence
              ritual, the principle is clear: kindness is more
              powerful than revenge. It conquers the enemy's heart
              rather than merely conquering his body.

Self-Control   — The capacity to rule one's own spirit (v.28).
              Hebrew concept of governing impulses, appetites, and
              reactions. Without it, a person is an unwalled city —
              defenseless against every attack. With it, a person
              is greater than a military conqueror (16:32).

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Speak golden words in silver settings (v.11). Before you speak,
   ask: Is this the right word? Is this the right time? Is this the
   right tone? When all three align, your words become priceless.
   Most of us get the content right but miss the timing or tone.

2. Feed your enemy (vv.21-22). This is not passive tolerance — it
   is ACTIVE kindness. When someone who hates you is hungry, give
   them bread. Don't just refrain from revenge — DO GOOD to those
   who harm you. God will reward it, and it may melt their heart.

3. Build your walls — develop self-control (v.28). What area of
   your life has "broken walls"? Anger? Lust? Spending? Appetite?
   Wherever you lack self-control, you are undefended. Ask the
   Spirit to rebuild what the flesh has destroyed.

4. Pursue moderation in all things (vv.16-17). "Hast thou found
   honey? eat so much as is sufficient." Even good things become
   harmful in excess. Friendships, pleasures, even ministry — know
   your limits. The person who never knows when to stop will
   eventually destroy what they love.

5. Take the lower seat (vv.6-7). Don't promote yourself. Don't
   claim honor you haven't been given. It is always better to be
   invited up than pushed down. Humility positions you for God's
   exaltation; self-promotion positions you for public humiliation.
