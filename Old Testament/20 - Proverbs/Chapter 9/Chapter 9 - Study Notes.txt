Proverbs - Chapter 9
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
The final contrast of the discourse section — Lady Wisdom builds her house and hosts a feast, inviting the simple; Folly also sits and calls, but her guests end in Sheol. 'The fear of the LORD is the beginning of wisdom.'

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Primarily Solomon (1:1, 10:1, 25:1), with sections by Agur
(ch.30) and King Lemuel (ch.31). Compiled over several centuries.
Proverbs is wisdom literature — practical instruction for godly
living. Hebrew title: 'Mishlei' — 'Proverbs/Comparisons.' Key
themes: the fear of the LORD as the beginning of wisdom (1:7, 9:10),
the two paths (wisdom vs. folly), the personification of Wisdom
(chs.1-9), and practical guidance for every area of life — speech,
money, work, relationships, leadership, and character.

Historical Context: Chapter 9 is the climax and conclusion of the
discourse section (chs.1-9). It presents the final choice between
two women — Lady Wisdom and Lady Folly — each hosting a banquet,
each calling out to the simple. The symmetry is deliberate: both
call from the high places (vv.3, 14), both address the simple
(vv.4, 16), both offer food and drink (vv.5, 17). But the outcomes
could not be more different: Wisdom's table leads to life; Folly's
banquet seats the dead. The "seven pillars" (v.1) represent perfect
completeness — Wisdom's house is fully established and secure. The
central pivot (vv.7-12) distinguishes the wise from the scoffer:
the wise love correction; scorners hate it. Verse 10 repeats the
motto of 1:7 — framing the entire discourse section with "the
fear of the LORD."

Structure:
- Lady Wisdom's Banquet (vv.1-6)
- The Wise vs. the Scoffer (vv.7-12)
- Lady Folly's Banquet (vv.13-18)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "Wisdom has built her house; she has hewn her seven
        pillars." (ESV) — Wisdom is established, prepared, stable.
        Seven pillars = perfection, completeness. Her house is not
        hastily constructed but carefully built. To enter wisdom is
        to enter security and abundance.

v.8  — "Do not reprove a scoffer, or he will hate you; reprove a
        wise man, and he will love you." (ESV) — The definitive
        test of character: how do you respond to correction? The
        wise man loves the person who corrects him. The scoffer
        hates the very one trying to help. Your response to rebuke
        reveals whether you are wise or foolish.

v.10 — "The fear of the LORD is the beginning of wisdom, and the
        knowledge of the Holy One is insight." (ESV) — The motto
        restated (cf. 1:7). Here "beginning" is "techillah" (the
        starting point) rather than "re'shith" (first principle/
        chief part) as in 1:7. Together: the fear of the LORD is
        both the foundation and the starting point of wisdom.

v.17 — "Stolen water is sweet, and bread eaten in secret is
        pleasant." (ESV) — Folly's only argument: forbidden things
        taste better. The thrill of the illicit, the excitement of
        secrecy. It is true — sin has pleasure (Hebrews 11:25). But
        v.18 reveals the price.

v.18 — "But he does not know that the dead are there, that her
        guests are in the depths of Sheol." (ESV) — The hidden
        truth behind Folly's banquet: her dining room is a morgue.
        Her guests are corpses. The "he" doesn't know — that's the
        horror. He walks in blind.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/proverbs-9/
  Guzik emphasizes the parallel structure: both Wisdom and Folly
  call from the same high places to the same audience. The simple
  person must choose which invitation to accept. He notes that
  Folly's only selling point is transgression itself — "stolen
  waters are sweet." She has no genuine product, only the thrill
  of the forbidden. On v.8, Guzik states the practical test:
  "If you can't receive correction, you're not wise — regardless
  of how much you know."

Charles Spurgeon:
  "Rebuke a wise man, and he will love thee. This is the infallible
  mark of wisdom: the love of correction. Not the mere tolerance of
  it — the LOVE of it. The wise man sees the rebuker as a friend,
  not an enemy. He knows that faithful wounds are better than
  deceitful kisses. If you want to know whether you are growing in
  wisdom, ask yourself: am I more or less receptive to correction
  than I was a year ago? The direction of your answer reveals the
  direction of your soul."
TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1  — KJV: "hewn out her seven pillars" / ESV: "hewn her seven
       pillars" — Hebrew "chatsab" = to cut, hew, quarry stone.
       Wisdom's house is built with cut stone — permanent, solid,
       crafted. The seven pillars have been variously interpreted
       as the seven days of creation, seven liberal arts, etc.
       Most likely: seven = completeness/perfection.

v.10 — KJV: "knowledge of the holy" / ESV: "knowledge of the Holy
       One" — Hebrew "da'ath qedoshim" (plural). KJV leaves it
       ambiguous; ESV capitalizes and singularizes: "the Holy One"
       (God). The plural "qedoshim" could be a plural of majesty
       (referring to God) or a general plural (holy things/beings).
       Most interpreters agree: knowing GOD is the essence of
       understanding.

v.13 — KJV: "A foolish woman is clamorous" / ESV: "The woman Folly
       is loud" — ESV personalizes her as "Woman Folly" (matching
       "Lady Wisdom"), making the parallel clearer. Both are loud —
       both compete for attention. The difference is their character
       and destination.

v.17 — KJV: "Stolen waters are sweet" / ESV: "Stolen water is
       sweet" — Identical sense. "Water" likely refers to sexual
       pleasure (cf. 5:15-18 where water = marital intimacy). Stolen
       water = sexual pleasure taken outside of marriage covenant.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Seven Pillars  — The structural supports of Wisdom's house.
              Seven represents completeness in Hebrew thought.
              Wisdom's dwelling is fully established — perfectly
              supported, lacking nothing. To enter her house is
              to enter a complete, stable, abundant life.

Scoffer/Scorner — Hebrew "lets" = the mocker, the one who ridicules
              wisdom, correction, and authority. Worse than the
              "simple" (who is merely naive) — the scoffer
              actively opposes wisdom. He cannot be taught because
              he refuses to be corrected. The most dangerous
              spiritual condition.

Lady Folly     — The personification of foolishness as a seductive
              woman, the anti-Wisdom. She is "loud" (aggressive),
              "simple" (empty-headed despite her confidence), and
              "knows nothing" (v.13). She mimics Wisdom's methods
              (calling from high places) but offers only death.
              A counterfeit always imitates the genuine.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Both wisdom and folly are calling (vv.3-4, 14-16). Every day
   you hear both voices. Both sound inviting. Both promise
   satisfaction. The question is: which invitation will you accept?
   Learn to recognize the voices. Wisdom calls openly with truth;
   Folly lures secretly with the thrill of the forbidden.

2. How you respond to correction reveals everything (v.8). Do you
   love correction or hate it? Do you thank the person who points
   out your error, or do you resent them? This single test
   separates the wise from the foolish more reliably than any
   other. Cultivate a heart that welcomes rebuke.

3. "If thou be wise, thou shalt be wise for thyself" (v.12). The
   benefits of wisdom accrue to YOU. The costs of folly are paid
   by YOU. No one else can be wise for you, and no one else suffers
   your foolishness as much as you do. This is deeply personal.
   Take ownership.

4. Sin's only appeal is the thrill of transgression (v.17). "Stolen
   waters are sweet." If it weren't forbidden, it wouldn't be
   exciting. Recognize this: sin has no genuine substance — only
   the adrenaline of rebellion. When you strip away the illicit
   thrill, what remains? Nothing. Emptiness. Death.

5. Know what's behind the door (v.18). Before you accept any
   invitation — to a relationship, a lifestyle, a decision —
   ask: who else accepted this invitation, and where are they now?
   Folly's guest list is the dead. Check the outcomes before you
   RSVP.
