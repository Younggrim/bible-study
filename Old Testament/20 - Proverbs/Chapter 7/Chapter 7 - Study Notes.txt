Proverbs - Chapter 7
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A vivid narrative warning — the father describes watching a naive young man seduced by an adulterous woman with flattery and false security; he follows her 'as an ox goeth to the slaughter.'

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Primarily Solomon (1:1, 10:1, 25:1), with sections by Agur
(ch.30) and King Lemuel (ch.31). Compiled over several centuries.
Proverbs is wisdom literature — practical instruction for godly
living. Hebrew title: 'Mishlei' — 'Proverbs/Comparisons.' Key
themes: the fear of the LORD as the beginning of wisdom (1:7, 9:10),
the two paths (wisdom vs. folly), the personification of Wisdom
(chs.1-9), and practical guidance for every area of life — speech,
money, work, relationships, leadership, and character.

Historical Context: Chapter 7 is the most vivid and dramatic passage
on sexual temptation in all of Scripture. It is a narrative — Solomon
tells what he witnessed from his window: a naive young man seduced
step by step. The scene is cinematic — twilight, darkness, a woman
dressed to seduce, bold speech, a bedroom prepared. The young man is
described as "void of understanding" (v.7) — not because he lacks
intelligence but because he put himself in the wrong place at the
wrong time. Every detail is designed as a warning: this is how it
happens. The chapter ends with a body count (v.26) — she has killed
many, including strong men. Her house is the highway to Sheol.

Structure:
- Command: Treasure Wisdom as Protection (vv.1-5)
- Narrative: The Seduction Witnessed (vv.6-23)
- Conclusion: Warning and Death Toll (vv.24-27)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.7  — "a young man lacking sense." (ESV) — The root problem: he
        lacks sense ("lev" = heart/mind). He is not actively wicked
        — he is naive and foolish. He wanders near her door because
        he hasn't internalized the danger. Naivety is not innocence;
        it is vulnerability.

v.9  — "in the twilight, in the evening, at the time of night and
        darkness." (ESV) — Three markers of darkness: twilight,
        evening, dark night. Sin loves darkness (John 3:19). The
        young man chose the wrong time as well as the wrong place.
        Darkness conceals shame — but not from God.

v.21 — "With much seductive speech she persuades him; with her
        smooth talk she compels him." (ESV) — "Compels" is striking
        — not physical force but the overwhelming power of seductive
        speech. Words have power to overcome resistance. This is why
        wisdom must be internalized BEFORE the moment of temptation.

v.22-23 — "All at once he follows her, as an ox goes to the
           slaughter...till an arrow pierces its liver." (ESV) —
           Three animal images: ox to slaughter (unaware of death),
           stag caught in a trap (trapped and pierced), bird rushing
           into a snare (eager for its own destruction). The young
           man doesn't realize he is dying.

v.26 — "for many a victim has she laid low, and all her slain are
        a mighty throng." (ESV) — Don't think you're too strong.
        STRONG men have fallen here. If Samson fell, if David fell
        — your strength will not save you. Her body count is
        massive. You are not the exception.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.2   — Deuteronomy 32:10 — "He kept him as the apple of his eye."
         (ESV) God guards Israel as Solomon urges guarding God's
         law.
v.4   — Ruth 2:1 — "Kinswoman" = intimate family relationship.
         Wisdom should be as close as family.
v.9   — John 3:19 — "People loved the darkness rather than the
         light because their works were evil." (ESV) Sin seeks the
         cover of night.
v.14  — Leviticus 7:16-17 — Peace offerings were partly eaten by
         the offerer. She uses religion as a pretext for the feast/
         seduction — profaning the sacred.
v.22  — Isaiah 53:7 — "Like a lamb that is led to the slaughter."
         (ESV) Same image applied to Christ — but willingly,
         redemptively.
v.26  — Judges 16:4-21 — Samson and Delilah. A strong man slain
         by seduction.
v.26  — 2 Samuel 11:1-4 — David and Bathsheba. A man after God's
         own heart, wounded by this very sin.
v.27  — Proverbs 2:18-19 — "Her house sinks down to death." (ESV)
         Repeated verdict from earlier warning.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/proverbs-7/
  Guzik emphasizes that the young man's first mistake was location:
  "passing through the street near her corner" (v.8). He didn't
  fall suddenly — he positioned himself for failure gradually. Guzik
  notes the woman's use of religious language (v.14) to justify sin
  — she has "paid her vows" and now invites him to a feast of
  leftover fellowship offerings. Religion becomes the alibi. The
  animal imagery (vv.22-23) shows that the seduced man has lost his
  human dignity — he is reduced to a dumb animal being led to death.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "Many STRONG men have been slain by her. Not merely the weak
  and foolish — the strong. Samson was strong; David was a man
  after God's own heart; Solomon himself was the wisest man alive.
  All fell. If you think your strength will preserve you, you have
  already begun to fall. The only safety is distance — do not go
  near her door. The man who trusts his own willpower in the
  presence of temptation is already an ox on its way to slaughter."
TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.10 — KJV: "subtil of heart" / ESV: "wily of heart" — Hebrew
       "netsurath lev" = guarded/hidden of heart (her true
       intentions are concealed). She is calculating, not
       spontaneous. Her seduction is planned and deliberate.

v.14 — KJV: "peace offerings" / ESV: "sacrifices" — Hebrew
       "shelamim" = peace/fellowship offerings. These were the
       only sacrifices where the offerer ate a portion (Leviticus
       7:15-16). She has food and wine at home from her offering —
       a ready-made feast, and a religious cover for the invitation.

v.22 — KJV: "as a fool to the correction of the stocks" / ESV:
       "as a stag is caught fast" — The Hebrew here is difficult
       and textually uncertain. KJV follows the Masoretic text
       more closely (fool/stocks); ESV follows ancient versions
       and emends slightly to maintain the animal imagery (stag/
       fetters). Both convey: he is trapped and will suffer.

v.26 — KJV: "many strong men have been slain" / ESV: "all her
       slain are a mighty throng" — Hebrew "atsumim" = mighty,
       numerous, powerful. KJV emphasizes their individual
       strength; ESV emphasizes the sheer number of victims.
       Both truths are devastating.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Apple of the Eye — Hebrew "ishon" = the pupil, the little man
              (from the reflection seen in another's pupil). The
              most sensitive, precious, carefully guarded part of
              the body. Guarding God's word as the apple of your
              eye means treating it as irreplaceably precious.

Peace Offering — Hebrew "shelamim" = the fellowship/peace offering.
              Unlike burnt offerings (entirely consumed) or sin
              offerings (eaten by priests), peace offerings were
              shared meals — God, priest, and offerer all received
              a portion. The adulteress uses this as pretext: she
              has a feast waiting. Religion masks sin.

Sheol          — The realm of the dead. Her house is not merely a
              path to trouble but to death itself. The repeated
              verdict: adultery's destination is Sheol — spiritual,
              relational, and ultimately physical death.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Location matters (v.8). The young man's first mistake was being
   near her corner at night. He didn't plan to sin — he planned to
   be nearby. Where are you putting yourself that makes temptation
   easy? Avoid the street, the app, the conversation, the
   situation. Your feet decide your fate.

2. Sin is planned; you must plan against it (vv.10-20). She
   prepared the bed, the perfume, the speech, the timing. If sin
   plans this carefully, your defense must be equally intentional.
   Casual resistance fails against calculated temptation. Plan
   your escape routes before you need them.

3. Words have seductive power (v.21). "With her much fair speech
   she caused him to yield." The ear is the gateway. What voices
   are you listening to? What smooth words are eroding your
   resolve? Guard your ears as carefully as your eyes.

4. You are not stronger than the strong men who fell (v.26).
   "Many strong men have been slain." Don't trust your own
   strength. If David fell, if Samson fell, if Solomon himself
   fell — humility is your only safety. Acknowledge your weakness
   and let that acknowledgment drive you to dependence on God.

5. The end is death (v.27). "Her house is the way to hell." No
   ambiguity. No middle ground. The path of sexual sin leads to
   death — death of marriage, death of reputation, death of
   intimacy with God, potentially death of the body. Count the
   cost before you take the first step.
