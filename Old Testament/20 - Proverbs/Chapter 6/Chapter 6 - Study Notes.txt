Proverbs - Chapter 6
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Warnings against foolish surety, laziness (consider the ant), and the seven things the LORD hates; then an extended warning against adultery — the man who commits it destroys himself.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Primarily Solomon (1:1, 10:1, 25:1), with sections by Agur
(ch.30) and King Lemuel (ch.31). Compiled over several centuries.
Proverbs is wisdom literature — practical instruction for godly
living. Hebrew title: 'Mishlei' — 'Proverbs/Comparisons.' Key
themes: the fear of the LORD as the beginning of wisdom (1:7, 9:10),
the two paths (wisdom vs. folly), the personification of Wisdom
(chs.1-9), and practical guidance for every area of life — speech,
money, work, relationships, leadership, and character.

Historical Context: Chapter 6 is distinctive within the discourse
section (chs.1-9) because it addresses multiple topics in a more
varied format. It covers four dangers: foolish financial pledges
(vv.1-5), laziness (vv.6-11), the worthless troublemaker (vv.12-15),
and adultery (vv.20-35). Embedded within is the famous "seven things
the LORD hates" list (vv.16-19) — one of the few places in Proverbs
that directly states what God abhors. The ant passage (vv.6-8) is
among the most memorable in wisdom literature — nature itself teaches
diligence. The adultery warning (vv.27-35) uses unforgettable images:
carrying fire in your chest, walking on hot coals.

Structure:
- Warning Against Foolish Pledges (vv.1-5)
- The Ant and the Sluggard (vv.6-11)
- The Worthless Troublemaker (vv.12-15)
- Seven Things the LORD Hates (vv.16-19)
- Warning Against Adultery (vv.20-35)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.6  — "Go to the ant, O sluggard; consider her ways, and be
        wise." (ESV) — Even a tiny insect shames the lazy man.
        The ant needs no overseer — she is self-motivated. If a
        creature without reason works diligently, how much more
        should a person made in God's image?

v.10-11 — "A little sleep, a little slumber, a little folding of
           the hands to rest, and poverty will come upon you like a
           robber, and want like an armed man." (ESV) — The
           sluggard's self-deception: "just a LITTLE more rest."
           But poverty doesn't announce itself — it arrives like a
           robber (suddenly overwhelming) and an armed man
           (irresistible).

v.16-19 — "There are six things that the LORD hates, seven that
           are an abomination to him: haughty eyes, a lying tongue,
           and hands that shed innocent blood, a heart that devises
           wicked plans, feet that make haste to run to evil, a
           false witness who breathes out lies, and one who sows
           discord among brothers." (ESV) — A complete anatomy of
           evil: eyes, tongue, hands, heart, feet, mouth, and social
           behavior. Note: the list moves from the individual
           (pride) to the communal (discord). God especially hates
           what destroys community.

v.27 — "Can a man carry fire next to his chest and his clothes not
        be burned?" (ESV) — The rhetorical question that destroys
        all excuses. You cannot hold adultery close and avoid being
        burned. The question isn't IF consequences will come but
        WHEN.

v.32 — "He who commits adultery lacks sense; he who does it
        destroys himself." (ESV) — Not a crime of passion but a
        crime of stupidity. The adulterer doesn't lack morals
        primarily — he lacks sense. He is destroying himself.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.6   — Proverbs 30:25 — "The ants are a people not strong, yet
         they provide their food in the summer." (ESV) Agur's echo.
v.10  — Proverbs 24:33-34 — Identical repetition of the sluggard's
         doom. This refrain was memorable enough to repeat.
v.16  — Proverbs 8:13 — "The fear of the LORD is hatred of evil.
         Pride and arrogance...I hate." (ESV) Pride tops both lists.
v.17  — Genesis 4:8 — "Hands that shed innocent blood." Cain was
         the first; this sin runs through human history.
v.19  — Matthew 5:9 — "Blessed are the peacemakers." (ESV) The
         opposite of sowing discord.
v.23  — Psalm 119:105 — "Your word is a lamp to my feet." (ESV)
         Same metaphor: God's commands illuminate.
v.27  — 2 Samuel 11 — David and Bathsheba. The ultimate
         illustration of taking fire and being burned.
v.32  — Proverbs 7:7 — "A young man lacking sense." (ESV) Same
         diagnosis applied to the seduced.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/proverbs-6/
  Guzik notes the numerical pattern "six...yea, seven" (v.16) is a
  Hebrew literary device that creates suspense — the seventh item
  is climactic. The climax is "sowing discord among brethren" —
  suggesting God especially hates the destruction of community and
  unity. On the ant (v.6), Guzik emphasizes that the ant works
  without external motivation — she has internalized the need. The
  sluggard needs someone to force him; the wise person is
  self-driven.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "Go to the ant, thou sluggard. God sends you to school — and
  your teacher is an insect. He does not send you to the lion for
  courage or the eagle for vision — He sends you to the ant for
  industry. The smallest creature shames the laziest man. And
  notice — the ant has no overseer. She does not work because
  someone watches; she works because the work needs doing. Oh
  that Christians had the ant's self-motivation in the service
  of their God!"
TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1  — KJV: "surety for thy friend" / ESV: "put up security for
       your neighbor" — Hebrew "'arab" = to pledge, guarantee
       another's debt. This is co-signing a loan. The warning is
       not against generosity but against putting yourself at
       financial risk for someone else's obligations without
       adequate security.

v.12 — KJV: "A naughty person" / ESV: "A worthless person" —
       Hebrew "adam beliyya'al" = man of worthlessness/wickedness.
       KJV's "naughty" meant "worthless/wicked" in 1611 English
       (not merely mischievous as today). ESV's "worthless" is the
       accurate modern equivalent. "Belial" later became a name
       for Satan (2 Corinthians 6:15).

v.16 — KJV: "abomination" / ESV: "abomination" — Hebrew "to'evah"
       = something detestable, morally abhorrent to God. The same
       word used for idolatry and the worst moral offenses. These
       seven things are not merely displeasing — they are
       loathsome to God's nature.

v.26 — KJV: "a man is brought to a piece of bread" / ESV: "the
       price of a prostitute is only a loaf of bread" — Difficult
       Hebrew. KJV reads: adultery reduces a man to poverty.
       ESV reads: a prostitute costs little (bread), but an
       adulteress costs everything (your life). The ESV contrast
       is sharper: the cheap sin has an expensive consequence.

v.1 — ASV: "neighbor, If" for KJV's "friend, if"
       NET: "you have become a guarantor for" for KJV's "thou hast stricken thy hand with"
       WEB: "you have struck your hands in pledge for" for KJV's "thou hast stricken thy hand with"
v.12 — ASV: "man of iniquity, Is he that" for KJV's "wicked man,"
       NET: "person walks around saying perverse things;" for KJV's "man, walketh with a froward mouth."
       WEB: "man of iniquity, is he who walks" for KJV's "wicked man, walketh"
v.16 — ASV: uses "Jehovah" for KJV's "LORD" (Hebrew YHWH).
       NET: "Lord hates, even" for KJV's "LORD hate: yea,"
       WEB: uses "Yahweh" for KJV's "LORD" (Hebrew YHWH).
v.26 — ASV: "on account" for KJV's "by means"
       NET: "wife of another man preys on your" for KJV's "adulteress will hunt for the"
       WEB: "prostitute reduces you" for KJV's "whorish woman a man is brought"

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Sluggard        — Hebrew "'atsel" = the lazy, slothful person.
              Appears 14 times in Proverbs. Not someone who can't
              work but someone who won't. His defining trait is
              self-deception — he always has a reason not to act
              ("a little more sleep"). He is contrasted with the
              ant who needs no external motivation.

Surety/Pledge  — A financial guarantee for another person's debt.
              To "strike hands" (v.1) was the ancient equivalent of
              co-signing a loan. If the borrower defaults, you pay.
              Proverbs consistently warns against this (11:15,
              17:18, 22:26) — not from selfishness but from the
              danger of binding yourself to another's financial
              irresponsibility.

Discord        — Hebrew "midyan/medan" = strife, contention,
              quarreling. The seventh and climactic abomination.
              God hates what tears apart human community — gossip,
              slander, manipulation, faction. The peacemaker
              reflects God; the discord-sower reflects Satan.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Learn from the ant (vv.6-8). Do you need an overseer to be
   productive? Or are you self-motivated by the work that needs
   doing? The ant prepares in summer FOR winter — she thinks ahead.
   What season are you in, and are you preparing for what's coming?

2. Beware the "little more" trap (v.10). "A little sleep, a little
   slumber" — the sluggard never decides to be lazy. He just delays
   a little longer. A little more scrolling, a little more rest, a
   little more procrastination. Small delays compound into massive
   loss. Start now.

3. Check yourself against the seven (vv.16-19). Pride? Lying?
   Violence? Wicked schemes? Running toward sin? False witness?
   Discord? These are what God HATES. Not merely dislikes —
   abhors. If any of these characterize you, urgent repentance is
   needed. Pay special attention to the last: do you sow unity
   or division?

4. You cannot carry fire without being burned (v.27). Stop
   believing you're the exception. You're not fireproof. Whatever
   sin you think you can hold close without consequences — you
   can't. Get rid of it before it burns your life down.

5. Adultery destroys beyond repair (vv.32-35). "His reproach shall
   not be wiped away." Some consequences are permanent. A betrayed
   spouse's jealousy will not be bought off. Guard your marriage
   not merely with willpower but with wisdom — avoid the path that
   leads to the fire.
