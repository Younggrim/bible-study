Proverbs - Chapter 5
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Warning against the adulterous woman — her lips drip honey but her end is bitter as wormwood; instead, rejoice in the wife of your youth and let her love satisfy you always.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Primarily Solomon (1:1, 10:1, 25:1), with sections by Agur
(ch.30) and King Lemuel (ch.31). Compiled over several centuries.
Proverbs is wisdom literature — practical instruction for godly
living. Hebrew title: 'Mishlei' — 'Proverbs/Comparisons.' Key
themes: the fear of the LORD as the beginning of wisdom (1:7, 9:10),
the two paths (wisdom vs. folly), the personification of Wisdom
(chs.1-9), and practical guidance for every area of life — speech,
money, work, relationships, leadership, and character.

Historical Context: Chapter 5 is the first extended warning against
adultery — a theme that dominates chapters 5-7. The tragic irony
is that Solomon himself would fall to this very sin (1 Kings 11:1-4),
making these words both prophetic warning and personal testimony.
The chapter divides clearly: the danger of the adulteress (vv.1-14),
the beauty of marital love (vv.15-20), and divine accountability
(vv.21-23). The "strange woman" (Hebrew "zarah") represents sexual
temptation outside of marriage covenant. Remarkably, vv.15-19
celebrate sexual love within marriage with vivid, joyful imagery —
Scripture is not prudish about marital intimacy. It exalts it
precisely to expose the counterfeit of adultery.

Structure:
- Warning: The Adulteress's Deception (vv.1-6)
- Command: Stay Far from Her (vv.7-14)
- Alternative: Rejoice in Your Wife (vv.15-20)
- Reason: God Sees Everything (vv.21-23)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.3-4 — "For the lips of a forbidden woman drip honey, and her
         speech is smoother than oil, but in the end she is bitter
         as wormwood, sharp as a two-edged sword." (ESV) — The
         contrast is devastating: honeycomb at the beginning,
         wormwood at the end. Oil on the surface, a sword beneath.
         Sin always advertises the beginning and hides the end.

v.8  — "Keep your way far from her, and do not go near the door of
        her house." (ESV) — The strategy is distance, not
        resistance up close. Don't test your willpower — avoid the
        situation entirely. Stay far away. The wisest course is
        prevention, not recovery.

v.15 — "Drink water from your own cistern, flowing water from your
        own well." (ESV) — Beautiful metaphor for marital
        exclusivity. Your wife is your well — drink deeply from
        her. Sexual satisfaction within marriage is God's designed
        alternative to temptation. The answer to illicit desire is
        not repression but sanctified enjoyment.

v.19 — "a lovely deer, a graceful doe. Let her breasts fill you at
        all times with delight; be intoxicated always in her love."
        (ESV) — Scripture commands ongoing, passionate, exclusive
        delight in one's wife. "Intoxicated" (shagah) =
        captivated, enraptured. God commands marital passion — not
        merely permits it.

v.22 — "The iniquities of the wicked ensnare him, and he is held
        fast in the cords of his sin." (ESV) — Sin is its own
        trap. You don't need God to punish you — your own sin
        becomes the rope that binds you. Every act of sin tightens
        the cord.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.3   — Proverbs 7:21 — "With much seductive speech she persuades
         him." (ESV) Same seductive technique.
v.4   — Hebrews 4:12 — "Sharper than any two-edged sword." (ESV)
         The same imagery applied to God's word — which can cut
         through sin's deception.
v.8   — Genesis 39:12 — Joseph fled from Potiphar's wife. The
         literal application of "keep your way far."
v.8   — 2 Timothy 2:22 — "Flee youthful passions." (ESV) NT
         command echoing Solomon's strategy.
v.15  — Song of Solomon 4:12 — "A garden locked is my sister, my
         bride." (ESV) Same metaphor of exclusive marital intimacy.
v.18  — Malachi 2:14-15 — "The wife of your youth...your
         companion, your wife by covenant." (ESV)
v.21  — Hebrews 4:13 — "No creature is hidden from his sight, but
         all are naked and exposed to the eyes of him to whom we
         must give account." (ESV)
v.22  — Romans 6:16 — "You are slaves of the one whom you obey."
         (ESV) Sin enslaves progressively.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/proverbs-5/
  Guzik notes the critical contrast between beginning and end:
  honey at the start, wormwood at the finish. He emphasizes that
  v.8 ("come not nigh") is about strategic avoidance — "many men
  have fallen because they put themselves in a place of temptation,
  presuming they were strong enough to resist." On vv.15-19, Guzik
  celebrates the positive vision: God's answer to temptation is not
  mere prohibition but a better alternative — the deep satisfaction
  of covenant love.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "Rejoice with the wife of thy youth. God's remedy for wandering
  desire is not cold prohibition but warm enjoyment. He does not
  merely say 'thou shalt not commit adultery' — He says 'be
  ravished with thy wife's love.' The positive command enables the
  negative. The man who drinks deeply at home will not thirst
  abroad. Cultivate your marriage with the same passion you would
  need to resist temptation, and temptation loses its power."
TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.3  — KJV: "strange woman" / ESV: "forbidden woman" — Hebrew
       "zarah" = foreign, strange, outside. KJV preserves the
       literal term; ESV interprets the moral category. She is
       "forbidden" because she is outside the covenant of marriage
       — whether literally foreign or simply another man's wife.

v.6  — KJV: "Lest thou shouldest ponder the path of life, her
       ways are moveable" / ESV: "she does not ponder the path of
       life; her ways wander" — A significant difference. KJV
       suggests she deliberately prevents YOU from pondering life;
       ESV says SHE doesn't ponder it. The Hebrew is ambiguous
       about the subject. Both readings are theologically true.

v.19 — KJV: "ravished" / ESV: "intoxicated" — Hebrew "shagah" =
       to swerve, to go astray, to be intoxicated. The same word
       is used in v.20 ("why wilt thou be ravished with a strange
       woman?"). Same intoxication — directed rightly (wife) or
       wrongly (adulteress). ESV's "intoxicated" captures the
       consistent metaphor.

v.22 — KJV: "holden with the cords of his sins" / ESV: "held fast
       in the cords of his sin" — Nearly identical. The image: sin
       is a rope. Each act adds a strand. Eventually the rope is
       too strong to break. Progressive bondage through repeated
       sin.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Wormwood       — A bitter plant (Artemisia) used as a metaphor for
              extreme bitterness and poison. The end result of sin
              — what seemed sweet becomes intolerably bitter. Also
              associated with divine judgment (Jeremiah 9:15;
              Revelation 8:11).

Cistern/Well   — Metaphors for one's wife and the exclusive sexual
              relationship of marriage. A cistern stores rainwater;
              a well has a living spring. Your wife is your source
              of refreshment — drink only from her. In the ancient
              Near East, water sources were private property — to
              drink from another's well was theft.

Sheol          — The Hebrew term for the realm of the dead, the
              grave, the underworld. Here it indicates that the
              path of adultery leads ultimately to death — not
              merely physical death but spiritual ruin and
              destruction of everything good.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Sin advertises the beginning, hides the end (vv.3-4). Every
   temptation shows you the honey and conceals the wormwood. Ask
   yourself with every temptation: what does this look like in 5
   years? 10 years? At the end? Think past the initial pleasure to
   the final consequence.

2. Distance is the strategy (v.8). Don't go near. Don't test
   yourself. Don't believe you're strong enough to be in the
   presence of temptation and resist. Joseph didn't stay to argue —
   he ran. What do you need to put distance between yourself and?
   Delete it, block it, avoid it, flee from it.

3. Invest in your marriage (vv.15-19). The best defense against
   temptation is a thriving marriage. Are you "ravished" with your
   spouse's love? If not, the solution is to invest there — not
   to look elsewhere. Cultivate romance, intimacy, and delight in
   your covenant relationship. Water your own garden.

4. God sees what no one else sees (v.21). "The ways of man are
   before the eyes of the LORD." Secret sin is not secret. There
   is no privacy from God. If the awareness of His gaze doesn't
   deter you, nothing will. Live as though watched — because you
   are.

5. Sin binds progressively (v.22). "Holden with the cords of his
   sins." Each repetition adds a strand to the rope. What starts
   as a thread becomes a cable. Break free while the cords are thin.
   The longer you wait, the harder it becomes. Today is the easiest
   day to repent — tomorrow the ropes will be thicker.
