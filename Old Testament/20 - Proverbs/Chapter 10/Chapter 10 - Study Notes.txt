Proverbs - Chapter 10
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
The beginning of Solomon's individual proverbs — primarily antithetical couplets contrasting the righteous and the wicked, with emphasis on the power of speech, diligence versus laziness, and the blessings of the upright.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Primarily Solomon (1:1, 10:1, 25:1), with sections by Agur
(ch.30) and King Lemuel (ch.31). Compiled over several centuries.
Proverbs is wisdom literature — practical instruction for godly
living. Hebrew title: 'Mishlei' — 'Proverbs/Comparisons.' Key
themes: the fear of the LORD as the beginning of wisdom (1:7, 9:10),
the two paths (wisdom vs. folly), the personification of Wisdom
(chs.1-9), and practical guidance for every area of life — speech,
money, work, relationships, leadership, and character.

Historical Context: Chapter 10 marks a major literary transition.
The extended discourses of chapters 1-9 give way to individual
two-line proverbs — the form that will dominate through chapter 22.
The heading "The proverbs of Solomon" (v.1) begins the first
Solomonic collection (10:1-22:16), containing 375 proverbs. These
are predominantly antithetical parallelisms — "the righteous...
but the wicked" — contrasting the two paths in compressed form.
Chapter 10 is dominated by two themes: (1) the power of speech
(tongue/lips/mouth appear in 11 of 32 verses) and (2) the contrast
between righteous and wicked outcomes. Each proverb stands alone
as a self-contained unit of wisdom.

Dominant Themes:
- The Power of the Tongue (vv.6, 8, 10-14, 18-21, 31-32)
- Righteous vs. Wicked Outcomes (vv.2-3, 6-7, 16, 24-25, 27-30)
- Diligence vs. Laziness (vv.4-5, 26)
- The Blessing of the LORD (v.22)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.4  — "A slack hand causes poverty, but the hand of the diligent
        makes rich." (ESV) — The basic economic principle of
        Proverbs: wealth follows diligence, poverty follows
        laziness. Not an absolute guarantee (other factors exist)
        but a general principle of God's created order.

v.12 — "Hatred stirs up strife, but love covers all offenses."
        (ESV) — One of the most quoted proverbs. Hatred exposes
        and exploits faults; love covers them. Not enabling sin but
        choosing not to broadcast others' failures. Quoted in
        1 Peter 4:8.

v.19 — "When words are many, transgression is not lacking, but
        whoever restrains his lips is prudent." (ESV) — The more
        you talk, the more likely you are to sin. Wisdom is often
        expressed in silence. Self-restraint in speech is a mark of
        true understanding.

v.22 — "The blessing of the LORD makes rich, and he adds no sorrow
        with it." (ESV) — Wealth that comes from God's blessing
        carries no guilt, no anxiety, no regret. Wealth gained
        wrongly always comes with sorrow attached. The source
        determines the quality.

v.25 — "When the tempest passes, the wicked is no more, but the
        righteous is established forever." (ESV) — The wicked are
        temporary as a storm — they blow through and vanish. The
        righteous are permanent as a foundation — they endure.
        Cf. Matthew 7:24-27, the house on the rock.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.1   — Proverbs 15:20; 17:25 — Repeated theme: a foolish child
         grieves parents. Wisdom begins at home.
v.4   — Proverbs 6:6-11 — The ant teaches the same lesson about
         diligence.
v.7   — Psalm 112:6 — "The righteous will be remembered forever."
         (ESV)
v.12  — 1 Peter 4:8 — "Love covers a multitude of sins." (ESV)
         Direct allusion to this proverb.
v.15  — Proverbs 18:11 — "The rich man's wealth is his strong
         city." Repeated — but cf. 18:10: "The name of the LORD
         is a strong tower."
v.19  — James 1:19 — "Let every person be quick to hear, slow to
         speak." (ESV) NT application of the same principle.
v.22  — Psalm 127:1-2 — "Unless the LORD builds the house, those
         who build it labor in vain." (ESV) God's blessing is the
         true source.
v.25  — Matthew 7:24-27 — The parable of the wise and foolish
         builders. Same contrast of permanence vs. destruction.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/proverbs-10/
  Guzik notes the dramatic shift from extended speeches to
  individual proverbs beginning at chapter 10. He emphasizes that
  these are general principles, not absolute promises — they
  describe how life normally works under God's moral order. On v.12,
  he states: "Love does not deny sin exists, but it does not
  broadcast or exploit others' failures." On v.22, Guzik
  distinguishes between wealth with sorrow (gotten by greed,
  dishonesty, or overwork) and wealth without sorrow (God's
  genuine blessing).

Charles Spurgeon:
  "In the multitude of words there wanteth not sin. The man who
  talks much will sin much. It is a mathematical certainty. If you
  fire a thousand arrows, some will go astray. If you speak a
  thousand words, some will be sinful. The wise man restrains his
  lips — not because he has nothing to say, but because he knows
  the danger of saying too much. Silence is often the highest
  wisdom, and brevity is always safer than verbosity."
TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.3  — KJV: "casteth away the substance of the wicked" / ESV:
       "thwarts the craving of the wicked" — Hebrew "hadvas" =
       desire/craving, and "yahdoph" = thrust away. KJV focuses
       on God removing what the wicked have; ESV on God blocking
       what they crave. Both are valid readings of the Hebrew.

v.8  — KJV: "prating fool" / ESV: "babbling fool" — Hebrew
       "evil sephathayim" = fool of lips. One who talks constantly
       and senselessly. KJV's "prating" (meaningless talk) is
       archaic; ESV's "babbling" is the modern equivalent. The
       contrast: the wise receive instruction silently; the fool
       talks himself into ruin.

v.12 — KJV: "love covereth all sins" / ESV: "love covers all
       offenses" — Hebrew "pesha'im" = transgressions, offenses.
       ESV's "offenses" avoids confusion with theological "sins"
       (atonement). The proverb is about interpersonal relations:
       love chooses not to expose others' faults. Not
       justification but grace in community.

v.26 — KJV: "as vinegar to the teeth" / ESV: "like vinegar to the
       teeth" — The imagery: just as vinegar sets teeth on edge and
       smoke stings the eyes, so the sluggard irritates those
       depending on him. He is an annoyance, a source of
       frustration to everyone who entrusts him with responsibility.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Antithetical Parallelism — The dominant literary form in Proverbs
              10-15. Two lines set in contrast, usually connected
              by "but." Line 1 states a truth; Line 2 presents the
              opposite. This forces the reader to see both sides:
              the path of wisdom AND the path of folly, the
              righteous outcome AND the wicked outcome.

Slack Hand     — Hebrew "remiyyah" = slackness, deceit, laxity.
              The hand that does nothing, or does its work
              carelessly. The opposite of "diligent" (charuts =
              sharp, decisive, cutting). Poverty is not a
              mystery — it often follows directly from laziness.

Prating/Babbling — Excessive, empty speech. A person who talks
              without substance or self-control. In Proverbs,
              verbosity is consistently associated with folly
              and silence with wisdom. The one who cannot stop
              talking will eventually say something destructive.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Control your tongue (v.19). "In the multitude of words there
   wanteth not sin." Are you talking too much? Texting too quickly?
   Posting too freely? The wise person restrains speech. Practice
   saying less — you'll sin less.

2. Love covers; hatred exposes (v.12). When someone wrongs you, do
   you broadcast it or cover it? Love doesn't pretend sin doesn't
   exist — it chooses not to exploit it for personal advantage.
   Stop gossiping about others' failures. Cover with grace.

3. Diligence is the path to provision (v.4). "The hand of the
   diligent maketh rich." God generally blesses hard work. Are you
   being diligent in your calling? Laziness is not merely
   unproductive — it is ungodly. Work with your whole strength as
   unto the Lord.

4. God's blessing comes without sorrow (v.22). If your wealth or
   success is accompanied by constant anxiety, guilt, or relational
   damage — question whether you got it God's way. His genuine
   blessing produces peace, not turmoil. Pursue gain that comes
   with His approval.

5. You are either a foundation or a whirlwind (v.25). Which are
   you — something lasting or something passing? Are you building
   permanence through righteousness, or will your life blow through
   and leave nothing? Build on what endures.
