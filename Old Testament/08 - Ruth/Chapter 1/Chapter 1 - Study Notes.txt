Ruth - Chapter 1
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Naomi loses her husband and two sons in Moab; Ruth refuses to leave her, declaring "thy people shall be my people, and thy God my God." They return to Bethlehem destitute at the beginning of barley harvest.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Unknown, traditionally attributed to Samuel. Ruth is set 'in the
days when the judges ruled' (1:1) but provides a beautiful contrast to
the moral chaos of Judges. While Judges ends with 'every man did that
which was right in his own eyes,' Ruth shows ordinary people living
faithfully in dark times. The book demonstrates God's providence, the
kinsman-redeemer (go'el) concept, and the inclusion of Gentiles in God's
plan. Ruth, a Moabite woman, becomes the great-grandmother of David and
an ancestor of Christ (Matthew 1:5).

Historical Context: Chapter 1 opens with famine in Bethlehem — whose
name ironically means "house of bread." Elimelech ("my God is king")
takes his family to Moab, a nation born of Lot's incest (Genesis 19:37)
and historically hostile to Israel (Numbers 22-25; Deuteronomy 23:3-6).
The Moabites were excluded from the assembly of the LORD "even to their
tenth generation" (Deuteronomy 23:3). Yet it is from Moab that God
brings a woman into the messianic line. The chapter records cascading
loss: Elimelech dies, then both sons Mahlon ("sickly") and Chilion
("wasting") die after ten years, leaving Naomi bereft of husband and
children in a foreign land. The names themselves are prophetic — sickness
and wasting. When Naomi hears that "the LORD had visited his people in
giving them bread" (v.6), she determines to return. The journey back
becomes the setting for one of Scripture's most profound declarations of
loyalty and faith: Ruth's refusal to leave Naomi (vv.16-17). This is
not merely human devotion — it is a Gentile woman choosing the God of
Israel over the gods of Moab. Ruth's declaration is a conversion
statement: "thy people shall be my people, and thy God my God." The
chapter ends with arrival in Bethlehem "in the beginning of barley
harvest" — a note of hope amid grief, and a detail that sets up the
events of chapter 2. Naomi's request to be called "Mara" (bitter)
reflects her theology: she attributes her suffering directly to God's
hand. She is honest in her pain but wrong in her conclusion — God has
not abandoned her. He has given her Ruth, and through Ruth, redemption
is coming.

Famine and Flight to Moab (vv.1-5): "In the days when the judges
ruled" places this story in the period of Israel's spiritual decline.
Famine in the promised land signals covenant judgment (Deuteronomy
28:23-24; Leviticus 26:19-20). Elimelech's decision to sojourn in Moab
is understandable but spiritually questionable — leaving the land of
promise for a pagan nation. The family settles, the sons marry Moabite
women (contrary to the spirit of Deuteronomy 7:3-4, though Moabites
are not explicitly listed among the prohibited nations in that passage).
Ten years pass. Then death claims all three men. Naomi is left with
nothing — no husband, no sons, no grandchildren, no inheritance, no
security. In the ancient world, a widow without sons was among the most
vulnerable people in society.

Naomi's Urging and Orpah's Departure (vv.6-14): Naomi urges both
daughters-in-law to return to their mothers' houses — not their fathers'
houses, which is unusual. Perhaps their fathers were dead, or perhaps
Naomi directs them to the comfort of their mothers. Her blessing is
generous: "The LORD deal kindly with you" (v.8). She invokes the LORD's
hesed (covenant loyalty/lovingkindness) upon these Moabite women. Her
argument is practical: she has no more sons to offer them (referencing
levirate marriage, Deuteronomy 25:5-10). Orpah's departure is not
condemned — she does the sensible thing. She kisses Naomi and returns
to "her people and her gods" (v.15). The text does not judge her, but
the contrast with Ruth is stark. Orpah represents the reasonable choice;
Ruth represents the faithful one.

Ruth's Declaration (vv.15-18): Ruth "clave unto her" (v.14) — the
Hebrew "davaq" is the same word used of a man cleaving to his wife in
Genesis 2:24. This is covenant language. Ruth's speech (vv.16-17) is
structured as a series of escalating commitments: where you go, where
you lodge, your people, your God, where you die, where you are buried.
She moves from geography to community to theology to death itself. The
oath formula — "the LORD do so to me, and more also" — invokes Israel's
God as witness. This is a complete renunciation of Moab and a total
embrace of Israel's God. Ruth asks nothing in return. She makes no
conditions. She binds herself to a destitute widow with no prospects.
This is grace — unmerited, unconditional commitment.

Return to Bethlehem (vv.19-22): The arrival stirs the whole city. Naomi
has been gone perhaps twelve years or more. The women's question — "Is
this Naomi?" — suggests she is physically changed by grief. Her response
is theologically loaded: "Call me not Naomi [pleasant], call me Mara
[bitter]: for the Almighty hath dealt very bitterly with me" (v.20).
She uses "Shaddai" (the Almighty) — the name emphasizing God's sovereign
power. She sees God as the agent of her suffering: "I went out full, and
the LORD hath brought me home again empty" (v.21). Yet she is not empty
— Ruth stands beside her. Naomi cannot yet see that God's provision is
already present. The chapter closes with a time marker: "the beginning
of barley harvest" — late April/early May. Harvest means food, work,
and the gleaning laws that will bring Ruth into Boaz's field.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Bethlehem: In Judah, about 5 miles south of Jerusalem — "house of bread" now experiencing famine
- Moab: The land east of the Dead Sea; reached by traveling south around the Dead Sea or crossing near its northern end
- The return journey: Moab → westward across the Jordan rift → up into the Judean hills → Bethlehem

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "Now it came to pass in the days when the judges ruled, that
        there was a famine in the land." — The setting establishes both
        the historical period and the spiritual condition. Famine in
        Bethlehem ("house of bread") is ironic and signals covenant
        unfaithfulness. Yet God is about to work redemption through
        ordinary people in this dark era.

v.6  — "Then she arose with her daughters in law, that she might return
        from the country of Moab: for she had heard in the country of
        Moab how that the LORD had visited his people in giving them
        bread." — God "visits" His people — the same language used of
        the Exodus (Exodus 4:31). The famine ends because God acts.
        Naomi's return is prompted by news of God's faithfulness.

v.14 — "And they lifted up their voice, and wept again: and Orpah
        kissed her mother in law; but Ruth clave unto her." — The
        contrast is simple but profound. Orpah kisses (farewell); Ruth
        clings (commitment). Both loved Naomi. Only one chose the cost
        of covenant loyalty.

v.16 — "But Ruth said, 'Do not urge me to leave you or to return from
        following you. For where you go I will go, and where you lodge I
        will lodge. Your people shall be my people, and your God my
        God.'" (ESV) — The greatest declaration of loyalty in Scripture.
        Ruth abandons homeland, family, gods, security, and future
        prospects to follow a destitute widow and her God. This is faith
        without guarantee — she has no promise of provision, husband, or
        acceptance in Israel.

v.20 — "She said to them, 'Do not call me Naomi; call me Mara, for the
        Almighty has dealt very bitterly with me.'" (ESV) — Naomi's
        theology is honest but incomplete. She rightly sees God's
        sovereignty over her suffering. She wrongly concludes that God
        has only bitterness for her. The rest of the book will prove
        her wrong — God is working redemption she cannot yet see.

v.22 — "So Naomi returned, and Ruth the Moabitess, her daughter in law,
        with her, which returned out of the country of Moab: and they
        came to Bethlehem in the beginning of barley harvest." — The
        identifier "the Moabitess" will follow Ruth throughout the book
        — she is always the outsider. Yet she arrives at harvest time:
        God's timing brings her to Bethlehem precisely when provision
        is available.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.1   — Judges 2:16-19 — The cycle of the judges period: sin, judgment,
         crying out, deliverance.
v.1   — Deuteronomy 28:23-24 — Famine as covenant curse for
         disobedience.
v.1   — Genesis 12:10 — Abraham also left the promised land during
         famine — a pattern of faith tested by scarcity.
v.2   — Genesis 19:37 — Moab's origin: son of Lot's eldest daughter.
v.4   — Deuteronomy 23:3 — "No Ammonite or Moabite may enter the
         assembly of the LORD." (ESV)
v.8   — Ruth 3:10 — Boaz later commends Ruth's "kindness" (hesed) —
         the same word Naomi uses here.
v.11  — Deuteronomy 25:5-10 — The levirate marriage law that underlies
         Naomi's argument.
v.14  — Genesis 2:24 — "Cleave" (davaq) — Ruth clings to Naomi with
         covenant intensity.
v.16  — 2 Kings 2:2,4,6 — Elisha's similar refusal to leave Elijah:
         "I will not leave you." (ESV)
v.16  — Joshua 24:15 — "Choose this day whom you will serve" (ESV) —
         Ruth has chosen.
v.20  — Exodus 15:23 — Marah — the bitter waters. The same root word.
v.20  — Job 27:2 — "The Almighty, who has made my soul bitter" (ESV) —
         Job's similar complaint against Shaddai.
v.22  — Ruth 2:23 — The barley harvest continues through chapter 2,
         providing the setting for Ruth's meeting with Boaz.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/ruth-1/
  Guzik emphasizes that Ruth's declaration is a conversion statement —
  she is choosing Israel's God, not merely Israel's people. He notes
  that Naomi's bitterness, while understandable, fails to account for
  God's provision already standing beside her in Ruth.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "Ruth's choice was the choice of faith. She had nothing to gain by
  following Naomi — no husband waiting, no wealth promised, no welcome
  assured. She chose a destitute widow, an unknown land, and an unseen
  God. This is precisely what faith does: it abandons the seen for the
  unseen, the certain for the promised, the comfortable for the costly.
  And God honored her faith beyond all she could have imagined — she
  who chose poverty with God's people became the ancestress of David
  and of David's greater Son."
TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1  — KJV: "a certain man of Bethlehemjudah" / ESV: "a man of
       Bethlehem in Judah" — Hebrew "ish mi-Beit Lechem Yehudah." KJV
       compounds the name; ESV separates it. "Bethlehem-judah"
       distinguishes this Bethlehem from Bethlehem in Zebulun (Joshua
       19:15). The specification matters: this is the Bethlehem of
       David, the Bethlehem of Christ.

v.8  — KJV: "the LORD deal kindly with you" / ESV: "May the LORD deal
       kindly with you" — Hebrew "ya'as Yahweh immakhem chesed." The
       key word is "chesed" — covenant loyalty, lovingkindness, faithful
       love. This is not mere niceness but God's steadfast, covenantal
       commitment. Naomi invokes God's deepest attribute upon Moabite
       women — remarkable theological generosity.

v.13 — KJV: "it grieveth me much for your sakes that the hand of the
       LORD is gone out against me" / ESV: "it is exceedingly bitter to
       me for your sake that the hand of the LORD has gone out against
       me" — Hebrew "mar-li m'od mikkem ki-yats'ah vi yad-Yahweh." KJV
       "grieveth" captures the emotional weight; ESV "exceedingly
       bitter" is more literal (from "mar" = bitter, the root of
       "Mara"). Naomi's grief is not self-pity but genuine sorrow that
       her misfortune affects these young women.

v.14 — KJV: "Ruth clave unto her" / ESV: "Ruth clung to her" — Hebrew
       "davqah bah." "Davaq" = to cling, cleave, hold fast. This is
       the word of Genesis 2:24 (a man shall "cleave" to his wife) and
       Deuteronomy 10:20 ("cleave" unto the LORD). It denotes permanent,
       unbreakable attachment. Ruth's clinging is both physical (she
       will not let go) and covenantal (she will not turn back).

v.16 — KJV: "Intreat me not to leave thee" / ESV: "Do not urge me to
       leave you" — Hebrew "al-tifg'i-vi l'ozvekh." "Paga" = to
       encounter, meet, press upon, urge. KJV's "intreat" (entreat)
       captures the pleading quality; ESV's "urge" is more direct. Ruth
       is saying: stop pressing me, stop trying to convince me. Her
       mind is made.

v.20 — KJV: "the Almighty hath dealt very bitterly with me" / ESV:
       "the Almighty has dealt very bitterly with me" — Hebrew
       "hemar Shaddai li m'od." "Shaddai" = the Almighty, the All-
       Sufficient One. This divine name emphasizes God's overwhelming
       power. Naomi uses it deliberately: the One who has all power has
       used that power to bring bitterness. The irony: Shaddai (the
       All-Sufficient) has made her insufficient. Yet Shaddai's
       sufficiency will be revealed through Boaz.

v.21 — KJV: "the LORD hath testified against me" / ESV: "the LORD has
       testified against me" — Hebrew "Yahweh anah vi." "Anah" = to
       answer, testify, afflict. The word carries legal overtones: God
       has testified against her as a witness in court. Naomi sees her
       suffering as God's verdict. ESV also adds "brought calamity upon
       me" for "hera li Shaddai" where KJV has "the Almighty hath
       afflicted me."

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Bethlehem      — Hebrew "Beit Lechem" = house of bread. A town in Judah,
               about 5 miles south of Jerusalem. The irony of famine in
               the "house of bread" is deliberate. This is the city of
               David's birth and later of Christ's birth (Micah 5:2).
               Also called Ephrath/Ephrathah (Genesis 35:19; Micah 5:2).

Elimelech      — Hebrew "Eli-melekh" = my God is king. The name
               professes faith in God's sovereignty, yet Elimelech
               leaves God's land during famine. His name becomes ironic:
               if God is truly his king, why flee to Moab?

Naomi          — Hebrew "No'omi" = pleasant, lovely, delightful. She
               later rejects this name for "Mara" (bitter), reflecting
               her perception that God has turned her pleasantness to
               pain.

Mara           — Hebrew "Marah" = bitter. The same word as the bitter
               waters of Exodus 15:23. Naomi adopts this as her identity
               — she defines herself by her suffering rather than by
               God's character.

Mahlon         — Hebrew "Machlon" = sickly, weak. The name may be
               prophetic of his early death. He is later identified as
               Ruth's husband (4:10).

Chilion        — Hebrew "Kilyon" = wasting, pining, destruction. Like
               his brother's name, it foreshadows death. Both sons die
               childless in Moab.

Moab           — The land east of the Dead Sea, descended from Lot's
               son by his eldest daughter (Genesis 19:37). Israel and
               Moab had a complex, often hostile relationship. Moabites
               were excluded from the assembly (Deuteronomy 23:3), yet
               God brings Ruth the Moabitess into the messianic line.

Ephrathites    — Residents of Ephrath/Ephrathah, an older name for
               Bethlehem or its surrounding district (Genesis 35:19;
               48:7). The term identifies the family as established
               Bethlehemites, not recent arrivals.

Hesed          — Hebrew "chesed" = covenant loyalty, lovingkindness,
               steadfast love, faithful mercy. The defining attribute
               of God's character toward His people. Used in v.8 by
               Naomi and throughout Ruth as the book's theological
               keyword. Hesed is love that acts, loyalty that costs,
               kindness that commits.

Go'el          — Hebrew "go'el" = kinsman-redeemer. Not used explicitly
               in chapter 1 but introduced conceptually. The go'el was
               a near relative responsible for redeeming family members
               from debt, slavery, or loss of inheritance (Leviticus
               25:25-55). The concept becomes central in chapters 2-4.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.6 — ASV: "Jehovah had visited his people in giving them bread" / WEB: "Yahweh had visited his people in giving them bread" / NET: "the Lord had shown concern for his people, reversing the famine by providing abundant crops" / KJV: "the LORD had visited his people in giving them bread" — NET expands and interprets where KJV/ASV/WEB preserve the beautiful simplicity: God "visited" (paqad — to attend to, care for, intervene) His people. NET's "shown concern" and "abundant crops" add detail not in the Hebrew. The verb "paqad" implies personal divine action.
- v.8 — ASV: "Jehovah deal kindly with you, as ye have dealt with the dead, and with me" / WEB: "May Yahweh deal kindly with you" / NET: "May the Lord show you the same kind of devotion" / KJV: "the LORD deal kindly with you" — The key word is "chesed" (loyal love/kindness/devotion). KJV/ASV/WEB use "deal kindly" — simple and warm. NET uses "devotion" which captures the covenant dimension but loses the tender quality. Chesed is the central theological word of Ruth.
- v.14 — ASV: "Ruth clave unto her" / WEB: "Ruth joined with her" / KJV: "Ruth clave unto her" / NET: "Ruth clung tightly to her" — Hebrew "davaq" (cling/cleave) — the same word used for marriage in Genesis 2:24 and for devotion to God in Deuteronomy 10:20. ASV/KJV's "clave" is archaic but carries the weight of covenant attachment. NET adds "tightly" (not in Hebrew). WEB's "joined with" is weaker.
- v.16 — ASV: "Entreat me not to leave thee" / WEB: "Don't urge me to leave you" / NET: "Stop urging me to abandon you!" / KJV: "Intreat me not to leave thee" — NET's "abandon" is stronger than the Hebrew "azav" (to leave/forsake) in this context. Ruth's declaration is tender, not aggressive. ASV/KJV's "entreat me not" preserves the respectful, poetic tone of one of Scripture's most beautiful speeches.
- v.17 — ASV: "Jehovah do so to me, and more also" / WEB: "May Yahweh do so to me, and more also" / NET: "May the Lord punish me severely" / KJV: "the LORD do so to me, and more also" — NET interprets the Hebrew oath formula ("do so to me and more") as "punish me severely." KJV/ASV/WEB preserve the literal oath without specifying the nature of the divine action, which adds solemnity and mystery.
- v.20 — ASV: "the Almighty hath dealt very bitterly with me" / WEB: "the Almighty has dealt very bitterly with me" / NET: "the Sovereign One has treated me very harshly" / KJV: "the Almighty hath dealt very bitterly with me" — Hebrew "Shaddai" (Almighty/All-Sufficient) is rendered "Sovereign One" by NET — an unusual choice. "Almighty" (KJV/ASV/WEB) is the traditional rendering, connecting to the patriarchal name for God (Genesis 17:1). The Mara/Naomi wordplay (bitter/pleasant) is preserved in all translations.
- v.21 — ASV: "Jehovah hath testified against me" / WEB: "Yahweh has testified against me" / NET: "the Lord has opposed me" — Hebrew "anah bi" (testified against me/answered against me) is legal language — God as witness in court. NET's "opposed" loses the judicial framework. Naomi sees herself in a divine court where God is both judge and witness.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. God works in the ordinary (vv.1-5). There are no miracles in Ruth,
   no parting seas, no fire from heaven. God works through famine,
   death, loyalty, harvest, and human decisions. Most of God's
   providence operates through ordinary circumstances — we must learn
   to see His hand in the mundane, not only in the spectacular.

2. Faith means choosing God without guarantees (vv.16-17). Ruth had no
   promise of provision, acceptance, or reward. She chose God's people
   and God's land with nothing but a destitute widow to show for it.
   True faith does not require a guaranteed outcome — it trusts the
   character of God when the circumstances offer nothing.

3. Honest grief is not unbelief (v.20). Naomi does not pretend to be
   fine. She names her pain before God and the community. The Psalms
   are full of such honesty (Psalm 88; Psalm 13). God does not require
   us to mask our suffering. But Naomi's error is in her conclusion —
   she sees only bitterness where God is already working redemption.
   Grief is permitted; despair is premature.

4. God's providence often looks like coincidence (v.22). They arrive
   "in the beginning of barley harvest" — perfect timing for Ruth to
   glean. What looks like chance is actually God's orchestration. We
   often cannot see providence until we look backward. The "accidents"
   of our lives are frequently God's appointments.

5. The outsider is welcomed into God's story (v.22). Ruth "the
   Moabitess" — always identified by her foreignness — is brought into
   the line of David and of Christ. God's grace crosses every boundary
   that human religion erects. The excluded become the included. The
   stranger becomes the ancestor of the King.
