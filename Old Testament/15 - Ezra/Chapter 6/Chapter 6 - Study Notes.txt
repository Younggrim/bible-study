Ezra - Chapter 6
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Darius finds Cyrus's original decree in the archives and orders the Temple work to continue with full imperial support and funding. The Temple is completed and dedicated with joy, and the returned exiles celebrate Passover together.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Ezra the priest and scribe (likely author/compiler). Written
approximately 450-400 BC. Ezra covers two returns from Babylonian
exile: (1) Zerubbabel's return and Temple rebuilding (538-516 BC,
chapters 1-6); (2) Ezra's return and spiritual reform (458 BC,
chapters 7-10). Key themes: God's faithfulness to His covenant
promises, the sovereignty of God over pagan kings, the centrality
of worship/Temple, and the danger of compromise with surrounding
peoples.

Historical Context: Chapter 6 is the triumphant conclusion to the
first half of Ezra. Darius searches the archives and finds Cyrus's
original decree — not in Babylon where expected, but in Ecbatana
(the Median summer capital), where Cyrus likely issued it. The
decree exceeded what the adversaries feared: not only did it
authorize the Temple, but it mandated that construction costs be
paid from the royal treasury and that sacrificial animals be
supplied daily at government expense. Darius then added his own
decree protecting the builders and threatening severe punishment
for anyone who interfered. The Temple was completed on March 12,
515 BC (Adar 3, in Darius's sixth year) — almost exactly 70 years
after its destruction in 586 BC. The dedication and subsequent
Passover celebration mirror Solomon's Temple dedication (2 Chronicles
7) on a smaller scale: 100 bulls, 200 rams, 400 lambs (compare
Solomon's 22,000 oxen and 120,000 sheep). The 12 goats for a sin
offering "according to the number of the tribes of Israel" (v.17)
is theologically significant — even though only Judah and Benjamin
returned, the offering represented all twelve tribes. The remnant
claimed the whole nation's identity. Chapter 6 closes the first
act of Ezra with celebration: Temple complete, Passover observed,
God's faithfulness vindicated. The work that began with God stirring
Cyrus (chapter 1) ends with God turning Darius's heart (v.22).

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- The geographic arc: Babylon → Jerusalem (~900-mile journey through the Fertile Crescent). Two returns: 538 BC under Zerubbabel and 458 BC under Ezra.
- Jerusalem is the destination — the Temple is rebuilt (516 BC) and worship restored.
- The journey followed major trade routes along the Euphrates River, through Syria, then south into Judah — a 4-month trek (Ezra 7:9).

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.7  — "Let the work on this house of God alone. Let the governor
        of the Jews and the elders of the Jews rebuild this house
        of God on its site." (ESV) — Darius's decree turned the
        adversaries into protectors. Those commanded to "keep away"
        (v.6) were the very officials who had been investigating
        and obstructing. God transformed opposition into mandated
        support.

v.8  — "The cost is to be paid to these men in full and without
        delay from the royal revenue, the tribute of the province
        from Beyond the River." (ESV) — The taxes collected from
        the very region whose officials opposed the Jews were now
        redirected to fund the Temple. The enemies' own tax revenue
        would finance God's house. Divine irony at its finest.

v.14 — "And the elders of the Jews built and prospered through the
        prophesying of Haggai the prophet and Zechariah the son of
        Iddo. They finished their building by decree of the God of
        Israel and by decree of Cyrus and Darius and Artaxerxes
        king of Persia." (ESV) — Three divine and three human
        authorities converge. The "decree of God" came first and
        was supreme; the decrees of three Persian kings were
        instruments of that divine command. Heavenly sovereignty
        operated through earthly governments.

v.17 — "As a sin offering for all Israel 12 male goats, according
        to the number of the tribes of Israel." (ESV) — Only two
        tribes (Judah and Benjamin, plus Levites) were present, yet
        they offered for all twelve tribes. The remnant represented
        the whole. Their worship embraced the entire covenant
        people — even those who had not returned.

v.22 — "For the LORD had made them joyful and had turned the heart
        of the king of Assyria to them, so that he aided them in
        the work of the house of God, the God of Israel" (ESV) —
        The chapter's closing statement attributes everything to
        God: He gave joy, He turned the king's heart, He
        strengthened their hands. The title "king of Assyria" for
        a Persian king reflects the understanding that Persia had
        inherited Assyria's former domain.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.3  — 2 Chronicles 36:23 — Cyrus's original decree as recorded
        at the end of Chronicles. The archival copy found in
        Ecbatana confirmed what Ezra had already recorded — God's
        purposes leave documentary evidence.

v.15 — Jeremiah 25:11-12 — The Temple completed in 515 BC, almost
        exactly 70 years after its destruction in 586 BC. God's
        prophetic timeline — "seventy years" — was fulfilled with
        mathematical precision.

v.16 — 1 Kings 8:62-66 — Solomon's Temple dedication featured
        vastly larger sacrifices but the same spirit of joy. The
        second Temple's dedication was humbler in scale but
        identical in theological significance — God once again
        had a dwelling among His people.

v.17 — Romans 11:5 — "So too at the present time there is a
        remnant, chosen by grace." (ESV) The twelve-goat offering
        by a two-tribe community anticipates the New Testament
        principle that God's faithful remnant represents and
        preserves the whole.

v.19 — Exodus 12:1-14 — The first Passover in Egypt. Now the
        post-exilic community celebrates Passover in freedom —
        delivered from a second "Egypt" (Babylon). The exodus
        pattern repeats: slavery, deliverance, worship, land.

v.22 — Proverbs 21:1 — "The king's heart is a stream of water in
        the hand of the LORD; he turns it wherever he will." (ESV)
        The theological principle stated by Solomon is demonstrated
        in Ezra 6 — God turned Persian kings' hearts to fund and
        protect His Temple.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):

  Guzik emphasizes the irony that the enemies' own investigation
  led to the discovery of a decree far more generous than they
  feared. He notes the 70-year fulfillment of Jeremiah's prophecy
  and highlights the twelve-goat offering as evidence that the
  remnant saw themselves as representing all Israel, not merely the

Charles Spurgeon:
  "God's decree, buried in an archive at Ecbatana, was found at
  the precise moment it was needed. Nothing is lost in God's
  administration — not a promise, not a purpose, not a document.
  When the time was ripe, the scroll appeared. And see how God
  multiplied the original grant: Cyrus authorized building; Darius
  added funding, supplies, and fierce protection. What the enemy
  meant for investigation, God turned to vindication. The Temple
  rose not merely by Jewish effort but by Persian wealth — the
  plunder of the nations flowing back to Zion. And at last,
  Passover! The lamb was slain, the feast was kept, and the LORD
  made them joyful. From exile to celebration — that is the arc
  of every redeemed life."

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1  — KJV: "the house of the rolls" / ESV: "the house of the
       archives" — Aramaic "beth siphrayya" (house of books/
       documents). KJV's "rolls" refers to scrolls; ESV's
       "archives" modernizes the concept. Ancient Persian record-
       keeping was remarkably thorough — administrative documents
       were stored in multiple locations throughout the empire.

v.2  — KJV: "Achmetha" / ESV: "Ecbatana" — The same city; KJV
       transliterates the Aramaic form while ESV uses the more
       familiar Greek form. This was the ancient Median capital,
       used as a summer residence by Persian kings due to its
       cooler mountain climate.

v.11 — KJV: "let him be hanged thereon" / ESV: "he shall be
       impaled on it" — Aramaic "yitmecheh" — the exact mode of
       execution is debated. It could mean impalement (a common
       Persian punishment), crucifixion-style hanging, or being
       hung on a stake. ESV's "impaled" reflects the likely
       Persian practice; KJV's "hanged" is more general.

v.14 — KJV: "commandment" / ESV: "decree" — Aramaic "taam"
       (decree/command/taste — i.e., the king's "taste" or will).
       Used for both divine and human authority in the same verse,
       creating a deliberate theological statement: human decrees
       serve divine purposes.

v.22 — KJV: "king of Assyria" / ESV: "king of Assyria" — Both
       retain this surprising title for Darius, the Persian king.
       The explanation is that the Persian Empire had absorbed
       Assyria's territory; Persian kings were understood as
       successors to Assyrian dominion. The title emphasizes
       continuity of the empire that originally exiled Israel.

v.1 — NET: "deposited there" for KJV's "laid up"
v.2 — ASV: adds "thus written for"
       NET: "inscribed as follows:“Memorandum:" for KJV's "a record thus written:"
       WEB: "Media, and in it this was written for" for KJV's "the Medes,"
v.11 — NET: "“I hereby give orders that if anyone changes this directive" for KJV's "Also I have made"
       WEB: "lifted up and fastened on it;" for KJV's "hanged thereon;"
v.14 — ASV: "prospered," for KJV's "they prospered"
       NET: "Iddo continued prophesying. They built" for KJV's "Iddo. And they builded,"
       WEB: "prospered," for KJV's "they prospered"
v.22 — ASV: uses "Jehovah" for KJV's "LORD" (Hebrew YHWH).
       NET: "toward them so that he assisted them" for KJV's "unto them, to strengthen their hands"
       WEB: uses "Yahweh" for KJV's "LORD" (Hebrew YHWH).

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Ecbatana/   — Ancient capital of Media (modern Hamadan, Iran),
Achmetha      located in the Zagros Mountains. Used as a summer
             residence by Persian kings because of its elevation
             and cool climate. Cyrus likely issued his decree here
             during a summer stay, explaining why it was not found
             in the Babylonian archives initially.

Adar        — The twelfth month of the Hebrew calendar (February-
             March). The Temple was completed on Adar 3, 515 BC.
             This is also the month in which Purim falls (Esther
             9:1) — a month that repeatedly witnessed divine
             deliverance for the Jewish people.

Passover    — The annual commemoration of Israel's deliverance
             from Egypt (Exodus 12). The returned exiles celebrated
             Passover immediately after the Temple dedication,
             connecting their deliverance from Babylon to the
             original deliverance from Egypt. Both were acts of
             divine redemption requiring ongoing remembrance.

Sin         — Hebrew "chattat" — an offering for purification from
Offering      sin. The twelve goats (one per tribe) acknowledged
             the nation's corporate guilt while seeking corporate
             cleansing. The offering confessed that even in
             restoration, the people needed atonement — they came
             back to God as sinners needing grace, not as heroes
             claiming reward.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. God keeps His records and reveals them at the right time (vv.1-5).
   The decree was hidden in an archive for 18 years, but surfaced
   exactly when needed. God's promises do not expire. What He has
   spoken over your life — even if buried under years of silence —
   remains in His archives. At the appointed time, He will bring it
   to light and add to it beyond what was originally given.

2. God can turn opposition into support (vv.6-10). The same officials
   who investigated the Jews were now commanded to fund them. The
   enemy's own tax revenue financed God's Temple. When God is for
   you, even your adversaries become instruments of your provision.
   Don't be surprised when opposition unexpectedly transforms into
   assistance — that's God's sovereignty at work.

3. The faithful remnant represents the whole (v.17). Twelve goats
   for twelve tribes — even though only two tribes were present.
   If you're in a small, faithful community that seems insignificant,
   remember: God's remnant carries the identity of the whole. You
   don't need large numbers to represent God's purposes; you need
   faithful obedience.

4. Completion deserves celebration (vv.16-22). After years of
   opposition, delay, and hard work, the people celebrated with
   joy and sacrifice. When God brings a long project to completion,
   pause to celebrate — don't immediately rush to the next task.
   Mark the milestone. Offer thanks. Let joy have its full
   expression.

5. All joy comes from God (v.22). "The LORD had made them joyful."
   Their celebration was not mere human relief — it was divinely
   produced joy. True spiritual joy is not something we manufacture;
   it is something God gives. Ask Him for it. The joy of completed
   obedience is one of God's greatest gifts.
