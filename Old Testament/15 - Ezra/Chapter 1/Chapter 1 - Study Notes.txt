Ezra - Chapter 1
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
God fulfills Jeremiah's prophecy by stirring the spirit of Cyrus king of Persia to issue a decree allowing the Jewish exiles to return and rebuild the Temple in Jerusalem. The surrounding people contribute silver, gold, and goods, and Cyrus returns the sacred Temple vessels Nebuchadnezzar had taken.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Ezra the priest and scribe (likely author/compiler). Written
approximately 450-400 BC. Ezra covers two returns from Babylonian
exile: (1) Zerubbabel's return and Temple rebuilding (538-516 BC,
chapters 1-6); (2) Ezra's return and spiritual reform (458 BC,
chapters 7-10). Key themes: God's faithfulness to His covenant
promises, the sovereignty of God over pagan kings, the centrality
of worship/Temple, and the danger of compromise with surrounding
peoples.

Historical Context: Chapter 1 records the pivotal moment when God
fulfilled Jeremiah's prophecy (Jeremiah 25:11-12; 29:10) that the
exile would last seventy years. In 539 BC, Cyrus the Great conquered
Babylon, and in his first year (538 BC) issued a decree permitting
the Jews to return to Jerusalem and rebuild the Temple. This was
not mere political generosity — the text declares "the LORD stirred
up the spirit of Cyrus" (v.1). God moved the heart of a pagan
emperor to accomplish His redemptive purposes. The chapter records
three responses to the decree: (1) Cyrus issued the proclamation
and restored the Temple vessels (vv.1-4, 7-8); (2) God stirred
the spirits of willing returnees from Judah, Benjamin, and the
priestly/Levitical families (v.5); (3) surrounding neighbors
provided material support (v.6). The return of the Temple vessels
— specifically numbered at 5,400 — symbolizes God's faithfulness
in restoring what was lost. What Nebuchadnezzar had stolen and
placed in pagan temples, God now brought back through a pagan king's
own hands. The chapter sets the theological tone for the entire
book: God is sovereign over empires, faithful to His promises, and
active in stirring hearts to accomplish His will.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- The geographic arc: Babylon → Jerusalem (~900-mile journey through the Fertile Crescent). Two returns: 538 BC under Zerubbabel and 458 BC under Ezra.
- Jerusalem is the destination — the Temple is rebuilt (516 BC) and worship restored.
- The journey followed major trade routes along the Euphrates River, through Syria, then south into Judah — a 4-month trek (Ezra 7:9).

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "The LORD stirred up the spirit of Cyrus king of Persia"
        (KJV/ESV) — This is the theological key to the entire book.
        God does not merely permit the return — He causes it by
        moving the heart of the most powerful ruler on earth. The
        Hebrew "he'ir" (stirred/awakened) implies God roused Cyrus
        to action as one wakes a sleeper. Divine sovereignty
        operates through human decisions without violating them.
        Isaiah had prophesied Cyrus by name 150 years earlier
        (Isaiah 44:28; 45:1-4).

v.2  — "The LORD, the God of heaven, has given me all the kingdoms
        of the earth, and he has charged me to build him a house"
        (ESV) — A pagan king acknowledges the God of Israel as
        the source of his power. Whether Cyrus personally believed
        this or simply used diplomatic religious language (the
        Cyrus Cylinder shows he similarly credited Marduk), the
        text presents his words as accomplishing God's purpose.
        God can use even incomplete faith or political diplomacy
        to fulfill His plans.

v.5  — "Then rose up the heads of the fathers' houses of Judah
        and Benjamin, and the priests and the Levites, everyone
        whose spirit God had stirred to go up to rebuild the house
        of the LORD that is in Jerusalem" (ESV) — God stirred both
        the king (v.1) and the people (v.5). The same God who moved
        an empire also moved individual hearts. Not all exiles
        returned — only those whose spirit God had stirred. The
        return was voluntary, Spirit-prompted, and faith-driven.

v.7  — "Cyrus the king also brought out the vessels of the house
        of the LORD that Nebuchadnezzar had carried away from
        Jerusalem and placed in the house of his gods" (ESV) — The
        Temple vessels' return reverses the tragedy of 2 Kings
        25:14-15. What was desecrated in pagan temples is now
        restored to sacred use. God keeps track of what belongs to
        Him and brings it back in His timing.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.1  — Jeremiah 25:11-12 — "This whole land shall be a desolation…
        and these nations shall serve the king of Babylon seventy
        years." God's word through Jeremiah set the timeline that
        Ezra 1 fulfills. Prophetic promise preceded historical
        fulfillment by exactly the stated interval.

v.1  — Isaiah 44:28 — "Who says of Cyrus, 'He is my shepherd, and
        he shall fulfill all my purpose'; saying of Jerusalem, 'She
        shall be built,' and of the temple, 'Your foundation shall
        be laid.'" Written 150+ years before Cyrus's birth, this
        prophecy names the very king who issues the decree in
        Ezra 1.

v.1  — 2 Chronicles 36:22-23 — The ending of 2 Chronicles is
        nearly identical to the opening of Ezra, creating a
        seamless narrative bridge. The Chronicler ends on hope;
        Ezra begins with fulfillment.

v.5  — Philippians 2:13 — "For it is God who works in you, both
        to will and to work for his good pleasure." (ESV) The New
        Testament principle of God stirring hearts to obedience
        echoes the same dynamic seen in Ezra 1:5.

v.7  — Daniel 5:1-4 — Belshazzar desecrated the Temple vessels at
        his feast, and that very night Babylon fell to Cyrus. The
        vessels' misuse triggered judgment; their restoration
        signals God's redemptive reversal.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/ezra-1/
  Guzik emphasizes the remarkable sovereignty of God in using Cyrus,
  noting that God "stirred" both king and people — showing that
  divine initiative precedes human response. He highlights the
  parallel with the Exodus: just as Israel left Egypt with Egyptian
  wealth (Exodus 12:35-36), the returning exiles left Babylon
  enriched by their neighbors.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "God can stir the spirit of a heathen king as easily as He stirs
  the heart of a saint. The same almighty power that awakens the
  sinner from spiritual death awakened Cyrus from political
  indifference. Mark well — the Lord 'stirred up' this monarch.
  Cyrus did not originate the thought; it was put into him by the
  Most High. Kings are but chess pieces on the board of Providence,
  and God moves them at His pleasure. Let this comfort every
  believer: if God can turn the heart of an empire's ruler, He
  can certainly turn the circumstances of your small life toward
  His good purposes."

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1  — KJV: "stirred up the spirit" / ESV: "stirred up the spirit"
       — Both render Hebrew "he'ir eth-ruach" identically. The verb
       "he'ir" means to rouse, awaken, or excite to action. The
       same word describes God stirring warriors to battle
       (Joel 3:7) and stirring the Medes against Babylon
       (Jeremiah 51:11).

v.4  — KJV: "whosoever remaineth" / ESV: "each survivor" — Hebrew
       "ha-nish'ar" (the one remaining/surviving). KJV's phrasing
       emphasizes universality ("whosoever"); ESV's "each survivor"
       emphasizes individual identity. Both refer to Jews remaining
       in Babylon who chose not to return but were commanded to
       support those who did.

v.9  — KJV: "thirty chargers of gold" / ESV: "30 basins of gold"
       — Hebrew "agartelei zahab." The term "agartal" appears only
       here in the Old Testament. KJV's "chargers" (large platters/
       dishes) follows older interpretation; ESV's "basins"
       reflects the likely Aramaic/Persian etymology suggesting a
       vessel for collecting liquid. The exact form is uncertain.

v.9  — KJV: "nine and twenty knives" / ESV: "29 censers" — Hebrew
       "machalaphim." KJV derives from "chalaph" (to cut/change),
       yielding "knives." ESV and most modern translations derive
       it from a root meaning "substitute" or connect it to
       censers/incense-related vessels. The precise identification
       of these Temple implements remains debated.

v.11 — KJV: "five thousand and four hundred" / ESV: "5,400" —
       Both agree on the total number of vessels, though the
       itemized list in vv.9-10 only accounts for about 2,499.
       The difference represents additional smaller or
       uncategorized vessels included in the total count.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Cyrus       — Cyrus II ("the Great"), founder of the Achaemenid
             Persian Empire. Conquered Babylon in 539 BC. Known
             for relative religious tolerance — his policy was to
             repatriate exiled peoples and restore their worship.
             The Cyrus Cylinder (a clay document) confirms this
             general policy. Scripture uniquely identifies him as
             God's "anointed" (mashiach) and "shepherd" (Isaiah
             44:28; 45:1) — extraordinary titles for a Gentile
             king, indicating God's sovereign use of him.

Sheshbazzar — "The prince of Judah" (v.8) entrusted with the
             Temple vessels. Likely a Davidic descendant (possibly
             Shenazzar of 1 Chronicles 3:18). He led the first
             return and laid the Temple foundation (Ezra 5:16) but
             is soon succeeded by Zerubbabel in the narrative.
             Some scholars identify them as the same person; most
             view them as successive leaders.

Stirred up  — Hebrew "he'ir" — to rouse, awaken, stir to action.
             Used of God's sovereign activation of human will.
             Does not imply coercion but divine initiative that
             prompts voluntary response. God "stirred" Cyrus (v.1)
             and "raised" the people's spirits (v.5) — same divine
             energy, different recipients.

Freewill    — Hebrew "nedabah" — a voluntary offering beyond what
Offering      is required. The exiles who stayed behind gave freely
             to support the returnees. This parallels the Exodus
             pattern where Egyptians gave Israel gold and silver
             (Exodus 12:35-36). God's people departing captivity
             are materially supplied by those remaining.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. God is sovereign over world leaders and governments (v.1). The
   most powerful emperor on earth was an instrument in God's hand.
   No political situation is beyond God's control. When you feel
   anxious about political events or unjust rulers, remember: "The
   LORD stirred up the spirit of Cyrus." God moves kings, presidents,
   and nations to accomplish His purposes. Pray with confidence that
   God can turn any leader's heart (Proverbs 21:1).

2. God fulfills His promises on His timetable (v.1). Seventy years
   is a long time — most of the original exiles died in Babylon. But
   God's word through Jeremiah did not fail. If you are waiting for
   God to fulfill a promise, the delay does not mean denial. God's
   timing is precise, even when it spans decades or generations.
   Trust the character of the Promise-Keeper, not the clock.

3. God stirs hearts before He moves circumstances (v.5). Before the
   journey began, God awakened desire in specific people. Spiritual
   readiness precedes physical action. If God is stirring something
   in your heart — a calling, a conviction, a holy restlessness —
   pay attention. That stirring is the same divine energy that moved
   Cyrus and the exiles. Respond to it with obedient action.

4. Not everyone responds to God's call (v.5). Many Jews stayed in
   Babylon — comfortable, established, unwilling to risk the
   difficult journey. Permission to return did not guarantee
   willingness to return. God opens doors, but we must choose to
   walk through them. Comfort and security can become enemies of
   obedience. What "Babylon" are you too comfortable in?

5. What was lost in judgment can be restored by grace (vv.7-11).
   The Temple vessels — taken by conquest, defiled in pagan temples
   — were returned intact. God tracks what belongs to Him and
   restores it in His time. Whatever the enemy has stolen from your
   life — years, relationships, purpose — God is a God of
   restoration. His redemptive reversals may take time, but they
   are thorough.
