Ezra - Chapter 5
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Stirred by the prophets Haggai and Zechariah, Zerubbabel and Jeshua resume building the Temple despite lacking official permission. The governor Tattenai investigates and writes to King Darius, but the Jews continue building unhindered as God watches over them.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Ezra the priest and scribe (likely author/compiler). Written
approximately 450-400 BC. Ezra covers two returns from Babylonian
exile: (1) Zerubbabel's return and Temple rebuilding (538-516 BC,
chapters 1-6); (2) Ezra's return and spiritual reform (458 BC,
chapters 7-10). Key themes: God's faithfulness to His covenant
promises, the sovereignty of God over pagan kings, the centrality
of worship/Temple, and the danger of compromise with surrounding
peoples.

Historical Context: Chapter 5 marks the restart of Temple
construction in 520 BC — the second year of Darius I — after 16
years of inactivity. The catalyst was not political change but
prophetic ministry: Haggai and Zechariah spoke God's word to the
discouraged community, and the leaders responded with renewed
obedience. Immediately, opposition resurfaced — Tattenai (the
Persian governor of the Trans-Euphrates province) investigated the
building project. But unlike chapter 4, the outcome here is
different. The text reveals why: "the eye of their God was upon
the elders of the Jews" (v.5). God's watchful providence ensured
that Tattenai's investigation became a path to vindication rather
than obstruction. Tattenai's letter to Darius (vv.6-17) is
remarkably fair — he reports facts without the malicious spin of
chapter 4's accusers. He even notes that the work "goeth fast on,
and prospereth" (v.8). The Jewish elders' response (vv.11-16) is
a model of faithful witness: they identify themselves as "servants
of the God of heaven and earth," acknowledge their fathers' sin
that caused the exile, and cite Cyrus's decree as their legal
authority. They request that the Persian archives be searched for
confirmation. The chapter demonstrates that when God's people obey
His prophetic word, even official inquiries become instruments of
divine vindication.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- The geographic arc: Babylon → Jerusalem (~900-mile journey through the Fertile Crescent). Two returns: 538 BC under Zerubbabel and 458 BC under Ezra.
- Jerusalem is the destination — the Temple is rebuilt (516 BC) and worship restored.
- The journey followed major trade routes along the Euphrates River, through Syria, then south into Judah — a 4-month trek (Ezra 7:9).

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "Now the prophets, Haggai and Zechariah the son of Iddo,
        prophesied to the Jews who were in Judah and Jerusalem"
        (ESV) — Prophetic ministry broke the 16-year paralysis.
        Human discouragement required divine encouragement. God's
        word through His prophets was the power that restarted
        what opposition had stopped. The work resumed not because
        circumstances changed but because God spoke.

v.2  — "Then Zerubbabel the son of Shealtiel and Jeshua the son
        of Jozadak arose and began to rebuild the house of God
        that is in Jerusalem, and the prophets of God were with
        them, supporting them." (ESV) — Leaders acted, and prophets
        supported. The combination of prophetic vision and
        administrative action accomplished what neither could
        alone. The prophets didn't build; the builders couldn't
        restart without prophetic encouragement. Both roles were
        essential.

v.5  — "But the eye of their God was on the elders of the Jews,
        and they did not stop them" (ESV) — The pivotal statement
        of the chapter. God's protective watchfulness prevented the
        governor from stopping the work. "The eye of God" is an
        anthropomorphism for divine attention, care, and
        providential protection. What chapter 4's opposition
        accomplished, chapter 5's opposition could not — because
        God's eye was actively guarding His people.

v.11 — "We are the servants of the God of heaven and earth" (ESV)
        — The elders' self-identification before a pagan official
        was bold, clear, and theologically precise. They claimed
        God's universal sovereignty ("heaven and earth") while
        presenting themselves as humble servants. Their identity
        was defined by their relationship to God, not by their
        political status.

v.12 — "But because our fathers had angered the God of heaven, he
        gave them into the hand of Nebuchadnezzar king of Babylon,
        the Chaldean, who destroyed this house and carried away
        the people to Babylonia" (ESV) — Remarkable honesty before
        a foreign government. The elders acknowledged their
        ancestors' sin as the cause of exile — they did not blame
        Babylon or play the victim. This theological interpretation
        of history gave credibility to their claim of divine
        authorization for the return.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.1  — Haggai 1:1-11 — Haggai's first message (August 520 BC):
        "Consider your ways. Go up to the hills and bring wood
        and build the house." (ESV) The prophet diagnosed the
        people's misplaced priorities and called them to action.
        Within 23 days of this prophecy, construction resumed
        (Haggai 1:14-15).

v.1  — Zechariah 4:6-10 — Zechariah's encouragement to Zerubbabel:
        "Not by might, nor by power, but by my Spirit, says the
        LORD of hosts." (ESV) The hands that laid the foundation
        would also complete it. This promise sustained the builders
        through the opposition described in this chapter.

v.5  — Psalm 33:18 — "Behold, the eye of the LORD is on those who
        fear him, on those who hope in his steadfast love." (ESV)
        The same "eye of God" that protected the Jewish elders
        watches over all who reverence Him. Divine surveillance is
        not threat but protection for the faithful.

v.11 — Daniel 2:37-38 — "You, O king, the king of kings, to whom
        the God of heaven has given the kingdom, the power, and
        the might, and the glory." (ESV) The elders used the same
        title ("God of heaven") that Daniel had used before
        Nebuchadnezzar — the standard way Jews identified their
        God to Persian/Babylonian officials.

v.14 — Ezra 1:7-8 — The return of the Temple vessels, now cited
        as evidence of Cyrus's authorization. The documented
        transfer of sacred objects provided legal proof that
        Cyrus had indeed decreed the Temple's reconstruction.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/ezra-5/
  Guzik highlights the essential role of the prophets in restarting
  the work, noting that God's people needed more than permission —
  they needed prophetic encouragement to overcome 16 years of
  complacency. He emphasizes that "the eye of their God" (v.5)
  made all the difference between the failed opposition of chapter
  5 and the successful opposition of chapter 4.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "Sixteen years the work lay still, and it would have lain still
  forever had not God sent His prophets. Mark this: the resumption
  of God's work requires the word of God freshly applied. It is
  not enough to know what Scripture said years ago — we need the
  Spirit to apply it with fresh power to present conditions. Haggai
  and Zechariah were the instruments; God was the power. And when
  the builders obeyed, note the beautiful phrase: 'the eye of their
  God was upon them.' That watchful eye turned a hostile inquiry
  into a vindication. What might have been a disaster became a
  triumph — because God was watching. Believer, when you step out
  in obedience, the eye of your God is upon you. Opposition may
  inquire, but Providence will protect."

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1  — KJV: "in the name of the God of Israel, even unto them" /
       ESV: "in the name of the God of Israel who was over them"
       — The Aramaic/Hebrew is ambiguous: does "over them" modify
       God (who was over them) or the prophets (who prophesied to
       them)? ESV interprets it as God's authority over the people;
       KJV leaves the connection open. Both readings affirm divine
       authority behind the prophetic message.

v.3  — KJV: "Who hath commanded you to build this house, and to
       make up this wall?" / ESV: "Who gave you a decree to build
       this house and to finish this structure?" — Aramaic "ushar-
       nah" could mean "wall" or "structure/furnishing." KJV's
       "wall" and ESV's "structure" reflect this ambiguity. The
       question concerns authorization: by whose authority do you
       build?

v.5  — KJV: "the eye of their God was upon the elders" / ESV:
       "the eye of their God was on the elders" — Both translate
       identically. The Aramaic "ein Elahahon" (eye of their God)
       is a beautiful anthropomorphism for divine watchfulness and
       providential care. This phrase provides the theological
       interpretation of why Tattenai could not stop the work.

v.8  — KJV: "great stones" / ESV: "huge stones" — Aramaic "eben
       gelal" (stones of rolling — i.e., stones so large they must
       be rolled, not carried). Both translations communicate the
       impressive scale of construction. The governor was clearly
       impressed by the quality and pace of the work.

v.14 — KJV: "whom he had made governor" / ESV: "whom he had made
       governor" — Both agree that Cyrus appointed Sheshbazzar as
       "pechah" (governor) — a Persian administrative title.
       Sheshbazzar held official Persian authority, making the
       building project a state-sanctioned enterprise from its
       inception.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Haggai      — Prophet active in 520 BC whose four recorded messages
             (August-December) specifically addressed the people's
             failure to rebuild the Temple. His ministry was brief,
             pointed, and immediately effective. His name means
             "festal/festive" — appropriate for one who called
             the people back to worship.

Zechariah   — Prophet contemporaneous with Haggai but with a
             longer ministry (520-518 BC). His messages were more
             visionary and messianic than Haggai's practical
             exhortations. Together they provided both practical
             motivation (Haggai) and spiritual vision (Zechariah)
             for the discouraged community.

Tattenai    — Persian governor (pechah) of the province "Beyond
             the River" (Trans-Euphrates). Unlike the earlier
             accusers (chapter 4), Tattenai appears relatively
             neutral — he investigated, reported facts fairly, and
             awaited orders. His name appears in contemporary
             Persian administrative documents, confirming the
             historical accuracy of Ezra's account.

Eye of God  — An anthropomorphism for God's attentive, protective
             providence. Not mere observation but active care —
             God seeing, guarding, and directing outcomes for His
             people's benefit. Used here to explain why opposition
             failed: divine watchfulness overruled human hostility.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. God's word breaks through paralysis (v.1). Sixteen years of
   inaction ended when God sent prophets to speak His word. If
   you've been spiritually paralyzed — by discouragement, fear, or
   complacency — the remedy is fresh engagement with God's word.
   The Bible, rightly applied, has power to restart what has stalled
   in your life. Seek it actively; let it challenge your
   complacency.

2. Prophetic encouragement and practical action must go together
   (v.2). The prophets spoke; the leaders built. Vision without
   action is empty; action without vision is directionless. In your
   life and church, value both the encouragers (who cast vision) and
   the doers (who execute). Both are essential for God's work.

3. God's watchful eye changes outcomes (v.5). The same type of
   opposition that succeeded in chapter 4 failed in chapter 5.
   The difference? "The eye of their God was upon them." When you
   walk in obedience to God's revealed will, His protective
   providence oversees the outcome. Opposition may come, but it
   cannot ultimately succeed against God's watchful purpose.

4. Honest confession strengthens credibility (v.12). The elders
   didn't hide their nation's sin — they acknowledged it openly
   before a foreign official. Paradoxically, admitting past failure
   strengthened their case: a people who recognize God's discipline
   are credible witnesses to His restoration. Don't hide your past;
   let God's redemptive work in your story give testimony to His
   grace.

5. When God is ready to act, even bureaucracy serves His purposes
   (vv.6-17). Tattenai's investigation — which could have halted
   the work — became the means by which Cyrus's original decree was
   rediscovered and confirmed (chapter 6). God turned a hostile
   inquiry into divine vindication. Trust that God can use even
   institutional processes for your good.
