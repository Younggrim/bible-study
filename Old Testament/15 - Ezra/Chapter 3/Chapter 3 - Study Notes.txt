Ezra - Chapter 3
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
The returned exiles rebuild the altar and restore daily sacrifices, then lay the foundation of the new Temple with celebration. The older men who remembered Solomon's Temple weep while the younger generation shouts for joy, creating a bittersweet scene.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Ezra the priest and scribe (likely author/compiler). Written
approximately 450-400 BC. Ezra covers two returns from Babylonian
exile: (1) Zerubbabel's return and Temple rebuilding (538-516 BC,
chapters 1-6); (2) Ezra's return and spiritual reform (458 BC,
chapters 7-10). Key themes: God's faithfulness to His covenant
promises, the sovereignty of God over pagan kings, the centrality
of worship/Temple, and the danger of compromise with surrounding
peoples.

Historical Context: Chapter 3 records two foundational acts of
worship restoration: the rebuilding of the altar (vv.1-6) and the
laying of the Temple foundation (vv.7-13). Both events carry deep
theological significance. The altar was rebuilt first — before walls,
before the Temple, before homes were fully established — because
worship of God takes priority over all other activity. The seventh
month (Tishri) was chosen deliberately: it was the month of the
Feast of Trumpets, the Day of Atonement, and the Feast of
Tabernacles. The returnees reestablished the entire liturgical
calendar before the building was complete. The foundation-laying
in the second year (536 BC) produced a remarkable scene: younger
people shouted with joy at the new beginning, while elderly priests
and leaders who remembered Solomon's glorious Temple wept at the
comparison. The two sounds merged into one indistinguishable roar
heard from far away. This mixture of joy and grief reflects the
theological tension of restoration — genuine gratitude for what God
is doing, mixed with honest sorrow over what was lost. The cedar
procurement from Lebanon echoes Solomon's original Temple
construction (1 Kings 5), intentionally connecting this new work
to the former glory.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- The geographic arc: Babylon → Jerusalem (~900-mile journey through the Fertile Crescent). Two returns: 538 BC under Zerubbabel and 458 BC under Ezra.
- Jerusalem is the destination — the Temple is rebuilt (516 BC) and worship restored.
- The journey followed major trade routes along the Euphrates River, through Syria, then south into Judah — a 4-month trek (Ezra 7:9).

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "Then arose Jeshua the son of Jozadak, with his fellow
        priests, and Zerubbabel the son of Shealtiel with his
        kinsmen, and they built the altar of the God of Israel,
        to offer burnt offerings on it, as it is written in the
        Law of Moses the man of God." (ESV) — The altar preceded
        the Temple. Worship did not wait for ideal conditions or
        complete facilities. The leaders prioritized sacrifice
        according to Scripture ("as it is written") — obedience
        to the Word governed their restoration, not human
        innovation.

v.3  — "They set the altar in its place, for fear was on them
        because of the peoples of the lands" (ESV) — Fear of
        hostile neighbors drove them toward God, not away from Him.
        Their response to external threat was to establish worship,
        not fortification. The altar was their first defense —
        communion with God their primary security.

v.11 — "And they sang responsively, praising and giving thanks to
        the LORD, 'For he is good, for his steadfast love endures
        forever toward Israel.'" (ESV) — This refrain (from Psalm
        136) was the anthem of Israel's worship. At every pivotal
        moment — Temple dedication, battle, revival — this song
        appears. God's enduring steadfast love (chesed) is the
        theological foundation for all restoration.

v.12 — "But many of the priests and Levites and heads of fathers'
        houses, old men who had seen the first house, wept with a
        loud voice when they saw the foundation of this house being
        laid" (ESV) — These elderly men had seen Solomon's Temple
        before its destruction in 586 BC — roughly 50 years
        earlier. Their weeping was not faithlessness but honest
        grief: the new foundation was visibly inferior to Solomon's
        glory. God acknowledged this grief through Haggai (Haggai
        2:3-9), promising that the latter house's glory would
        exceed the former.

v.13 — "The people could not distinguish the sound of the joyful
        shout from the sound of the people's weeping" (ESV) — Joy
        and grief coexisted authentically. Both responses were
        valid; neither was rebuked. Mature faith holds both —
        gratitude for present grace and honest sorrow over past
        loss — without demanding that one silence the other.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.2  — Deuteronomy 12:5-6 — God's command to offer sacrifices at
        the place He chose. The returnees obeyed by rebuilding
        the altar at the exact site of Solomon's altar on Mount
        Moriah — the divinely designated location.

v.4  — Leviticus 23:33-43 — The instructions for the Feast of
        Tabernacles (Booths). The returnees kept this feast in
        the seventh month, remembering both the wilderness
        wandering and God's provision — especially meaningful for
        those who had just completed their own wilderness journey
        from Babylon.

v.10 — 1 Chronicles 16:4-6 — David's establishment of the worship
        order with trumpets and cymbals. The phrase "according to
        the directions of David" (v.10, ESV) shows the community
        following David's liturgical blueprint, not creating their
        own.

v.11 — 2 Chronicles 5:13 — At Solomon's Temple dedication, the
        same psalm was sung: "For he is good, for his steadfast
        love endures forever." (ESV) The repetition links the
        second Temple to the first — the same God, the same mercy,
        the same song of praise.

v.12 — Haggai 2:3-9 — "Who is left among you who saw this house in
        its former glory?... The latter glory of this house shall
        be greater than the former." (ESV) God's response to the
        weeping elders was a promise that surpassed their grief —
        the latter Temple would see the Messiah Himself enter it.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/ezra-3/
  Guzik emphasizes that the altar was built before the Temple —
  showing that access to God through sacrifice is more fundamental
  than buildings. He notes the "fear" of v.3 actually motivated
  worship rather than paralysis, and draws attention to the
  bittersweet mixture of weeping and joy as a model for honest
  emotional response in worship.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "The old men wept and the young men shouted — and both were
  right. The elders remembered a greater glory and mourned its
  loss; the youth rejoiced in present mercy and thanked God for
  new beginnings. Let neither rebuke the other. In every work of
  God there is cause for both tears and triumph. The past had
  glories we shall not see again this side of heaven; the present
  has mercies we must not despise. But note well: they built the
  altar first, before the Temple walls arose. Worship before
  architecture! Sacrifice before structure! If your heart is
  right with God, you need not wait for perfect circumstances
  to begin praising Him."

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.3  — KJV: "set the altar upon his bases" / ESV: "set the altar
       in its place" — Hebrew "al-mekonothav" (upon its
       foundations/bases). The altar was placed on the exact
       original foundation — not a new location but the historic
       site where Solomon's altar had stood. Both translations
       convey the precision of placement, though KJV's "bases"
       emphasizes the physical foundation stones.

v.4  — KJV: "feast of tabernacles" / ESV: "Feast of Booths" —
       Hebrew "chag ha-sukkoth." Both are valid translations:
       "tabernacles" (temporary shelters) is the traditional
       English term; "booths" is a more direct rendering of
       "sukkoth." The feast commemorated Israel's wilderness
       dwelling and God's provision.

v.7  — KJV: "meat, and drink, and oil" / ESV: "food, drink, and
       oil" — KJV's "meat" means food generally (archaic usage),
       not flesh specifically. ESV updates to modern English.
       These provisions paid Phoenician workers for cedar
       transport, exactly as Solomon had paid Hiram of Tyre
       (1 Kings 5:11).

v.10 — KJV: "after the ordinance of David" / ESV: "according to
       the directions of David" — Hebrew "al-yedei David" (by/
       according to the hands of David). David established the
       worship protocols for trumpets, cymbals, and antiphonal
       singing (1 Chronicles 16; 25). The post-exilic community
       followed his pattern rather than innovating.

v.12 — KJV: "ancient men" / ESV: "old men" — Hebrew "zeqenim"
       (elders/aged ones). These would have been at least 70-80
       years old in 536 BC to have childhood memories of the
       Temple destroyed in 586 BC. Their survival through exile
       itself testifies to God's preserving grace.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Altar       — The bronze altar of burnt offering, the central
             piece of Israelite sacrificial worship. Rebuilt on
             the exact site of Solomon's altar before the Temple
             itself was reconstructed. The altar represented
             access to God through substitutionary sacrifice —
             without it, no approach to God was possible under
             the Mosaic covenant.

Feast of    — Hebrew "Sukkoth" — one of the three pilgrimage
Tabernacles/  festivals (with Passover and Pentecost). Celebrated
Booths        in the seventh month for seven days. Israelites
             dwelt in temporary shelters, remembering the
             wilderness wandering and celebrating the harvest.
             For the returnees, this feast held special resonance
             — they had just completed their own "wilderness
             journey" from Babylon.

Foundation  — The base/platform upon which the Temple would be
             constructed. Its laying was a ceremonial event
             (comparable to a modern cornerstone ceremony) marking
             the official start of construction. The visible
             dimensions of this foundation revealed the scale of
             the new Temple — which appeared inferior to Solomon's
             to those who remembered.

Sang by     — KJV: "sang together by course" — antiphonal or
Course        responsive singing where groups alternated verses.
             This liturgical practice, established by David
             (1 Chronicles 16), involved two choirs singing back
             and forth, creating a dynamic worship experience that
             involved the entire congregation.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Worship doesn't wait for perfect conditions (vv.2-3). The altar
   was built before the Temple, before the walls, before safety was
   secured. You don't need perfect circumstances to worship — you
   need a willing heart. If you're waiting for your life to be in
   order before you commit to worship, you've reversed God's
   priority. Worship first; let everything else be built around it.

2. Fear can drive you toward God rather than away (v.3). The people
   were afraid of hostile neighbors — and their response was to
   build an altar, not a fortress. When you face threats, is your
   first instinct to seek God or to seek self-protection? Make
   worship your first response to fear, not your last resort.

3. It's okay to grieve while others celebrate (vv.12-13). The old
   men wept; the young men shouted. Neither group was wrong. If you
   have experienced loss — of a spouse, a ministry, a season of
   life — you may feel grief even in moments of new beginning.
   That grief is not faithlessness; it is honest love for what was.
   Give yourself permission to feel both joy and sorrow in God's
   presence.

4. Start where you are with what you have (vv.6-7). The foundation
   was modest compared to Solomon's. But they didn't wait for
   resources equal to Solomon's wealth — they began with what they
   had. God does not require you to match previous generations'
   achievements. He requires faithfulness with your present
   resources. Begin the work; let God handle the comparison.

5. Follow God's Word, not human innovation (v.2). Everything was
   done "as it is written in the law of Moses." Restoration meant
   returning to Scripture, not inventing new approaches. In your
   own spiritual rebuilding, let the Bible set the pattern. Don't
   look to culture or convenience — look to "what is written."
