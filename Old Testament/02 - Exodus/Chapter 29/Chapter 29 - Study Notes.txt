Exodus - Chapter 29
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
God prescribes the consecration ceremony for Aaron and his sons as priests, including sacrifices and the promise to dwell among Israel.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 29 describes the seven-day consecration ceremony
for Aaron and his sons — the ordination of Israel's priesthood. The ceremony
involves washing, clothing, anointing, and three sacrifices: a sin offering
(bull), a burnt offering (first ram), and a consecration/ordination offering
(second ram). Blood is applied to the priests' right ear, right thumb, and
right big toe — consecrating what they hear, what they do, and where they
go. The chapter concludes with God's magnificent promise: "I will dwell among
the children of Israel, and will be their God" (v.45). The entire Tabernacle
system exists for one purpose: God dwelling with His people.

The Consecration Ceremony (vv.1-9): Requirements: one bull, two rams without
blemish, unleavened bread/cakes/wafers (vv.1-3). Aaron and sons are washed
at the Tabernacle entrance (v.4). Aaron is dressed in the priestly garments
(vv.5-6), anointed with oil (v.7). His sons are dressed (vv.8-9). "The
priest's office shall be theirs for a perpetual statute" (v.9).

The Sin Offering (vv.10-14): Aaron and sons lay hands on the bull's head —
identification/transfer of sin (v.10). The bull is killed; blood goes on the
altar's horns and base (vv.11-12). Fat burned on the altar; flesh burned
outside the camp (vv.13-14). "It is a sin offering" (v.14).

The Burnt Offering (vv.15-18): Hands laid on the first ram (v.15). Killed;
blood sprinkled on the altar (v.16). Cut in pieces; entirely burned (vv.17-
18). "A sweet savour, an offering made by fire unto the LORD" (v.18).

The Ram of Consecration (vv.19-28): Hands laid on the second ram (v.19).
Blood applied to the right ear, right thumb, right big toe of Aaron and sons
(v.20). Blood and oil sprinkled on them and their garments (v.21). Fat,
bread, and the right shoulder waved before the LORD (vv.22-25). The breast
becomes the priest's portion (vv.26-28).

Seven Days of Consecration (vv.29-37): The ceremony repeats for seven days
(v.35). A bull offered daily for atonement (v.36). The altar itself is
consecrated: "whatsoever toucheth the altar shall be holy" (v.37).

The Daily Offerings (vv.38-46): Two lambs daily — morning and evening (v.38).
With flour, oil, and wine (vv.40-41). "A continual burnt offering throughout
your generations" (v.42). God's promise: "I will meet you... I will dwell
among the children of Israel, and will be their God" (vv.42, 45-46).

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Israel remains encamped at Mount Sinai. The priestly ordination ceremony instructions are given on the mountain.
- "At the door of the tabernacle of the congregation" (v.4): All consecration occurs at the Tabernacle entrance — the meeting point between God and man.

  "The right EAR — consecrate what you hear. Listen to God, not the world.
  The right THUMB — consecrate what you do. Work for God, not self. The
  right great TOE — consecrate where you go. Walk in God's paths, not your
  own. Blood on the ear, blood on the hand, blood on the foot — the whole
  person, from hearing to doing to walking, covered by atoning blood and
  set apart for God. Christian, you are not your own. Your ears belong to
  Christ. Your hands belong to Christ. Your feet belong to Christ. Every
  faculty, every action, every step — consecrated by blood."

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1  — Hebrew "leqaddesh otam" — to make them holy, to set them apart.
       KJV: "to hallow them"
       ESV: "to consecrate them"
       ASV: "to hallow them"
       NET: "to consecrate them"
       WEB: "to sanctify them"
       Analysis: KJV/ASV's "hallow" (make holy), ESV/NET's "consecrate"
       (set apart for sacred use), and WEB's "sanctify" are all synonymous.
       Each captures the idea of separation for God's service.

v.9  — Hebrew "mille et yad" — literally "fill the hand" (ordain).
       KJV: "consecrate"
       ESV: "ordain"
       ASV: "consecrate"
       NET: "the ministry of priesthood will belong to them by a perpetual ordinance"
       WEB: "consecrate"
       Analysis: The ESV "ordain" captures the official appointment; KJV/ASV/
       WEB "consecrate" emphasizes sacred setting-apart. NET diverges by using
       "ministry of priesthood" rather than simply "priesthood," emphasizing
       the functional/service aspect. NET also uses "ordinance" where others
       use "statute" (Hebrew "chuqqah"). The Hebrew idiom "fill the hand" may
       refer to placing the offerings in the priests' hands (v.24).

v.11 — Hebrew "lifnei YHWH" — before the LORD.
       KJV: "before the LORD"
       ESV: "before the LORD"
       ASV: "before Jehovah"
       NET: "before the Lord"
       WEB: "before Yahweh"
       Analysis: The divine name (Hebrew YHWH) rendered three different ways.
       ASV uses the traditional English hybrid "Jehovah"; WEB restores the
       probable Hebrew pronunciation "Yahweh"; NET follows the conventional
       substitution "the Lord." This distinction runs throughout the chapter
       (vv.11, 18, 23, 25, 42, 46).

v.14 — Hebrew "chattat" — sin offering / purification offering.
       KJV: "it is a sin offering"
       ESV: "it is a sin offering"
       ASV: "it is a sin offering"
       NET: "It is the purification offering"
       WEB: "it is a sin offering"
       Analysis: NET renders "chattat" as "purification offering" rather than
       "sin offering" to emphasize that the ritual purifies the altar and
       sanctuary from contamination, not just moral guilt. This is a
       significant interpretive choice in NET throughout Levitical texts.
       The flesh is burned outside the camp — foreshadowing Christ who
       "suffered without the gate" (Hebrews 13:12).

v.18 — Hebrew "re'ach nichoach" — a soothing/restful fragrance.
       KJV: "a sweet savour"
       ESV: "a pleasing aroma"
       ASV: "a sweet savor"
       NET: "a soothing aroma"
       WEB: "a pleasant aroma"
       Analysis: ASV retains KJV's archaic "savour"; WEB modernizes to
       "pleasant aroma"; NET interprets as "soothing" — capturing the sense
       that the offering brings rest/satisfaction to God. ESV's "pleasing
       aroma" is most natural in modern English.

v.21 — Hebrew "veqadash" — and he shall be holy/consecrated.
       KJV: "he shall be hallowed"
       ESV: "he shall be holy"
       ASV: "he shall be hallowed"
       NET: "so that he may be holy"
       WEB: "he shall be made holy"
       Analysis: ASV/KJV retain the archaic "hallowed"; WEB uses plain
       modern English "made holy"; NET restructures as a purpose clause
       ("so that"), making the consecration goal-oriented rather than merely
       declarative. ESV is most concise.

v.22 — Hebrew "eil hammilu'im" — the ram of filling (the hands).
       KJV: "ram of consecration"
       ESV: "ram of ordination"
       ASV: "ram of consecration"
       NET: "the ram for consecration"
       WEB: "the ram of consecration"
       Analysis: Same distinction as v.9: KJV/ASV/WEB/NET emphasize holiness
       ("consecration"); ESV emphasizes official appointment ("ordination").

v.33 — Hebrew "zar" — a stranger, an unauthorized person, a non-priest.
       KJV: "a stranger shall not eat thereof"
       ESV: "an outsider shall not eat of them"
       ASV: "a stranger shall not eat thereof, because they are holy"
       NET: "no one else may eat them, for they are holy"
       WEB: "a stranger shall not eat of them, because they are holy"
       Analysis: NET avoids both "stranger" (KJV/ASV/WEB) and "outsider"
       (ESV), using the more expansive "no one else" — any non-priest,
       regardless of nationality. This removes potential confusion with
       foreigners.

v.36 — Hebrew "chattat" — sin/purification offering.
       KJV: "thou shalt offer the bullock of sin-offering for atonement"
       ESV: "you shall offer a bull as a sin offering for atonement"
       ASV: "thou shalt offer the bullock of sin-offering for atonement"
       NET: "you are to prepare a bull for a purification offering for atonement"
       WEB: "you shall offer a bull for a sin offering for atonement"
       Analysis: Again NET's consistent "purification offering" for "chattat."
       Additionally, NET adds "you are to prepare" (an instructional tone)
       where others retain the more direct "thou shalt offer" / "you shall
       offer."

v.45 — Hebrew "veshakhanti betokh benei Yisra'el" — I will tabernacle/dwell
       in the midst of the sons of Israel.
       KJV: "I will dwell among the children of Israel"
       ESV: "I will dwell among the people of Israel"
       ASV: "I will dwell among the children of Israel"
       NET: "I will reside among the Israelites"
       WEB: "I will dwell among the children of Israel"
       Analysis: NET uniquely uses "reside" rather than "dwell," a less
       theologically loaded term. KJV/ASV/WEB retain "dwell" which echoes
       "tabernacle" (mishkan) and carries richer covenantal weight. This is
       the climactic promise — the purpose of the entire Tabernacle system.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Consecration/Ordination — Setting apart for sacred service. The seven-day
               ceremony transforms ordinary men into priests. Involves
               washing (purification), clothing (identity), anointing
               (empowerment), and sacrifice (atonement).

Laying on of hands — Identification and transfer. The priest identifies with
               the animal; his sin is symbolically transferred to it. The
               animal dies in his place.

Blood on ear, thumb, toe — Total consecration of the whole person: what you
               hear (ear), what you do (hand), where you go (foot). Every
               faculty belongs to God.

Sin offering   — Deals with the guilt of sin. The flesh is burned outside
               the camp — sin is removed from God's presence. Christ
               "suffered without the gate" (Hebrews 13:12).

Burnt offering — Entirely consumed on the altar. Represents total
               dedication/surrender to God. Nothing held back.

"Fill the hand" — Hebrew idiom for ordination. The offerings are placed in
               the priests' hands — they are "filled" with their sacred
               duty.

Daily offering — Two lambs, morning and evening, perpetually. Unceasing
               atonement pointing to Christ's once-for-all sacrifice that
               never needs repeating (Hebrews 10:12).

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "I will dwell among the children of Israel, and will be their God" (v.45).
   This is the purpose of EVERYTHING — the Tabernacle, the priesthood, the
   sacrifices. God wants to live with you. Do you want to live with Him?

2. Blood on the ear, thumb, and toe (v.20). Your whole life is consecrated:
   what you listen to, what you do with your hands, where your feet take
   you. Is every part of your life under Christ's lordship?

3. The priests were washed BEFORE being clothed (v.4). Cleansing precedes
   service. You can't serve God effectively while carrying unconfessed sin.
   Let Him wash you first.

4. Seven days of consecration (v.35). Sanctification takes time — it's not
   instant. God works in you progressively. Be patient with the process.

5. "That I may dwell among them" (v.46). God brought Israel out of Egypt
   FOR THIS — not just freedom from slavery but fellowship with Himself.
   Your salvation is not just escape from hell — it's entrance into God's
   presence. Are you living in that presence daily?
