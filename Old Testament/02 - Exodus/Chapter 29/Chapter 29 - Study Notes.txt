Exodus - Chapter 29
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
God prescribes the consecration ceremony for Aaron and his sons as priests, including sacrifices and the promise to dwell among Israel.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 29 describes the seven-day consecration ceremony
for Aaron and his sons — the ordination of Israel's priesthood. The ceremony
involves washing, clothing, anointing, and three sacrifices: a sin offering
(bull), a burnt offering (first ram), and a consecration/ordination offering
(second ram). Blood is applied to the priests' right ear, right thumb, and
right big toe — consecrating what they hear, what they do, and where they
go. The chapter concludes with God's magnificent promise: "I will dwell among
the children of Israel, and will be their God" (v.45). The entire Tabernacle
system exists for one purpose: God dwelling with His people.

The Consecration Ceremony (vv.1-9): Requirements: one bull, two rams without
blemish, unleavened bread/cakes/wafers (vv.1-3). Aaron and sons are washed
at the Tabernacle entrance (v.4). Aaron is dressed in the priestly garments
(vv.5-6), anointed with oil (v.7). His sons are dressed (vv.8-9). "The
priest's office shall be theirs for a perpetual statute" (v.9).

The Sin Offering (vv.10-14): Aaron and sons lay hands on the bull's head —
identification/transfer of sin (v.10). The bull is killed; blood goes on the
altar's horns and base (vv.11-12). Fat burned on the altar; flesh burned
outside the camp (vv.13-14). "It is a sin offering" (v.14).

The Burnt Offering (vv.15-18): Hands laid on the first ram (v.15). Killed;
blood sprinkled on the altar (v.16). Cut in pieces; entirely burned (vv.17-
18). "A sweet savour, an offering made by fire unto the LORD" (v.18).

The Ram of Consecration (vv.19-28): Hands laid on the second ram (v.19).
Blood applied to the right ear, right thumb, right big toe of Aaron and sons
(v.20). Blood and oil sprinkled on them and their garments (v.21). Fat,
bread, and the right shoulder waved before the LORD (vv.22-25). The breast
becomes the priest's portion (vv.26-28).

Seven Days of Consecration (vv.29-37): The ceremony repeats for seven days
(v.35). A bull offered daily for atonement (v.36). The altar itself is
consecrated: "whatsoever toucheth the altar shall be holy" (v.37).

The Daily Offerings (vv.38-46): Two lambs daily — morning and evening (v.38).
With flour, oil, and wine (vv.40-41). "A continual burnt offering throughout
your generations" (v.42). God's promise: "I will meet you... I will dwell
among the children of Israel, and will be their God" (vv.42, 45-46).

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Israel remains encamped at Mount Sinai. The priestly ordination ceremony instructions are given on the mountain.
- "At the door of the tabernacle of the congregation" (v.4): All consecration occurs at the Tabernacle entrance — the meeting point between God and man.

        anoint him." (ESV) — Consecration by anointing.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1  — KJV: "to hallow them" / ESV: "to consecrate them" — Hebrew
       "leqaddesh otam" — to make them holy, to set them apart. The KJV's
       "hallow" (make holy) and the ESV's "consecrate" (set apart for sacred
       use) are synonymous. Both capture the idea of separation for God's
       service.

v.9  — KJV: "consecrate" / ESV: "ordain" — Hebrew "mille et yad" —
       literally "fill the hand." An idiom for ordination/installation into
       office. The ESV's "ordain" captures the official appointment; the
       KJV's "consecrate" emphasizes the sacred setting-apart. The Hebrew
       idiom "fill the hand" may refer to placing the offerings in the
       priests' hands (v.24).

v.14 — KJV: "without the camp" / ESV: "outside the camp" — Hebrew
       "michutz lamachaneh." Both are equivalent. The sin offering's flesh
       is burned outside — foreshadowing Christ who "suffered without the
       gate" (Hebrews 13:12). Sin is removed from God's presence.

v.18 — KJV: "a sweet savour" / ESV: "a pleasing aroma" — Hebrew "re'ach
       nichoach" — a soothing/restful fragrance. The ESV's "pleasing aroma"
       is more natural in modern English. The sacrifice is pleasing to God —
       not the smell of burning flesh but the obedience and faith it
       represents.

v.22 — KJV: "ram of consecration" / ESV: "ram of ordination" — Hebrew
       "eil hammilu'im" — the ram of filling (the hands). Same distinction
       as v.9: KJV emphasizes holiness; ESV emphasizes official appointment.

v.33 — KJV: "a stranger shall not eat thereof" / ESV: "an outsider shall
       not eat of them" — Hebrew "zar" — a stranger, an unauthorized person,
       a non-priest. The ESV's "outsider" clarifies that this means anyone
       not authorized for priestly service, not merely a foreigner.

v.45 — KJV: "I will dwell among the children of Israel" / ESV: "I will
       dwell among the people of Israel" — Hebrew "veshakhanti betokh benei
       Yisra'el" — I will tabernacle/dwell in the midst of the sons of
       Israel. Both translations are equivalent. This is the climactic
       promise: God's presence among His people — the purpose of the entire
       Tabernacle system.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Consecration/Ordination — Setting apart for sacred service. The seven-day
               ceremony transforms ordinary men into priests. Involves
               washing (purification), clothing (identity), anointing
               (empowerment), and sacrifice (atonement).

Laying on of hands — Identification and transfer. The priest identifies with
               the animal; his sin is symbolically transferred to it. The
               animal dies in his place.

Blood on ear, thumb, toe — Total consecration of the whole person: what you
               hear (ear), what you do (hand), where you go (foot). Every
               faculty belongs to God.

Sin offering   — Deals with the guilt of sin. The flesh is burned outside
               the camp — sin is removed from God's presence. Christ
               "suffered without the gate" (Hebrews 13:12).

Burnt offering — Entirely consumed on the altar. Represents total
               dedication/surrender to God. Nothing held back.

"Fill the hand" — Hebrew idiom for ordination. The offerings are placed in
               the priests' hands — they are "filled" with their sacred
               duty.

Daily offering — Two lambs, morning and evening, perpetually. Unceasing
               atonement pointing to Christ's once-for-all sacrifice that
               never needs repeating (Hebrews 10:12).

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "I will dwell among the children of Israel, and will be their God" (v.45).
   This is the purpose of EVERYTHING — the Tabernacle, the priesthood, the
   sacrifices. God wants to live with you. Do you want to live with Him?

2. Blood on the ear, thumb, and toe (v.20). Your whole life is consecrated:
   what you listen to, what you do with your hands, where your feet take
   you. Is every part of your life under Christ's lordship?

3. The priests were washed BEFORE being clothed (v.4). Cleansing precedes
   service. You can't serve God effectively while carrying unconfessed sin.
   Let Him wash you first.

4. Seven days of consecration (v.35). Sanctification takes time — it's not
   instant. God works in you progressively. Be patient with the process.

5. "That I may dwell among them" (v.46). God brought Israel out of Egypt
   FOR THIS — not just freedom from slavery but fellowship with Himself.
   Your salvation is not just escape from hell — it's entrance into God's
   presence. Are you living in that presence daily?
