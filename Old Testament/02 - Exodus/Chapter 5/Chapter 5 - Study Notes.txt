Exodus - Chapter 5
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Moses and Aaron confront Pharaoh who refuses to release Israel and increases their workload; Israel blames Moses for making things worse.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 5 records Moses and Aaron's first confrontation
with Pharaoh — and its disastrous immediate result. Pharaoh's response to
"Let my people go" is defiant: "Who is the LORD, that I should obey his
voice?" (v.2). He increases Israel's labor — demanding the same quota of
bricks without providing straw. The chapter is a study in how obedience to
God can initially make things worse before they get better. Israel's officers
blame Moses; Moses questions God. This is the dark before the dawn — the
situation must get worse to demonstrate that only God (not diplomacy or human
effort) can deliver.

First Confrontation with Pharaoh (vv.1-5): Moses and Aaron deliver God's
message: "Let my people go, that they may hold a feast unto me in the
wilderness" (v.1). Pharaoh's response: "Who is the LORD, that I should obey
his voice? I know not the LORD, neither will I let Israel go" (v.2). He
accuses Moses of making the people idle (v.5).

Pharaoh's Cruelty Increases (vv.6-14): Pharaoh commands: no more straw for
bricks, but the same production quota (vv.7-8). His reasoning: "they be
idle; therefore they cry" (v.8). The people scatter to gather stubble (v.12).
Taskmasters beat the Israelite officers for failing to meet quotas (v.14).

Israel Blames Moses (vv.15-21): The officers appeal to Pharaoh — rejected
(vv.15-18). They confront Moses and Aaron: "The LORD look upon you, and
judge; because ye have made our savour to be abhorred" (v.21). The very
people who worshipped in 4:31 now curse their deliverer.

Moses Questions God (vv.22-23): Moses returns to God with raw honesty:
"Lord, wherefore hast thou so evil entreated this people? why is it that
thou hast sent me?" (v.22). "Neither hast thou delivered thy people at all"
(v.23). Moses expected immediate deliverance — God has a longer plan.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Egypt — Pharaoh's court: Moses and Aaron confront Pharaoh in his palace, likely in the eastern Delta near Goshen.
- The land of Goshen: Where the Israelite slaves gathered straw for bricks and labored under Egyptian taskmasters.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "Thus says the LORD, the God of Israel, 'Let my people go, that
        they may hold a feast to me in the wilderness.'" (ESV) — The demand
        that defines the exodus narrative.

v.2  — "Who is the LORD, that I should obey his voice and let Israel go? I
        do not know the LORD, and moreover, I will not let Israel go." (ESV)
        — Pharaoh's defiance. He will learn who the LORD is.

v.8  — "They are idle; therefore they cry, 'Let us go and offer sacrifice
        to our God.'" (ESV) — Pharaoh interprets worship as laziness.

v.22 — "O Lord, why have you done evil to this people? Why did you ever
        send me?" (ESV) — Moses' honest complaint to God.

v.23 — "You have not delivered your people at all." (ESV) — The gap between
        God's promise and present reality.

  better, Pharaoh's deliberate ignorance of God, and Moses' honest prayer

Charles Spurgeon:
  Sermon: "Who Is the LORD?" on v.2 (MTP Vol. 19, No. 1098)
  "Who is the LORD? — Pharaoh asks this in defiance. He will receive his
  answer in ten plagues. He will learn who the LORD is — through frogs and
  flies and darkness and death. God does not leave this question unanswered.
  Every man who asks 'Who is the LORD that I should obey Him?' will receive
  an answer — either in grace or in judgment. Better to learn who He is at
  the burning bush than at the Red Sea. Better to bow willingly than to be
  broken."

  Sermon: "Things Getting Worse Before Better" on v.22 (MTP Vol. 24, No. 1420)
  "Neither hast thou delivered thy people AT ALL — Moses expected instant
  results. God had a process. The bricks without straw were necessary — to
  break Israel's attachment to Egypt, to demonstrate that only GOD could
  deliver, to prove that human diplomacy was useless. Christian, when you
  obey God and things get worse — do not conclude that God has failed. He is
  working. The darkest hour precedes the dawn."

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1  — Hebrew "shallach et ammi" — send away/release my people.
       KJV: "Let my people go, that they may hold a feast unto me"
       ESV: "Let my people go, that they may hold a feast to me"
       ASV: "Let my people go, that they may hold a feast unto me"
       NET: "Release my people so that they may hold a pilgrim feast"
       WEB: "Let my people go, that they may hold a feast to me"
       Analysis: NET uniquely adds "pilgrim" to qualify the feast,
       interpreting Hebrew "chag" as a pilgrimage festival. All others simply
       say "feast." A royal command from the King of kings to an earthly king
       — the defining phrase of the Exodus narrative.

v.2  — Hebrew "lo yadati et YHWH" — I do not acknowledge/recognize YHWH.
       KJV: "I know not the LORD"
       ESV: "I do not know the LORD"
       ASV: "I know not Jehovah"
       NET: "I do not know the LORD"
       WEB: "Who is Yahweh, that I should listen to his voice to let Israel go? I don't know Yahweh"
       Analysis: WEB spells out the divine name, making Pharaoh's defiance
       more personal and confrontational — he rejects a named deity, not an
       abstract title. Not ignorance but defiance — Pharaoh refuses to
       recognize YHWH's authority. ASV uses "Jehovah" for the same effect.

v.7  — Hebrew "teben" — chopped straw mixed with clay to strengthen bricks.
       KJV: "straw"
       ESV: "straw"
       ASV: "straw"
       NET: "straw"
       WEB: "straw"
       Analysis: All translations identical. Without straw, bricks crumble.
       This is Pharaoh's cruel escalation — demanding results without
       providing necessary materials.

v.8  — Hebrew "matkonet hallevenim" — the tally/quota of bricks; "nirpim" — idle/slack.
       KJV: "the tale of the bricks... they be idle"
       ESV: "the number of bricks... they are idle"
       ASV: "the number of bricks... they are idle"
       NET: "the same quota of bricks... they are slackers"
       WEB: "the same number of bricks... they are idle"
       Analysis: KJV's "tale" = tally/count (archaic English). NET's
       "slackers" is a vivid modern idiom for "nirpim" — capturing Pharaoh's
       contempt effectively. All others use "idle." The production quota
       remains unchanged despite removing straw — impossible cruelty.

v.21 — Hebrew "hiv'ashtem et reichenu" — you have made our smell/reputation stink.
       KJV: "ye have made our savour to be abhorred"
       ESV: "you have made us stink"
       ASV: "ye have made our savor to be abhorred"
       NET: "you have made us stink in the opinion of Pharaoh"
       WEB: "you have made us a stench"
       Analysis: ESV/WEB are blunt and literal. KJV/ASV are more formal.
       NET captures the Hebrew idiom most forcefully by adding "in the
       opinion of Pharaoh" — making the social dimension explicit. Moses has
       made Israel repulsive to Pharaoh.

v.22 — Hebrew "hare'ota" — to do evil to, to bring harm upon.
       KJV: "Lord, wherefore hast thou so evil entreated this people?"
       ESV: "O Lord, why have you done evil to this people?"
       ASV: "Lord, wherefore hast thou dealt ill with this people?"
       NET: "Lord, why have you caused trouble for this people?"
       WEB: "Lord, why have you treated this people so badly?"
       Analysis: Moses accuses God of harming His own people — raw, honest
       prayer. NET softens to "caused trouble." WEB paraphrases with
       "treated badly." ESV's "done evil" is most direct. ASV's "dealt ill"
       is archaic but forceful. God can handle our frustration.

v.23 — Hebrew "v'hatzel lo hitzalta" — emphatic infinitive absolute construction.
       KJV: "neither hast thou delivered thy people at all"
       ESV: "you have not delivered your people at all"
       ASV: "neither hast thou delivered thy people at all"
       NET: "you have certainly not rescued them!"
       WEB: "you haven't delivered your people at all"
       Analysis: The Hebrew infinitive absolute creates intense emphasis.
       NET mirrors this with "certainly not" and an exclamation mark.
       KJV/ASV use the double negative "neither... at all" for the same
       effect. Moses expected instant deliverance — God has a longer plan.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
"Let my people go" — God's demand through Moses. The defining phrase of the
               exodus. A command from the true King to a false one.

"Who is the LORD?" — Pharaoh's defiant question. The ten plagues are God's
               answer: "I AM the LORD" — demonstrated in power.

Bricks without straw — Pharaoh's cruel escalation. Demanding the same
               output without providing necessary materials. A picture of
               oppressive systems that demand results without support.

"Idle" (v.8)   — Pharaoh's interpretation of worship. The world often views
               devotion to God as laziness or escapism.

Moses' lament (vv.22-23) — Honest complaint to God when obedience produces
               worse results. Not faithlessness but the raw prayer of a
               struggling servant. God answers in chapter 6.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Who is the LORD, that I should obey his voice?" (v.2). Pharaoh's
   question is answered by the plagues. Is there an area where YOU are
   asking this question — resisting God's authority? He will answer.

2. Obedience made things worse (vv.6-14). Following God doesn't guarantee
   immediate improvement. Sometimes the situation gets harder before
   deliverance comes. Don't measure God's faithfulness by short-term
   results.

3. "Ye have made our savour to be abhorred" (v.21). The people blamed Moses
   — their deliverer. Have you ever blamed the person God sent to help you?
   Discouragement makes us attack allies.

4. "Lord, wherefore hast thou so evil entreated this people?" (v.22). Moses
   prays honestly. God can handle your frustration, your questions, your
   "why?" Bring it to Him — don't stuff it down or walk away.

5. "Neither hast thou delivered thy people at all" (v.23). Moses expected
   instant deliverance. God had a ten-plague process. Are you frustrated
   because God isn't working on YOUR timetable? Trust His process.
