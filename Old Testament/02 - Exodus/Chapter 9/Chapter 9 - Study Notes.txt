Exodus - Chapter 9
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
God strikes Egypt with livestock disease, boils, and devastating hail; Pharaoh admits sin but hardens his heart again.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 9 records plagues five through seven: livestock
disease (murrain), boils, and hail. The plagues intensify dramatically —
moving from discomfort to destruction. Key developments: (1) The magicians
are personally afflicted and cannot even stand (v.11); (2) God explicitly
states His purpose: "that my name may be declared throughout all the earth"
(v.16) — quoted by Paul in Romans 9:17; (3) God offers mercy within judgment
— warning Egyptians to shelter their livestock before the hail (vv.19-21);
(4) Pharaoh makes his most dramatic confession yet: "I have sinned... the
LORD is righteous, and I and my people are wicked" (v.27) — yet still
hardens when relief comes.

Fifth Plague: Livestock Disease (vv.1-7): God warns: "the hand of the LORD
is upon thy cattle" (v.3). A "very grievous murrain" (pestilence) strikes
horses, donkeys, camels, oxen, and sheep (v.3). God severs between Israel's
cattle and Egypt's (v.4). "All the cattle of Egypt died: but of the cattle
of the children of Israel died not one" (v.6). Pharaoh investigates —
confirms Israel is untouched — yet hardens his heart (v.7). Targets: Hathor
(cow goddess), Apis (bull god).

Sixth Plague: Boils (vv.8-12): No warning — God strikes directly. Moses
sprinkles furnace ashes toward heaven — boils break out on man and beast
(vv.8-10). "The magicians could not stand before Moses because of the boils"
(v.11) — they are personally defeated, unable even to appear. "The LORD
hardened the heart of Pharaoh" (v.12) — first time God actively hardens
(previously Pharaoh hardened himself). Targets: Isis (goddess of medicine),
Thoth (god of healing).

Seventh Plague: Hail (vv.13-35): God's longest warning speech. Purpose:
"that thou mayest know that there is none like me in all the earth" (v.14).
"For this cause have I raised thee up, for to shew in thee my power" (v.16).
God offers mercy: "gather thy cattle... into the houses" (v.19). Some
Egyptians fear God's word and shelter their animals (v.20); others do not
(v.21). Hail with fire — unprecedented destruction (vv.23-25). Goshen
protected (v.26). Pharaoh confesses: "I have sinned... the LORD is righteous"
(v.27). Moses prays; hail stops (v.33). Pharaoh "sinned yet more, and
hardened his heart" (v.34). Targets: Nut (sky goddess), Set (storm god).

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Egypt: Plagues 5-7 (pestilence, boils, hail) fall on Egypt; Israel in Goshen is spared.
- Goshen (v.26): "Only in the land of Goshen, where the children of Israel were, was there no hail." Geographic distinction shows God's covenant protection.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.6  — "All the livestock of Egypt died, but not one of the livestock of
        the people of Israel died." (ESV) — God's precise discrimination.

v.11 — "The magicians could not stand before Moses because of the boils."
        (ESV) — Counterfeit power completely defeated.

v.14 — "That you may know that there is none like me in all the earth."
        (ESV) — The purpose of the plagues: revelation.

v.16 — "But for this purpose I have raised you up, to show you my power,
        so that my name may be proclaimed in all the earth." (ESV) — God's
        sovereign purpose in Pharaoh.

v.27 — "This time I have sinned; the LORD is in the right, and I and my
        people are in the wrong." (ESV) — Pharaoh's confession — true words
        without true repentance.

  "For this cause have I RAISED THEE UP — God raised Pharaoh to the throne
  of Egypt for a purpose: to display divine power and declare the divine
  name throughout all the earth. And it worked. Rahab in Jericho heard about
  it (Joshua 2:10). The Philistines heard (1 Samuel 4:8). The whole ancient

  building God's reputation. Every tyrant who opposes God becomes an
  unwitting advertisement for God's power. The harder Pharaoh resisted, the
  more glorious God's victory became."

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.3  — Hebrew "dever" — pestilence, plague.
       KJV: "a very grievous murrain"
       ESV: "a very severe plague"
       ASV: "a very grievous murrain"
       NET: "a very terrible plague"
       WEB: "a very grievous pestilence"
       Analysis: KJV/ASV's "murrain" is archaic English for livestock plague
       — directly linking to traditional English plague vocabulary. ESV's
       "severe plague" and NET's "terrible plague" are accessible to modern
       readers. WEB uses "pestilence" — a middle ground between archaic and
       modern.

v.9  — Hebrew "shechin pore'ach avabu'ot" — inflamed sore erupting in blisters.
       KJV: "a boil breaking forth with blains"
       ESV: "boils breaking out in sores"
       ASV: "a boil breaking forth with blains"
       NET: "boils to break out and fester"
       WEB: "a boil breaking out with boils"
       Analysis: KJV/ASV's "blains" is archaic for blisters/pustules —
       revealing the Hebrew "avabu'ot" precisely. NET adds "fester"
       interpretively, emphasizing the ongoing infection. ESV's "sores" is
       clear. WEB's repetition of "boil... boils" is awkward but literal.

v.14 — Hebrew "al libbeka" — upon your heart.
       KJV: "all my plagues upon thine heart"
       ESV: "all my plagues on you yourself"
       ASV: "all my plagues upon thy heart"
       NET: "all my plagues on your heart"
       WEB: "all my plagues against your heart"
       Analysis: KJV/ASV/NET/WEB are more literal — "heart." ESV interprets
       "heart" as "you yourself." The plagues target Pharaoh's will — the
       seat of decision. WEB's "against" adds a combative tone the Hebrew
       supports.

v.16 — Hebrew "he'emadtikha" — caused you to stand, established you.
       KJV: "raised thee up"
       ESV: "raised you up"
       ASV: "for this cause have I made thee to stand"
       NET: "for this purpose I have caused you to stand"
       WEB: "for this cause I have made you stand"
       Analysis: The verse quoted in Romans 9:17. NET's "caused you to stand"
       subtly implies divine sovereignty over Pharaoh's existence. ASV/WEB
       preserve the causative force. God sustained Pharaoh through the
       plagues for a purpose — not creating evil but using an evil man for
       divine glory.

v.23 — Hebrew "esh" — fire (within the hail).
       KJV: "the fire ran along upon the ground"
       ESV: "fire flashing continually in the midst of the hail"
       ASV: "fire ran down unto the earth"
       NET: "fire fell to the earth"
       WEB: "lightning flashed down to the earth"
       Analysis: WEB interprets "esh" (fire) as "lightning" — a reasonable
       identification but moves beyond the Hebrew. ASV's "fire ran down" is
       most literal. NET's "fire fell" is a middle rendering. ESV is most
       descriptive. Lightning within hailstones — supernatural and terrifying.

v.27 — Hebrew "YHWH hatsaddiq" — YHWH is the righteous one.
       KJV: "the LORD is righteous"
       ESV: "the LORD is in the right"
       ASV: "Jehovah is righteous"
       NET: "The LORD is righteous"
       WEB: "Yahweh is righteous"
       Analysis: ASV/WEB spell out the divine name, making Pharaoh's
       confession more personal — he acknowledges YHWH by name. ESV
       paraphrases to "in the right" — emphasizing the forensic/legal sense.
       True words without true repentance — "this time" (happa'am) suggests
       Pharaoh admits fault only for this instance.

v.31 — Hebrew "giv'ol" — in bud/bloom.
       KJV: "the flax was bolled"
       ESV: "the flax was in bud"
       ASV: "the flax was in bloom"
       NET: "the flax was in bud"
       WEB: "the flax was in bloom"
       Analysis: KJV's "bolled" is extremely archaic — unintelligible to
       modern readers. ESV/NET use "in bud." ASV/WEB use "in bloom" — a
       slightly later stage. This botanical detail helps date the plague to
       January-February.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Murrain        — Severe livestock pestilence. Targets Hathor (cow goddess)
               and Apis (sacred bull). Egypt's animal gods cannot protect
               Egypt's animals.

Boils          — Painful, blistering skin disease. Targets Isis and Thoth
               (healing deities). Egypt's healing gods cannot heal Egypt.

"Raised thee up" — God's sovereign purpose in establishing Pharaoh on the
               throne. Not creating evil but using an evil ruler to display
               divine power and spread God's fame worldwide.

Mercy within judgment — God warns before the hail, giving Egyptians the
               chance to shelter (vv.19-21). Even in wrath, God remembers
               mercy (Habakkuk 3:2).

False confession — Pharaoh says the right words (v.27) but lacks genuine
               repentance. True repentance produces lasting change; false
               confession evaporates when pressure lifts.

"Fire mingled with hail" — Supernatural weather: lightning within
               hailstones. Demonstrates God's absolute control over nature
               — the sky goddess Nut is powerless.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "For this cause have I raised thee up" (v.16). God has a purpose for
   every person — even those who oppose Him. Nothing is wasted in God's
   economy. What is God accomplishing through your circumstances — even the
   difficult ones?

2. "He that feared the word of the LORD... made his servants and his cattle
   flee into the houses" (v.20). God offers warning before judgment. Some
   Egyptians listened; some didn't. When God warns you — through Scripture,
   through conscience, through others — do you listen?

3. "I have sinned... the LORD is righteous" (v.27). Pharaoh says the right
   words but doesn't mean them. Is your repentance genuine — producing
   lasting change? Or is it pressure-driven confession that evaporates when
   things improve?

4. The magicians "could not stand" (v.11). They started by imitating God's
   power; now they can't even show up. Every counterfeit eventually fails.
   Don't be impressed by imitations — they have an expiration date.

5. "That my name may be declared throughout all the earth" (v.16). God's
   ultimate purpose is His glory — His name known everywhere. Is your life
   contributing to that purpose? Are people learning who God is by watching
   your story?
