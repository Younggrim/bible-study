Exodus - Chapter 14
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
God parts the Red Sea, Israel crosses on dry ground, and Pharaoh's army is destroyed when the waters return.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 14 is the climax of the exodus — the crossing
of the Red Sea. This is Israel's defining moment: trapped between Pharaoh's
army and the sea, God parts the waters, Israel crosses on dry ground, and
Egypt's army is destroyed. This event is referenced more than any other in
the Old Testament as proof of God's power and faithfulness. It is Israel's
"baptism" (1 Corinthians 10:1-2) — the definitive break from Egypt. God
deliberately engineers the situation: He leads Israel to a dead end (v.2),
hardens Pharaoh to pursue (v.4), and then displays His glory by doing what
only He can do. The chapter contains Moses' greatest declaration of faith:
"Stand still, and see the salvation of the LORD" (v.13).

God Sets the Trap (vv.1-9): God directs Israel to camp by the sea — a
seemingly trapped position (v.2). Purpose: Pharaoh will think "they are
entangled" (v.3) and pursue. "I will be honoured upon Pharaoh" (v.4).
Pharaoh takes 600 chosen chariots plus all Egypt's chariots (vv.6-7). He
overtakes Israel at the sea (v.9).

Israel's Fear and Moses' Faith (vv.10-14): Israel sees the army and panics:
"Because there were no graves in Egypt, hast thou taken us away to die in
the wilderness?" (v.11). Moses responds with one of Scripture's greatest
faith declarations: "Fear ye not, stand still, and see the salvation of the
LORD" (v.13). "The LORD shall fight for you, and ye shall hold your peace"
(v.14).

The Crossing (vv.15-22): God tells Moses: stop praying and ACT — "speak
unto the children of Israel, that they go forward" (v.15). "Lift thou up thy
rod... and divide it" (v.16). The Angel of God and the pillar move behind
Israel — darkness to Egypt, light to Israel (vv.19-20). Moses stretches his
hand; God sends a strong east wind all night; the sea divides (v.21). Israel
walks through on dry ground — "the waters were a wall unto them on their
right hand, and on their left" (v.22).

Egypt's Destruction (vv.23-28): The Egyptians pursue into the sea (v.23).
God looks through the pillar and troubles them (v.24). Chariot wheels come
off (v.25). The Egyptians realize: "the LORD fighteth for them" (v.25).
Moses stretches his hand — the sea returns — "there remained not so much as
one of them" (v.28).

The Result (vv.29-31): Israel safe on dry land (v.29). Egypt dead on the
shore (v.30). "The people feared the LORD, and believed the LORD, and his
servant Moses" (v.31).

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Pi-hahiroth (v.2): Israel camps here, between Migdol and the sea, before Baal-zephon. The exact location is debated but is on the western shore of the Yam Suph.
- The Red Sea / Sea of Reeds (Yam Suph) (v.21): The site of the miraculous crossing. God divides the waters with a strong east wind.
- The wilderness on the eastern shore (v.29): Israel emerges on the Sinai side; Egypt is behind them.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.13 — "Fear not, stand firm, and see the salvation of the LORD, which he
        will work for you today." (ESV) — Faith in impossible circumstances.

v.14 — "The LORD will fight for you, and you have only to be silent." (ESV)
        — God fights; you rest.

v.15 — "Why do you cry to me? Tell the people of Israel to go forward."
        (ESV) — There's a time to pray and a time to move.

v.21 — "The LORD drove the sea back by a strong east wind all night and
        made the sea dry land, and the waters were divided." (ESV) —
        God's power over creation.

v.31 — "Israel saw the great power that the LORD used against the
        Egyptians, and the people feared the LORD, and they believed in the
        LORD and in his servant Moses." (ESV) — Seeing produces fearing and believing.

Charles Spurgeon:
  Sermon: "Stand Still" on v.13 (MTP Vol. 1, No. 16)
  "Stand still — these two words are among the hardest in the Christian
  language. We can march forward; we can retreat; we can fight. But STAND
  STILL? Do nothing? Wait? This is the hardest command. Yet it is often
  God's command. Stand still — not in despair but in faith. Stand still —
  not in idleness but in expectation. Stand still — and SEE. You cannot see
  God's salvation while you're running in panic. You cannot see it while
  you're scheming your own escape. Stand still. Be quiet. Watch. God is
  about to do something you've never seen before."

  Sermon: "The LORD Shall Fight for You" on v.14 (MTP Vol. 23, No. 1348)
  "The LORD shall fight for you, and ye shall hold your peace — your part is
  silence; God's part is warfare. Your part is rest; God's part is victory.
  You hold your peace — He fights. You are still — He moves. This is the
  gospel in miniature: we contribute nothing to our salvation except the sin
  that made it necessary. God does the fighting. God parts the sea. God
  destroys the enemy. We walk through on dry ground — and then we sing."

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.2  — Hebrew "Pi-hachiroth" — possibly "mouth of the gorges."
       KJV: "Pi-hahiroth"
       ESV: "Pi-hahiroth"
       ASV: "Pi-hahiroth"
       NET: "Pi-hahiroth"
       WEB: "Pi-hahiroth"
       Analysis: All transliterate. The exact location is debated, but the point
       is clear: God leads Israel to a place with no human escape route.

v.4  — Hebrew "ve'ikkavdah" — from "kavod" (glory/honor).
       KJV: "I will be honoured upon Pharaoh"
       ESV: "I will get glory over Pharaoh"
       ASV: "I will get me honor upon Pharaoh"
       NET: "I will gain honor because of Pharaoh"
       WEB: "I will get honor over Pharaoh"
       Analysis: ASV's reflexive "get me honor" is closest to the Hebrew hithpael
       form. God glorifies Himself through Pharaoh's defeat. NET's "because of"
       softens the preposition; KJV/ESV/WEB use "upon/over" preserving the force.

v.8  — Hebrew "beyad ramah" — with a raised/high hand.
       KJV: "with an high hand"
       ESV: "defiantly"
       ASV: "the children of Israel went out with a high hand"
       NET: "the Israelites were going out defiantly"
       WEB: "the children of Israel went out with a high hand"
       Analysis: ASV/WEB preserve the vivid Hebrew idiom of a raised hand — a bold,
       triumphant departure, not a cowering escape. ESV/NET interpret it as
       "defiantly" — capturing the meaning but losing the physical imagery.

v.13 — Hebrew "hityatzvu... yeshu'at YHWH" — take your stand... salvation of the LORD.
       KJV: "stand still, and see the salvation of the LORD"
       ESV: "stand firm, and see the salvation of the LORD"
       ASV: "stand still, and see the salvation of Jehovah"
       NET: "stand firm, and see the salvation of the Lord"
       WEB: "stand still, and see the salvation of Yahweh"
       Analysis: The ESV/NET "stand firm" emphasizes resolve; the KJV/ASV/WEB
       "stand still" emphasizes cessation of panic. Both capture active, deliberate
       positioning in faith. The divine name attached to "yeshua" (salvation) — the
       root of "Joshua" and "Jesus." Each translation renders the divine name
       characteristically.

v.14 — Hebrew "tacharishun" — to be silent/still.
       KJV: "ye shall hold your peace"
       ESV: "you have only to be silent"
       ASV: "ye shall hold your peace"
       NET: "The Lord will fight for you, and you can be still"
       WEB: "you shall be still"
       Analysis: NET's "can be still" suggests permission; KJV/ASV's "hold your
       peace" conveys commanded silence. Hebrew means to be silent/still — both
       restraint from panic and trust in God's action. ESV's "you have only to be
       silent" emphasizes the minimal requirement: do nothing but trust.

v.15 — Hebrew "veyissa'u" — break camp and march.
       KJV: "go forward"
       ESV: "go forward"
       ASV: "go forward"
       NET: "go forward"
       WEB: "go forward"
       Analysis: All identical. There's a time to stand still (v.13) and a time to
       move (v.15). Both require faith.

v.21 — Hebrew "yam suph" — the Sea of Reeds.
       KJV: "the Red sea"
       ESV: "the sea"
       ASV: "the sea"
       NET: "the sea"
       WEB: "the sea"
       Analysis: The KJV adds "Red" (following tradition); the ESV/ASV/NET/WEB
       simply say "the sea" here. Whether this is the modern Red Sea, the Gulf of
       Suez, or a marshy lake is debated — but the miracle is clear.

v.24 — Hebrew "ashmoret haboqer... vayaham" — the morning watch... and He confused/panicked them.
       KJV: "in the morning watch... troubled the host of the Egyptians"
       ESV: "in the morning watch... threw the Egyptian forces into a panic"
       ASV: "Jehovah looked forth upon the host of the Egyptians... and discomfited the host"
       NET: "the Lord looked down on the Egyptian army... and he threw the Egyptian army into a panic"
       WEB: "Yahweh looked out on the Egyptian army... and confused the Egyptian army"
       Analysis: Hebrew "vayaham" — to confuse, throw into panic, discomfit. NET's
       "threw into a panic" is most dynamic; ASV's archaic "discomfited" is precise
       but unfamiliar to modern readers; WEB's "confused" is neutral.

v.25 — Hebrew "vayasar" — removed, turned aside.
       KJV: "took off their chariot wheels"
       ESV: "clogged their chariot wheels"
       ASV: "he took off their chariot wheels"
       NET: "He jammed the wheels of their chariots"
       WEB: "He took off their chariot wheels"
       Analysis: KJV/ASV/WEB say "took off" (wheels removed); ESV says "clogged";
       NET interprets as "jammed." The Septuagint supports "bound/clogged." All
       convey the same result: God disabled Egypt's greatest military advantage.

v.27 — Hebrew "le'etano" — to its permanence/strength.
       KJV: "the sea returned to his strength"
       ESV: "the sea returned to its normal course"
       ASV: "the sea returned to its strength"
       NET: "the sea returned to its normal state"
       WEB: "the sea returned to its strength"
       Analysis: ASV/WEB/KJV preserve the personifying Hebrew image — the sea has
       "strength." ESV/NET neutralize to "normal course/state," losing the poetic
       force.

v.31 — Hebrew "hayad hagedolah" — the great hand.
       KJV: "that great work which the LORD did"
       ESV: "the great power that the LORD used"
       ASV: "Israel saw the great work which Jehovah did upon the Egyptians"
       NET: "the great power that the Lord had exercised over the Egyptians"
       WEB: "the great work which Yahweh did to the Egyptians"
       Analysis: The literal Hebrew is "the great hand" — connecting to the repeated
       "strong hand" theme throughout Exodus. NET/ESV interpret as "power";
       ASV/WEB/KJV render as "work." The "hand" imagery links God's redemptive acts
       throughout the Exodus narrative.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
"Stand still and see" — Moses' command of faith. Active waiting — not
               passive despair but deliberate trust that God will act.

The Red Sea (Yam Suph) — "Sea of Reeds." The body of water God parted for
               Israel's crossing. The defining miracle of the Old Testament.

"The LORD shall fight for you" — God as warrior. Israel's deliverance is
               entirely God's work — they contribute nothing but obedience.

Pillar repositioned — The cloud/fire moves from leading position to rear
               guard (v.19). God adapts His protection to the need: now He
               stands between Israel and the enemy.

"Dry ground" — The sea floor becomes walkable land. God doesn't just part
               the water — He prepares the path. Complete provision.

"Not so much as one" — Total destruction of Egypt's army (v.28). God's
               victory is complete — no remnant of the enemy survives.

Baptism typology — Paul reads the Red Sea crossing as baptism (1 Corinthians
               10:1-2). Passing through water = death to the old life
               (Egypt/slavery) and resurrection to the new (freedom/promise).

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Stand still, and see the salvation of the LORD" (v.13). When you're
   trapped — enemy behind, sea ahead — stop panicking and watch God work.
   Your impossible situation is God's opportunity for glory.

2. "The LORD shall fight for you, and ye shall hold your peace" (v.14). You
   don't have to fight every battle yourself. Some battles are God's to
   fight — your job is to be still and trust. Which battle do you need to
   hand over to Him?

3. "Speak unto the children of Israel, that they go forward" (v.15). There's
   a time to pray and a time to move. Faith isn't always waiting — sometimes
   it's stepping into the sea before it parts. What step of obedience is God
   calling you to take?

4. The pillar moved behind them (v.19). God positions Himself between you
   and your enemy. Whatever is pursuing you — God stands in the gap. You are
   protected.

5. "Israel saw the Egyptians dead upon the sea shore" (v.30). The enemies
   that terrified you yesterday will be dead on the shore tomorrow. God
   finishes what He starts. The enemy that chases you will not survive God's
   intervention.
