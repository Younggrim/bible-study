Exodus - Chapter 28
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
God designs the priestly garments for Aaron — the ephod, breastplate, robe, and turban — 'for glory and for beauty.'

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 28 describes the priestly garments — specifically
the high priest's garments "for glory and for beauty" (v.2). Aaron is
appointed as Israel's first high priest, with his sons as priests. The high
priest's garments are rich with symbolism: he carries Israel's names on his
shoulders (strength) and over his heart (love). The golden plate on his
forehead reads "HOLINESS TO THE LORD" (v.36). Every detail points to Christ,
our great High Priest (Hebrews 4:14): He carries us on His shoulders and
over His heart, He bears our iniquity, and He is holy to the Lord. The
garments include: the ephod, the breastplate of judgment (with twelve stones
and the Urim and Thummim), the blue robe with bells and pomegranates, the
linen coat, the turban with the gold plate, and the sash.

The Priestly Appointment (vv.1-5): Aaron and his sons are set apart for
priestly service (v.1). Holy garments "for glory and for beauty" (v.2).
Skilled craftsmen "filled with the spirit of wisdom" make them (v.3). The
garments listed: breastplate, ephod, robe, embroidered coat, turban, sash
(v.4).

The Ephod (vv.6-14): Gold, blue, purple, scarlet, fine linen (v.6). Two
shoulder pieces (v.7). Two onyx stones on the shoulders — six tribal names
on each (vv.9-10). "Aaron shall bear their names before the LORD upon his
two shoulders for a memorial" (v.12).

The Breastplate of Judgment (vv.15-30): Same materials as the ephod (v.15).
Square, doubled, a span by a span (v.16). Twelve precious stones in four
rows — each engraved with a tribal name (vv.17-21). Attached to the ephod
by gold chains and blue cord (vv.22-28). "Aaron shall bear the names of the
children of Israel in the breastplate of judgment upon his heart" (v.29).
The Urim and Thummim placed inside — for discerning God's will (v.30).

The Blue Robe (vv.31-35): All blue (v.31). Pomegranates and golden bells
alternating on the hem (vv.33-34). "His sound shall be heard when he goeth
in unto the holy place... that he die not" (v.35).

The Gold Plate (vv.36-38): Pure gold, engraved: "HOLINESS TO THE LORD"
(v.36). On the turban, on Aaron's forehead (v.37). "Aaron may bear the
iniquity of the holy things" (v.38) — even Israel's worship is imperfect;
the priest bears its guilt.

The Other Garments (vv.39-43): Embroidered coat, linen turban, sash (v.39).
Aaron's sons: coats, sashes, caps — "for glory and for beauty" (v.40).
Linen undergarments for modesty (v.42). "A statute for ever" (v.43).

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Israel remains encamped at Mount Sinai. The priestly garment instructions are given on the mountain for future worship in the Tabernacle.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/exodus-28/
  Guzik covers each garment as a type of Christ's priestly ministry — the
  names on the shoulders (strength), the names on the heart (love), the
  bells (intercession heard), and the gold plate (holiness).

Charles Spurgeon:
  Sermon: "The High Priest's Breastplate" on v.29 (MTP Vol. 13, No. 756)
  "Upon his HEART — not merely on his shoulders (that is strength) but on
  his HEART (that is love). Christ carries you not only by His power but by
  His affection. Your name is engraved — not written in ink that fades but
  engraved in stone that endures. And He carries you INTO the holy place —
  into God's very presence. When Christ enters heaven, He enters with YOUR
  name on His heart. You are never forgotten. You are never dropped. You are
  always before the LORD — because your High Priest never takes off His
  breastplate."

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.2  — Hebrew "lekhavod uletif'aret" — for honor/glory and for splendor/beauty.
       KJV: "for glory and for beauty"
       ESV: "for glory and for beauty"
       ASV: "for glory and for beauty"
       NET: "for glory and for beauty"
       WEB: "for glory and for beauty"
       Analysis: All translations are identical. The priestly garments are
       not merely functional — they reflect God's own glory and beauty.

v.4  — Hebrew "mitznephet... avnet" — turban/mitre... sash/girdle.
       KJV: "a mitre, and a girdle"
       ESV: "a turban, and a sash"
       ASV: "a mitre, and a girdle"
       NET: "a turban, and a sash"
       WEB: "a turban, and a sash"
       Analysis: KJV/ASV use archaic "mitre" (from Latin mitra) and "girdle";
       ESV/NET/WEB use modern "turban" and "sash." Also, ASV has "coat of
       checker work" while NET has "fitted tunic" — Hebrew "ketonet tashbetz"
       describes a woven/checkered linen garment. WEB/NET modernize all terms.

v.12 — Hebrew "lifnei YHWH" — before the LORD.
       KJV: "before the LORD"
       ESV: "before the LORD"
       ASV: "before Jehovah upon his two shoulders for a memorial"
       NET: "before the Lord on his two shoulders for a memorial"
       WEB: "before Yahweh on his two shoulders for a memorial"
       Analysis: The divine name rendered three ways in a deeply personal
       context: the priest carries Israel's names before the covenant God.
       ASV's "Jehovah" and WEB's "Yahweh" preserve the personal name; NET's
       "the Lord" follows convention but slightly obscures the intimate,
       covenantal tone.

v.15 — Hebrew "choshen mishpat" — breastplate/breastpiece of judgment.
       KJV: "breastplate of judgment"
       ESV: "breastpiece of judgment"
       ASV: "a breastplate of judgment"
       NET: "a breastpiece for use in making decisions"
       WEB: "a breastplate of judgment"
       Analysis: KJV/ASV/WEB "breastplate" suggests armor; ESV/NET
       "breastpiece" is more neutral. NET's "for use in making decisions" is
       highly interpretive, explaining the breastpiece's function (housing
       the Urim and Thummim for divine guidance). ASV/WEB retain the literal
       "of judgment." NET clarifies purpose but loses the broader Hebrew
       sense of "mishpat" (justice, judgment, legal decision).

v.30 — Hebrew "mishpat benei Yisrael" — the judgment of the children of Israel.
       KJV: "the judgment of the children of Israel"
       ESV: "the judgment of the people of Israel"
       ASV: "the judgment of the children of Israel"
       NET: "the decisions of the Israelites"
       WEB: "the judgment of the children of Israel"
       Analysis: NET renders "mishpat" as "decisions" (what the Urim and
       Thummim determined), narrowing the meaning to practical decision-
       making. KJV/ASV/WEB's "judgment" encompasses the broader concept of
       divine justice carried on the priest's heart.

v.32 — Hebrew "ke-phi tachra" — like the opening of a coat of mail.
       KJV: "habergeon"
       ESV: "garment"
       ASV: "as it were the hole of a coat of mail"
       NET: "like the opening of a collar"
       WEB: "as it were the hole of a coat of mail"
       Analysis: KJV's "habergeon" is extremely archaic (a type of armor).
       ESV simplifies to "garment." ASV/WEB preserve the military imagery
       (coat of mail/armor). NET domesticates it to "collar." The point: the
       robe's neckhole is reinforced so it won't tear. ASV/WEB preserve the
       striking image of armor, hinting at spiritual armor.

v.36 — Hebrew "qodesh la-YHWH" — Holy to the LORD.
       KJV: "HOLINESS TO THE LORD"
       ESV: "Holy to the LORD"
       ASV: "HOLY TO JEHOVAH"
       NET: "Holiness to the Lord"
       WEB: "HOLY TO YAHWEH"
       Analysis: This inscription on the gold plate reveals the divine name
       in each translation's preferred form. ASV/WEB use the personal name
       (Jehovah/Yahweh), making the inscription more intimate — the priest
       belongs to a named Person. NET's "the Lord" and "Holiness" (vs.
       "Holy") slightly abstracts the relationship. KJV capitalizes the
       entire phrase for emphasis.

v.38 — Hebrew "nasa et avon haqqodashim" — bear the iniquity of the holy things.
       KJV: "bear the iniquity of the holy things"
       ESV: "bear any guilt from the holy things"
       ASV: "bear the iniquity of the holy things"
       NET: "bear the guilt of the holy things"
       WEB: "bear the iniquity of the holy things"
       Analysis: Even Israel's worship is imperfect — tainted by sin. The
       high priest bears this guilt so that the offerings are "accepted
       before the LORD." All convey the same profound truth: even our best
       worship needs atonement.

v.41 — Hebrew "mashach... mille et yad... qiddesh" — anoint... fill the
       hand (ordain)... sanctify.
       KJV: "anoint them, and consecrate them, and sanctify them"
       ESV: "anoint them and ordain them and consecrate them"
       ASV: "anoint them, and consecrate them, and sanctify them"
       NET: "anoint them and ordain them and set them apart as holy"
       WEB: "anoint them, and consecrate them, and sanctify them"
       Analysis: NET unpacks the Hebrew into plain language: "ordain" for
       "consecrate" (literally "fill their hands") and "set them apart as
       holy" for "sanctify." NET explains; ASV/WEB preserve the traditional
       theological vocabulary. The three actions: anointing (oil), ordination
       (authority), sanctification (holiness).

v.43 — Hebrew "chuqqat olam lo ulezar'o acharav" — a statute forever to him
       and to his seed after him.
       KJV: "a statute for ever unto him and his seed after him"
       ESV: "a statute forever for him and for his offspring after him"
       ASV: "a statute for ever unto him and unto his seed after him"
       NET: "a perpetual ordinance"
       WEB: "a statute forever to him and to his offspring after him"
       Analysis: NET's compact "perpetual ordinance" compresses what ASV/WEB
       express fully — the statute extends not only to Aaron but to all his
       descendants. ASV's "seed" and WEB's "offspring" (Hebrew "zera")
       emphasize the generational nature of priestly succession.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
High Priest    — Israel's chief mediator between God and the people. A type
               of Christ, our great High Priest (Hebrews 4:14). He alone
               enters the Most Holy Place; he carries the people's names
               and bears their guilt.

Ephod          — A vest-like garment of the high priest. Gold, blue, purple,
               scarlet, fine linen. The shoulder stones carry Israel's names
               — the priest bears his people by strength.

Breastplate of Judgment — Twelve stones over the heart, each engraved with
               a tribal name. The priest carries his people by love. Contains
               the Urim and Thummim for divine guidance.

Urim and Thummim — "Lights and Perfections." Sacred objects for discerning
               God's will. Their exact nature is unknown — possibly sacred
               lots giving yes/no answers from God.

"HOLINESS TO THE LORD" — The gold plate inscription on the high priest's
               forehead. His defining identity. Christ is "holy, harmless,
               undefiled, separate from sinners" (Hebrews 7:26).

Bells and pomegranates — Alternating on the robe's hem. Bells: the priest's
               sound is heard (intercession). Pomegranates: fruitfulness.
               The priest's ministry produces both sound (prayer) and fruit.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Upon his two shoulders... upon his heart" (vv.12, 29). Christ carries
   you by His strength (shoulders) and by His love (heart). You are never
   forgotten, never dropped. Your name is engraved — permanent, personal,
   precious.

2. "HOLINESS TO THE LORD" (v.36). This was the priest's identity — and it's
   yours. You are set apart for God. Does your life read "HOLINESS TO THE
   LORD" — or something else?

3. "Aaron may bear the iniquity of the holy things" (v.38). Even your
   worship is imperfect. Even your prayers are tainted. But Christ bears
   that guilt — making your imperfect offerings acceptable to God. Don't
   let perfectionism keep you from worshipping.

4. "For glory and for beauty" (v.2). God cares about beauty — not just
   function. Worship should be beautiful, not merely correct. Does your
   worship reflect God's glory and beauty?

5. The bells sounded so the priest would "not die" (v.35). Approaching God
   is serious — even dangerous without proper preparation. Do you approach
   God casually or with reverent awareness of His holiness? Thank Christ
   that He has made the way safe for you.
