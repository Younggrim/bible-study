Exodus - Chapter 15
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Moses and Israel sing a victory song celebrating God's triumph over Egypt; Miriam leads the women in worship; God provides water at Marah.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 15 contains the Song of Moses (vv.1-18) — the
first recorded song in Scripture and one of the oldest pieces of Hebrew
poetry. Sung on the eastern shore of the Red Sea with Egypt's army destroyed,
it celebrates God as warrior, redeemer, and king. Miriam leads the women in
responsive worship (vv.20-21). But the chapter also records Israel's first
complaint in the wilderness — only three days after the greatest miracle in
their history (vv.22-24). At Marah, bitter water is made sweet by a tree
God shows Moses (v.25) — a picture of the cross transforming bitterness.
God reveals Himself as "the LORD that healeth thee" — Jehovah Rapha (v.26).

The Song of Moses (vv.1-18): Structured in three movements:
  (1) Praise for victory (vv.1-12): "The LORD is my strength and song, and
  he is become my salvation" (v.2). "The LORD is a man of war" (v.3).
  Pharaoh's army destroyed — "they sank into the bottom as a stone" (v.5),
  "they sank as lead" (v.10). "Who is like unto thee, O LORD, among the
  gods?" (v.11).
  (2) God's guidance to the Promised Land (vv.13-16): Nations will hear and
  tremble — Philistia, Edom, Moab, Canaan (vv.14-16). "Till thy people pass
  over, which thou hast purchased" (v.16).
  (3) God's eternal reign (vv.17-18): "Thou shalt bring them in, and plant
  them" (v.17). "The LORD shall reign for ever and ever" (v.18).

Miriam's Song (vv.19-21): Miriam the prophetess leads the women with
timbrels and dancing (v.20). Her refrain echoes Moses: "Sing ye to the LORD,
for he hath triumphed gloriously" (v.21).

Marah: Bitter Water Made Sweet (vv.22-27): Three days without water (v.22).
At Marah, the water is bitter — undrinkable (v.23). The people murmur (v.24).
Moses cries to God; God shows him a tree — cast into the water, it becomes
sweet (v.25). God gives a conditional promise: obey, and "I will put none of
these diseases upon thee... for I am the LORD that healeth thee" (v.26). They
arrive at Elim: twelve wells and seventy palm trees (v.27) — abundance after
testing.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- The eastern shore of the Red Sea: Israel sings the victory song on the far side of the crossing.
- Marah (v.23): "Bitter" — three days into the Wilderness of Shur, they find water but it is undrinkable. God makes it sweet.
- Elim (v.27): An oasis with twelve wells and seventy palm trees — provision and rest after testing.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/exodus-15/
  Guzik covers the Song of Moses as Israel's first worship, the rapid
  transition from praise to complaint, and the tree at Marah as a type of
  the cross.

Charles Spurgeon:
  Sermon: "Jehovah Rapha" on v.26 (MTP Vol. 24, No. 1443)
  "I am the LORD that HEALETH thee — not merely 'that heals thy body' but
  'that heals THEE.' Body, soul, spirit — relationships, memories, wounds.
  God is the comprehensive Healer. And how does He heal? At Marah, He heals
  bitter water with a TREE. The tree cast into the bitter waters makes them
  sweet. Christian, do you see it? The cross — that tree on which Christ
  hung — cast into the bitter waters of your life, makes them sweet. Every
  bitterness is transformed by the cross. Every grief is sweetened by
  Calvary. The tree heals the water."

  Sermon: "The Song of Moses" on v.1 (MTP Vol. 4, No. 195)
  "THEN sang Moses — not before. Not while the sea was parting. Not while
  they were walking through. AFTER. After the victory was complete. After
  the enemy was dead. After the salvation was finished. We sing best when
  we look back at completed deliverances. And one day we will sing the song
  of Moses AND the Lamb (Revelation 15:3) — looking back at the completed
  work of redemption from the shores of eternity."

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1  — Hebrew "ga'oh ga'ah" — exalted exceedingly, risen in majesty.
       KJV: "triumphed gloriously"
       ESV: "triumphed gloriously"
       ASV: "triumphed gloriously"
       NET: "triumphed gloriously"
       WEB: "triumphed gloriously"
       Analysis: All translations identical. An emphatic doubling: God has supremely
       triumphed. The first recorded song in Scripture opens with this declaration.

v.2  — Hebrew "vayehi li lishu'ah" — He has become to me salvation.
       KJV: "he is become my salvation"
       ESV: "he has become my salvation"
       ASV: "Jehovah is my strength and song"
       NET: "The Lord is my strength and my song"
       WEB: "Yah is my strength and song"
       Analysis: WEB uniquely uses the abbreviated divine name "Yah" (Hebrew "Yah")
       — the shortened poetic form found in "Hallelujah." ASV uses "Jehovah," NET
       uses "the Lord." Not abstract theology but personal experience — God has
       become salvation to the individual.

v.3  — Hebrew "ish milchamah" — a warrior, a man of battle.
       KJV: "a man of war"
       ESV: "a man of war"
       ASV: "a man of war"
       NET: "The Lord is a warrior"
       WEB: "a man of war"
       Analysis: NET modernizes to "warrior," avoiding the literal but potentially
       misleading "man of war." The Hebrew idiom attributes personal combat to God;
       NET preserves the meaning while updating the phrasing. KJV/ESV/ASV/WEB
       retain the vivid anthropomorphism.

v.8  — Hebrew "qaph'u" — congealed, thickened, solidified.
       KJV: "the depths were congealed in the heart of the sea"
       ESV: "the deeps congealed in the heart of the sea"
       ASV: "The deeps were congealed in the heart of the sea"
       NET: "the deep waters were solidified in the heart of the sea"
       WEB: "The deeps were congealed in the heart of the sea"
       Analysis: NET's "solidified" and KJV/ESV/ASV/WEB's "congealed" both capture
       the miraculous stiffening of water into walls. The poetic imagery conveys
       liquid becoming solid — supernatural transformation.

v.11 — Hebrew "nora tehillot" — awesome/terrifying in praiseworthy deeds.
       KJV: "fearful in praises"
       ESV: "awesome in glorious deeds"
       ASV: "fearful in praises"
       NET: "fearful in praises"
       WEB: "fearful in praises"
       Analysis: The ESV interprets "tehillot" as "glorious deeds"; KJV/ASV/NET/WEB
       keep "praises." Both are valid: God's acts inspire praise AND holy fear. The
       ESV takes a more interpretive approach while others preserve the ambiguity.

v.13 — Hebrew "chesed" — covenant love, loyal love.
       KJV: "thou in thy mercy hast led the people"
       ESV: "you have led in your steadfast love"
       ASV: "Thou in thy lovingkindness hast led the people"
       NET: "By your loyal love you will lead the people"
       WEB: "in your loving kindness, have led"
       Analysis: Hebrew "chesed" rendered multiple ways: ASV's "lovingkindness"
       (classic), WEB's "loving kindness," NET's "loyal love" (emphasizing covenant
       faithfulness), ESV's "steadfast love," KJV's "mercy." All capture aspects of
       this rich Hebrew term.

v.20 — Hebrew "toph" — a small frame drum.
       KJV: "a timbrel"
       ESV: "a tambourine"
       ASV: "a timbrel"
       NET: "a hand drum"
       WEB: "a tambourine"
       Analysis: NET's "hand drum" is most precise; WEB/ESV's "tambourine" is modern
       but adds jingles not in the original; ASV/KJV's "timbrel" is archaic but
       accurate. Miriam is called "prophetess" — the first woman given this title.

v.25 — Hebrew "vayorehu YHWH etz... vayimtequ hamayim" — the LORD showed him a tree... and the waters became sweet.
       KJV: "shewed him a tree... the waters were made sweet"
       ESV: "showed him a log... the water became sweet"
       ASV: "showed him a tree... the waters were made sweet"
       NET: "showed him a piece of wood... the water became safe to drink"
       WEB: "showed him a tree... the waters were made sweet"
       Analysis: The ESV's "log" is more specific; KJV/ASV/WEB's "tree" is broader.
       NET interprets functionally ("safe to drink") rather than literally ("sweet"),
       losing the contrast with "Marah" (bitter). "Yorah" is the root of "Torah"
       (teaching). God teaches through the tree — a type of the cross transforming
       bitterness.

v.26 — Hebrew "ani YHWH rophekha" — I am YHWH your healer.
       KJV: "I am the LORD that healeth thee"
       ESV: "I am the LORD, your healer"
       ASV: "I am Jehovah that healeth thee"
       NET: "I, the Lord, am your healer"
       WEB: "I am Yahweh who heals you"
       Analysis: This reveals the divine name Jehovah Rapha — God the Healer. The
       ESV/NET make "healer" a title; the KJV/ASV use a relative clause; WEB uses
       the participial form most literally. He heals body, soul, and spirit — and
       He heals bitterness through the "tree" (the cross).

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Song of Moses  — The first recorded song in Scripture. Celebrates God as
               warrior, redeemer, and eternal king. Sung again in eternity
               (Revelation 15:3).

"Man of war"   — God as warrior who fights for His people. Not a metaphor
               but a declaration: God personally engages in battle against
               His enemies.

Jehovah Rapha  — "The LORD who heals." God's compound name revealed at
               Marah. He heals body, soul, and spirit — and He heals
               bitterness through the "tree" (the cross).

Marah          — "Bitter." The first testing place after the Red Sea. Three
               days from miracle to murmuring. The tree makes bitter water
               sweet — the cross transforms bitterness.

The tree       — God shows Moses a specific tree to cast into the bitter
               water. A type of the cross: Christ's sacrifice transforms
               the bitterness of life into sweetness.

Elim           — "Trees/oaks." Twelve wells (one per tribe) and seventy
               palm trees (one per elder). God's abundant provision after
               the test at Marah.

Miriam         — Moses' sister, called "prophetess." She watched over baby
               Moses (2:4), now leads worship after the Red Sea. The first
               woman called a prophet in Scripture.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Then sang Moses" (v.1). Worship follows deliverance. When God delivers
   you — do you stop to praise? Or do you rush to the next problem? Take
   time to sing after your Red Sea moments.

2. "Who is like unto thee, O LORD?" (v.11). No one. Nothing. No problem, no
   enemy, no circumstance compares to God. When you're overwhelmed, ask this
   question — and let the answer settle your heart.

3. Three days from the Red Sea to murmuring (vv.22-24). How quickly we
   forget God's faithfulness. Yesterday's miracle doesn't prevent today's
   complaint. Guard your heart against short-term memory.

4. God showed Moses a tree — and the bitter water became sweet (v.25). The
   cross transforms bitterness. Whatever bitter circumstance you're facing
   — bring it to the cross. Let Christ's sacrifice change its taste.

5. "I am the LORD that healeth thee" (v.26). God is your healer — not just
   physically but comprehensively. Bring your wounds, your bitterness, your
   brokenness to Jehovah Rapha. He heals.
