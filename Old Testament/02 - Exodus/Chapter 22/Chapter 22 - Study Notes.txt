Exodus - Chapter 22
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Laws concerning theft, property damage, social responsibility, and moral purity — God's standard for a just community.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 22 continues the Book of the Covenant with laws
covering property crimes, social responsibility, and moral/religious duties.
The chapter moves from theft and restitution (vv.1-15) to sexual ethics and
capital religious offenses (vv.16-20) to the protection of the vulnerable
(vv.21-27) and duties to God (vv.28-31). These laws reveal God's character:
He cares about property rights, sexual purity, the vulnerable (strangers,
widows, orphans), and economic justice (no usury against the poor). The
chapter ends with the call to holiness: "ye shall be holy men unto me" (v.31).

Laws of Theft and Restitution (vv.1-6): Stealing an ox: restore five-fold;
a sheep: four-fold (v.1). A thief killed during a nighttime break-in: no
guilt; killed in daylight: guilt (vv.2-3). Stolen animal found alive: double
restitution (v.4). Damage to fields by livestock or fire: full restitution
from the best of one's own property (vv.5-6).

Laws of Trust and Custody (vv.7-15): Property entrusted and stolen: the thief
pays double; if no thief found, the custodian swears before God (vv.7-9).
Animals in custody that die or are injured: an oath settles it (vv.10-11).
If stolen: the custodian pays (v.12). If torn by beasts: evidence suffices
(v.13). Borrowed items damaged: full restitution unless the owner was present
(vv.14-15).

Sexual and Religious Laws (vv.16-20): Seduction of an unbetrothed virgin:
the man must pay the bride-price and marry her (vv.16-17). Three capital
offenses: sorcery (v.18), bestiality (v.19), and sacrificing to other gods
(v.20).

Protection of the Vulnerable (vv.21-27): Do not oppress strangers — "ye were
strangers in the land of Egypt" (v.21). Do not afflict widows or orphans —
God will hear their cry and His wrath will burn (vv.22-24). No usury/interest
on loans to the poor (v.25). Return a poor man's cloak by sunset — "for I am
gracious" (vv.26-27).

Duties to God (vv.28-31): Do not revile God or curse rulers (v.28). Do not
delay firstfruits or firstborn offerings (vv.29-30). "Ye shall be holy men
unto me" — do not eat flesh torn by beasts (v.31).

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Israel remains encamped at Mount Sinai. These laws continue the Book of the Covenant.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.21 — "You shall not wrong a sojourner or oppress him, for you were
        sojourners in the land of Egypt." (ESV) — Empathy-based ethics.

v.22-23 — "You shall not mistreat any widow or fatherless child. If you do
        mistreat them, and they cry out to me, I will surely hear their
        cry." (ESV) — God as defender of the vulnerable.

v.25 — "If you lend money to any of my people with you who is poor, you
        shall not be like a moneylender to him." (ESV) — Economic justice.

v.27 — "I will hear, for I am compassionate." (ESV) — God's character: compassionate.

v.31 — "You shall be consecrated to me." (ESV) — The call to holiness.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1  — KJV: "restore five oxen for an ox, and four sheep for a sheep" /
       ESV: "repay five oxen for an ox, and four sheep for a sheep." The
       Hebrew "yeshallem" means to make complete, to repay. Why five for an
       ox but four for a sheep? An ox is a working animal (greater economic
       loss); a sheep is not. Restitution is proportional to damage.

v.8  — KJV: "the judges" / ESV: "God" — Hebrew "ha'elohim." Same
       translation issue as 21:6. The KJV reads "judges" (God's
       representatives in court); the ESV reads "God" (the case is brought
       before God Himself). The ESV interpretation emphasizes that all
       justice ultimately comes before God.

v.18 — KJV: "Thou shalt not suffer a witch to live" / ESV: "You shall not
       permit a sorceress to live." Hebrew "mekhashshephah" — a female
       practitioner of sorcery/harmful magic. The KJV "witch" carries
       medieval connotations; the ESV "sorceress" is more precise. The
       feminine form is used because women were more commonly associated
       with this practice in the ancient Near East, but the prohibition
       applies to both genders (Deuteronomy 18:10-12).

v.20 — KJV: "utterly destroyed" / ESV: "devoted to destruction" — Hebrew
       "yocharam" — placed under the "cherem" (ban of total destruction).
       The ESV's "devoted to destruction" captures the technical meaning of
       "cherem" — something irrevocably given over to God's judgment.

v.21 — KJV: "stranger" / ESV: "sojourner" — Hebrew "ger" — a resident
       alien, a foreigner living among Israel. The ESV's "sojourner" is more
       precise than the KJV's "stranger" (which could mean anyone unknown).
       A "ger" has chosen to live in Israel's community.

v.25 — KJV: "usurer... usury" / ESV: "moneylender... interest" — Hebrew
       "nosheh... neshekh." "Neshekh" literally means "a bite" — interest
       that bites/devours the poor. The ESV's "interest" is clearer to
       modern readers; the KJV's "usury" originally meant any interest (not
       just excessive interest as in modern usage).

v.27 — KJV: "I am gracious" / ESV: "I am compassionate" — Hebrew "channun"
       — gracious, compassionate, showing favor. Both translations are valid.
       The KJV emphasizes God's unmerited favor; the ESV emphasizes His
       emotional compassion. The Hebrew encompasses both.

v.31 — KJV: "ye shall be holy men unto me" / ESV: "You shall be consecrated
       to me" — Hebrew "anshei qodesh tihyun li" — men of holiness you
       shall be to me. The KJV preserves "holy"; the ESV uses "consecrated"
       (set apart). Both capture the idea: Israel is to be distinct,
       separated, belonging to God.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Restitution    — Making the victim whole. Biblical justice is restorative,
               not merely punitive. The thief doesn't just go to prison —
               he restores what was taken, with increase.

"Five for an ox, four for a sheep" — Proportional restitution based on
               economic impact. Greater loss = greater restoration.

Cherem (ban)   — Total devotion to destruction. Applied to idolaters and
               their possessions. Nothing is salvageable — complete judgment.

"For ye were strangers" — Empathy-based ethics. Israel's own experience of
               oppression in Egypt should produce compassion for the
               vulnerable. Memory of suffering should create mercy.

Usury/Interest (neshekh) — Literally "a bite." Charging interest on loans
               to the poor. Forbidden because it exploits vulnerability.
               Loans to the poor are acts of mercy, not business.

"I am gracious" — God's self-description as the motivation for justice.
               We protect the vulnerable because GOD is compassionate —
               and we are to reflect His character.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Ye were strangers in the land of Egypt" (v.21). Your own experience of
   suffering should produce compassion for others who suffer. Does it? Or
   has your pain made you hard rather than tender?

2. "If thou afflict them... I will surely hear their cry" (vv.22-23). God
   hears the cry of the vulnerable. Are you on the right side of this
   equation — protecting the vulnerable or exploiting them?

3. "Thou shalt not be to him as an usurer" (v.25). God forbids profiting
   from the poor's desperation. How do you treat those in financial need?
   With generosity or with exploitation?

4. "I am gracious" (v.27). God's compassion is the foundation of social
   ethics. We are kind to others because God is kind to us. Is your
   treatment of others reflecting God's character?

5. "Ye shall be holy men unto me" (v.31). Holiness is the goal — set apart,
   distinct, belonging to God. Not just in worship but in business, in
   relationships, in how you treat the poor. Holiness is comprehensive.
