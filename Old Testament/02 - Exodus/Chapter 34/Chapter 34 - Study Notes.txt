Exodus - Chapter 34
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
God renews the covenant, gives Moses new tablets, and proclaims His name — merciful, gracious, slow to anger; Moses' face shines from God's presence.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Exodus 34 records the renewal of the covenant after the
golden calf disaster. God commands Moses to cut two new tablets of stone to
replace those broken in chapter 32, and Moses ascends Sinai again for another
forty days. This chapter contains one of the most important theological
passages in the entire Old Testament: God's self-revelation of His character
(vv.6–7), often called "the Gospel of the Old Testament."

The proclamation of God's name in verses 6–7 — "The LORD, The LORD God,
merciful and gracious, longsuffering, and abundant in goodness and truth" — is
the most frequently quoted passage within the Old Testament itself. It becomes
the foundation for Israel's understanding of who God is, echoed in Numbers
14:18, Nehemiah 9:17, Psalms 86:15, 103:8, 145:8, Joel 2:13, Jonah 4:2, and
Nahum 1:3. This is God's definitive self-description.

The chapter also records the covenant renewal with its stipulations (vv.10–26),
Moses' second forty-day fast (v.28), and the remarkable detail that Moses' face
shone with reflected glory when he descended (vv.29–35). The shining face
becomes a visible sign that Moses has been in God's presence — and Paul later
uses this image in 2 Corinthians 3 to contrast the fading glory of the old
covenant with the surpassing glory of the new.

Section Summaries:
vv.1–4 — New tablets cut; Moses ascends Sinai alone.
vv.5–9 — God proclaims His name and character; Moses worships.
vv.10–26 — Covenant renewed with stipulations: no idolatry, feasts, firstborn.
vv.27–28 — Moses writes; forty days without food or water.
vv.29–35 — Moses' face shines; he veils himself before the people.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Mount Sinai: Moses ascends again with new stone tablets (v.4). He remains 40 days and 40 nights a second time (v.28).
- The covenant is renewed at the same mountain where it was first given and then broken.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.6 — "The LORD, the LORD, a God merciful and gracious, slow to anger, and
 abounding in steadfast love and faithfulness." (ESV) — God's
 definitive self-revelation; the most quoted OT verse within the OT itself.

v.7 — "Keeping steadfast love for thousands, forgiving iniquity and
 transgression and sin, but who will by no means clear the guilty."
 (ESV) — Mercy and justice held together without contradiction.

v.9 — "Please pardon our iniquity and our sin, and take us for your
 inheritance." (ESV) — Moses' response: worship, confession, and petition.

v.14 — "The LORD, whose name is Jealous, is a jealous God." (ESV) — God's
 jealousy is not petty envy but the righteous passion of a faithful
 husband for His bride's exclusive devotion.

v.29 — "Moses did not know that the skin of his face shone because he had
 been talking with God." (ESV) — Proximity to God's glory transforms;
 Moses was unaware of his own radiance.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/exodus-34/
  Guzik emphasizes vv.6-7 as the theological center of the OT — God's
  self-description that balances mercy and justice. He notes Moses' shining

Charles Spurgeon:
  Sermon: "The LORD's Name Proclaimed" on vv.6-7 (MTP Vol. 30, No. 1803)
  "Here is the gospel before the gospel. Merciful — He feels for our misery.
  Gracious — He acts for our relief. Longsuffering — He bears with our
  provocations. Abundant in goodness — His mercy overflows. Abundant in
  truth — His promises never fail. Forgiving iniquity, transgression, and
  sin — every shade and degree of evil is covered. Yet He will by no means
  clear the guilty — there is no cheap grace. The cross alone reconciles
  these two truths: mercy and justice met at Calvary."
  Sermon: "The Veil of Moses" on vv.33-35 (MTP Vol. 15, No. 886)
  "Moses veiled his face because the glory was fading. He did not want Israel
  to see the end of that which was passing away. But we, with unveiled face,
  beholding the glory of the Lord, are changed into the same image. Our glory
  does not fade — it increases, from glory to glory."

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1  — Hebrew "pesal-leka" — to cut/carve stone.
       KJV: "Hew thee two tables of stone"
       ESV: "Cut for yourself two tablets of stone"
       ASV: "Hew thee two tables of stone"
       NET: "Cut out two tablets of stone"
       WEB: "Chisel two stone tablets"
       Analysis: ASV/KJV retain "hew" (archaic); WEB uses "chisel" (most precise craft terminology for stonecutting); NET uses "cut out"; ESV uses "cut." The verb "pasal" means to cut/carve stone — WEB's "chisel" is the most specific English equivalent.

v.5  — Hebrew "YHWH" — God proclaims His own name.
       KJV: "proclaimed the name of the LORD"
       ESV: "proclaimed the name of the LORD"
       ASV: "proclaimed the name of Jehovah"
       NET: "proclaimed the Lord by name"
       WEB: "proclaimed Yahweh's name"
       Analysis: The divine name in the theophany itself. ASV/WEB make clear that YHWH proclaimed His own covenant name; NET's "the Lord by name" is grammatically different — it could mean proclaiming the title "Lord" or proclaiming God's personal name. In context, God is revealing His character by speaking His own name aloud before Moses.

v.6  — Hebrew "ʼerek ʼappayim verav-chesed veʼemet" — the great divine proclamation.
       KJV: "longsuffering, and abundant in goodness and truth"
       ESV: "slow to anger, and abounding in steadfast love and faithfulness"
       ASV: "slow to anger, and abundant in lovingkindness and truth"
       NET: "slow to anger, and abounding in loyal love and faithfulness"
       WEB: "slow to anger, and abundant in loving kindness and truth"
       Analysis: The most frequently quoted OT passage within the OT itself. The doubled divine name (YHWH YHWH) is rendered "the LORD, the LORD" (KJV/ESV), "Jehovah, Jehovah" (ASV), "Yahweh! Yahweh" (WEB), "The Lord, the Lord" (NET). Hebrew "rachum" = merciful/compassionate; NET's "compassionate" is a legitimate alternative. The key divergence: "chesed" — KJV "goodness," ESV "steadfast love," ASV "lovingkindness," WEB "loving kindness," NET "loyal love." NET's "loyal love" emphasizes covenantal obligation; ASV's classic "lovingkindness" has become a beloved theological term; ESV's "steadfast love" captures the covenantal nature.

v.7  — Hebrew "naqeh lo yenaqqeh" — emphatic double negative.
       KJV: "that will by no means clear the guilty"
       ESV: "who will by no means clear the guilty"
       ASV: "that will by no means clear the guilty"
       NET: "But he by no means leaves the guilty unpunished"
       WEB: "who will by no means clear the guilty"
       Analysis: An emphatic double negative: "clearing, He will NOT clear." Absolute certainty that guilt will be addressed. WEB uniquely renders Hebrew "peshaʻ" as "disobedience" rather than "transgression" (ASV/NET). NET paraphrases as "leaves the guilty unpunished" — more accessible but losing the emphatic Hebrew double construction.

v.9  — Hebrew "qesheh-ʻoreph" — stiff-necked.
       KJV: "for it is a stiffnecked people"
       ESV: "for it is a stiff-necked people"
       ASV: "for it is a stiffnecked people"
       NET: "for we are a stiff-necked people"
       WEB: "although this is a stiff-necked people"
       Analysis: A significant difference: KJV/ESV/ASV's "it is" and WEB's "this is" keep Moses at a distance; NET's "we are" makes Moses identify with the people's stubbornness — a humbler posture. The Hebrew "hu" (it/he) is ambiguous and most translations follow "it is" rather than NET's inclusive "we are."

v.10 — Hebrew "noraʼ" — fearsome, awe-inspiring.
       KJV: "it is a terrible thing that I will do with thee"
       ESV: "it is an awesome thing that I will do with you"
       ASV: "it is a terrible thing that I do with thee"
       NET: "it is a fearful thing that I am doing with you"
       WEB: "it is an awesome thing that I do with you"
       Analysis: KJV/ASV "terrible" meant "inspiring terror/awe" (not "bad"). ESV/WEB "awesome" captures the original sense for modern readers. NET's "fearful" lands between them. All attempt to convey holy dread at God's mighty acts.

v.13 — Hebrew "ʼasherim" — wooden poles sacred to Canaanite goddess Asherah.
       KJV: "groves"
       ESV: "Asherim"
       ASV: "their Asherim"
       NET: "their Asherah poles"
       WEB: "their Asherah poles"
       Analysis: KJV translates generically as "groves"; ESV/ASV transliterate the proper noun; NET/WEB add "poles" to clarify the physical object. These were cultic installations, not merely natural trees.

v.14 — Hebrew "Qannaʼ" — jealous, zealous (as a divine name).
       KJV: "the LORD, whose name is Jealous, is a jealous God"
       ESV: "the LORD, whose name is Jealous, is a jealous God"
       ASV: "for Jehovah, whose name is Jealous, is a jealous God"
       NET: "for the Lord, whose name is Jealous, is a jealous God"
       WEB: "for Yahweh, whose name is Jealous, is a jealous God"
       Analysis: All five capitalize "Jealous" as a divine name (not merely an attribute). ASV/WEB make the covenant name explicit alongside "Jealous" — Jehovah/Yahweh whose name IS Jealous — a double identification of God's passionate, exclusive devotion. God's jealousy is holy, protective, and covenantal.

v.29 — Hebrew "qaran" — to send out rays, to shine, to have horns (of light).
       KJV: "the skin of his face shone"
       ESV: "the skin of his face shone"
       ASV: "Moses knew not that the skin of his face shone by reason of his speaking with him"
       NET: "Moses did not know that the skin of his face shone while he talked with him"
       WEB: "Moses didn't know that the skin of his face shone by reason of his speaking with him"
       Analysis: The Vulgate translated "qaran" as "horned," leading to artistic depictions of Moses with horns. The correct meaning is "radiated light." ASV/WEB's "by reason of his speaking" emphasizes causation (glory resulted from communion); NET's "while he talked" merely notes timing. The Hebrew "bedabbero" supports both readings.

v.33 — Hebrew "masveh" — a covering, mask.
       KJV: "Moses put a vail on his face"
       ESV: "Moses put a veil over his face"
       ASV: "Moses put a veil upon his face"
       NET: "Moses would put a veil over his face"
       WEB: "Moses put a veil on his face"
       Analysis: Used only here and in vv.34-35 in the OT. KJV spells "vail" (archaic); ESV/NET/WEB use "veil." Moses covered the fading glory — Paul interprets this typologically in 2 Corinthians 3. NET's "would put" reflects the habitual aspect.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Merciful — Hebrew "rachum" — compassionate, from "rechem" (womb). God's
 tender, maternal compassion toward His people.

Gracious — Hebrew "channun" — disposed to show favor freely, without
 obligation. Grace given to the undeserving.

Longsuffering — Hebrew "ʼerek ʼappayim" — literally "long of nostrils."
 Slow to anger; patient before judgment. God's anger is slow to
 ignite and quick to relent.

Chesed — Steadfast love, covenant loyalty, lovingkindness. The most
 important word for God's love in the OT. Faithful, enduring,
 covenantal commitment.

Jealous — Hebrew "qannaʼ" — zealous, passionate for exclusive devotion.
 Not petty envy but the righteous passion of a faithful covenant
 partner who will not share His beloved with rivals.

Veil — A covering Moses placed over his face to hide the fading glory.
 Paul uses it as a metaphor for spiritual blindness removed in
 Christ (2 Cor 3:14-16).

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. God's self-description (vv.6-7) balances mercy and justice. Which aspect
 do you tend to emphasize — God's love or God's holiness? How does holding
 both together change your view of Him?

2. Moses' face shone and he "wist not" — he was unaware (v.29). True
 spiritual transformation is often invisible to the one being transformed
 but visible to others. Are others seeing Christ's glory reflected in you?

3. "The LORD, whose name is Jealous" (v.14). God's jealousy is for your
 exclusive devotion. What rivals compete with God for first place in your
 heart? How does knowing God is jealous for you affect your worship?

4. Moses spent forty days in God's presence without food or water (v.28).
 What sustained him? What does this teach about the sufficiency of God's
 presence? How do you prioritize extended time with God?

5. The covenant is renewed after catastrophic failure (vv.10-27). What does
 this teach about God's willingness to restore after sin? How does this
 encourage you in areas where you have failed?
