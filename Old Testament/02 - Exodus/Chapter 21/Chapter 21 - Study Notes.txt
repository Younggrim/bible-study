Exodus - Chapter 21
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
God gives laws governing servants, personal injury, and property rights — justice that protects the vulnerable and values human life.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 21 begins the "Book of the Covenant" (21:1-23:33)
— the detailed case law that applies the Ten Commandments to daily life in
ancient Israel. These are "judgments" (mishpatim) — legal rulings for
specific situations. Chapter 21 covers: (1) Laws regarding Hebrew servants
(vv.1-11); (2) Capital offenses (vv.12-17); (3) Laws of personal injury
(vv.18-27); (4) Laws regarding dangerous animals and property (vv.28-36).
These laws were revolutionary for the ancient Near East — protecting slaves,
women, and the vulnerable in ways no other ancient code did. They establish
the principle of proportional justice ("eye for eye") which LIMITS vengeance
rather than encouraging it.

Laws Regarding Servants (vv.1-11): Hebrew servants serve six years, go free
in the seventh (v.2). If a servant loves his master and chooses to stay
permanently, his ear is pierced at the doorpost — a voluntary bond-servant
(vv.5-6). Female servants have additional protections: they cannot be sold
to foreigners, must be treated as daughters if betrothed to the master's
son, and go free if their rights are violated (vv.7-11).

Capital Offenses (vv.12-17): Murder (v.12), striking parents (v.15),
kidnapping (v.16), cursing parents (v.17). Distinction between murder and
manslaughter: unintentional killing allows flight to a city of refuge (v.13);
premeditated murder has no sanctuary (v.14).

Laws of Personal Injury (vv.18-27): Non-fatal assault: the attacker pays for
lost time and healing (vv.18-19). Striking a slave fatally: the slave is
"avenged" (v.20). Injury to a pregnant woman: if no harm, a fine; if harm,
"life for life, eye for eye, tooth for tooth" (vv.22-25). Injuring a slave's
eye or tooth: the slave goes free (vv.26-27).

Laws Regarding Animals and Property (vv.28-36): A goring ox: first offense,
the ox is stoned but the owner is not liable (v.28); known dangerous ox, the
owner is liable for death (v.29). A slave killed by an ox: thirty shekels of
silver to the master (v.32) — the price Judas received for Christ (Matthew
26:15). Open pits and ox injuries: restitution required (vv.33-36).

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Israel remains encamped at Mount Sinai. These civil laws (the "Book of the Covenant") are given at the mountain where God spoke the Ten Commandments.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "If you buy a Hebrew slave, six years he shall serve, and in the
        seventh he shall go out free." (ESV) — Limits on servitude; freedom
        built into the system.

v.5-6 — "I love my master... I will not go out free... he shall serve him
        forever." (ESV) — The voluntary bond-servant: a picture of Christ's
        willing service.

v.23-25 — "Life for life, eye for eye, tooth for tooth." (ESV) — Proportional
        justice: the punishment fits the crime — no more, no less.

v.32 — "The owner shall give to their master thirty shekels of silver."
        (ESV) — The price of a slave — the price paid for Christ.

  forever as belonging to his master. And here is Christ: He could have gone

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1  — KJV: "judgments" / ESV: "rules" — Hebrew "mishpatim" — judicial
       decisions, case laws, ordinances. The KJV "judgments" captures the
       legal/judicial sense; the ESV "rules" is more accessible but loses
       the courtroom connotation. These are legal precedents for specific
       situations.

v.2  — KJV: "Hebrew servant" / ESV: "Hebrew slave" — Hebrew "eved ivri."
       The KJV uses "servant" throughout; the ESV uses "slave." The Hebrew
       "eved" covers a range from household servant to chattel slave. Hebrew
       servitude was debt-bondage with protections and a built-in expiration
       — fundamentally different from chattel slavery.

v.6  — KJV: "unto the judges" / ESV: "to God" — Hebrew "el ha'elohim."
       "Elohim" can mean "God" or "judges/authorities" (as God's
       representatives). The ESV takes it as "God" (the ceremony is sacred);
       the KJV takes it as "judges" (the ceremony is legal). Both are valid.

v.6  — KJV: "aul" / ESV: "awl" — A pointed tool for piercing. The KJV
       spelling is archaic; the ESV uses the modern spelling.

v.22 — KJV: "her fruit depart from her" / ESV: "her children come out" —
       Hebrew "veyatze'u yeladeha." KJV's "fruit depart" is euphemistic;
       ESV's "children come out" is more literal. The key interpretive
       question: does this describe premature birth (child survives) or
       miscarriage? The Hebrew "yeled" (child) and the "no harm" clause
       suggest premature birth with the child surviving.

v.23 — KJV: "mischief" / ESV: "harm" — Hebrew "ason" — serious injury,
       calamity, fatal harm. The ESV's "harm" is clearer to modern readers
       than the KJV's archaic "mischief" (which now implies minor trouble).

v.24 — KJV: "Eye for eye" / ESV: "eye for eye" — Hebrew "ayin tachat ayin"
       — eye in place of eye. The lex talionis (law of retaliation). Both
       translations are identical. This LIMITS punishment to proportional
       response — it does not demand literal mutilation but establishes that
       the penalty must match the crime.

v.32 — KJV/ESV: "thirty shekels of silver" — The standard price of a slave.
       Prophetically significant: Zechariah 11:12-13 and Matthew 26:15 —
       the price paid for Christ, valuing Him as a slave.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Mishpatim      — "Judgments" / "rules" — case law applying the Ten
               Commandments to specific situations. The practical outworking
               of moral principles.

Bond-servant   — A servant who voluntarily chooses permanent service out of
               love (vv.5-6). A type of Christ who willingly became a
               servant forever (Philippians 2:7).

Lex talionis   — "Law of retaliation" — eye for eye, tooth for tooth.
               LIMITS vengeance to proportional justice. The punishment must
               fit the crime — no more, no less.

Thirty shekels — The price of a slave (v.32). The price Judas received for
               betraying Christ (Matthew 26:15). God's Son valued at a
               slave's price.

Cities of refuge — Places where those guilty of manslaughter (not murder)
               could flee for protection (v.13). Established in Numbers 35
               and Joshua 20.

"Her fruit depart" — A pregnant woman struck, causing premature birth. The
               law protects both mother and unborn child — affirming the
               value of life before birth.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "I love my master... I will not go out free" (v.5). Christ chose to serve
   us — not by compulsion but by love. His ear was "pierced" at the cross.
   How do you respond to a Savior who CHOSE bondage for your freedom?

2. "Eye for eye" (v.24). Justice must be proportional — not excessive
   vengeance, not inadequate response. In your relationships, do you seek
   proportional justice or disproportionate revenge? Jesus goes further:
   "resist not evil" (Matthew 5:39) — grace exceeds justice.

3. These laws protect the vulnerable: slaves, women, the unborn, the
   injured. God cares about justice for the powerless. Do you? Are you
   advocating for those who cannot advocate for themselves?

4. The servant goes free in the seventh year (v.2). God builds freedom into
   the system. Bondage is never meant to be permanent. What bondage in your
   life has an expiration date that you haven't claimed?

5. Thirty shekels — the price of a slave (v.32). That's what Judas received
   for Jesus. The world valued God's Son at a slave's price. How do YOU
   value Him?
