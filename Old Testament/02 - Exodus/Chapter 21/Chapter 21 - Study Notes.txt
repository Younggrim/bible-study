Exodus - Chapter 21
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
God gives laws governing servants, personal injury, and property rights — justice that protects the vulnerable and values human life.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 21 begins the "Book of the Covenant" (21:1-23:33)
— the detailed case law that applies the Ten Commandments to daily life in
ancient Israel. These are "judgments" (mishpatim) — legal rulings for
specific situations. Chapter 21 covers: (1) Laws regarding Hebrew servants
(vv.1-11); (2) Capital offenses (vv.12-17); (3) Laws of personal injury
(vv.18-27); (4) Laws regarding dangerous animals and property (vv.28-36).
These laws were revolutionary for the ancient Near East — protecting slaves,
women, and the vulnerable in ways no other ancient code did. They establish
the principle of proportional justice ("eye for eye") which LIMITS vengeance
rather than encouraging it.

Laws Regarding Servants (vv.1-11): Hebrew servants serve six years, go free
in the seventh (v.2). If a servant loves his master and chooses to stay
permanently, his ear is pierced at the doorpost — a voluntary bond-servant
(vv.5-6). Female servants have additional protections: they cannot be sold
to foreigners, must be treated as daughters if betrothed to the master's
son, and go free if their rights are violated (vv.7-11).

Capital Offenses (vv.12-17): Murder (v.12), striking parents (v.15),
kidnapping (v.16), cursing parents (v.17). Distinction between murder and
manslaughter: unintentional killing allows flight to a city of refuge (v.13);
premeditated murder has no sanctuary (v.14).

Laws of Personal Injury (vv.18-27): Non-fatal assault: the attacker pays for
lost time and healing (vv.18-19). Striking a slave fatally: the slave is
"avenged" (v.20). Injury to a pregnant woman: if no harm, a fine; if harm,
"life for life, eye for eye, tooth for tooth" (vv.22-25). Injuring a slave's
eye or tooth: the slave goes free (vv.26-27).

Laws Regarding Animals and Property (vv.28-36): A goring ox: first offense,
the ox is stoned but the owner is not liable (v.28); known dangerous ox, the
owner is liable for death (v.29). A slave killed by an ox: thirty shekels of
silver to the master (v.32) — the price Judas received for Christ (Matthew
26:15). Open pits and ox injuries: restitution required (vv.33-36).

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Israel remains encamped at Mount Sinai. These civil laws (the "Book of the Covenant") are given at the mountain where God spoke the Ten Commandments.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/exodus-21/
  Guzik covers the humanitarian advances of these laws over other ancient
  codes, the bond-servant as a type of Christ, and "eye for eye" as
  limiting (not encouraging) vengeance.

Charles Spurgeon:
  Sermon: "The Willing Servant" on vv.5-6 (MTP Vol. 14, No. 800)
  "I LOVE my master — I will not go out free. Here is the bond-servant who
  CHOOSES slavery out of love. His ear is pierced at the doorpost — marked
  forever as belonging to his master. And here is Christ: He could have gone
  free. He was under no obligation. But He said, 'I love my Father. I love
  my bride. I will not go out free.' And His ear was pierced — not with an
  awl but with a nail. At the doorpost of the cross, He was marked forever
  as the willing Servant. 'Lo, I come to do thy will, O God' (Hebrews 10:9).
  He serves us forever — not by compulsion but by love."

REFLECTION
------------------------------------
1. "I love my master... I will not go out free" (v.5). Christ chose to serve
   us — not by compulsion but by love. His ear was "pierced" at the cross.
   How do you respond to a Savior who CHOSE bondage for your freedom?

2. "Eye for eye" (v.24). Justice must be proportional — not excessive
   vengeance, not inadequate response. In your relationships, do you seek
   proportional justice or disproportionate revenge? Jesus goes further:
   "resist not evil" (Matthew 5:39) — grace exceeds justice.

3. These laws protect the vulnerable: slaves, women, the unborn, the
   injured. God cares about justice for the powerless. Do you? Are you
   advocating for those who cannot advocate for themselves?

4. The servant goes free in the seventh year (v.2). God builds freedom into
   the system. Bondage is never meant to be permanent. What bondage in your
   life has an expiration date that you haven't claimed?

5. Thirty shekels — the price of a slave (v.32). That's what Judas received
   for Jesus. The world valued God's Son at a slave's price. How do YOU
   value Him?
