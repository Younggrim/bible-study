Exodus - Chapter 21
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
God gives laws governing servants, personal injury, and property rights — justice that protects the vulnerable and values human life.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 21 begins the "Book of the Covenant" (21:1-23:33)
— the detailed case law that applies the Ten Commandments to daily life in
ancient Israel. These are "judgments" (mishpatim) — legal rulings for
specific situations. Chapter 21 covers: (1) Laws regarding Hebrew servants
(vv.1-11); (2) Capital offenses (vv.12-17); (3) Laws of personal injury
(vv.18-27); (4) Laws regarding dangerous animals and property (vv.28-36).
These laws were revolutionary for the ancient Near East — protecting slaves,
women, and the vulnerable in ways no other ancient code did. They establish
the principle of proportional justice ("eye for eye") which LIMITS vengeance
rather than encouraging it.

Laws Regarding Servants (vv.1-11): Hebrew servants serve six years, go free
in the seventh (v.2). If a servant loves his master and chooses to stay
permanently, his ear is pierced at the doorpost — a voluntary bond-servant
(vv.5-6). Female servants have additional protections: they cannot be sold
to foreigners, must be treated as daughters if betrothed to the master's
son, and go free if their rights are violated (vv.7-11).

Capital Offenses (vv.12-17): Murder (v.12), striking parents (v.15),
kidnapping (v.16), cursing parents (v.17). Distinction between murder and
manslaughter: unintentional killing allows flight to a city of refuge (v.13);
premeditated murder has no sanctuary (v.14).

Laws of Personal Injury (vv.18-27): Non-fatal assault: the attacker pays for
lost time and healing (vv.18-19). Striking a slave fatally: the slave is
"avenged" (v.20). Injury to a pregnant woman: if no harm, a fine; if harm,
"life for life, eye for eye, tooth for tooth" (vv.22-25). Injuring a slave's
eye or tooth: the slave goes free (vv.26-27).

Laws Regarding Animals and Property (vv.28-36): A goring ox: first offense,
the ox is stoned but the owner is not liable (v.28); known dangerous ox, the
owner is liable for death (v.29). A slave killed by an ox: thirty shekels of
silver to the master (v.32) — the price Judas received for Christ (Matthew
26:15). Open pits and ox injuries: restitution required (vv.33-36).

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Israel remains encamped at Mount Sinai. These civil laws (the "Book of the Covenant") are given at the mountain where God spoke the Ten Commandments.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "If you buy a Hebrew slave, six years he shall serve, and in the
        seventh he shall go out free." (ESV) — Limits on servitude; freedom
        built into the system.

v.5-6 — "I love my master... I will not go out free... he shall serve him
        forever." (ESV) — The voluntary bond-servant: a picture of Christ's
        willing service.

v.23-25 — "Life for life, eye for eye, tooth for tooth." (ESV) — Proportional
        justice: the punishment fits the crime — no more, no less.

v.32 — "The owner shall give to their master thirty shekels of silver."
        (ESV) — The price of a slave — the price paid for Christ.

  Sermon: "The Willing Servant" on vv.5-6 (MTP Vol. 14, No. 800)
  "I LOVE my master — I will not go out free. Here is the bond-servant who
  CHOOSES slavery out of love. His ear is pierced at the doorpost — marked
  forever as belonging to his master. And here is Christ: He could have gone
  free. He was under no obligation. But He said, 'I love my Father. I love
  my bride. I will not go out free.' And His ear was pierced — not with an
  awl but with a nail. At the doorpost of the cross, He was marked forever
  as the willing Servant. 'Lo, I come to do thy will, O God' (Hebrews 10:9).
  He serves us forever — not by compulsion but by love."

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1  — Hebrew "mishpatim" — judicial decisions, case laws, ordinances.
       KJV: "judgments"
       ESV: "rules"
       ASV: "ordinances"
       NET: "ordinances"
       WEB: "ordinances"
       Analysis: The KJV "judgments" captures the legal/judicial sense. The
       ESV "rules" is more accessible but loses the courtroom connotation.
       ASV/WEB/NET all use "ordinances," preserving the formal, judicial
       character of these case laws better than the ESV's more casual "rules."
       These are legal precedents for specific situations.

v.2  — Hebrew "eved ivri" — a Hebrew bond-servant/slave.
       KJV: "Hebrew servant"
       ESV: "Hebrew slave"
       ASV: "Hebrew servant"
       NET: "Hebrew slave"
       WEB: "Hebrew servant"
       Analysis: The KJV/ASV/WEB use "servant"; ESV/NET use "slave." The
       Hebrew "eved" covers a range from household servant to chattel slave.
       Hebrew servitude was debt-bondage with protections and a built-in
       expiration — fundamentally different from chattel slavery.

v.6  — Hebrew "el ha'elohim" — to God / to the judges.
       KJV: "unto the judges"
       ESV: "to God"
       ASV: "bring him unto God"
       NET: "bring him to the judges"
       WEB: "bring him to God"
       Analysis: "Elohim" can mean "God" or "judges/authorities" (as God's
       representatives). The ESV/ASV/WEB take it as "God" (the ceremony is
       sacred); the KJV/NET take it as "judges" (the ceremony is legal). The
       NET emphasizes the legal process; ASV/WEB emphasize the sacred
       ceremony. Both interpretations are valid.

v.13 — Hebrew "lo tsadah... ha'elohim innah leyado" — did not lie in wait...
       God delivered into his hand.
       KJV: "if a man lie not in wait, but God deliver him into his hand"
       ESV: "if he did not lie in wait for him, but God let him fall into his hand"
       ASV: "if a man lie not in wait, but God deliver him into his hand"
       NET: "if he does not do it with premeditation, but it happens by accident"
       WEB: "not if it is unintentional, but God allows it to happen"
       Analysis: The NET paraphrases dynamically, removing the theological
       element ("God deliver him") in favor of a purely legal distinction
       ("by accident"). WEB retains God's providential role. ASV follows KJV
       closely. The NET's rendering loses the profound theological concept
       that even accidental death is under God's sovereignty.

v.16 — Hebrew "gonev ish" — one who steals a man (kidnaps).
       KJV: "he that stealeth a man"
       ESV: "whoever steals a man"
       ASV: "he that stealeth a man"
       NET: "Whoever kidnaps someone"
       WEB: "Anyone who kidnaps someone"
       Analysis: NET and WEB modernize with "kidnaps," making the crime
       immediately recognizable. KJV/ASV retain the literal "stealeth a man,"
       closer to the Hebrew. All convey the same capital offense.

v.21 — Hebrew "kaspo hu" — he is his silver/money.
       KJV: "for he is his money"
       ESV: "for the slave is his money"
       ASV: "for he is his money"
       NET: "for he has suffered the loss"
       WEB: "for he is his property"
       Analysis: A theologically sensitive verse. ASV is most literal (Hebrew
       "kaspo hu" = "he is his silver/money"). WEB softens to "property."
       NET diverges most, interpreting the loss as the master's financial
       loss rather than describing the servant as property — a significant
       interpretive shift.

v.22 — Hebrew "veyatze'u yeladeha" — and her children come out.
       KJV: "her fruit depart from her"
       ESV: "her children come out"
       ASV: "her fruit depart"
       NET: "her child is born prematurely"
       WEB: "she gives birth prematurely"
       Analysis: KJV/ASV use the euphemistic "fruit depart." ESV is more
       literal ("children come out"). NET and WEB explicitly interpret this
       as premature birth (child survives). The Hebrew "yeled" (child) and
       the "no harm" clause suggest premature birth — a major exegetical
       decision affecting the verse's application to the sanctity of
       unborn life.

v.23 — Hebrew "ason" — serious injury, calamity, fatal harm.
       KJV: "mischief"
       ESV: "harm"
       ASV: "mischief"
       NET: "serious injury"
       WEB: "harm"
       Analysis: The ESV/WEB "harm" and NET's "serious injury" are clearer to
       modern readers than the KJV/ASV's archaic "mischief" (which now implies
       minor trouble rather than the grave harm the Hebrew intends).

v.24 — Hebrew "ayin tachat ayin" — eye in place of eye.
       KJV: "Eye for eye"
       ESV: "eye for eye"
       ASV: "eye for eye"
       NET: "eye for eye"
       WEB: "eye for eye"
       Analysis: All translations are identical. The lex talionis (law of
       retaliation) LIMITS punishment to proportional response — it does not
       demand literal mutilation but establishes that the penalty must match
       the crime.

v.32 — Hebrew "sheloshim sheqalim kesef" — thirty shekels of silver.
       KJV: "thirty shekels of silver"
       ESV: "thirty shekels of silver"
       ASV: "thirty shekels of silver"
       NET: "thirty shekels of silver"
       WEB: "thirty shekels of silver"
       Analysis: All translations agree. The standard price of a slave.
       Prophetically significant: Zechariah 11:12-13 and Matthew 26:15 —
       the price paid for Christ, valuing Him as a slave.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Mishpatim      — "Judgments" / "rules" — case law applying the Ten
               Commandments to specific situations. The practical outworking
               of moral principles.

Bond-servant   — A servant who voluntarily chooses permanent service out of
               love (vv.5-6). A type of Christ who willingly became a
               servant forever (Philippians 2:7).

Lex talionis   — "Law of retaliation" — eye for eye, tooth for tooth.
               LIMITS vengeance to proportional justice. The punishment must
               fit the crime — no more, no less.

Thirty shekels — The price of a slave (v.32). The price Judas received for
               betraying Christ (Matthew 26:15). God's Son valued at a
               slave's price.

Cities of refuge — Places where those guilty of manslaughter (not murder)
               could flee for protection (v.13). Established in Numbers 35
               and Joshua 20.

"Her fruit depart" — A pregnant woman struck, causing premature birth. The
               law protects both mother and unborn child — affirming the
               value of life before birth.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "I love my master... I will not go out free" (v.5). Christ chose to serve
   us — not by compulsion but by love. His ear was "pierced" at the cross.
   How do you respond to a Savior who CHOSE bondage for your freedom?

2. "Eye for eye" (v.24). Justice must be proportional — not excessive
   vengeance, not inadequate response. In your relationships, do you seek
   proportional justice or disproportionate revenge? Jesus goes further:
   "resist not evil" (Matthew 5:39) — grace exceeds justice.

3. These laws protect the vulnerable: slaves, women, the unborn, the
   injured. God cares about justice for the powerless. Do you? Are you
   advocating for those who cannot advocate for themselves?

4. The servant goes free in the seventh year (v.2). God builds freedom into
   the system. Bondage is never meant to be permanent. What bondage in your
   life has an expiration date that you haven't claimed?

5. Thirty shekels — the price of a slave (v.32). That's what Judas received
   for Jesus. The world valued God's Son at a slave's price. How do YOU
   value Him?
