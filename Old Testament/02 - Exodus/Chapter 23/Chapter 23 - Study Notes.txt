Exodus - Chapter 23
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
God gives laws about justice, Sabbath rest, three annual feasts, and promises an angel to lead Israel into the Promised Land.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 23 concludes the Book of the Covenant with three
sections: (1) Laws of justice and compassion (vv.1-9); (2) Sabbath laws and
the three annual feasts (vv.10-19); (3) God's promises for the conquest of
Canaan (vv.20-33). The chapter moves from daily ethics to national destiny.
God promises an Angel to lead them, gradual conquest ("little by little"),
and defined borders from the Red Sea to the Euphrates. The chapter ends with
a warning: make no covenant with Canaan's inhabitants or their gods — "it
will surely be a snare unto thee" (v.33). The Book of the Covenant is
formally ratified in chapter 24.

Laws of Justice and Compassion (vv.1-9): No false reports or malicious
witness (v.1). Don't follow the crowd into evil or pervert justice (v.2).
Don't show partiality — even to the poor (v.3). Help your enemy's lost
animal (v.4) and his overburdened donkey (v.5). Don't pervert justice for
the poor (v.6). Stay far from falsehood; don't kill the innocent (v.7). Take
no bribes — "the gift blindeth the wise" (v.8). Don't oppress strangers —
"ye know the heart of a stranger" (v.9).

Sabbath Laws and Feasts (vv.10-19): Sabbath year: let the land rest every
seventh year — the poor and animals eat what grows (vv.10-11). Weekly
Sabbath: rest for all — including servants and animals (v.12). Three annual
feasts: (1) Unleavened Bread — Passover season (v.15); (2) Harvest/Firstfruits
— Pentecost (v.16a); (3) Ingathering — Tabernacles (v.16b). All males appear
before God three times yearly (v.17). "Thou shalt not seethe a kid in his
mother's milk" (v.19) — possibly a Canaanite fertility ritual forbidden.

Promises for the Conquest (vv.20-33): God sends His Angel — "my name is in
him" (vv.20-21). Obey and God fights your enemies (v.22). God will drive out
the nations (v.23). Don't worship their gods — destroy their images (v.24).
Blessings: bread, water, health, fertility, long life (vv.25-26). God sends
terror and hornets before Israel (vv.27-28). Gradual conquest: "little by
little" — not all at once (vv.29-30). Borders: Red Sea to Mediterranean,
wilderness to Euphrates (v.31). No covenants with the inhabitants (v.32).
Warning: their gods will be a snare (v.33).

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Israel remains encamped at Mount Sinai. These laws continue the Book of the Covenant, given at the mountain.
- References to "the land" (vv.10-11, 23-33): The laws anticipate Israel's future life in Canaan — agricultural festivals, borders "from the Red Sea to the Sea of the Philistines, and from the desert to the River [Euphrates]" (v.31).

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "You shall not fall in with the many to do evil." (ESV) — Moral
        courage against crowd pressure.

v.4-5 — "If you meet your enemy's ox going astray, you shall bring it
        back." (ESV) — Love your enemy in practical action.

v.8  — "You shall take no bribe, for a bribe blinds the clear-sighted."
        (ESV) — The corruption of bribery.

v.21 — "My name is in him." (ESV) — The Angel carries God's authority and
        identity.

v.30 — "Little by little I will drive them out from before you." (ESV) —
        God's gradual, progressive work.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/exodus-23/
  Guzik covers the ethics of justice (including helping enemies), the three
  feasts as prophetic pictures, the Angel as a Christophany, and the
  principle of gradual conquest.

Charles Spurgeon:
  Sermon: "Little by Little" on v.30 (MTP Vol. 18, No. 1065)
  "Little by little I will drive them out — not all at once. Not in a single
  dramatic victory. Little by little. This is God's usual method in
  sanctification. He does not make you perfect overnight. He drives out your
  sins little by little — one habit at a time, one temptation at a time, one
  victory at a time. Why? 'Lest the land become desolate' — lest you be
  overwhelmed by the emptiness of sudden change. God fills the space as He
  clears it. Be patient with His process. He is working — little by little."

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1  — Hebrew "lo tissa shema shav" — do not carry/lift up a worthless/false report.
       KJV: "raise a false report"
       ESV: "spread a false report"
       ASV: "raise a false report"
       NET: "spread a false report"
       WEB: "spread a false report"
       Analysis: KJV/ASV "raise" suggests originating; ESV/NET/WEB "spread"
       suggests passing along. The Hebrew covers both: don't create OR
       circulate false information.

v.2  — Hebrew "lo tihyeh acharei rabbim lera'ot" — do not be after the many to do evil.
       KJV: "follow a multitude to do evil"
       ESV: "fall in with the many to do evil"
       ASV: "follow a multitude to do evil"
       NET: "follow the crowd in doing evil"
       WEB: "follow a crowd to do evil"
       Analysis: ESV's "fall in with" captures being swept along by the
       crowd. KJV/ASV's "follow" is more deliberate. NET/WEB modernize with
       "crowd." All warn against mob mentality overriding individual
       conscience.

v.3  — Hebrew "lo tehdar" — do not favor/show partiality.
       KJV: "countenance a poor man"
       ESV: "be partial to a poor man"
       ASV: "be partial to a poor man"
       NET: "show partiality to a poor man"
       WEB: "favor a poor man"
       Analysis: Justice must be impartial — neither favoring the rich (v.6)
       NOR the poor (v.3). The ESV/ASV/NET "be partial" and WEB "favor" are
       clearer than the KJV's archaic "countenance."

v.8  — Hebrew "shochad" — a bribe, a gift given to influence judgment.
       KJV: "gift"
       ESV: "bribe"
       ASV: "a bribe"
       NET: "a bribe"
       WEB: "a bribe"
       Analysis: ESV/ASV/NET/WEB "bribe" is more precise; the KJV's "gift"
       could be misread as innocent generosity. This is specifically a
       payment to corrupt justice — "it blindeth the wise."

v.9  — Hebrew "ger... nephesh" — sojourner/foreigner... soul/heart/life.
       KJV: "stranger... ye know the heart of a stranger"
       ESV: "sojourner... you know the heart of a sojourner"
       ASV: "sojourner... ye know the heart of a sojourner"
       NET: "resident foreigner... you know the life of a foreigner"
       WEB: "alien... you know the heart of an alien"
       Analysis: NET renders "nephesh" as "life" rather than "heart" (ASV/WEB),
       emphasizing experiential knowledge. ASV/WEB's "heart" emphasizes
       emotional empathy. NET uses "resident foreigner" for "ger" — the most
       explanatory rendering. Both capture knowing what it feels like to be
       an outsider.

v.17 — Hebrew "ha'Adon YHWH" — the Lord GOD.
       KJV: "the Lord GOD"
       ESV: "the Lord GOD"
       ASV: "the Lord Jehovah"
       NET: "the Sovereign Lord"
       WEB: "the Lord Yahweh"
       Analysis: ASV combines "Lord" with "Jehovah"; WEB combines "Lord"
       with "Yahweh"; NET renders this as "the Sovereign Lord" — a title
       rather than a name. This is one of the few places where "Adon"
       (Lord/Master) appears alongside the Tetragrammaton.

v.19 — Hebrew "lo tevashel gedi bachalev immo" — do not boil a kid in its
       mother's milk.
       KJV: "seethe a kid in his mother's milk"
       ESV: "boil a young goat in its mother's milk"
       ASV: "Thou shalt not boil a kid in its mother's milk"
       NET: "You must not cook a young goat in its mother's milk"
       WEB: "You shall not boil a young goat in its mother's milk"
       Analysis: ESV/WEB modernize "seethe" to "boil" and "kid" to "young
       goat." NET's "cook" is broader than "boil/seethe" (Hebrew "tevashel"
       specifically means to boil/cook). This prohibition (repeated three
       times in Torah) likely forbids a Canaanite fertility ritual. It became
       the basis for Jewish kosher separation of meat and dairy.

v.20 — Hebrew "mal'akh" — an angel/messenger.
       KJV: "an Angel" (capitalized)
       ESV: "an angel" (lowercase)
       ASV: "an angel"
       NET: "an angel"
       WEB: "an angel"
       Analysis: The KJV alone capitalizes "Angel," suggesting a divine being
       (the Angel of the LORD / pre-incarnate Christ). All others use
       lowercase. Context supports the divine interpretation: "my name is in
       him" (v.21) and "he will not pardon your transgressions" — only God
       can pardon or withhold pardon. Neither ASV nor WEB capitalizes
       "angel," leaving identification to the reader.

v.20-21 — Hebrew "lishmorkha" — to keep/guard/protect you.
       KJV: "to keep thee in the way"
       ESV: "to guard you on the way"
       ASV: "to keep thee"
       NET: "to protect you"
       WEB: "to keep you by the way"
       Analysis: NET adds "going to" (future intent) and renders "lishmorkha"
       as "protect" rather than "keep" (ASV/WEB). KJV/ASV/WEB preserve the
       broader "keep" which encompasses protection, guidance, and provision.

v.24 — Hebrew "mattsevot" — standing stones used in pagan worship.
       KJV: "break down their images"
       ESV: "break their pillars in pieces"
       ASV: "break in pieces their pillars"
       NET: "smash their standing stones to pieces"
       WEB: "demolish their pillars"
       Analysis: NET is most descriptive ("standing stones"), clarifying that
       these are sacred objects, not architectural pillars. WEB uses
       "demolish" (stronger than ASV's "break"). NET's rendering makes the
       pagan worship context clearer.

v.25 — Hebrew "YHWH eloheikhem" — the LORD your God.
       KJV: "the LORD your God"
       ESV: "the LORD your God"
       ASV: "ye shall serve Jehovah your God"
       NET: "You must serve the Lord your God"
       WEB: "You shall serve Yahweh your God"
       Analysis: The divine name pattern continues: ASV "Jehovah," WEB
       "Yahweh," NET "the Lord." In a chapter about covenant faithfulness and
       exclusive worship, the choice of whether to use God's personal name or
       a title carries theological weight.

v.31 — Hebrew "min hamidbar ad hanahar" — from the wilderness to the River.
       KJV: "from the desert unto the river"
       ESV: "from the wilderness to the Euphrates"
       ASV: "from the wilderness to the River"
       NET: "from the wilderness to the Euphrates"
       WEB: "from the wilderness to the River"
       Analysis: ESV/NET specify "the Euphrates" (which is what "the river"
       means in context). KJV/ASV/WEB leave it as "the river" — but in
       Hebrew, "the River" (with the article) always means the Euphrates.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
"Follow a multitude to do evil" — The danger of crowd morality. Right and
               wrong are not determined by majority vote. Individual
               conscience before God must override group pressure.

Sabbath year   — Every seventh year, the land rests. No planting, no
               harvesting. The poor eat what grows naturally. Trust in God's
               provision — the land needs rest, and the poor need food.

Three feasts   — (1) Unleavened Bread (Passover) — redemption; (2) Harvest/
               Firstfruits (Pentecost) — provision; (3) Ingathering
               (Tabernacles) — completion. Together they picture the full
               scope of God's work.

"My name is in him" — The Angel carries God's identity and authority. This
               is not a created angel but a divine being — the pre-incarnate
               Christ who bears God's name.

"Little by little" — God's method of gradual conquest/sanctification. Not
               instant perfection but progressive victory. God fills the
               space as He clears it.

"A snare" — Canaanite religion as a trap. Compromise with false worship
               doesn't lead to tolerance — it leads to bondage.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Thou shalt not follow a multitude to do evil" (v.2). The crowd is not
   your moral compass — God is. When everyone around you is doing wrong, do
   you have the courage to stand alone? Popularity is not righteousness.

2. "If thou meet thine enemy's ox... thou shalt surely bring it back" (v.4).
   Love your enemy in practical ways — not just in feelings but in actions.
   Who is your "enemy" whose donkey you need to help?

3. "The gift blindeth the wise" (v.8). Bribery corrupts even good people.
   What "gifts" are blinding your judgment? Money, flattery, comfort,
   approval — anything that compromises your integrity.

4. "Little by little I will drive them out" (v.30). God's work in you is
   gradual. Don't be discouraged by slow progress. He is driving out your
   "Canaanites" — one by one, little by little. Be patient with His process.

5. "It will surely be a snare unto thee" (v.33). Compromise with sin always
   becomes a trap. What are you tolerating that God says to remove? Don't
   make covenants with what God has condemned.
