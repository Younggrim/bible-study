Exodus - Chapter 20
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
God speaks the Ten Commandments directly to Israel from Mount Sinai, establishing the moral foundation of the covenant.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 20 contains the Ten Commandments (the Decalogue)
— the moral foundation of God's covenant with Israel and the bedrock of
Western civilization's legal tradition. God speaks these words directly to
the people — "God spake all these words" (v.1) — the only time in Scripture
God addresses an entire nation audibly. The commandments are prefaced by
grace: "I am the LORD thy God, which have brought thee out of the land of
Egypt" (v.2) — God redeems FIRST, then commands. The Law is given to a
redeemed people, not to earn redemption. The first four commandments govern
the relationship with God (vertical); the last six govern relationships with
others (horizontal). Jesus summarized them: love God, love neighbor (Matthew
22:37-40).

The Preamble (vv.1-2): "God spake all these words" (v.1) — divine origin,
not human invention. "I am the LORD thy God, which have brought thee out of
the land of Egypt, out of the house of bondage" (v.2) — grace precedes law.
God identifies Himself by His redemptive act, not merely His power.

The Ten Commandments:

1st: No other gods (v.3) — "Thou shalt have no other gods before me."
Exclusive loyalty. God alone is God.

2nd: No graven images (vv.4-6) — No representation of God in created form.
God is jealous — visiting iniquity to the third and fourth generation of
those who hate Him, but showing mercy to thousands who love Him.

3rd: God's name not in vain (v.7) — Do not misuse, empty, or trivialize
God's name. He will not hold the guilty guiltless.

4th: Remember the Sabbath (vv.8-11) — Six days work; seventh day rest. Based
on creation pattern. Rest for all — family, servants, animals, foreigners.

5th: Honor parents (v.12) — The bridge commandment (vertical to horizontal).
Promise attached: long life in the land.

6th: Do not kill (v.13) — The sanctity of human life.

7th: Do not commit adultery (v.14) — The sanctity of marriage.

8th: Do not steal (v.15) — The sanctity of property.

9th: Do not bear false witness (v.16) — The sanctity of truth.

10th: Do not covet (v.17) — The sanctity of the heart. The only commandment
addressing internal desire, not external action.

The People's Response (vv.18-21): The people see the thunder, lightning,
smoke, and trumpet — they tremble and stand far off (v.18). They ask Moses
to mediate: "Speak thou with us... but let not God speak with us, lest we
die" (v.19). Moses: "God is come to prove you, and that his fear may be
before your faces, that ye sin not" (v.20). Moses approaches the thick
darkness; the people stand afar (v.21).

Altar Instructions (vv.22-26): No gods of silver or gold (v.23). Altars of
earth or unhewn stone — no human craftsmanship (vv.24-25). No steps — to
prevent exposure (v.26). Worship must be simple, God-directed, and humble.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Mount Sinai: God speaks the Ten Commandments from the mountain. Israel remains encamped at the base.
- "Out of the land of Egypt, out of the house of bondage" (v.2): The law is given in the context of deliverance — they have left Egypt and now stand at Sinai.

        (ESV) — Grace precedes law. God redeems before He commands.

        restrains sin.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/exodus-20/
  Guzik provides detailed exposition of each commandment, their New
  Testament fulfillment in Christ, and the relationship between grace
  (v.2) and law (vv.3-17).

Charles Spurgeon:
  Sermon: "The First Commandment" on v.3 (MTP Vol. 37, No. 2195)
  "Thou shalt have no other gods BEFORE ME — not 'beside me' or 'after me'
  but 'before me' — in my presence, in my face. Every idol is an insult to
  God's face. And what are our idols? Not golden calves — but careers,
  relationships, comfort, reputation, money, self. Anything that occupies
  the throne that belongs to God alone. The first commandment is not merely
  'believe God exists' — it is 'give God FIRST place.' Not a place — THE
  place. Not some loyalty — ALL loyalty."

  Sermon: "Thou Shalt Not Covet" on v.17 (MTP Vol. 29, No. 1708)
  "The tenth commandment is the one that kills us all. The others can be
  outwardly kept — you can refrain from murder, theft, adultery. But 'thou
  shalt not covet' — this reaches the HEART. This is where Paul was slain:
  'I had not known lust, except the law had said, Thou shalt not covet'
  (Romans 7:7). The tenth commandment proves that the Law is spiritual —
  it demands not just right actions but right desires. And no human being
  can control desire by willpower alone. We need a new heart. We need grace."

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.2  — Hebrew "Anokhi YHWH Elohekha" — I (emphatic) am YHWH your God.
       KJV: "I am the LORD thy God"
       ESV: "I am the LORD your God"
       ASV: "I am Jehovah thy God"
       NET: "I, the Lord, am your God"
       WEB: "I am Yahweh your God"
       Analysis: The divine name in the covenant preamble. This is God's self-
       identification before giving the commandments. ASV/WEB preserve the personal
       name; NET subordinates it into a title phrase. The "I AM" (ehyeh) of Exodus
       3:14 stands behind this declaration. The personal pronoun "anokhi" (rather
       than "ani") adds emphasis.

v.3  — Hebrew "al panay" — before my face, in my presence.
       KJV: "before me"
       ESV: "before me"
       ASV: "before me"
       NET: "before me"
       WEB: "before me"
       Analysis: All identical. Not merely "instead of me" but "in addition to me"
       or "in my sight." No rivals tolerated in God's presence.

v.4  — Hebrew "pesel" — a carved/hewn image.
       KJV: "graven image"
       ESV: "carved image"
       ASV: "graven image"
       NET: "a carved image"
       WEB: "an idol"
       Analysis: WEB's "idol" is interpretive — pesel specifically means a crafted
       image, not all forms of idolatry. KJV/ASV's "graven image" and ESV/NET's
       "carved image" are more literal to the Hebrew.

v.5  — Hebrew "El qanna... poqed avon avot al-banim" — a zealous God... visiting iniquity of fathers upon children.
       KJV: "jealous God, visiting the iniquity of the fathers upon the children"
       ESV: "a jealous God, visiting the iniquity of the fathers on the children"
       ASV: "a jealous God, visiting the iniquity of the fathers upon the children"
       NET: "a jealous God, responding to the transgression of fathers by dealing with children to the third and fourth generations"
       WEB: "a jealous God, visiting the iniquity of the fathers on the children"
       Analysis: Not petty jealousy but the righteous jealousy of a husband for his
       wife. NET heavily paraphrases "poqed avon avot al-banim" — softening the
       force. The literal "visiting iniquity upon" (KJV/ESV/ASV/WEB) implies active
       judicial consequence.

v.6  — Hebrew "chesed" — covenant love, loyal love.
       KJV: "shewing mercy unto thousands"
       ESV: "showing steadfast love to thousands"
       ASV: "showing lovingkindness unto thousands"
       NET: "showing covenant faithfulness to a thousand generations"
       WEB: "showing loving kindness to thousands"
       Analysis: Hebrew "chesed" rendered as "mercy" (KJV), "steadfast love" (ESV),
       "lovingkindness" (ASV), "loving kindness" (WEB), and "covenant faithfulness"
       (NET). NET adds "generations" interpretively and emphasizes the covenantal
       aspect. The contrast: judgment to 3-4 generations vs. mercy to thousands —
       God's grace far outweighs His judgment.

v.7  — Hebrew "lo tissa et-shem YHWH elohekha lashav" — do not carry/lift the name of YHWH your God to emptiness.
       KJV: "Thou shalt not take the name of the LORD thy God in vain"
       ESV: "You shall not take the name of the LORD your God in vain"
       ASV: "Thou shalt not take the name of Jehovah thy God in vain"
       NET: "You shall not take the name of the Lord your God in vain"
       WEB: "You shall not misuse the name of Yahweh your God"
       Analysis: "In vain" (lashav) means emptily, falsely, without substance. WEB
       uniquely renders "misuse" rather than "take... in vain." Using God's name
       without reverence, truth, or weight — treating the sacred as common.

v.11 — Hebrew "vayeqaddesh oto" — and sanctified/hallowed it.
       KJV: "the LORD blessed the sabbath day, and hallowed it"
       ESV: "the LORD blessed the Sabbath day and made it holy"
       ASV: "Jehovah blessed the sabbath day, and hallowed it"
       NET: "the Lord blessed the Sabbath day and set it apart as holy"
       WEB: "Yahweh blessed the Sabbath day, and made it holy"
       Analysis: ASV's "hallowed," WEB/ESV's "made it holy," and NET's "set it apart
       as holy" all render "qiddesh." NET's phrase most explicitly defines what
       holiness means: separation for sacred purpose.

v.13 — Hebrew "lo tirtzach" — you shall not murder (unlawful killing).
       KJV: "Thou shalt not kill"
       ESV: "You shall not murder"
       ASV: "Thou shalt not kill"
       NET: "You shall not murder"
       WEB: "You shall not murder"
       Analysis: ESV/NET/WEB correctly distinguish "ratzach" (murder — unlawful
       killing) from "harag" or "muth" (killing generally). KJV/ASV's broader
       "kill" can be misleading — the commandment forbids unlawful killing, not all
       taking of life (capital punishment, war, self-defense use different verbs).

v.17 — Hebrew "chamad" — to desire, to delight in, to crave.
       KJV: "covet"
       ESV: "covet"
       ASV: "covet"
       NET: "covet"
       WEB: "covet"
       Analysis: All identical. The only commandment addressing internal motivation
       rather than external action. The commandment that exposes the heart and proves
       we all need grace (Romans 7:7).

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Decalogue      — "Ten Words" (Greek). The Ten Commandments. God's moral law
               summarizing human duty to God and neighbor.

"Grace before law" — The preamble (v.2) establishes that God redeemed Israel
               BEFORE giving commands. Obedience is a response to grace, not
               a means of earning it.

"Jealous God"  — God's righteous zeal for exclusive relationship. Not petty
               envy but holy love that refuses to share its beloved with
               rivals.

"In vain" (lashav) — Emptily, without substance. Using God's name without
               reverence, truth, or weight. Treating the sacred as common.

"Thou shalt not murder" — The sanctity of human life. Humans bear God's
               image; to murder is to assault God's image-bearer.

"Thou shalt not covet" — The commandment of the heart. Exposes that sin
               begins internally — in desire — before it becomes action.
               The commandment that proves we all need grace.

The Mediator   — The people request Moses to mediate (v.19). They cannot
               bear God's direct presence. This points to the need for THE
               Mediator — Christ (1 Timothy 2:5).

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "I am the LORD thy God, which have brought thee out" (v.2). God's
   commands come AFTER His grace. You don't obey to be saved — you obey
   BECAUSE you're saved. Is your obedience motivated by gratitude or by
   fear of rejection?

2. "Thou shalt have no other gods before me" (v.3). What occupies God's
   throne in your life? Career? Relationship? Comfort? Money? Reputation?
   Be honest — and dethrone the idol.

3. "Thou shalt not covet" (v.17). This is the commandment that exposes
   everyone. You can control your actions — but can you control your
   desires? The tenth commandment drives us to grace: we need a new heart,
   not just better behavior.

4. "God is come to prove you, and that his fear may be before your faces,
   that ye sin not" (v.20). The Law's purpose is not to crush you but to
   create holy reverence that restrains sin. Do you have a healthy fear of
   God — not terror, but reverent awe?

5. The people asked for a mediator (v.19). They couldn't bear God's direct
   presence. You have a Mediator — Jesus Christ (1 Timothy 2:5). Through
   Him, you can approach the holy God without terror. Thank Him for that
   access.
