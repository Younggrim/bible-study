Exodus - Chapter 1
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Israel multiplies in Egypt despite a new Pharaoh's brutal slavery and genocide decree, but God preserves His people through courageous midwives who fear Him more than the king.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses. Exodus is the second of the five books of Moses (the
Pentateuch/Torah). Internal evidence: Exodus 17:14, 24:4, 34:27 — God
commands Moses to write. Jesus attributes Exodus to Moses (Mark 7:10,
12:26). The book covers events Moses personally witnessed and participated
in — from his birth through the construction of the Tabernacle.

Date of Writing: Approximately 1445-1405 BC (early date of the Exodus),
written during the 40 years of wilderness wandering.

Title: "Exodus" comes from the Greek "exodos" — "a going out" / "departure."
The Hebrew title is "Shemot" — "Names" — from the opening words: "these are
the names."

Historical Context: Chapter 1 bridges Genesis and Exodus. Approximately 350-
400 years pass between Genesis 50 and Exodus 1. The 70 souls who entered
Egypt have become a nation of perhaps 2-3 million. A new dynasty ("a new
king... which knew not Joseph" — v.8) enslaves Israel. The chapter introduces
the conflict that drives the entire book: Pharaoh's oppression vs. God's
covenant faithfulness. Despite slavery, genocide attempts, and brutal
affliction — "the more they afflicted them, the more they multiplied" (v.12).

Israel's Growth (vv.1-7): The chapter opens by naming the sons of Jacob who
came to Egypt — 70 souls (v.5). Joseph and his generation die (v.6). Then
the explosion: "fruitful, and increased abundantly, and multiplied, and waxed
exceeding mighty" (v.7) — five verbs of growth, echoing God's creation
mandate (Genesis 1:28) and His promise to Abraham (Genesis 15:5).

Pharaoh's Oppression (vv.8-14): A new king "which knew not Joseph" (v.8) —
a dynasty change. He fears Israel's numbers (v.9) and enslaves them with
"taskmasters to afflict them" (v.11). They build Pithom and Raamses (v.11).
But oppression backfires: "the more they afflicted them, the more they
multiplied" (v.12). So Egypt intensifies: "rigour" — crushing, breaking
labor (vv.13-14).

The Midwives' Courage (vv.15-21): Pharaoh commands the Hebrew midwives
Shiphrah and Puah to kill all male babies (vv.15-16). "But the midwives
feared God" (v.17) — they obey God rather than Pharaoh. When questioned,
they claim Hebrew women deliver too quickly (v.19). "God dealt well with the
midwives" and "made them houses" — established their families (vv.20-21).

Pharaoh's Final Decree (v.22): Failing with the midwives, Pharaoh commands
ALL his people: "Every son that is born ye shall cast into the river" (v.22)
— state-sponsored genocide. This sets the stage for Moses' birth in chapter 2.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Goshen: Region in the eastern Nile Delta where Israel settled (Genesis 46:28-34). Fertile land, separate from Egyptian population centers.
- Egypt (the land): The broader kingdom under Pharaoh's rule; Israel dwelt in Goshen but labored on Egyptian building projects.
- Pithom and Raamses (v.11): Store-cities built by Israelite slaves, located in the eastern Delta region.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.7  — "But the people of Israel were fruitful and increased greatly; they
        multiplied and grew exceedingly strong, so that the land was filled
        with them." (ESV) — God's covenant promise fulfilled despite circumstances.

v.8  — "Now there arose a new king over Egypt, who did not know Joseph."
        (ESV) — The turning point: from favor to slavery.

v.12 — "But the more they were oppressed, the more they multiplied and
        the more they spread abroad." (ESV) — God's blessing cannot be stopped by human oppression.

v.17 — "But the midwives feared God and did not do as the king of Egypt
        commanded them, but let the male children live." (ESV) — Civil
        disobedience grounded in the fear of God.

v.22 — "Every son that is born to the Hebrews you shall cast into the
        Nile." (ESV) — Satan's attempt to destroy the covenant line — and the deliverer (Moses).

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.7  — KJV: "were fruitful, and increased abundantly, and multiplied, and
       waxed exceeding mighty" / ESV: "were fruitful and increased greatly;
       they multiplied and grew exceedingly strong" — Five Hebrew verbs of
       growth: "paru" (were fruitful), "vayyishretzu" (swarmed/teemed),
       "vayyirbu" (multiplied), "vayyaatzmu" (became strong), "bim'od m'od"
       (exceedingly). The ESV captures the force well with "exceedingly
       strong." The language echoes Genesis 1 — God's creative power at work.

v.8  — KJV: "knew not Joseph" / ESV: "did not know Joseph" — Hebrew "lo
       yada" — did not know/acknowledge. Not mere ignorance but deliberate
       disregard — refusing to honor the debt Egypt owed to Joseph. Both
       translations are equivalent.

v.11 — KJV: "taskmasters" / ESV: "taskmasters" — Hebrew "sarei missim" —
       chiefs of forced labor gangs. Both translations use the same word.
       KJV: "treasure cities" / ESV: "store cities" — Hebrew "arei
       miskenot" — storage/supply cities. The ESV's "store cities" is more
       accurate than the KJV's "treasure cities."

v.13 — KJV: "rigour" / ESV: "ruthlessly" — Hebrew "perekh" — harshness,
       crushing severity, breaking labor. The ESV's "ruthlessly" captures
       the brutality more vividly for modern readers. Used only of the
       Egyptian oppression and forbidden for Israelites (Leviticus 25:43).

v.15 — KJV: "Hebrew midwives" / ESV: "Hebrew midwives" — Could mean
       "midwives who were Hebrew" or "midwives to the Hebrews." Most
       scholars take them as Hebrew women — their names (Shiphrah =
       "beautiful"; Puah = "splendid") are Semitic.

v.16 — KJV: "stools" / ESV: "birthstool" — Hebrew "obnayim" — literally
       "two stones" — the birthing stones/stool on which women knelt during
       delivery. The ESV's "birthstool" is clearer than the KJV's "stools."

v.21 — KJV: "he made them houses" / ESV: "he gave them families" — Hebrew
       "vayya'as lahem battim" — He made/established houses for them.
       "Houses" (battim) can mean households/families/dynasties. The ESV
       interprets: God blessed them with families of their own. The KJV
       leaves it more literal.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.5 — ASV: "all the souls that came out of the loins of Jacob" — extremely literal rendering of Hebrew idiom; NET modernizes to "directly descended from Jacob," losing the physical/generational imagery.
- v.7 — NET: "The Israelites, however, were fruitful, increased greatly, multiplied, and became extremely strong" — uses "Israelites" rather than "children of Israel" (ASV/WEB), a consistent NET choice that loses the patronymic connection to Jacob.
- v.11 — ASV: "Raamses" / NET: "Rameses" — ASV preserves the older English spelling closer to the Hebrew "Ra'amses"; NET modernizes to match Egyptian convention.
- v.13 — WEB: "ruthlessly" — matches ESV; ASV retains "with rigor" (KJV phrasing). NET expands to "serve rigorously" — a middle path that retains the Hebrew "perekh" concept.
- v.16 — ASV: "birth-stool" / NET: "observe at the delivery" — NET completely paraphrases the Hebrew "obnayim" (two stones), avoiding the cultural artifact; ASV hyphenates for clarity while preserving the object reference.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Exodus         — Greek "exodos" — departure, going out. The book of Israel's
               deliverance from Egypt — a picture of salvation from sin.

"Knew not Joseph" — A new dynasty that refused to honor Joseph's legacy.
               Ingratitude and political expediency replace gratitude.

Taskmasters    — Egyptian overseers of forced labor. Organized oppression
               designed to break Israel's spirit and limit their growth.

Rigour (perekh) — Crushing, breaking severity. Labor designed not just to
               produce but to destroy.

Shiphrah and Puah — Hebrew midwives who feared God more than Pharaoh. Models
               of courageous civil disobedience when human law contradicts
               divine law.

"Made them houses" — God established the midwives' families/dynasties as
               reward for their faithfulness. Obedience to God brings
               blessing, even when it risks earthly punishment.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "The more they afflicted them, the more they multiplied" (v.12). God's
   purposes cannot be stopped by opposition. What opposition are you facing?
   Trust that God can use it to multiply His work in your life.

2. "The midwives feared God" (v.17). When human authority contradicts God's
   commands, whom do you obey? The midwives chose God over Pharaoh — and God
   honored them. Where might you need this courage?

3. "A new king... which knew not Joseph" (v.8). Favor with the world is
   temporary. The world's gratitude fades. Don't build your security on
   human favor — build it on God's covenant.

4. Pharaoh's plan escalates: taskmasters → midwife murder → public genocide
   (vv.11, 16, 22). Evil escalates when unchecked. Are you tolerating
   "small" sins that are growing? Address them now.

5. God is silent in this chapter — no theophany, no voice, no miracle. Yet
   He is clearly at work: multiplying Israel, empowering the midwives,
   overruling Pharaoh. Sometimes God works silently. His silence is not
   absence.
