Exodus - Chapter 8
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
God sends the plagues of frogs, gnats, and flies on Egypt; Pharaoh bargains but hardens his heart each time relief comes.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 8 records plagues two through four: frogs, lice
(gnats), and flies. Key developments: (1) Pharaoh makes his first false
promise — "I will let the people go" (v.8) — then reneges when relief comes
(v.15); (2) The magicians reach their limit at the third plague — "This is
the finger of God" (v.19) — they can no longer imitate; (3) God introduces
a "division" (Hebrew "pedut" — redemption) between Israel in Goshen and
Egypt (v.23) — the plagues now discriminate. Each plague targets Egyptian
deities: Heqet (frog goddess), Geb (earth god), and Khepri (scarab/fly god).

Second Plague: Frogs (vv.1-15): God warns Pharaoh: "I will smite all thy
borders with frogs" (v.2). Frogs invade everywhere — beds, ovens, kneading
troughs (v.3). The magicians replicate it — more frogs, not fewer (v.7).
Pharaoh begs Moses: "Intreat the LORD" (v.8) — his first acknowledgment of
YHWH's power. Moses lets Pharaoh choose the timing: "When?" Pharaoh says
"Tomorrow" (v.10) — inexplicably choosing one more night with frogs. The
frogs die; the land stinks (v.14). "When Pharaoh saw that there was respite,
he hardened his heart" (v.15).

Third Plague: Lice/Gnats (vv.16-19): No warning given — God strikes without
announcement. Aaron smites the dust — lice on man and beast (v.17). The
magicians TRY to replicate but CANNOT (v.18). Their confession: "This is the
finger of God" (v.19). Yet Pharaoh's heart remains hard (v.19).

Fourth Plague: Flies (vv.20-32): God warns Pharaoh at the water (v.20). A
new element: "I will sever in that day the land of Goshen" (v.22) — Israel
is protected. "I will put a division between my people and thy people" (v.23).
Grievous swarms corrupt the land (v.24). Pharaoh offers a compromise:
"sacrifice... in the land" (v.25). Moses refuses — they must go three days
into the wilderness (v.27). Pharaoh agrees with conditions: "ye shall not go
very far away" (v.28). Moses prays; flies removed — "there remained not one"
(v.31). Pharaoh hardens his heart again (v.32).

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Egypt — the land: Frogs, lice, and flies cover "all the land of Egypt" (vv.6, 17, 24).
- Goshen (v.22): God makes a "division" — no flies in Goshen where Israel dwells. The separation between God's people and Egypt becomes geographically visible.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.10 — "He said, 'Tomorrow.'" (ESV) — Pharaoh chooses one more night with
        frogs rather than immediate relief. The irrationality of sin.

v.15 — "But when Pharaoh saw that there was a respite, he hardened his
        heart." (ESV) — Relief produces complacency, not repentance.

v.19 — "This is the finger of God." (ESV) — The magicians' confession. Even
        Satan's servants recognize God's power.

v.22 — "But on that day I will set apart the land of Goshen, where my
        people dwell, so that no swarms of flies shall be there." (ESV) —
        God distinguishes between His people and the world.

v.23 — "I will put a division between my people and your people." (ESV) —
        God's redemptive separation.

Charles Spurgeon:
  Sermon: "Tomorrow" on v.10 (MTP Vol. 5, No. 240)
  "TOMORROW! — Pharaoh could have said 'today' — 'this very hour, remove
  the frogs!' But he said 'tomorrow.' One more night with the frogs. One
  more night in misery. This is the madness of the sinner who says 'tomorrow
  I will repent, tomorrow I will believe, tomorrow I will turn to God.' Why
  tomorrow? Why one more night with your frogs — your sins, your guilt, your
  bondage? Today, if ye will hear his voice, harden not your hearts. Every
  'tomorrow' is a 'today' that never comes for the procrastinator."

  Sermon: "The Finger of God" on v.19 (MTP Vol. 33, No. 1954)
  "This is the FINGER of God — even the magicians confess it. If the finger
  of God produces lice from dust, what does the ARM of God do? (Exodus 6:6).
  What does the HAND of God do? (Exodus 9:3). If His finger confounds the
  wise men of Egypt, His arm redeems a nation, and His hand destroys an
  army. And Jesus said: 'If I with the FINGER of God cast out devils, no
  doubt the kingdom of God is come upon you' (Luke 11:20). The finger is
  enough."

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1  — Hebrew chapter/verse note: Hebrew Bible starts chapter 8 at what English Bibles call 7:26.
       KJV: "let my people go"
       ESV: "Let my people go"
       ASV: "Let my people go"
       NET: "Release my people" (with Hebrew verse reference 7:26)
       WEB: "Let my people go"
       Analysis: NET uniquely preserves the Hebrew verse numbering in
       parentheses — a distinctive feature showing awareness of the Masoretic
       text divisions. All translations convey the same demand.

v.2  — Hebrew "tsefarde'im" — frogs.
       KJV: "frogs"
       ESV: "frogs"
       ASV: "frogs"
       NET: "frogs"
       WEB: "frogs"
       Analysis: All translations identical. The frog was sacred in Egypt —
       the goddess Heqet (fertility, depicted with a frog's head). God turns
       their sacred animal into a plague — their goddess becomes their
       torment.

v.16 — Hebrew "kinnim" — possibly lice, gnats, or mosquitoes.
       KJV: "lice"
       ESV: "gnats"
       ASV: "lice"
       NET: "gnats"
       WEB: "lice"
       Analysis: A significant translation difference. ASV/WEB follow KJV's
       "lice," while NET/ESV follow the LXX (Septuagint) tradition of
       "gnats." The creature is uncertain — the Hebrew word is rare. The
       theological point is clear regardless: the very dust of Egypt becomes
       a plague, and the magicians cannot replicate it.

v.19 — Hebrew "etzba Elohim" — the finger of God.
       KJV: "This is the finger of God"
       ESV: "This is the finger of God"
       ASV: "This is the finger of God"
       NET: "It is the finger of God!"
       WEB: "This is God's finger"
       Analysis: WEB's phrasing with "God's" rather than "of God" is
       slightly more possessive, emphasizing divine ownership of the power.
       NET adds an exclamation mark for emphasis. The magicians use "Elohim"
       (not YHWH) — they acknowledge divine power but not necessarily YHWH
       specifically.

v.21 — Hebrew "arob" — a swarm (the word "flies" is not in the Hebrew).
       KJV: "swarms of flies"
       ESV: "swarms of flies"
       ASV: "swarms of flies"
       NET: "swarms of flies"
       WEB: "swarms of flies"
       Analysis: All translations add "flies" for clarity — the Hebrew says
       only "swarm." Could be flies, beetles, or a mixture of insects.
       Possibly targeting Khepri, the scarab beetle god. The ambiguity in
       Hebrew may be intentional — a mixed swarm of every biting, stinging
       thing.

v.22 — Hebrew "hifleiti" — to set apart, to make distinct.
       KJV: "I will sever in that day the land of Goshen"
       ESV: "I will set apart the land of Goshen"
       ASV: "I will set apart the land of Goshen"
       NET: "I will mark off the land of Goshen"
       WEB: "I will set apart the land of Goshen in that day"
       Analysis: KJV's "sever" emphasizes the division. ESV/ASV/WEB use "set
       apart." NET uniquely uses "mark off" — suggesting a visible boundary.
       WEB also adds "I am Yahweh on the earth" — by using the actual name,
       it makes God's claim of sovereignty over Pharaoh's territory more
       personal and direct.

v.23 — Hebrew "pedut" — redemption, ransom, distinction.
       KJV: "I will put a division between my people and thy people"
       ESV: "I will put a distinction between my people and your people"
       ASV: "I will put a division between my people and thy people"
       NET: "I will put a distinction between my people and your people"
       WEB: "I will put a division between my people and your people"
       Analysis: ESV/NET use "distinction"; KJV/ASV/WEB use "division." The
       same Hebrew root as "redemption" — God's separation of His people
       from judgment IS their redemption. This is a turning point: from
       plague 4 onward, Israel in Goshen is protected.

v.26 — Hebrew: Moses explains why they cannot sacrifice in Egypt.
       KJV: "we shall sacrifice the abomination of the Egyptians"
       ESV: "we shall sacrifice to the LORD our God the abomination of the Egyptians"
       ASV: "we shall sacrifice the abomination of the Egyptians"
       NET: "the sacrifices we make to the Lord our God would be an abomination to the Egyptians"
       WEB: "we shall sacrifice the abomination of the Egyptians"
       Analysis: NET restructures and clarifies the confusing Hebrew —
       Israel's sacrificial animals (cattle, sheep) were sacred to Egypt.
       KJV/ASV/WEB are more literal but require the reader to understand
       this cultural context. NET makes it explicit that Egyptians would
       find Israel's sacrifices abominable.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Heqet          — Egyptian frog goddess of fertility and childbirth. The frog
               plague turns Egypt's sacred animal into a disgusting
               infestation — their goddess becomes their torment.

"The finger of God" — The magicians' confession that supernatural power
               beyond their ability is at work. Even minimal divine power
               ("a finger") exceeds all human/demonic ability.

"Tomorrow"     — Pharaoh's irrational delay. The procrastinator's word —
               choosing one more day of misery over immediate repentance.

Division/Separation — God distinguishes between His people and the world.
               Beginning with plague 4, Israel in Goshen is protected while
               Egypt suffers. God's people are IN the world but not subject
               to its judgments.

False repentance — Pharaoh's pattern: promise during pressure, renege during
               relief (vv.8, 15, 28, 32). Pressure-driven "repentance" is
               not genuine — it evaporates when the pressure lifts.

Pedut (redemption) — The Hebrew word for "division" (v.23) is related to
               "redemption." God's separation of His people from judgment
               IS redemption.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Tomorrow" (v.10). Why do we delay repentance? Why one more night with
   our "frogs"? If God is offering relief TODAY — take it today. Don't
   choose another night in bondage.

2. "When Pharaoh saw that there was respite, he hardened his heart" (v.15).
   Relief can produce complacency. When God removes pressure, do you forget
   your promises to Him? Don't let comfort harden what crisis softened.

3. "This is the finger of God" (v.19). Even the magicians recognized God's
   power. Are you more stubborn than Pharaoh's sorcerers? If the evidence
   is clear, respond to it.

4. "I will put a division between my people and thy people" (v.23). God
   protects His own. You are not subject to the same fate as those who
   reject Him. Trust His covering over your life.

5. Pharaoh's compromises: "sacrifice in the land" (v.25), "don't go far"
   (v.28). The enemy always offers partial obedience. Don't settle for
   compromise — go the full three days' journey. Complete obedience, not
   negotiated half-measures.
