Exodus - Chapter 18
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Jethro, Moses' father-in-law, visits and advises him to delegate judgment to capable leaders — a model of wise administration.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 18 is a pause between the wilderness journey and
the giving of the Law at Sinai. Jethro (Moses' father-in-law, priest of
Midian) visits, bringing Zipporah and Moses' two sons. He hears the exodus
story, worships God, and then observes Moses judging the people alone from
morning to evening. Jethro's counsel — delegate to qualified leaders — is
one of the Bible's clearest teachings on leadership, delegation, and
organizational structure. Moses humbly receives wisdom from a non-Israelite
— demonstrating that God can speak through unexpected sources. This chapter
establishes the principle: no one person should carry the entire burden of
leadership.

Jethro's Visit (vv.1-12): Jethro hears what God has done (v.1). He brings
Zipporah and Moses' sons — Gershom ("stranger there") and Eliezer ("God is
my help") (vv.2-4). Moses tells the full story (v.8). Jethro rejoices and
confesses: "Now I know that the LORD is greater than all gods" (v.11). He
offers sacrifices and eats with Aaron and the elders "before God" (v.12).

Moses' Unsustainable Pattern (vv.13-16): Moses judges the people alone —
from morning to evening (v.13). Jethro observes and asks: "What is this
thing that thou doest?" (v.14). Moses explains: the people come to him for
God's judgment and to know God's statutes (vv.15-16).

Jethro's Counsel (vv.17-23): "The thing that thou doest is not good" (v.17).
"Thou wilt surely wear away" — burnout (v.18). Jethro's solution: (1) Moses
teaches God's laws (v.20); (2) Appoint qualified leaders — "able men, such
as fear God, men of truth, hating covetousness" (v.21); (3) Organize in
tiers: rulers of thousands, hundreds, fifties, and tens (v.21); (4) They
handle small matters; Moses handles great matters (v.22). Result: "thou shalt
be able to endure, and all this people shall also go to their place in peace"
(v.23).

Moses Implements (vv.24-27): Moses listens and acts (v.24). He appoints
leaders at every level (v.25). The system works: hard cases go to Moses,
small matters are handled locally (v.26). Jethro departs (v.27).

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- The wilderness near Mount Sinai: Jethro comes from Midian to meet Moses at "the mount of God" (v.5).
- Israel is now encamped at the base of Sinai, where they will remain for approximately 11 months.

------------------------------------
v.11 — "Now I know that the LORD is greater than all gods." (ESV) — Jethro's
        confession of faith.

        wise observer.

        (ESV) — The danger of unsustainable leadership.

v.21 — "Moreover, look for able men from all the people, men who fear God,
        who are trustworthy and hate a bribe." (ESV) — Qualifications for leadership.

v.23 — "Then you will be able to endure, and all this people also will go
        to their place in peace." (ESV) — The fruit of wise delegation.

Charles Spurgeon:
  Sermon: "Jethro's Counsel" on v.17 (MTP Vol. 26, No. 1553)
  "The thing that thou doest is NOT GOOD — sometimes the most spiritual
  people need the most practical advice. Moses was doing a good thing —
  judging the people, teaching God's law. But he was doing it in a bad way
  — alone, exhausted, unsustainable. Good work done badly is still not good.
  And God sent the counsel through Jethro — not a prophet, not an Israelite,
  but a father-in-law with common sense. Christian, don't be so spiritual
  that you reject practical wisdom. Don't be so busy serving God that you
  burn out serving God. Delegation is not laziness — it is wisdom."

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1  — Hebrew "YHWH" — the covenant name of God.
       KJV: "the LORD had brought Israel out of Egypt"
       ESV: "the LORD had done for Moses and for Israel"
       ASV: "Jehovah had brought Israel out of Egypt"
       NET: "the Lord had brought Israel out of Egypt"
       WEB: "Yahweh had brought Israel out of Egypt"
       Analysis: The consistent divine name pattern continues. Note v.1 uses "God"
       (Elohim) for Jethro's initial hearing, then switches to YHWH — Jethro comes
       to recognize the covenant name. ASV "Jehovah," WEB "Yahweh," NET/KJV/ESV
       "the LORD/Lord."

v.3  — Hebrew "ger sham" — "stranger there."
       KJV: "Gershom"
       ESV: "Gershom"
       ASV: "Gershom"
       NET: "Gershom"
       WEB: "Gershom"
       Analysis: All transliterate. Moses named his first son after his exile —
       "stranger there." The name testifies to Moses' displacement from home.

v.4  — Hebrew "eli ezer" — "my God is help."
       KJV: "Eliezer"
       ESV: "Eliezer"
       ASV: "Eliezer"
       NET: "Eliezer"
       WEB: "Eliezer"
       Analysis: All transliterate. Named after God's deliverance from Pharaoh —
       a testimony embedded in a name.

v.10 — Hebrew "barukh YHWH" — Blessed be the LORD.
       KJV: "Blessed be the LORD"
       ESV: "Blessed be the LORD"
       ASV: "Blessed be Jehovah"
       NET: "Blessed be the Lord"
       WEB: "Blessed be Yahweh"
       Analysis: Jethro's confession of YHWH's supremacy. A Midianite priest
       recognizes Israel's God as supreme — a significant interfaith moment rendered
       with each translation's characteristic divine name.

v.11 — Hebrew "badavar asher zadu aleihem" — in the matter where they dealt proudly against them.
       KJV: "in the thing wherein they dealt proudly he was above them"
       ESV: "because in the affair in which they dealt arrogantly with the people, he was above them"
       ASV: "in the thing wherein they dealt proudly against them"
       NET: "for in the thing in which they dealt proudly against them he has destroyed them"
       WEB: "in the matter where they dealt arrogantly against them"
       Analysis: All translations struggle with this verse's abrupt ending. The
       Hebrew is elliptical — NET adds "he has destroyed them" to complete the
       thought; ASV leaves it incomplete as the Hebrew does. God defeated Egypt
       precisely in the areas of their pride.

v.18 — Hebrew "navol tibbol" — wither, fade, wear out completely.
       KJV: "wear away"
       ESV: "wear yourselves out"
       ASV: "Thou wilt surely wear away"
       NET: "You will surely wear out, both you and these people"
       WEB: "You will surely wear away"
       Analysis: An emphatic infinitive absolute meaning to completely wither/fade.
       NET's "wear out" is contemporary; ASV/WEB's "wear away" captures the gradual
       decay more precisely. Both capture the emphatic doubling. Burnout is real —
       even for God's servants.

v.21 — Hebrew "anshei chayil... yir'ei Elohim... anshei emet... son'ei batza'" — men of strength, God-fearers, men of truth, haters of unjust gain.
       KJV: "able men, such as fear God, men of truth, hating covetousness"
       ESV: "men who are trustworthy and who fear God, are trustworthy and hate a bribe"
       ASV: "able men, such as fear God, men of truth, hating unjust gain"
       NET: "capable men, God-fearing men, men of truth, those who hate bribes"
       WEB: "able men, such as fear God, men of truth, hating unjust gain"
       Analysis: Four qualifications for leaders: (1) capable/competent ("anshei
       chayil"); (2) God-fearing ("yir'ei Elohim"); (3) truthful/trustworthy
       ("anshei emet"); (4) free from greed ("son'ei batza'"). KJV emphasizes
       ability; ESV emphasizes character. NET's "hate bribes" narrows "batza'"
       (covetousness/unjust gain) to only bribery. ASV/WEB are most balanced.

v.23 — Hebrew "beshalom" — in peace/wholeness.
       KJV: "shall also go to their place in peace"
       ESV: "will also go to their place in peace"
       ASV: "shall go to their place in peace"
       NET: "all these people will be able to go home satisfied"
       WEB: "shall also go to their place in peace"
       Analysis: NET's "satisfied" is more interpretive; "in peace" (KJV/ESV/ASV/WEB)
       captures the Hebrew shalom better — justice brings communal well-being, not
       just personal satisfaction. Good leadership structure benefits everyone.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Jethro         — Moses' father-in-law, priest of Midian. A non-Israelite
               who recognizes YHWH's supremacy and offers wise counsel.
               God speaks through unexpected sources.

Delegation     — Distributing responsibility to qualified others. Not
               abandoning duty but multiplying capacity. Biblical leadership
               is shared leadership.

"Able men, such as fear God" — Four qualifications for leaders: (1) capable/
               competent; (2) God-fearing; (3) truthful/trustworthy;
               (4) free from greed. Character before skill.

Burnout        — "Thou wilt surely wear away." Even godly work done in
               ungodly patterns leads to exhaustion. Sustainability matters.

Tiered leadership — Rulers of thousands, hundreds, fifties, tens. Organized
               structure that distributes the burden and brings justice
               closer to the people.

"God command thee so" — Jethro's caveat (v.23): implement this IF God
               confirms it. Wise counsel still requires divine confirmation.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "The thing that thou doest is not good" (v.17). Are you doing good work
   in an unsustainable way? Burnout is not a badge of honor — it's a sign
   that something needs to change. Who is your Jethro — speaking honest
   truth into your life?

2. "Thou wilt surely wear away" (v.18). You are not indispensable. If you
   try to do everything yourself, you'll destroy yourself AND the people you
   serve. Delegation is not weakness — it's wisdom.

3. "Able men, such as fear God, men of truth, hating covetousness" (v.21).
   Character qualifications for leadership: competence, godliness, integrity,
   and freedom from greed. Do these describe you? Do they describe the
   leaders you follow?

4. Moses received counsel from a non-Israelite (v.24). God can speak wisdom
   through unexpected people. Are you humble enough to receive good advice
   from outside your usual circle?

5. "All this people shall also go to their place in peace" (v.23). Good
   leadership structure benefits everyone — not just the leader. When you
   delegate well, the whole community thrives.
