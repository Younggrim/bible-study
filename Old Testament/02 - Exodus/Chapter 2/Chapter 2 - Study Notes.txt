Exodus - Chapter 2
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Moses is born, hidden in a basket, drawn from the Nile by Pharaoh's daughter, and later flees to Midian after killing an Egyptian.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 2 covers 80 years of Moses' life in 25 verses:
his birth and rescue (vv.1-10), his failed attempt at deliverance at age 40
(vv.11-14), his flight to Midian and 40 years of preparation (vv.15-22),
and God's response to Israel's cry (vv.23-25). Moses is born under the death
decree of Exodus 1:22, preserved by faith (Hebrews 11:23), raised in
Pharaoh's palace, trained in "all the wisdom of the Egyptians" (Acts 7:22),
yet identifies with his people. His premature attempt at deliverance by
human strength fails — God spends 40 years in the wilderness preparing him
for the task. The chapter ends with four powerful verbs: God heard, God
remembered, God looked, God had respect (vv.24-25).

Moses' Birth and Rescue (vv.1-10): A Levite couple (Amram and Jochebed —
6:20) bear a son (v.1-2). Jochebed hides him three months, then places him
in an "ark" (same Hebrew word as Noah's ark) of bulrushes, waterproofed with
pitch (v.3). Miriam watches (v.4). Pharaoh's daughter finds him, has
compassion, and adopts him (vv.5-6). Miriam arranges for Jochebed to nurse
him — paid by Pharaoh's household to raise her own son (vv.7-9). He is named
"Moses" — "drawn out" (v.10).

Moses' Failed Deliverance (vv.11-14): At age 40 (Acts 7:23), Moses "went
out unto his brethren, and looked on their burdens" (v.11). He kills an
Egyptian oppressor and hides the body (v.12). The next day, a Hebrew rejects
his authority: "Who made thee a prince and a judge over us?" (v.14). Moses
realizes his act is known and fears (v.14).

Flight to Midian (vv.15-22): Moses flees to Midian — the wilderness east of
the Gulf of Aqaba (v.15). He sits by a well (v.15) — a recurring biblical
scene (Genesis 24, 29; John 4). He defends Reuel's (Jethro's) seven
daughters from shepherds (vv.16-17). He marries Zipporah (v.21). His son
Gershom = "stranger there" — Moses feels his exile (v.22).

God Responds (vv.23-25): The Pharaoh dies; Israel cries out (v.23). Four
verbs describe God's response: "God heard... God remembered... God looked...
God had respect" (vv.24-25). God's silence is broken. The exodus is about
to begin.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- The Nile River (v.3): Where Moses was placed in the basket; the lifeblood of Egypt, also site of the first plague later.
- Pharaoh's daughter's bathing place (v.5): Along the Nile, likely near the royal palace in the Delta.
- Midian (v.15): East of the Gulf of Aqaba, in the northwestern Arabian peninsula. Moses flees here and lives 40 years as a shepherd.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "When she saw that he was a fine child, she hid him three months."
        (ESV) — Faith sees what God is doing (Hebrews 11:23).

v.10 — "She named him Moses, 'Because,' she said, 'I drew him out of the
        water.'" (ESV) — The deliverer's name means "drawn out" — he who was
        drawn out will draw out a nation.

v.14 — "Who made you a prince and a judge over us?" (ESV) — Moses is rejected
        by his own people — foreshadowing Christ's rejection.

v.24-25 — "And God heard their groaning, and God remembered his covenant...
        God saw the people of Israel—and God knew." (ESV) — God's fourfold
        response to suffering.

Charles Spurgeon:
  Sermon: "Moses — His Faith and Decision" on v.11 (MTP Vol. 17, No. 978)
  "Moses went out unto his brethren and LOOKED ON THEIR BURDENS. He did not
  look away. He did not pretend he was Egyptian. He LOOKED — and looking led
  to action. But his action was premature. He tried to deliver by the arm of
  flesh — and failed. God would use Moses, but not Moses' way. Not by
  killing one Egyptian but by defeating all of Egypt. Not by human strength
  but by divine power. Moses needed 40 years in the wilderness to learn that
  the deliverer must be delivered first — from self-reliance, from human
  wisdom, from his own timetable. God's delays are not God's denials."

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.2  — Hebrew "tov" — good, beautiful (same word as Genesis 1: "God saw that it was good").
       KJV: "a goodly child"
       ESV: "a fine child"
       ASV: "a goodly child"
       NET: "a beautiful child"
       WEB: "a fine child"
       Analysis: KJV/ASV's "goodly" captures the sense of something
       remarkable — Moses' parents saw God's purpose. ESV/WEB's "fine" is
       understated. NET's "beautiful" aligns with Acts 7:20. The same word
       declared creation "good" — God's hand is on this child.

v.3  — Hebrew "tebat gome" — a chest/basket of papyrus; "chemar" and "zepheth" — bitumen and pitch.
       KJV: "ark of bulrushes... slime and pitch"
       ESV: "basket made of bulrushes... bitumen and pitch"
       ASV: "ark of bulrushes... slime and with pitch"
       NET: "papyrus basket... sealed it with bitumen and pitch"
       WEB: "papyrus basket... sealed it with bitumen and with pitch"
       Analysis: "Tebah" is used only here and for Noah's ark — both vessels
       of salvation through water. KJV/ASV retain "ark," preserving the
       deliberate Hebrew connection to Noah. NET/WEB use "papyrus basket,"
       losing that theological link. ESV's "basket" also loses it but adds
       "made of" for clarity. KJV's "slime" is archaic for tar/asphalt —
       modern translations rightly use "bitumen."

v.10 — Hebrew "Mosheh" — from "mashah" (to draw out).
       KJV: "Moses... Because I drew him out of the water"
       ESV: "Moses... Because I drew him out of the water"
       ASV: "Moses... Because I drew him out of the water"
       NET: "Moses... Because I drew him from the water"
       WEB: "Moses... Because I drew him out of the water"
       Analysis: All translations transliterate the name. NET uses "from"
       rather than "out of" — a subtle shift that slightly weakens the
       physical rescue imagery. An Egyptian name element (as in Thut-mose,
       Ra-meses) meaning "born of" — but given Hebrew etymology by
       Pharaoh's daughter. The name is prophetic: he who was drawn out will
       draw out a nation.

v.12 — Hebrew "vayyakh" — he struck/killed.
       KJV: "he slew the Egyptian"
       ESV: "he struck down the Egyptian"
       ASV: "he smote the Egyptian"
       NET: "he attacked the Egyptian"
       WEB: "he killed the Egyptian"
       Analysis: WEB is strikingly direct — "killed" makes the lethal outcome
       explicit. ASV's "smote" can mean merely struck. NET's "attacked"
       softens to the action without specifying death. KJV's "slew" and
       ESV's "struck down" both clearly indicate death. The Hebrew verb
       range shows the translations balancing directness with euphemism.

v.22 — Hebrew "ger sham" — a stranger there.
       KJV: "I have been a stranger in a strange land"
       ESV: "I have been a sojourner in a foreign land"
       ASV: "I have been a sojourner in a foreign land"
       NET: "I have become a resident foreigner in a foreign land"
       WEB: "I have lived as a foreigner in a foreign land"
       Analysis: NET is uniquely interpretive, distinguishing legal status
       ("resident foreigner") from mere strangeness. ASV/ESV use
       "sojourner" — a theological term connecting to Abraham's experience.
       WEB emphasizes the lived experience. Moses names his son after his
       exile — he belongs neither to Egypt nor to Midian.

v.25 — Hebrew "vayyeda Elohim" — literally "God knew."
       KJV: "God had respect unto them"
       ESV: "God knew"
       ASV: "God took knowledge of them"
       NET: "God understood"
       WEB: "God was concerned about them"
       Analysis: Five remarkably different renderings of the same Hebrew. ESV
       is most literal. KJV interprets knowing as caring attention. ASV
       preserves ambiguity. NET emphasizes cognition. WEB emphasizes
       compassion. All are valid facets: God knew their condition intimately
       — personal, compassionate, active knowledge that leads to
       intervention.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Ark (tebah)    — A chest or vessel of salvation. Used only for Noah's ark
               and Moses' basket — both preserve life through water.

"Drawn out"    — Moses' name and destiny. He was drawn from the water; he
               will draw Israel from Egypt. The name is prophetic.

Midian         — Moses' place of preparation. 40 years as a shepherd —
               learning patience, humility, and the wilderness terrain he
               will later lead Israel through.

"God remembered" — Not that God forgot, but that He now acts on His
               covenant. "Remembering" in Hebrew means moving to action
               based on a prior commitment.

Gershom        — "Stranger there" — Moses' sense of exile. He belongs
               neither to Egypt nor to Midian. His true identity is with
               his people and his God.

The four verbs (vv.24-25) — Heard, remembered, looked, knew. God's
               comprehensive response to suffering: He hears the cry,
               remembers His promise, sees the condition, and knows
               intimately.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Moses' parents acted in faith despite the death decree (v.2; Hebrews
   11:23). Faith doesn't wait for safe circumstances — it acts in dangerous
   ones. Where is God calling you to act in faith despite the risk?

2. Moses tried to deliver by his own strength and timing (v.12) — and
   failed. Have you ever run ahead of God? His delays are preparation, not
   denial. What might God be preparing you for right now?

3. "Who made thee a prince and a judge?" (v.14). Moses was rejected by the
   very people he tried to help. If you're serving others and facing
   rejection — you're in good company. Keep serving.

4. God heard, remembered, looked, and knew (vv.24-25). If you're crying out
   and heaven seems silent — God hears. He remembers His promises. He sees
   your situation. He knows you intimately. His response is coming.

5. Moses spent 40 years in a palace and 40 years in a wilderness before God
   used him. Preparation takes time. Don't despise the wilderness season —
   it's where God shapes deliverers.
