Exodus - Chapter 38
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
The bronze altar, basin, and courtyard are built; an accounting of all the gold, silver, and bronze used in construction.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Exodus 38 continues the construction narrative, moving from
the interior furnishings (ch. 37) to the exterior elements: the Bronze Altar of
Burnt Offering (vv.1–7), the Bronze Laver (v.8), and the Courtyard with its
hangings, pillars, and gate (vv.9–20). The chapter concludes with a detailed
inventory of materials used (vv.21–31) — a financial accounting of the
tabernacle project that demonstrates both transparency and the magnitude of
Israel's generosity.

The Bronze Altar was the first thing a worshiper encountered upon entering the
courtyard — before approaching God's presence, sacrifice must be made. The
Laver, made from the bronze mirrors of the women who served at the entrance,
provided water for priestly washing — cleansing before service. These outer
court items were bronze (not gold), representing judgment and purification
rather than the divine glory of the inner sanctuary.

The materials inventory (vv.21–31) is remarkable: 29 talents and 730 shekels of
gold (approximately 2,200 pounds), 100 talents and 1,775 shekels of silver
(approximately 7,500 pounds), and 70 talents and 2,400 shekels of bronze
(approximately 5,300 pounds). The silver came from the half-shekel census tax
of 603,550 men — connecting the tabernacle's foundation literally to the
redemption price of every Israelite male. God's dwelling rests on the
foundation of His people's redemption.

Section Summaries:
vv.1–7 — The Bronze Altar of Burnt Offering constructed.
v.8 — The Bronze Laver made from women's mirrors.
vv.9–20 — The Courtyard: hangings, pillars, bases, and gate screen.
vv.21–23 — Record-keeping; Bezalel and Oholiab named.
vv.24–31 — Materials inventory: gold, silver, and bronze totals.

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1  — Hebrew measurements for the Bronze Altar.
       KJV: "five cubits... five cubits... three cubits"
       ESV: "five cubits... five cubits... three cubits"
       ASV: "five cubits... five cubits... three cubits"
       NET: "7½ feet long and 7½ feet wide... and its height was 4½ feet"
       WEB: "five cubits... five cubits... three cubits"
       Analysis: NET converts all cubit measurements to feet, making the substantial size tangible: approximately 7½ feet square and 4½ feet tall. KJV/ESV/ASV/WEB retain the original Hebrew cubits.

v.3  — Hebrew "mizlagot" — forks or flesh-hooks.
       KJV: "fleshhooks"
       ESV: "forks"
       ASV: "flesh-hooks"
       NET: "meat forks"
       WEB: "meat forks"
       Analysis: Used to handle sacrificial meat on the altar. KJV/ASV retain the compound "fleshhooks"; ESV modernizes to simple "forks"; NET/WEB combine both ideas as "meat forks."

v.4  — Hebrew "mikbar maʻaseh reshet" — a grating of net-work.
       KJV: "a brasen grate of network"
       ESV: "a grating, a network of bronze"
       ASV: "a grating of network of brass"
       NET: "a grating, a network of bronze"
       WEB: "a grating of a network of bronze"
       Analysis: A bronze mesh placed inside the altar to hold the burning sacrifice. Note ASV/WEB/KJV use "brass" in some instances while NET/ESV use "bronze" — Hebrew "nechoshet" is more accurately "bronze" (an alloy of copper and tin), though "brass" (copper and zinc) was the traditional English rendering.

v.8  — Hebrew "marʼot" — mirrors (polished bronze); "kiyyor" — laver/basin.
       KJV: "the laver of brass, and the foot of it of brass, of the lookingglasses of the women"
       ESV: "the basin of bronze and its stand of bronze, from the mirrors of the ministering women"
       ASV: "he made the laver of brass, and the base thereof of brass, of the mirrors of the women"
       NET: "He made the large basin of bronze and its pedestal of bronze from the mirrors of the women"
       WEB: "He made the basin of brass, and its base of brass, of the mirrors of the women"
       Analysis: KJV "lookingglasses" reflects the older English term for mirrors (polished bronze). NET uses "large basin" for "kiyyor," emphasizing its size. ASV uses the traditional "laver." NET's "pedestal" (vs. "base/stand/foot") is the most specific English term. These were the women's personal possessions sacrificed for sacred use.

v.8  — Hebrew "hatsoveʼot ʼasher tsaveʼu" — the women who served/assembled.
       KJV: "the women assembling, which assembled"
       ESV: "the ministering women who ministered"
       ASV: "the serving women that served"
       NET: "the women who served"
       WEB: "the ministering women who ministered"
       Analysis: These women had a recognized role of service at the tabernacle entrance. All translations acknowledge their official function, though the exact nature of their service is debated.

v.9  — Hebrew courtyard measurements in cubits.
       KJV: "an hundred cubits"
       ESV: "a hundred cubits"
       ASV: "a hundred cubits"
       NET: "150 feet long"
       WEB: "one hundred cubits"
       Analysis: NET's modern measurement conversion: the courtyard was approximately 150 × 75 feet (100 × 50 cubits), giving perspective on the tabernacle complex's substantial size.

v.21 — Hebrew "pequdey hamishkan" — accounts/records/inventory.
       KJV: "the sum of the tabernacle"
       ESV: "the records of the tabernacle"
       ASV: "This is the sum of the things for the tabernacle"
       NET: "This is the inventory of the tabernacle"
       WEB: "This is the amount of material used for the tabernacle"
       Analysis: Three different renderings of "pequdey": KJV/ASV "sum" (a reckoning), ESV "records" (formal documentation), NET "inventory" (formal accounting), WEB "amount of material" (most interpretive). A formal reckoning of materials used, demonstrating accountability.

v.22 — Hebrew "kaʼasher tsivvah YHWH ʼet-Mosheh" — as YHWH commanded Moses.
       KJV: "all that the LORD commanded Moses"
       ESV: "everything that the LORD had commanded Moses"
       ASV: "all that Jehovah commanded Moses"
       NET: "everything that the Lord had commanded Moses"
       WEB: "all that Yahweh commanded Moses"
       Analysis: The obedience formula using each translation's standard rendering of the divine name. This phrase of obedient completion recurs throughout chapters 36–39 like a drumbeat of faithfulness.

v.26 — Hebrew "beqaʻ" — a half-shekel.
       KJV: "a bekah for every man"
       ESV: "a beka a head"
       ASV: "a beka a head"
       NET: "one beka per person"
       WEB: "a beka a head"
       Analysis: Approximately 0.2 ounces of silver — the redemption price per person, establishing each Israelite's stake in the tabernacle. The silver came from 603,550 men, connecting the tabernacle's foundation literally to redemption.

v.27 — Hebrew "adanim" — bases/sockets for the veil.
       KJV: "sockets of the sanctuary... sockets of the vail"
       ESV: "bases for the sanctuary... bases for the veil"
       ASV: "the sockets of the sanctuary, and the sockets of the veil"
       NET: "the bases of the sanctuary... the bases of the special curtain"
       WEB: "the sockets of the sanctuary, and the sockets of the veil"
       Analysis: NET again uses "special curtain" instead of "veil" for Hebrew "paroket," and "bases" instead of "sockets" (ASV/WEB/KJV) for Hebrew "adanim" (the silver foundation blocks).

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Bronze Altar — The altar of burnt offering in the outer courtyard. Five
 cubits square, three cubits high. The place where animal sacrifices
 were burned. A type of the cross.

Laver — A bronze basin for priestly washing, made from women's mirrors.
 Priests washed hands and feet before serving. A type of
 sanctification through God's Word.

Courtyard — The enclosed area surrounding the tabernacle, 100 x 50 cubits,
 bounded by linen hangings on pillars. The public area of worship.

Bekah/Half-shekel — The census tax paid by every male twenty years and
 older. A redemption price that funded the silver bases of the
 tabernacle — literally building God's house on redemption.

Talent — Hebrew "kikkar" — approximately 75 pounds (34 kg). The largest
 unit of weight measurement in ancient Israel.

Ithamar — Aaron's youngest son, placed in charge of the Levites' service
 and the tabernacle inventory. Responsible for accountability.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. The Bronze Altar was the first thing encountered (v.1). There is no
 approach to God without sacrifice. How does the cross of Christ stand at
 the entrance of your relationship with God? Do you try to bypass it?

2. The women gave their mirrors to make the laver (v.8). They surrendered
 instruments of self-focus to create an instrument of cleansing. What
 "mirrors" (sources of vanity or self-absorption) might God be asking you
 to surrender for His purposes?

3. The materials inventory (vv.21-31) shows careful accounting. How do you
 practice transparency and accountability with the resources God has
 entrusted to you — time, money, influence?

4. Every Israelite male contributed a half-shekel (v.26) — rich and poor
 alike, the same amount. What does this teach about equal standing before
 God? How does the gospel level all distinctions?

5. The silver bases (foundation) came from the redemption money (v.27).
 God's dwelling literally rests on redemption. How is your life built on
 the foundation of Christ's redemptive work?
