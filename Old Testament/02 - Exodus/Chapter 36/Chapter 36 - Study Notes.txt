Exodus - Chapter 36
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
The craftsmen begin building the Tabernacle — its curtains, frames, and coverings — exactly as God commanded.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Exodus 36 records the beginning of actual tabernacle
construction and contains one of the most remarkable details in the entire
Pentateuch: the people gave so generously that Moses had to command them to
stop (vv.5–7). This is the only time in Scripture where God's people are
restrained from giving because there was more than enough. It stands as a
testimony to what Spirit-stirred generosity looks like in practice.

The chapter then details the construction of the tabernacle's fabric coverings
(vv.8–19), the wooden framework (vv.20–34), and the interior veil and entrance
screen (vv.35–38). These descriptions closely parallel the instructions given
in chapters 26, demonstrating that the craftsmen followed God's blueprint
precisely. The repeated phrase "as the LORD commanded" (found throughout
chapters 36–40) emphasizes faithful obedience to divine instruction.

The construction narrative may seem repetitive, but the repetition carries
theological weight. God's instructions (chs. 25–31) and Israel's execution
(chs. 35–40) mirror each other like creation itself: God speaks, and it is
done. Just as Genesis 1 records God speaking the world into existence, Exodus
35–40 records God's people building His dwelling according to His word. The
tabernacle is a new creation — a sacred space where heaven and earth meet.

Section Summaries:
vv.1–7 — Construction begins; the people give too much; Moses stops them.
vv.8–13 — Ten curtains of fine linen with cherubim; joined by gold clasps.
vv.14–19 — Goat hair curtains and outer coverings of rams' skins.
vv.20–34 — Acacia wood frames, silver bases, and gold-overlaid bars.
vv.35–38 — The inner veil and the entrance screen constructed.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Israel remains encamped at Mount Sinai. Bezalel and the craftsmen construct the Tabernacle curtains and frames.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2 — "Everyone whose heart stirred him up to come to do the work." (ESV)
 — The workers were internally motivated, not conscripted.

v.5 — "The people bring much more than enough for doing the work." (ESV) —
 Overflow generosity; more than sufficient provision.

v.6 — "Let no man or woman do anything more for the contribution for the
 sanctuary." So the people were restrained from bringing." (ESV) — The
 only time in Scripture people must be told to stop giving.

v.7 — "For the material they had was sufficient to do all the work, and
 more." (ESV) — God's provision through His people exceeds the need.

v.8 — "All the craftsmen among the workmen... made ten curtains." (ESV) —
 Spirit-filled skill applied to faithful construction.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/exodus-36/
  Guzik marvels at the overflow of giving and notes that this is what happens
  when God's Spirit genuinely moves hearts. He draws the parallel between
  God's creative word in Genesis and the obedient construction here.

Charles Spurgeon:
  Sermon: "More Than Enough" on vv.5-7 (MTP Vol. 19, No. 1108)
  "The people bring much more than enough! What a sentence! When did you
  last hear it in a church? The problem is never God's supply but our
  willingness. When hearts are truly stirred, hands open wide. The early
  church had all things common — not because they were commanded but because
  they were compelled by love. Oh, that God would so stir our hearts that
  the treasurer would cry, 'Enough! Bring no more!' That would be revival."

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1  — Hebrew "chokmah" — wisdom/skill.
       KJV: "the LORD hath put wisdom"
       ESV: "the LORD has put skill"
       ASV: "Jehovah hath put wisdom"
       NET: "the Lord has put skill"
       WEB: "Yahweh has put wisdom"
       Analysis: The divine name pattern continues: ASV "Jehovah," WEB "Yahweh," NET/ESV "the Lord." Additionally, ESV/NET render Hebrew "chokmah" as "skill" rather than "wisdom," emphasizing practical craftsmanship over abstract knowledge. ASV/WEB/KJV retain "wisdom" — the broader theological term.

v.5  — Hebrew "marbim... midday" — they multiply beyond sufficiency.
       KJV: "much more than enough"
       ESV: "much more than enough"
       ASV: "much more than enough"
       NET: "more than enough"
       WEB: "much more than enough"
       Analysis: The people's giving exceeded the need abundantly. All translations agree on the sense of overflow. NET slightly understates by dropping "much."

v.7  — Hebrew "vehoter" — and surplus, remainder.
       KJV: "too much"
       ESV: "and more"
       ASV: "too much"
       NET: "more than enough"
       WEB: "more than enough"
       Analysis: There was excess beyond what was needed — a holy surplus. KJV/ASV's "too much" and ESV's "and more" both capture the remarkable overflow.

v.8  — Hebrew "keruvim maʻaseh choshev" — cherubim of inventive/skillful work.
       KJV: "cherubims of cunning work"
       ESV: "cherubim skillfully worked"
       ASV: "cherubim, the work of the skilful workman"
       NET: "he made them with cherubim, the work of an artisan"
       WEB: "cherubim, the work of a skillful workman"
       Analysis: Woven into the fabric, not appliquéd on. KJV "cunning" = skillful (archaic). NET uses "artisan" (modern occupational language). The cherubim were integral to the linen weaving.

v.9  — Hebrew measurements in cubits.
       KJV: "eight and twenty cubits... four cubits"
       ESV: "twenty-eight cubits... four cubits"
       ASV: "eight and twenty cubits... four cubits"
       NET: "The length of one curtain was 42 feet, and the width was 6 feet"
       WEB: "twenty-eight cubits... four cubits"
       Analysis: NET converts all measurements to modern units (feet/inches) throughout the tabernacle chapters, while KJV/ESV/ASV/WEB retain the original Hebrew cubits. Each curtain was approximately 42 × 6 feet.

v.19 — Hebrew "tachash" — uncertain animal skin.
       KJV: "badgers' skins"
       ESV: "goatskins"
       ASV: "sealskins"
       NET: "fine leather"
       WEB: "sea cow hides"
       Analysis: The ongoing divergence over this uncertain term. NET's neutral "fine leather" avoids specifying the unknown animal, while others attempt identification. The outermost covering of the tabernacle.

v.20 — Hebrew "qeresh" — debated: boards or frames.
       KJV: "boards for the tabernacle"
       ESV: "frames for the tabernacle"
       ASV: "the boards for the tabernacle"
       NET: "He made the frames for the tabernacle"
       WEB: "He made the boards for the tabernacle"
       Analysis: NET/ESV use "frames" (suggesting an open lattice structure) while KJV/ASV/WEB use "boards" (solid planks). Hebrew "qeresh" is debated; modern scholarship increasingly favors "frames."

v.35 — Hebrew "paroket" — the inner curtain separating Holy Place from Most Holy.
       KJV: "he made a vail"
       ESV: "he made the veil"
       ASV: "he made the veil"
       NET: "He made the special curtain"
       WEB: "He made the veil"
       Analysis: NET avoids the traditional term "veil" in favor of "special curtain," making the function clearer for modern readers but losing the theological resonance of "veil" — torn at Christ's death (Matt 27:51). Paul's extended metaphor in 2 Corinthians 3 depends on this term.

v.37 — Hebrew "masak" — a screen/hanging for the entrance.
       KJV: "a hanging for the tabernacle door"
       ESV: "a screen for the entrance of the tent"
       ASV: "a screen for the door of the Tent"
       NET: "a hanging for the entrance of the tent"
       WEB: "a screen for the door of the tent"
       Analysis: NET/KJV use "hanging"; ESV/ASV/WEB use "screen." Both translate Hebrew "masak" — the outer entrance covering distinct from the inner "paroket" (veil). NET distinguishes between the inner "special curtain" and the outer "hanging."

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Restrained from bringing — The people's generosity was so abundant that
 Moses issued a command to stop. A unique event demonstrating what
 Spirit-stirred hearts produce.

Curtains — The innermost layer of the tabernacle, made of fine linen with
 cherubim woven in. Visible from inside, they formed the "ceiling"
 of God's dwelling.

Clasps/Taches — Gold or bronze fasteners joining curtain sections together,
 making the tabernacle "one" (v.13). Unity in construction
 reflecting unity of purpose.

Frames — Acacia wood uprights forming the walls of the tabernacle, overlaid
 with gold and set in silver bases. Structure supporting beauty.

Tenons — Projections on the bottom of each frame that fit into silver
 socket-bases, providing stability and connection.

Veil — The inner curtain of blue, purple, and scarlet with cherubim,
 separating the Holy Place from the Most Holy Place. Only the high
 priest passed through it, once a year.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. The people gave "much more than enough" and had to be restrained (vv.5-7).
 What would it look like for your church community to give with such
 generosity? What holds you back from giving with abandon?

2. The craftsmen came because their hearts "stirred them up" (v.2). Are you
 serving in God's work from internal motivation or external obligation?
 What stirs your heart toward service?

3. The construction followed God's blueprint exactly (paralleling ch. 26).
 How important is it to do God's work God's way? Where are you tempted to
 improvise rather than follow His instructions?

4. The tabernacle was made "one" through clasps joining the curtains (v.13).
 How does this picture the unity God desires in His church? What role do
 you play in holding the body together?

5. The veil separated the Holy Place from God's immediate presence (v.35).
 Christ's death tore that veil (Matt 27:51). How does open access to God
 change your daily approach to prayer and worship?
