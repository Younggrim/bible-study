Exodus - Chapter 24
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Israel ratifies the covenant with blood; Moses, Aaron, and the elders see God on the mountain; Moses ascends for forty days.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 24 is the formal ratification of the Sinai
covenant — one of the most extraordinary chapters in Scripture. It contains:
(1) The covenant ceremony with blood (vv.1-8); (2) The elders' vision of God
— they SAW God and lived (vv.9-11); (3) Moses' ascent for forty days to
receive the stone tablets (vv.12-18). The blood ceremony establishes the
covenant: half the blood on the altar (representing God), half on the people
— binding both parties. Jesus directly references this at the Last Supper:
"This is my blood of the new testament" (Matthew 26:28). The vision of God
on the sapphire pavement is one of the most remarkable theophanies in
Scripture — seventy-four men see God and eat and drink in His presence.

The Covenant Ceremony (vv.1-8): God invites Moses, Aaron, Nadab, Abihu, and
seventy elders to come up — but only Moses approaches God directly (vv.1-2).
Moses tells the people all God's words; they respond: "All the words which
the LORD hath said will we do" (v.3). Moses writes "all the words of the
LORD" (v.4) — Scripture being recorded. He builds an altar with twelve
pillars (v.4). Young men offer burnt offerings and peace offerings (v.5).
Half the blood goes on the altar; half is sprinkled on the people (vv.6, 8).
Moses reads the Book of the Covenant aloud; the people pledge again: "All
that the LORD hath said will we do, and be obedient" (v.7). Moses declares:
"Behold the blood of the covenant" (v.8).

The Vision of God (vv.9-11): The seventy-four go up and "they saw the God of
Israel" (v.10). Under His feet: "a paved work of a sapphire stone, and as it
were the body of heaven in his clearness" (v.10). "He laid not his hand" on
them — they survived (v.11). "They saw God, and did eat and drink" (v.11) —
covenant fellowship in God's presence.

Moses' Forty Days (vv.12-18): God calls Moses higher: "I will give thee
tables of stone" (v.12). Moses takes Joshua partway (v.13). Aaron and Hur
are left in charge (v.14). The cloud covers Sinai six days; on the seventh,
God calls Moses into the cloud (vv.15-16). God's glory appears as "devouring
fire" (v.17). Moses enters the cloud — forty days and forty nights (v.18).

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Mount Sinai: Moses, Aaron, Nadab, Abihu, and 70 elders ascend part of the mountain (vv.1, 9-11). Moses alone goes to the summit (v.12).
- Moses enters the cloud atop the mountain for 40 days and 40 nights (v.18). The glory of God appears "like devouring fire on the top of the mount" (v.17).

Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/exodus-24/

Charles Spurgeon:
  Sermon: "The Blood of the Covenant" on v.8 (MTP Vol. 35, No. 2091)
  "Behold the BLOOD of the covenant — Moses sprinkles blood on the people.
  They are bound to God by blood. And at the Last Supper, Jesus takes the
  cup and says the same words: 'This is my blood of the new covenant.' The
  old covenant was sealed with animal blood — temporary, repeated, symbolic.
  The new covenant is sealed with Christ's blood — eternal, once-for-all,
  effectual. But the principle is the same: no covenant without blood. No
  relationship with God without sacrifice. No forgiveness without shedding
  of blood (Hebrews 9:22). The blood binds us to God and God to us —
  permanently, irrevocably, eternally."

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1  — Hebrew "YHWH" — the divine name.
       KJV: "the LORD"
       ESV: "the LORD"
       ASV: "Come up unto Jehovah"
       NET: "Come up to the Lord"
       WEB: "Come up to Yahweh"
       Analysis: The divine name distinction is especially significant in
       this covenant ratification chapter. ASV uses "Jehovah" (traditional
       English rendering), WEB uses "Yahweh" (scholarly reconstruction of
       YHWH), and NET substitutes the title "the Lord." In a chapter where
       Israel formally enters covenant with God, whether His personal name
       is spoken carries relational weight.

v.3  — Hebrew "mishpatim" — judgments, ordinances, rules.
       KJV: "all the judgments"
       ESV: "all the rules"
       ASV: "all the ordinances"
       NET: "all the decisions"
       WEB: "all the ordinances"
       Analysis: NET uniquely renders "mishpatim" as "decisions" rather than
       "ordinances" (ASV/WEB) or "rules" (ESV). This emphasizes the judicial,
       case-law character — they are precedent-setting decisions, not
       abstract regulations.

v.4  — Hebrew "mattsevot" — standing stones/pillars.
       KJV: "twelve pillars"
       ESV: "twelve pillars"
       ASV: "twelve pillars"
       NET: "twelve standing stones"
       WEB: "twelve pillars"
       Analysis: NET's "standing stones" distinguishes these memorial markers
       from architectural pillars. The same Hebrew word "mattsevot" is used
       negatively for pagan standing stones (23:24) but here positively as
       covenant memorials — context determines meaning.

v.6  — Hebrew "vayizroq" — to dash, to throw, to sprinkle by throwing.
       KJV: "sprinkled on the altar"
       ESV: "threw against the altar"
       ASV: "sprinkled on the altar"
       NET: "splashed on the altar"
       WEB: "sprinkled on the altar"
       Analysis: ESV's "threw" and NET's "splashed" capture the forceful
       action better than KJV/ASV/WEB's gentler "sprinkled." Blood was
       dashed against the altar with force — a dramatic covenant ceremony.

v.7  — Hebrew "na'aseh venishma" — we will do and we will hear/obey.
       KJV: "will we do, and be obedient"
       ESV: "we will do, and we will be obedient"
       ASV: "will we do, and be obedient"
       NET: "We are willing to do and obey"
       WEB: "will we do, and be obedient"
       Analysis: NET's "We are willing" adds an element of volition not
       explicit in the Hebrew. ASV/WEB preserve the straightforward pledge.
       NET softens from an absolute vow to a declaration of willingness — a
       subtle but theologically significant difference.

v.8  — Hebrew "hinneh dam haberit" — behold the blood of the covenant.
       KJV: "Behold the blood of the covenant"
       ESV: "Behold the blood of the covenant"
       ASV: "Behold the blood of the covenant, which Jehovah hath made with you"
       NET: "This is the blood of the covenant that the Lord has made with you"
       WEB: "Look, this is the blood of the covenant, which Yahweh has made with you"
       Analysis: WEB's "Look" and ASV's "Behold" retain the Hebrew
       interjection "hinneh" — a dramatic call to attention. NET omits it.
       Jesus echoes these exact words at the Last Supper (Matthew 26:28),
       making the divine name choice here especially resonant.

v.10 — Hebrew "ma'aseh livnat hassappir" — a work/construction of sapphire
       brilliance.
       KJV: "a paved work of a sapphire stone, and as it were the body of heaven in his clearness"
       ESV: "a pavement of sapphire stone, like the very heaven for clearness"
       ASV: "a paved work of sapphire stone, and as it were the very heaven for clearness"
       NET: "something like a pavement made of sapphire, clear like the sky itself"
       WEB: "like a paved work of sapphire stone, like the skies for clearness"
       Analysis: NET's hedging language ("something like") reflects the
       difficulty of describing a theophany. ASV's "the very heaven for
       clearness" is the most evocative. All preserve "sapphire" (Hebrew
       "sappir," possibly lapis lazuli — a brilliant blue). "Sappir" may be
       lapis lazuli rather than modern sapphire.

v.11 — Hebrew "atsilei benei Yisra'el" — the nobles/chosen ones of Israel.
       KJV: "the nobles"
       ESV: "the chief men"
       ASV: "the nobles of the children of Israel"
       NET: "the leaders of the Israelites"
       WEB: "the nobles of the children of Israel"
       Analysis: NET modernizes to "leaders," losing the aristocratic dignity
       of "nobles" (KJV/ASV/WEB). ESV's "chief men" is more accessible. All
       record the astonishing statement: they saw God, and ate and drank —
       covenant fellowship in the divine presence, prefiguring the Lord's
       Supper.

v.12 — Hebrew "luchot ha'even" — tablets of stone.
       KJV: "tables of stone"
       ESV: "tablets of stone"
       ASV: "tables of stone"
       NET: "stone tablets"
       WEB: "stone tablets"
       Analysis: ESV/NET/WEB use "tablets" — the modern term. KJV/ASV use
       "tables" — archaic English for flat writing surfaces (not furniture).
       These are the Ten Commandments written by God's own finger.

v.13 — Hebrew "meshareto" — his attendant, his servant/minister.
       KJV: "his minister Joshua"
       ESV: "his assistant Joshua"
       ASV: "his minister Joshua"
       NET: "his servant Joshua"
       WEB: "his servant Joshua"
       Analysis: KJV/ASV's "minister" has religious connotations today; ESV's
       "assistant" is most neutral. NET/WEB's "servant" captures the Hebrew
       sense of one who serves. Joshua serves Moses as an aide/apprentice.

v.17 — Hebrew "esh okheleth" — fire that consumes/eats.
       KJV: "devouring fire"
       ESV: "devouring fire"
       ASV: "devouring fire"
       NET: "a consuming fire"
       WEB: "a devouring fire"
       Analysis: All translations are nearly identical. God's glory appears
       as fire that consumes everything it touches — terrifying to behold.
       NET's "consuming" is virtually synonymous with "devouring."

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Blood of the covenant — Blood that seals and ratifies the covenant between
               God and Israel. Half on the altar (God's side), half on the
               people (their side). Both are bound by blood. Fulfilled in
               Christ's blood (Matthew 26:28).

Book of the Covenant — The written record of God's laws (Exodus 20:22-23:33)
               read publicly to the people. The first formal "Scripture
               reading" in Israel's history.

Sapphire pavement — The floor beneath God's feet in the elders' vision.
               Brilliant blue, clear as heaven itself. A glimpse of God's
               throne room (compare Ezekiel 1:26, Revelation 4:6).

"They saw God, and did eat and drink" — Covenant fellowship. Eating together
               in God's presence = peace, acceptance, communion. A foretaste
               of the marriage supper of the Lamb (Revelation 19:9).

Forty days and nights — Moses' time on Sinai receiving the Law. Paralleled
               by Jesus' forty days in the wilderness (Matthew 4:2) and
               Elijah's forty days to Horeb (1 Kings 19:8).

Tables/Tablets of stone — The Ten Commandments written by God's own finger
               (31:18). Stone = permanence. God's writing = divine authority.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Behold the blood of the covenant" (v.8). Your relationship with God is
   sealed in blood — Christ's blood. It's not based on your performance but
   on His sacrifice. When you doubt your standing, look at the blood.

2. "They saw God, and did eat and drink" (v.11). God invites you into His
   presence — not just for worship but for fellowship. Communion (the Lord's
   Supper) echoes this: eating and drinking in God's presence. Do you
   approach the table with awe?

3. "All that the LORD hath said will we do, and be obedient" (v.7). Israel
   pledged obedience — and failed within forty days (the golden calf). Our
   promises to God are fragile. Thank God that the new covenant depends on
   HIS faithfulness, not ours.

4. Moses entered the cloud — the devouring fire (v.18). What terrified the
   people, Moses walked into. Intimacy with God requires going where others
   won't go. Are you willing to enter the cloud — the unknown, the
   uncomfortable — to be with God?

5. God wrote the Law Himself (v.12). Scripture is not human invention — it
   is God's own word, written by His own hand. Do you treat it with that
   level of authority and reverence?
