Exodus - Chapter 39
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
The priestly garments are completed — the ephod, breastplate, robe, and turban — and all the Tabernacle work is finished exactly as God commanded.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Exodus 39 records the making of the priestly garments and
the completion of all tabernacle work. The chapter closely parallels the
instructions given in chapter 28, demonstrating that the craftsmen followed
God's design for the priestly vestments with the same precision they applied to
the tabernacle structure. The phrase "as the LORD commanded Moses" appears seven
times in this chapter (vv.1, 5, 7, 21, 26, 29, 31) — a deliberate echo of the
seven days of creation, where God spoke and it was done.

The priestly garments were not mere clothing but theological statements worn on
the body. The ephod with its shoulder stones bore the names of Israel's twelve
tribes — the high priest literally carried the nation on his shoulders before
God. The breastplate with its twelve gems placed the tribes over the priest's
heart. The golden plate inscribed "HOLINESS TO THE LORD" crowned the turban —
declaring that the priest (and through him, the nation) was consecrated to God.

The chapter culminates with the completion of all work (v.32) and Moses'
inspection and blessing (v.43). The language deliberately echoes Genesis 1–2:
God saw all He had made and it was "very good"; Moses saw all they had made and
blessed them. The tabernacle is a new creation — a microcosm of Eden where God
dwells with His people. The work is finished; all that remains is the
inauguration.

Section Summaries:
vv.1–7 — The ephod made: gold, blue, purple, scarlet, linen; shoulder stones.
vv.8–21 — The breastplate made: twelve stones, chains, rings, blue lace.
vv.22–26 — The robe of the ephod: blue, with pomegranates and bells.
vv.27–29 — Tunics, turban, caps, undergarments, and sash.
vv.30–31 — The golden plate: "HOLINESS TO THE LORD."
vv.32–43 — All work completed; brought to Moses; Moses inspects and blesses.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Israel remains encamped at Mount Sinai. The priestly garments are completed and all work is brought to Moses for inspection.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.30 — "They made the plate of the holy crown of pure gold, and wrote on it
 an inscription, like the engraving of a signet, 'Holy to the LORD.'"
 (ESV) — The crown declaration: Israel's priest (and nation) belongs
 wholly to God.

v.32 — "Thus all the work of the tabernacle of the tent of meeting was
 finished." (ESV) — Completion; echoing "God finished His work"
 (Gen 2:2).

v.42 — "According to all that the LORD had commanded Moses, so the people of
 Israel had done all the work." (ESV) — Perfect obedience to divine
 instruction.

v.43 — "And Moses saw all the work, and behold, they had done it; as the
 LORD had commanded, so had they done it. Then Moses blessed them."
 (ESV) — Inspection, approval, and blessing — echoing God's "very
 good" in Genesis 1:31.

v.7 — "He put them on the shoulder pieces of the ephod to be stones of
 remembrance for the sons of Israel." (ESV) — The priest carries the
 nation before God; a picture of Christ bearing His people.

Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/exodus-39/
  Guzik draws the creation parallel: seven repetitions of "as the LORD
  commanded" echo the seven days of creation. Moses' inspection and blessing

Charles Spurgeon:
  Sermon: "Holiness to the Lord" on v.30 (MTP Vol. 9, No. 525)
  "HOLINESS TO THE LORD — written on the priest's forehead where all could
  see it. This is what God requires first and foremost: not cleverness, not
  eloquence, not success — holiness. And it was on a plate of pure gold,
  fastened with blue lace. Holiness is precious (gold) and heavenly (blue).
  It is not natural to us — it must be inscribed upon us by grace. But when
  it is there, it is the crown of all virtues, the beauty of the saint, the
  glory of the church."
  Sermon: "The Priest's Breastplate" on vv.8-21 (MTP Vol. 5, No. 283)
  "Twelve stones over the heart — every tribe represented, none forgotten.
  So Christ carries every believer upon His heart before the Father. Your
  name is there. Not on His shoulders only (where He bears your burdens)
  but on His heart (where He loves you). You are engraved upon the heart
  of your great High Priest."

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1 — KJV: "cloths of service" / ESV: "finely woven garments" — Hebrew
 "bigdey haserad" — garments of service/ministry. Special vestments
 worn for priestly duties in the Holy Place.

v.3 — KJV: "wires" / ESV: "threads" — Hebrew "petilim" — threads or cords.
 Gold was hammered thin and cut into threads to be woven into the
 fabric. Extraordinary craftsmanship.

v.5 — KJV: "curious girdle" / ESV: "skillfully woven band" — Hebrew
 "cheshev" — a skillfully woven band or sash. KJV "curious" meant
 "made with care/skill" (archaic). The waistband of the ephod.

v.23 — KJV: "habergeon" / ESV: "garment" — Hebrew "tachra" — a coat of
 mail or armored garment. The robe's neckline was reinforced like
 armor to prevent tearing. KJV uses the archaic military term.

v.28 — KJV: "mitre" / ESV: "turban" — Hebrew "mitsnephet" — a turban or
 head-wrapping. KJV "mitre" (from Latin mitra) and ESV "turban"
 both describe the priestly head covering.

v.28 — KJV: "goodly bonnets" / ESV: "caps" — Hebrew "migbaʻot" — caps or
 headbands for the ordinary priests (distinct from the high priest's
 turban). KJV "goodly bonnets" = fine caps.

v.30 — KJV: "HOLINESS TO THE LORD" / ESV: "Holy to the LORD" — Hebrew
 "qodesh laYHWH" — holiness/holy belonging to Yahweh. Both
 translations are valid; KJV emphasizes the abstract quality.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Ephod — The high priest's outer vestment of gold, blue, purple, scarlet,
 and fine linen. Worn over the robe, it held the breastplate and
 shoulder stones. A garment of glory and beauty.

Breastplate — Hebrew "choshen" — a square pouch of woven fabric set with
 twelve precious stones representing the twelve tribes. Worn over
 the priest's heart, carrying Israel before God.

Pomegranates and bells — Alternating decorations on the hem of the robe.
 Bells announced the priest's movements; pomegranates symbolized
 fruitfulness and life.

HOLINESS TO THE LORD — The inscription on the golden plate worn on the
 high priest's turban. Declared the priest's (and nation's) total
 consecration to God. The crown of the priestly office.

Urim and Thummim — "Lights and Perfections" — objects placed in the
 breastplate pouch for discerning God's will. Their exact nature
 is unknown; they provided divine guidance.

Moses blessed them — After inspecting the completed work and finding it
 faithful to God's commands, Moses pronounced blessing. Approval
 followed by benediction — echoing God's pattern in creation.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. The priest carried Israel's names on his shoulders (strength) and over his
 heart (love) — vv.6-7, 8-21. Christ carries you the same way. How does
 knowing you are borne on Christ's shoulders and engraved on His heart
 affect your sense of security?

2. "HOLINESS TO THE LORD" was the crown inscription (v.30). If there were an
 inscription on your life visible to all, what would it read? What would
 you want it to read? What needs to change?

3. "As the LORD commanded Moses" appears seven times. Faithful obedience to
 God's instructions — not innovation or improvement — is what God blesses.
 Where do you need to simply obey what God has already clearly said?

4. Moses inspected the work and blessed them (v.43). One day Christ will
 inspect your work (1 Cor 3:13-14). Are you building according to His
 pattern? Will your work stand the test?

5. The work was "finished" (v.32). There is a time for completion. What work
 has God given you that needs to be brought to completion? Are you
 persevering to the end?
