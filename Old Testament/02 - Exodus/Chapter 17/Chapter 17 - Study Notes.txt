Exodus - Chapter 17
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
God provides water from the rock at Horeb, and Israel defeats Amalek in battle as long as Moses' hands remain raised.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 17 records two critical events: (1) Water from
the rock at Rephidim — God provides from an impossible source; (2) The
battle with Amalek — Israel's first military engagement. Both events reveal
essential truths: God provides what nature cannot (water from rock), and
victory depends on God's power (Moses' raised hands), not human strength
alone. Joshua appears for the first time as a military leader. Moses names
the altar "Jehovah-nissi" — "The LORD is my banner." God declares perpetual
war against Amalek — the enemy who attacked Israel's weakest (Deuteronomy
25:17-18).

Water from the Rock (vv.1-7): Israel camps at Rephidim — no water (v.1).
They "chide" with Moses: "Give us water" (v.2). Moses: "Why do ye tempt the
LORD?" (v.2). The people are "almost ready to stone me" (v.4). God instructs:
take the rod, go to the rock at Horeb, "thou shalt smite the rock, and there
shall come water out of it" (v.6). God says "I will stand before thee there
upon the rock" — God positions Himself ON the rock that is struck (v.6).
Moses obeys; water flows (v.6). He names the place "Massah" (testing) and
"Meribah" (quarreling) (v.7). Israel's question: "Is the LORD among us, or
not?" (v.7).

The Battle with Amalek (vv.8-16): Amalek attacks (v.8) — descendants of
Esau, attacking Israel's rear (Deuteronomy 25:18). Moses sends Joshua to
fight and goes to the hilltop with the rod of God (v.9). When Moses' hands
are raised, Israel prevails; when lowered, Amalek prevails (v.11). Moses'
hands grow heavy — Aaron and Hur support them on a stone until sunset (v.12).
Joshua defeats Amalek (v.13). God commands: "Write this for a memorial" —
the first command to write Scripture (v.14). God declares: "I will utterly
put out the remembrance of Amalek" (v.14). Moses builds an altar: "Jehovah-
nissi" — "The LORD is my banner" (v.15).

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Rephidim (v.1): The camp before Sinai, where there is no water. God commands Moses to strike the rock at Horeb.
- Horeb / the rock (v.6): God stands on the rock; Moses strikes it and water flows. The site is named Massah ("testing") and Meribah ("quarreling").
- Rephidim is also where Amalek attacks (v.8) — Israel's first battle after the Exodus.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.6  — "Strike the rock, and water shall come out of it." (ESV) — God
        provides from impossible sources.

v.7  — "Is the LORD among us or not?" (ESV) — Israel's faithless question.

v.11 — "Whenever Moses held up his hand, Israel prevailed, and whenever he
        lowered his hand, Amalek prevailed." (ESV) — Victory depends on
        God's power, not human strength alone.

v.12 — "Aaron and Hur held up his hands." (ESV) — The necessity of community
        support in spiritual warfare.

v.15 — "The LORD Is My Banner." (ESV) — God as our rallying point and
        source of victory.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/exodus-17/
  Guzik covers the rock as a type of Christ (smitten once, living water
  flows), Moses' raised hands as intercession, and Amalek as a type of the
  flesh that wars against the Spirit.

Charles Spurgeon:
  Sermon: "The Smitten Rock" on v.6 (MTP Vol. 9, No. 487)
  "Smite the rock — and God stands UPON the rock when it is smitten. Do you
  see it? God receives the blow. The rock is Christ (1 Corinthians 10:4).
  The rod is the law's judgment. God stands on the rock — God IN Christ
  receives the stroke of justice. And what flows? WATER — life, refreshment,
  salvation. Christ was smitten ONCE on the cross — and living water has
  flowed ever since. You need not smite Him again. The rock is already
  broken. The water already flows. Come and drink."

  Sermon: "Jehovah Nissi" on v.15 (MTP Vol. 20, No. 1178)
  "The LORD is my BANNER — not my sword, not my shield only, but my banner.
  A banner is what you rally around. A banner identifies whose army you
  belong to. A banner is lifted HIGH for all to see. God is our rallying
  point. We fight under His flag. And when the battle is won, it is His
  banner that flies over the field — not ours. The victory belongs to the
  One whose banner we carry."

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.2  — Hebrew "riv" / "tenassun et-YHWH" — to contend, to quarrel / to test God.
       KJV: "chide" / "Wherefore do ye tempt the LORD?"
       ESV: "quarreled" / "Why do you test the LORD?"
       ASV: "strove" / "Wherefore do ye tempt Jehovah?"
       NET: "contended" / "Why do you test the Lord?"
       WEB: "quarreled" / "Why do you test Yahweh?"
       Analysis: The ESV/WEB's "quarreled" is most accessible; the KJV's "chide"
       is archaic. ASV's "tempt" means "test" here (not seduce). All capture Moses
       reframing their complaint about water as a theological challenge against God.
       Israel puts God on trial.

v.6  — Hebrew "hikkita vatsur" — strike the rock.
       KJV: "smite the rock"
       ESV: "strike the rock"
       ASV: "smite the rock"
       NET: "strike the rock"
       WEB: "strike the rock"
       Analysis: All equivalent. The rock is struck ONCE — water flows. In Numbers
       20, Moses strikes again (wrongly). God says "I will stand before thee there
       upon the rock" — God positions Himself ON the rock that is struck. The rock
       is Christ (1 Corinthians 10:4).

v.7  — Hebrew "Massah... Meribah" — testing... quarreling/contention.
       KJV: "Massah, and Meribah"
       ESV: "Massah and Meribah"
       ASV: "Massah, and Meribah"
       NET: "Massah and Meribah"
       WEB: "Massah, and Meribah"
       Analysis: All transliterate. Israel's faithless question follows: "Is the
       LORD among us, or not?" The irony is pronounced: God's visible pillar stands
       over them while they ask if He is present. ASV renders "Is Jehovah among us?"
       / WEB "Is Yahweh among us?" / NET "Is the Lord among us?"

v.9  — Hebrew "Yehoshua" — "YHWH saves."
       KJV: "Joshua"
       ESV: "Joshua"
       ASV: "Joshua"
       NET: "Joshua"
       WEB: "Joshua"
       Analysis: All identical. First appearance of Moses' successor. His name is
       the Hebrew form of "Jesus." He appears as a military leader — fitting, as
       Jesus is our warrior.

v.11 — Hebrew "yarim Mosheh yado" — Moses raised his hand(s).
       KJV: "held up his hand"
       ESV: "held up his hands"
       ASV: "held up his hand"
       NET: "held his hands up"
       WEB: "held up his hand"
       Analysis: The ESV/NET use plural "hands"; KJV/ASV/WEB use singular. The
       raised hand with the rod represents dependence on God's power. When raised,
       Israel prevails; when lowered, Amalek prevails.

v.13 — Hebrew "vayachalosh" — to weaken, disable, overwhelm.
       KJV: "Joshua discomfited Amalek"
       ESV: "Joshua overwhelmed Amalek"
       ASV: "Joshua discomfited Amalek"
       NET: "Joshua destroyed Amalek and his army with the sword"
       WEB: "Joshua defeated Amalek"
       Analysis: ASV's "discomfited" (archaic for routed), WEB's "defeated," and
       NET's "destroyed" represent increasing intensity. The Hebrew suggests
       weakening/overpowering rather than total annihilation. ESV's "overwhelmed"
       captures the Hebrew well.

v.14 — Hebrew "macho emcheh" — utterly blotting out.
       KJV: "I will utterly put out the remembrance of Amalek from under heaven"
       ESV: "I will utterly blot out the memory of Amalek from under heaven"
       ASV: "I will utterly blot out the remembrance of Amalek from under heaven"
       NET: "I will surely wipe out the remembrance of Amalek from under heaven"
       WEB: "I will utterly blot out the memory of Amalek from under the sky"
       Analysis: WEB uses "sky" for "shamayim"; all others use "heaven." NET's
       "wipe out" renders the emphatic Hebrew infinitive absolute ("macho emcheh").
       God commands Moses to write this — the first command to write Scripture.

v.15 — Hebrew "YHWH nissi" — The LORD is my banner.
       KJV: "Jehovah-nissi"
       ESV: "The LORD Is My Banner"
       ASV: "Jehovah-nissi"
       NET: "The Lord is my Banner"
       WEB: "Yahweh our Banner"
       Analysis: KJV/ASV transliterate the compound divine name; ESV/NET fully
       translate; WEB translates with "Yahweh" retained. "Nissi" (my banner/
       standard) is a military term — the rallying flag in battle. A banner
       identifies whose army you belong to.

v.16 — Hebrew "kes Yah" — an obscure phrase, debated among scholars.
       KJV: "the LORD hath sworn"
       ESV: "A hand upon the throne of the LORD!"
       ASV: "Jehovah hath sworn"
       NET: "For a hand was lifted up to the throne of the Lord"
       WEB: "Yah has sworn"
       Analysis: WEB uses the abbreviated "Yah" (as in Hallelujah). NET diverges
       entirely, rendering the notoriously difficult Hebrew phrase ("kes Yah") as
       "hand lifted to the throne." The Hebrew is obscure — this is one of the most
       debated phrases in Exodus. KJV/ASV/WEB take it as an oath; ESV/NET see a
       reference to God's throne.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
The Rock       — A type of Christ. Smitten once, living water flows.
               "That Rock was Christ" (1 Corinthians 10:4). Christ was
               struck once on the cross — and life flows to all who drink.

Massah and Meribah — "Testing" and "Quarreling." Named for Israel's sin of
               doubting God's presence. A perpetual warning against testing
               God (Psalm 95:8-9).

Amalek         — Descendants of Esau. They attacked Israel's weakest —
               the stragglers at the rear (Deuteronomy 25:18). A type of
               the flesh that wars against the Spirit. God declares
               perpetual war against them.

Jehovah-nissi  — "The LORD is my banner." God as the rallying point in
               battle. Victory belongs to Him; we fight under His standard.

Moses' raised hands — Intercession and dependence on God. When hands are
               raised (faith active), Israel prevails. When lowered (faith
               weakens), the enemy prevails.

Aaron and Hur  — They support Moses' weary hands. A picture of community
               in spiritual warfare — we need others to hold us up when
               we're exhausted.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Thou shalt smite the rock, and there shall come water out of it" (v.6).
   God provides from impossible sources. The rock that looks dead and dry
   contains living water. Where do you need God to provide from an
   impossible source?

2. "Is the LORD among us, or not?" (v.7). Israel asked this AFTER the Red
   Sea, AFTER the manna, AFTER the pillar of fire. How quickly we forget.
   Don't let present thirst erase the memory of past provision.

3. "When Moses held up his hand, Israel prevailed" (v.11). Your spiritual
   posture matters. Prayer, faith, dependence on God — these determine the
   outcome of your battles. Are your hands raised or lowered?

4. "Aaron and Hur stayed up his hands" (v.12). Moses couldn't do it alone.
   Neither can you. Who is holding up your arms? Whose arms are you holding
   up? We need each other in the fight.

5. "Jehovah-nissi" — "The LORD is my banner" (v.15). Whose banner are you
   fighting under? Whose flag flies over your life? Rally to God — He is
   your identity, your cause, and your victory.
