Exodus - Chapter 7
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Moses and Aaron confront Pharaoh; Aaron's rod becomes a serpent and swallows the magicians' rods, then the first plague turns the Nile to blood.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 7 begins the plague narrative — God's systematic
judgment on Egypt and its gods. God establishes the relationship between
Moses, Aaron, and Pharaoh: Moses is "a god to Pharaoh" and Aaron is his
"prophet" (v.1). The chapter records the sign of Aaron's rod becoming a
serpent (swallowing the magicians' serpents) and the first plague: water
turned to blood. Each plague targets a specific Egyptian deity — the Nile
(Hapi, the Nile god) is struck first. The magicians can imitate but not
reverse the plague. Pharaoh's heart hardens. The battle between YHWH and
Egypt's gods has begun.

God's Commission Restated (vv.1-7): God makes Moses "a god to Pharaoh" —
His authoritative representative (v.1). Aaron is Moses' "prophet" —
spokesman (v.1). God forewarns: "I will harden Pharaoh's heart" (v.3) so
that He may "multiply my signs and my wonders" (v.3). Purpose: "The
Egyptians shall KNOW that I am the LORD" (v.5). Moses is 80, Aaron 83 (v.7).

The Sign of the Serpent (vv.8-13): Pharaoh demands a miracle (v.9). Aaron
casts his rod — it becomes a serpent (v.10). Pharaoh's magicians (Jannes and
Jambres — 2 Timothy 3:8) replicate it (v.11). But Aaron's rod SWALLOWS
theirs (v.12) — God's power consumes counterfeit power. Pharaoh's heart
hardens (v.13).

The First Plague: Water to Blood (vv.14-25): God sends Moses to meet Pharaoh
at the Nile in the morning (v.15). The demand: "Let my people go" (v.16).
The warning: "In this thou shalt know that I am the LORD" (v.17). Aaron
strikes the Nile — all water becomes blood (vv.19-20). Fish die, the river
stinks, Egyptians cannot drink (v.21). The magicians replicate it (v.22) —
they can make the problem worse but cannot fix it. Pharaoh is unmoved (v.23).
The plague lasts seven days (v.25).

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Egypt — Pharaoh's court: The confrontation between Moses/Aaron and Pharaoh continues.
- The Nile River (v.17): Site of the first plague — water turned to blood. The Nile was worshipped as a god (Hapi); God strikes at the heart of Egypt's religion and economy.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "See, I have made you like God to Pharaoh, and your brother Aaron
        shall be your prophet." (ESV) — God's authority structure for the confrontation.

v.3  — "I will harden Pharaoh's heart, and though I multiply my signs and
        wonders in the land of Egypt." (ESV) — God's sovereign purpose in the plagues.

v.5  — "The Egyptians shall know that I am the LORD." (ESV) — The purpose of
        judgment: revelation of God's identity.

v.12 — "Aaron's staff swallowed up their staffs." (ESV) — God's power consumes
        counterfeit power.

v.17 — "By this you shall know that I am the LORD." (ESV) — Each plague answers
        Pharaoh's question: "Who is the LORD?" (5:2).

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1  — Hebrew "elohim le-Pharaoh" — a god to Pharaoh.
       KJV: "a god to Pharaoh"
       ESV: "like God to Pharaoh"
       ASV: "as God to Pharaoh"
       NET: "like God to Pharaoh"
       WEB: "as God to Pharaoh"
       Analysis: ESV/NET add "like," softening the statement. KJV is most
       direct. ASV/WEB use "as" — preserving the bolder Hebrew construction
       where Moses IS God's representative, not merely similar to one. Moses
       stands before Pharaoh with God's full backing and authority.

v.3  — Hebrew "aqsheh" — to make hard, to stiffen.
       KJV: "I will harden Pharaoh's heart"
       ESV: "I will harden Pharaoh's heart"
       ASV: "I will harden Pharaoh's heart"
       NET: "I will harden Pharaoh's heart"
       WEB: "I will harden Pharaoh's heart"
       Analysis: All translations identical. In the plague narrative, three
       different Hebrew words are used: "chazaq" (strengthen), "kaved"
       (make heavy), "qashah" (make hard). This verse uses "qashah" — the
       most severe term, implying stubbornness beyond reason.

v.4  — Hebrew "tsiv'ot" — hosts/armies.
       KJV: "my armies"
       ESV: "my hosts"
       ASV: "my hosts"
       NET: "my regiments, my people the Israelites"
       WEB: "my armies"
       Analysis: NET's "regiments" suggests organized military units — unique
       among translations. ASV/ESV preserve "hosts" — the theological term
       used in "LORD of hosts." KJV/WEB use "armies." Israel leaves Egypt as
       an organized force, not a fleeing mob.

v.9  — Hebrew "tannin" — a large serpent, dragon, or sea monster.
       KJV: "a serpent"
       ESV: "a serpent"
       ASV: "a serpent"
       NET: "a snake"
       WEB: "a serpent"
       Analysis: NET uses the common term "snake," while all others retain
       "serpent." Hebrew "tannin" is different from "nachash" (serpent) in
       4:3 — it can mean sea monster or dragon. Possibly connected to
       Egyptian dragon/crocodile imagery — Pharaoh's own royal symbol
       (uraeus) turned against him.

v.11 — Hebrew "b'lahateihem" — by their enchantments/flames (from "lahat" — to blaze).
       KJV: "with their enchantments"
       ESV: "by their secret arts"
       ASV: "with their enchantments"
       NET: "by their secret arts"
       WEB: "with their enchantments"
       Analysis: ESV/NET interpret with "secret arts" — emphasizing the
       hiddenness of the magic. KJV/ASV/WEB retain "enchantments." The
       Hebrew root suggests fire/flames — possibly pointing to the demonic
       power behind their tricks. The magicians can imitate but not reverse.

v.14 — Hebrew "kaved lev Pharaoh" — heavy is the heart of Pharaoh.
       KJV: "Pharaoh's heart is hardened"
       ESV: "Pharaoh's heart is hardened"
       ASV: "Pharaoh's heart is stubborn"
       NET: "Pharaoh's heart is hard"
       WEB: "Pharaoh's heart is stubborn"
       Analysis: Hebrew "kaved" means heavy/weighty. ASV/WEB's "stubborn"
       interprets the weight as resistance. KJV/ESV use "hardened."
       NET's "hard" is most concise. The "heavy heart" imagery connects to
       Egyptian belief — the heart weighed against ma'at (truth) after death.

v.17 — Hebrew "nehepku ledam" — transformed into blood.
       KJV: "they shall be turned to blood"
       ESV: "they shall turn into blood"
       ASV: "they shall be turned to blood"
       NET: "they will be turned into blood"
       WEB: "they will be turned to blood"
       Analysis: All translations are essentially equivalent. Whether literal
       blood or a blood-like appearance is debated — but the text presents
       it as supernatural, and the fish dying confirms it was lethal. The
       Nile god Hapi is struck at Egypt's heart.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
"A god to Pharaoh" — Moses' role: God's authoritative representative.
               Pharaoh claims to be a god; God makes Moses function as one
               before him — with real divine power behind him.

Hardening of heart — God's judicial action confirming Pharaoh's own choice.
               Three Hebrew words used: strengthen, make heavy, make hard.
               Pharaoh hardens himself; God confirms the hardening.

The Plagues    — Ten judgments on Egypt, each targeting an Egyptian deity.
               Purpose: to demonstrate YHWH's supremacy, answer "Who is the
               LORD?" (5:2), and compel Israel's release.

Hapi           — Egyptian god of the Nile and its annual flooding. The first
               plague strikes Egypt's most sacred river — their source of
               life becomes death.

Jannes and Jambres — Traditional names of Pharaoh's magicians (2 Timothy
               3:8). They can imitate God's signs but cannot reverse them
               — counterfeit power has limits.

"Tannin" (serpent/dragon) — A large reptilian creature. Possibly connected
               to Pharaoh's own royal symbol — the uraeus serpent/cobra on
               his crown. God turns Pharaoh's symbol against him.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "The Egyptians shall know that I am the LORD" (v.5). God's judgments have
   a revelatory purpose — they reveal who He is. Even in difficult
   circumstances, God is making Himself known. Are you paying attention?

2. "Aaron's rod swallowed up their rods" (v.12). God's power doesn't merely
   compete with counterfeit power — it consumes it. Whatever false thing
   you're trusting in, God's truth will ultimately swallow it. Trust the
   real over the imitation.

3. The magicians could imitate but not reverse (v.22). Satan can make
   problems worse but cannot solve them. Only God can turn blood back to
   water, death back to life, bondage back to freedom. Where are you looking
   for solutions — to counterfeits or to God?

4. "Pharaoh turned and went into his house, neither did he set his heart to
   this" (v.23). Pharaoh saw the miracle and walked away unmoved. You can
   witness God's power and still refuse to respond. Don't harden your heart
   to what God is showing you.

5. Moses is 80 years old (v.7). God's greatest work through Moses begins at
   80. It's never too late for God to use you. Your age, your past failures,
   your "uncircumcised lips" — none of it disqualifies you when God
   commissions you.
