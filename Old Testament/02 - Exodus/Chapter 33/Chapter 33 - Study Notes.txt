Exodus - Chapter 33
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
God threatens to withdraw His presence; Moses intercedes and asks to see God's glory; God reveals Himself while hiding Moses in the rock.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Exodus 33 follows the golden calf disaster and records the
crisis of God's presence. God tells Israel He will send an angel but will not
go with them Himself, lest His holiness consume them (v.3). This chapter
captures the tension between God's justice (which demands distance from sin)
and His grace (which ultimately restores relationship). It is one of the most
intimate chapters in the Old Testament regarding the relationship between God
and Moses.

The chapter reveals three movements: the threat of God's withdrawal (vv.1–6),
the tent of meeting where God speaks with Moses "face to face, as a man
speaketh unto his friend" (vv.7–11), and Moses' bold requests — first for
God's presence, then for a vision of God's glory (vv.12–23). Moses refuses to
move forward without God's presence, understanding that what distinguishes
Israel from all other nations is not their strength or numbers but God's
presence among them.

The climax is Moses' request "Shew me thy glory" (v.18) — the deepest desire
of the human heart expressed in four words. God's response reveals both His
accessibility and His transcendence: He will show His goodness and proclaim His
name, but Moses cannot see His face and live. God places Moses in the cleft of
the rock and covers him with His hand — a stunning picture of divine protection
that enables encounter with the Holy.

Section Summaries:
vv.1–6 — God threatens to withdraw His presence; the people mourn.
vv.7–11 — The tent of meeting; God speaks with Moses face to face.
vv.12–17 — Moses pleads for God's presence; God agrees to go with them.
vv.18–23 — Moses asks to see God's glory; God reveals His plan for the cleft.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Mount Sinai / the camp: God threatens to withdraw His presence from among Israel. The "tent of meeting" is pitched "without the camp, afar off" (v.7) — symbolizing the distance sin creates.
- "The cleft of the rock" (v.22): God places Moses in a rock on Sinai to reveal His glory.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.11 — "Thus the LORD used to speak to Moses face to face, as a man speaks
 to his friend." (ESV) — The extraordinary intimacy of Moses'
 relationship with God; unparalleled in the OT.

v.14 — "My presence will go with you, and I will give you rest." (ESV) —
 God's gracious reversal; His presence is the answer to every need.

v.15 — "If your presence will not go with me, do not bring us up from
 here." (ESV) — Moses values God's presence above the Promised Land itself.

v.18 — "Please show me your glory." (ESV) — The ultimate human request;
 the deepest longing of the redeemed heart.

v.19 — "I will make all my goodness pass before you and will proclaim before
 you my name 'The LORD.'" (ESV) — God's glory is revealed through
 His goodness and His name (character).

v.22 — "I will put you in a cleft of the rock, and I will cover you with my
 hand while I pass by." (ESV) — Divine protection enabling divine
 encounter; a picture of grace.

Charles Spurgeon:
  Sermon: "Show Me Thy Glory" on v.18 (MTP Vol. 34, No. 2029)
  "Show me thy glory! It is the cry of every renewed heart. We have tasted
  and we want more. We have seen the skirts of His garment and we long to
  see His face. But notice — God did not rebuke the request. He did not say,
  'How dare you ask so great a thing?' He said, 'I will make all my goodness
  pass before thee.' God loves to be wanted. He delights in the soul that
  pants after Him. Ask largely. Ask boldly. He will answer beyond your
  asking — though not always in the way you expect."

  and live. Apart from Christ, the glory would consume us. In Christ, the
  glory transforms us."

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1  — Hebrew "YHWH" — the divine covenant name.
       KJV: "the LORD said unto Moses"
       ESV: "the LORD said to Moses"
       ASV: "And Jehovah spake unto Moses"
       NET: "The Lord said to Moses"
       WEB: "Yahweh spoke to Moses"
       Analysis: The divine name pattern throughout this intimate chapter. ASV uses "Jehovah" (vv.1, 5, 7, 9, 11, 17, 19, 21); WEB uses "Yahweh"; NET uses "the Lord." Notably in v.19, where God proclaims His own name, ASV has "the name of Jehovah," WEB has "Yahweh's name," and NET has "the Lord by name."

v.3  — Hebrew "qesheh-ʻoreph" — hard of neck; "pen-ʼakhelka" — lest I consume.
       KJV: "for thou art a stiffnecked people: lest I consume thee in the way"
       ESV: "for you are a stiff-necked people, lest I consume you on the way"
       ASV: "for thou art a stiffnecked people, lest I consume thee in the way"
       NET: "for you are a stiff-necked people, and I might destroy you on the way"
       WEB: "for you are a stiff-necked people, lest I consume you on the way"
       Analysis: ASV/WEB/KJV/ESV retain the purposive "lest I consume" (Hebrew "pen-ʼakhelka"); NET renders as "I might destroy" — less of a warning and more of a possibility statement. "Consume" (KJV/ASV/WEB/ESV) captures the devouring fire of God's holiness; NET's "destroy" is flatter.

v.4  — Hebrew "hadavar haraʻ" — the evil/bad word/matter.
       KJV: "these evil tidings"
       ESV: "this disastrous word"
       ASV: "these evil tidings"
       NET: "this troubling word"
       WEB: "this evil news"
       Analysis: ASV/KJV's "evil tidings" is archaic but close; WEB modernizes to "evil news." NET softens "evil" to "troubling" — losing the Hebrew force of "raʻ" (genuinely bad/harmful), which emphasizes how devastating the threatened withdrawal of God's presence truly was.

v.7  — Hebrew "ʼohel" — tent. NOT the tabernacle (mishkan).
       KJV: "the tabernacle of the congregation"
       ESV: "the tent of meeting"
       ASV: "the Tent of meeting"
       NET: "the tent of meeting"
       WEB: "the Tent of Meeting"
       Analysis: This is NOT the tabernacle (mishkan) of chapters 25–31 (not yet built). It was a temporary tent Moses used for meeting with God. KJV's "tabernacle of the congregation" is misleading; all modern translations correctly distinguish it as "tent of meeting."

v.11 — Hebrew "panim ʼel-panim" — face to face (idiom for intimate communication).
       KJV: "the LORD spake unto Moses face to face, as a man speaketh unto his friend"
       ESV: "the LORD used to speak to Moses face to face, as a man speaks to his friend"
       ASV: "And Jehovah spake unto Moses face to face, as a man speaketh unto his friend"
       NET: "The Lord would speak to Moses face to face, the way a person speaks to a friend"
       WEB: "Yahweh spoke to Moses face to face, as a man speaks to his friend"
       Analysis: NET adds "would speak" (habitual/frequentative), correctly reflecting the Hebrew imperfect tense indicating repeated action. ASV/WEB use simple past. NET also generalizes "man" to "person" — a stylistic modernization. The idiom describes relational closeness, not full visual revelation (cf. v.20).

v.14 — Hebrew "panay" — literally "my face" (God's personal presence).
       KJV: "My presence shall go with thee, and I will give thee rest"
       ESV: "My presence will go with you, and I will give you rest"
       ASV: "My presence shall go with thee, and I will give thee rest"
       NET: "My presence will go with you, and I will give you rest"
       WEB: "My presence will go with you, and I will give you rest"
       Analysis: All translations agree. God's "face" going with them means His personal, immediate presence — not merely an angel or representative.

v.19 — Hebrew "channoti ʼet-ʼasher ʼachon" — sovereign grace.
       KJV: "I will make all my goodness pass before thee, and I will proclaim the name of the LORD before thee"
       ESV: "I will make all my goodness pass before you and will proclaim before you my name 'The LORD'"
       ASV: "I will make all my goodness pass before thee, and will proclaim the name of Jehovah before thee"
       NET: "I will make all my goodness pass before your face, and I will proclaim the Lord by name before you"
       WEB: "I will make all my goodness pass before you, and will proclaim Yahweh's name before you"
       Analysis: A theologically crucial verse. NET adds "before your face" (translating "ʻal-paneycha" literally). The divine name in the proclamation: ASV's "the name of Jehovah" and WEB's "Yahweh's name" make God's covenant identity explicit where NET's "the Lord by name" is less direct. God reveals His glory through goodness and name (character).

v.20 — Hebrew "lo-yir'ani haʼadam vachai" — man shall not see me and live.
       KJV: "Thou canst not see my face: for there shall no man see me, and live"
       ESV: "you cannot see my face, for man shall not see me and live"
       ASV: "Thou canst not see my face; for man shall not see me and live"
       NET: "You cannot see my face, for no one can see me and live"
       WEB: "You cannot see my face, for man may not see me and live"
       Analysis: ASV/KJV's "man shall not" implies a divine decree; WEB's "may not" suggests permission denied; NET's "no one can" emphasizes impossibility. The theological weight shifts subtly: decree vs. prohibition vs. inherent limitation of creaturely finitude before infinite holiness.

v.22 — Hebrew "niqrat hatsur" — a crevice or hollow in the rock.
       KJV: "I will put thee in a clift of the rock"
       ESV: "I will put you in a cleft of the rock"
       ASV: "I will put thee in a cleft of the rock"
       NET: "I will put you in a cleft in the rock"
       WEB: "I will put you in a cleft of the rock"
       Analysis: Identical in meaning across all five. Note the slight grammatical difference: KJV/ESV/ASV/WEB say "cleft of the rock" (the rock possessing the cleft); NET says "cleft in the rock" (locational). Minor but shows NET's preference for natural modern English. A place of shelter and protection.

v.23 — Hebrew "ʼachoray" — my back, my aftereffects.
       KJV: "thou shalt see my back parts: but my face shall not be seen"
       ESV: "you shall see my back, but my face shall not be seen"
       ASV: "thou shalt see my back; but my face shall not be seen"
       NET: "you will see my back, but my face must not be seen"
       WEB: "you will see my back; but my face shall not be seen"
       Analysis: NET's "must not be seen" conveys prohibition; KJV/ASV/WEB/ESV's "shall not be seen" is more declarative. All render "ʼachoray" as "back" — the trailing radiance, the afterglow of God's passing glory rather than His unveiled face. Moses sees the aftereffects of God's passing.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Face to face — An idiom for direct, personal, intimate communication. Does
 not contradict v.20 ("no man can see my face and live") — it
 describes relational closeness, not full visual revelation.

Tent of meeting — A temporary structure Moses pitched outside the camp for
 communion with God. Distinct from the tabernacle (not yet built).
 Foreshadows the permanent tabernacle's function.

Glory — Hebrew "kavod" — weight, heaviness, splendor. The visible
 manifestation of God's presence, character, and majesty. The
 fullness of who God is made perceptible.

Cleft of the rock — A crevice in the rock where Moses was sheltered while
 God's glory passed. Typologically points to Christ as the Rock in
 whom believers are hidden (Col 3:3).

Goodness — Hebrew "tuv" — all that is good, beautiful, and beneficial in
 God's character. God's glory is revealed through His goodness.

Sovereign mercy — God's freedom to bestow grace according to His own will,
 not human merit. "I will be gracious to whom I will be gracious."

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Moses said, "If thy presence go not with me, carry us not up hence" (v.15).
 Do you value God's presence more than His blessings? Would you choose a
 harder path with God over an easier path without Him?

2. God spoke with Moses "as a man speaketh unto his friend" (v.11). What
 characterized Moses' relationship with God that made this possible? What
 would it look like to pursue that kind of intimacy with God?

3. "Shew me thy glory" (v.18). What is the deepest desire of your heart? Is
 it for God Himself, or for what God can give you? How do you cultivate a
 hunger for God's presence?

4. God placed Moses in the cleft of the rock (v.22). How does being "in
 Christ" enable you to encounter God's holiness without being consumed?
 What does it mean to be hidden in the Rock?

5. God revealed His glory through His "goodness" and His "name" (v.19). How
 does this reshape your understanding of glory — not just power and
 splendor, but character and kindness?
