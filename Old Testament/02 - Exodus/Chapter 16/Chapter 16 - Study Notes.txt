Exodus - Chapter 16
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
God provides manna from heaven and quail for Israel in the wilderness, teaching them daily dependence on Him.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 16 records God's provision of manna and quail —
bread from heaven and meat for the evening. One month after leaving Egypt
(v.1), Israel murmurs about food, romanticizing slavery: "when we sat by the
flesh pots" (v.3). God responds not with rebuke but with provision — yet the
provision is also a test: "that I may prove them, whether they will walk in
my law, or no" (v.4). The manna teaches daily dependence (gather daily, not
hoarding), Sabbath rest (double portion on the sixth day, none on the
seventh), and trust in God's sufficiency. Jesus identifies Himself as the
true manna: "I am the bread of life" (John 6:35, 48-51).

Israel's Murmuring (vv.1-3): One month out of Egypt (v.1). The whole
congregation murmurs (v.2). They wish they had died in Egypt: "when we sat
by the flesh pots, and when we did eat bread to the full" (v.3) — selective
memory romanticizes bondage.

God's Provision Announced (vv.4-12): "I will rain bread from heaven" (v.4).
Purpose: "that I may prove them" (v.4). Double portion on the sixth day for
Sabbath (v.5). Moses clarifies: "your murmurings are not against us, but
against the LORD" (v.8). God's glory appears in the cloud (v.10). Promise:
quail at evening, bread in the morning (v.12).

The Manna (vv.13-21): Quail covers the camp at evening (v.13). In the
morning: "a small round thing, as small as the hoar frost" (v.14). "It is
manna" — Hebrew "man hu" — "What is it?" (v.15). Instructions: gather an
omer per person daily (v.16). Miraculous equality: "he that gathered much
had nothing over, and he that gathered little had no lack" (v.18). Don't
keep it overnight — those who do find worms and stench (vv.19-20). Gather
in the morning before the sun melts it (v.21).

The Sabbath Pattern (vv.22-30): Double portion on the sixth day (v.22). It
keeps overnight without spoiling (v.24). No manna on the seventh day (v.26).
Some go out anyway — find nothing (v.27). God rebukes: "How long refuse ye
to keep my commandments?" (v.28). "The people rested on the seventh day"
(v.30).

Description and Preservation (vv.31-36): Manna: like coriander seed, white,
tasting like honey wafers (v.31). An omer preserved in a pot before the LORD
for future generations (vv.32-34). Israel ate manna for forty years (v.35).

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Wilderness of Sin (v.1): Between Elim and Sinai — a barren region where Israel has no food supply. God provides manna and quail here.
- The journey continues south toward Mount Sinai through the Sinai Peninsula's interior desert.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.4  — "I will rain bread from heaven for you... that I may test them,
        whether they will walk in my law or not." (ESV) — Provision as testing.

v.15 — "It is the bread that the LORD has given you to eat." (ESV) — God's
        daily provision.

v.18 — "Whoever gathered much had nothing left over, and whoever gathered
        little had no lack." (ESV) — God's perfect sufficiency.

v.29 — "The LORD has given you the Sabbath; therefore on the sixth day he
        gives you bread for two days." (ESV) — Sabbath rest built into
        provision.

v.35 — "The people of Israel ate the manna forty years." (ESV) — God's
        faithfulness sustained daily for four decades.

Charles Spurgeon:
  Sermon: "The Bread of Life" on v.15 (MTP Vol. 15, No. 880)
  "This is the bread which the LORD hath given you — manna was God's gift,
  not Israel's earning. They didn't plant it, grow it, or bake it. They
  simply gathered what God gave. And Christ — the true Manna — is God's
  gift, not our achievement. We don't earn Him, deserve Him, or produce Him.
  We receive Him. Daily. Fresh every morning. And like manna, yesterday's
  grace won't sustain today. You need fresh bread — fresh communion with
  Christ — every single day. You cannot store up enough grace on Sunday to
  last until next Sunday. Gather daily."

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.3  — Hebrew "lu-matnu" — would that we had died.
       KJV: "Would to God we had died by the hand of the LORD"
       ESV: "Would that we had died by the hand of the LORD"
       ASV: "Would that we had died by the hand of Jehovah"
       NET: "If only we had died by the hand of the Lord"
       WEB: "We wish that we had died by Yahweh's hand"
       Analysis: All preserve the optative wish, with characteristic divine name
       differences. The irony: they wished for death in Egypt while romanticizing
       slavery — "when we sat by the flesh pots."

v.4  — Hebrew "mamtir lachem lechem min hashamayim" — cause to rain for you bread from the heavens.
       KJV: "rain bread from heaven"
       ESV: "rain bread from heaven"
       ASV: "I will rain bread from heaven for you"
       NET: "I am going to rain bread from heaven for you"
       WEB: "I will rain bread from the sky for you"
       Analysis: WEB uses "sky" for Hebrew "shamayim" while others keep "heaven."
       The distinction matters theologically: "heaven" connects to John 6:31-35
       where Jesus is "bread from heaven." NET uses progressive "am going to rain"
       for emphasis. God provides supernaturally.

v.14 — Hebrew "daq mechuspas" — thin/fine and scale-like.
       KJV: "a small round thing, as small as the hoar frost"
       ESV: "a fine, flake-like thing, fine as frost"
       ASV: "a small round thing, small as the hoar-frost"
       NET: "a thin flaky substance, thin like frost on the earth"
       WEB: "a small round thing, small as the frost"
       Analysis: NET's "thin flaky substance" diverges — interpreting as flaky.
       KJV/ASV/WEB say "round"; ESV/NET say "flake-like/flaky." The Hebrew supports
       both interpretations. All capture the mysterious, frost-like appearance.

v.15 — Hebrew "man hu" — "What is it?"
       KJV: "It is manna"
       ESV: "It is manna"
       ASV: "the house of Israel called the name thereof Manna"
       NET: "The house of Israel called its name 'manna'"
       WEB: "The house of Israel called its name Manna"
       Analysis: All translations preserve the name. NET puts it in quotes to signal
       its etymological significance. The name "manna" comes from the question "What
       is it?" — Israel's wonder at God's provision.

v.16 — Hebrew "omer" — a dry measure, approximately 2.3 liters.
       KJV: "omer"
       ESV: "omer"
       ASV: "omer"
       NET: "omer"
       WEB: "omer"
       Analysis: All transliterate. Enough for one person's daily need — about 2
       quarts. God's provision is measured, sufficient, not excessive.

v.18 — Hebrew "lo he'edif... lo hechsir" — did not have excess... did not have deficiency.
       KJV: "had nothing over... had no lack"
       ESV: "had nothing left over... had no lack"
       ASV: "had nothing over... had no lack"
       NET: "had nothing left over... had no lack"
       WEB: "had nothing over... had no lack"
       Analysis: All equivalent. Miraculous equalization — God provides exactly what
       each person needs. Paul quotes this in 2 Corinthians 8:15.

v.23 — Hebrew "shabbaton" — solemn rest.
       KJV: "a rest of the holy sabbath unto the LORD"
       ESV: "a day of solemn rest, a holy Sabbath to the LORD"
       ASV: "a solemn rest, a holy sabbath unto Jehovah"
       NET: "Tomorrow is a time of cessation from work, a holy Sabbath to the Lord"
       WEB: "a solemn rest, a holy Sabbath to Yahweh"
       Analysis: NET's "time of cessation from work" interprets "shabbaton" (solemn
       rest) functionally. ASV/WEB preserve the Hebrew terms more closely. This is
       the first use of "sabbath" in Exodus — the principle predates Sinai but is
       now formalized.

v.31 — Hebrew "gad" — coriander.
       KJV: "coriander seed"
       ESV: "coriander seed"
       ASV: "coriander seed"
       NET: "coriander seed"
       WEB: "coriander seed"
       Analysis: All identical. A small, round, whitish seed. Manna was small,
       round, and white — humble in appearance but sustaining.

v.34 — Hebrew "lifnei ha'edut" — before the Testimony.
       KJV: "laid it up before the Testimony, to be kept"
       ESV: "placed it before the testimony to be kept"
       ASV: "Aaron laid it up before the Testimony, to be kept"
       NET: "Aaron placed it before the ark of the testimony for safekeeping"
       WEB: "Aaron laid it up before the Testimony, to be kept"
       Analysis: NET adds "the ark of" interpretively. Hebrew simply says "before
       the Testimony" (edut). NET clarifies for readers unfamiliar with tabernacle
       terminology. The manna was preserved as a memorial for future generations.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Manna          — "What is it?" — bread from heaven. Small, round, white,
               honey-flavored. A type of Christ: the bread of life that
               comes down from heaven (John 6:48-51).

Daily dependence — Manna could not be hoarded (except for Sabbath). God
               teaches Israel to depend on Him daily — not to stockpile
               security but to trust fresh provision each morning.

Sabbath rest   — Double manna on the sixth day; none on the seventh. God
               builds rest into His provision. Trust Him enough to stop
               working.

"He that gathered much had nothing over" — God's perfect sufficiency.
               Whether you gather much or little, God provides exactly what
               you need. No excess, no lack.

Quail          — Birds provided at evening for meat. God provides variety —
               not just bread but flesh. He meets desires, not just needs.

Omer           — A daily measure (about 2 quarts). Enough for one person.
               God's provision is measured — sufficient, not excessive.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "I will rain bread from heaven" (v.4). God provides — supernaturally,
   daily, sufficiently. Are you trusting Him for today's bread? Or are you
   anxious about tomorrow's? "Give us THIS DAY our daily bread."

2. "That I may prove them" (v.4). God's provision is also God's test. How
   you handle what God gives reveals your heart. Do you hoard? Do you
   disobey? Do you trust?

3. "He that gathered much had nothing over, and he that gathered little had
   no lack" (v.18). God equalizes. Stop comparing your portion to others'.
   God gives you exactly what YOU need.

4. The manna spoiled overnight (v.20). Yesterday's grace doesn't sustain
   today. You need fresh communion with Christ every morning. Are you
   gathering daily — or trying to live on last week's spiritual food?

5. "The LORD hath given you the sabbath" (v.29). Rest is a GIFT, not a
   punishment. God provides enough on the sixth day so you can rest on the
   seventh. Do you trust Him enough to stop working?
