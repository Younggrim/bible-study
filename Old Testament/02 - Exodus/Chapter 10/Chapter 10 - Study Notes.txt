Exodus - Chapter 10
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
The plagues of locusts and thick darkness cover Egypt; Pharaoh offers compromises but Moses insists all Israel must go with everything.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 10 records plagues eight and nine: locusts and
darkness. The plagues reach their penultimate intensity. Pharaoh's own
servants break ranks: "Knowest thou not yet that Egypt is destroyed?" (v.7).
Pharaoh offers more compromises — "go, but leave your children" (v.11),
"go, but leave your livestock" (v.24) — each rejected by Moses. The ninth
plague — three days of supernatural darkness — strikes at the heart of
Egyptian religion: Ra, the sun god, is Egypt's supreme deity. Pharaoh is
considered Ra's son. When God extinguishes the sun, He declares Ra powerless
and Pharaoh a fraud. The chapter ends with Pharaoh's final threat: "See my
face no more" (v.28) — and Moses agrees.

Eighth Plague: Locusts (vv.1-20): God states His purpose: "that thou mayest
tell in the ears of thy son, and of thy son's son" (v.2) — the plagues are
for generational testimony. God asks: "How long wilt thou refuse to humble
thyself before me?" (v.3). Pharaoh's servants urge him to yield: "Egypt is
destroyed" (v.7). Pharaoh offers a compromise: only the men may go (v.11).
Moses refuses. Locusts come on an east wind — covering the land, eating
everything the hail left (vv.13-15). "There remained not any green thing"
(v.15). Pharaoh confesses again: "I have sinned" (v.16). God sends a west
wind — locusts cast into the Red Sea (v.19). Pharaoh hardens again (v.20).
Targets: Serapia (locust deity), Isis/Osiris (agriculture gods).

Ninth Plague: Darkness (vv.21-29): No warning — God strikes directly.
"Darkness which may be felt" (v.21) — supernatural, tangible darkness for
three days (v.22). Egyptians cannot see each other or move (v.23). "But all
the children of Israel had light in their dwellings" (v.23) — God's
distinction continues. Pharaoh offers another compromise: go, but leave your
livestock (v.24). Moses refuses: "there shall not an hoof be left behind"
(v.26). God hardens Pharaoh (v.27). Pharaoh threatens death: "See my face
no more" (v.28). Moses agrees: "I will see thy face again no more" (v.29).
Target: Ra, the sun god — Egypt's supreme deity defeated.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Egypt: Plagues 8-9 (locusts, darkness) devastate the land. Locusts cover "the face of the whole earth" (v.15).
- Goshen (implied, v.23): "All the children of Israel had light in their dwellings" — darkness only covered Egyptian territory.

        Pharaoh's servants see the futility of resistance.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/exodus-10/
  Guzik emphasizes the darkness as a direct assault on Ra (Egypt's supreme
  god), Pharaoh's escalating compromises, and Moses' insistence on total
  consecration ("not a hoof left behind").

Charles Spurgeon:
  Sermon: "Not a Hoof Left Behind" on v.26 (MTP Vol. 16, No. 916)
  "Not a hoof left behind — Moses will not negotiate. Not the men without
  the children. Not the people without the cattle. Not partial obedience.
  Not a compromise. EVERYTHING goes with God or nothing goes at all. This is
  the principle of consecration: you cannot serve God with half your life
  and give the other half to Egypt. Not a hoof — not a habit, not a
  relationship, not a dollar, not an hour — left behind in bondage. Total
  surrender. Complete consecration. Everything belongs to God."

  Sermon: "Light in Darkness" on v.23 (MTP Vol. 29, No. 1712)
  "All the children of Israel had LIGHT in their dwellings — when Egypt was
  in midnight darkness, Israel had light. This is the believer's privilege:
  when the world sits in darkness and confusion, God's people have light.
  Not because we are better but because God gives it. The light is not ours
  — it is His, dwelling with us. And this light is Christ: 'I am the light
  of the world' (John 8:12)."

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1  — Hebrew "YHWH" — the divine name.
       KJV: "the LORD said unto Moses"
       ESV: "the LORD said to Moses"
       ASV: "Jehovah said unto Moses"
       NET: "The Lord said to Moses"
       WEB: "Yahweh said to Moses"
       Analysis: The divine name (YHWH) rendered three different ways. ASV
       uses the traditional English form "Jehovah," WEB uses the scholarly
       transliteration "Yahweh," and KJV/ESV/NET follow the convention of
       "the LORD." This chapter's events demonstrate the meaning of the name
       in action.

v.2  — Hebrew "hit'alal'ti" — to deal severely with, to mock.
       KJV: "what things I have wrought in Egypt"
       ESV: "how I have dealt harshly with the Egyptians"
       ASV: "what things I have wrought upon them"
       NET: "how I made fools of the Egyptians"
       WEB: "what things I have done to them"
       Analysis: NET's "made fools of" is a dynamic rendering capturing the
       contemptuous tone God intends toward Egypt's gods and king. ESV's
       "dealt harshly" emphasizes severity. KJV/ASV's "wrought" is archaic
       but dignified. WEB is neutral. The purpose: generational testimony.

v.3  — Hebrew "le'anot" — to humble, to afflict oneself, to bow down.
       KJV: "How long wilt thou refuse to humble thyself before me?"
       ESV: "How long will you refuse to humble yourself before me?"
       ASV: "How long wilt thou refuse to humble thyself before me?"
       NET: "How long will you refuse to humble yourself before me?"
       WEB: "How long will you refuse to humble yourself before me?"
       Analysis: All translations are virtually identical — a rare point of
       complete agreement. God demands what Pharaoh refuses: submission.
       The question implies God's patience has a limit.

v.7  — Hebrew "moqesh" — a snare/trap.
       KJV: "How long shall this man be a snare unto us?"
       ESV: "How long shall this man be a snare to us?"
       ASV: "How long shall this man be a snare unto us?"
       NET: "How long will this man be a menace to us?"
       WEB: "How long will this man be a snare to us?"
       Analysis: NET's "menace" is more dynamic but loses the hunting/trap
       imagery. KJV/ESV/ASV/WEB all preserve the literal "snare" — Pharaoh's
       servants see Moses as a trap that has ensnared them. Even his own
       court tells Pharaoh: "Egypt is destroyed" (v.7).

v.10 — Hebrew "ra'ah neged peneikhem" — evil/calamity is before your faces.
       KJV: "Look to it; for evil is before you"
       ESV: "Look, you have some evil purpose in mind"
       ASV: "for evil is before you"
       NET: "Watch out! Trouble is right in front of you."
       WEB: "for evil is clearly before your faces"
       Analysis: NET heavily paraphrases, interpreting Pharaoh's words as a
       threat. ESV uniquely suggests evil intent on Israel's part. KJV/ASV
       are more literal. WEB preserves the "faces" imagery. Pharaoh threatens
       disaster if they try to take everyone.

v.19 — Hebrew "Yam Suph" — Sea of Reeds.
       KJV: "the Red sea"
       ESV: "the Red Sea"
       ASV: "the Red Sea"
       NET: "the Red Sea"
       WEB: "the Red Sea"
       Analysis: All translations use "Red Sea" here. The literal Hebrew is
       "Sea of Reeds" — debated whether this is the modern Red Sea or a
       marshy lake region. NET notes the literal meaning elsewhere. The
       locusts are cast into the sea by a west wind.

v.21 — Hebrew "choshekh... veyamesh choshekh" — darkness... and one may feel/grope the darkness.
       KJV: "darkness which may be felt"
       ESV: "a darkness to be felt"
       ASV: "darkness which may be felt"
       NET: "a darkness so thick it can be felt"
       WEB: "darkness which may be felt"
       Analysis: NET adds "so thick" to make the tangible quality explicit,
       going beyond the Hebrew but capturing the implication well.
       KJV/ESV/ASV/WEB are more restrained. Supernatural, oppressive,
       paralyzing — a reversal of creation (Genesis 1:3). Ra, the sun god
       and Egypt's supreme deity, is defeated.

v.26 — Hebrew "lo tisha'er parsah" — not a hoof shall remain.
       KJV: "there shall not an hoof be left behind"
       ESV: "not a hoof shall be left behind"
       ASV: "there shall not a hoof be left behind"
       NET: "not a hoof will be left behind"
       WEB: "not a hoof will be left behind"
       Analysis: All translations are virtually identical. Total, complete
       departure — nothing of Israel's belongs to Egypt. Moses' insistence
       on complete consecration: you cannot serve God with half your life.

v.9  — Hebrew "chag" — a feast/festival (pilgrimage feast).
       KJV: "we must hold a feast unto the LORD"
       ESV: "we must hold a feast to the LORD"
       ASV: "we must hold a feast unto Jehovah"
       NET: "we are to hold a pilgrim feast for the Lord"
       WEB: "we must hold a feast to Yahweh"
       Analysis: NET adds "pilgrim" to translate Hebrew "chag" — a festival
       involving pilgrimage, distinguishing it from ordinary feasting.
       ASV/WEB make the divine name explicit. The feast demands complete
       participation — men, women, children, and livestock.
------------------------------------
Ra (sun god)   — Egypt's supreme deity. Pharaoh was considered Ra's son and
               earthly representative. Three days of darkness declares Ra
               powerless and Pharaoh a fraud.

"Darkness which may be felt" — Supernatural darkness beyond natural
               explanation. Tangible, oppressive, paralyzing. A reversal of
               creation (Genesis 1:3) — God un-creates Egypt's light.

Locusts        — Devastating swarms that consume all vegetation. A common
               instrument of divine judgment in Scripture (Joel 1-2,
               Revelation 9).

Pharaoh's compromises — A pattern of partial concessions: (1) sacrifice in
               the land (8:25); (2) don't go far (8:28); (3) men only
               (10:11); (4) leave your livestock (10:24). Each rejected.
               God demands complete freedom, not negotiated half-measures.

"Not a hoof left behind" — Moses' insistence on total consecration.
               Everything Israel has belongs to God — nothing remains in
               Egypt's possession.

Generational testimony — God's stated purpose (v.2): the plagues create a
               story to tell children and grandchildren. Faith is passed
               down through remembered acts of God.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "How long wilt thou refuse to humble thyself before me?" (v.3). God asks
   this of Pharaoh — and of us. Is there an area where you're refusing to
   humble yourself? How long will you resist?

2. "That thou mayest tell in the ears of thy son" (v.2). God's mighty acts
   are meant to be told to the next generation. What stories of God's
   faithfulness are you passing down? Are your children hearing them?

3. "Not a hoof left behind" (v.26). Complete consecration — nothing held
   back. Is there a "hoof" you're leaving in Egypt? A habit, a relationship,
   a possession you won't surrender to God? Everything must go.

4. "All the children of Israel had light in their dwellings" (v.23). When
   the world is in darkness, God's people have light. Are you living in that
   light? Are you sharing it with those in darkness around you?

5. Pharaoh's servants saw the truth before Pharaoh did (v.7). Sometimes the
   people around a stubborn person see reality more clearly. Are you
   listening to the wise counsel of others — or are you the last to see
   what everyone else already knows?
