Malachi - Chapter 1
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
God declares His love for Israel over Edom, then rebukes the priests for offering blemished sacrifices and despising His table — "My name will be great among the nations" yet they profane it.


AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Malachi ("my messenger"). The last OT prophet,
approximately 450-430 BC. Key theme: post-exilic spiritual
lethargy — the people go through religious motions without
heart. Structured as disputations (God accuses, people
challenge, God responds). Famous: "I have loved you" (1:2),
"Will a man rob God?" (3:8-10), "the Sun of righteousness
arise with healing in his wings" (4:2), and Elijah's return
(4:5-6). The OT ends with a promise and a warning.

Classification: Prophetic Disputation — God's Love Questioned
and Contemptible Worship Exposed
Key Themes: God's sovereign electing love, the ingratitude of
Israel, contemptible worship from contemptuous priests, offering
God the leftovers, God's name great among the nations, the
contrast between Israel's dishonor and Gentile worship

Historical Context: Malachi writes approximately 80-100 years
after the return from exile. The Temple has been rebuilt (516 BC),
Nehemiah has restored the walls (445 BC), and the immediate
crises are past. But a more subtle danger has emerged: spiritual
apathy. The people are going through religious motions without
heart engagement. They have not abandoned God outright — they
have simply stopped caring.

The disputation format is distinctive: God makes a statement,
the people challenge it with "How?" or "Wherein?", and God
responds with evidence. This structure reveals a community that
has grown argumentative toward God — questioning His love, His
justice, and His requirements while simultaneously offering Him
blind, lame, and sick animals in worship.

Malachi's situation parallels many of the reforms Nehemiah
addressed in his second visit (Neh 13): mixed marriages,
neglected tithes, corrupt priests, and spiritual indifference.
The passionate God of Sinai faces a yawning congregation.

Structure:
- Superscription (v.1)
- The First Disputation: "I Have Loved You" (vv.2-5)
- The Second Disputation: Priests Who Despise God's Name (vv.6-9)
- God's Name Great Among the Nations (vv.10-11)
- The Weariness of Worship (vv.12-13)
- Cursed is the Deceiver (v.14)

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.2   — Romans 9:13 — "Jacob have I loved, but Esau have I
         hated" — Paul quotes this in discussing God's sovereign
         election.
v.2   — Deuteronomy 7:6-8 — "The LORD did not set his love upon
         you because ye were more in number... but because the
         LORD loved you."
v.6   — Exodus 20:12 — "Honour thy father" — if human fathers
         deserve honor, how much more the heavenly Father?
v.8   — Leviticus 22:20-22 — The law requiring unblemished
         sacrifices — no blind, broken, or diseased animals.
v.11  — Isaiah 60:3 — "The Gentiles shall come to thy light" —
         worldwide worship of Israel's God.
v.11  — John 4:21-23 — "Neither in this mountain, nor yet at
         Jerusalem... true worshippers shall worship the Father
         in spirit and in truth."
v.13  — Amos 8:5 — "When will the new moon be gone, that we may
         sell corn?" — worship as impatient obligation.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------

REFLECTION
------------------------------------
1. "I have loved you" (v.2). God's first word to a cold people
   is not anger but affection. Before the rebuke comes the
   reminder: I LOVE YOU. If you feel distant from God, hear His
   first word: love. But don't respond like Israel — demanding
   proof while surrounded by evidence. Open your eyes to the
   love that is already there.

2. "Where is my honour?" (v.6). If you call God "Father" and
   "Lord," does your life reflect a son's honor and a servant's
   reverence? Titles without corresponding behavior are empty.
   Words without worship are noise. Honor God not just with your
   lips but with your LIFE.

3. "Offer it to your governor" (v.8). The God-test: would you
   give this to someone you truly respect? The leftover time,
   the half-hearted attention, the minimal effort — would you
   give that to your boss? Your spouse? Then why give it to God?
   He deserves your BEST, not your remainders.

4. "What a weariness!" (v.13). When worship becomes boring, the
   problem is never God — it is the state of your heart. A heart
   alive to grace finds worship compelling. If church, prayer,
   or Scripture feels like drudgery, don't blame the format —
   examine your heart. Reignite your first love.

5. "I am a great King" (v.14). God is not diminished by our
   neglect. He remains a great King whether we honor Him or not.
   The nations will praise Him even if Israel does not. The
   question is not whether God will be worshipped — it is whether
   YOU will be among the worshippers.
