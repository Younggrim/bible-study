Jonah - Chapter 4
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Jonah is angry that God showed mercy to Nineveh, and God uses a plant, a worm, and a scorching wind to teach Jonah about compassion — should not God pity a city of 120,000 who cannot tell their right hand from their left?


AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Jonah son of Amittai (2 Kings 14:25). Set approximately
780-760 BC. Key theme: God's mercy extends beyond Israel to all
nations — even Israel's enemies (Nineveh/Assyria). Jonah resists;
God persists. "Salvation is of the LORD" (2:9). Jesus references
Jonah as a sign (Matthew 12:39-40).

Classification: Prophetic Narrative — The Angry Prophet and the
God Who Asks Questions
Key Themes: The prophet angry at God's mercy, God's right to show
compassion, the gourd/worm object lesson, valuing comfort over
souls, the book's unanswered question

Historical Context: Chapter 4 is the CLIMAX and theological center
of Jonah. Nineveh repented (ch.3); now Jonah is FURIOUS. His
anger reveals his true motive for fleeing: he KNEW God would be
merciful (v.2) and didn't want Israel's enemies saved. The book
ends with God's unanswered question: "Should not I spare Nineveh?"
(v.11). The question hangs in the air — addressed not just to
Jonah but to every reader who limits God's mercy to their own
group. God uses an object lesson: a gourd (shade plant) that grows
and dies in one day. Jonah grieves the plant more than he would
120,000 people. The contrast exposes his distorted values.

Structure:
- Jonah's Anger: "I Knew You Were Merciful!" (vv.1-4)
- Jonah Watches and Waits (v.5)
- The Object Lesson: Gourd, Worm, and Wind (vv.6-8)
- God's Final Question: Should I Not Spare Nineveh? (vv.9-11)

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Joppa (modern Jaffa/Tel Aviv) — Mediterranean port city where Jonah boards a ship fleeing God's call.
- Tarshish (likely Tartessus in modern Spain) — the farthest west one could sail; the exact OPPOSITE direction from Nineveh.
- Nineveh (modern Mosul, Iraq) — capital of the Assyrian Empire, located on the Tigris River, approximately 500 miles northeast of Israel.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "for I knew that you are a gracious God and merciful, slow
        to anger and abounding in steadfast love, and relenting
        from disaster" (ESV) — Jonah's confession: his theology is
        PERFECT. He quotes Exodus 34:6 accurately. He KNOWS God is
        merciful. And he HATES it — at least when it applies to his
        enemies. Correct theology without correct compassion is
        spiritually monstrous.

v.3  — "for it is better for me to die than to live" (ESV) —
        Jonah would rather DIE than see Nineveh spared. His hatred
        is so deep that God's mercy to others feels like personal
        injury. This is the danger of nationalism replacing
        kingdom thinking.

v.4  — "Do you do well to be angry?" (ESV) — God's first
        question. Gentle. Patient. Not "you have no right to be
        angry" but "is this anger GOOD? Is it serving you? Is it
        righteous?" God questions rather than condemns.

v.9  — "Yes, I do well to be angry, angry enough to die" (ESV) —
        Jonah doubles down. He DEFENDS his anger — even claims it's
        righteous anger deserving death rather than compromise.
        This is a prophet in full rebellion AFTER deliverance,
        AFTER obedience, AFTER success. Obedience without
        heart-change is still rebellion.

v.11 — "And should not I pity Nineveh, that great city, in which
        there are more than 120,000 persons who do not know their
        right hand from their left, and also much cattle?" (ESV) —
        The book's FINAL WORD is a QUESTION. God doesn't answer
        it — He leaves it for Jonah (and every reader) to answer.
        120,000 innocent people (children who can't tell right from
        left). And even cattle. God cares about ALL life. Do you?

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.2   — Exodus 34:6-7 — The original revelation of God's character
         to Moses. Jonah quotes it precisely.
v.2   — Joel 2:13 — "He is gracious and merciful, slow to anger."
         (ESV) Same formula.
v.3   — 1 Kings 19:4 — Elijah: "It is enough; now, O LORD, take
         away my life." (ESV) Same despair from a discouraged
         prophet.
v.4   — Genesis 4:6 — God to Cain: "Why are you angry?" (ESV) Same
         gentle questioning of anger.
v.6   — Isaiah 4:6 — "A shade by day from the heat." (ESV) God
         provides shade/comfort.
v.11  — Matthew 6:26 — "Are you not of more value than they?"
         (ESV) God's care extends to all creatures.
v.11  — Luke 15:28 — The elder brother angry that the prodigal is
         received back. Same spirit as Jonah.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.2 — "repentest thee of the evil" (KJV) / "relents over disaster"
      (ESV) — Hebrew "nacham." God relents from sending calamity
      when people repent. Jonah uses this as a COMPLAINT rather
      than a praise.

v.6 — "gourd" (KJV) — Hebrew "qiqayon" = likely a castor oil
      plant or gourd. Fast-growing, large-leafed, providing
      excellent shade. Grows quickly and dies quickly.

v.7 — "prepared a worm" — Hebrew "tola'at." God "prepared" (same
      verb as the fish in 1:17). The worm is as much God's tool
      as the fish was. God orchestrates even the small things.

v.8 — "vehement east wind" — Hebrew "ruach qadim charishit" =
      a scorching, silent east wind (sirocco). Everything
      conspires to make Jonah miserable: heat, wind, no shade.
      God strips away comfort to teach compassion.

v.11 — "sixscore thousand" = 120,000 people. "Cannot discern
       between their right hand and their left hand" — either
       children (too young to know right from wrong) or morally
       ignorant people. Either way: innocent, needing teaching,
       not destruction.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. You can have correct theology and wrong compassion (v.2). Jonah
   KNOWS God is merciful — and RESENTS it. Theological accuracy
   without love is demonic (James 2:19 — "the devils also believe,
   and tremble"). Check: does your theology produce compassion or
   contempt for others?

2. "Doest thou well to be angry?" (v.4). God's question to every
   resentful believer: Is your anger GOOD? Is it SERVING you? Is
   it RIGHTEOUS? Or is it the self-centered anger of someone who
   can't stand God being generous to others? (Cf. the workers in
   Matthew 20:1-16.)

3. You grieve a plant more than people (vv.10-11). We mourn lost
   COMFORT (the gourd) more than lost SOULS. Our convenience
   means more to us than others' eternity. God's question forces
   the comparison: you cared about a plant — shouldn't I care
   about 120,000 people?

4. The book ends with a QUESTION (v.11). No resolution. No answer
   from Jonah. The question is left for YOU. Should God spare
   those you consider unworthy? Should mercy extend to your
   enemies? What is your answer?

5. God is more concerned about people than you are (v.11). Even
   "much cattle" — God's compassion extends to animals. His mercy
   is wider, deeper, more generous than yours will ever be. Stop
   trying to limit it.
