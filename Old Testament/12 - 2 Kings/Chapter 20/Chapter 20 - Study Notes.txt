2 Kings - Chapter 20
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Hezekiah falls deathly ill and God adds fifteen years to his life, confirmed by the shadow retreating ten steps. He then foolishly shows Babylon's envoys all his treasures, and Isaiah prophesies the Babylonian exile.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Unknown, traditionally attributed to Jeremiah or a prophetic
school. Originally one book with 1 Kings. Covers approximately 250
years (850-586 BC) from Elijah's translation through the Babylonian
exile. Key themes: Elisha's ministry (double portion), the decline of
both kingdoms, God's patience and warnings through prophets, the fall
of Israel to Assyria (722 BC), Judah's reforms and relapses, and the
final judgment — exile to Babylon (586 BC). The book ends in darkness
but with a glimmer of hope: Jehoiachin released from prison.

Historical Context: Chapter 20 records three episodes from the latter
years of Hezekiah's reign (c. 701-686 BC): his miraculous healing
from terminal illness, the sign of the reversed shadow, and his
disastrous reception of Babylonian envoys. Chronologically, these
events likely precede the Assyrian crisis of chapters 18-19 (the
Babylonian embassy fits the period of Merodach-baladan's rebellion
against Assyria, c. 703-702 BC), but the author places them here as
a theological bridge — Hezekiah's faithfulness during the Assyrian
siege contrasts with his pride before the Babylonians, and the
Babylonian prophecy points forward to the exile that dominates the
rest of 2 Kings.

Hezekiah stands as one of Judah's greatest kings, yet this chapter
reveals his complexity. The man who trusted God against Sennacherib
also wept for his own life and then foolishly displayed his wealth to
future enemies. The chapter functions as a pivot point in the book:
after the miraculous deliverance from Assyria, the narrative turns
toward Babylon — the empire that will ultimately destroy what Assyria
could not. Isaiah's prophecy in verses 17-18 is the first explicit
prediction of the Babylonian exile in 2 Kings, planting the seed of
judgment that will grow through the remaining chapters. Hezekiah's
response — "Good is the word of the LORD... Is it not good, if peace
and truth be in my days?" — reveals a troubling complacency. The man
who prayed fervently for his own healing shows little urgency about
his descendants' fate.

Hezekiah's Illness and Recovery (vv.1-11): Hezekiah falls terminally
ill, and Isaiah delivers God's verdict: "Set thine house in order;
for thou shalt die, and not live" (v.1). Hezekiah turns to the wall
— a posture of intimate, desperate prayer — and weeps bitterly,
appealing to his faithful walk before God (v.3). Before Isaiah even
leaves the middle court, God reverses the sentence: fifteen years
will be added to Hezekiah's life, and deliverance from Assyria is
promised (vv.5-6). The healing involves both supernatural will and
natural means — a lump of figs applied to the boil (v.7). Hezekiah
requests a confirming sign, and God provides an extraordinary one:
the shadow on the dial (or steps) of Ahaz moves backward ten degrees
(vv.9-11). This miracle reverses the natural order — time itself
appears to retreat — confirming that the God who reverses death can
reverse the sun's shadow.

The Babylonian Embassy and Isaiah's Prophecy (vv.12-19): Merodach-
baladan (Berodach-baladan in KJV) sends envoys to congratulate
Hezekiah on his recovery — but the political motive is transparent:
Babylon sought allies against Assyria. Hezekiah, perhaps flattered
or seeking alliance, shows them "all the house of his precious
things" — every treasure, weapon, and storehouse (v.13). "There was
nothing in his house, nor in all his dominion, that Hezekiah shewed
them not." Isaiah confronts the king with probing questions, then
delivers God's judgment: everything Hezekiah showed will be carried
to Babylon, and his own descendants will serve as eunuchs in the
Babylonian palace (vv.17-18). Hezekiah's response (v.19) is
troubling — he accepts the verdict as "good" because it won't
happen in his lifetime. The chapter closes with a summary of
Hezekiah's accomplishments, including his famous water tunnel
(the Siloam tunnel, v.20), and his death. Manasseh succeeds him.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Samaria — capital of the northern kingdom (Israel); falls to Assyria in 722 BC (ch.17). Jerusalem — capital of Judah; falls to Babylon in 586 BC (ch.25).
- Key locations: Jordan River (Elisha's miracles, chs.2, 5), Damascus (Syria/Aram), Nineveh (Assyrian capital), Babylon (where Judah is exiled).
- Sennacherib's invasion route threatens Jerusalem (chs.18-19). Exile destinations: Assyria (for Israel) and Babylon (for Judah).

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.3  — "I beseech you, O LORD, remember now how I have walked
        before you in truth and with a perfect heart, and have
        done that which is good in your sight. And Hezekiah wept
        sore." (ESV) — Hezekiah appeals not to personal merit as
        grounds for salvation, but to covenant faithfulness as
        evidence of a genuine relationship with God. His "perfect
        heart" (ESV: "whole heart") means undivided loyalty, not
        sinless perfection. The weeping reveals both his humanity
        and the depth of his communion with God — he brings his
        whole self, including grief, into prayer.

v.5  — "Turn again, and tell Hezekiah the captain of my people,
        Thus says the LORD, the God of David your father, I have
        heard your prayer, I have seen your tears: behold, I will
        heal you." (ESV) — God calls Hezekiah "captain of my
        people" — a military title emphasizing his role as
        leader under God's authority. "The God of David your
        father" invokes the covenant promises. God responds to
        both prayer and tears — He is not unmoved by genuine
        emotion. The healing is certain: "I will heal you."

v.11 — "And Isaiah the prophet cried to the LORD: and he
        brought the shadow ten degrees backward, by which it had
        gone down in the dial of Ahaz." (ESV) — A miracle that
        defies natural law. The "dial of Ahaz" (ESV: "steps of
        Ahaz") was likely a stairway sundial. God who controls
        time and creation uses Ahaz's own construction — that
        idolatrous king's legacy — to confirm His power. What
        Ahaz built for timekeeping, God uses for sign-giving.

v.13 — "And Hezekiah hearkened to them, and showed them all
        the house of his precious things... there was nothing in
        his house, nor in all his dominion, that Hezekiah showed
        them not." (ESV) — The repetition of "all" and "nothing"
        emphasizes the totality of Hezekiah's disclosure. Pride
        and political ambition replace the humility that
        characterized his prayer life. The man who laid
        Sennacherib's letter before God now lays his treasures
        before Babylon. What you display to the world's powers
        becomes what they eventually take from you.

v.17 — "Behold, the days come, that all that is in your house,
        and that which your fathers have laid up in store to this
        day, shall be carried into Babylon: nothing shall be left,
        says the LORD." (ESV) — The first explicit prophecy of
        the Babylonian exile in 2 Kings. "Nothing shall be left"
        — the totality of loss matches the totality of display.
        What Hezekiah showed, Babylon will take. Generations of
        accumulated treasure, destroyed in a single judgment.
        This prophecy was fulfilled in 586 BC, roughly a century
        after it was spoken.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.1  — Isaiah 38:1-8 — The parallel account with Hezekiah's psalm
        of thanksgiving (Isaiah 38:9-20), which provides his
        personal reflection on facing death and receiving life.
        The psalm reveals the depth of his spiritual crisis.

v.3  — Psalm 26:1-3 — "Vindicate me, O LORD, for I have walked in
        my integrity." Like Hezekiah, David appeals to faithful
        conduct not as merit but as evidence of genuine faith.
        Both men approach God on the basis of relationship, not
        performance.

v.5  — 2 Samuel 7:12-16 — The Davidic covenant. God heals
        Hezekiah "for my own sake, and for my servant David's
        sake" (v.6). The covenant promises sustain even individual
        kings — God's faithfulness to David extends to his
        descendants.

v.7  — James 5:14-16 — "Is any sick among you? let him call for
        the elders." God often heals through means (figs, oil,
        medicine) combined with prayer. The natural remedy does
        not diminish the supernatural healing — God works through
        both.

v.11 — Joshua 10:12-14 — Joshua's long day, when the sun stood
        still. Both miracles demonstrate God's sovereignty over
        celestial bodies. What He did for Joshua in battle, He
        does for Hezekiah in sickness — nature serves God's
        redemptive purposes.

v.12 — Isaiah 39:1-8 — The parallel account of the Babylonian
        embassy. Isaiah 39 provides identical details, confirming
        the historical reliability of both accounts and the
        centrality of this event to Isaiah's prophetic message.

v.17 — 2 Kings 24:13 — The fulfillment: "He carried out from there
        all the treasures of the house of the LORD... as the LORD
        had said." Isaiah's prophecy finds exact fulfillment when
        Nebuchadnezzar strips the Temple in 597 BC.

v.19 — Philippians 2:4 — "Look not every man on his own things,
        but every man also on the things of others." Hezekiah's
        failure was caring only about peace in his own generation.
        Genuine faith considers the inheritance we leave to those
        who come after us.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/2-kings-20/
  Guzik notes that Hezekiah's prayer is remarkable for its bold
  honesty — he does not passively accept death but wrestles with
  God for life. However, Guzik also observes that the additional
  fifteen years produced Manasseh (born during this period), the
  worst king in Judah's history. God gave Hezekiah what he asked
  for, but the unintended consequences were devastating.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "Hezekiah's tears moved the heart of God, and fifteen years
  were added like a postscript to a life that was nearly finished.
  But mark this: in those fifteen years he fathered Manasseh, who
  undid all his father's good. And he showed his treasures to
  Babylon, who would one day take them all. Sometimes God gives
  us what we ask, and we find the gift more costly than the
  denial. Let us pray not merely for longer life, but for wisdom
  to use whatever days we are given. The brevity of faithful
  service outweighs the length of careless years."

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1  — KJV: "sick unto death" / ESV: "sick and was at the point
       of death" — Both convey mortal illness. The Hebrew phrase
       indicates not merely severe sickness but a condition with
       no natural hope of recovery. The ESV's expanded phrasing
       makes the immediacy clearer for modern readers.

v.5  — KJV: "the captain of my people" / ESV: "the leader of my
       people" — Hebrew "nagid" means ruler, prince, or
       commander. KJV's "captain" emphasizes military leadership;
       ESV's "leader" is more general. The term is significant —
       God still claims Hezekiah as His appointed ruler despite
       the death sentence.

v.9  — KJV: "shall the shadow go forward ten degrees, or go back
       ten degrees?" / ESV: "shall the shadow go forward ten
       steps, or go back ten steps?" — Hebrew "ma'aloth" means
       "steps" or "ascents." KJV interprets it as degrees on a
       dial; ESV retains the literal "steps" (likely a stairway
       used as a sundial). The distinction matters: "steps of
       Ahaz" suggests a physical staircase whose shadow served
       as a timepiece.

v.12 — KJV: "Berodach-baladan" / ESV: "Merodach-baladan" — A
       textual variant in the first letter. The correct Babylonian
       name is Marduk-apla-iddina II ("Merodach" = Marduk). The
       KJV follows a Hebrew manuscript variant with "B" instead
       of "M." Isaiah 39:1 has "Merodach" in both translations.

v.19 — KJV: "Is it not good, if peace and truth be in my days?" /
       ESV: "Why not, if there will be peace and security in my
       days?" — The ESV renders Hezekiah's response more candidly.
       KJV's "peace and truth" (Hebrew: shalom ve'emeth) could
       imply stability and faithfulness; ESV's "peace and security"
       suggests Hezekiah's relief that judgment falls after his
       lifetime — a more critical reading of his attitude.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Dial/Steps  — Hebrew "ma'aloth" — The "dial of Ahaz" (KJV) or
of Ahaz       "steps of Ahaz" (ESV) was likely a stairway
             structure whose shadow served as a primitive sundial.
             Built during Ahaz's reign (perhaps influenced by
             Assyrian astronomy), it measured time by the shadow's
             position on the steps. God's reversal of the shadow
             demonstrated His mastery over creation itself.

Merodach-   — (Marduk-apla-iddina II) King of Babylon who
baladan       rebelled against Assyrian control (c. 721-710 and
             703-702 BC). A Chaldean chieftain from the Bit-Yakin
             tribe, he sought alliances against Assyria. His
             embassy to Hezekiah was political — he wanted Judah
             as an ally against their common Assyrian enemy.

Lump of     — A poultice made from pressed figs, used medicinally
Figs          in the ancient Near East for treating boils and
             infections. The fig cake had known healing properties
             — it drew out infection. God's use of natural means
             alongside supernatural healing demonstrates that faith
             and medicine are not opposed but complementary.

Boil        — Hebrew "shechin" — A severe skin infection or
             abscess, possibly a carbuncle or staph infection.
             The same word describes the sixth plague of Egypt
             (Exodus 9:9-11). Without antibiotics, such infections
             were often fatal in the ancient world. The application
             of figs was the best available treatment.

Siloam      — Referenced in v.20 as "the pool and the conduit."
Tunnel        Hezekiah's engineering marvel — a 1,750-foot tunnel
             cut through solid rock to bring water from the Gihon
             Spring inside the city walls. Discovered in 1838, with
             its ancient inscription found in 1880, it confirms
             the biblical record. Built in preparation for
             Sennacherib's siege (2 Chronicles 32:30).

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. God hears desperate prayer (vv.2-5). Hezekiah turned his face
   to the wall — shutting out the world to face God alone — and
   wept bitterly. Before Isaiah left the courtyard, God had already
   reversed the sentence. There is no prayer too desperate, no
   tears too many, no situation too final for God to hear and
   respond. Do not mistake God's initial word as His final word
   when you have not yet brought your whole heart to Him in prayer.

2. Pride follows deliverance if we are not careful (vv.12-13).
   Hezekiah survived death, saw Assyria defeated, and then — in
   his prosperity — showed off to Babylon. The most dangerous
   moment spiritually is often just after God's greatest victory
   in your life. Success, healing, deliverance can breed a pride
   that undoes the very faith that brought the blessing. Stay
   humble after the miracle.

3. What you display to the world, the world will eventually take
   (v.13,17). Hezekiah showed everything; Babylon took everything.
   This is a spiritual principle: whatever you flaunt before
   worldly powers — your resources, your strategies, your
   vulnerabilities — becomes what they exploit. Wisdom does not
   parade its treasures before those who would steal them. Guard
   what God has entrusted to you.

4. Short-term peace is not faithfulness (v.19). Hezekiah's relief
   that judgment wouldn't come in his lifetime reveals a failure
   of generational responsibility. Faithfulness cares about what
   we leave behind, not just what we experience now. Ask yourself:
   Am I making decisions that bless future generations, or am I
   content with peace in my own days while sowing seeds of
   destruction for my children?

5. God's sovereignty includes natural means (v.7). The fig poultice
   did not diminish the miracle — God chose to heal through both
   His word and a natural remedy. Faith does not reject medicine,
   wisdom, or practical means. It trusts that God works through
   all things — supernatural and natural — to accomplish His
   purposes. Use the means God provides while trusting the God
   who provides them.
