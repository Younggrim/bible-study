2 Kings - Chapter 6
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Elisha makes a lost axe head float and blinds an entire Syrian army, leading them into Samaria where they are fed and released. Ben-hadad besieges Samaria until famine drives mothers to cannibalism.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Unknown, traditionally attributed to Jeremiah or a prophetic
school. Originally one book with 1 Kings. Covers approximately 250
years (850-586 BC) from Elijah's translation through the Babylonian
exile. Key themes: Elisha's ministry (double portion), the decline
of both kingdoms, God's patience and warnings through prophets, the
fall of Israel to Assyria (722 BC), Judah's reforms and relapses,
and the final judgment — exile to Babylon (586 BC). The book ends
in darkness but with a glimmer of hope: Jehoiachin released from
prison.

Historical Context: Chapter 6 moves from a small domestic miracle
(a floating axe head) to one of the most dramatic scenes in
Scripture (the invisible army at Dothan) to one of the most
horrifying (cannibalism during siege). The chapter spans the full
range of Elisha's ministry — from caring about a borrowed tool to
revealing heavenly armies to navigating the desperation of a
besieged city. The Syrian wars continue to dominate Israel's
political landscape. Ben-hadad's siege of Samaria produces famine
so severe that a donkey's head sells for eighty pieces of silver
and mothers eat their own children — the ultimate covenant curse
(Deuteronomy 28:53-57). The chapter ends with the king blaming
God and threatening Elisha's life, setting up the miraculous
deliverance of chapter 7.

The Floating Axe Head (vv.1-7): The sons of the prophets need
larger quarters — their community is growing under Elisha's
leadership. While cutting timber at the Jordan, a borrowed axe
head falls into the water. The man's distress — "Alas, master! for
it was borrowed" — reveals both poverty (he cannot afford his own
tools) and integrity (he feels responsible for another's property).
Elisha cuts a stick and throws it in; the iron floats. This miracle
seems small compared to raising the dead or parting rivers, but it
reveals God's concern for ordinary problems and honest people. Iron
does not float — but God is not bound by natural law. The God who
parts seas also retrieves axe heads. No concern is too small for
His attention.

Elisha and the Syrian Army at Dothan (vv.8-23): The king of Syria
cannot understand how Israel anticipates his every move. His servant
explains: "Elisha, the prophet that is in Israel, telleth the king
of Israel the words that thou speakest in thy bedchamber" (v.12).
Syria sends a great army to capture one prophet — the same
disproportionate response Ahaziah showed in chapter 1. Elisha's
servant wakes to find the city surrounded and panics: "Alas, my
master! how shall we do?" Elisha's response is one of Scripture's
greatest declarations of faith: "Fear not: for they that be with us
are more than they that be with them" (v.16). He prays for his
servant's eyes to be opened — and the mountain is revealed full of
horses and chariots of fire. The heavenly army was always there;
only perception changed. Elisha then prays for the Syrians to be
struck with blindness, leads them into Samaria, and opens their
eyes in the midst of Israel's capital. Rather than slaughter them,
Elisha commands a feast: "set bread and water before them." The
result: "the bands of Syria came no more into the land of Israel"
(v.23). Mercy accomplished what military force could not.

The Siege of Samaria (vv.24-33): Despite the earlier peace, Ben-
hadad eventually besieges Samaria with his full army. The famine
becomes catastrophic — a donkey's head (unclean meat) sells for
eighty shekels of silver; dove's dung (possibly a plant name, or
literal excrement used as fuel) for five shekels. The horror
deepens when a woman appeals to the king: she and another woman
agreed to eat their sons — she fulfilled her part, but the other
woman hid her child. This is the ultimate degradation — the
covenant curse of Deuteronomy 28:53-57 fulfilled literally. The
king tears his clothes, revealing sackcloth beneath — outward
piety without inward repentance. His response is not to seek God
but to blame Elisha: "if the head of Elisha... shall stand on him
this day" (v.31). He blames the prophet for the siege rather than
acknowledging that national sin brought national judgment. The
chapter ends with the king's despairing question: "what should I
wait for the LORD any longer?" — the voice of faith exhausted,
setting up God's dramatic answer in chapter 7.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Samaria — capital of the northern kingdom (Israel); falls to Assyria in 722 BC (ch.17). Jerusalem — capital of Judah; falls to Babylon in 586 BC (ch.25).
- Key locations: Jordan River (Elisha's miracles, chs.2, 5), Damascus (Syria/Aram), Nineveh (Assyrian capital), Babylon (where Judah is exiled).
- Sennacherib's invasion route threatens Jerusalem (chs.18-19). Exile destinations: Assyria (for Israel) and Babylon (for Judah).

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.16 — "Fear not: for they that be with us are more than they that
        be with them." (ESV) — The foundational truth of spiritual
        warfare. The visible threat is never the full picture. God's
        forces always outnumber the enemy's. Fear is a vision
        problem — it sees only the natural and misses the
        supernatural.

v.17 — "LORD, I pray you, open his eyes, that he may see. And the
        LORD opened the eyes of the young man; and he saw: and,
        behold, the mountain was full of horses and chariots of fire
        round about Elisha." (ESV) — The heavenly army was already
        there — the prayer did not summon it but revealed it. God's
        protection does not begin when we become aware of it. It was
        present before the servant woke up afraid.

v.22 — "You shall not smite them... set bread and water before
        them, that they may eat and drink, and go to their master."
        (ESV) — Elisha commands mercy toward enemies — feeding those
        who came to kill. This anticipates Christ's teaching: "Love
        your enemies... do good to them that hate you" (Luke 6:27).
        Mercy ended the raids; violence would have escalated them.

v.33 — "This evil is of the LORD; what should I wait for the LORD
        any longer?" (ESV) — The voice of despair that has given up
        on God. Yet God's deliverance comes in the very next chapter.
        The moment we stop waiting is often the moment before God
        acts.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.6  — Exodus 15:25 — Moses cast a tree into water to heal it.
        Elisha casts a stick to make iron float. Wood as God's
        instrument of reversal.
v.16 — Romans 8:31 — "If God be for us, who can be against us?"
        The New Testament echo of Elisha's declaration.
v.17 — 2 Kings 2:11 — Chariots of fire at Elijah's translation.
        The same heavenly host now surrounds Elisha — the double
        portion includes divine protection.
v.22 — Proverbs 25:21-22 — "If your enemy be hungry, give him
        bread to eat." Romans 12:20 quotes this principle.
v.28 — Deuteronomy 28:53-57 — The covenant curse of cannibalism
        during siege, prophesied by Moses, now fulfilled.
v.31 — 1 Kings 19:2 — Jezebel threatened Elijah's life; now the
        king threatens Elisha's. Prophets are always targets of
        desperate rulers.
v.33 — Psalm 13:1-2 — "How long will you forget me, O LORD?"
        The cry of those who feel God has abandoned them in crisis.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/2-kings-6/
  Guzik emphasizes that the floating axe head demonstrates God's
  care for small, everyday concerns — not just national crises.
  On the Dothan scene, he notes that Elisha's calm reveals a man
  who already sees what his servant cannot. The prayer is not for
  Elisha's benefit but for the servant's — faith does not need
  visible confirmation, but God graciously provides it for the
  weak. The feast for enemies demonstrates that grace disarms more
  effectively than force.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "The mountain was full of horses and chariots of fire — and they
  were there before the servant saw them. They were there before
  the Syrians arrived. They were there before Elisha prayed. The
  prayer did not create the protection; it revealed it. Beloved,
  the angels of God are encamped around you right now. You do not
  see them, but they are there. Your fear does not mean God has
  abandoned you — it means your eyes have not yet been opened.
  Pray not for deliverance but for sight. When you see what God
  sees, fear becomes impossible."

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.5  — KJV: "the axe head fell into the water" / ESV: "his axe
       head fell into the water" — Hebrew "barzel" = iron. The axe
       head was iron — valuable and heavy. Its loss in the Jordan
       was both financially devastating for a poor prophet and
       physically irrecoverable without divine intervention.

v.6  — KJV: "the iron did swim" / ESV: "made the iron float" —
       Hebrew "tsuf" = to float, to swim. The same word used of
       Moses' basket floating on the Nile (Exodus 2:5 in some
       readings). Iron floating contradicts natural law — this is
       pure miracle.

v.25 — KJV: "dove's dung" / ESV: "dove's dung" — Hebrew "chiryone"
       is debated. Some scholars believe it is a plant name (a type
       of seed pod); others take it literally as dried dung used for
       fuel or even food in extreme famine. Either interpretation
       reveals the severity of the siege.

v.25 — KJV: "fourscore pieces of silver" / ESV: "eighty shekels of
       silver" — A donkey's head was unclean food (Leviticus 11:2-7)
       and the least desirable part of an unclean animal. Eighty
       shekels was an enormous sum — approximately two years' wages
       for a laborer. Desperation destroys all normal values.

v.32 — KJV: "this son of a murderer" / ESV: "this murderer" —
       Elisha calls the king "son of a murderer" — likely referring
       to Ahab and Jezebel's murder of Naboth and the prophets.
       The king's violent intent proves he has inherited his
       parents' character.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Dothan       — A city approximately 10 miles north of Samaria,
             located on a hill with surrounding plains. Also where
             Joseph was sold by his brothers (Genesis 37:17). Its
             elevated position made the surrounding chariots of
             fire visible from above.

Chariots of  — The angelic army of God, appearing in military form.
Fire           First seen at Elijah's translation (2:11), now
             revealed surrounding Elisha. They represent God's
             invisible but ever-present protection of His servants.
             Psalm 68:17: "The chariots of God are twenty thousand,
             even thousands of angels."

Ben-hadad    — King of Syria (Aram-Damascus). The name may be a
             dynastic title rather than a personal name (meaning
             "son of Hadad," the storm god). He besieges Samaria
             with devastating effect, producing famine and
             cannibalism.

Samaria      — The capital city of the northern kingdom of Israel,
             built by Omri (1 Kings 16:24) on a hill. Its elevated
             position made it defensible but vulnerable to prolonged
             siege that cut off food and water supplies.

Sackcloth    — Rough, dark cloth made from goat or camel hair, worn
             against the skin as a sign of mourning or repentance.
             The king wore it secretly beneath his royal robes —
             outward show of kingship over hidden distress.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. God cares about borrowed axe heads (vv.5-7). No problem is too
   small for God's attention. The man's concern was real — he was
   poor, the tool was borrowed, and he could not replace it. God
   did not dismiss the concern as trivial. Bring your small problems
   to God with the same confidence you bring your large ones. He
   who commands armies also retrieves tools.

2. Fear is a vision problem (vv.15-17). The servant saw the Syrian
   army and panicked. Elisha saw the same army and was calm — because
   he also saw what the servant could not. Your circumstances have
   not changed, but your perception of them can. Ask God to open
   your eyes to the reality behind the visible. The chariots of fire
   are already there.

3. Mercy disarms enemies (vv.22-23). Elisha could have destroyed
   the blinded Syrians. Instead, he fed them. The result: "the bands
   of Syria came no more." Kindness accomplished what killing could
   not. When you have power over an enemy, mercy is more effective
   than revenge. Feed those who came to harm you — it confuses evil
   and breaks cycles of retaliation.

4. National sin produces national suffering (vv.28-29). The
   cannibalism in Samaria was not random horror — it was the
   covenant curse of Deuteronomy 28 fulfilled. Sin has consequences
   that extend beyond the individual to the community. When a
   nation abandons God, the most vulnerable suffer most.

5. Do not stop waiting one moment before deliverance (v.33). The
   king asked "what should I wait for the LORD any longer?" — and
   God's miraculous provision came the very next day (chapter 7).
   The darkest moment is often immediately before dawn. Do not
   abandon faith at the threshold of breakthrough.
