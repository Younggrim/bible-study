2 Kings - Chapter 23
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Josiah leads the greatest reformation in Judah's history: destroying high places, defiling Topheth, removing every trace of idolatry, and celebrating a Passover unmatched since the judges. Yet God's wrath remains because of Manasseh.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Unknown, traditionally attributed to Jeremiah or a prophetic
school. Originally one book with 1 Kings. Covers approximately 250
years (850-586 BC) from Elijah's translation through the Babylonian
exile. Key themes: Elisha's ministry (double portion), the decline of
both kingdoms, God's patience and warnings through prophets, the fall
of Israel to Assyria (722 BC), Judah's reforms and relapses, and the
final judgment — exile to Babylon (586 BC). The book ends in darkness
but with a glimmer of hope: Jehoiachin released from prison.

Historical Context: Chapter 23 describes the most sweeping religious
reform in the history of Judah — Josiah's systematic destruction of
every form of idolatry throughout the land. Following the discovery
of the Book of the Law (chapter 22), Josiah moves from conviction to
action with extraordinary thoroughness. He cleanses the Temple, tears
down the high places from Geba to Beersheba (the full extent of
Judah), defiles Topheth where children were sacrificed, removes astral
worship, destroys the shrines Solomon had built for foreign wives'
gods (standing for 350 years!), and extends his purge into former
Israelite territory — including the altar at Bethel that Jeroboam I
had erected. He then celebrates a Passover of unprecedented scope.
Yet the chapter ends with devastating irony: despite all this, "the
LORD turned not from the fierceness of his great wrath" (v.26).

The historical setting is the collapse of Assyria (c. 627-609 BC).
Josiah exploited Assyrian weakness to extend Judah's sovereignty
into the former northern kingdom, explaining his authority at Bethel
and the cities of Samaria (vv.15-20). His death at Megiddo in 609 BC
came when Pharaoh Neco marched to support the last Assyrian remnant
against Babylon at Carchemish. Josiah tried to stop Neco — perhaps
seeing Babylon as a lesser threat, or perhaps fulfilling his
covenantal obligation against Egypt (Deuteronomy 17:16). Whatever his
reasoning, he was killed, and with him died Judah's last hope of
reform. His four successors — Jehoahaz, Jehoiakim, Jehoiachin, and
Zedekiah — were uniformly evil. The chapter functions as Judah's
finest hour and its funeral: the greatest king, the greatest reform,
the greatest Passover — all unable to turn aside God's determined
judgment because of Manasseh's irreversible corruption.

Covenant Renewal and Temple Cleansing (vv.1-14): Josiah gathers all
Judah — elders, priests, prophets, and "all the people, both small
and great" (v.2) — and reads the entire Book of the Covenant aloud.
He then stands by the pillar (the traditional place of covenant-
making) and commits the nation to obedience "with all their heart and
all their soul" (v.3). The cleansing begins in the Temple itself:
vessels for Baal, Asherah, and the host of heaven are burned in the
Kidron Valley (v.4). He deposes the idolatrous priests, removes the
Asherah pole from the Temple, breaks down the houses of cult
prostitutes (v.7), defiles Topheth to prevent child sacrifice (v.10),
removes the horses and chariots dedicated to the sun god (v.11), and
destroys the altars Manasseh had built in the Temple courts (v.12).
He even defiles the high places Solomon built for Ashtoreth, Chemosh,
and Milcom (v.13) — shrines that had stood for over 350 years.

Bethel, the Passover, and Josiah's Death (vv.15-37): Josiah's reform
extends to Bethel, where he tears down Jeroboam's altar and high
place — burning it to powder and burning the Asherah (v.15). He then
fulfills the prophecy of 1 Kings 13:2: taking bones from nearby
tombs, he burns them on the altar to defile it (v.16). When he sees
the tomb of the unnamed prophet who originally spoke against this
altar (1 Kings 13), Josiah commands it be left undisturbed (vv.17-18).
He extends this purge through the cities of Samaria (v.19) and
executes the priests of the high places (v.20). Returning to
Jerusalem, he commands a Passover "as it is written in the book of
this covenant" (v.21) — a Passover unmatched since the days of the
judges (v.22). Verse 25 gives the ultimate eulogy: "like unto him
was there no king before him... nor did any like him arise after him."
Yet verse 26 shatters the hope: God's wrath remains kindled because
of Manasseh. Josiah dies at Megiddo fighting Pharaoh Neco (v.29),
and the rapid succession of evil kings — Jehoahaz (3 months),
Jehoiakim (11 years) — begins Judah's final descent.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Samaria — capital of the northern kingdom (Israel); falls to Assyria in 722 BC (ch.17). Jerusalem — capital of Judah; falls to Babylon in 586 BC (ch.25).
- Key locations: Jordan River (Elisha's miracles, chs.2, 5), Damascus (Syria/Aram), Nineveh (Assyrian capital), Babylon (where Judah is exiled).
- Sennacherib's invasion route threatens Jerusalem (chs.18-19). Exile destinations: Assyria (for Israel) and Babylon (for Judah).

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.3  — "And the king stood by a pillar, and made a covenant before
        the LORD, to walk after the LORD, and to keep his
        commandments and his testimonies and his statutes with all
        their heart and all their soul." (ESV) — Covenant renewal
        at its most comprehensive. "All their heart and all their
        soul" echoes the Shema (Deuteronomy 6:5). Josiah commits
        not just to belief but to obedience — commandments,
        testimonies, and statutes cover the full scope of Torah.
        "All the people stood to the covenant" — the entire nation
        joins. This is what covenantal faithfulness looks like:
        public, comprehensive, wholehearted.

v.10 — "And he defiled Topheth, which is in the valley of the
        children of Hinnom, that no man might make his son or his
        daughter to pass through the fire to Molech." (ESV) —
        Josiah made child sacrifice physically impossible. He did
        not merely prohibit it; he rendered the site unusable. True
        reform does not merely forbid sin — it removes the
        infrastructure of sin. "Defiled" means he made it
        ceremonially unclean so no one would approach it for
        sacred purposes again.

v.16 — "And as Josiah turned himself, he spied the sepulchres that
        were there in the mount, and sent, and took the bones out
        of the sepulchres, and burned them upon the altar, and
        polluted it, according to the word of the LORD which the
        man of God proclaimed." (ESV) — The fulfillment of 1 Kings
        13:2, spoken roughly 300 years earlier: "A son shall be
        born to the house of David, Josiah by name; and upon
        you shall he offer the priests of the high places, and
        men's bones shall be burnt upon you." Named prophecy,
        fulfilled to the letter. God's word never returns void,
        even after three centuries.

v.25 — "And like to him was there no king before him, that turned
        to the LORD with all his heart, and with all his soul, and
        with all his might, according to all the law of Moses;
        neither after him arose there any like him." (ESV) — The
        highest eulogy in Kings. "Heart, soul, and might" — the
        full Deuteronomy 6:5 formula. Josiah loved God completely,
        obeyed comprehensively, and reformed thoroughly. Yet even
        this was not enough to save the nation. The lesson is
        sobering: human faithfulness, however complete, cannot undo
        the accumulated consequences of generations of sin.

v.26 — "Notwithstanding the LORD turned not from the fierceness of
        his great wrath, wherewith his anger was kindled against
        Judah, because of all the provocations that Manasseh had
        provoked him withal." (ESV) — The chapter's devastating
        verdict. Despite the greatest king and the greatest reform,
        judgment stands. "Notwithstanding" — one word that crushes
        all hope of national restoration. Manasseh's sin was so
        deep, so pervasive, so corrupting that even Josiah's
        wholehearted obedience could not counterbalance it. This
        is the theology of irreversible corporate judgment.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.2  — Deuteronomy 31:10-13 — Moses commanded that the Law be read
        publicly every seven years at the Feast of Tabernacles.
        Josiah obeys this command — perhaps for the first time in
        generations — reading the covenant words to all the people.

v.3  — 2 Kings 11:14,17 — Joash's earlier covenant renewal,
        facilitated by Jehoiada the priest. Josiah's covenant
        follows the same pattern but is more comprehensive in both
        scope and execution. Each generation must renew its
        commitment — inherited faith must become personal faith.

v.10 — Jeremiah 7:31-32 — "They have built the high places of
        Topheth... to burn their sons and their daughters in the
        fire." Jeremiah prophesied during Josiah's reign that this
        valley would become a place of slaughter — judgment on the
        site of Judah's greatest sin.

v.16 — 1 Kings 13:1-3 — The original prophecy against Jeroboam's
        altar at Bethel: "Josiah by name" — one of the most
        specific named prophecies in Scripture, given approximately
        300 years before fulfillment. This validates the prophetic
        word and demonstrates God's sovereignty over history.

v.21 — Exodus 12:1-14 — The original Passover instructions. Josiah
        restored what had been neglected since the judges — proper
        observance of Israel's foundational redemption celebration.
        Passover remembers the lamb whose blood saved from judgment
        — a truth Judah desperately needed to recall.

v.25 — Deuteronomy 6:5 — "You shall love the LORD your God with
        all your heart, and with all your soul, and with all your
        might." Josiah is the only king explicitly said to have
        fulfilled this command completely. He is the Deuteronomic
        ideal — and still it was not enough without national
        repentance.

v.29 — 2 Chronicles 35:20-24 — The expanded account of Josiah's
        death at Megiddo. Neco explicitly warned Josiah that his
        march was God's will; Josiah's refusal to listen cost him
        his life. Even the best king can err in discernment.

v.34 — Jeremiah 22:11-12 — Jeremiah's prophecy about Jehoahaz
        (Shallum): "He shall not return thither any more: but he
        shall die in the place where they have led him captive."
        Fulfilled when Pharaoh Neco took Jehoahaz to Egypt.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/2-kings-23/
  Guzik draws attention to the thoroughness of Josiah's reform —
  it was not selective or partial but comprehensive and violent.
  He destroyed everything associated with false worship, from the
  Temple to Bethel to the cities of Samaria. Guzik also emphasizes
  the tragic irony: the greatest reform in Judah's history was
  insufficient to avert judgment. This demonstrates that external
  reformation, however thorough, cannot substitute for deep
  heart-change across a people. One king's faithfulness cannot
  atone for a nation's apostasy.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "Josiah stands as the noblest of kings — heart, soul, and might
  given wholly to God. He tore down what took generations to build,
  cleansed what decades had polluted, and restored what centuries
  had neglected. And yet — and here is the solemn word — it was
  not enough. One man's obedience cannot undo a nation's rebellion.
  Josiah is the proof that we need more than a reformer — we need
  a Redeemer. No earthly king, however righteous, can bear the
  weight of corporate sin. Only One greater than Josiah can turn
  away the fierce wrath of God — and He does so not by tearing
  down altars but by offering Himself upon one."

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.5  — KJV: "idolatrous priests" / ESV: "priests whom the kings of
       Judah had ordained" — Hebrew "kemarim" is a distinct word
       from the regular "kohanim" (Levitical priests). "Kemarim"
       refers specifically to pagan or illegitimate priests — those
       ordained not by God's law but by royal appointment for
       idolatrous service. KJV correctly identifies them as
       "idolatrous" priests; ESV describes their appointment.

v.7  — KJV: "the houses of the sodomites" / ESV: "the houses of
       the male cult prostitutes" — Hebrew "qedeshim" (literally
       "holy ones") refers to men dedicated to ritual sexual
       service in Canaanite fertility worship. KJV's "sodomites"
       imports a Genesis 19 reference; ESV's "male cult
       prostitutes" more accurately describes the religious
       context. These were not merely sexual sinners but
       participants in ritualized idolatry within the Temple
       precincts.

v.10 — KJV: "to pass through the fire to Molech" / ESV: "as an
       offering to Molech" — Again, ESV makes explicit what KJV
       renders euphemistically. "Passing through the fire" was
       child sacrifice — the burning of children alive as offerings
       to the Ammonite deity Molech. ESV removes any ambiguity
       about the nature of this practice.

v.13 — KJV: "mount of corruption" / ESV: "mount of corruption" —
       Both translations render Hebrew "har ha-mashchith" — a
       wordplay on "mount of anointing" (har ha-mishchah), the
       Mount of Olives. The hill of anointing became the hill of
       destruction/corruption because of Solomon's high places
       built there. The pun reveals how sacred geography becomes
       defiled geography through idolatry.

v.29 — KJV: "Pharaoh-nechoh king of Egypt went up against the
       king of Assyria" / ESV: "Pharaoh Neco king of Egypt went
       up to the king of Assyria" — A critical preposition. Hebrew
       "al" can mean "against" or "to/toward." KJV reads it as
       hostility; ESV correctly reads it as alliance — Neco went
       to aid the Assyrian remnant against Babylon at Carchemish
       (605 BC). The ESV rendering fits the historical context:
       Egypt was allied with Assyria against rising Babylon.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Josiah      — King of Judah (c. 640-609 BC), son of Amon, grandson
             of Manasseh. Became king at age eight. His name means
             "the LORD supports" or "the LORD heals." He is the
             last righteous king of Judah and receives the highest
             commendation in Kings. Prophesied by name 300 years
             before his birth (1 Kings 13:2). Killed at age 39 at
             Megiddo by Pharaoh Neco.

Topheth     — The place of child sacrifice in the Valley of Hinnom
             (Ge-Hinnom/Gehenna) south of Jerusalem. The name may
             derive from "toph" (drum, beaten to mask screams) or
             from a word meaning "fireplace/burning." Josiah
             defiled it permanently. Jeremiah prophesied it would
             become a valley of slaughter (Jeremiah 7:32; 19:6).
             Jesus later used "Gehenna" as the name for hell.

Bethel      — "House of God" — originally named by Jacob (Genesis
             28:19). Jeroboam I established it as a rival worship
             center with a golden calf (1 Kings 12:28-33). For
             over 300 years it was the chief site of idolatrous
             worship in the northern kingdom. Josiah's destruction
             of its altar fulfilled the prophecy of 1 Kings 13:2
             by name.

Passover    — Israel's foundational feast, commemorating the
             Exodus (Exodus 12). Josiah's Passover was unmatched
             "since the days of the judges" (v.22) — meaning it
             had been poorly observed or entirely neglected for
             centuries. Proper Passover observance required the
             written Torah as guide — which had only just been
             rediscovered. It pointed forward to Christ, the
             ultimate Passover Lamb (1 Corinthians 5:7).

Megiddo     — A strategic city in the Jezreel Valley where Josiah
             was killed by Pharaoh Neco (609 BC). Its location
             controlled the Via Maris — the main north-south trade
             and military route. "Armageddon" (Revelation 16:16)
             derives from "Har Megiddo" (Mountain of Megiddo).
             Throughout biblical history, Megiddo is associated
             with decisive battles and divine judgment.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. True reform is thorough, not partial (vv.4-20). Josiah did not
   merely condemn idolatry — he physically destroyed every site,
   object, and infrastructure of false worship. He burned, broke,
   defiled, and demolished. Half-measures in dealing with sin leave
   the door open for return. Whatever needs to be removed from your
   life — remove it completely. Do not leave the altar standing
   while removing only the offering.

2. Obedience cannot be inherited — it must be personally chosen
   (v.3). Josiah renewed the covenant for his generation because
   each generation must choose for itself. Your parents' faith,
   your church's tradition, your denomination's history — none of
   these substitute for your own wholehearted commitment. Stand by
   the pillar yourself. Make the covenant personal.

3. God's word fulfilled after 300 years is still God's word (v.16).
   The prophecy of 1 Kings 13 named Josiah three centuries before
   his birth. God's timing is not our timing, but His word never
   fails. If God has spoken a promise or a warning, it will come
   to pass — whether in three years or three hundred. Live in
   light of what God has said, not in light of how long it seems
   to take.

4. The greatest human effort cannot substitute for a Redeemer
   (v.26). Josiah did everything humanly possible — and it was not
   enough. The nation still faced judgment. This is the Old
   Testament's clearest demonstration that we need more than law-
   keeping, more than reformation, more than wholehearted human
   obedience. We need a Savior who can do what the best king could
   not: bear the accumulated weight of corporate sin and turn away
   God's wrath. Josiah points beyond himself to Christ.

5. The death of the righteous does not mean God has failed (v.29).
   Josiah died young in battle. The best king of Judah was cut down
   at 39. This seems wrong — unfair, even. But God's purposes
   extend beyond individual lifespans. Josiah fulfilled his calling
   completely; his death did not negate his faithfulness. Do not
   measure God's approval by length of life or ease of death.
   Faithfulness is measured by obedience, not by outcomes we can
   see.
