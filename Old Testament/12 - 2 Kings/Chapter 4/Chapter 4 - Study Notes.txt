2 Kings - Chapter 4
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Elisha multiplies a widow's oil to pay her debts and raises the Shunammite woman's dead son back to life. He also purifies poisoned stew and multiplies twenty loaves to feed a hundred men.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Unknown, traditionally attributed to Jeremiah or a prophetic
school. Originally one book with 1 Kings. Covers approximately 250
years (850-586 BC) from Elijah's translation through the Babylonian
exile. Key themes: Elisha's ministry (double portion), the decline
of both kingdoms, God's patience and warnings through prophets, the
fall of Israel to Assyria (722 BC), Judah's reforms and relapses,
and the final judgment — exile to Babylon (586 BC). The book ends
in darkness but with a glimmer of hope: Jehoiachin released from
prison.

Historical Context: Chapter 4 is the great miracle chapter of
Elisha's ministry — four distinct miracles that demonstrate the
double portion at work. Where Elijah's miracles were often dramatic
and confrontational (fire from heaven, drought), Elisha's miracles
are predominantly compassionate and restorative: providing for a
widow, giving a son, raising the dead, healing poison, multiplying
food. These miracles parallel and exceed Elijah's: Elijah multiplied
oil for one widow (1 Kings 17:14-16); Elisha multiplies oil to pay
all debts. Elijah raised one child (1 Kings 17:22); Elisha raises
one here and later revives a dead man by his bones (13:21). The
chapter reveals Elisha as a prophet of grace — God's power directed
toward ordinary people in ordinary need. The Shunammite woman's
story is one of the most detailed and emotionally powerful narratives
in the Old Testament, showing both the gift of life and the agony
of its loss — and the God who restores both.

The Widow's Oil (vv.1-7): A prophet's widow faces the loss of her
sons to debt slavery — a legal practice in ancient Israel (cf.
Leviticus 25:39; Nehemiah 5:5). Her husband "feared the LORD" but
died in poverty — faithfulness does not guarantee prosperity. Elisha
asks two questions: "What shall I do for thee?" and "What hast thou
in the house?" God's miracles often begin with what we already have.
She has "a pot of oil" — almost nothing. Elisha's instruction: borrow
empty vessels, "not a few." The miracle's extent depends on her faith
and preparation — the oil flows until the vessels run out. "The oil
stayed" (v.6) — it stopped not because God's supply was exhausted
but because her vessels were. The limitation was human capacity, not
divine provision. She sells the oil, pays her debts, and lives on
the surplus. God provides abundantly — enough to solve the crisis
and sustain the future.

The Shunammite's Son — Birth (vv.8-17): A wealthy woman in Shunem
recognizes Elisha as "a holy man of God" and provides him a permanent
room — bed, table, stool, candlestick. Her hospitality is deliberate
and sustained. When Elisha asks what he can do for her, she declines
political favors: "I dwell among mine own people" — she is content,
not ambitious. Gehazi identifies her hidden sorrow: no son, and her
husband is old. Elisha promises: "thou shalt embrace a son." Her
response — "do not lie unto thine handmaid" — reveals not doubt but
the pain of hope deferred. She has learned not to hope for what
seems impossible. But God gives what He promises: the son is born
"according to the time of life."

The Shunammite's Son — Death and Resurrection (vv.18-37): The child
grows, then one day collapses in the field: "My head, my head!" —
likely sunstroke or a cerebral hemorrhage. He dies on his mother's
lap at noon. Her response is extraordinary: she lays him on Elisha's
bed (the room she built for the prophet), tells no one the truth,
and rides urgently to Mount Carmel. When Gehazi asks "Is it well?"
she answers "It is well" — not denial but faith refusing to accept
death as final. Her words to Elisha are raw: "Did I desire a son of
my lord? did I not say, Do not deceive me?" — the gift has become
grief. Elisha sends Gehazi with his staff — but delegated power
fails. The mother refuses to leave Elisha: "I will not leave thee"
(echoing Elisha's own words to Elijah in 2:2,4,6). Elisha comes
personally, prays, and stretches himself upon the child — mouth to
mouth, eyes to eyes, hands to hands. Life returns: the child sneezes
seven times and opens his eyes. The resurrection requires personal
presence, persistent prayer, and intimate contact — not a distant
wave of a staff.

Death in the Pot (vv.38-41): During famine, a prophet unknowingly
adds poisonous wild gourds to the communal stew. "There is death in
the pot!" — the cry of those who recognize danger. Elisha casts in
meal (flour) and the poison is neutralized. The flour does not
naturally counteract poison — this is miraculous. God uses simple
means (flour, salt, a mantle) to accomplish supernatural results.
The prophet who raises the dead also removes death from daily bread.

Feeding a Hundred (vv.42-44): Twenty barley loaves and fresh grain
— enough for perhaps ten men — are brought to Elisha. He commands:
"Give unto the people, that they may eat." The servant protests:
"What, should I set this before an hundred men?" Elisha repeats the
command with God's promise: "They shall eat, and shall leave thereof."
They eat and have leftovers. This miracle directly foreshadows
Christ's feeding of the 5,000 (John 6:9-13) — the same pattern of
inadequate supply, divine command, human obedience, and supernatural
abundance with surplus.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Samaria — capital of the northern kingdom (Israel); falls to Assyria in 722 BC (ch.17). Jerusalem — capital of Judah; falls to Babylon in 586 BC (ch.25).
- Key locations: Jordan River (Elisha's miracles, chs.2, 5), Damascus (Syria/Aram), Nineveh (Assyrian capital), Babylon (where Judah is exiled).
- Sennacherib's invasion route threatens Jerusalem (chs.18-19). Exile destinations: Assyria (for Israel) and Babylon (for Judah).

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "What shall I do for you? tell me, what have you in the
        house?" (ESV) — God's miracles begin with what we already
        have. The widow had almost nothing — one pot of oil. But
        "almost nothing" in God's hands becomes more than enough.
        God does not ask for what you do not have; He multiplies
        what you bring.

v.6  — "There is not a vessel more. And the oil stayed." (ESV) —
        The oil stopped when the vessels ran out, not when God's
        supply was exhausted. Our capacity to receive limits God's
        provision more than His willingness to give. Borrow more
        vessels. Expand your faith. The oil is still flowing.

v.26 — "Is it well with you? is it well with your husband? is it
        well with the child? And she answered, It is well." (ESV) —
        Her child is dead on the prophet's bed, yet she says "It is
        well." This is not denial — it is faith that refuses to
        accept death as the final word when God's prophet is
        accessible.

v.43 — "They shall eat, and shall leave of it." (ESV) — God's
        provision always exceeds the need. Not barely enough — more
        than enough. The surplus proves the miracle and demonstrates
        God's generous character.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.1  — Leviticus 25:39 — Debt slavery was legal but regulated.
        God's law limited its duration and required humane treatment.
v.3  — 1 Kings 17:12-16 — Elijah and the widow of Zarephath. Oil
        multiplied for one meal at a time; Elisha multiplies enough
        to pay all debts and sustain the future.
v.16 — Genesis 18:10,14 — Sarah received the same promise and
        responded with similar disbelief. God gives sons to the
        barren as a sign of His sovereign power.
v.34 — 1 Kings 17:21 — Elijah stretched himself on the widow's son
        three times. Elisha stretches himself twice. Both prophets
        use intimate physical contact in resurrection.
v.41 — Exodus 15:25 — Moses cast a tree into bitter water to heal
        it. Elisha casts flour into poisoned stew. God uses simple
        means for supernatural healing.
v.43 — John 6:9-13 — Jesus feeds 5,000 with five loaves and two
        fish, with twelve baskets left over. The pattern is
        identical: inadequate supply, divine command, supernatural
        multiplication, surplus remaining.
v.44 — Matthew 14:20 — "They did all eat, and were filled: and
        they took up of the fragments that remained twelve baskets
        full." Christ's miracle exceeds Elisha's — the double
        portion's double portion.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/2-kings-4/
  Guzik emphasizes that the widow's oil miracle teaches that God's
  provision is limited only by our capacity to receive. "Borrow not
  a few" — God wanted to give more than she was prepared to contain.
  On the Shunammite, Guzik notes that her faith was tested to the
  extreme: the gift became grief, yet she pressed through to the
  prophet rather than accepting death as final. Her persistence
  models the prayer that will not take no for an answer.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "The oil flowed until the vessels failed. Think of it — the
  supply of heaven was not exhausted; the woman's preparation was
  exhausted. God was still pouring when she stopped receiving. How
  often this is true of us! We bring our small faith, our few
  vessels, our limited expectations — and God fills them all and
  then waits for more. But we have no more vessels. The fault is
  never in the supply; it is always in the capacity. Bring larger
  vessels, believer. Bring more of them. The oil has not stopped
  flowing — you have merely stopped preparing to receive."

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1  — KJV: "to take unto him my two sons to be bondmen" / ESV:
       "to take my two children to be his slaves" — Hebrew "abadim"
       = servants/slaves. Debt slavery was legal under Mosaic law
       (Leviticus 25:39) but limited to six years (Exodus 21:2).
       The creditor had legal right but not moral justification.

v.8  — KJV: "a great woman" / ESV: "a wealthy woman" — Hebrew
       "ishah gedolah" literally = "a great/prominent woman." KJV
       preserves the ambiguity (great in status, wealth, or
       character); ESV interprets it as wealth. Both are likely
       true — she was prominent in the community.

v.16 — KJV: "according to the time of life" / ESV: "about this
       time next year" — Hebrew "ka'et chayyah" is the same phrase
       used of Sarah's promised son (Genesis 18:10). It means "at
       the living/reviving time" — likely spring, the season of
       new life.

v.34 — KJV: "the flesh of the child waxed warm" / ESV: "the flesh
       of the child became warm" — The warming is the first sign of
       returning life. Elisha's posture — mouth to mouth, eyes to
       eyes, hands to hands — is not resuscitation technique but
       prophetic identification with the dead child, transferring
       life through intimate contact.

v.42 — KJV: "full ears of corn in the husk thereof" / ESV: "fresh
       ears of grain in his sack" — KJV "corn" is archaic English
       for grain (not maize). "In the husk" (KJV) vs. "in his sack"
       (ESV) reflects different readings of the Hebrew "tsiqlon" —
       either the grain's husk or a carrying bag.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Shunem       — A town in the territory of Issachar, near Mount
             Gilboa and the Jezreel Valley. Located on a main
             travel route, making it a natural stopping point for
             Elisha's regular journeys. Modern Solem/Sulam.

Shunammite   — A woman from Shunem. This unnamed woman is one of
             the most fully developed female characters in Kings —
             wealthy, perceptive, hospitable, faithful under
             extreme grief, and persistent in seeking God's prophet.

Gehazi       — Elisha's servant, who appears throughout chapters
             4-8. He serves as intermediary but sometimes fails
             (the staff does not raise the child). His character
             deteriorates — by chapter 5 he succumbs to greed.

Baal-        — A town whose location is uncertain, possibly in
shalishah      Ephraim. The man who brings firstfruits to Elisha
             rather than to the corrupt priesthood shows that
             faithful Israelites recognized the prophets as God's
             true representatives.

Sons of the  — Prophetic communities or guilds who lived together
Prophets       under the authority of a lead prophet (here Elisha).
             They were often poor, dependent on provision, and
             served as witnesses and students of prophetic ministry.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. God starts with what you have (v.2). "What hast thou in the
   house?" The widow had one pot of oil — nearly nothing. But God
   did not ask her to produce what she lacked; He multiplied what
   she possessed. Stop focusing on what you do not have. Bring what
   you do have to God, however small, and watch Him multiply it.

2. Your capacity limits your blessing (v.6). The oil stopped when
   the vessels ran out. How many vessels have you borrowed? How much
   room have you made for God's provision? Small faith receives
   small provision — not because God is stingy but because we stop
   preparing to receive. Expand your expectations.

3. Contentment and generosity attract God's attention (vv.8-13).
   The Shunammite was content ("I dwell among mine own people") and
   generous (she built a room for the prophet). She did not seek
   God's favor through ambition but received it through faithfulness.
   Serve God's servants without expecting return — God notices and
   rewards what others overlook.

4. Bring your dead things to God's presence (v.21). When her son
   died, the Shunammite laid him on the prophet's bed — in the
   place of God's presence. She did not accept death as final; she
   brought death to the one place where life could be restored.
   Whatever has died in your life — a dream, a relationship, a
   calling — bring it to God before you bury it.

5. God's provision exceeds the need (v.44). Twenty loaves fed a
   hundred men with leftovers. God does not merely meet needs — He
   overflows them. His character is generous, abundant, excessive.
   Trust Him not just for enough but for more than enough.
