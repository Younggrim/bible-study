Daniel - Chapter 4
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Nebuchadnezzar's personal testimony — written as a royal decree to all peoples. He dreams of a great tree sheltering the world, then cut down by divine command with only a stump remaining. Daniel interprets: the king himself will be driven mad, living as an animal for seven years until he acknowledges "the most High ruleth in the kingdom of men." It happens exactly as foretold, and the humbled king is restored, declaring: "those that walk in pride he is able to abase."

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Daniel son of nobility, exiled to Babylon in 605 BC.
Uniquely, this chapter is Nebuchadnezzar's OWN testimony —
written in his voice as a royal proclamation. Daniel preserved
it within his book. The chapter continues in Aramaic, the
international language.

Classification: Royal Testimony / Historical Narrative
Key Themes: Divine humbling of the proud, God's sovereignty over
all rulers, the danger of pride, madness as divine judgment,
restoration after humbling, the stump of hope

Historical Context: This event likely occurred late in
Nebuchadnezzar's reign, after his major building projects were
complete. Babylon was the wonder of the ancient world — the
Hanging Gardens, the Ishtar Gate, massive ziggurats, walls wide
enough for chariot races. The king's boast in v.30 ("Is not this
great Babylon, that I have built?") reflects genuine
architectural achievement — but attributed to himself rather than
God.

The condition described ("driven from men, eating grass like
oxen, hair like eagles' feathers, nails like birds' claws") is
consistent with a rare psychological disorder called boanthropy
or clinical lycanthropy — where a person believes themselves to
be an animal. Historical records show a gap in Nebuchadnezzar's
later reign where no building inscriptions appear — possibly the
seven-year period of madness.

The chapter is structured as ancient testimony: (1) Life before
humbling (vv.1-4), (2) The warning dream (vv.5-18), (3) The
interpretation (vv.19-27), (4) The fulfillment (vv.28-33),
(5) Restoration and praise (vv.34-37). It is one of the few
chapters in Scripture written by a Gentile king.

Structure:
- Nebuchadnezzar's Proclamation: "I was at rest..." (vv.1-4)
- The Dream: The Great Tree Cut Down (vv.5-18)
- Daniel's Reluctant Interpretation (vv.19-27)
- Twelve Months Later: "Is not this great Babylon?" (vv.28-33)
- Seven Years of Madness (v.33)
- Restoration: "I lifted up mine eyes unto heaven" (vv.34-37)

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Babylon (v.6, v.29, v.30): Capital of the Babylonian empire; place of Judah's exile (modern Iraq).

Charles Spurgeon:
  "Nebuchadnezzar was the greatest monarch the world had seen,
  and he was brought to eat grass like a beast. Learn this,
  proud man: there is no throne so high that God cannot topple
  it, no pride so deep that God cannot reach it. But oh, the
  mercy! He did not leave the king in the field forever. He
  RESTORED him — humbled, yes, but restored. God breaks in
  order to remake."

REFLECTION
------------------------------------
1. "Is not this great Babylon, that I HAVE BUILT?" (v.30). Where
   are you taking credit for what God has given? Career success,
   family, abilities, resources — all are gifts. The moment you
   claim "I built this," you're walking on Nebuchadnezzar's
   rooftop.

2. God gave twelve months of grace (v.29). Between the dream and
   the fulfillment, God waited a full year. He warned first.
   If you're being convicted of pride right now — this IS your
   twelve months. Repent before the discipline arrives.

3. "He lifted up mine eyes unto heaven" (v.34). Recovery began
   when he looked UP. If you've been brought low — by failure,
   by consequence, by discipline — the path back starts with
   lifting your eyes. Look up. Worship. Sanity returns.

4. "Those that walk in pride he is able to abase" (v.37). This
   isn't theory — it's testimony from the most powerful man of
   his era. If God could humble Nebuchadnezzar, He can humble
   you. The question is: will you humble yourself first, or wait
   for God to do it?

5. The stump remains (v.15). God's discipline preserves a root.
   If you're in a "stump season" — cut down, stripped bare —
   take hope. The roots are alive. Restoration is coming. God
   doesn't cut down to destroy; He cuts down to regrow.
