Daniel - Chapter 4
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Nebuchadnezzar's personal testimony — written as a royal decree to all peoples. He dreams of a great tree sheltering the world, then cut down by divine command with only a stump remaining. Daniel interprets: the king himself will be driven mad, living as an animal for seven years until he acknowledges "the most High ruleth in the kingdom of men." It happens exactly as foretold, and the humbled king is restored, declaring: "those that walk in pride he is able to abase."

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Daniel son of nobility, exiled to Babylon in 605 BC.
Uniquely, this chapter is Nebuchadnezzar's OWN testimony —
written in his voice as a royal proclamation. Daniel preserved
it within his book. The chapter continues in Aramaic, the
international language.

Classification: Royal Testimony / Historical Narrative
Key Themes: Divine humbling of the proud, God's sovereignty over
all rulers, the danger of pride, madness as divine judgment,
restoration after humbling, the stump of hope

Historical Context: This event likely occurred late in
Nebuchadnezzar's reign, after his major building projects were
complete. Babylon was the wonder of the ancient world — the
Hanging Gardens, the Ishtar Gate, massive ziggurats, walls wide
enough for chariot races. The king's boast in v.30 ("Is not this
great Babylon, that I have built?") reflects genuine
architectural achievement — but attributed to himself rather than
God.

The condition described ("driven from men, eating grass like
oxen, hair like eagles' feathers, nails like birds' claws") is
consistent with a rare psychological disorder called boanthropy
or clinical lycanthropy — where a person believes themselves to
be an animal. Historical records show a gap in Nebuchadnezzar's
later reign where no building inscriptions appear — possibly the
seven-year period of madness.

The chapter is structured as ancient testimony: (1) Life before
humbling (vv.1-4), (2) The warning dream (vv.5-18), (3) The
interpretation (vv.19-27), (4) The fulfillment (vv.28-33),
(5) Restoration and praise (vv.34-37). It is one of the few
chapters in Scripture written by a Gentile king.

Structure:
- Nebuchadnezzar's Proclamation: "I was at rest..." (vv.1-4)
- The Dream: The Great Tree Cut Down (vv.5-18)
- Daniel's Reluctant Interpretation (vv.19-27)
- Twelve Months Later: "Is not this great Babylon?" (vv.28-33)
- Seven Years of Madness (v.33)
- Restoration: "I lifted up mine eyes unto heaven" (vv.34-37)

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.17 — "to the intent that the living may know that the most High
        ruleth in the kingdom of men, and giveth it to whomsoever
        he will, and setteth up over it the basest of men" (KJV)
        — The PURPOSE of the entire ordeal: so that ALL living
        people know God rules. He gives kingdoms to whomever He
        chooses — even "the basest" (lowest) of men. God is not
        obligated to use the impressive.

v.30 — "The king spake, and said, Is not this great Babylon, that
        I have built for the house of the kingdom by the might of
        my power, and for the honour of my majesty?" (KJV) — The
        fatal boast. Seven words of pride: "I... I... my... my..."
        Self-glory where God-glory belonged. The words were still
        in his mouth when judgment fell (v.31).

v.34 — "And at the end of the days I Nebuchadnezzar lifted up
        mine eyes unto heaven, and mine understanding returned
        unto me, and I blessed the most High, and I praised and
        honoured him that liveth for ever" (KJV) — The moment of
        restoration: he LOOKED UP. Sanity returned when his gaze
        shifted from himself to God. Recovery began with worship.

v.35 — "and he doeth according to his will in the army of heaven,
        and among the inhabitants of the earth: and none can stay
        his hand, or say unto him, What doest thou?" (KJV) —
        Nebuchadnezzar's final theology: God's will is
        unstoppable in heaven AND earth. No one can resist His
        hand or question His authority. The most powerful man on
        earth confessing his own powerlessness.

v.37 — "those that walk in pride he is able to abase" (KJV) —
        The final sentence — the thesis of the entire chapter.
        God can bring down ANYONE. No height of human achievement
        is beyond His reach. This is both warning and testimony.

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.13 — "a watcher and an holy one" (KJV) / "a watcher, a holy
      one" (ESV) — Aramaic "ir veqaddish" = a wakeful one and a
      holy one. "Ir" (watcher) appears only in Daniel in the
      Bible — a class of angelic beings who "watch" and execute
      God's decrees. Always alert, always on duty.

v.16 — "seven times" (KJV/ESV) — Aramaic "shib'ah iddanin" =
      seven periods/seasons. Most interpret this as seven years,
      though some suggest shorter periods. The number seven
      signifies completeness — the discipline would last exactly
      as long as God determined.

v.27 — "break off thy sins by righteousness" (KJV) / "break off
      your sins by practicing righteousness" (ESV) — Aramaic
      "peraq" = to break off, separate from. Daniel counseled
      repentance — not as a guarantee of avoiding judgment, but
      as the appropriate response. "If it may be a lengthening
      of thy tranquillity" — perhaps mercy would extend.

v.37 — "abase" (KJV) / "humble" (ESV) — Aramaic "hashpalah" =
      to bring low, to humble. The word implies a forced lowering
      — God ACTIVELY brings down the proud. It is not merely that
      pride eventually fails; God personally intervenes against it.

v.13 — NET: "on" for KJV's "of my head upon"
v.16 — NET: "periods of time go by for" for KJV's "times pass over"
       WEB: "to him. Then" for KJV's "unto him; and"
v.27 — NET: "pleasing to you. Break away from your" for KJV's "acceptable unto thee, and break off thy"
       WEB: "your tranquility.”" for KJV's "thy tranquility."
v.37 — ASV: "I, Nebuchadnezzar," for KJV's "I Nebuchadnezzar"
       NET: "I, Nebuchadnezzar," for KJV's "I Nebuchadnezzar"
       WEB: "I, Nebuchadnezzar," for KJV's "I Nebuchadnezzar"

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
The Great Tree — Nebuchadnezzar himself: visible to all the
              earth, sheltering nations, magnificent in power.
              But a tree can be cut. No matter how tall, it grows
              at God's pleasure and falls at God's command.

The Stump Remains — Hope within judgment. The roots are
              preserved — restoration is possible. God's discipline
              is severe but not final. He cuts down but does not
              uproot completely.

Watcher — An angelic being unique to Daniel's vocabulary (Aramaic
              "ir"). A celestial sentinel executing God's decrees.
              Always alert, always obedient, carrying out divine
              judgments on earth.

Seven Times — The complete duration of discipline. Not arbitrary
              but precisely measured by God. Long enough to break
              the pride; not a moment longer than necessary.

Boanthropy — A psychological condition where a person believes
              they are an animal. Whether natural or supernatural
              in origin, God used it as the instrument of humbling.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Is not this great Babylon, that I HAVE BUILT?" (v.30). Where
   are you taking credit for what God has given? Career success,
   family, abilities, resources — all are gifts. The moment you
   claim "I built this," you're walking on Nebuchadnezzar's
   rooftop.

2. God gave twelve months of grace (v.29). Between the dream and
   the fulfillment, God waited a full year. He warned first.
   If you're being convicted of pride right now — this IS your
   twelve months. Repent before the discipline arrives.

3. "He lifted up mine eyes unto heaven" (v.34). Recovery began
   when he looked UP. If you've been brought low — by failure,
   by consequence, by discipline — the path back starts with
   lifting your eyes. Look up. Worship. Sanity returns.

4. "Those that walk in pride he is able to abase" (v.37). This
   isn't theory — it's testimony from the most powerful man of
   his era. If God could humble Nebuchadnezzar, He can humble
   you. The question is: will you humble yourself first, or wait
   for God to do it?

5. The stump remains (v.15). God's discipline preserves a root.
   If you're in a "stump season" — cut down, stripped bare —
   take hope. The roots are alive. Restoration is coming. God
   doesn't cut down to destroy; He cuts down to regrow.
