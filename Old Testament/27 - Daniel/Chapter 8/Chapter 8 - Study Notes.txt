Daniel - Chapter 8
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Daniel sees a ram with two horns (Media-Persia) trampling everything until a goat with one great horn (Greece/Alexander) shatters it. The great horn breaks and four horns replace it (Alexander's four generals). From one grows a "little horn" (Antiochus Epiphanes) who defiles the sanctuary, halts daily sacrifices, and tramples truth — for 2,300 days until the sanctuary is cleansed.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Daniel son of nobility, exiled to Babylon in 605 BC.
This vision came in Belshazzar's third year (approximately 551
BC). Importantly, chapter 8 returns to HEBREW — the language
shifts back because from here forward, the prophecies primarily
concern Israel rather than Gentile world powers.

Classification: Apocalyptic Vision / Predictive Prophecy
Key Themes: The Medo-Persian and Greek empires, Alexander the
Great, the four successor kingdoms, Antiochus Epiphanes as a
type of Antichrist, the desecration and restoration of the
temple, the sovereignty of God over future history

Historical Context: This is among the most precisely fulfilled
prophecies in Scripture. The ram's two horns (one higher than
the other, v.3) represent Media and Persia — with Persia
(the higher horn) dominant. The goat "from the west" with a
"notable horn" (v.5) is Alexander the Great, who conquered
Persia with stunning speed (the goat "touched not the ground,"
v.5 — flying across the map in just 10 years, 334-323 BC).

Alexander's sudden death at 32 ("the great horn was broken," v.8)
resulted in his empire splitting among four generals (the four
"notable horns"): Cassander (Macedonia), Lysimachus (Thrace/Asia
Minor), Seleucus (Syria/East), and Ptolemy (Egypt). From the
Seleucid line came Antiochus IV Epiphanes (175-164 BC), who
desecrated Jerusalem's temple, erected a statue of Zeus on the
altar, sacrificed a pig, and banned Torah observance — provoking
the Maccabean revolt.

The angel Gabriel himself interprets this vision (v.16) — one of
only two Old Testament appearances of Gabriel (the other is 9:21).
Gabriel says the vision is "for the time of the end" (v.17),
suggesting Antiochus is both a historical figure AND a type/
foreshadowing of the end-times Antichrist.

Structure:
- The Ram: Medo-Persia Conquering (vv.1-4)
- The Goat: Greece/Alexander Attacks (vv.5-8)
- The Great Horn Broken; Four Horns Rise (v.8)
- The Little Horn: Antiochus Epiphanes (vv.9-12)
- "How Long?" — 2,300 Days (vv.13-14)
- Gabriel Interprets the Vision (vv.15-26)
- Daniel Overcome and Astonished (v.27)

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.5  — "And as I was considering, behold, an he goat came from
        the west on the face of the whole earth, and touched not
        the ground: and the goat had a notable horn between his
        eyes" (KJV) — "Touched not the ground" — Alexander's
        conquests were so swift it was as if he flew. From Greece
        to India in 10 years. The "notable horn" = the singular
        genius of one man reshaping the known world.

v.12 — "and it cast down the truth to the ground; and it
        practised, and prospered" (KJV) — Truth cast to the
        GROUND. When Antiochus banned Torah reading, burned
        Scriptures, and punished observance with death — he was
        literally casting truth to the ground. And he
        "prospered" — temporarily. Evil prospers for a season.

v.14 — "And he said unto me, Unto two thousand and three hundred
        days; then shall the sanctuary be cleansed" (KJV) — 2,300
        days (approximately 6 years and 4 months). The period from
        Antiochus's initial interference (171 BC) to the
        Maccabean rededication of the temple (December 165 BC)
        fits remarkably. The sanctuary WILL be cleansed — evil's
        time is measured.

v.25 — "he shall also stand up against the Prince of princes; but
        he shall be broken without hand" (KJV) — The "Prince of
        princes" = God Himself (or Messiah). Antiochus ultimately
        stood against GOD. And "broken without hand" — not by
        human armies but by divine intervention (Antiochus died of
        a mysterious, agonizing disease). Same phrase as the stone
        "cut without hands" in 2:34.

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1  — NOTE: Chapter 8 returns to HEBREW from the Aramaic section
      (2:4b-7:28). The remainder of Daniel (chs.8-12) is in
      Hebrew because these visions concern Israel specifically,
      not the Gentile empires generally.

v.9  — "little horn" (KJV/ESV) — Hebrew "qeren mits'irah" = a
      horn from smallness/insignificance. Antiochus began as a
      minor hostage in Rome before seizing the Seleucid throne.
      "Little" in origin, enormous in impact. Note: this is a
      DIFFERENT little horn from Daniel 7:8 (different empire,
      different time).

v.14 — "cleansed" (KJV) / "restored to its rightful state" (ESV)
      — Hebrew "nitsdaq" = to be justified/vindicated/put right.
      Related to "tsedek" (righteousness). The sanctuary would
      be declared RIGHTEOUS again — not just cleaned physically
      but restored to proper, holy function.

v.16 — "Gabriel" (KJV/ESV) — Hebrew "Gavri'el" = "mighty one of
      God" or "God is my strength." One of only two angels named
      in Scripture (with Michael). Gabriel appears here and in
      9:21, then 500 years later announces Christ's birth (Luke
      1:19,26). The same messenger for Israel's darkest hour and
      brightest hope.

v.1 — NET: "King Belshazzar’s reign," for KJV's "the reign of king Belshazzar"
       WEB: "to me," for KJV's "unto me"
v.9 — ASV: "glorious [land]." for KJV's "pleasant land."
       NET: "but it grew to be very great" for KJV's "which waxed exceeding great,"
       WEB: "Out" for KJV's "And out"
v.14 — NET: "“To 2,300 evenings" for KJV's "Unto two thousand"
       WEB: "evenings and mornings. Then" for KJV's "days; then shall"
v.16 — ASV: adds "[the banks of]"
       NET: "the Ulai. It called out, “Gabriel, enable" for KJV's "Ulai, which called, and said, Gabriel, make"

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
The Notable Horn — Alexander the Great (356-323 BC). Conquered
              the Persian Empire in 10 years, spreading Greek
              culture from Egypt to India. Died suddenly at 32,
              and his empire fractured into four.

The Little Horn (ch.8) — Antiochus IV Epiphanes (175-164 BC).
              Seleucid king who desecrated the Jerusalem temple,
              banned Jewish worship, and provoked the Maccabean
              revolt. A historical figure and a TYPE of the future
              Antichrist.

2,300 Days — The duration of the desecration period. From
              Antiochus's interference to the temple's
              rededication. God measured the suffering precisely
              — it would last exactly as long as He allowed.

Sanctuary Cleansed — The restoration of proper temple worship,
              historically fulfilled in the Maccabean rededication
              (165 BC), now celebrated as Hanukkah (the Feast of
              Dedication, John 10:22).

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. God names empires before they exist (vv.20-21). Persia and
   Greece are named by NAME — 200 years before Alexander was
   born. Nothing surprises God. Whatever "empire" looms over
   your life — He named it before it arose and He has already
   determined its end.

2. Evil prospers for a measured time (v.12). "It practised, and
   prospered." Temporarily. 2,300 days — not forever. If you
   watch evil prospering today, remember: its days are NUMBERED.
   God has a "then" (v.14) — "THEN shall the sanctuary be
   cleansed."

3. "Broken without hand" (v.25). God doesn't always use armies
   or politics to end oppression. Sometimes He simply... ends it.
   A mysterious disease, an unexpected death, a sudden collapse.
   God has tools you cannot see.

4. Gabriel explains (vv.16-17). God doesn't leave Daniel to
   figure it out alone. He sends an interpreter. When you're
   confused by what you see in the world — ask. "If any of you
   lack wisdom, let him ask of God" (James 1:5). Understanding
   is available.

5. Daniel was "astonished" and "sick certain days" (v.27). Some
   revelations are HEAVY. The weight of seeing future suffering
   physically affected Daniel. If the state of the world
   burdens you — you're in good company. Feel the weight, then
   look at the throne.
