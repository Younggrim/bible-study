Daniel - Chapter 1
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Daniel and three young Hebrew nobles are carried captive to Babylon in 605 BC during Nebuchadnezzar's first siege. Selected for royal training, they refuse the king's food and wine, choosing faithfulness over compromise. God rewards their resolve with wisdom and understanding ten times greater than all the magicians of Babylon.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Daniel son of nobility, exiled to Babylon in 605 BC.
Daniel was likely a teenager of royal or noble descent (v.3) when
taken captive. He served in Babylonian and Persian courts for
over 60 years, writing this book in both Hebrew (chs.1, 8-12)
and Aramaic (chs.2:4b-7:28).

Classification: Historical Narrative / Prophetic Literature
Key Themes: Faithfulness in exile, God's sovereignty over
nations, divine wisdom surpassing pagan knowledge, the test of
compromise, identity preservation under pressure

Historical Context: In 605 BC, Nebuchadnezzar defeated Pharaoh
Necho at the Battle of Carchemish and immediately marched on
Jerusalem. This was the FIRST of three deportations (605, 597,
586 BC). Rather than destroy the city immediately, Nebuchadnezzar
took hostages — the brightest young nobles — as a strategic move
to ensure Judah's compliance and to assimilate their talent into
Babylonian culture.

The program Daniel entered was essentially a three-year
"re-education" — learn Chaldean language, literature, religion,
and customs. The name changes were deliberate: strip away Hebrew
identity (names referencing Yahweh and El) and replace them with
Babylonian gods. Daniel ("God is my judge") became Belteshazzar
("Bel protect his life"). The food test wasn't merely dietary —
it was offered to idols first. To eat was to participate in
pagan worship.

God's sovereignty frames the entire chapter: "the Lord GAVE
Jehoiakim king of Judah into his hand" (v.2). Even exile was
God's doing. And God "brought Daniel into favour" (v.9) with
his captors. The sovereign God works on BOTH ends — allowing
the exile AND protecting His faithful within it.

Structure:
- The Historical Setting: Babylon Conquers Judah (vv.1-2)
- The Selection and Re-Education Program (vv.3-7)
- Daniel's Resolve: The Food Test (vv.8-16)
- God's Reward: Wisdom Beyond All Others (vv.17-21)

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "And the Lord gave Jehoiakim king of Judah into his hand,
        with part of the vessels of the house of God: which he
        carried into the land of Shinar to the house of his god"
        (KJV) — "The Lord GAVE." Exile wasn't Nebuchadnezzar's
        victory — it was God's sovereign act of discipline. Even
        the temple vessels moving to Babylon were under God's
        direction.

v.8  — "But Daniel purposed in his heart that he would not defile
        himself with the portion of the king's meat, nor with the
        wine which he drank" (KJV) — "Purposed in his heart" —
        the decision was made BEFORE the temptation arrived. This
        is the key: resolve precedes resistance. Daniel didn't
        deliberate in the moment; he had already decided.

v.9  — "Now God had brought Daniel into favour and tender love
        with the prince of the eunuchs" (KJV) — God works in
        pagan hearts. The same God who allowed the exile now
        softens the captor. Favor in hostile territory is God's
        specialty (cf. Joseph in Egypt, Esther in Persia).

v.17 — "As for these four children, God gave them knowledge and
        skill in all learning and wisdom: and Daniel had
        understanding in all visions and dreams" (KJV) — God
        GAVE. Every advantage was divine gift. Their faithfulness
        didn't earn the wisdom — God's grace bestowed it.

v.20 — "And in all matters of wisdom and understanding, that the
        king enquired of them, he found them ten times better than
        all the magicians and astrologers that were in all his
        realm" (KJV) — TEN TIMES. Not marginally better — an
        order of magnitude. God's wisdom doesn't just match the
        world's; it dwarfs it entirely.

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.2  — "Shinar" (KJV/ESV) — Hebrew "Shin'ar" = the ancient name
      for Babylonia (cf. Genesis 11:2 — the Tower of Babel was
      built in Shinar). The use of this archaic name links
      Nebuchadnezzar's empire to humanity's oldest rebellion
      against God.

v.4  — "Chaldeans" (KJV/ESV) — Hebrew "Kasdim" = both an ethnic
      group and a priestly/scholarly class in Babylon. These were
      the learned elite — astronomers, diviners, interpreters.
      Daniel was being trained to JOIN their ranks.

v.7  — "Belteshazzar" (KJV/ESV) — Babylonian "Balat-su-usur" =
      "Bel, protect his life." Daniel's Hebrew name meant "God
      is my judge." The renaming was an attempt to transfer
      allegiance from Yahweh to Bel (Marduk). It failed.

v.8  — "defile himself" (KJV) / "defile himself" (ESV) — Hebrew
      "yitga'al" = to make oneself ceremonially unclean. The food
      had been offered to idols (Deuteronomy 32:38) and included
      unclean animals (Leviticus 11). This wasn't preference —
      it was Torah obedience.

v.2 — ASV: "treasure-house" for KJV's "treasure house"
       NET: "God. He brought them to" for KJV's "God: which he carried into"
       WEB: "God; and" for KJV's "God: which"
v.4 — ASV: "king`s palace;" for KJV's "king’s palace,"
       NET: "physical defect and who were handsome," for KJV's "blemish, but"

       NET: "court officials for permission" for KJV's "king’s meat, nor with the wine which he drank: therefore ..."
       WEB: "drank. Therefore" for KJV's "drank: therefore"

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Purposed in His Heart — Pre-decided conviction. The resolve was
              fixed before the test came. A settled, internal
              commitment that precedes external obedience.

The King's Meat — Royal food from Nebuchadnezzar's table,
              previously offered to Babylonian idols. To eat was
              to participate in idol worship and violate Mosaic
              dietary law simultaneously.

Pulse — Hebrew "zero'im" = vegetables, seeds, plant-based food.
              Not a permanent diet but a ten-day test proving God
              could sustain without compromise.

Ten Times Better — An order of magnitude of superiority. God's
              wisdom through faithful vessels doesn't merely
              compete with worldly wisdom — it overwhelms it.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Daniel purposed in his heart" (v.8). The battle is won or
   lost BEFORE the temptation arrives. What have you already
   decided? What convictions are settled so deeply that no
   pressure can move them? Purpose it NOW — before the test.

2. God gives favour in hostile territory (v.9). You may be in a
   workplace, school, or family that is spiritually hostile.
   God can soften hearts you thought were stone. Don't assume
   hostility is permanent — pray for favour.

3. Faithfulness in small things precedes promotion (vv.17-20).
   Daniel was faithful with FOOD before God trusted him with
   KINGDOMS. What "small" area of obedience are you
   rationalizing away? The big calling requires small
   faithfulness first.

4. The world will try to rename you (v.7). Culture wants to
   redefine your identity — strip away your God-given purpose
   and replace it with its own labels. But Daniel never forgot
   who he was. Don't let the world's renaming stick.

5. "The Lord GAVE" (v.2). Even exile was God's sovereign act.
   If you're in a season that feels like captivity — job loss,
   illness, relocation — remember: God gave you into this
   season. He has purposes IN it, not just AFTER it.
