Daniel - Chapter 5
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Belshazzar hosts a drunken feast using the sacred vessels stolen from Jerusalem's temple. A disembodied hand writes on the wall: MENE MENE TEKEL UPHARSIN. The terrified king summons Daniel, who delivers God's verdict: "Thou art weighed in the balances, and art found wanting." That very night, Babylon falls to the Medes and Persians, and Belshazzar is slain.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Daniel son of nobility, exiled to Babylon in 605 BC.
By this chapter, Daniel is approximately 80 years old — having
served through the entire Neo-Babylonian empire. This chapter
remains in the Aramaic section (2:4b-7:28).

Classification: Historical Narrative / Prophetic Judgment
Key Themes: The sacrilege of misusing holy things, God's patience
running out, divine judgment weighed and measured, the suddenness
of divine reckoning, the fall of empires in a single night

Historical Context: Belshazzar was NOT Nebuchadnezzar's son but
his grandson (through Nabonidus). The term "father" (v.2) means
"ancestor/predecessor" in Aramaic. Secular records confirm
Belshazzar as co-regent while Nabonidus was away. The date is
October 12, 539 BC — the night Cyrus the Great's general
Ugbaru diverted the Euphrates River and Persian troops entered
under Babylon's walls through the dry riverbed.

The feast was an act of supreme arrogance. The Persians were
literally camped outside the walls, and Belshazzar threw a party
for 1,000 lords — trusting in Babylon's "impregnable" defenses.
Using the temple vessels was deliberate blasphemy: a mocking
declaration that Marduk had triumphed over Yahweh. God's response
was immediate and terrifying.

Daniel had been forgotten by the new court (the queen mother had
to remind Belshazzar of his existence, vv.10-12). Yet God's
prophet was still there — still ready, still faithful, still
speaking truth to power even after decades of silence. Daniel
refused the rewards (v.17) and delivered the devastating
interpretation without compromise.

Structure:
- Belshazzar's Feast: Sacrilege with Holy Vessels (vv.1-4)
- The Handwriting on the Wall (vv.5-9)
- The Queen Mother Remembers Daniel (vv.10-12)
- Daniel Before the King: Refusing Rewards (vv.13-17)
- The Sermon: Remember Nebuchadnezzar's Lesson (vv.18-21)
- The Verdict: "Thou Knewest All This" (vv.22-24)
- MENE MENE TEKEL UPHARSIN Interpreted (vv.25-28)
- That Night: Babylon Falls, Belshazzar Slain (vv.29-31)

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.5  — "In the same hour came forth fingers of a man's hand, and
        wrote over against the candlestick upon the plaister of
        the wall of the king's palace: and the king saw the part
        of the hand that wrote" (KJV) — "In the same hour." The
        moment sacrilege reached its peak, God's hand appeared.
        Not a voice, not a prophet — a HAND. Visible, undeniable,
        terrifying. God writing His own verdict.

v.22 — "And thou his son, O Belshazzar, hast not humbled thine
        heart, though thou knewest all this" (KJV) — The damning
        phrase: "THOU KNEWEST." Belshazzar had Nebuchadnezzar's
        testimony — the lesson of chapter 4 — and ignored it.
        Judgment falls heaviest on those who KNOW the truth and
        reject it anyway.

v.25-27 — "MENE, MENE, TEKEL, UPHARSIN... God hath numbered thy
        kingdom, and finished it... Thou art weighed in the
        balances, and art found wanting... Thy kingdom is divided,
        and given to the Medes and Persians" (KJV) — Three
        verdicts: NUMBERED (your days are counted and ended),
        WEIGHED (measured against God's standard and found
        deficient), DIVIDED (your kingdom torn apart and given
        to another). Comprehensive judgment.

v.30 — "In that night was Belshazzar the king of the Chaldeans
        slain" (KJV) — Five devastating words. "In that night."
        No delay, no appeal, no second chance. Between the feast
        and the dawn, an empire ended. This is the speed of divine
        judgment when God's patience expires.

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.2  — "father Nebuchadnezzar" (KJV) / "father Nebuchadnezzar"
      (ESV) — Aramaic "ab" can mean father, grandfather, or
      predecessor. Belshazzar was actually the grandson (through
      his mother, Nebuchadnezzar's daughter, married to
      Nabonidus). This Aramaic usage is not inaccurate — it's
      how the language works.

v.25 — "MENE, MENE, TEKEL, UPHARSIN" (KJV/ESV) — Aramaic words
      functioning as both nouns and verbs. MENE (mena) = numbered/
      a mina (unit of weight). TEKEL (teqal) = weighed/a shekel.
      UPHARSIN (peres) = divided/a half-mina. A wordplay on
      weights and measures AND divine judgments simultaneously.

v.28 — "PERES" (KJV/ESV) — The singular form of UPHARSIN
      (pharsin). Also a pun on "Paras" = Persia. Triple meaning:
      (1) divided, (2) a half-mina coin, (3) Persia. The kingdom
      is divided and given to PERSIA — the name embedded in the
      word itself.

v.31 — "Darius the Median" (KJV/ESV) — Aramaic "Daryavesh
      Mada'ah." His identity is debated: possibly Ugbaru/Gobryas
      (Cyrus's general), or Cyrus himself under a throne name.
      The point is clear: Babylon's rule ended and Medo-Persian
      rule began that very night.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
MENE — "Numbered." God has counted the days of your kingdom and
              brought it to an end. Every regime has a divine
              expiration date — and Belshazzar's had arrived.

TEKEL — "Weighed." Placed on God's balance and found deficient.
              God's standard is not relative (compared to others)
              but absolute (compared to His holiness). All fall
              short.

UPHARSIN/PERES — "Divided." The kingdom torn in two and given to
              Medes and Persians. Also a wordplay on "Persia" —
              the conqueror's name hidden in the verdict.

The Handwriting on the Wall — Now a common English idiom meaning
              "an ominous warning of doom." Originates entirely
              from this chapter. When the warning is on the wall,
              judgment is imminent.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Thou knewest all this" (v.22). Belshazzar's greatest sin
   wasn't ignorance — it was KNOWLEDGE without obedience. He
   knew Nebuchadnezzar's story and ignored it. What truth do
   you KNOW but haven't acted on? Knowledge increases
   accountability.

2. God keeps count (v.26 — MENE). Every day is numbered. Every
   opportunity for repentance is counted. The calendar doesn't
   stretch infinitely. What are you assuming you'll "get to
   eventually" that may have fewer days left than you think?

3. You are being weighed (v.27 — TEKEL). Not against other
   people's standards but against GOD's. The question isn't "Am
   I better than most?" but "Do I measure up to His holiness?"
   The answer is always "no" — which is exactly why you need
   a Savior.

4. The sacred can be profaned (vv.2-4). Holy things — Scripture,
   worship, the Lord's Supper, God's name — can be treated
   casually until they feel common. When you handle holy things
   carelessly, you're drinking from temple vessels at a pagan
   feast. Guard your reverence.

5. "In that night" (v.30). How quickly everything can change.
   Belshazzar went to bed a king and never woke up. Don't
   presume on tomorrow. Today — right now — is the day of
   salvation (2 Corinthians 6:2).
