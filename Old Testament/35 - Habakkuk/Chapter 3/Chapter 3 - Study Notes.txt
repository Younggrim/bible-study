Habakkuk - Chapter 3
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Habakkuk's prayer-psalm recounts God's mighty acts in theophany and concludes with radical faith: "Though the fig tree should not blossom... yet I will rejoice in the LORD."


AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Habakkuk the prophet. Prophesied approximately 609-605 BC
(Babylonian rise). Key theme: the problem of evil — why does God
use the wicked to punish the less wicked? God's answer: 'the just
shall live by his faith' (2:4) — quoted three times in the NT
(Romans 1:17, Galatians 3:11, Hebrews 10:38). Ends with a hymn
of trust despite circumstances (ch.3).

Classification: Prophetic Hymn/Prayer — The Song of Absolute Trust
Key Themes: Theophany and God's power recalled, trembling before
divine majesty, faith that triumphs over circumstance, joy
independent of provision, God as personal strength

Historical Context: Chapter 3 is the crown jewel of Habakkuk — a
prayer set to music (note "upon Shigionoth" in v.1 and "To the
chief singer on my stringed instruments" in v.19). This is not
merely a poem but a liturgical hymn designed for congregational
worship. The prophet who wrestled with God in chapters 1-2 now
WORSHIPS God in chapter 3.

The structure mirrors the book's journey: Habakkuk recalls God's
mighty acts in history (vv.3-15) — the theophany at Sinai, the
Red Sea crossing, the conquest of Canaan — and this remembrance
produces trembling (v.16) followed by the most magnificent
confession of faith in the Old Testament (vv.17-19). The
progression is: from complaint (ch.1) to faith (ch.2) to worship
(ch.3). This is the pattern for every believer's journey through
suffering.

Verses 17-19 are among the most beloved in all Scripture. The
prophet imagines the WORST possible scenario — total agricultural
and economic collapse — and declares that EVEN THEN he will
rejoice in the LORD. This is faith stripped of every external
support. When fig trees, vines, olives, fields, flocks, and
herds all fail — God alone remains. And God alone is enough.

Structure:
- Superscription and Prayer for Revival (vv.1-2)
- Theophany: God's Coming in Glory (vv.3-7)
- God's Power Over Nature and Nations (vv.8-12)
- God's Salvation of His People (vv.13-15)
- The Prophet's Trembling and Waiting (v.16)
- The Supreme Confession of Faith (vv.17-19)

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "O LORD, I have heard thy speech, and was afraid: O LORD,
        revive thy work in the midst of the years, in the midst
        of the years make known; in wrath remember mercy" (KJV) /
        "O LORD, I have heard the report of you, and your work,
        O LORD, do I fear. In the midst of the years revive it;
        in wrath remember mercy" (ESV) — The prophet's posture
        has shifted completely from chapter 1. No more "how long?"
        — now "revive your work!" And the plea "in wrath remember
        mercy" — even in judgment, let mercy prevail. This is
        mature faith: acknowledging God's righteous wrath while
        appealing to His mercy.

v.13 — "Thou wentest forth for the salvation of thy people, even
        for salvation with thine anointed" (KJV) / "You went out
        for the salvation of your people, for the salvation of
        your anointed" (ESV) — The PURPOSE of all God's power:
        SALVATION. Every theophany, every shaking of nations,
        every splitting of seas — was for the rescue of His
        people. God's power is never arbitrary; it is always
        redemptive.

v.16 — "When I heard, my belly trembled; my lips quivered at the
        voice: rottenness entered into my bones, and I trembled
        in myself, that I might rest in the day of trouble" (KJV)
        — Habakkuk's response to the theophany: physical terror.
        Yet the RESULT of this trembling is REST. He trembles NOW
        so that he can rest THEN. The one who has seen God's power
        in worship can be calm in the day of crisis.

v.17 — "Although the fig tree shall not blossom, neither shall
        fruit be in the vines; the labour of the olive shall
        fail, and the fields shall yield no meat; the flock shall
        be cut off from the fold, and there shall be no herd in
        the stalls" (KJV) / "Though the fig tree should not
        blossom, nor fruit be on the vines, the produce of the
        olive fail and the fields yield no food, the flock be cut
        off from the fold and there be no herd in the stalls"
        (ESV) — Six items of total loss: figs, vines, olives,
        fields, flocks, herds. Every source of sustenance gone.
        This is not hypothetical — Babylonian invasion would bring
        exactly this devastation. Habakkuk pre-decides his
        response BEFORE the crisis arrives.

v.18 — "Yet I will rejoice in the LORD, I will joy in the God of
        my salvation" (KJV) / "yet I will rejoice in the LORD; I
        will take joy in the God of my salvation" (ESV) — The
        most defiant "YET" in Scripture. After listing total loss,
        the prophet CHOOSES joy. Not joy in circumstances (they
        are destroyed), not joy in provision (it is gone), but
        joy in the LORD HIMSELF. This is faith at its purest: God
        alone as the source of joy.

v.19 — "The LORD God is my strength, and he will make my feet
        like hinds' feet, and he will make me to walk upon mine
        high places" (KJV) / "GOD, the Lord, is my strength; he
        makes my feet like the deer's; he makes me tread on my
        high places" (ESV) — The final declaration. God is not
        just my comfort — He is my STRENGTH. "Hinds' feet" = the
        sure-footedness of a mountain deer on treacherous terrain.
        God enables you to navigate the heights that would destroy
        others. "My high places" — not just survival but TRIUMPH.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.2   — Psalm 85:6 — "Wilt thou not revive us again: that thy
         people may rejoice in thee?"
v.3   — Deuteronomy 33:2 — "The LORD came from Sinai, and rose
         up from Seir; he shined forth from mount Paran" — the
         same theophanic imagery.
v.11  — Joshua 10:12-13 — The sun and moon standing still at
         Gibeon — the event Habakkuk recalls.
v.13  — Exodus 14:13-14 — "Stand still, and see the salvation of
         the LORD" — God goes forth for His people at the Red Sea.
v.17  — Joel 1:10-12 — Agricultural devastation as divine
         judgment; the same crops listed.
v.18  — Philippians 4:4 — "Rejoice in the Lord alway: and again
         I say, Rejoice" — Paul echoes Habakkuk from prison.
v.19  — Psalm 18:33 — "He maketh my feet like hinds' feet, and
         setteth me upon my high places."
v.19  — 2 Samuel 22:34 — David's identical declaration after
         deliverance from enemies.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1 — "Shigionoth" — plural of "Shiggaion" (cf. Psalm 7 title).
      The exact meaning is debated — possibly a passionate,
      emotional type of song, or a dirge-like melody. It
      indicates this prayer was set to a specific musical form
      for liturgical worship.

v.2 — "revive" — Hebrew "chayah" = to give life, restore to
      life, make alive again. Habakkuk asks God to make His work
      LIVE again — to bring fresh vitality to what seems dead.
      This is a prayer for spiritual awakening.

v.4 — "horns coming out of his hand" (KJV) / "rays flashed from
      his hand" (ESV) — Hebrew "qarnayim" = horns OR rays (both
      meanings). The same word is used of Moses' face "shining"
      in Exodus 34:29-30. God's power radiates from His hands
      like beams of light — yet this is only the "hiding" (veil)
      of His power.

v.16 — "that I might rest in the day of trouble" — Hebrew "anuach"
       = to rest, be quiet, settle. The purpose of present
       trembling before God is FUTURE rest in crisis. Worship
       now produces peace later.

v.19 — "To the chief singer on my stringed instruments" — a
       musical notation confirming this chapter was used in
       corporate worship. "Neginoth" = stringed instruments.
       This theological wrestling match ends in the hymnal.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "In wrath remember mercy" (v.2). Even when you know judgment
   is deserved, you can appeal to mercy. This is not denying
   God's justice but trusting His character. Every prayer for
   mercy acknowledges that judgment would be fair — but asks
   God to be MORE than fair. He delights to show mercy (Micah
   7:18).

2. Remember God's past faithfulness (vv.3-15). When the present
   is terrifying, recall what God has ALREADY done. Habakkuk
   recounts Sinai, the Red Sea, the conquest — and this
   REMEMBERING produces faith for the future. Your testimony is
   your arsenal. What has God already done for you?

3. Pre-decide your response before the crisis arrives (vv.17-18).
   Habakkuk makes his declaration of faith BEFORE the Babylonians
   come. He decides in advance: "Even if everything fails, I will
   rejoice." Make your "yet I will rejoice" decision NOW — before
   the diagnosis, before the loss, before the collapse. Decisions
   made in peace sustain you in crisis.

4. Joy in God is independent of circumstances (v.18). Biblical
   joy is not happiness (which depends on happenings). It is a
   deliberate choice to find your delight in WHO GOD IS, not in
   what He GIVES. When every gift is stripped away, the Giver
   remains. And the Giver is enough.

5. God makes you sure-footed on dangerous heights (v.19). The
   deer doesn't avoid the mountain — it navigates it with grace.
   God doesn't always remove the treacherous terrain; He gives
   you the feet to walk it. Your high places of difficulty
   become your high places of victory.
