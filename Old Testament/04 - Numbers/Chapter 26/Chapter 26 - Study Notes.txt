Numbers - Chapter 26
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A second census numbers the new generation at 601,730 — nearly the same total, confirming God's faithfulness despite forty years of death.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses. Numbers is the fourth book of the Pentateuch, written during
the wilderness period (approximately 1445-1405 BC). Hebrew title: "Bemidbar"
— "In the wilderness." The book records Israel's 38 years of wilderness
wandering between Sinai and the Promised Land — a journey that should have
taken 11 days (Deuteronomy 1:2) but took 40 years due to unbelief.

Historical Context: Chapter 26 records the second census of Israel — taken
"after the plague" of Baal-Peor (25:9). Nearly 40 years have passed since
the first census at Sinai (chapter 1). The entire generation that refused
to enter Canaan at Kadesh-barnea has now died in the wilderness, exactly
as God decreed (14:29-35). This new census counts the generation that will
actually conquer and inherit the Promised Land. The total: 601,730 fighting
men — remarkably close to the first census total of 603,550 (a decrease of
only 1,820). God has preserved His people despite 38 years of judgment.
The census also establishes the basis for land distribution: larger tribes
receive larger portions. The chapter ends with the solemn confirmation:
"among these there was not a man of them whom Moses and Aaron the priest
numbered... in the wilderness of Sinai" (v.64) — except Caleb and Joshua.
God's word of judgment was fulfilled to the letter.

The Census Commanded (vv.1-4): After the plague, God commands Moses and
Eleazar (Aaron's son and successor as high priest) to number all males
twenty years and older who can go to war. The location: the plains of Moab
by the Jordan at Jericho — they are on the doorstep of the Promised Land.

The Tribes Numbered (vv.5-51):
  Reuben: 43,730 (was 46,500 — decreased by 2,770)
  Simeon: 22,200 (was 59,300 — decreased by 37,100! — the largest decline)
  Gad: 40,500 (was 45,650 — decreased by 5,150)
  Judah: 76,500 (was 74,600 — increased by 1,900) — largest tribe
  Issachar: 64,300 (was 54,400 — increased by 9,900)
  Zebulun: 60,500 (was 57,400 — increased by 3,100)
  Manasseh: 52,700 (was 32,200 — increased by 20,500! — largest growth)
  Ephraim: 32,500 (was 40,500 — decreased by 8,000)
  Benjamin: 45,600 (was 35,400 — increased by 10,200)
  Dan: 64,400 (was 62,700 — increased by 1,700)
  Asher: 53,400 (was 41,500 — increased by 11,900)
  Naphtali: 45,400 (was 53,400 — decreased by 8,000)
  TOTAL: 601,730 (was 603,550 — net decrease of 1,820)

Notable: Simeon's catastrophic decline (63% loss) likely reflects heavy
involvement in the Baal-Peor sin — Zimri was a Simeonite prince (25:14).

Historical Notes Within the Census (vv.8-11): The census pauses to recall
Dathan and Abiram's rebellion with Korah (chapter 16) — "they became a
warning" (ESV) / "a sign" (KJV). But "the sons of Korah did not die"
(v.11) — their descendants later write Psalms (42, 44-49, 84-85, 87-88).

Zelophehad's Daughters Introduced (v.33): Zelophehad of Manasseh "had no
sons, but daughters" — Mahlah, Noah, Hoglah, Milcah, and Tirzah. Their
case will be addressed in chapter 27.

Land Distribution Principles (vv.52-56): Land is divided by two principles:
(1) proportional to tribe size — larger tribes get more land; and (2) by
lot — God sovereignly determines location. Size determines quantity; lot
determines placement.

The Levites (vv.57-62): Levites numbered separately: 23,000 males from one
month old (was 22,000 — increased by 1,000). They receive no land
inheritance — God is their inheritance.

The Solemn Conclusion (vv.63-65): "Not one of them was left, except Caleb
the son of Jephunneh and Joshua the son of Nun" (v.65). The entire first
generation has perished — God's word of judgment fulfilled completely.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Plains of Moab: The second census is taken here, east of Jordan, preparing for the conquest of Canaan. The old generation has perished in the wilderness.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.11 — "Notwithstanding the children of Korah died not." — Grace in the
        midst of judgment. The rebels perished, but their children were
        spared — and became psalmists and worship leaders.

v.51 — "These were the numbered of the children of Israel, six hundred
        thousand and a thousand seven hundred and thirty." — God preserved
        a nation through 38 years of wilderness judgment. His purposes
        cannot be thwarted.

v.53 — "Among these the land shall be divided for inheritance according to
        the number of names." (ESV) — The inheritance is real, specific,
        and proportional. God's promises are not vague — they are measured.

v.65 — "For the LORD had said of them, They shall surely die in the
        wilderness. And there was not left a man of them, save Caleb the
        son of Jephunneh, and Joshua the son of Nun." — God's word of
        judgment is as certain as His word of promise. Not one exception.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/numbers-26/
  Guzik notes the remarkable stability of Israel's total population despite
  38 years of judgment — God preserved the nation even while disciplining
  it. He highlights Simeon's dramatic decline as evidence of corporate
  consequences for tribal sin.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "Here is a new generation standing where their fathers fell. The bones
  of six hundred thousand unbelievers bleach in the desert sand, and their
  children stand on the border of Canaan. Let the young take warning: your
  fathers' unbelief cost them everything. But let them also take courage:
  God's promise outlives the generation that rejected it. He will have a
  people in His land. If one generation refuses, He raises another. The
  promise is not cancelled — only the participants change."

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.2  — Hebrew "se'u et rosh kol adat b'nei Yisrael" — lift the head/take
       a census of the whole congregation.
       KJV: "Take the sum of all the congregation of the children of Israel"
       ESV: "Take a census of all the congregation of the people of Israel"
       ASV: "Take the sum of all the congregation of the children of Israel, from twenty years old and upward"
       NET: "Take a census of the whole community of Israelites, from twenty years old and upward"
       WEB: "Take a census of all the congregation of the children of Israel, from twenty years old and upward"
       Analysis: NET and WEB/ESV both use "census" for "se'u et-rosh"
       (lift the head). KJV/ASV use "take the sum." The same counting
       language as chapter 1 — the second census, 40 years after the first.

v.4  — Hebrew text with ambiguous sentence division.
       KJV: "as the LORD commanded Moses and the children of Israel, which went forth out of the land of Egypt"
       ESV: "as the LORD commanded Moses. The people of Israel who came out of the land of Egypt were:"
       ASV: "as Jehovah commanded Moses and the children of Israel, that came forth out of the land of Egypt"
       NET: "those who came out of the land of Egypt"
       WEB: "as Yahweh commanded Moses and the children of Israel, that came forth out of the land of Egypt"
       Analysis: ESV treats "The people of Israel who came out of Egypt" as
       the beginning of the census list. KJV/ASV/WEB connect it to the
       command. ESV's reading flows more naturally into the tribal listing.

v.9  — Hebrew "qeru'ei ha-edah" — the called ones/summoned ones of the
       congregation.
       KJV: "which were famous in the congregation"
       ESV: "chosen from the congregation"
       ASV: "who were called of the congregation"
       NET: "chosen from the community"
       WEB: "who were called of the congregation"
       Analysis: "Qeru'ei" means "called ones" or "summoned ones" — those
       called to leadership. KJV's "famous" captures their notoriety;
       ESV/NET's "chosen" captures their appointed status; ASV/WEB's
       "called" is most literal.

v.10 — Hebrew "vayyihyu l'nes" — they became a sign/warning.
       KJV: "they became a sign"
       ESV: "they became a warning"
       ASV: "they became a sign"
       NET: "they became a warning"
       WEB: "they became a sign"
       Analysis: "Nes" can mean sign, banner, or warning signal. ESV/NET
       interpret the purpose (warning); KJV/ASV/WEB translate more
       literally (sign). The context supports "warning" — their fate
       serves as a cautionary example against rebellion.

v.11 — Hebrew "uv'nei Qorach lo metu" — but the sons of Korah did not die.
       KJV: "Notwithstanding the children of Korah died not"
       ESV: "But the sons of Korah did not die"
       ASV: "Notwithstanding the sons of Korah died not"
       NET: "But the descendants of Korah did not die"
       WEB: "Notwithstanding, the sons of Korah didn't die"
       Analysis: Identical meaning across all translations. This
       parenthetical note preserves an important theological point:
       judgment on the father does not automatically condemn the children
       (cf. Ezekiel 18:20). NET uses "descendants" to emphasize continuity.

v.53 — Hebrew "la-eleh techalek ha-aretz b'nachalah" — to these the land
       shall be divided for an inheritance.
       KJV: "Unto these the land shall be divided for an inheritance"
       ESV: "Among these the land shall be divided for inheritance"
       ASV: "Unto these the land shall be divided for an inheritance"
       NET: "Among these the land is to be divided as an inheritance"
       WEB: "To these the land shall be divided for an inheritance"
       Analysis: The census determines land distribution. Larger tribes
       receive larger portions — proportional justice in inheritance
       allocation.

v.54 — Hebrew "la-rav tarbeh nachalato v'la-me'at tam'it nachalato" — to
       the many, increase inheritance; to the few, decrease inheritance.
       KJV: "To many thou shalt give the more inheritance, and to few thou shalt give the less inheritance"
       ESV: "To a large tribe you shall give a large inheritance, and to a small tribe you shall give a small inheritance"
       ASV: "To the more thou shalt give the more inheritance, and to the fewer thou shalt give the less inheritance"
       NET: "To the larger group you will give a larger inheritance, and to the smaller group you will give a smaller one"
       WEB: "To the more you shall give the more inheritance, and to the fewer you shall give the less inheritance"
       Analysis: ESV/NET clarify that "many" and "few" refer to tribal
       population size, not individuals. The principle: proportional
       allocation based on need.

v.55 — Hebrew "akh b'goral yechalek et ha-aretz" — surely by lot the land
       shall be divided.
       KJV: "the land shall be divided by lot"
       ESV: "the land shall be divided by lot"
       ASV: "the land shall be divided by lot"
       NET: "the land must be divided by lot"
       WEB: "the land shall be divided by lot"
       Analysis: Both human calculation (proportional to tribe size) and
       divine sovereignty (by lot) determine inheritance. God controls the
       outcome even through apparently random means. NET's "must" conveys
       divine imperative.

v.61 — Hebrew "esh zarah" — strange/unauthorized fire.
       KJV: "when they offered strange fire before the LORD"
       ESV: "when they offered unauthorized fire before the LORD"
       ASV: "when they offered strange fire before Jehovah"
       NET: "when they offered strange fire before the Lord"
       WEB: "when they offered strange fire before Yahweh"
       Analysis: "Zarah" means foreign, strange, unauthorized. ESV's
       "unauthorized" is more precise — the issue was not that the fire
       was exotic but that it was not commanded. KJV/ASV/NET/WEB's
       "strange" is more literal but less clear to modern readers.

v.65 — Hebrew "lo notar mehem ish" — not a man of them was left.
       KJV: "there was not left a man of them, save Caleb the son of Jephunneh, and Joshua the son of Nun"
       ESV: "not one of them was left, except Caleb the son of Jephunneh and Joshua the son of Nun"
       ASV: "there was not left a man of them, save Caleb the son of Jephunneh, and Joshua the son of Nun"
       NET: "not one of them was left except Caleb son of Jephunneh and Joshua son of Nun"
       WEB: "there was not a man left of them, except Caleb the son of Jephunneh, and Joshua the son of Nun"
       Analysis: Two survivors from 603,550 men. Faithfulness preserved
       them; unbelief destroyed the rest. All translations agree on this
       solemn summary — God's word of judgment fulfilled completely.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Plains of Moab — The flat area east of the Jordan River, opposite Jericho.
               Israel's final encampment before crossing into Canaan. The
               setting for much of Deuteronomy and the end of Numbers.

Census (Hebrew: "paqad") — To number, muster, appoint. More than a head
               count — it is a military muster of fighting men and an
               administrative ordering of the nation for inheritance.

Lot (Hebrew: "goral") — A means of determining God's will, likely using
               marked stones or sticks. Not random chance but divine
               direction: "The lot is cast into the lap; but the whole
               disposing thereof is of the LORD" (Proverbs 16:33).

Sons of Korah — Descendants of the rebel Korah who were spared in the
               judgment of Numbers 16. They became temple musicians and
               worship leaders. Their psalms include some of the most
               beloved in Scripture (Psalms 42, 84, 87).

Zelophehad — A man of Manasseh who died in the wilderness (not in Korah's
               rebellion) leaving only daughters. His case becomes the
               basis for inheritance law regarding women (ch. 27, 36).

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. A new generation on the border (vv.1-4). The old generation died in
   unbelief; the new generation stands ready to inherit. God's purposes
   are not defeated by one generation's failure — He raises up another.
   Are you part of the generation that refuses, or the one that enters?

2. "They became a warning" (v.10). Korah's rebellion is preserved in the
   census record as a cautionary tale. God memorializes both faith and
   failure. What "warnings" from the past are you heeding — or ignoring?

3. "The sons of Korah did not die" (v.11). Grace breaks the cycle of
   generational sin. You are not condemned to repeat your parents' failures.
   The sons of rebels became psalmists. God can redeem any lineage.

4. Simeon's catastrophic decline (v.14). Sin has corporate consequences.
   One prince's brazen sin (Zimri, 25:14) may reflect a tribal culture of
   compromise. The choices of leaders affect entire communities.

5. The land divided by lot AND by proportion (vv.53-56). God's sovereignty
   (the lot) works alongside practical wisdom (proportional allocation).
   Divine guidance does not eliminate human responsibility — it works
   through it.

6. "Not one was left" (v.65). God's word of judgment was fulfilled
   completely — and so will His word of promise. The same faithfulness
   that executed judgment now guarantees inheritance. If God keeps His
   hard words, He certainly keeps His good ones.
