Numbers - Chapter 26
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A second census numbers the new generation at 601,730 — nearly the same total, confirming God's faithfulness despite forty years of death.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses. Numbers is the fourth book of the Pentateuch, written during
the wilderness period (approximately 1445-1405 BC). Hebrew title: "Bemidbar"
— "In the wilderness." The book records Israel's 38 years of wilderness
wandering between Sinai and the Promised Land — a journey that should have
taken 11 days (Deuteronomy 1:2) but took 40 years due to unbelief.

Historical Context: Chapter 26 records the second census of Israel — taken
"after the plague" of Baal-Peor (25:9). Nearly 40 years have passed since
the first census at Sinai (chapter 1). The entire generation that refused
to enter Canaan at Kadesh-barnea has now died in the wilderness, exactly
as God decreed (14:29-35). This new census counts the generation that will
actually conquer and inherit the Promised Land. The total: 601,730 fighting
men — remarkably close to the first census total of 603,550 (a decrease of
only 1,820). God has preserved His people despite 38 years of judgment.
The census also establishes the basis for land distribution: larger tribes
receive larger portions. The chapter ends with the solemn confirmation:
"among these there was not a man of them whom Moses and Aaron the priest
numbered... in the wilderness of Sinai" (v.64) — except Caleb and Joshua.
God's word of judgment was fulfilled to the letter.

The Census Commanded (vv.1-4): After the plague, God commands Moses and
Eleazar (Aaron's son and successor as high priest) to number all males
twenty years and older who can go to war. The location: the plains of Moab
by the Jordan at Jericho — they are on the doorstep of the Promised Land.

The Tribes Numbered (vv.5-51):
  Reuben: 43,730 (was 46,500 — decreased by 2,770)
  Simeon: 22,200 (was 59,300 — decreased by 37,100! — the largest decline)
  Gad: 40,500 (was 45,650 — decreased by 5,150)
  Judah: 76,500 (was 74,600 — increased by 1,900) — largest tribe
  Issachar: 64,300 (was 54,400 — increased by 9,900)
  Zebulun: 60,500 (was 57,400 — increased by 3,100)
  Manasseh: 52,700 (was 32,200 — increased by 20,500! — largest growth)
  Ephraim: 32,500 (was 40,500 — decreased by 8,000)
  Benjamin: 45,600 (was 35,400 — increased by 10,200)
  Dan: 64,400 (was 62,700 — increased by 1,700)
  Asher: 53,400 (was 41,500 — increased by 11,900)
  Naphtali: 45,400 (was 53,400 — decreased by 8,000)
  TOTAL: 601,730 (was 603,550 — net decrease of 1,820)

Notable: Simeon's catastrophic decline (63% loss) likely reflects heavy
involvement in the Baal-Peor sin — Zimri was a Simeonite prince (25:14).

Historical Notes Within the Census (vv.8-11): The census pauses to recall
Dathan and Abiram's rebellion with Korah (chapter 16) — "they became a
warning" (ESV) / "a sign" (KJV). But "the sons of Korah did not die"
(v.11) — their descendants later write Psalms (42, 44-49, 84-85, 87-88).

Zelophehad's Daughters Introduced (v.33): Zelophehad of Manasseh "had no
sons, but daughters" — Mahlah, Noah, Hoglah, Milcah, and Tirzah. Their
case will be addressed in chapter 27.

Land Distribution Principles (vv.52-56): Land is divided by two principles:
(1) proportional to tribe size — larger tribes get more land; and (2) by
lot — God sovereignly determines location. Size determines quantity; lot
determines placement.

The Levites (vv.57-62): Levites numbered separately: 23,000 males from one
month old (was 22,000 — increased by 1,000). They receive no land
inheritance — God is their inheritance.

The Solemn Conclusion (vv.63-65): "Not one of them was left, except Caleb
the son of Jephunneh and Joshua the son of Nun" (v.65). The entire first
generation has perished — God's word of judgment fulfilled completely.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Plains of Moab: The second census is taken here, east of Jordan, preparing for the conquest of Canaan. The old generation has perished in the wilderness.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/numbers-26/
  Guzik notes the remarkable stability of Israel's total population despite
  38 years of judgment — God preserved the nation even while disciplining
  it. He highlights Simeon's dramatic decline as evidence of corporate
  consequences for tribal sin.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "Here is a new generation standing where their fathers fell. The bones
  of six hundred thousand unbelievers bleach in the desert sand, and their
  children stand on the border of Canaan. Let the young take warning: your
  fathers' unbelief cost them everything. But let them also take courage:
  God's promise outlives the generation that rejected it. He will have a
  people in His land. If one generation refuses, He raises another. The
  promise is not cancelled — only the participants change."

REFLECTION
------------------------------------
1. A new generation on the border (vv.1-4). The old generation died in
   unbelief; the new generation stands ready to inherit. God's purposes
   are not defeated by one generation's failure — He raises up another.
   Are you part of the generation that refuses, or the one that enters?

2. "They became a warning" (v.10). Korah's rebellion is preserved in the
   census record as a cautionary tale. God memorializes both faith and
   failure. What "warnings" from the past are you heeding — or ignoring?

3. "The sons of Korah did not die" (v.11). Grace breaks the cycle of
   generational sin. You are not condemned to repeat your parents' failures.
   The sons of rebels became psalmists. God can redeem any lineage.

4. Simeon's catastrophic decline (v.14). Sin has corporate consequences.
   One prince's brazen sin (Zimri, 25:14) may reflect a tribal culture of
   compromise. The choices of leaders affect entire communities.

5. The land divided by lot AND by proportion (vv.53-56). God's sovereignty
   (the lot) works alongside practical wisdom (proportional allocation).
   Divine guidance does not eliminate human responsibility — it works
   through it.

6. "Not one was left" (v.65). God's word of judgment was fulfilled
   completely — and so will His word of promise. The same faithfulness
   that executed judgment now guarantees inheritance. If God keeps His
   hard words, He certainly keeps His good ones.
