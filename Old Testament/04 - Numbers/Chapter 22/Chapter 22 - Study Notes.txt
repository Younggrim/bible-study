Numbers - Chapter 22
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Balak hires Balaam to curse Israel; God confronts Balaam through his own donkey, which sees the angel of the Lord blocking the way.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses. Numbers is the fourth book of the Pentateuch, written during
the wilderness period (approximately 1445-1405 BC). Hebrew title: "Bemidbar"
— "In the wilderness." The book records Israel's 38 years of wilderness
wandering between Sinai and the Promised Land — a journey that should have
taken 11 days (Deuteronomy 1:2) but took 40 years due to unbelief.

Historical Context: Chapters 22-24 form the Balaam narrative — one of the
most remarkable episodes in the Old Testament. Israel has conquered Sihon
and Og and now camps in the plains of Moab, directly across the Jordan
from Jericho — on the doorstep of the Promised Land. Balak, king of Moab,
is terrified. Unable to defeat Israel militarily, he turns to spiritual
warfare: hiring Balaam, a renowned diviner from Pethor (near the Euphrates
River in Mesopotamia), to curse Israel. Chapter 22 introduces the drama:
Balak's summons, Balaam's negotiations with God, the famous episode of the
talking donkey, and Balaam's arrival in Moab. Balaam is a complex figure —
he knows the LORD's name, receives genuine revelation from God, and speaks
truth — yet he is motivated by greed and later leads Israel into sexual
sin and idolatry (Numbers 31:16; Revelation 2:14). He is a prophet for
hire, a man who knows God's word but loves money more. The chapter reveals
that no weapon formed against God's people can prosper — not even
supernatural curses — because "they are blessed" (v.12).

Balak's Fear and First Summons (vv.1-7): Israel camps in the plains of
Moab (v.1). Balak sees what Israel did to the Amorites and is terrified
(vv.2-3). He allies with Midian (v.4) and sends messengers to Balaam at
Pethor "by the river" (the Euphrates — approximately 400 miles away) with
"the rewards of divination" (v.7). His request: "Curse me this people; for
they are too mighty for me" (v.6). His belief: "he whom thou blessest is
blessed, and he whom thou cursest is cursed" (v.6). Balak believes in the
power of words/curses — but he does not know that Israel's blessing comes
from God Himself and cannot be reversed by any human.

God's First Prohibition (vv.8-14): Balaam tells the messengers to stay
the night while he consults the LORD (v.8). God comes to Balaam: "Thou
shalt not go with them; thou shalt not curse the people: for they are
blessed" (v.12). Clear, absolute, final. Balaam sends them away: "the LORD
refuseth to give me leave" (v.13). Note: Balaam says "the LORD refuseth"
— not "I refuse." His obedience is external, not internal.

Balak's Second Summons (vv.15-21): Balak sends more princes, more
honorable, with greater promises: "I will promote thee unto very great
honour" (v.17). Balaam's response sounds pious: "If Balak would give me
his house full of silver and gold, I cannot go beyond the word of the LORD
my God" (v.18). But then: "tarry ye also here this night, that I may know
what the LORD will say unto me MORE" (v.19). God already said no — why ask
again? Balaam hopes God will change His mind. God permits him to go: "If
the men come to call thee, rise up, and go with them; but yet the word
which I shall say unto thee, that shalt thou do" (v.20). Permission, not
approval.

The Donkey and the Angel (vv.22-35): "God's anger was kindled because he
went" (v.22) — even though God permitted it. God saw Balaam's heart: he
went hoping to find a way to curse for profit. The Angel of the LORD
(a pre-incarnate appearance of Christ) stands in the road with drawn sword
(v.22). The donkey sees the Angel three times and avoids him: (1) turns
into a field — Balaam strikes her (v.23); (2) crushes Balaam's foot
against a wall — he strikes again (v.25); (3) lies down — he strikes with
his staff (v.27). God opens the donkey's mouth: "What have I done unto
thee, that thou hast smitten me these three times?" (v.28). Balaam argues
with the donkey without apparent surprise (v.29). Then God opens Balaam's
eyes — he sees the Angel and falls on his face (v.31). The Angel: "thy way
is perverse before me" (v.32). The donkey saved Balaam's life — "surely
now also I had slain thee, and saved her alive" (v.33). Balaam confesses
sin (v.34). The Angel permits him to continue but restricts his speech
(v.35).

Balaam Meets Balak (vv.36-41): Balak meets Balaam at the border (v.36).
Balaam warns: "Have I now any power at all to say any thing? the word that
God putteth in my mouth, that shall I speak" (v.38). Balak brings Balaam
to "the high places of Baal" to see Israel (v.41) — setting up the
oracles of chapters 23-24.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Plains of Moab: Israel is now camped east of the Jordan, opposite Jericho. Balak king of Moab sees them from above.
- Pethor (v.5): Balaam's home, near the Euphrates River — a long journey east/north to summon a diviner.
- Moab's territory: East of the Dead Sea, between the Arnon and the Zered.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.6  — "Come now, curse this people for me, since they are too mighty for
        me." (ESV) — Balak's strategy: what he cannot defeat militarily,
        he will attack spiritually. But God's blessing cannot be reversed
        by human curses.

v.12 — "You shall not go with them. You shall not curse the people, for
        they are blessed." (ESV) — God's absolute declaration. Israel is
        blessed — period. No prophet, no diviner, no king can undo what
        God has
        done.

v.18 — "If Balak would give me his house full of silver and gold, I
        cannot go beyond the word of the LORD my God." — Sounds pious —
        but Balaam's heart wants the gold. His words are right; his
        motivation is corrupt.

v.28 — "And the LORD opened the mouth of the ass, and she said unto
        Balaam, What have I done unto thee?" — God uses the humblest
        creature to rebuke the prophet. A donkey sees what the diviner
        cannot. Spiritual blindness is worse than animal instinct.

v.32 — "Thy way is perverse before me." — The Angel's verdict on Balaam.
        His path is crooked — he goes toward money while claiming to
        follow God. You cannot serve God and mammon.

v.38 — "The word that God putteth in my mouth, that shall I speak." —
        Balaam is constrained by God's sovereignty. Even a corrupt prophet
        cannot speak beyond what God allows. God's word overrules human
        intention.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/numbers-22/
  Guzik emphasizes Balaam's divided heart: he knows God's word but loves
  money. His pious language ("I cannot go beyond the word of the LORD")
  masks a heart that desperately wants to find a loophole. He notes the
  irony of the donkey episode: the professional seer is blind; the dumb
  animal sees clearly. Spiritual gifts without spiritual character are
  dangerous.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "Balaam could not curse Israel — not because he did not want to, but
  because God would not let him. Here is comfort for every believer: your
  enemies may hire the most eloquent tongue, the most powerful sorcerer,
  the most cunning adversary — and they cannot curse what God has blessed.
  'If God be for us, who can be against us?' (Romans 8:31). The donkey
  saw more than the prophet. Let that humble every man who thinks his
  gifts make him spiritual. A man may prophesy and perish. A man may speak
  God's word and love God's enemy. Balaam is the most terrifying figure in
  Scripture: a man who knew the truth and sold it for gold."

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.5  — Hebrew text difficult — "by the river of the land of his people."
       KJV: "by the river of the land of the children of his people"
       ESV: "near the River in the land of the people of Amaw"
       ASV: "to Pethor, which is by the River"
       NET: "Balaam son of Beor"
       WEB: "Balaam the son of Beor"
       Analysis: "The River" (ha-nahar) typically refers to the Euphrates.
       Pethor was in Mesopotamia — Balaam traveled approximately 400 miles
       to reach Moab. KJV and ESV differ on "his people" vs. "Amaw" — a
       textual variant. Balaam is a non-Israelite diviner yet God speaks
       through him.

v.7  — Hebrew "q'samim b'yadam" — divination fees in their hand.
       KJV: "the rewards of divination"
       ESV: "the fees for divination"
       ASV: "the rewards of divination"
       NET: "the fee for divination"
       WEB: "the wages of divination"
       Analysis: Balaam is a professional — he charges for his services.
       This is not a prophet of the LORD serving freely but a diviner for
       hire. KJV/ASV use "rewards," ESV/NET use "fees," WEB uses "wages."

v.12 — Hebrew "baruk hu" — blessed is he (they are blessed).
       KJV: "Thou shalt not go with them; thou shalt not curse the people: for they are blessed"
       ESV: "You shall not go with them. You shall not curse the people, for they are blessed"
       ASV: "Thou shalt not go with them; thou shalt not curse the people; for they are blessed"
       NET: "You must not go with them; you must not curse the people, for they are blessed"
       WEB: "You shall not go with them. You shall not curse the people, for they are blessed"
       Analysis: God's absolute declaration. "They are blessed" — a state
       of divine favor that no human magic can reverse. Three prohibitions
       from God: do not go, do not curse, because they are blessed. Clear,
       absolute, final.

v.18 — Hebrew "lo uchal la'avor et pi YHWH Elohai" — I cannot transgress
       the mouth/word of the LORD my God.
       KJV: "I cannot go beyond the word of the LORD my God"
       ESV: "I could not go beyond the command of the LORD my God"
       ASV: "I cannot go beyond the word of Jehovah my God"
       NET: "I could not do anything small or great that would go against the commandment of the Lord my God"
       WEB: "I can't go beyond the word of Yahweh my God"
       Analysis: Balaam acknowledges YHWH's sovereignty even while desiring
       Balak's reward. ASV uses "Jehovah," WEB uses "Yahweh." His
       confession is correct theology without genuine submission — he
       sounds pious but his heart wants the gold.

v.22 — Hebrew "l'satan lo" — as a satan/adversary to him.
       KJV: "the angel of the LORD stood in the way for an adversary against him"
       ESV: "the angel of the LORD took his stand in the way as his adversary"
       ASV: "the angel of Jehovah stood in the way for an adversary against him"
       NET: "the angel of the Lord stood in the road to oppose him"
       WEB: "Yahweh's angel stood in the way as an adversary against him"
       Analysis: The word "satan" here means "opponent/adversary" — the
       Angel opposes Balaam's perverse journey. God Himself stands against
       those whose way is crooked. Note WEB's unique "Yahweh's angel" —
       possessive rather than construct state.

v.28 — Hebrew "vayyiftach YHWH et pi ha-aton" — the LORD opened the mouth
       of the donkey.
       KJV: "the LORD opened the mouth of the ass"
       ESV: "the LORD opened the mouth of the donkey"
       ASV: "Jehovah opened the mouth of the ass"
       NET: "Then the Lord opened the mouth of the donkey"
       WEB: "Yahweh opened the mouth of the donkey"
       Analysis: A supernatural miracle — God gives speech to an animal.
       Peter treats this as literal history (2 Peter 2:16). The God who
       created speech can grant it to any creature. God gives speech to a
       beast while the professional prophet cannot see what the donkey sees.

v.32 — Hebrew "ki yarat ha-derekh l'negdi" — because the way is perverse/
       reckless before me.
       KJV: "thy way is perverse before me"
       ESV: "your way is perverse before me"
       ASV: "thy way is perverse before me"
       NET: "your way is reckless before me"
       WEB: "your way is perverse before me"
       Analysis: "Yarat" = to be precipitous, reckless, perverse. NET uses
       "reckless" where others use "perverse." Balaam's path is crooked in
       God's sight — he goes with permission but with corrupt motivation.

v.38 — Hebrew "ha-davar asher yasim Elohim b'fi oto adabber" — the word
       that God puts in my mouth, that I shall speak.
       KJV: "the word that God putteth in my mouth, that shall I speak"
       ESV: "the word that God puts in my mouth, that must I speak"
       ASV: "The word that God putteth in my mouth, that shall I speak"
       NET: "The word that God puts in my mouth, that is what I must speak"
       WEB: "The word that God puts in my mouth, that shall I speak"
       Analysis: Balaam is controlled by God's word despite his own
       desires. True prophecy is not the prophet's word but God's. He is
       constrained by God's sovereignty — even a corrupt prophet cannot
       speak beyond what God allows.

v.41 — Hebrew "Bamot Ba'al" — high places of Baal.
       KJV: "the high places of Baal"
       ESV: "Bamoth-baal"
       ASV: "the high places of Baal"
       NET: "the high places of Baal"
       WEB: "the high places of Baal"
       Analysis: KJV/ASV/NET/WEB translate the term ("high places of
       Baal"); ESV transliterates as a place name (Bamoth-baal). These
       were elevated worship sites dedicated to the Canaanite deity Baal.
       Balak brings Balaam to a pagan worship site to curse Israel — the
       wrong god, the wrong place, the wrong purpose.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Balaam — Son of Beor, a diviner/prophet from Pethor in Mesopotamia.
               Known internationally for the power of his blessings and
               curses. He knows the LORD's name and receives genuine
               revelation — but is motivated by greed. He cannot curse
               Israel (chapters 23-24) but later counsels Balak to seduce
               Israel through Moabite women (31:16). He becomes a
               proverbial figure of corrupt religion: knowing truth but
               loving money (2 Peter 2:15; Jude 11; Revelation 2:14).

Balak — Son of Zippor, king of Moab. Terrified by Israel's victories over
               Sihon and Og, he attempts to defeat Israel through
               supernatural curses rather than military force. He
               represents the futility of opposing God's purposes — no
               strategy, human or spiritual, can reverse God's blessing.

The Angel of the LORD — A pre-incarnate appearance of Christ (theophany).
               Appears with drawn sword as Balaam's adversary. The donkey
               sees Him; Balaam does not until God opens his eyes. He
               represents God's active opposition to those whose way is
               perverse — even when God has given permission to proceed.

Divination (Hebrew: "qesem") — The practice of seeking supernatural
               knowledge or influence through occult means. Forbidden for
               Israel (Deuteronomy 18:10) but practiced throughout the
               ancient Near East. Balaam is a professional diviner — his
               services are purchased with "fees for divination." His
               knowledge of the LORD does not make his practice legitimate.

Pethor — Balaam's home city, located near the Euphrates River in
               Mesopotamia (modern Syria/Iraq border region). The great
               distance (approximately 400 miles) shows how far Balak's
               reputation-search extended and how desperate he was.

"They Are Blessed" (v.12) — God's absolute declaration about Israel. Not
               "they will be blessed if they obey" but "they ARE blessed"
               — present tense, unconditional in this context. God's
               covenant blessing on Abraham's seed cannot be reversed by
               any external curse. This is the foundation of chapters 23-24.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "They are blessed" (v.12). No curse can reverse what God has blessed.
   If you belong to God through Christ, no enemy — human or spiritual —
   can undo His blessing on your life. "If God be for us, who can be
   against us?" (Romans 8:31).

2. Balaam's divided heart (vv.18-19). His words sound right: "I cannot go
   beyond the word of the LORD." But his heart wants the money. He asks
   God again, hoping for a different answer. Do you keep asking God to
   approve what He has already forbidden? Repeated asking after a clear
   "no" reveals the heart's true desire.

3. The donkey saw what the prophet could not (vv.23-31). Spiritual gifts
   do not guarantee spiritual sight. A man can prophesy and be blind to
   God's opposition. Balaam had revelation but lacked discernment because
   his heart was corrupt. Character matters more than gifting.

4. "Thy way is perverse before me" (v.32). God permitted Balaam to go —
   but was angry that he went. Permission is not approval. God sometimes
   allows us to pursue what He does not endorse, and the consequences
   reveal our hearts. Just because a door opens does not mean God is
   pleased with your walking through it.

5. God uses even corrupt instruments (v.38). Balaam will speak God's word
   despite his corrupt motivation. God's sovereignty overrules human
   intention. This is both comforting (God's purposes cannot be thwarted)
   and sobering (being used by God does not mean you are approved by God).

6. The "way of Balaam" (2 Peter 2:15). Knowing truth and selling it for
   profit. Using spiritual gifts for personal gain. Speaking God's word
   while loving God's enemy (money). This is the most dangerous spiritual
   condition: orthodoxy without integrity.
