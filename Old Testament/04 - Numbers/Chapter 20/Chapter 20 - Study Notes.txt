Numbers - Chapter 20
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Miriam dies; Moses strikes the rock in anger instead of speaking to it, and God bars him from the Promised Land; Aaron dies on Mount Hor.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses. Numbers is the fourth book of the Pentateuch, written during
the wilderness period (approximately 1445-1405 BC). Hebrew title: "Bemidbar"
— "In the wilderness." The book records Israel's 38 years of wilderness
wandering between Sinai and the Promised Land — a journey that should have
taken 11 days (Deuteronomy 1:2) but took 40 years due to unbelief.

Historical Context: Chapter 20 marks the transition from the old generation
to the new. Nearly 38 years have passed since the rebellion at Kadesh
(chapters 13-14) — the text jumps from year 2 to year 40 with almost no
narrative in between. The chapter opens with Miriam's death (v.1) and
closes with Aaron's death (vv.23-29) — two of the three original leaders
are gone. Between these deaths, the pivotal event: Moses strikes the rock
at Meribah instead of speaking to it as God commanded, and is barred from
entering the Promised Land (vv.2-13). The chapter also records Edom's
refusal to grant Israel passage (vv.14-21) — a brother nation (descendants
of Esau) rejecting their kin. This is a chapter of loss: Miriam dies,
Moses loses his inheritance, Edom rejects them, and Aaron dies on Mount
Hor. Yet God still provides water, still leads them forward, and still
transfers the priesthood to the next generation (Eleazar). The old is
passing away; the new is coming.

Miriam's Death (v.1): "Miriam died there, and was buried there" — a single
sentence for the woman who led Israel in worship at the Red Sea (Exodus
15:20-21), who watched over baby Moses in the Nile (Exodus 2:4-8). She
dies at Kadesh in the wilderness of Zin, in the first month of the
fortieth year. No elaborate funeral, no eulogy recorded — just death and
burial. The old generation is passing.

Water from the Rock at Meribah (vv.2-13): No water; the people quarrel
with Moses (vv.2-5) — the same complaint as their parents 38 years earlier
(Exodus 17:1-7). Moses and Aaron fall on their faces; God's glory appears
(v.6). God's command: "Take the rod... and SPEAK ye unto the rock before
their eyes" (v.8). Moses takes the rod (v.9), gathers the people, and
says: "Hear now, ye rebels; must WE fetch you water out of this rock?"
(v.10). Then he strikes the rock twice with the rod (v.11). Water comes
abundantly — God provides despite Moses' disobedience. But God's verdict:
"Because ye believed me not, to sanctify me in the eyes of the children
of Israel, therefore ye shall not bring this congregation into the land"
(v.12). Moses' sin: (1) he spoke in anger ("ye rebels"), (2) he took
credit ("must WE fetch"), (3) he struck instead of speaking, (4) he
failed to sanctify God before the people. The place is named Meribah —
"strife/quarreling" (v.13).

Edom Refuses Passage (vv.14-21): Moses sends messengers to Edom (Esau's
descendants — Israel's "brother," v.14). He recounts their history and
asks for peaceful passage on the King's Highway (vv.14-17). Edom refuses
twice (vv.18, 20) and comes out with military force (v.20). Israel turns
away without fighting (v.21) — God does not authorize war against Edom
(Deuteronomy 2:4-5). A brother's rejection adds to the chapter's grief.

Aaron's Death on Mount Hor (vv.22-29): God tells Moses: Aaron will die
because "ye rebelled against my word at the water of Meribah" (v.24).
The transfer: Moses strips Aaron of his priestly garments and puts them
on Eleazar his son (vv.25-26). Aaron dies on the mountaintop (v.28).
Moses and Eleazar descend alone. Israel mourns thirty days (v.29). The
priesthood continues — Aaron dies, but the office lives on in his son.
A picture of the imperfect priesthood that must be replaced by an eternal
one (Hebrews 7:23-24).

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Kadesh (v.1): Israel returns to Kadesh in the wilderness of Zin after 38 years. Miriam dies and is buried here.
- Meribah (v.13): "Quarreling" — the place where Moses strikes the rock instead of speaking to it, and is barred from entering Canaan.
- Mount Hor (vv.22-29): On the border of Edom. Aaron dies here; Eleazar succeeds him as high priest.
- Edom refuses passage (vv.14-21): Israel cannot go through Edom (east of the Arabah) and must detour around.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.8  — "Tell the rock before their eyes to yield its water." (ESV) — God's
        command: speak, not strike. The rock had been struck once before
        (Exodus 17:6); now it need only be spoken to.
        Christ was struck once; He need not be crucified again.

v.10 — "Hear now, ye rebels; must we fetch you water out of this rock?" —
        Moses' anger and self-exaltation. "Must WE" — taking credit for
        God's miracle. Anger led to presumption; presumption led to
        disobedience.

v.11 — "Moses lifted up his hand, and with his rod he smote the rock
        twice." — Striking instead of speaking. Doing it twice suggests
        frustration, not faith. God still provides water — grace despite
        disobedience — but the leader bears consequences.

v.12 — "Because you did not believe in me, to uphold me as holy in the
        eyes of the people of Israel, therefore you shall not bring this
        assembly into the land." (ESV) — The sentence on Moses. Unbelief
        and failure to
        honor God publicly. The higher the position, the greater the
        accountability.

v.24 — "Aaron shall be gathered to his people, for he shall not enter the
        land." (ESV) — Aaron shares Moses' sentence. Both leaders barred
        from the Promised Land for the same sin at Meribah.

v.26 — "Strip Aaron of his garments, and put them upon Eleazar his son."
        — The priesthood transfers. The garments represent the office, not
        the man. Aaron dies; the priesthood continues.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/numbers-20/
  Guzik emphasizes the typological significance: the rock struck at
  Rephidim (Exodus 17) pictures Christ crucified once; the rock spoken to
  at Meribah pictures Christ accessed by faith/prayer after His one
  sacrifice. Moses' striking the rock twice "re-crucifies" Christ
  typologically. He also notes the poignancy of Moses stripping his own
  brother of the priestly garments on the mountaintop.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "Moses struck the rock. God had said 'speak.' But Moses was angry, and
  angry men do not obey carefully. He struck — and struck twice — and
  water came. But God said, 'You shall not enter.' Oh, the severity of
  grace! God gave the water — He did not withhold mercy from the people —
  but He judged His servant. Let every minister, every leader, every
  teacher tremble: the higher your office, the stricter your judgment.
  God will use you to bless others even while He disciplines you. The
  water flowed — but Moses wept."

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1  — Hebrew "vayyavo v'nei Yisrael kol ha-edah midbar Tzin" — the
       children of Israel, the whole congregation, came into the wilderness
       of Zin.
       KJV: "the children of Israel, even the whole congregation, came into the desert of Zin"
       ESV: "the people of Israel, the whole congregation, came into the wilderness of Zin"
       ASV: "the children of Israel, even the whole congregation, came into the wilderness of Zin"
       NET: "the entire community of Israelites entered the desert of Zin"
       WEB: "the children of Israel, even the whole congregation, came into the wilderness of Zin"
       Analysis: NET uses "desert" for "midbar" while others use
       "wilderness." The "wilderness of Zin" is the final stage of
       wilderness wandering — near Kadesh, where the first generation
       failed (ch.13-14).

v.3  — Hebrew "vayyarev ha-am im Moshe" — the people contended/quarreled
       with Moses.
       KJV: "the people chode with Moses"
       ESV: "the people quarreled with Moses"
       ASV: "the people strove with Moses"
       NET: "the people contended with Moses"
       WEB: "the people strove with Moses"
       Analysis: "Chode" is archaic English for "quarreled/contended." The
       same root gives us "Meribah" (strife/contention). ESV's "quarreled"
       and NET's "contended" are modern equivalents. ASV/WEB use "strove."

v.8  — Hebrew "v'dibartem el ha-sela" — and you shall speak to the rock.
       KJV: "speak ye unto the rock"
       ESV: "tell the rock... to yield its water"
       ASV: "speak ye unto the rock before their eyes"
       NET: "speak to the rock before their eyes"
       WEB: "speak to the rock before their eyes"
       Analysis: "Dibartem" = speak (plural — both Moses and Aaron). The
       command is clear: speak, not strike. At Rephidim (Exodus 17:6), God
       said "strike"; here He says "speak." The change is deliberate and
       significant — Moses instead STRIKES it (v.11). The distinction
       between speaking and striking becomes Moses' fatal error,
       misrepresenting God's character.

v.10 — Hebrew "ha-min ha-sela ha-zeh notzi lakhem mayim?" — shall we bring
       forth water for you from this rock?
       KJV: "must we fetch you water out of this rock?"
       ESV: "shall we bring water for you out of this rock?"
       ASV: "shall we bring you forth water out of this rock?"
       NET: "must we bring water out of this rock for you?"
       WEB: "shall we bring water out of this rock for you?"
       Analysis: The "we" is the problem — Moses and Aaron take credit for
       what only God can do. The question may also express doubt: "Can we
       really bring water from this rock?" KJV's "must we fetch" and NET's
       "must we bring" convey the presumption more forcefully.

v.12 — Hebrew "lo he'emantem bi l'haqdisheni" — you did not believe in Me,
       to sanctify Me.
       KJV: "ye believed me not, to sanctify me"
       ESV: "you did not believe in me, to uphold me as holy"
       ASV: "Because ye believed not in me, to sanctify me in the eyes of the children of Israel"
       NET: "Because you did not trust me enough to show me as holy before the Israelites"
       WEB: "Because you didn't believe in me, to sanctify me in the eyes of the children of Israel"
       Analysis: Two failures: (1) unbelief — "lo he'emantem" (did not
       trust/believe), (2) failure to sanctify — "l'haqdisheni" (to make
       Me holy/set Me apart in their eyes). NET adds "enough" — not total
       unbelief but insufficient trust in the moment. Moses made himself
       the focus instead of God.

v.13 — Hebrew "mei Merivah" — waters of Meribah (quarreling).
       KJV: "This is the water of Meribah"
       ESV: "These are the waters of Meribah"
       ASV: "These are the waters of Meribah"
       NET: "These are the waters of Meribah"
       WEB: "These are the waters of Meribah"
       Analysis: "Meribah" = "quarreling/contention." All translations
       preserve the Hebrew name. The place-name memorializes both Israel's
       rebellion and Moses' failure.

v.24 — Hebrew "ye'asef Aharon el ammav" — Aaron shall be gathered to his
       people.
       KJV: "Aaron shall be gathered unto his people"
       ESV: "Let Aaron be gathered to his people"
       ASV: "Aaron shall be gathered unto his people"
       NET: "Aaron will be gathered to his ancestors"
       WEB: "Aaron shall be gathered to his people"
       Analysis: A euphemism for death — "gathered to his people" implies
       reunion with ancestors. NET uses "ancestors" where others use
       "people" (Hebrew "ammav"). The idiom implies afterlife: death is not
       extinction but reunion with those who went before. It is a gentle
       way to describe death, suggesting continuity beyond the grave.

v.26 — Hebrew "hafsheet et Aharon et b'gadav" — strip Aaron of his
       garments.
       KJV: "strip Aaron of his garments"
       ESV: "strip Aaron of his garments"
       ASV: "strip Aaron of his garments, and put them upon Eleazar his son"
       NET: "strip Aaron of his garments and put them on Eleazar his son"
       WEB: "strip Aaron of his garments, and put them on Eleazar his son"
       Analysis: The priestly garments represent the office. Removing them
       from Aaron and placing them on Eleazar is the visible transfer of
       authority. The man dies; the office continues. The garments ARE the
       authority — high priestly succession passes from father to son.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Meribah — Hebrew: "strife" or "quarreling." The name given to the place
               where Israel quarreled with God over water and Moses struck
               the rock. There are two Meribahs: one at Rephidim early in
               the journey (Exodus 17:7) and this one at Kadesh near the
               end. Both involve water from rock; both involve Israel's
               complaint; but God's instructions differ.

The Rock — In both water-from-rock incidents, the rock is identified as
               Christ (1 Corinthians 10:4). At Rephidim, the rock is
               struck — Christ crucified once. At Meribah, the rock should
               be spoken to — Christ accessed by faith after His one
               sacrifice. Moses' error in striking it again typologically
               "re-crucifies" Christ.

Mount Hor — The mountain on the border of Edom where Aaron dies and the
               priesthood transfers to Eleazar. Its exact location is
               debated — traditionally identified with Jebel Harun near
               Petra, Jordan. Aaron dies in sight of the congregation,
               and they mourn thirty days.

King's Highway — An ancient trade route running north-south through
               Transjordan (modern Jordan). Israel requests passage on
               this road through Edom's territory. Edom's refusal forces
               Israel to take a longer route around Edom's borders.

Gathered to His People — A Hebrew euphemism for death, implying reunion
               with ancestors. Used of Abraham (Genesis 25:8), Isaac
               (Genesis 35:29), Jacob (Genesis 49:33), and here Aaron.
               It suggests belief in continued existence after death —
               the dead are not annihilated but "gathered" to those who
               went before.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Moses' sin at Meribah (vv.10-12). After 40 years of faithful service,
   one act of disobedience costs Moses the Promised Land. The lesson is
   sobering: faithfulness in the past does not excuse disobedience in the
   present. Leaders are held to a higher standard. How you finish matters
   as much as how you start.

2. "Must WE fetch you water?" (v.10). Moses takes credit for God's work.
   When God uses you, the temptation is to believe you are the source.
   Every gift, every fruit, every miracle belongs to God — not to the
   instrument He uses.

3. God still provides (v.11). The water flows despite Moses' disobedience.
   God's grace to the people is not contingent on the leader's perfection.
   He blesses His people even through imperfect servants — but the servant
   still bears consequences.

4. "Speak ye unto the rock" (v.8). Christ was struck once (crucified);
   now we come to Him by speaking — by faith, by prayer, by calling on
   His name. We do not need to re-crucify Him. His one sacrifice is
   sufficient; we access its benefits by the word of faith.

5. Aaron's garments transferred (v.26). The priesthood outlives the priest.
   Aaron dies, but the office continues in Eleazar. Human priests are
   temporary — which is why we need Christ, whose priesthood is eternal
   (Hebrews 7:24). He never needs a successor.

6. A chapter of loss (vv.1, 12, 21, 28). Miriam dies. Moses loses his
   inheritance. Edom rejects them. Aaron dies. Sometimes life is a
   succession of losses. But God is still present, still providing, still
   leading forward. Loss does not mean abandonment.
