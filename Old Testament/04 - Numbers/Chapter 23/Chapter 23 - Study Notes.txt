Numbers - Chapter 23
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Balaam blesses Israel instead of cursing them — twice he pronounces God's blessing, unable to reverse what God has declared.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses. Numbers is the fourth book of the Pentateuch, written during
the wilderness period (approximately 1445-1405 BC). Hebrew title: "Bemidbar"
— "In the wilderness." The book records Israel's 38 years of wilderness
wandering between Sinai and the Promised Land — a journey that should have
taken 11 days (Deuteronomy 1:2) but took 40 years due to unbelief.

Historical Context: Chapter 23 contains Balaam's first two oracles — both
blessings instead of curses. Balak has hired Balaam to curse Israel, but
God overrules: every time Balaam opens his mouth, blessing comes out. The
chapter follows a repeated pattern: Balak builds seven altars and offers
sacrifices, Balaam goes apart to meet God, God puts words in Balaam's
mouth, and Balaam delivers an oracle that blesses Israel. Balak is
frustrated and moves to a different location, hoping a change of vantage
point will change the oracle — but God's word does not depend on geography.
The first oracle (vv.7-10) declares Israel's uniqueness and innumerability.
The second oracle (vv.18-24) contains one of the most important theological
statements in Scripture: "God is not a man, that he should lie; neither
the son of man, that he should repent" (v.19). It also declares that God
has "not beheld iniquity in Jacob" (v.21) — not because Israel is sinless,
but because God's covenant covers their sin. These oracles reveal God's
unchangeable commitment to His people, spoken through the mouth of a
reluctant, corrupt prophet — proving that the blessing depends on God's
character, not the instrument's worthiness.

First Oracle: Israel's Uniqueness (vv.1-12): Balaam instructs Balak to
build seven altars with seven bulls and seven rams (v.1) — an attempt to
manipulate God through elaborate sacrifice. Balaam goes apart; God meets
him and puts a word in his mouth (vv.4-5). The first oracle (vv.7-10):
"How shall I curse, whom God hath not cursed?" (v.8) — Balaam cannot do
what God has not done. Israel is "a people dwelling alone, and not counting
itself among the nations" (v.9) — unique, set apart, not like other
peoples. "Who can count the dust of Jacob?" (v.10) — fulfillment of
Genesis 13:16. Balaam's personal wish: "Let me die the death of the
righteous, and let my last end be like his!" (v.10) — he desires the
righteous man's death without living the righteous man's life. Balak is
furious: "thou hast blessed them altogether" (v.11).

Second Oracle: God's Unchangeable Blessing (vv.13-30): Balak moves to
Pisgah — "thou shalt see but the utmost part of them" (v.13) — perhaps
seeing fewer will make cursing easier. Same ritual: seven altars, seven
bulls, seven rams (v.14). God meets Balaam again (v.16). The second oracle
(vv.18-24) is even stronger: "God is not a man, that he should lie;
neither the son of man, that he should repent: hath he said, and shall he
not do it?" (v.19). God's word is irreversible. "I have received
commandment to bless: and he hath blessed; and I cannot reverse it" (v.20).
"He hath not beheld iniquity in Jacob, neither hath he seen perverseness
in Israel" (v.21) — God looks at Israel through covenant eyes, not
according to their failures. "The LORD his God is with him, and the shout
of a king is among them" (v.21). "There is no enchantment against Jacob,
neither is there any divination against Israel" (v.23) — no curse, no
spell, no sorcery can touch them. Israel is like a lion that devours its
prey (v.24). Balak: "Neither curse them at all, nor bless them at all"
(v.25) — if you cannot curse, at least stop blessing! Balak tries a third
location: the top of Peor (vv.27-30).

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- The high places of Baal (from 22:41): Balaam delivers oracles from elevated positions overlooking Israel's camp on the plains of Moab.
- "The top of the rocks" / "the top of Pisgah" (vv.9, 14): Multiple viewing locations; Balak moves Balaam hoping a different vantage point will produce a curse.
- The top of Peor (v.28): The third location overlooking "the wilderness" (Jeshimon).

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.8  — "How can I curse whom God has not cursed? How can I denounce whom
        the LORD has not denounced?" (ESV) — The impossibility of cursing
        what God has blessed. Human words cannot override divine decree.

v.9  — "The people shall dwell alone, and shall not be reckoned among the
        nations." — Israel's uniqueness: set apart, distinct, not
        assimilated into the nations. This remains true of the Jewish
        people to this day.

v.10 — "Let me die the death of the righteous, and let my last end be
        like his!" — Balaam wants the righteous man's reward without the
        righteous man's life. He desires the destination without the
        journey. Tragically, he dies in battle against Israel (31:8).

v.19 — "God is not man, that he should lie, or a son of man, that he
        should change his mind. Has he said, and will he not do it?" (ESV)
        — One of the most important theological statements in Scripture.
        God's character: truthful, unchanging, faithful to His word.

v.21 — "He has not beheld misfortune in Jacob, nor has he seen trouble in
        Israel." (ESV) — Not that Israel is sinless, but that God's
        covenant covers their sin. He looks at them through grace.

v.23 — "For there is no enchantment against Jacob, no divination against
        Israel." (ESV) — No spiritual weapon can touch God's people.
        Curses, spells, divination — all powerless against those God has
        blessed.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/numbers-23/
  Guzik emphasizes verse 19 as one of the foundational statements about
  God's nature in all of Scripture. He notes the irony: this truth about
  God's faithfulness comes through the mouth of a man who IS a liar and
  who DOES change his mind for money. God's truth is not diminished by the
  imperfection of the messenger. He also highlights v.21 as a picture of
  justification: God does not "see" Israel's sin because He has chosen to
  cover it.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "'God is not a man, that he should lie.' Here is the rock on which faith
  stands. Men lie — God does not. Men change — God does not. Men promise
  and forget — God promises and performs. Has He said He will save you?
  He will. Has He said He will keep you? He will. Has He said nothing
  shall separate you from His love? Nothing shall. Rest here, trembling
  soul: God is not a man. His word is as certain as His nature. And His
  nature is eternal, unchangeable, and true."

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.3  — KJV: "he went to an high place" / ESV: "he went to a bare height"
       — Hebrew "vayyeelekh shefi." "Shefi" = a bare, windswept height.
       Balaam goes to an elevated, exposed place to meet God — a common
       practice for diviners seeking revelation.

v.7  — KJV: "he took up his parable" / ESV: "Balaam took up his
       discourse" — Hebrew "vayyissa m'shalo." "Mashal" = a proverb,
       oracle, parable, or prophetic discourse. KJV's "parable" and ESV's
       "discourse" both capture aspects of this poetic, prophetic speech.

v.10 — KJV: "the death of the righteous" / ESV: "the death of the
       upright" — Hebrew "mot y'sharim." "Yesharim" = the upright, the
       straight ones. Balaam recognizes Israel's blessed state and wishes
       for their end — but will not live their life. He wants heaven
       without holiness.

v.19 — KJV: "that he should repent" / ESV: "that he should change his
       mind" — Hebrew "v'yitnecham." "Nacham" = to be sorry, to relent,
       to change one's mind. KJV's "repent" can confuse modern readers
       (God does not repent of sin); ESV's "change his mind" is clearer.
       The point: God does not vacillate or reverse His declared purposes.

v.21 — KJV: "He hath not beheld iniquity in Jacob, neither hath he seen
       perverseness in Israel" / ESV: "He has not beheld misfortune in
       Jacob, nor has he seen trouble in Israel" — Hebrew "lo hibit aven
       b'Ya'aqov v'lo ra'ah amal b'Yisra'el." This verse is debated:
       "aven" can mean "iniquity" or "misfortune/trouble"; "amal" can
       mean "perverseness" or "trouble/toil." KJV reads it as God not
       imputing sin; ESV reads it as God not allowing trouble/misfortune.
       Both are theologically valid; the KJV reading connects more
       directly to justification theology.

v.22 — KJV: "the strength of an unicorn" / ESV: "the horns of the wild
       ox" — Hebrew "k'to'afot r'em." "Re'em" is an extinct wild ox
       (aurochs), not a mythical unicorn. KJV followed the Septuagint's
       "monokeros" (one-horned); ESV correctly identifies the animal. The
       point: God's strength for Israel is like the unstoppable power of
       a wild ox.

v.23 — KJV: "there is no enchantment against Jacob, neither is there any
       divination against Israel" / ESV: "there is no enchantment against
       Jacob, no divination against Israel" — Hebrew "lo nachash
       b'Ya'aqov v'lo qesem b'Yisra'el." "Nachash" = enchantment,
       sorcery, snake-charming. "Qesem" = divination. No form of occult
       power can touch God's people. This is spoken by a diviner — who
       admits his own craft is powerless against Israel.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Oracle / Parable / Discourse (Hebrew: "mashal") — A prophetic utterance,
               often in poetic form. Balaam's oracles are structured
               Hebrew poetry — parallel lines, vivid imagery, prophetic
               declaration. They are among the finest poetry in the
               Pentateuch, despite coming from a corrupt source.

"God Is Not a Man" (v.19) — A foundational theological statement about
               God's nature: He does not lie (truthfulness), He does not
               change His mind (immutability), He fulfills what He speaks
               (faithfulness). This distinguishes the God of Israel from
               all pagan deities, who were portrayed as capricious and
               unreliable.

"Dwell Alone" (v.9) — Israel's distinctiveness among the nations. Not
               isolation but uniqueness — they are not to be assimilated
               or counted among the nations. This has been remarkably
               fulfilled: the Jewish people have maintained distinct
               identity for over 3,000 years despite dispersion.

Enchantment / Divination — Occult practices used to manipulate spiritual
               forces. "Nachash" (enchantment/sorcery) and "qesem"
               (divination) represent the full range of pagan spiritual
               warfare. Balaam declares them powerless against Israel —
               an extraordinary admission from a professional diviner.

Wild Ox / Unicorn (Hebrew: "re'em") — The aurochs, an extinct species of
               massive wild cattle. Known for incredible strength and
               untameable nature. Used as a metaphor for God's power on
               Israel's behalf — unstoppable, overwhelming force.

Seven Altars — Balaam's attempt to manipulate God through elaborate
               sacrifice. Seven is the number of completeness; seven
               altars with seven bulls and seven rams represents maximum
               ritual effort. But God cannot be bribed or manipulated —
               He speaks what He wills regardless of the offerings.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.7 — NET: "he took up his oracle and said" / ASV: "he took up his parable, and said" / WEB: "he took up his parable, and said" / KJV: "he took up his parable, and said" — NET uses "oracle" where ASV/WEB/KJV use "parable" (Hebrew "mashal" — a prophetic utterance/saying/oracle). Balaam's speeches are poetic oracles, not parables in the NT sense.

- v.8 — NET: "How can I curse one whom God has not cursed?" / ASV: "How shall I curse, whom God hath not cursed?" / WEB: "How shall I curse, whom God has not cursed?" — All translations agree on this key rhetorical question. No human divination can override divine blessing. The oracle structure is poetic parallelism.

- v.10 — NET: "Who has counted the dust of Jacob?" / ASV: "Who can count the dust of Jacob?" / WEB: "Who can count the dust of Jacob?" — An allusion to God's promise to Abraham (Gen 13:16). NET changes "can" to "has" — implying the number is already beyond counting. Balaam's blessing echoes the Abrahamic covenant.

- v.19 — NET: "God is not a man, that he should lie, nor a human being, that he should change his mind" / ASV: "God is not a man, that he should lie, neither the son of man, that he should repent" / WEB: "God is not a man, that he should lie, nor the son of man, that he should repent" — NET uses "human being" and "change his mind" where ASV/WEB use "son of man" and "repent" (Hebrew "yinnachem"). NET clarifies that God's "repenting" in other passages must be understood through this lens.

- v.21 — NET: "He has not looked on iniquity in Jacob, nor has he seen trouble in Israel" / ASV: "He hath not beheld iniquity in Jacob; neither hath he seen perverseness in Israel" / WEB: "He has not seen iniquity in Jacob. Neither has he seen perverseness in Israel" — NET uses "trouble" where ASV/WEB use "perverseness" (Hebrew "amal"). God does not see Israel through the lens of their failures but through His covenant grace.

- v.21 — NET: "the Lord their God is with them; the shout of their king is among them" / ASV: "Jehovah his God is with him, and the shout of a king is among them" / WEB: "Yahweh his God is with him. The shout of a king is among them" — "The shout of a king" (Hebrew "teru'at melek") — God is Israel's king, and His royal presence among them is what makes them unconquerable. The divine name difference is vivid here.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "God is not a man, that he should lie" (v.19). When you doubt God's
   promises, remember: He is not like humans who forget, exaggerate, or
   break their word. What He has said, He will do. What He has spoken, He
   will fulfill. Your doubts do not change His faithfulness.

2. "He hath not beheld iniquity in Jacob" (v.21). This is justification:
   God chooses not to see Israel's sin because of His covenant. In Christ,
   God does not "behold iniquity" in you — not because you are sinless,
   but because Christ's righteousness covers you. "There is therefore now
   no condemnation" (Romans 8:1).

3. "There is no enchantment against Jacob" (v.23). No spiritual weapon
   formed against God's people can succeed. No curse, no spell, no
   divination, no demonic strategy can override God's blessing. If you
   belong to Christ, you are untouchable by the enemy's curses.

4. "Let me die the death of the righteous" (v.10). Balaam wants the
   righteous man's end without the righteous man's life. Many desire
   heaven who will not walk the narrow road. You cannot have the
   destination without the journey. Balaam dies in battle against Israel
   (31:8) — the opposite of what he wished.

5. Balak's strategy: change the location (vv.13, 27). When the first
   oracle blesses, Balak moves to a new spot — as if geography determines
   God's word. We do the same: changing churches, changing circumstances,
   hoping God will say something different. But God's word does not change
   with your vantage point.

6. God speaks through imperfect instruments (v.5). Balaam is corrupt —
   yet God puts true words in his mouth. This is both encouraging (God's
   truth is not limited by the messenger's character) and sobering (being
   used by God does not validate your life). Judge truth by its content,
   not its source.
