Numbers - Chapter 28
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
God reiterates the daily, weekly, and monthly offerings — a rhythm of continual worship that keeps Israel's relationship with Him at the center.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses. Numbers is the fourth book of the Pentateuch, written during
the wilderness period (approximately 1445-1405 BC). Hebrew title: "Bemidbar"
— "In the wilderness." The book records Israel's 38 years of wilderness
wandering between Sinai and the Promised Land — a journey that should have
taken 11 days (Deuteronomy 1:2) but took 40 years due to unbelief.

Historical Context: Chapters 28-29 form a unit prescribing the complete
sacrificial calendar for Israel — the regular offerings that structure
worship life in the Promised Land. These instructions are given to the new
generation (after the second census of chapter 26) as they prepare to enter
Canaan. While similar offerings were prescribed at Sinai (Exodus 29:38-42;
Leviticus 23), this restatement emphasizes their ongoing obligation in the
land. The offerings move from most frequent to least: daily (morning and
evening), weekly (Sabbath), monthly (new moon), and annual festivals
(Passover/Unleavened Bread, Feast of Weeks/Firstfruits). Chapter 28 covers
daily through Feast of Weeks; chapter 29 covers the seventh-month festivals.
The key phrase repeated throughout: "a pleasing aroma to the LORD" (ESV) /
"a sweet savour unto the LORD" (KJV) — these offerings are God's "food"
(v.2), not because He needs sustenance, but because they represent
communion between God and His people. The regularity of sacrifice teaches
that access to God requires constant atonement — a truth fulfilled in
Christ's once-for-all sacrifice (Hebrews 7:27; 10:1-14).

The Daily Offering — Tamid (vv.1-8): God calls these "my offering, my
bread" (v.2) — language of personal relationship. The continual burnt
offering: two unblemished yearling lambs daily — one in the morning, one
at twilight (evening). Each accompanied by a grain offering (1/10 ephah
of fine flour mixed with 1/4 hin of beaten oil) and a drink offering
(1/4 hin of wine/strong drink poured out in the Holy Place). This offering
was "ordained at Mount Sinai" (v.6) — it has been in effect since Exodus
29:38-42. The tamid ("continual") offering frames every day with sacrifice
— Israel's first and last act each day is worship.

The Sabbath Offering (vv.9-10): On the Sabbath: two additional yearling
lambs (beyond the daily two), with double grain and drink offerings. The
Sabbath offering is "beside the continual burnt offering" — it adds to,
not replaces, the daily sacrifice. The Sabbath doubles the daily worship.

The Monthly Offering — New Moon (vv.11-15): At the beginning of each month:
two young bulls, one ram, seven yearling lambs (all as burnt offerings),
with proportional grain offerings (3/10 ephah per bull, 2/10 per ram,
1/10 per lamb) and drink offerings (1/2 hin per bull, 1/3 per ram, 1/4
per lamb). Plus one male goat as a sin offering. All "beside the continual
burnt offering" — the monthly adds to the daily.

Passover and Unleavened Bread (vv.16-25): The 14th of the first month:
Passover. The 15th through 21st: Feast of Unleavened Bread. Days 1 and 7
are holy convocations (no ordinary work). Daily for seven days: same
offerings as the new moon (2 bulls, 1 ram, 7 lambs + 1 goat for sin
offering) — all beside the daily tamid. The Passover lamb itself is not
mentioned here (it was a household offering, not a public altar sacrifice).

Feast of Weeks / Firstfruits (vv.26-31): "The day of the firstfruits" —
also called Feast of Weeks (Shavuot) or Pentecost (50 days after
Firstfruits). A holy convocation with no ordinary work. Offerings: 2 bulls,
1 ram, 7 yearling lambs as burnt offerings + 1 male goat for sin offering.
The "new grain offering" (v.26) celebrates the wheat harvest — the first
fruits belong to God.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Plains of Moab: Offering laws given in preparation for worship in the Promised Land. "My offering, and my bread" (v.2) — God claims the firstfruits of the land they will inhabit.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "My offering, and my bread for my sacrifices made by fire, for a
        sweet savour unto me, shall ye observe to offer unto me in their
        due season." — God claims the offerings as "mine" — worship is
        not optional but obligatory, and timing matters ("due season").

v.3  — "Two lambs of the first year without spot day by day, for a
        continual burnt offering." — Two lambs daily, 365 days a year,
        730 lambs annually — the relentless cost of maintaining fellowship
        with a holy God. Each one points to the Lamb of God.

v.6  — "It is a continual burnt offering, which was ordained in mount
        Sinai." — Continuity: what God established at Sinai remains in
        force. God's worship requirements do not change with geography.

v.10 — "This is the burnt offering of every sabbath, beside the continual
        burnt offering." — The Sabbath adds to the daily — rest does not
        mean less worship but more.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/numbers-28/
  Guzik emphasizes the relentless regularity of the sacrificial system —
  every morning and evening, every week, every month, every festival. The
  sheer volume of blood and sacrifice taught Israel that sin is serious
  and access to God is costly. All of it points to Christ's sufficient,
  once-for-all sacrifice.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "Morning and evening, a lamb. Day after day, year after year, the knife
  fell and the blood flowed. Was God satisfied? Never — for the next
  morning required another lamb. The repetition was the confession of
  insufficiency. But when the Lamb of God hung upon the cross, He cried
  'It is finished!' — and no morning lamb was ever needed again. The
  continual offering has found its end in the one offering that continues
  forever."

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.2  — Hebrew "qorbani lachmi l'ishai re'ach nichochi" — "Lechem" (bread/food) is used
       metaphorically — God does not eat (Psalm 50:12-13). "Re'ach nichoach" — the scent
       of satisfaction/acceptance.
       KJV: "My offering, and my bread for my sacrifices made by fire, for a sweet savour unto me"
       ESV: "My offering, my food for my food offerings, my pleasing aroma"
       ASV: "My oblation, my food for my offerings made by fire, for a sweet savor"
       NET: "My offering, my food for my gifts, of a soothing aroma"
       WEB: "My offering, my food for my offerings made by fire, for a pleasant aroma"
       Analysis: KJV's "bread" is more literal; ESV's "food" is clearer for modern readers.
       NET compresses the Hebrew with "my food for my gifts." The aroma phrase shows variety:
       "sweet savour" (KJV), "pleasing aroma" (ESV), "sweet savor" (ASV), "soothing aroma"
       (NET), "pleasant aroma" (WEB). Each captures one aspect of the Hebrew: calming, sweet,
       pleasing. "Food" (Hebrew "lechem") does not mean God eats — it is His appointed portion
       that satisfies/pleases Him, like food satisfies a person.

v.3  — Hebrew "kevasim benei shanah temimim" — the daily offering (tamid).
       KJV: "Two lambs of the first year without spot day by day, for a continual burnt offering"
       ESV: "two lambs a year old without blemish, day by day, for a regular burnt offering"
       ASV: "two he-lambs a year old without blemish, day by day, for a continual burnt-offering"
       NET: "two lambs of the first year without blemish each day for a continual burnt offering"
       WEB: "two male lambs a year old without defect, day by day, for a continual burnt offering"
       Analysis: Morning and evening without interruption. NET specifies "without blemish"; WEB
       says "without defect"; ASV "without blemish"; KJV "without spot." All convey the Hebrew
       "tamim" — wholeness, completeness, no deficiency.

v.4  — Hebrew "bein ha-arbayim" — literally "between the two evenings."
       KJV: "at even"
       ESV: "at twilight"
       ASV: "at even"
       NET: "in the late afternoon"
       WEB: "at evening"
       Analysis: This phrase is debated: either between noon and sunset, or between sunset and
       dark. ESV's "twilight" captures the general sense; KJV's "even" (evening) is traditional.

v.5  — Hebrew "minchah" — grain/flour offering.
       KJV: "a meat offering"
       ESV: "a grain offering"
       ASV: "a meal-offering"
       NET: "a grain offering"
       WEB: "a meal offering"
       Analysis: In KJV English, "meat" meant "food" generally (not flesh). The minchah is a
       grain/flour offering. ESV's "grain offering" prevents confusion for modern readers. This
       is a significant KJV archaism. ASV/WEB use "meal offering" as an intermediate option.

v.6  — Hebrew "olat tamid ha-asuyah b'har sinai" — continual offering ordained at Sinai.
       KJV: "It is a continual burnt offering, which was ordained in mount Sinai"
       ESV: "It is a regular burnt offering, which was ordained at Mount Sinai"
       ASV: "which was ordained in mount Sinai for a sweet savor"
       NET: "which was established on Mount Sinai as a soothing aroma"
       WEB: "which was ordained in Mount Sinai for a pleasant aroma"
       Analysis: The daily sacrifice was established at Sinai — showing continuity from
       revelation to practice. It runs from Moses until the temple's destruction in AD 70.

v.7  — Hebrew "shekhar" — fermented drink.
       KJV: "the strong wine"
       ESV: "a drink offering of strong drink"
       ASV: "strong drink"
       NET: "strong drink"
       WEB: "strong drink"
       Analysis: Shekhar is fermented drink (beer, strong wine, or other intoxicant). It is
       poured out to God, not consumed by the worshiper. The drink offering symbolizes life
       poured out completely.

v.9  — Hebrew "u'v'yom ha-shabbat" — and on the Sabbath day.
       KJV: "on the sabbath day"
       ESV: "On the Sabbath day"
       ASV: "on the sabbath day"
       NET: "on the Sabbath day"
       WEB: "On the Sabbath day"
       Analysis: Sabbath doubles the daily offering (4 lambs instead of 2). Greater holiness
       requires greater sacrifice — a principle running through the entire calendar.

v.11 — Hebrew "roshei chodshekem" — the heads/beginnings of your months.
       KJV: "in the beginnings of your months"
       ESV: "At the beginnings of your months"
       ASV: "And in the beginnings of your months"
       NET: "On the first of each month"
       WEB: "In the beginnings of your months"
       Analysis: NET modernizes with "On the first of each month." New Moon celebrations
       marked time's passage and renewal.

v.14 — Hebrew "chatzi ha-hin yayin la-par" — half a hin of wine for a bull.
       KJV: "half an hin of wine unto a bullock"
       ESV: "half a hin of wine for a bull"
       ASV: "half a hin of wine for a bullock"
       NET: "half a hin for each bull"
       WEB: "half a hin of wine for a bull"
       Analysis: A hin is approximately 6 quarts/liters. The drink offerings are proportional
       to the animal's size: 1/2 hin for a bull, 1/3 for a ram, 1/4 for a lamb.

v.18 — Hebrew "kol m'lekhet avodah lo ta'asu" — no laborious/occupational work.
       KJV: "ye shall do no manner of servile work"
       ESV: "You shall not do any ordinary work"
       ASV: "ye shall do no servile work"
       NET: "you must not do any ordinary work"
       WEB: "you shall do no servile work"
       Analysis: "Servile work" (KJV/ASV/WEB) means laborious/occupational work. ESV/NET's
       "ordinary work" is clearer — regular daily labor ceases, but festival preparation
       (cooking, etc.) is permitted.

v.26 — Hebrew "yom ha-bikkurim" — day of firstfruits (Feast of Weeks/Shavuot/Pentecost).
       KJV: "in the day of the firstfruits, when ye bring a new meat offering unto the LORD, after your weeks be out"
       ESV: "On the day of the firstfruits, when you offer a grain offering of new grain to the LORD at your Feast of Weeks"
       ASV: "Also in the day of the first-fruits, when ye offer a new meal-offering unto Jehovah in your feast of weeks"
       NET: "On the day of the firstfruits, when you bring a new grain offering to the Lord"
       WEB: "Also in the day of the first fruits, when you offer a new meal offering to Yahweh in your feast of weeks"
       Analysis: ESV explicitly names the festival ("Feast of Weeks"); KJV describes it ("after
       your weeks be out"). Both refer to Shavuot/Pentecost — 50 days after the wave sheaf of
       Firstfruits.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Tamid (Continual Offering) — The daily burnt offering of two lambs, one
               morning and one evening. The word "tamid" means "continual,
               perpetual, regular." It formed the backbone of Israel's
               worship — the fire on the altar was never to go out
               (Leviticus 6:13).

Burnt Offering (Hebrew: "olah") — "That which ascends." An offering
               entirely consumed by fire — nothing kept by the worshiper
               or priest. Represents total consecration and acceptance
               before God.

Grain Offering (Hebrew: "minchah") — Fine flour mixed with oil, sometimes
               baked. Represents the fruit of human labor offered to God.
               Always accompanies the burnt offering — dedication of both
               life (animal) and livelihood (grain).

Drink Offering (Hebrew: "nesek") — Wine or strong drink poured out at the
               base of the altar. Represents life poured out completely —
               not consumed but given entirely to God.

Ephah — A dry measure of approximately 22 liters (about 3/5 bushel). A
               tenth of an ephah (an "omer") is roughly 2 quarts of flour.

Hin — A liquid measure of approximately 6 liters (about 1.5 gallons). The
               drink offerings use fractions: 1/2, 1/3, and 1/4 hin.

Sweet Savour / Pleasing Aroma (Hebrew: "re'ach nichoach") — Literally
               "scent of rest/satisfaction." Not that God smells smoke,
               but that the offering brings Him satisfaction — it is
               accepted and pleasing. The same phrase describes Noah's
               sacrifice (Genesis 8:21).

Holy Convocation (Hebrew: "miqra qodesh") — A sacred assembly; a called
               gathering for worship. Work ceases and the community
               assembles before God.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "My offering... in their due season" (v.2). God prescribes both the
   content and the timing of worship. Worship is not merely spontaneous
   emotion — it is disciplined, regular, and ordered. Do you have a
   "due season" for meeting with God, or only when you feel like it?

2. Morning and evening, every day (vv.3-4). The day begins and ends with
   sacrifice. Before any work is done, a lamb dies. Before sleep comes,
   blood is shed. The Christian life is similarly framed: begin and end
   each day at the cross.

3. The Sabbath doubles the offering (vv.9-10). Rest is not less worship
   but more. The day of ceasing from labor is the day of increased
   sacrifice. True rest is found not in inactivity but in intensified
   communion with God.

4. The relentless repetition (vv.3, 9, 11, 16, 26). Daily, weekly,
   monthly, annually — the sacrifices never stop. This repetition confesses
   insufficiency: no single offering is enough. Only Christ's once-for-all
   sacrifice ends the cycle (Hebrews 10:11-14).

5. "Without blemish" — repeated throughout. Every animal must be perfect.
   God accepts no defective worship. This points to Christ, "a lamb
   without blemish and without spot" (1 Peter 1:19). Are you offering God
   your best, or your leftovers?

6. The drink offering poured out (v.7). Wine poured at the altar — not
   consumed but given entirely. Paul describes his life as a drink offering
   (Philippians 2:17; 2 Timothy 4:6). Total self-giving, holding nothing
   back. Is your life being poured out for God's purposes?
