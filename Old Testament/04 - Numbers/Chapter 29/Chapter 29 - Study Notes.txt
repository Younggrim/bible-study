Numbers - Chapter 29
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
God prescribes offerings for the fall feasts — Trumpets, Day of Atonement, and the Feast of Tabernacles with its abundant sacrifices.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses. Numbers is the fourth book of the Pentateuch, written during
the wilderness period (approximately 1445-1405 BC). Hebrew title: "Bemidbar"
— "In the wilderness." The book records Israel's 38 years of wilderness
wandering between Sinai and the Promised Land — a journey that should have
taken 11 days (Deuteronomy 1:2) but took 40 years due to unbelief.

Historical Context: Chapter 29 continues the sacrificial calendar begun in
chapter 28, covering the three festivals of the seventh month (Tishri) —
the most sacred month in Israel's liturgical year. These are: (1) the Feast
of Trumpets (1st day), (2) the Day of Atonement (10th day), and (3) the
Feast of Tabernacles/Booths (15th-22nd days). The seventh month is to the
year what the Sabbath is to the week — a climactic time of intensified
worship. The Feast of Tabernacles is the grandest festival, requiring more
sacrifices than any other occasion: over the seven days, 70 bulls are
offered (13+12+11+10+9+8+7), decreasing by one each day. Many scholars
see the 70 bulls as representing the 70 nations of Genesis 10 — Israel
interceding for the whole world. The eighth day ("solemn assembly") reduces
to a single bull — perhaps representing Israel alone with God after the
nations are addressed. These festivals prophetically point to end-time
realities: Trumpets → the return of Christ; Atonement → Israel's national
repentance; Tabernacles → the Millennial Kingdom of joy and rest.

Feast of Trumpets (vv.1-6): The first day of the seventh month — a holy
convocation, no ordinary work. "A day of blowing the trumpets" (KJV) /
"a day for you to blow the trumpets" (ESV). Offerings: 1 bull, 1 ram,
7 yearling lambs (burnt offerings) + 1 male goat (sin offering), with
proportional grain offerings. All "beside" the monthly new moon offering
and the daily tamid. The trumpets (shofar — ram's horn) signal awakening,
assembly, and preparation. This feast begins the "Ten Days of Awe" leading
to the Day of Atonement.

Day of Atonement — Yom Kippur (vv.7-11): The tenth day of the seventh
month — a holy convocation. "Ye shall afflict your souls" (KJV) / "afflict
yourselves" (ESV) — fasting and self-denial. No work whatsoever. Offerings:
1 bull, 1 ram, 7 yearling lambs (burnt offerings) + 1 male goat (sin
offering). These are "beside the sin offering of atonement" (v.11) — the
special Yom Kippur ritual of Leviticus 16 (the scapegoat, the blood
sprinkled on the mercy seat) is separate from and in addition to these
public offerings. This is the most solemn day of the year — the one day
the high priest enters the Holy of Holies.

Feast of Tabernacles / Booths — Sukkot (vv.12-34): The fifteenth day of
the seventh month — a holy convocation, no ordinary work, a seven-day
feast. This is the harvest festival of joy and thanksgiving. The offerings
are extraordinary:
  Day 1: 13 bulls, 2 rams, 14 lambs + 1 goat (sin offering)
  Day 2: 12 bulls, 2 rams, 14 lambs + 1 goat
  Day 3: 11 bulls, 2 rams, 14 lambs + 1 goat
  Day 4: 10 bulls, 2 rams, 14 lambs + 1 goat
  Day 5:  9 bulls, 2 rams, 14 lambs + 1 goat
  Day 6:  8 bulls, 2 rams, 14 lambs + 1 goat
  Day 7:  7 bulls, 2 rams, 14 lambs + 1 goat
  TOTAL: 70 bulls, 14 rams, 98 lambs, 7 goats = 189 animals

The bulls decrease by one each day (13→7), totaling 70. The rams (14) and
lambs (98) remain constant. Each day also includes proportional grain and
drink offerings. All "beside the continual burnt offering."

The Eighth Day — Solemn Assembly (vv.35-38): The 22nd day — "a solemn
assembly" (Hebrew: "atzeret" — a closing/restraining). No ordinary work.
Offerings dramatically reduce: 1 bull, 1 ram, 7 lambs + 1 goat. After
the grandeur of seven days, the eighth day is intimate — a quiet conclusion.
Jesus stood on this day and cried, "If any man thirst, let him come unto
me, and drink" (John 7:37).

Conclusion (vv.39-40): These are the "appointed feasts" (ESV) / "set
feasts" (KJV) — in addition to personal vows and freewill offerings.
The prescribed calendar does not eliminate spontaneous worship; it provides
the framework within which personal devotion operates.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Plains of Moab: Festival calendar laws given here in preparation for worship life in the Promised Land.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "And in the seventh month, on the first day of the month... It is a
        day for you to blow the trumpets." (ESV) — The trumpet awakens,
        assembles, and announces. Prophetically: the trumpet of Christ's
        return (1 Thessalonians 4:16; 1 Corinthians 15:52).

v.7  — "And you shall afflict yourselves. You shall not do any work." (ESV)
        — The Day of Atonement: the only required fast in the Mosaic law.
        Self-denial accompanies atonement — you cannot celebrate while
        sin remains unaddressed.

v.12 — "you shall keep a feast to the LORD seven days." (ESV) — Tabernacles
        is the feast of joy — after atonement comes celebration. After the
        cross comes resurrection life.

v.35 — "On the eighth day you shall have a solemn assembly." (ESV) — The
        eighth day: beyond the seven of creation, pointing to new creation.
        Jesus chooses this day for His greatest invitation (John 7:37-38).

Charles Spurgeon:
  "Seventy bullocks for the seventy nations — Israel standing as priest
  for the world, offering sacrifice on behalf of all peoples. And on the
  eighth day, one bullock — Israel alone with her God in intimate communion.
  First the world is blessed through Israel's intercession; then Israel
  herself rests in God's presence. So it shall be: through Israel's
  Messiah, all nations are blessed, and then comes the eternal rest where
  God and His people dwell together, and the sacrifices cease because the
  Lamb Himself is the temple's light."

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1  — KJV: "it is a day of blowing the trumpets unto you" / ESV: "It is
       a day for you to blow the trumpets" — Hebrew "yom teru'ah yihyeh
       lakhem." "Teru'ah" means a shout, blast, or alarm — specifically
       the staccato blasts of the shofar (ram's horn). KJV and ESV both
       capture the meaning; the Hebrew emphasizes the sound itself rather
       than the instrument.

v.7  — KJV: "ye shall afflict your souls" / ESV: "afflict yourselves" —
       Hebrew "v'innitem et nafshoteikhem." "Innah" means to humble, deny,
       afflict. "Nefesh" (soul) here means the whole self — body and soul.
       Traditionally understood as fasting, but includes all forms of
       self-denial. ESV's "yourselves" is simpler; KJV's "your souls"
       preserves the Hebrew "nefesh."

v.11 — KJV: "beside the sin offering of atonement" / ESV: "besides the
       sin offering of atonement" — This refers to the special Yom Kippur
       goat of Leviticus 16 (the scapegoat ritual). The offerings listed
       here in Numbers 29 are additional public offerings on the same day.

v.12 — KJV: "ye shall keep a feast unto the LORD seven days" / ESV: "you
       shall keep a feast to the LORD seven days" — Hebrew "v'chagotem chag
       la-YHWH shiv'at yamim." "Chag" (feast/festival) comes from a root
       meaning "to dance, to make a pilgrimage." This is the most joyous
       festival — pure celebration after the solemnity of Atonement.

v.35 — KJV: "ye shall have a solemn assembly" / ESV: "you shall have a
       solemn assembly" — Hebrew "atzeret tihyeh lakhem." "Atzeret" comes
       from "atzar" — to restrain, hold back, close. It is a closing
       ceremony — the conclusion of the festival season. The word suggests
       being held back in God's presence — lingering rather than rushing
       away.

v.39 — KJV: "in your set feasts" / ESV: "at your appointed feasts" —
       Hebrew "b'mo'adeikhem." "Mo'ed" means appointed time, fixed season,
       meeting. These are not arbitrary celebrations but divinely scheduled
       appointments with God. ESV's "appointed" captures the intentionality.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Feast of Trumpets (Hebrew: Yom Teru'ah) — "Day of Blasting." The first
               day of the seventh month. Marked by shofar blasts. Later
               tradition identifies this as Rosh Hashanah (New Year). It
               begins the "Ten Days of Awe" culminating in Yom Kippur.

Day of Atonement (Hebrew: Yom Kippur) — "Day of Covering." The 10th of
               Tishri. The most solemn day of the year — the only required
               fast. The high priest enters the Holy of Holies to make
               atonement for the nation's sins (Leviticus 16).

Feast of Tabernacles / Booths (Hebrew: Sukkot) — "Booths/Shelters." The
               15th-21st of Tishri. Israel dwells in temporary shelters
               remembering the wilderness journey. The harvest festival of
               joy and thanksgiving. The most elaborate sacrificial
               requirements of any feast.

Solemn Assembly (Hebrew: Atzeret) — "Closing/Restraining." The eighth day
               (22nd of Tishri) — a concluding sacred gathering. Intimate
               and restrained after the grandeur of Tabernacles.

Shofar — Ram's horn trumpet. Used for the Feast of Trumpets, warfare,
               coronations, and announcements. Its sound calls to
               attention, repentance, and assembly.

Seventy Bulls — The total bulls offered during the seven days of
               Tabernacles (13+12+11+10+9+8+7=70). Possibly representing
               the 70 nations of Genesis 10 — Israel as priestly nation
               interceding for the world.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.1 — NET: "On the first day of the seventh month" / ASV: "And in the seventh month, on the first day of the month" / WEB: "In the seventh month, on the first day of the month" — The Feast of Trumpets (Rosh Hashanah). NET restructures for clarity; ASV/WEB follow Hebrew word order. The seventh month begins the most sacred period in Israel's calendar.

- v.1 — NET: "you must not do any ordinary work" / ASV: "ye shall do no servile work" / WEB: "you shall do no servile work" / KJV: "ye shall do no servile work" — NET modernizes "servile work" (Hebrew "melekhet avodah" — work of labor/service) to "ordinary work." Sacred time requires cessation of common activity.

- v.7 — NET: "you must humble yourselves" / ASV: "ye shall afflict your souls" / WEB: "you shall afflict your souls" / KJV: "ye shall afflict your souls" — The Day of Atonement command. NET interprets "te'annu et-nafshotekem" (afflict your souls/selves) as "humble yourselves." The primary expression is fasting, but it encompasses all self-denial before God.

- v.12 — NET: "you must celebrate a festival to the Lord for seven days" / ASV: "ye shall keep a feast unto Jehovah seven days" / WEB: "you shall keep a feast to Yahweh seven days" — The Feast of Tabernacles (Sukkot). "Celebrate a festival" (Hebrew "chag") — the joyous harvest festival requiring 71 bulls over seven days (a staggering amount).

- v.35 — NET: "On the eighth day you are to have a holy assembly" / ASV: "On the eighth day ye shall have a solemn assembly" / WEB: "On the eighth day you shall have a solemn assembly" — Hebrew "atzeret" — a closing assembly. After the grandeur of Tabernacles, this intimate eighth day is restrained and personal. Jesus stood and cried out on this day (John 7:37).

- v.39 — NET: "These things you must present to the Lord" / ASV: "These ye shall offer unto Jehovah" / WEB: "You shall offer these to Yahweh" — The chapter ends with the divine name. All sacrifices are presented TO YHWH — worship is directed to a Person, not performed for a principle.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. The trumpet sounds (v.1). Before atonement comes awakening. The shofar
   blast says: "Wake up! Prepare! God is coming!" Are you spiritually
   awake, or do you need the trumpet's call to rouse you from complacency?

2. "Afflict your souls" (v.7). The Day of Atonement requires self-denial —
   you cannot feast while sin is being addressed. There is a time for
   sober self-examination, fasting, and repentance. When did you last
   truly "afflict your soul" before God?

3. After atonement, celebration (vv.7→12). The order matters: first the
   solemnity of Yom Kippur, then the joy of Tabernacles. You cannot
   celebrate rightly until sin is dealt with. But once it is — rejoice!
   The Christian life moves from the cross to resurrection joy.

4. Seventy bulls for the nations (vv.13-34). Israel's worship was never
   meant to be insular. As a priestly nation, they intercede for the
   world. The church likewise exists not for itself but for the nations.
   Is your worship self-focused or world-encompassing?

5. The eighth day — intimate communion (vv.35-38). After the grand public
   festival, one quiet day with God. After the crowds disperse, the
   individual remains. Corporate worship is essential, but so is personal,
   intimate time alone with God. Do you linger in His presence after the
   "festival" ends?

6. "If any man thirst" (John 7:37). Jesus chose the eighth day — the
   climax of the festival calendar — to offer Himself as the fulfillment
   of everything the sacrifices pointed toward. Every bull, ram, and lamb
   in this chapter whispers His name. He is the feast.
