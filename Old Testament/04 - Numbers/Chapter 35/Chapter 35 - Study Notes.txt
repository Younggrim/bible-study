Numbers - Chapter 35
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
God designates forty-eight Levitical cities and six cities of refuge — justice for the manslayer and protection from the blood avenger.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses. Numbers is the fourth book of the Pentateuch, written during
the wilderness period (approximately 1445-1405 BC). Hebrew title: "Bemidbar"
— "In the wilderness." The book records Israel's 38 years of wilderness
wandering between Sinai and the Promised Land — a journey that should have
taken 11 days (Deuteronomy 1:2) but took 40 years due to unbelief.

Historical Context: Chapter 35 addresses two related topics: (1) the 48
Levitical cities distributed throughout all the tribes (vv.1-8), and (2)
the six cities of refuge for those who kill unintentionally (vv.9-34). The
Levites receive no tribal territory — God is their inheritance (18:20) —
but they need places to live. Each tribe contributes cities proportional
to its size, totaling 48 cities with surrounding pasturelands. Six of these
48 are designated "cities of refuge" — three east of the Jordan, three
west — where a person who kills accidentally ("without intent") may flee
from the "avenger of blood" (the victim's nearest male relative, who has
the right/duty to execute the killer). The chapter carefully distinguishes
murder (intentional killing with hatred, enmity, or premeditation) from
manslaughter (accidental, unintentional killing). Murderers receive no
refuge — they must be executed. Manslayers are protected in the city of
refuge until the death of the high priest, after which they may return
home. This system balances justice (murder is punished), mercy (accidental
killers are protected), and holiness (bloodshed pollutes the land where
God dwells). The death of the high priest functions as a kind of atonement
— releasing the manslayer from his exile. This powerfully foreshadows
Christ: our High Priest whose death sets captives free.

Levitical Cities (vv.1-8): The Levites receive 48 cities with pasturelands
extending 1,000 cubits from the city wall (for livestock) and 2,000 cubits
measured from outside the city on each side (vv.4-5). The apparent
discrepancy between 1,000 and 2,000 cubits is likely: 1,000 cubits =
pastureland immediately around the wall; 2,000 cubits = the total measured
area including the city itself. Larger tribes give more cities; smaller
tribes give fewer (v.8) — proportional contribution.

Cities of Refuge Established (vv.9-15): Six cities total — three east of
Jordan, three in Canaan (v.14). They serve Israelites, strangers, and
sojourners alike (v.15) — justice is not limited to citizens. The purpose:
protect the unintentional killer from the "avenger of blood" until he can
stand trial before the congregation (v.12).

Murder Defined (vv.16-21): Murder is intentional killing characterized by:
(1) use of a deadly weapon — iron, stone, or wood capable of causing death
(vv.16-18); (2) hatred or enmity as motive (vv.20-21); (3) premeditation
— "lying in wait" (v.20). The penalty: death, executed by the avenger of
blood (v.19). No ransom or substitute is accepted for a murderer (v.31).

Manslaughter Defined (vv.22-24): Manslaughter is unintentional killing
characterized by: (1) "suddenly without enmity" (v.22); (2) without "lying
in wait" (v.22); (3) "without seeing him" (v.23); (4) the victim "was not
his enemy" and the killer "did not seek his harm" (v.23). The congregation
judges between the manslayer and the avenger (v.24).

The City of Refuge — Protection and Limitation (vv.25-28): The congregation
"rescues" the manslayer from the avenger and returns him to his city of
refuge (v.25). He must remain there until the death of the high priest
(v.25). If he leaves the city boundaries, the avenger may kill him without
guilt (vv.26-27). After the high priest's death, the manslayer may return
to his own land (v.28). The high priest's death functions as a release —
a kind of atonement that ends the exile.

Judicial Safeguards (vv.29-34): (1) Multiple witnesses required — no one
may be executed on one witness's testimony (v.30). (2) No ransom for
murderers — the death penalty cannot be bought off (v.31). (3) No ransom
for the manslayer's early release — he must stay until the high priest
dies (v.32). (4) The theological basis: "blood pollutes the land" (v.33)
and "no atonement can be made for the land... except by the blood of the
one who shed it" (v.33). (5) The ultimate reason: "I the LORD dwell in
the midst of the people of Israel" (v.34) — God's presence demands a
pure land.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.11 — "then you shall select cities to be cities of refuge for you, that
        the manslayer who kills any person without intent may flee there."
        (ESV) — God provides refuge for the unintentional sinner. Justice
        and mercy coexist in God's law.

v.25 — "he shall live in it until the death of the high priest who was
        anointed with the holy oil." (ESV) — The high priest's death
        releases the manslayer. A profound type of Christ: His death sets
        captives
        free.

v.30 — "One witness shall not testify against any person to cause him to
        die." — The requirement of multiple witnesses protects against
        false accusation. Justice requires evidence, not mere allegation.

v.33 — "Blood it defileth the land: and the land cannot be cleansed of
        the blood that is shed therein, but by the blood of him that shed
        it." — Unpunished bloodshed pollutes the land. Justice is not
        optional — it is necessary for the land's holiness.

v.34 — "You shall not defile the land in which you live, in the midst of
        which I dwell, for I the LORD dwell in the midst of the people of
        Israel." (ESV) — The ultimate reason for justice: God lives among
        His people. His
        presence demands purity.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/numbers-35/
  Guzik highlights the typological significance of the cities of refuge:
  Christ is our refuge from the avenger (divine justice). The manslayer
  must flee to the city — he must actively seek refuge, not passively wait.
  The high priest's death releasing the manslayer beautifully pictures
  Christ's death releasing us from condemnation.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "The city of refuge — what a picture of Christ! The manslayer flees for
  his life, the avenger hot behind him. He reaches the gate, enters, and
  is safe. Not because he is innocent — blood is on his hands — but because
  the city receives him. So the sinner flees to Christ: not innocent, but
  received. And when does his exile end? When the High Priest dies. Our
  High Priest has died — and risen! — and therefore our exile is ended
  forever. We are not merely protected in Christ; we are released by His
  death into full inheritance. The avenger has no claim on those for whom
  the High Priest has died."

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.2  — Hebrew "migrash" — an open area, common land, pastureland surrounding a city.
       KJV: "suburbs for the cities"
       ESV: "pasturelands around the cities"
       ASV: (not separately noted)
       NET: (not separately noted)
       WEB: (not separately noted)
       Analysis: KJV's "suburbs" is misleading to modern readers (suggesting residential neighborhoods). ESV's "pasturelands" accurately describes the purpose: grazing land for Levitical livestock.

v.6  — Hebrew "natenu" — to give/designate.
       KJV: (implied in context)
       ESV: (implied in context)
       ASV: "ye shall give six cities for refuge"
       NET: "you must select for yourselves cities of refuge"
       WEB: "you shall give six cities for refuge"
       Analysis: NET uses "select" emphasizing deliberate choice; ASV/WEB use "give." Six cities (3 east, 3 west of Jordan) provide accessible refuge throughout the land.

v.11 — Hebrew "makkeh nefesh bi-shgagah" — inadvertently, unintentionally, by mistake.
       KJV: "which killeth any person at unawares"
       ESV: "who kills any person without intent"
       ASV: "that the manslayer that killeth any person unwittingly may flee thither"
       NET: "you must select for yourselves towns to be cities of refuge for you, to which a person who kills someone unintentionally may flee"
       WEB: (not separately noted)
       Analysis: "Bi-shgagah" means inadvertently, unintentionally, by mistake. KJV's "at unawares" (unknowingly) and ESV's "without intent" both capture the meaning — the killing was accidental, not planned. NET's "person who kills someone unintentionally" is clearer than ASV's "manslayer that killeth... unwittingly." The distinction between murder (intentional) and manslaughter (unintentional) is foundational.

v.12 — Hebrew "go'el ha-dam" — literally "redeemer of blood."
       KJV: "the avenger"
       ESV: "the avenger"
       ASV: "for refuge from the avenger"
       NET: "a place of refuge from the avenger"
       WEB: "for refuge from the avenger"
       Analysis: The "go'el" is the nearest male kinsman with the duty to avenge, redeem, or restore. The same word is used for the kinsman-redeemer (Ruth 3:9) and for God as Redeemer (Isaiah 41:14). The avenger of blood is a redeemer — restoring justice for the slain. The city of refuge protects the unintentional killer from this legitimate but potentially unjust vengeance.

v.19 — Hebrew "go'el ha-dam" — avenger/redeemer of blood.
       KJV: "The revenger of blood"
       ESV: "The avenger of blood"
       ASV: (not separately noted)
       NET: (not separately noted)
       WEB: (not separately noted)
       Analysis: KJV uses "revenger"; ESV uses "avenger." Both translate the same Hebrew. "Avenger" is more standard in modern English; "revenger" is archaic but carries the same meaning.

v.25 — Hebrew (concerning the high priest anointed with holy oil).
       KJV: "the high priest, which was anointed with the holy oil"
       ESV: "the high priest who was anointed with the holy oil"
       ASV: "the congregation shall deliver the manslayer out of the hand of the avenger of blood"
       NET: "the community must deliver the accidental killer from the hand of the avenger of blood"
       WEB: "the congregation shall deliver the man slayer out of the hand of the avenger of blood"
       Analysis: The specification "anointed with the holy oil" emphasizes the high priest's sacred status. His death is not ordinary — it carries atoning significance because of his anointed office. The community (through its judges) has the responsibility to protect the innocent from vengeance. Justice requires discernment, not automatic retribution.

v.30 — Hebrew (concerning the requirement of multiple witnesses).
       KJV: (implied: "One witness shall not testify against any person to cause him to die")
       ESV: (implied in judicial safeguards)
       ASV: "one witness shall not testify against any person that he die"
       NET: "one witness is not enough"
       WEB: "one witness shall not testify against any person that he die"
       Analysis: A fundamental legal principle: capital punishment requires multiple witnesses. This prevents false accusation and ensures due process — cited in Deuteronomy 19:15 and Matthew 18:16.

v.33 — Hebrew "v'la-aretz lo y'khuppar la-dam asher shuppakh bah ki im b'dam shofkho" / "chaneph" — to pollute/profane.
       KJV: "the land cannot be cleansed of the blood that is shed therein, but by the blood of him that shed it"
       ESV: "no atonement can be made for the land for the blood that is shed in it, except by the blood of the one who shed it"
       ASV: "ye shall not pollute the land wherein ye are"
       NET: "you must not pollute the land where you are"
       WEB: "you shall not pollute the land in which you are"
       Analysis: ESV's "no atonement can be made" uses the technical term (kipper = atone/cover). KJV's "cannot be cleansed" interprets the result. The Hebrew is specifically about atonement — covering/purging the land's defilement. Unpunished bloodshed defiles the land itself (not just the people). Only the blood of the murderer can purify land defiled by murder.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Levitical Cities — 48 cities distributed throughout all twelve tribes,
               given to the Levites as dwelling places with surrounding
               pasturelands. Since Levites received no tribal territory,
               these cities ensured their presence throughout Israel —
               serving as teachers, judges, and worship leaders in every
               region.

Cities of Refuge — Six designated cities (three east, three west of
               Jordan) where unintentional killers could flee for
               protection from the avenger of blood. Named in Joshua 20:
               Kedesh, Shechem, Hebron (west); Bezer, Ramoth-gilead,
               Golan (east).

Avenger of Blood (Hebrew: "go'el ha-dam") — The nearest male kinsman of
               a murder/manslaughter victim, with the right and duty to
               pursue and execute the killer. The same root ("go'el")
               means "redeemer" — the avenger restores justice by
               "redeeming" the blood of the slain.

Manslayer — One who kills unintentionally (without hatred, enmity, or
               premeditation). Distinguished from a murderer. Protected
               in the city of refuge but exiled from home until the high
               priest's death.

High Priest's Death — The event that releases the manslayer from exile
               in the city of refuge. Functions as a kind of atonement —
               the death of the anointed one sets the captive free. A
               powerful type of Christ's atoning death.

Pastureland (Hebrew: "migrash") — Open land surrounding the Levitical
               cities, extending 1,000-2,000 cubits from the walls. Used
               for grazing livestock. Not to be sold or built upon —
               permanently attached to the Levitical cities.


PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. God provides refuge (v.11). The cities of refuge are God's provision
   for the imperfect — those who cause harm without intent. Christ is our
   city of refuge: we flee to Him not because we are innocent but because
   He receives the guilty. Have you fled to Him?

2. The manslayer must flee (v.11). Refuge is provided, but it must be
   sought. The city does not come to the manslayer — he must run to it.
   Salvation is freely offered, but you must actively receive it. Are you
   running toward Christ or standing still in danger?

3. The high priest's death sets captives free (v.25). The manslayer's
   exile ends when the high priest dies. Our High Priest has died — and
   risen! — and therefore our condemnation is ended forever (Romans 8:1).
   You are not merely protected; you are released.

4. Murder vs. manslaughter (vv.16-24). God distinguishes between intent
   and accident. He judges the heart, not merely the outcome. Two identical
   actions (a death) receive opposite verdicts based on motive. God sees
   your heart — not just your actions.

5. "Blood pollutes the land" (v.33). Injustice is not merely a social
   problem — it is a spiritual contamination. Unpunished evil defiles the
   very ground. God takes justice seriously because His presence demands
   purity. Do you take injustice as seriously as God does?

6. "I the LORD dwell among the children of Israel" (v.34). The ultimate
   reason for all these laws: God lives among His people. His presence is
   both the greatest privilege and the greatest responsibility. Everything
   in the community must be ordered to honor His holiness.
