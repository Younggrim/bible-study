Numbers - Chapter 5
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
God commands the removal of the unclean from camp, gives restitution laws, and prescribes the test for a wife suspected of unfaithfulness.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses. Numbers is the fourth book of the Pentateuch, written during
the wilderness period (approximately 1445-1405 BC). Hebrew title: "Bemidbar"
— "In the wilderness." The book records Israel's 38 years of wilderness
wandering between Sinai and the Promised Land — a journey that should have
taken 11 days (Deuteronomy 1:2) but took 40 years due to unbelief.

Historical Context: Chapter 5 addresses three issues of camp purity: (1)
the removal of unclean persons from the camp, (2) restitution for wrongs
committed, and (3) the test for suspected adultery (the "jealousy
offering" or "bitter water" test). The logic flows from Chapters 1-4: God
has organized His people around the Tabernacle; now He ensures the camp's
purity because "I dwell" in their midst (v.3). God's presence demands
holiness. Uncleanness, unconfessed sin, and hidden unfaithfulness all
threaten the community's relationship with God. The chapter moves from
external defilement (leprosy, discharge, corpse contact) to interpersonal
sin (trespass requiring restitution) to the most intimate betrayal
(marital unfaithfulness). The jealousy test (vv.11-31) is unique in
Scripture — it is a trial by ordeal where God Himself renders the verdict.
No human judge can determine hidden sin; God alone knows the heart. The
procedure places judgment entirely in God's hands.

Removal of the Unclean (vv.1-4): Three categories must be put outside the
camp: lepers, those with a discharge, and those defiled by contact with
the dead (v.2). Both male and female (v.3). The reason: "that they defile
not their camps, in the midst whereof I dwell" (v.3). God's presence
requires a clean camp. The people obey (v.4). This is not cruelty but
protection — God's holiness and human uncleanness cannot coexist in the
same space without consequence.

Restitution for Trespass (vv.5-10): When someone sins against another
("a trespass against the LORD," v.6), they must: (1) confess the sin (v.7),
(2) make full restitution plus one-fifth (20%) added (v.7), (3) give it to
the wronged party (v.7). If the wronged person has no kinsman to receive
it, restitution goes to the LORD through the priest, plus a ram of
atonement (v.8). Sin against man is sin against God. Forgiveness requires
both confession and restitution — making it right with God AND with the
person wronged.

The Jealousy Test — Suspected Adultery (vv.11-31): The scenario: a wife
is suspected of unfaithfulness, but there are no witnesses and she was not
caught (vv.12-13). The "spirit of jealousy" comes upon the husband —
whether she is guilty or innocent (v.14). The husband brings her to the
priest with a grain offering of barley (no oil, no frankincense — this is
not a joyful offering but a somber one) (v.15). The priest sets her before
the LORD (v.16). He takes holy water in an earthen vessel, adds dust from
the Tabernacle floor (v.17). He unbinds/uncovers her hair, places the
grain offering in her hands, and holds the "bitter water that causeth the
curse" (v.18). The oath: if innocent, be free; if guilty, may the LORD
make you a curse — "thy thigh to rot, and thy belly to swell" (vv.19-22).
The woman says "Amen, amen" (v.22). The curses are written and washed into
the water (v.23). She drinks (v.24). The grain offering is waved and
burned (vv.25-26). The result: if guilty, the curse takes effect — swelling
and wasting (v.27); if innocent, she is cleared and "shall conceive seed"
(v.28). The man is "guiltless from iniquity" for bringing the accusation
(v.31). This places judgment entirely in God's hands — no human verdict,
no mob justice, no false accusation without divine arbitration.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Wilderness of Sinai: Laws concerning camp purity — the unclean are put "without the camp" (v.3). The camp's holiness reflects God's presence at its center.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.3  — "That they defile not their camps, in the midst whereof I dwell."
        — God's presence demands purity. Defilement and God's dwelling
        cannot coexist.

v.6  — "When a man or woman shall commit any sin that men commit, to do a
        trespass against the LORD." — All sin against others is ultimately
        a trespass against God.

v.7  — "Then they shall confess their sin which they have done: and he
        shall recompense his trespass with the principal thereof, and add
        unto it the fifth part thereof." — Confession plus restitution.
        Words and actions together.

v.17 — "The priest shall take holy water in an earthen vessel; and of the
        dust that is in the floor of the tabernacle the priest shall take,
        and put it into the water." — Holy water + Tabernacle dust = God's
        presence in the judgment.

v.21 — "The LORD make you a curse and an oath among your people." (ESV) —
        God renders the verdict. No human judge needed for hidden sin.

v.28 — "And if the woman be not defiled, but be clean; then she shall be
        free, and shall conceive seed." — Vindication for the innocent.
        God clears the falsely accused.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/numbers-5/
  Guzik notes that the jealousy test actually protects the woman — it
  prevents a jealous husband from taking matters into his own hands. The
  matter is placed before God, and if she is innocent, she is publicly
  vindicated. He emphasizes that God alone judges hidden things.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "God dwells in the midst of His people, and therefore the camp must be
  clean. Sin cannot be hidden where God is present. He sees what no witness
  saw. He knows what no court can prove. The bitter water is God's way of
  saying: 'Nothing is hidden from Me.' Let us live as those who know that
  all things are naked and opened unto the eyes of Him with whom we have
  to do (Hebrews 4:13)."

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.2  — Hebrew "kol tsarua v'khol zav" — every leprous one and every one
       with a discharge.
       KJV: "every leper, and every one that hath an issue"
       ESV: "everyone who is leprous or has a discharge"
       ASV: "every leper"
       NET: "every diseased person"
       WEB: "every leper"
       Analysis: "Tsarua" = one afflicted with skin disease (broader than
       modern leprosy). "Zav" = one with a bodily discharge (Leviticus 15).
       NET avoids "leper" for the more accurate "diseased person." The
       unclean must be sent outside to preserve the camp's holiness.

v.2  — Hebrew "shalach" — to send away, expel.
       KJV: "put out of the camp"
       ESV: "put out of the camp"
       ASV: "put out of the camp"
       NET: "expel from the camp"
       WEB: "put out of the camp"
       Analysis: NET uses "expel" where others use "put out." Both convey
       the necessary separation of the unclean from God's dwelling place.

v.3  — Hebrew "asher ani shokhein b'tokham" — where I am dwelling in
       their midst.
       KJV: "in the midst whereof I dwell"
       ESV: "in the midst of which I dwell"
       ASV: "in the midst whereof I dwell"
       NET: "among which I live"
       WEB: "in the midst of which I dwell"
       Analysis: God's ongoing, present-tense dwelling among them is the
       reason for purity. The reason for camp purity is not social hygiene
       but God's presence. NET's "live" and KJV/ESV/ASV/WEB's "dwell" both
       translate "shokhein" — God tabernacles among them.

v.6  — Hebrew "lim'ol ma'al ba-YHWH" — to trespass unfaithfully against
       the LORD.
       KJV: "to do a trespass against the LORD"
       ESV: "breaking faith with the LORD"
       ASV: "so as to do a trespass against Jehovah, and that soul be guilty"
       NET: "and that person is found guilty"
       WEB: "to do a trespass against Yahweh"
       Analysis: Sin against a neighbor is trespass against God. ASV
       includes "against Jehovah"; WEB uses "Yahweh"; KJV/ESV use "the
       LORD." The divine name personalizes the offense. NET focuses on the
       result ("found guilty") rather than the direction of the offense.

v.7  — Hebrew "v'heshiv et ashamo b'rosho" — return his guilt in its
       head/capital.
       KJV: "recompense his trespass with the principal thereof"
       ESV: "make full restitution for his wrong"
       ASV: "make restitution for his guilt in full"
       NET: "make full reparation"
       WEB: "make restitution for his guilt in full"
       Analysis: KJV's "principal" means the full original amount. ESV/ASV/
       WEB's "full restitution" captures the same meaning. NET uses
       "reparation." All convey that the original amount must be returned
       completely before the one-fifth penalty is added.

v.12 — Hebrew "tisteh" — to turn aside, go astray, deviate.
       KJV: "go aside"
       ESV: "goes astray"
       ASV: "go aside"
       NET: "goes astray"
       WEB: "go astray"
       Analysis: A euphemism for sexual unfaithfulness. The word implies
       wandering from the proper path. KJV/ASV's "go aside" is more
       neutral; ESV/NET/WEB's "goes astray" carries stronger moral
       connotation.

v.15 — Hebrew "qemach s'orim" — barley flour; "minchat q'na'ot" —
       offering of jealousy/suspicion.
       KJV: "barley meal... an offering of jealousy, an offering of memorial"
       ESV: "barley flour... a grain offering of jealousy"
       ASV: "barley meal... a meal-offering of jealousy, a meal-offering of memorial"
       NET: "barley flour... a grain offering of suspicion, a grain offering for remembering"
       WEB: "barley meal... a meal offering of jealousy, a meal offering of memorial"
       Analysis: Barley is the grain of the poor and of mourning (not fine
       wheat flour). No oil or frankincense — stripped of all elements of
       joy. NET uses "suspicion" where others use "jealousy" (Hebrew
       "qena'ot"). The offering memorializes the accusation before God.

v.18 — Hebrew "u-fara et rosh ha-ishah" — to let loose/unbind the head.
       KJV: "uncover the woman's head"
       ESV: "unbind the hair of the woman's head"
       ASV: "let the hair of the woman's head go loose"
       NET: "uncover the woman's head"
       WEB: "let the woman's hair hang loose"
       Analysis: Loosening the hair signifies exposure, vulnerability, and
       the removal of normal dignity. She stands exposed before God's
       judgment. ESV/ASV/WEB specify the hair; KJV/NET say "uncover."

v.21 — Hebrew "y'rekhekh nofelet" — your thigh/hip falling.
       KJV: "the LORD make thee a curse and an oath among thy people... thy thigh to rot"
       ESV: "the LORD make you a curse... your thigh fall away"
       ASV: "Jehovah make thee a curse and an oath among thy people"
       NET: "May the Lord make you an attested curse among your people"
       WEB: "May Yahweh make you a curse and an oath among your people"
       Analysis: The exact medical meaning is debated — likely refers to
       reproductive organs wasting or barrenness. KJV's "rot" is more
       graphic; ESV's "fall away" is more literal. The "waters of bitterness"
       test is an appeal to divine omniscience — God alone knows the truth
       of hidden sin.

v.22 — Hebrew "bitnekh la-ts'bot" — your belly/womb to swell/distend.
       KJV: "thy belly to swell"
       ESV: "your womb swell"
       ASV: "thy body to swell"
       NET: "your abdomen to swell"
       WEB: "your body to swell"
       Analysis: The physical manifestation of the curse — visible evidence
       of God's judgment. ESV specifies "womb"; NET uses "abdomen"; KJV
       says "belly"; ASV/WEB use "body." All refer to the same physical
       sign.

v.23 — Hebrew "machah el mei ha-marim" — wipe/blot into the bitter waters.
       KJV: "blot them out with the bitter water"
       ESV: "wash them off into the water of bitterness"
       ASV: "blot them out into the water of bitterness"
       NET: "wash them off into the water of bitterness"
       WEB: "blot them into the water of bitterness"
       Analysis: The written curses are dissolved into the water — the
       woman literally drinks the judgment. KJV's "blot out" suggests
       erasure; ESV/NET's "wash off" emphasizes transfer into the water.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Camp Purity     — The requirement that God's dwelling place (the camp
               surrounding the Tabernacle) be free from ritual defilement.
               God's holiness cannot coexist with uncleanness. Those who
               are unclean must be temporarily removed until purified.

Trespass/Wrong (Hebrew: "ma'al") — Unfaithfulness, breach of trust. Used
               for both sins against God and sins against others. The same
               word describes marital unfaithfulness (v.12) and general
               wrongdoing (v.6) — all unfaithfulness is ultimately against
               God.

Restitution (Hebrew: "hashav") — Returning what was taken or damaged, plus
               20% (one-fifth). Repentance without restitution is
               incomplete. God requires both vertical reconciliation (with
               Him) and horizontal reconciliation (with the wronged party).

Jealousy Offering (Hebrew: "minchat q'na'ot") — The barley grain offering
               brought in cases of suspected adultery. No oil or
               frankincense — stripped of all elements of joy and worship.
               It "brings iniquity to remembrance" (v.15).

Bitter Water (Hebrew: "mei ha-marim") — Holy water mixed with Tabernacle
               dust and the dissolved ink of written curses. It becomes
               the instrument of God's judgment — harmless to the innocent,
               destructive to the guilty.

"Spirit of jealousy" (Hebrew: "ruach qin'ah") — The suspicion or passion
               that comes upon a husband. Whether the wife is guilty or
               innocent, the procedure provides resolution. It prevents
               both unpunished adultery and unjust accusation.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. God's presence demands purity (v.3). You cannot harbor uncleanness and
   enjoy God's presence simultaneously. "If we say that we have fellowship
   with him, and walk in darkness, we lie" (1 John 1:6). What defilement
   needs to be "put outside the camp" of your life?

2. Confession AND restitution (v.7). It is not enough to say "I'm sorry"
   to God. If you have wronged someone, make it right — and add to it.
   Grace does not eliminate the obligation to restore what was taken.
   Zacchaeus understood this (Luke 19:8).

3. Nothing is hidden from God (vv.12-27). The jealousy test exists because
   human courts cannot judge hidden sin. But God sees all. "Neither is
   there any creature that is not manifest in his sight: but all things
   are naked and opened unto the eyes of him with whom we have to do"
   (Hebrews 4:13). Live accordingly.

4. God protects the innocent (v.28). If the woman is clean, she is
   vindicated and blessed with fertility. God does not only judge the
   guilty — He clears the innocent. Trust Him with your reputation when
   falsely accused.

5. Sin affects the community (vv.1-4). Individual uncleanness defiles the
   whole camp. Your sin is never merely private — it affects the body.
   "A little leaven leaveneth the whole lump" (Galatians 5:9).
