Numbers - Chapter 6
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
God establishes the Nazirite vow of special consecration and gives the priestly blessing — 'The LORD bless you and keep you.'

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses. Numbers is the fourth book of the Pentateuch, written during
the wilderness period (approximately 1445-1405 BC). Hebrew title: "Bemidbar"
— "In the wilderness." The book records Israel's 38 years of wilderness
wandering between Sinai and the Promised Land — a journey that should have
taken 11 days (Deuteronomy 1:2) but took 40 years due to unbelief.

Historical Context: Chapter 6 contains two distinct but related sections:
the law of the Nazirite vow (vv.1-21) and the Aaronic Blessing (vv.22-27).
The Nazirite vow allows any Israelite — man or woman — to consecrate
themselves to God in a special way for a set period. This is remarkable:
in a system where only Levites serve the Tabernacle and only Aaron's sons
are priests, the Nazirite vow opens a path of extraordinary devotion to
any person from any tribe. It is voluntary, temporary (usually), and
intensely personal. The three restrictions — no wine/grapes, no razor, no
contact with the dead — mirror aspects of the high priest's consecration,
suggesting the Nazirite takes on a priest-like holiness. The chapter
concludes with the Aaronic Blessing (vv.24-26), one of the most beloved
passages in all Scripture — the words God commands the priests to speak
over Israel. It is God's own blessing, spoken through human lips, placing
His name upon His people.

The Nazirite Vow — Separation unto the LORD (vv.1-8): Either man or woman
may take this vow (v.2) — "to separate themselves unto the LORD." Three
requirements:
  (1) No wine, strong drink, grape juice, grapes (fresh or dried), or
      anything from the vine — "from the kernels even to the husk" (vv.3-4).
      Total abstinence from the fruit of the vine.
  (2) No razor on the head — hair grows freely as a visible sign of the
      vow (v.5). "He shall be holy."
  (3) No contact with any dead body — not even father, mother, brother, or
      sister (vv.6-7). "The consecration of his God is upon his head" (v.7).
"All the days of his separation he is holy unto the LORD" (v.8). The
Nazirite is set apart — visibly (long hair), behaviorally (no wine), and
relationally (no corpse contact). The vow is total.

Defilement and Restoration (vv.9-12): If someone dies suddenly near the
Nazirite, defiling his consecrated head (v.9): he shaves his head on the
seventh day (day of cleansing) (v.9). On the eighth day: two turtledoves
or pigeons — one for sin offering, one for burnt offering (vv.10-11).
Atonement is made (v.11). He re-consecrates and brings a lamb for a
trespass/guilt offering (v.12). Critical: "the days that were before shall
be lost" (v.12) — the previous time does not count. He starts over. The
vow cannot be partially fulfilled. Defilement resets the clock entirely.

Completion of the Vow (vv.13-21): When the days are fulfilled, the
Nazirite comes to the Tabernacle entrance (v.13). He brings: one male lamb
(burnt offering), one ewe lamb (sin offering), one ram (peace offering),
plus unleavened bread, grain offering, and drink offerings (vv.14-15). The
priest offers them (vv.16-17). The Nazirite shaves his consecrated head
and puts the hair in the fire under the peace offering (v.18) — the hair
itself becomes an offering to God. The priest places the boiled shoulder,
one cake, and one wafer in the Nazirite's hands, then waves them (vv.19-20).
"After that the Nazarite may drink wine" (v.20) — the vow is complete.
The separation ends with celebration.

The Aaronic Blessing (vv.22-27): God commands Aaron and his sons to bless
Israel with these words (v.23):
  "The LORD bless thee, and keep thee" (v.24) — provision and protection.
  "The LORD make his face shine upon thee, and be gracious unto thee" (v.25)
    — favor and grace.
  "The LORD lift up his countenance upon thee, and give thee peace" (v.26)
    — attention and shalom.
"And they shall put my name upon the children of Israel; and I will bless
them" (v.27). The priests speak the words, but GOD does the blessing. His
name is placed upon His people — they bear His identity. Three lines, three
blessings, the name "LORD" (Yahweh) three times — many see a foreshadowing
of the Trinity.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Wilderness of Sinai: The Nazirite vow and Aaronic blessing are given while Israel is still encamped at Sinai.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "If either a man or a woman makes a special vow, the vow of a
        Nazirite, to separate himself to the LORD." (ESV) — Voluntary,
        personal consecration. Open to all — man or woman.

v.5  — "he shall be holy. He shall let the locks of hair of his head grow
        long." (ESV) — The uncut hair is the visible sign of the vow.
        Holiness made visible.

v.8  — "All the days of his separation he is holy to the LORD." (ESV) —
        The Nazirite's entire life during the vow belongs to God.

v.24 — "The LORD bless you and keep you." (ESV) — God's provision and
        protection.

v.25 — "The LORD make his face to shine upon you and be gracious to you."
        (ESV) — God's favor and grace.

v.26 — "The LORD lift up his countenance upon you and give you peace."
        (ESV) — God's attention and shalom.

v.27 — "So shall they put my name upon the people of Israel, and I will
        bless them." (ESV) — God's name on His people. He Himself blesses.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/numbers-6/
  Guzik emphasizes that the Nazirite vow democratizes holiness — any
  Israelite can take on extraordinary consecration. He notes the three
  restrictions parallel the high priest's life, making the Nazirite a
  "lay priest." On the Aaronic Blessing, he highlights the threefold
  repetition of "the LORD" as a Trinitarian hint.

Charles Spurgeon:
  On the Aaronic Blessing: "Three times the sacred name is spoken. Three
  blessings are pronounced. The Father blesses and keeps. The Son makes
  His face to shine and is gracious. The Spirit lifts up His countenance
  and gives peace. Here is the whole Trinity engaged in blessing the
  people of God. And mark the last verse: 'I will bless them.' The priests
  speak the words, but God performs the deed. The blessing is not in the
  priest's lips but in God's hands."

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.2  — Hebrew "nazir" — from "nazar" (to separate, consecrate).
       KJV: "a Nazarite, to separate himself unto the LORD"
       ESV: "a Nazirite, to separate himself to the LORD"
       ASV: "the vow of a Nazirite, to separate himself unto Jehovah"
       NET: "the vow of a Nazirite, to separate himself to the Lord"
       WEB: "the vow of a Nazirite, to separate himself to Yahweh"
       Analysis: The word means "separated one" or "consecrated one." Both
       "Nazarite" (KJV) and "Nazirite" (ESV/ASV/NET/WEB) are acceptable
       English transliterations. The divine name marks who the separation
       is toward — it is separation TO God, not just FROM things. ASV uses
       "Jehovah"; WEB uses "Yahweh."

v.3  — Hebrew "yayin" (wine) and "shekhar" (strong drink/fermented
       beverage).
       KJV: "he shall separate himself from wine and strong drink"
       ESV: "he shall separate himself from wine and strong drink"
       ASV: "he shall separate himself from wine and strong drink"
       NET: "he must abstain from wine and strong drink"
       WEB: "he shall separate himself from wine and strong drink"
       Analysis: "Shekhar" = any fermented beverage other than grape wine
       (beer, mead, etc.). The Nazirite abstains from ALL intoxicants.
       NET's "abstain" is more natural English; ASV/WEB/KJV's "separate
       himself from" emphasizes the active choice of distancing.

v.4  — Hebrew "me-chartsannim v'ad zag" — from grape seeds to grape skins.
       KJV: "from the kernels even to the husk"
       ESV: "not even the seeds or the skins"
       ASV: "from the kernels even to the husk"
       NET: "from the seeds to the skins"
       WEB: "from the seeds even to the skins"
       Analysis: Total abstinence from everything vine-related. The
       extremity emphasizes completeness — not merely avoiding intoxication
       but avoiding any association with the vine.

v.5  — Hebrew "gadel pera s'ar rosho" — grow the loose/free hair of his
       head.
       KJV: "shall let the locks of the hair of his head grow"
       ESV: "shall let the locks of hair of his head grow long"
       ASV: "there shall no razor come upon his head"
       NET: "no razor may be used on his head"
       WEB: "no razor shall come upon his head"
       Analysis: "Pera" means unbound, free-growing hair. The hair is the
       visible badge of consecration — a public witness to one's dedication.
       KJV/ESV focus on the growth; ASV/NET/WEB focus on the prohibition
       against cutting. Samson's hair (Judges 16) illustrates the principle.

v.7  — Hebrew "nezer elohav al rosho" — the consecration/crown of his God
       is on his head.
       KJV: "the consecration of his God is upon his head"
       ESV: "his separation to God is on his head"
       ASV: "the separation of his God is upon his head"
       NET: "the sign of the consecration to his God is on his head"
       WEB: "the crown of his God is on his head"
       Analysis: "Nezer" is the same word used for the high priest's crown/
       diadem (Exodus 29:6). The Nazirite's hair IS his crown. WEB alone
       uses "crown" explicitly; NET adds "sign of"; KJV/ASV use
       "consecration/separation."

v.12 — Hebrew "v'ha-yamim ha-rishonim yiplu" — the former days shall fall
       (be lost/void).
       KJV: "the days that were before shall be lost"
       ESV: "the previous period shall be void"
       ASV: "the former days shall be void"
       NET: "the former days will not be counted"
       WEB: "the former days shall be void"
       Analysis: Complete reset. No partial credit. KJV's "lost" is vivid;
       ESV/ASV/WEB's "void" is more legal; NET's "not be counted" explains
       the practical effect. Failure requires starting over entirely.

v.18 — Hebrew: the hair offered in the fire of the peace offering.
       KJV: "put it in the fire which is under the sacrifice of the peace offerings"
       ESV: "put it on the fire that is under the sacrifice of the peace offering"
       ASV: "put it on the fire which is under the sacrifice of peace-offerings"
       NET: "put it on the fire where the peace offering is burning"
       WEB: "put it on the fire which is under the sacrifice of peace offerings"
       Analysis: The hair — the sign of the vow — is offered to God in the
       fire of the peace offering. The symbol of consecration becomes a
       sacrifice of thanksgiving. NET clarifies the burning is ongoing.

v.24 — Hebrew "y'varekh'kha YHWH v'yishm'rekha" — May YHWH bless you and
       guard you.
       KJV: "The LORD bless thee, and keep thee"
       ESV: "The LORD bless you and keep you"
       ASV: "Jehovah bless thee, and keep thee"
       NET: "The Lord bless you and protect you"
       WEB: "Yahweh bless you, and keep you"
       Analysis: NET uses "protect" where others use "keep" (Hebrew "shamar"
       — guard/watch over/preserve). The three-fold blessing contains the
       divine name three times — a hint of trinitarian blessing.

v.25 — Hebrew "ya'er YHWH panav eleikha vi-chuneka" — May YHWH cause His
       face to shine upon you and be gracious to you.
       KJV: "The LORD make his face shine upon thee, and be gracious unto thee"
       ESV: "the LORD make his face to shine upon you and be gracious to you"
       ASV: "Jehovah make his face to shine upon thee, and be gracious unto thee"
       NET: "The Lord make his face to shine upon you, and be gracious to you"
       WEB: "Yahweh make his face to shine on you, and be gracious to you"
       Analysis: God's shining face represents favor, pleasure, and
       acceptance (opposite of "hiding His face" in judgment). Hebrew
       "ya'er panav" — cause His face to illumine/radiate toward you.
       All translations agree closely.

v.26 — Hebrew "yissa YHWH panav eleikha v'yasem l'kha shalom" — May YHWH
       lift His face to you and give you peace.
       KJV: "The LORD lift up his countenance upon thee, and give thee peace"
       ESV: "the LORD lift up his countenance upon you and give you peace"
       ASV: "Jehovah lift up his countenance upon thee, and give thee peace"
       NET: "The Lord lift up his countenance upon you and give you peace"
       WEB: "Yahweh lift up his face on you, and give you peace"
       Analysis: Hebrew "shalom" — not just absence of conflict but
       wholeness, completeness, well-being, prosperity, and right-
       relationship. The blessing culminates in shalom — God's
       comprehensive well-being poured over His people.

v.27 — Hebrew "v'samu et sh'mi al b'nei yisrael" — they shall place my
       name upon the sons of Israel.
       KJV: "they shall put my name upon the children of Israel"
       ESV: "so shall they put my name upon the people of Israel"
       ASV: "so shall they put my name upon the children of Israel"
       NET: "so they will put my name on the Israelites"
       WEB: "so they shall put my name on the children of Israel"
       Analysis: God's name is placed on His people — they are identified
       with Him, they belong to Him, they bear His reputation. NET
       modernizes to "Israelites"; others use "children/people of Israel."

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Nazirite (Hebrew: "nazir") — A "separated one." A person who voluntarily
               takes a vow of special consecration to God for a set period.
               Three restrictions: no vine products, no haircut, no corpse
               contact. Open to men and women of any tribe. Famous
               Nazirites: Samson (lifelong), Samuel (lifelong), Paul
               (temporary — Acts 18:18).

Separation (Hebrew: "nezer" / "nazar") — The root means to separate or
               consecrate. "Nezer" also means crown or diadem — the
               Nazirite's uncut hair is his crown of consecration, parallel
               to the high priest's golden crown.

"From the kernels to the husk" — Total abstinence from everything
               grape-related. Not merely avoiding intoxication but avoiding
               any association with the vine. The vine represents earthly
               joy and celebration — the Nazirite sets aside earthly
               pleasure for divine devotion.

Aaronic Blessing — The three-line blessing God commands the priests to
               pronounce over Israel (vv.24-26). Still used in Jewish
               synagogues and Christian worship today. The oldest known
               biblical text — found on silver scrolls from the 7th
               century BC (Ketef Hinnom scrolls).

Shalom (Hebrew: "peace") — More than absence of conflict. Wholeness,
               completeness, well-being, harmony, prosperity. The final
               word of the Aaronic Blessing — the culmination of God's
               blessing is total shalom.

"Put my name upon" — To place God's name on someone is to identify them
               as His, to claim them, to give them His protection and
               reputation. Like a father's name on a child. God's people
               bear His name — they are called by Him and belong to Him.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Voluntary consecration (v.2). The Nazirite vow is not commanded — it is
   chosen. God honors those who voluntarily set themselves apart for Him.
   What are you willing to give up — not because you must, but because you
   want to draw closer to God?

2. Visible holiness (v.5). The uncut hair is a public sign. The Nazirite's
   devotion is not hidden. Are you willing to be visibly identified with
   God, even when it draws attention or questions?

3. Total commitment (v.4). Not just "no wine" but "nothing from the vine —
   from kernels to husk." God is not interested in partial devotion. Half-
   measures insult the vow. "Thou shalt love the Lord thy God with ALL thy
   heart" (Deuteronomy 6:5).

4. Failure resets the clock (v.12). If the Nazirite is defiled, previous
   days are lost. You cannot bank holiness. Past faithfulness does not
   cover present failure. But note: the vow can be restarted. Failure is
   not final — restoration is possible.

5. The Aaronic Blessing (vv.24-26). God wants to bless His people. He
   commands the priests to speak blessing — not curse, not warning, but
   BLESSING. And He Himself performs it: "I will bless them" (v.27). Rest
   in this: God's disposition toward His people is blessing. "The LORD
   bless thee, and keep thee."
