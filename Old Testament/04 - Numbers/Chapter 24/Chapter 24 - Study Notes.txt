Numbers - Chapter 24
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Balaam delivers his final oracles, blessing Israel a third and fourth time and prophesying a star and scepter rising from Jacob.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses. Numbers is the fourth book of the Pentateuch, written during
the wilderness period (approximately 1445-1405 BC). Hebrew title: "Bemidbar"
— "In the wilderness." The book records Israel's 38 years of wilderness
wandering between Sinai and the Promised Land — a journey that should have
taken 11 days (Deuteronomy 1:2) but took 40 years due to unbelief.

Historical Context: Chapter 24 contains Balaam's third and fourth oracles
— the climax of the Balaam narrative. In the third oracle (vv.3-9), Balaam
abandons his divination practices entirely and the Spirit of God comes upon
him directly. He pronounces Israel's beauty, abundance, and military
supremacy, culminating in a direct echo of the Abrahamic covenant: "Blessed
is he that blesseth thee, and cursed is he that curseth thee" (v.9). Balak
is furious and dismisses Balaam. But before departing, Balaam delivers a
fourth oracle (vv.15-24) — the most far-reaching and messianic of all:
"There shall come a Star out of Jacob, and a Sceptre shall rise out of
Israel" (v.17). This is one of the earliest and clearest messianic
prophecies in the Pentateuch, pointing to a future King who will arise from
Israel to exercise universal dominion. The Star and Sceptre imagery has
been understood as referring to David in the near term and to Christ in the
ultimate fulfillment. The chapter also contains brief oracles against
Amalek, the Kenites, Asshur, and Eber (vv.20-24) — a panoramic sweep of
future history. Balaam then departs — but not before later counseling Balak
to seduce Israel through Moabite women (Numbers 31:16), revealing that his
heart remained corrupt despite the true words God forced through his mouth.

Third Oracle: Israel's Beauty and Power (vv.1-9): A significant shift:
Balaam "went not, as at other times, to seek for enchantments" (v.1) — he
abandons divination and faces the wilderness directly. "The spirit of God
came upon him" (v.2) — genuine divine inspiration, not occult manipulation.
He introduces himself: "the man whose eyes are open... which heard the
words of God, which saw the vision of the Almighty" (vv.3-4). The oracle:
"How goodly are thy tents, O Jacob!" (v.5) — Israel's encampment is
beautiful, orderly, blessed. Imagery of abundance: valleys, gardens,
rivers, aloes, cedars (v.6). "His king shall be higher than Agag, and his
kingdom shall be exalted" (v.7) — a future king greater than the Amalekite
king (fulfilled in Saul's defeat of Agag, 1 Samuel 15). God's strength:
"the strength of an unicorn [wild ox]" (v.8). Israel as a lion: "who shall
stir him up?" (v.9). The climax echoes Genesis 12:3: "Blessed is he that
blesseth thee, and cursed is he that curseth thee" (v.9). Balak strikes
his hands in fury (v.10) and dismisses Balaam (v.11).

Fourth Oracle: The Star and Sceptre (vv.14-19): Before departing, Balaam
offers a final oracle — "what this people shall do to thy people in the
latter days" (v.14). His introduction is expanded: he now "knew the
knowledge of the most High" (v.16). The great prophecy: "I shall see him,
but not now: I shall behold him, but not nigh" (v.17) — a future figure,
distant in time. "There shall come a Star out of Jacob, and a Sceptre
shall rise out of Israel" (v.17). The Star = a royal figure, a light, a
guide. The Sceptre = a king, a ruler, sovereign authority. This figure
will "smite the corners of Moab, and destroy all the children of Sheth"
(v.17). "Edom shall be a possession" (v.18). "Out of Jacob shall come he
that shall have dominion" (v.19). Near fulfillment: David conquers Moab
and Edom (2 Samuel 8:2, 14). Ultimate fulfillment: Christ, the Star of
Bethlehem (Matthew 2:2), the King of kings who exercises universal dominion.

Oracles Against the Nations (vv.20-24): Amalek: "the first of the nations;
but his latter end shall be that he perish for ever" (v.20) — Amalek was
first to attack Israel (Exodus 17:8); they will be utterly destroyed
(fulfilled under Saul and David, completed under Hezekiah; 1 Chronicles
4:43). The Kenites: "Strong is thy dwellingplace" but "shall be wasted,
until Asshur shall carry thee away captive" (vv.21-22). A final oracle:
"ships shall come from the coast of Chittim [Kittim], and shall afflict
Asshur, and shall afflict Eber" (v.24) — a distant prophecy, possibly
referring to Greek/Roman power from the west overwhelming Mesopotamian
empires. "Alas, who shall live when God doeth this!" (v.23).

Balaam Departs (v.25): "Balaam rose up, and went and returned to his
place: and Balak also went his way" (v.25). Balaam leaves — but Numbers
31:16 reveals he later counseled Balak to use Moabite women to seduce
Israel into idolatry (the sin of Baal-peor, chapter 25). He could not
curse them with words, so he corrupted them with women. He dies by
Israel's sword (Numbers 31:8).

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- The top of Peor (continuing from ch. 23): Balaam looks down at Israel's camp on the plains of Moab arranged by tribes.
- The "Star out of Jacob" prophecy (v.17) points to a future ruler from Israel who will crush Moab and Edom.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "The spirit of God came upon him." — Even a corrupt prophet can
        be genuinely inspired. The Spirit's presence validates the message,
        not the messenger.

v.5  — "How lovely are your tents, O Jacob, your encampments, O Israel!"
        (ESV) — God sees beauty in His people's dwelling. What looks like a
        wilderness camp to human eyes is "lovely" to God.

v.9  — "Blessed are those who bless you, and cursed are those who curse
        you." (ESV) — The Abrahamic covenant (Genesis 12:3) confirmed
        through a pagan prophet. God's blessing on Israel is tied to how
        nations
        treat them.

v.17 — "There shall come a Star out of Jacob, and a Sceptre shall rise
        out of Israel." — The great messianic prophecy. A Star (light,
        guidance, royalty) and a Sceptre (kingship, authority, dominion)
        from Israel. Fulfilled in David; ultimately fulfilled in Christ.

v.19 — "Out of Jacob shall come he that shall have dominion." — Universal
        rule from Jacob's line. Christ: "All power is given unto me in
        heaven and in earth" (Matthew 28:18).

v.23 — "Alas, who shall live when God doeth this!" — The awesome,
        terrifying scope of God's future actions. When God moves in
        history, nations tremble.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/numbers-24/
  Guzik emphasizes verse 17 as one of the most important messianic
  prophecies in the Pentateuch. He notes that the Star and Sceptre imagery
  combines light (revelation, guidance) with authority (kingship, rule).
  The Magi in Matthew 2 may have known this prophecy — Balaam was from
  their region (Mesopotamia). He also notes the tragic irony: Balaam
  speaks the most beautiful prophecy of Christ but later leads Israel into
  the worst sin of the wilderness period.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "'A Star out of Jacob, and a Sceptre out of Israel.' Here is Christ in
  the wilderness, prophesied by a man who will not follow Him. The Star
  shines in the darkness — and the darkness comprehends it not. Balaam
  sees the Star from afar: 'I shall see him, but not now; I shall behold
  him, but not nigh.' He sees the future King but will not bow to Him. He
  prophesies the Sceptre but will not submit to its rule. How many see
  Christ clearly and perish! How many speak of Him eloquently and are lost!
  It is not seeing the Star that saves — it is following it. It is not
  knowing the Sceptre that redeems — it is bowing to it."

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1  — KJV: "to seek for enchantments" / ESV: "to look for omens" —
       Hebrew "liqrat n'chashim." "Nechashim" = enchantments, omens,
       divination signs. Previously Balaam used divination techniques;
       now he abandons them and receives direct revelation from God's
       Spirit. A significant shift in method, though not in heart.

v.3  — KJV: "the man whose eyes are open" / ESV: "the man whose eye is
       opened" — Hebrew "sh'tum ha-ayin." "Shetum" is debated: it may
       mean "opened" (seeing clearly) or "closed" (in trance, with inner
       eyes opened). Most translations favor "opened" — Balaam claims
       spiritual sight. The irony: the man whose donkey saw more than he
       did now claims open eyes.

v.4  — KJV: "falling into a trance, but having his eyes open" / ESV:
       "falling down with his eyes uncovered" — Hebrew "nofel u-g'luy
       einayim." Balaam falls prostrate (overwhelmed by the vision) but
       his eyes remain open — he sees the vision while conscious. This
       describes ecstatic prophetic experience.

v.7  — KJV: "his king shall be higher than Agag" / ESV: "his king shall
       be higher than Agag" — "Agag" was a title (like Pharaoh) for
       Amalekite kings. Israel's future king will surpass even the
       greatest of the Amalekite rulers. Fulfilled when Saul defeats Agag
       (1 Samuel 15).

v.17 — KJV: "a Star out of Jacob, and a Sceptre shall rise out of Israel"
       / ESV: "a star shall come out of Jacob, and a scepter shall rise
       out of Israel" — Hebrew "darakh kokhav mi-Ya'aqov v'qam shevet
       mi-Yisra'el." "Kokhav" = star. "Shevet" = sceptre, rod, tribe.
       The Star and Sceptre are parallel — both refer to the same royal
       figure. "Darakh" = to tread, march forth — the Star actively comes.

v.17 — KJV: "shall smite the corners of Moab" / ESV: "shall crush the
       forehead of Moab" — Hebrew "u-machatz pa'atei Mo'av." "Pa'atei"
       can mean "corners/borders" or "temples/forehead" (sides of the
       head). KJV reads geographically (borders of Moab); ESV reads
       physically (crushing the skull). Both convey total defeat.

v.17 — KJV: "destroy all the children of Sheth" / ESV: "break down all
       the sons of Sheth" — Hebrew "v'qarqar kol b'nei Sheth." "Sheth"
       may refer to Seth (all humanity as descendants of Seth) or to a
       Moabite people/place. If "all the sons of Seth" = all humanity,
       this is a prophecy of universal dominion — the Messiah rules all.

v.24 — KJV: "ships... from the coast of Chittim" / ESV: "ships... from
       Kittim" — "Kittim" originally referred to Cyprus, then broadly to
       the western Mediterranean coastlands (Greece, Rome). This prophecy
       looks far into the future: western naval powers will afflict
       Assyria and the descendants of Eber (Hebrews/Semites). Possibly
       fulfilled by Alexander the Great or Rome.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Star out of Jacob — A messianic title. Stars represent royalty, glory,
               and divine guidance. A king will arise from Jacob's line
               who shines with divine authority. Near fulfillment: David.
               Ultimate fulfillment: Christ — "the bright and morning
               star" (Revelation 22:16). The Magi followed a literal star
               to find the newborn King (Matthew 2:2).

Sceptre out of Israel — A symbol of royal authority and sovereign rule.
               The sceptre represents the right to govern. Combined with
               Genesis 49:10 ("the sceptre shall not depart from Judah"),
               this points to an eternal King from Israel who exercises
               universal dominion. Fulfilled in Christ's kingship.

"The Latter Days" (v.14) — Hebrew "b'acharit ha-yamim" — in the end of
               days, in the future time. A prophetic formula pointing to
               the distant future — beyond the immediate historical
               context. The Star/Sceptre prophecy reaches to the coming
               of Messiah and beyond.

Agag — A royal title for Amalekite kings (like "Pharaoh" for Egyptian
               kings). Israel's king will be "higher than Agag" — greater
               than the greatest pagan ruler. Fulfilled when Saul defeats
               Agag (1 Samuel 15), and ultimately in Christ who is "King
               of kings" (Revelation 19:16).

Kittim / Chittim — Originally Cyprus; later used broadly for western
               Mediterranean peoples (Greeks, Romans). Ships from Kittim
               represent western military power. In Daniel 11:30, "ships
               of Kittim" refers to Roman intervention. Balaam's prophecy
               sweeps across centuries of future history.

Children of Sheth / Sons of Sheth — Debated: either a Moabite clan/place,
               or all humanity (as descendants of Seth, Adam's son). If
               the latter, the Sceptre's dominion is universal — He rules
               all the sons of men. This supports the messianic
               interpretation pointing to Christ's universal kingship.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.1 — NET: "he did not go as at the other times to seek for omens" / ASV: "he went not, as at the other times, to meet with enchantments" / WEB: "he didn't go, as at the other times, to meet with enchantments" / KJV: "he went not, as at other times, to seek for enchantments" — NET uses "omens" where ASV/WEB/KJV use "enchantments" (Hebrew "nechashim"). Balaam abandons divination techniques — he now simply opens himself to God's Spirit.

- v.2 — NET: "the Spirit of God came upon him" / ASV: "the Spirit of God came upon him" / WEB: "God's Spirit came on him" — All agree that the Spirit comes on Balaam. The Spirit empowers even a pagan diviner for God's purposes — God's sovereignty over all human instruments.

- v.3 — NET: "the oracle of Balaam son of Beor; the oracle of the man whose eyes are open" / ASV: "the oracle of Balaam the son of Beor, and the oracle of the man whose eye was opened" / WEB: "the oracle of Balaam the son of Beor, the oracle of the man whose eye was opened" — Hebrew "shethum ha'ayin" — whose eye is opened/uncovered. NET pluralizes "eyes"; ASV/WEB keep singular. The seer's spiritual perception is activated by God.

- v.17 — NET: "I see him, but not now; I behold him, but not close" / ASV: "I see him, but not now; I behold him, but not nigh" / WEB: "I see him, but not now. I behold him, but not near" — The messianic prophecy looks into the distant future. NET uses "close"; ASV "nigh"; WEB "near" — all translating Hebrew "qarov." The Star and Sceptre are future realities.

- v.17 — NET: "A star will march forth out of Jacob, and a scepter will rise out of Israel" / ASV: "There shall come forth a star out of Jacob, and a sceptre shall rise out of Israel" / WEB: "A star will come out of Jacob. A scepter will rise out of Israel" — The most significant messianic prophecy in Numbers. NET's "march forth" (Hebrew "darak" — to tread/march) is more dynamic than ASV/WEB's "come forth."

- v.23 — NET: "Who can survive when God does this?" / ASV: "Alas, who shall live when God doeth this?" / WEB: "Alas, who shall live when God does this?" — Balaam's final oracle ends in awe — the power of God's future actions is overwhelming. NET's "Who can survive" captures the rhetorical force.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "A Star out of Jacob, and a Sceptre out of Israel" (v.17). The Messiah
   is both Star (light, beauty, guidance) and Sceptre (authority, rule,
   judgment). Christ is not merely a gentle guide — He is a sovereign King.
   Have you submitted to His sceptre, or only admired His star?

2. "I shall see him, but not now" (v.17). Balaam sees the Messiah from
   afar — across centuries — but will never know Him personally. You can
   see Christ clearly in prophecy and theology and still miss Him
   relationally. Head knowledge without heart surrender is Balaam's
   tragedy.

3. "Blessed is he that blesseth thee" (v.9). How you treat God's people
   determines how God treats you. This principle runs from Genesis 12:3
   through all of history. Bless what God blesses; do not curse what God
   has declared blessed.

4. Balaam abandons enchantments (v.1). When he stops manipulating and
   simply faces God, the Spirit comes. Sometimes our spiritual techniques
   and formulas are obstacles to genuine encounter. Stop performing and
   simply turn your face toward God.

5. "How goodly are thy tents, O Jacob!" (v.5). God sees beauty in His
   people that they cannot see in themselves. Israel is a wilderness camp
   of complaining, rebellious people — and God calls them "goodly." He
   sees you through covenant eyes, not through the lens of your failures.

6. Balaam's tragedy (v.25). He speaks the most beautiful prophecy of
   Christ — then goes home and counsels Israel's destruction through
   sexual sin (31:16). A man can have the clearest theology and the most
   corrupt heart. Truth in the mouth without truth in the life is the
   most dangerous spiritual condition. Balaam dies by the sword of the
   very people he blessed (31:8).
