Numbers - Chapter 31
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Israel takes vengeance on Midian for the Baal-Peor seduction; the spoil is divided and purified, and not one Israelite soldier is lost.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses. Numbers is the fourth book of the Pentateuch, written during
the wilderness period (approximately 1445-1405 BC). Hebrew title: "Bemidbar"
— "In the wilderness." The book records Israel's 38 years of wilderness
wandering between Sinai and the Promised Land — a journey that should have
taken 11 days (Deuteronomy 1:2) but took 40 years due to unbelief.

Historical Context: Chapter 31 records the war against Midian — the
fulfillment of God's command in 25:16-18 to "harass the Midianites and
strike them down" for their role in the Baal-Peor seduction. This is
explicitly called "the LORD's vengeance on Midian" (v.3) — divine judgment
executed through human agency. God tells Moses this will be his final act
before death: "Afterward you shall be gathered to your people" (v.2).
Twelve thousand Israelites (1,000 per tribe) go to war under Phinehas
(the zealous priest of chapter 25), carrying the holy vessels and trumpets.
They kill every Midianite male, including the five kings and Balaam himself
(v.8). The chapter also records Moses' anger when the army spares the
Midianite women — the very women who "caused the children of Israel...
to commit trespass against the LORD in the matter of Peor" (v.16). The
spoils are divided systematically: half to the warriors, half to the
congregation, with portions for the LORD (via the priests) and the Levites.
Remarkably, not a single Israelite soldier dies (v.49) — a miraculous
testimony to God's protection. The officers bring a voluntary gold offering
of 16,750 shekels as thanksgiving.

The Command (vv.1-4): God's final command to Moses: "Avenge the children
of Israel of the Midianites" (KJV). This is vengeance for Baal-Peor —
the Midianites used their women as weapons of spiritual warfare (25:1-18;
31:16). One thousand men from each tribe — 12,000 total. A relatively
small force, emphasizing that victory depends on God, not numbers.

The Campaign (vv.5-12): Moses sends them with Phinehas (not Joshua — this
is priestly war, holy war), carrying "the vessels of the sanctuary and the
trumpets" (v.6). They kill every male, including the five kings of Midian:
Evi, Rekem, Zur, Hur, and Reba (v.8). Zur is the father of Cozbi (25:15)
— the woman Phinehas killed. They also kill Balaam son of Beor "with the
sword" (v.8) — the prophet-for-hire meets his end among those whose
destruction he engineered. They burn all cities and camps, take captives,
livestock, and plunder.

Moses' Anger (vv.13-18): Moses is furious that the officers spared the
women — "these caused the children of Israel, through the counsel of
Balaam, to commit trespass against the LORD in the matter of Peor" (v.16).
This verse explicitly identifies Balaam as the architect of the Baal-Peor
strategy. Moses commands: kill every male child and every woman who has
"known man" — spare only the young virgins (v.18). The severity reflects
the severity of the threat: these women were weapons of spiritual
destruction.

Purification (vv.19-24): Warriors who killed or touched the dead must
purify themselves outside the camp for seven days (the standard corpse-
contamination period). All plunder must be purified: metals through fire
and the "water of separation" (the red heifer water of chapter 19);
non-metals through water alone. War defiles even the victorious — contact
with death requires cleansing before returning to the holy camp.

Division of Spoils (vv.25-47): God prescribes the division:
  — Half to the 12,000 warriors; half to the congregation
  — From the warriors' half: 1/500 to the LORD (given to priests)
  — From the congregation's half: 1/50 to the Levites
The spoils: 675,000 sheep, 72,000 cattle, 61,000 donkeys, 32,000 persons
(virgin women). The LORD's tribute from the warriors: 675 sheep, 72 cattle,
61 donkeys, 32 persons. The Levites' portion from the congregation: larger
(1/50 vs. 1/500) because they serve the tabernacle full-time.

The Miraculous Preservation (vv.48-54): The officers report: "there is not
a man missing from us" (v.49). Not one Israelite soldier died in the
entire campaign — a stunning miracle. In gratitude, the officers bring a
voluntary offering of gold jewelry: 16,750 shekels (approximately 420
pounds of gold). This is "to make atonement for our souls before the LORD"
(v.50) — not for sin, but thanksgiving for preservation. The gold is
placed in the tabernacle "as a memorial for the people of Israel" (v.54).

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Midian: Israel wages war against the Midianites who seduced them at Baal-peor (ch. 25). The war is fought in the plains and highlands east of the Jordan/Dead Sea.
- The kings of Midian slain include Evi, Rekem, Zur, Hur, and Reba (v.8). Balaam is also killed (v.8).

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "Avenge the children of Israel of the Midianites: afterward shalt
        thou be gathered unto thy people." — Moses' final military act.
        God's vengeance is executed through human agency, and then Moses
        can rest.

v.8  — "Balaam also the son of Beor they slew with the sword." — The
        prophet who could not curse Israel dies among Israel's enemies.
        He chose Midian's side and shares Midian's fate.

v.16 — "Behold, these caused the children of Israel, through the counsel
        of Balaam, to commit trespass against the LORD in the matter of
        Peor." — The explicit connection: Balaam advised the seduction
        strategy. What his oracles could not accomplish, his counsel did.

v.49 — "There lacketh not one man of us." — Zero casualties in a war
        against an entire nation. When God fights for His people, the
        victory is total and the cost is nothing.

v.50 — "We have therefore brought an oblation for the LORD... to make an
        atonement for our souls before the LORD." — Gratitude expressed
        in sacrifice. They survived miraculously and respond with
        extravagant generosity.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/numbers-31/
  Guzik addresses the difficulty of this chapter for modern readers: the
  severity of the judgment reflects the severity of the spiritual threat.
  Midian used women as weapons of mass spiritual destruction — the response
  is proportional to the danger. He notes that Balaam's death among the
  Midianites confirms his true allegiance: he died with the enemies of God.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "Balaam died by the sword — the prophet who blessed Israel with his lips
  but cursed them with his counsel. He could not be hired to speak against
  God's people, so he taught their enemies how to seduce them. Many a man
  who will not openly oppose the gospel will quietly undermine it. Balaam
  is the patron saint of those who serve God with their mouths and Mammon
  with their hearts. His end is instructive: he perished among the very
  people whose gold he coveted. The wages of unrighteousness purchased
  only a grave among the ungodly."

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.2  — Hebrew "n'qom niqmat b'nei Yisrael me-et ha-Midyanim" — to avenge, take vengeance.
       KJV: "Avenge the children of Israel of the Midianites"
       ESV: "Avenge the people of Israel on the Midianites"
       ASV: "Avenge the children of Israel of the Midianites"
       NET: "Execute vengeance for the Israelites on the Midianites"
       WEB: "Avenge the children of Israel on the Midianites"
       Analysis: NET uses "execute vengeance" (Hebrew "neqam") — this is divine justice administered through human agency. The vengeance belongs to God (v.3: "the Lord's vengeance"); Israel merely executes it. Both KJV and ESV are accurate; the vengeance is Israel's (for the 24,000 who died) but executed as God's command.

v.3  — Hebrew "latet niqmat YHWH b'Midyan" — the LORD's vengeance.
       KJV: "avenge the LORD of Midian"
       ESV: "execute the LORD's vengeance on Midian"
       ASV: "Jehovah's vengeance on Midian"
       NET: "the Lord's vengeance on Midian"
       WEB: "Yahweh's vengeance on Midian"
       Analysis: Note the shift: v.2 says "avenge Israel"; v.3 says "the LORD's vengeance." The offense against Israel IS an offense against God. ESV's "execute the LORD's vengeance" is clearer than KJV's compressed "avenge the LORD of Midian." The divine name in ASV/NET/WEB emphasizes this war is YHWH's, not Israel's. Midian seduced Israel (ch.25) and must bear the consequences.

v.6  — Hebrew "k'lei ha-qodesh" — vessels, instruments, articles of the sanctuary.
       KJV: "the holy instruments"
       ESV: "the vessels of the sanctuary"
       ASV: "the vessels of the sanctuary and the trumpets for the alarm"
       NET: "the holy articles and the signal trumpets"
       WEB: "the vessels of the sanctuary and the trumpets for the alarm"
       Analysis: "K'lei" means vessels, instruments, articles. These are sacred objects carried into battle — possibly the ark or elements of it. The trumpets are mentioned separately. NET's "signal trumpets" for "chatsotsrot ha-teru'ah" — the trumpets of chapter 10 now sound for warfare. Spiritual authority leads military action. This is holy war — God's presence goes with the army.

v.8  — Hebrew "al challaleihem" — with/beside the rest of the slain.
       KJV: "beside the rest of them that were slain"
       ESV: "with the rest of their slain"
       ASV: "Balaam also the son of Beor they slew with the sword"
       NET: "and Balaam son of Beor they also killed with the sword"
       WEB: "Balaam also the son of Beor they killed with the sword"
       Analysis: The five kings and Balaam are killed "in addition to" the general male population already slain. They are not special targets found later but killed during the general battle. Balaam's death is recorded almost as an aside — the man who blessed Israel dies with Israel's enemies. His double-mindedness led to his destruction (cf. James 1:8).

v.17 — Hebrew "harog kol zakar ba-taf" — kill every male among the children.
       KJV: "kill every male among the little ones"
       ESV: "kill every male among the little ones"
       ASV: "kill every male among the little ones"
       NET: "kill every male among the little ones"
       WEB: "kill every male among the little ones"
       Analysis: Identical across all translations. The severity: male children would grow to avenge their fathers and perpetuate Midianite enmity. This is total war as divine judgment — not genocide for territorial gain but execution of God's sentence.

v.23 — Hebrew "mei niddah" — water of impurity/separation.
       KJV: "the water of separation"
       ESV: "the water for impurity"
       ASV: "the water of separation"
       NET: "the water of purification"
       WEB: "the water for impurity"
       Analysis: "Niddah" means impurity, separation, menstrual uncleanness. This is the water mixed with the ashes of the red heifer (Numbers 19) — used for purification from death-contamination. ESV's "water for impurity" describes its purpose; KJV's "water of separation" describes its nature.

v.49 — Hebrew "lo nifqad mimmenu ish" — not one man is missing from us.
       KJV: "there lacketh not one man of us"
       ESV: "there is not one man missing from us"
       ASV: "there lacketh not one man of us"
       NET: "not one is missing"
       WEB: "there is not one man of us missing"
       Analysis: Zero casualties in battle — a miracle. NET's concise "not one is missing" captures the wonder. The officers bring a freewill offering in gratitude for this supernatural protection. When God fights for His people, the victory is total and the cost is nothing.

v.50 — Hebrew "nafshoteinu" — our souls/ourselves.
       KJV: "jewels of gold, chains, and bracelets, rings, earrings, and tablets... to make atonement for our souls before the LORD"
       ESV: "articles of gold, armlets and bracelets, signet rings, earrings, and beads... to make atonement for our souls before the LORD"
       ASV: "to make atonement for our souls before Jehovah"
       NET: "to make atonement for ourselves before the Lord"
       WEB: "to make atonement for our souls before Yahweh"
       Analysis: KJV's "tablets" (Hebrew "kumaz") is uncertain — possibly a gold bead, ornament, or amulet. ESV's "beads" is a reasonable guess. The exact identification of some ancient jewelry terms is uncertain. Even in victory, atonement is needed. Success does not remove the need for humility before God. NET uses "ourselves" where ASV/WEB use "souls" (Hebrew "nafshoteinu").

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Midianites — Descendants of Abraham through Keturah (Genesis 25:1-2).
               They lived east of the Jordan and in the Sinai region.
               Moses' father-in-law Jethro was a Midianite priest (Exodus
               2:16-21). Despite this earlier positive connection, the
               Midianites allied with Moab to destroy Israel through
               seduction at Baal-Peor.

Balaam — Son of Beor, a prophet/diviner from Pethor in Mesopotamia. Hired
               by Balak to curse Israel (chapters 22-24), he was compelled
               by God to bless instead. But he then counseled Balak to use
               Midianite women to seduce Israel into idolatry (31:16;
               Revelation 2:14). He dies by the sword among the Midianites.

Holy Instruments / Vessels of the Sanctuary — Sacred objects carried into
               battle, signifying God's presence with the army. Possibly
               included elements of the ark or other tabernacle articles.
               Their presence makes this "holy war" — God Himself fights.

Water of Separation / Water for Impurity — The purification water made
               from the ashes of the red heifer (Numbers 19). Used to
               cleanse anyone contaminated by contact with death. Required
               for warriors returning from battle.

Heave Offering (Hebrew: "terumah") — A contribution lifted up and
               presented to God. The warriors' 1/500 tribute is called a
               "heave offering" — it is lifted from the spoils and given
               to the priests as God's portion.

Memorial (Hebrew: "zikkaron") — A physical reminder placed before God.
               The gold offering becomes a "memorial for the children of
               Israel before the LORD" (v.54) — a permanent testimony of
               God's miraculous protection.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Balaam dies with his allies (v.8). You will share the fate of those
   you align with. Balaam blessed Israel with his mouth but allied with
   Midian in his heart — and died among them. Where is your true allegiance?
   Not what you say, but where you stand when the sword falls.

2. "Through the counsel of Balaam" (v.16). The most dangerous enemies are
   not those who attack openly but those who advise compromise. Who is
   counseling you toward "small" compromises that lead to spiritual
   destruction?

3. Not one man missing (v.49). When God sends you into battle, He is
   responsible for the outcome. Obedience to God's command carries God's
   protection. The 12,000 faced an entire nation and lost nothing. What
   battle is God calling you to that you're avoiding out of fear?

4. Gratitude expressed in gold (v.50). The officers' response to miraculous
   preservation is extravagant generosity — 420 pounds of gold. When God
   delivers you, do you respond with proportional gratitude? Or do you
   pocket the blessing and forget the Deliverer?

5. Purification after victory (vv.19-24). Even righteous warfare defiles.
   Contact with death — even in obedience — requires cleansing. Victory
   does not exempt you from the need for purification. After every
   spiritual battle, return to God for cleansing.

6. The severity of judgment (vv.17-18). This chapter is difficult — but
   the severity reflects the severity of the threat. The Midianite women
   were weapons that killed 24,000 Israelites. God's judgment is always
   proportional to the danger. Do you take spiritual threats as seriously
   as God does?
