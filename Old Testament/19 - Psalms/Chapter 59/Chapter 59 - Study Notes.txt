Psalms - Chapter 59
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A lament — David prays for deliverance when Saul sends men to watch his house; enemies prowl like howling dogs, but God is David's fortress and strength; he will sing of God's power and steadfast love in the morning.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Individual Lament Psalm
Attributed Author: David
Key Themes: Deliverance from enemies, innocence under persecution,
God as fortress and defense, morning praise, God's sovereign
laughter over the wicked

Historical Context: The superscription connects this psalm to when
Saul sent men to watch David's house in order to kill him (1 Samuel
19:11-17). Michal, David's wife, warned him and helped him escape
through a window. The "dogs" prowling the city (vv.6,14) likely
represent Saul's agents circling David's house at night. This
Miktam is set to "Do Not Destroy" (Al Tashheth). David protests
his innocence — he has committed no transgression against Saul —
yet is hunted like a criminal. The psalm uses a refrain structure
with the dog imagery (vv.6,14) creating a frame around the
central section.

Structure:
- Cry for Deliverance (vv.1-5)
- The Prowling Enemies (vv.6-7)
- God's Sovereign Derision (vv.8-10)
- Prayer for Just Judgment (vv.11-13)
- The Prowling Enemies Repeated (vv.14-15)
- Morning Song of Praise (vv.16-17)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.8  — "But thou, O LORD, shalt laugh at them; thou shalt have
        all the heathen in derision." (KJV) — God's response to
        human threats is laughter (cf. Psalm 2:4). The enemies
        think they are powerful; God sees them as absurd. His
        sovereignty makes all opposition laughable.

v.9  — "Because of his strength will I wait upon thee: for God is
        my defence." (KJV) / ESV: "O my Strength, I will watch
        for you, for you, O God, are my fortress." — God is
        David's strength AND his fortress. He does not merely
        GIVE strength; He IS strength. Waiting and watching
        express active trust.

v.16 — "But I will sing of thy power; yea, I will sing aloud of
        thy mercy in the morning: for thou hast been my defence
        and refuge in the day of my trouble." (KJV) — The
        contrast: enemies howl like dogs at evening (v.6); David
        sings praise in the morning. Night brings danger; morning
        brings deliverance and song.

v.17 — "Unto thee, O my strength, will I sing: for God is my
        defence, and the God of my mercy." (KJV) — The psalm's
        closing declaration matches v.9. The structure reinforces
        the message: God as strength and fortress frames
        everything.

------------------------------------

         assassins, the historical setting.
v.6   — Philippians 3:2 — "Beware of dogs." Dogs as a biblical
         image for the unclean and hostile.

         Same divine laughter at human opposition.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.9 — KJV: "Because of his strength will I wait upon thee" / ESV:
      "O my Strength, I will watch for you" — The Hebrew is
      ambiguous: "his strength" (the enemy's) or "my Strength"
      (addressed to God). ESV reads it as a divine title; KJV reads
      it as the enemy's strength prompting David to wait on God.
      ESV is more likely correct given the parallel in v.17.

v.10 — KJV: "The God of my mercy shall prevent me" / ESV: "My God
       in his steadfast love will meet me" — "Prevent" in 1611
       English meant "go before" (from Latin praevenire). ESV
       clarifies: God goes ahead of David to meet him. God's mercy
       anticipates our need.

v.15 — KJV: "grudge if they be not satisfied" / ESV: "growl if
       they do not get their fill" — The image of dogs scavenging
       for food and growling in frustration. The enemies are
       reduced from threatening hunters to pathetic scavengers.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.3 — ASV: "O Jehovah" / WEB: "Yahweh" vs. KJV: "O LORD" — The covenant name is made explicit, emphasizing David's personal relationship with God as he protests his innocence. NET uses "O Lord."

- v.5 — ASV: "O Jehovah God of hosts" / WEB: "Yahweh God of Armies" / NET: "O Lord God of Heaven's Armies" — Three renderings of "Yahweh Elohey Tseba'ot." WEB/NET interpret "hosts" as "Armies," making the military metaphor explicit. ASV preserves the traditional "hosts."

- v.9 — NET: "You are my source of strength. I will wait for you. For God is my refuge" vs. KJV: "Because of his strength will I wait upon thee: for God is my defence" — NET resolves the ambiguity by addressing God directly as "my source of strength." ASV: "my high tower"; WEB: "my high tower" — both use "high tower" for KJV's "defence."

- v.10 — ASV: "My God with his lovingkindness will meet me" / WEB: "My God will go before me with his loving kindness" / NET: "The God who loves me will help me" — The "chesed" spectrum again: ASV "lovingkindness," WEB "loving kindness," NET "who loves me" (completely restructured). KJV: "The God of my mercy shall prevent me."

- v.16 — ASV: "I will sing aloud of thy lovingkindness in the morning" / WEB: "I will sing aloud of your loving kindness in the morning" / NET: "I will praise your loyal love in the morning" — KJV: "thy mercy in the morning." Four different renderings of "chesed" in one verse.

- v.11 — NET: "Use your power to make them homeless vagabonds and then bring them down" — NET dramatically interprets the scattering as creating "homeless vagabonds," making the punishment social rather than merely geographic. KJV/ASV/WEB: "scatter them by thy/your power."

- v.17 — ASV: "the God of my mercy" / WEB: "the God of my mercy" / NET: "the God who loves me" — NET transforms the Hebrew construct "God of my chesed" from a title into a relational statement. KJV/ASV/WEB preserve the title form.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Innocence does not prevent persecution (vv.3-4). David is clear:
   "not for my transgression, nor for my sin." Sometimes you
   suffer not because you've done wrong but because others are
   doing wrong. Don't assume persecution means you deserve it.

2. God laughs at what terrifies you (v.8). The enemies that keep
   you awake at night are a joke to the God who rules the universe.
   His perspective should inform yours — what seems overwhelming
   from earth is laughable from heaven.

3. Morning is for singing (v.16). After the long night of danger,
   choose to greet the morning with praise. Don't let last night's
   fears set the tone for today. Morning is for mercy and music.

4. God is both strength and fortress (v.17). He is offensive power
   (strength) and defensive protection (fortress). Whatever you
   need — energy to advance or shelter to rest — He provides both.
