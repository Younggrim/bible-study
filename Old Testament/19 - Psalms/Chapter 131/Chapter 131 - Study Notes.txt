Psalms - Chapter 131
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A Song of Ascents of David — the shortest Song of Ascents: David professes childlike humility and quieted contentment before God, like a weaned child with its mother; 'Let Israel hope in the LORD.'

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Song of Ascents / Psalm of Trust
Attributed Author: David (superscription: "A Song of degrees
of David")
Key Themes: Humility before God, contentment like a weaned child,
quieting the soul, not striving beyond one's place, childlike
trust

Historical Context: Psalm 131 is one of the shortest and most
tender psalms — only three verses. David, the king of Israel,
describes himself as a weaned child on its mother's lap. The
weaned child (typically 2-3 years old in ancient Israel) no
longer nurses anxiously but rests contentedly with the mother —
needing nothing but presence. This is extraordinary from a warrior
king: he has deliberately set aside ambition, pride, and
striving for things "too high" for him. Spurgeon called this
psalm "one of the shortest to read but one of the longest to
learn." It is the psalm of spiritual maturity through chosen
humility.

Structure:
- Negation: What David Does NOT Do (v.1)
- Positive Image: A Weaned Child (v.2)
- Call to Israel: Hope (v.3)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "LORD, my heart is not haughty, nor mine eyes lofty:
        neither do I exercise myself in great matters, or in
        things too high for me." (KJV) — David has CHOSEN humility.
        Not because he lacks ability (he's the king!) but because
        he knows his place. He doesn't reach for what's not his.
        He doesn't pretend to understand everything.

v.2  — "Surely I have behaved and quieted myself, as a child that
        is weaned of his mother: my soul is even as a weaned
        child." (KJV) — A WEANED child — not a hungry infant
        screaming for milk but a satisfied child resting in the
        mother's arms wanting nothing but presence. This is
        spiritual maturity: resting without demanding.

v.3  — "Let Israel hope in the LORD from henceforth and for
        ever." (KJV) — From personal quietness to corporate call.
        The individual's rest becomes the nation's posture: hope
        in the LORD. Always. Forever.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.2 — "weaned" (Hebrew "gamul") = completed, matured. The weaned
      child has moved past infantile need into quiet contentment.
      This is not regression to infancy but growth into rest.

v.1 — "things too high for me" — Hebrew "niphla'ot" = wonderful/
      extraordinary things beyond my capacity. David doesn't deny
      mystery — he accepts what is beyond him without striving.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.1 — KJV: "LORD, my heart is not haughty, nor mine eyes lofty" / NET: "O Lord, my heart is not proud, nor do I have a haughty look" — NET redistributes: KJV's "haughty" (heart) and "lofty" (eyes) become "proud" (heart) and "haughty look" (eyes).
- v.1 — KJV: "neither do I exercise myself in great matters, or in things too high for me" / ASV: "things too wonderful for me" / WEB: "things too wonderful for me" / NET: "I do not have great aspirations, or concern myself with things that are beyond me" — ASV/WEB's "wonderful" (Hebrew "niph'la'ot") adds positive connotation; NET neutralizes to "beyond me."
- v.2 — KJV: "I have behaved and quieted myself" / ASV/WEB: "I have stilled and quieted my soul" / NET: "I have calmed and quieted myself" — ASV/WEB distinguish between "stilled" (action) and "quieted" (result); NET combines both into the gentler "calmed and quieted."
- v.2 — NET: "I am content like a young child" — Adds "content" (not in Hebrew) to interpret the weaned-child metaphor. This makes explicit what the other translations leave implicit through imagery.
- v.3 — ASV: "O Israel, hope in Jehovah" / WEB: "Israel, hope in Yahweh" / NET: "O Israel, hope in the Lord" — Standard divine name pattern in the psalm's closing exhortation.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Choose humility (v.1). Humility is not weakness — it's the
   king of Israel refusing to overreach. Don't exercise yourself
   in matters too great for you. Not everything is your burden.

2. Become a weaned child (v.2). Stop screaming for what you don't
   have. Learn contentment. The weaned child has nothing in hand
   but rests in the mother's arms — satisfied with PRESENCE
   alone. Can you be satisfied with God Himself without His gifts?

3. This psalm is the antidote to ambition-anxiety. The constant
   striving for more, higher, greater — David renounces it. Rest.
   Be content. Not everything "high" is yours to reach for.

4. Three verses. The greatest truths sometimes need the fewest
   words. Entire libraries on humility couldn't say it better.
