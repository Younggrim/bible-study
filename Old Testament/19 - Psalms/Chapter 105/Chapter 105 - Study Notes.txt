Psalms - Chapter 105
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A historical praise psalm — recounts God's faithfulness from Abraham's covenant through Joseph in Egypt, the plagues, the Exodus, and the gift of the promised land; 'He remembered his holy promise to Abraham his servant.'

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Historical Psalm / Hymn of Praise
Attributed Author: Anonymous (portions appear in 1 Chr 16:8-22,
attributed to David for the ark's arrival in Jerusalem)
Key Themes: God's faithfulness to His covenant, Israel's history
as God's story, the Abrahamic covenant, Joseph's suffering and
exaltation, the Exodus plagues, providence through history,
remembering God's works

Historical Context: Psalm 105 is a sweeping narrative of Israel's
history told as PRAISE — from Abraham's covenant through the
Exodus to the Promised Land. It is paired with Psalm 106 (which
tells the same history emphasizing Israel's failures). Together
they show: God was faithful (105) even when Israel was not (106).
Verses 1-15 appear in 1 Chronicles 16:8-22 as part of the song
David composed when the ark was brought to Jerusalem. This psalm
emphasizes God as the active agent throughout — HE remembered, HE
sent, HE brought out. The purpose of Israel's rescue: "that they
might observe his statutes" (v.45).

Structure:
- Call to Praise and Remember (vv.1-6)
- God's Covenant with Abraham (vv.7-11)
- Protection of the Patriarchs (vv.12-15)
- Joseph: Suffering to Exaltation (vv.16-22)
- Israel in Egypt and the Plagues (vv.23-36)
- The Exodus and Wilderness (vv.37-41)
- The Promise Fulfilled (vv.42-45)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.4  — "Seek the LORD, and his strength: seek his face
        evermore." (KJV) — Three things to seek: the LORD Himself,
        His strength, His face (presence). And the duration:
        EVERMORE — not occasionally, not in crisis only, but
        continually.

v.8  — "He hath remembered his covenant for ever, the word which
        he commanded to a thousand generations." (KJV) — God
        remembers. When everything seems forgotten, God has not
        forgotten His word. A thousand generations = perpetual
        faithfulness.

v.19 — "Until the time that his word came: the word of the LORD
        tried him." (KJV) — Joseph's imprisonment was not
        purposeless. The word of the LORD TESTED him. Between the
        promise and the fulfillment is the trial. God's word
        refines those who carry it.

v.42 — "For he remembered his holy promise, and Abraham his
        servant." (KJV) — The entire Exodus happened because God
        remembered His promise to Abraham. Every deliverance in
        Israel's history flows from one covenant made centuries
        earlier.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.15 — "Touch not mine anointed" — Hebrew "meshichay" (my
       anointed ones). In context refers to the patriarchs as
       set apart by God, not specifically kings. Often misapplied
       to modern leaders; originally about Abraham, Isaac, Jacob.

v.19 — KJV: "the word of the LORD tried him" / ESV: "the word of
       the LORD tested him" — Hebrew "tsaraph" = to refine, test
       by fire. The same word used for refining precious metals.
       Joseph's character was being purified by waiting.

v.28 — KJV: "they rebelled not against his word" / ESV: "they did
       not rebel against his words" — Refers to Moses and Aaron
       obeying God's commands regarding the plagues. Some
       manuscripts and versions differ on this reading.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.1 — ASV: "Oh give thanks unto Jehovah" / WEB: "Give thanks to
  Yahweh!" / NET: "Give thanks to the Lord" — The divine name
  pattern: ASV renders YHWH as "Jehovah," WEB as "Yahweh," NET as
  "the Lord." NET adds "Call on his name. Make known his
  accomplishments among the nations" — turning "deeds" into
  "accomplishments," a more interpretive choice.

- v.1 — NET: "Make known his accomplishments among the nations" /
  KJV: "make known his deeds among the people" — NET changes
  "people" (singular, could mean Israel) to "nations" (plural,
  Gentiles), and "deeds" to "accomplishments" — subtly shifting
  from covenant community to universal witness.

- v.8 — NET: "He always remembers his covenantal decree, the
  promise he made to a thousand generations" — NET interprets
  "covenant" as "covenantal decree" and "word which he commanded"
  as "promise." This is helpful but introduces interpretation
  where KJV/ASV/WEB stay closer to the Hebrew parallelism.

- v.15 — WEB: "Don't touch my anointed ones! Do my prophets no
  harm!" / NET: "Don't touch my chosen ones. Don't harm my
  prophets." — NET changes "anointed" (meshichay) to "chosen ones,"
  losing the messianic overtone. WEB/ASV retain "anointed," which
  connects to the concept of God setting apart His people.

- v.19 — ASV: "The word of Jehovah tried him" / NET: "The Lord's
  word proved him right" / WEB: "Yahweh's word proved him true" —
  KJV/ASV use "tried" (tested/refined), while NET/WEB shift to
  "proved right/true" — nearly opposite meanings. ASV emphasizes
  the refining process; NET emphasizes vindication.

- v.40 — WEB: "satisfied them with the bread of the sky" / KJV:
  "satisfied them with the bread of heaven" / NET: "satisfied them
  with food from the sky" — Hebrew "lechem shamayim." WEB/NET
  render "shamayim" as "sky" rather than "heaven," reducing the
  theological resonance with John 6 where Jesus calls himself the
  "bread from heaven."

- v.45 — WEB: "Praise Yah!" / ASV: "Praise ye Jehovah" / NET:
  "Praise the Lord" — Again WEB uniquely preserves the Hebrew
  exclamation "Hallelu-Yah" in its shortened divine name form.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Tell the story (vv.1-2). "Make known his deeds among the
   people." Faith is strengthened by rehearsing what God has done.
   Tell your children, tell your community, tell yourself — God
   has been faithful.

2. Between promise and fulfillment is testing (v.19). Joseph had
   a dream from God — then spent years in a pit and a prison.
   The word of the LORD tested him. If you're in the gap between
   God's promise and its fulfillment, you're being refined.

3. God sends people ahead (v.17). Before the famine came, God
   sent Joseph. Before you face your crisis, God has already
   positioned provision. Providence works ahead of time.

4. The purpose of deliverance is obedience (v.45). God didn't
   rescue Israel just to be free. He rescued them "that they
   might observe his statutes." Freedom is not an end in itself
   — it is freedom FOR serving God.

5. God remembers His promises (v.42). If you've been waiting a
   long time, remember: the time between Abraham's promise and
   the Exodus was 400+ years. God does not forget. His timeline
   is not yours, but His memory is perfect.
