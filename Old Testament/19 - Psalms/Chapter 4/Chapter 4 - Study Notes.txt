Psalms - Chapter 4
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
An evening psalm of trust — David calls on God in distress, rebukes those who love vanity, and declares he will lie down in peace because the LORD alone makes him dwell in safety.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Individual Lament / Evening Trust Psalm
Attributed Author: David — "To the chief Musician on Neginoth,
A Psalm of David"
Key Themes: Prayer in distress, God's favor vs. worldly gain, joy
that surpasses material prosperity, peaceful sleep, safety in God

Historical Context: Psalm 4 is the traditional "evening psalm,"
paired with Psalm 3 as a "morning psalm." The instruction "on
Neginoth" (stringed instruments) indicates it was set to music for
worship. The context appears similar to Psalm 3 — David facing
opposition, possibly during Absalom's rebellion or another period
of political crisis. The "sons of men" (v.2) may be influential
leaders turning against David's honor. The psalm moves from urgent
prayer to serene confidence, ending with one of Scripture's most
beautiful expressions of trust: sleeping in safety because God
alone is enough.

Structure:
- Opening Prayer (v.1)
- Address to Opponents (vv.2-3)
- Instruction for the Troubled (vv.4-5)
- Joy Greater Than Prosperity (vv.6-7)
- Peaceful Rest in God (v.8)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.4  — "Stand in awe, and sin not: commune with your own heart
        upon your bed, and be still. Selah." (KJV) / "Be angry,
        and do not sin; ponder in your own hearts on your beds,
        and be silent." (ESV) — Quoted by Paul in Ephesians 4:26.
        A remarkable permission: anger itself is not sin. The
        command is to process anger privately before God (on your
        bed, in silence) rather than acting rashly.

v.7  — "Thou hast put gladness in my heart, more than in the time
        that their corn and their wine increased." (KJV) — God-
        given joy exceeds harvest-time prosperity. Material
        abundance is the world's highest happiness; David claims
        something greater — joy that comes from God's presence
        rather than God's gifts.

v.8  — "I will both lay me down in peace, and sleep: for thou,
        LORD, only makest me dwell in safety." (KJV) — The
        companion to Psalm 3:5. The key word is "only" — God ALONE
        is sufficient for safety. Not armies, not walls, not
        circumstances — God only.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.1   — Psalm 18:36 — "Thou hast enlarged my steps under me."
         God "enlarges" in distress — gives room to breathe in
         tight places.
v.4   — Ephesians 4:26 — "Be ye angry, and sin not: let not the
         sun go down upon your wrath." Paul directly quotes this
         psalm for Christian instruction.
v.5   — Psalm 51:19 — "Then shalt thou be pleased with the
         sacrifices of righteousness." Right sacrifices from a
         right heart.
v.6   — Numbers 6:24-26 — "The LORD make his face shine upon thee."
         The Aaronic blessing — the "light of countenance" David
         requests.
v.7   — Psalm 63:3 — "Thy lovingkindness is better than life."
         Same theology: God's favor exceeds any earthly good.
v.7   — Habakkuk 3:17-18 — "Although the fig tree shall not
         blossom... yet I will rejoice in the LORD." Joy
         independent of material prosperity.
v.8   — Psalm 3:5 — "I laid me down and slept; I awaked; for the
         LORD sustained me." The morning counterpart.
v.8   — Proverbs 3:24 — "When thou liest down, thou shalt not be
         afraid: yea, thou shalt lie down, and thy sleep shall be
         sweet." Same promise of peaceful rest in trust.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1 — KJV: "thou hast enlarged me when I was in distress" / ESV:
      "You have given me relief when I was in distress" — Hebrew
      "hirchavta" = you made wide/spacious. The image is of being
      squeezed in a tight place and God making room. KJV's
      "enlarged" preserves the spatial metaphor better.

v.2 — KJV: "seek after leasing" / ESV: "seek after lies" — KJV
      "leasing" is archaic English for "lies/falsehood." ESV
      clarifies for modern readers. Hebrew "kazav" = lies,
      emptiness.

v.4 — KJV: "Stand in awe, and sin not" / ESV: "Be angry, and do
      not sin" — Hebrew "rigzu" can mean "tremble" (awe/fear) or
      "be agitated/angry." The Septuagint translated it as "be
      angry" (orgizesthe), and Paul followed the LXX in Ephesians
      4:26. ESV follows this tradition. Both readings work: either
      "tremble before God and do not sin" or "when you are angry,
      do not let it lead to sin."

v.6 — KJV: "LORD, lift thou up the light of thy countenance upon
      us" / ESV: "Lift up the light of your face upon us, O LORD!"
      — This echoes the Aaronic Benediction (Numbers 6:25-26).
      "Light of face" = God's favorable attention, His pleasure
      turned toward His people.

v.7 — KJV: "corn and wine" / ESV: "grain and wine" — Hebrew
      "dagan" = grain (KJV's "corn" is British English for grain
      generally, not American maize). These represent harvest
      abundance — the peak of material joy in an agrarian society.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.1 — NET: "O God who vindicates me" — NET interprets "God of my righteousness" (KJV/ESV) as God who establishes David's rightness. NET also paraphrases the spatial metaphor: "Though I am hemmed in, you will lead me into a wide, open place" — rendering the single Hebrew verb "hirchavta" (you enlarged) into a full interpretive clause.
- v.3 — ASV: "Jehovah hath set apart for himself him that is godly" — ASV uses "Jehovah" for the divine name (Hebrew YHWH), where KJV/ESV use "LORD" (small caps). This preserves the personal covenant name of God rather than replacing it with a title.
- v.3 — WEB: "Yahweh has set apart for himself him who is godly" — WEB renders the tetragrammaton as "Yahweh," the most likely pronunciation of YHWH. This is theologically significant: David invokes God's personal, revealed name — the name of covenant relationship.
- v.4 — NET: "Tremble with fear and do not sin" — NET follows the "tremble" reading of Hebrew "rigzu" (like KJV's "stand in awe"), rejecting the LXX/Ephesians 4:26 "be angry" tradition followed by ESV. NET also renders "commune with your own heart" as "Meditate as you lie in bed, and repent of your ways" — a significantly more interpretive rendering.
- v.6 — NET: "Smile upon us, Lord!" — A dramatic paraphrase of KJV's "lift thou up the light of thy countenance upon us." NET reduces the Aaronic Benediction echo (Numbers 6:25-26) to a simple expression. The theological richness of "light of countenance" — God's favorable attention and radiant presence — is lost.
- v.6 — WEB: "Yahweh, let the light of your face shine on us" — WEB preserves both the divine name "Yahweh" AND the face/light imagery, making the Numbers 6:25 connection clear while using the covenant name.
- v.7 — NET: "You make me happier than those who have abundant grain and wine" — NET renders the comparative idiom into plain modern English. KJV's "Thou hast put gladness in my heart, more than in the time that their corn and their wine increased" preserves the Hebrew structure and the word "heart" (lev), indicating internal joy rather than mere happiness.
- v.8 — WEB: "for you, Yahweh alone, make me live in safety" — WEB renders "dwell in safety" (KJV) as "live in safety" and importantly includes "Yahweh alone" — preserving both the divine name and the exclusivity ("alone"/levadad) that is the psalm's theological climax.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. God enlarges us IN distress, not just FROM distress (v.1). The
   relief is not always removal of the situation but expansion
   within it — room to breathe, think, and trust even while the
   pressure remains.

2. Anger is not sin; unprocessed anger is (v.4). The permission to
   feel anger is also the command to handle it properly: take it
   to your bed, take it to silence, take it to God. Do not act
   from it, speak from it, or let it fester overnight.

3. Joy from God exceeds joy from prosperity (v.7). Harvest time
   was the happiest season in the ancient world. David claims a
   joy GREATER than the best the world can offer. This is not
   anti-material — it is beyond-material.

4. "Thou ONLY" — sufficiency theology (v.8). The word "only"
   (levadad) is the psalm's climax. Not God plus security
   measures. Not God plus favorable circumstances. God ALONE makes
   David dwell in safety. When everything else is stripped away,
   God alone remains — and He is enough.

5. This psalm moves from petition to peace (vv.1→8). The journey
   of a single evening prayer: begin by crying out, work through
   the injustice, process the anger, offer right worship — and
   end in sleep. The psalm itself IS the process of finding rest.
