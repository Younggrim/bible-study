Psalms - Chapter 89
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A royal lament closing Book III — Ethan celebrates God's covenant with David ('his seed shall endure forever'), praises God's faithfulness and power, then agonizes because the anointed king appears rejected and humiliated.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Royal / Covenant / Lament Psalm
Attributed Author: Ethan the Ezrahite
Key Themes: God's covenant faithfulness (hesed), the Davidic
covenant, God's incomparability, the tension between divine
promises and present suffering, the apparent failure of God's oath

Historical Context: This massive psalm by Ethan the Ezrahite
(1 Kings 4:31 — one of Israel's wisest men) closes Book III of
the Psalter. It begins with exuberant praise of God's hesed and
faithfulness, then recounts the Davidic covenant in detail
(2 Samuel 7), and then — shockingly — laments that the covenant
seems BROKEN (vv.38-51). The psalm likely dates to the Babylonian
exile or shortly before, when the Davidic dynasty was destroyed
or severely threatened. The agonizing tension: God SWORE to David
an eternal throne, yet the throne lies in the dust. How can God's
oath fail? The psalm ends not with resolution but with a
desperate question (v.49) and a brief doxology (v.52) closing
Book III.

Structure:
- Praise for God's Hesed and Faithfulness (vv.1-4)
- Hymn to God's Incomparability (vv.5-18)
- The Davidic Covenant Rehearsed (vv.19-37)
- The Apparent Failure of the Covenant (vv.38-45)
- Lament: How Long? (vv.46-51)
- Doxology Closing Book III (v.52)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "I will sing of the mercies of the LORD for ever: with my
        mouth will I make known thy faithfulness to all
        generations." (KJV) — The psalm's opening theme: hesed
        (steadfast love/mercy) forever, faithfulness to all
        generations. This makes the later lament all the more
        painful — the very attributes celebrated seem to have
        failed.

v.14 — "Justice and judgment are the habitation of thy throne:
        mercy and truth shall go before thy face." (KJV) — God's
        throne rests on justice AND righteousness. Before His face
        march mercy AND truth. This is the same fourfold attribute
        convergence as Psalm 85:10.

v.33-34 — "Nevertheless my lovingkindness will I not utterly take
        from him, nor suffer my faithfulness to fail. My covenant
        will I not break, nor alter the thing that is gone out of
        my lips." (KJV) — The unconditional core: even when
        David's sons disobey, God will NOT break His covenant. He
        may discipline but never abandon. The promise is
        irrevocable.

v.38-39 — "But thou hast cast off and abhorred, thou hast been
        wroth with thine anointed. Thou hast made void the
        covenant of thy servant." (KJV) — The devastating turn.
        Everything vv.1-37 celebrate seems negated. The covenant
        appears void. The crown is in the dust. This is
        theological crisis at its sharpest.

v.49 — "Lord, where are thy former lovingkindnesses, which thou
        swarest unto David in thy truth?" (KJV) — The psalm's
        agonizing question. Where did Your hesed go? You SWORE.
        What happened to Your oath? The answer comes not in this
        psalm but in Christ — David's ultimate Son whose throne
        truly endures forever.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.3-4 — 2 Samuel 7:12-16 — The Davidic covenant. The basis of
         everything in this psalm.
v.9   — Mark 4:39 — Jesus stilling the storm: "Peace, be still."
         Fulfilling what only God can do (Ps 89:9).
v.20  — 1 Samuel 16:13 — David's anointing with oil.
v.26  — Romans 8:15 — "Abba, Father." Jesus as the Son who calls
         God "Father" (fulfilling v.26).
v.27  — Colossians 1:15,18 — Christ as "firstborn" — the highest
         of kings, fulfilling v.27.
v.34  — Romans 11:29 — "The gifts and calling of God are without
         repentance." God's covenant stands.
v.52  — Psalm 41:13; 72:18-19; 106:48 — The doxologies closing
         each of the five books of the Psalter.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.10 — KJV: "Thou hast broken Rahab in pieces" / ESV: "You
       crushed Rahab like a carcass" — "Rahab" = Egypt (poetic
       name), or the mythological chaos-sea-monster. God crushed
       Egypt/chaos at the Exodus.

v.19 — KJV: "thy holy one" / ESV: "your godly one" — Hebrew
       "chasid" = the one to whom the vision was given. Could
       refer to Nathan the prophet or to David himself receiving
       God's covenant promise.

v.52 — This verse is the doxology closing Book III of the Psalter.
       It is not part of Ethan's psalm proper but an editorial
       addition marking the book division: "Blessed be the LORD
       for evermore. Amen, and Amen."

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. You can celebrate God's faithfulness AND question His apparent
   failure (vv.1-4, 38-51). This psalm holds both together without
   resolving the tension. Faith does not require understanding.
   Praise and lament can coexist.

2. God's covenant is unconditional even when discipline is real
   (vv.33-34). He will punish disobedience but NEVER break His
   word. Discipline is not abandonment. Consequences are not
   covenant annulment.

3. The answer to this psalm is Christ. The Davidic throne DID seem
   to fail. But Jesus — born of David's line — sits on an eternal
   throne. What seemed like covenant failure was covenant delay.
   God's timing exceeds human timelines.

4. "Where are your former lovingkindnesses?" (v.49) is a prayer
   for all who feel God has forgotten His promises. Hold Him to
   His word. Remind Him of what He swore. This is not irreverence
   — it is covenant partnership.
