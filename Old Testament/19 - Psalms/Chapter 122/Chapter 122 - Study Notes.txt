Psalms - Chapter 122
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A Song of Ascents — 'I was glad when they said unto me, Let us go into the house of the LORD'; a celebration of arriving at Jerusalem, praying for the city's peace and prosperity.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Song of Ascents / Song of Zion
Attributed Author: David (superscription: "A Song of degrees
of David")
Key Themes: Joy in going to God's house, love for Jerusalem,
prayer for peace, unity of God's people, worship as communal
destination

Historical Context: Psalm 122 is the third Song of Ascents and
the pilgrim's song of ARRIVAL. After the distress of 120 and
the journey of 121, the pilgrim reaches Jerusalem and expresses
joy. The psalm celebrates Jerusalem as the place where tribes
gather, where God's throne of justice stands, and where peace
should be prayed for. "Pray for the peace of Jerusalem" (v.6)
remains one of the most quoted phrases in all of Scripture.
Jerusalem means "city of peace" (shalom), making the prayer
for peace a play on the city's own name.

Structure:
- Joy at the Invitation (v.1)
- Arrival in Jerusalem (v.2)
- Description of the City (vv.3-5)
- Prayer for Peace (vv.6-9)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "I was glad when they said unto me, Let us go into the
        house of the LORD." (KJV) — Gladness at the invitation
        to worship. Not duty, not reluctance — JOY. The call to
        worship is received as good news. This is the heart test
        of any worshiper: are you glad when it's time?

v.6  — "Pray for the peace of Jerusalem: they shall prosper that
        love thee." (KJV) — A command with a promise. Pray for
        Jerusalem's shalom (peace, wholeness, prosperity). Those
        who love her will prosper. God connects our welfare to our
        concern for His people and His city.

v.8  — "For my brethren and companions' sakes, I will now say,
        Peace be within thee." (KJV) — Peace is prayed for the
        sake of OTHERS — brethren and companions. Peace is not
        selfish; it's communal. We pray for peace because others
        need it.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.3 — KJV: "compact together" / ESV: "bound firmly together" —
      Hebrew "chubberah" = joined together, compacted. Jerusalem
      as a unified, cohesive city — architecturally and
      spiritually. No gaps, no divisions.

v.6 — "Peace of Jerusalem" — Hebrew wordplay: "sha'alu shalom
      Yerushalayim" — pray (sha'al) peace (shalom) for the city
      whose name contains shalom. The name and the prayer are one.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.1 — ASV: "the house of Jehovah" / WEB: "Yahweh's house" / NET: "the Lord's temple" — NET adds "temple" where the Hebrew simply says "house" (bayit). This makes the identification explicit but narrows the meaning.
- v.3 — NET: "Jerusalem is a city designed to accommodate an assembly" — Radically different from KJV's "Jerusalem is builded as a city that is compact together." NET interprets the architectural description as functional purpose; KJV/ASV/WEB describe physical unity.
- v.4 — KJV: "the tribes of the LORD" / ASV: "the tribes of Jehovah" / WEB: "Yah's tribes" / NET: "the tribes of the Lord" — WEB uniquely uses short form "Yah" here. KJV: "unto the testimony of Israel" vs. ASV/WEB: "an ordinance for Israel" vs. NET: "where it is required that Israel give thanks."
- v.5 — NET: "Indeed, the leaders sit there on thrones and make legal decisions" — Paraphrases KJV's "there are set thrones of judgment." NET adds "leaders" and "make legal decisions," interpreting the judicial function.
- v.6 — KJV/ASV/WEB: "Pray for the peace of Jerusalem" — All translations preserve this famous alliteration (sha'alu shalom Yerushalayim). The Hebrew wordplay connecting "pray" (sha'al), "peace" (shalom), and "Jerusalem" (Yerushalayim) is noted similarly across versions.
- v.7 — KJV/ASV: "prosperity within thy palaces" / WEB: "prosperity within your palaces" / NET: "prosperity inside your fortresses" — NET changes "palaces" to "fortresses" (Hebrew "armon" can mean either), shifting from royal luxury to military security.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Be glad to worship (v.1). If the invitation to gather with
   God's people produces reluctance instead of gladness, examine
   your heart. Corporate worship is a privilege, not an
   obligation.

2. Pray for peace (v.6). Pray for the peace of your church, your
   city, your nation. Peace doesn't happen by accident — it comes
   through prayer.

3. Unity matters (vv.3-4). The tribes go up TOGETHER. Jerusalem
   is a city "compact together." God values the unity of His
   people gathered in one place.

4. For the sake of others (v.8). Pursue peace not just for your
   own comfort but for the sake of your brothers and sisters.
   Community peace is everyone's responsibility.
