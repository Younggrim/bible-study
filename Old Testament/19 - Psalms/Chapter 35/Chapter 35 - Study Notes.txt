Psalms - Chapter 35
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
An imprecatory lament — David calls on God to fight against those who fight him, repay his enemies who return evil for good, and vindicate his righteousness before those who falsely accuse him.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Imprecatory Psalm / Individual Lament
Attributed Author: David ("A Psalm of David")
Key Themes: Enemies repaying evil for good, false accusation, plea
for divine warrior intervention, vindication of the righteous,
betrayal by former friends

Historical Context: Psalm 35 is one of the major imprecatory
(cursing) psalms in which David calls upon God to judge his
enemies. The historical context likely relates to the period when
David was persecuted by Saul and those who conspired with him.
David's enemies have repaid his kindness with cruelty (vv.12-14)
— he mourned for them when they were sick, yet they rejoiced at
his stumbling.

The psalm is striking for its military imagery. David pictures God
as a Divine Warrior taking up shield, buckler, and spear to fight
on his behalf (vv.1-3). This is not David seeking personal
revenge but appealing to God as the righteous Judge to intervene
where human justice has failed. The imprecatory elements must be
understood in their covenantal context — David is asking God to
fulfill His covenant promises to defend the righteous and punish
the wicked.

The psalm divides into three cycles, each ending with a vow of
praise (vv.9-10, v.18, vv.27-28). This structure shows that
David's ultimate aim is not destruction of enemies but the
vindication of God's justice, leading to worship. The phrase "hate
me without a cause" (v.19) is applied to Christ in John 15:25,
connecting David's experience of unjust persecution to the
ultimate unjust persecution of the Messiah.

Structure:
- First Appeal: God as Divine Warrior (vv. 1-10)
- Second Appeal: False Witnesses and Betrayal (vv. 11-18)
- Third Appeal: Plea for Vindication (vv. 19-28)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
Verse 1-3 — "Plead my cause, O LORD, with them that strive with
me: fight against them that fight against me. Take hold of shield
and buckler, and stand up for mine help. Draw out also the spear,
and stop the way against them that persecute me: say unto my soul,
I am thy salvation." (KJV)
David does not take up arms himself — he asks God to fight. The
imagery of God with shield, buckler, and spear portrays the LORD
as a personal bodyguard and champion warrior. The climax is the
request for reassurance: "say unto my soul, I am thy salvation."

Verse 12-14 — "They rewarded me evil for good to the spoiling of
my soul. But as for me, when they were sick, my clothing was
sackcloth: I humbled my soul with fasting...I behaved myself as
though he had been my friend or brother." (KJV)
This is the emotional core of the psalm. David's enemies are not
strangers but former friends for whom he grieved and prayed. The
betrayal of trusted companions makes their cruelty especially
piercing. Jesus experienced this same pattern (John 13:18).

Verse 19 — "Let not them that are mine enemies wrongfully rejoice
over me: neither let them wink with the eye that hate me without
a cause." (KJV)
Jesus quotes this phrase in John 15:25: "They hated me without a
cause." David's unjust suffering foreshadows Christ's, who was
hated despite perfect love and sinless life.

Verse 27 — "Let them shout for joy, and be glad, that favour my
righteous cause: yea, let them say continually, Let the LORD be
magnified, which hath pleasure in the prosperity of his servant."
(KJV)
The psalm ends not with vindictiveness but with doxology. God
takes "pleasure in the prosperity of his servant" — He delights
in the wellbeing of His people. This reveals God's disposition
toward His children.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.1-3 — Exodus 15:3; Psalm 144:1-2: God as warrior — fighting
for His people as He fought at the Red Sea and empowers David
for battle.

v.5 — Psalm 1:4; Hosea 13:3: The wicked as chaff — weightless,
purposeless, blown away by divine judgment.

v.7 — Psalm 7:15-16; Psalm 57:6: The principle of enemies falling
into their own traps — a recurring theme of divine poetic justice
throughout the Psalms.

v.11 — Matthew 26:59-60; Mark 14:56: False witnesses against
David prefigure false witnesses at Jesus' trial.

v.12-14 — Psalm 109:4-5: Same theme of repaying evil for good,
hatred for love — the deep wound of betrayed friendship.

v.19 — John 15:25: Jesus directly applies "hated without a cause"
to Himself, identifying with David's unjust persecution as
fulfilled in His own.

v.23 — Psalm 7:6; Psalm 44:23: The appeal for God to "awake" —
anthropomorphic language expressing the felt absence of divine
intervention during prolonged suffering.

v.27 — Psalm 149:4; Jeremiah 32:41: God delighting in the
prosperity (shalom) of His servants — divine joy over human
flourishing.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.3 — "stop the way" (KJV) / "javelin" (ESV): Hebrew segor is
debated. KJV interprets it as "close up the way" (block the path
of pursuers). ESV follows those who see it as a type of weapon
(javelin or battle-axe). The Septuagint renders it "close up the
way," supporting KJV. The exact meaning of this Hebrew word
remains uncertain.

v.12 — "to the spoiling of my soul" (KJV) / "my soul is bereft"
(ESV): Hebrew shekol lanaphshi — literally "bereavement to my
soul." KJV's "spoiling" suggests plundering; ESV's "bereft"
captures the sense of loss and desolation. David feels robbed,
orphaned by the betrayal of those he loved.

v.15 — "the abjects" (KJV) / "wretches" (ESV): Hebrew nekim
refers to those who are smitten, lowly strikers — perhaps
ruffians or base persons. KJV's archaic "abjects" means people
of the lowest class. ESV's "wretches" conveys the contemptible
character of those who joined the mob against David.

v.17 — "my darling" (KJV) / "my precious life" (ESV): Hebrew
yechidati — literally "my only one," meaning my unique, solitary
life (the only one I have). KJV's "darling" is an older English
meaning "dear one." ESV clarifies the meaning as David's
irreplaceable, singular life.

v.27 — "which hath pleasure in the prosperity of his servant"
(KJV) / "who delights in the welfare of his servant" (ESV):
Hebrew chaphets bishalom avdo — "delights in the shalom (peace,
wholeness, prosperity) of his servant." Both translations capture
the remarkable truth that God finds pleasure in the flourishing
of His people.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. David's first instinct in conflict was not retaliation but
appeal to God: "Plead my cause, O LORD" (v.1). When you are
wronged, is your instinct to fight back, to gossip, to scheme —
or to bring the case before God as Judge? The imprecatory psalms
teach us to channel anger toward prayer rather than vengeance.

2. The pain of betrayal by friends (vv.12-14) is among life's
deepest wounds. David had genuinely loved these people — fasted
for them, mourned for them. Have you experienced the unique pain
of kindness repaid with cruelty? How do you process that grief
before God without becoming bitter?

3. "They hated me without a cause" (v.19) connects David's
experience to Christ's (John 15:25). When you suffer unjustly,
you participate in a pattern experienced by David and ultimately
by Jesus Himself. How does this connection comfort you in
undeserved suffering?

4. Three times David vows future praise (vv.9-10, 18, 27-28).
Even in the midst of pain, he anticipates the thanksgiving that
will follow deliverance. Can you praise God in advance — trusting
that your current lament will eventually become testimony?

5. God "hath pleasure in the prosperity of his servant" (v.27).
God is not indifferent to your wellbeing. He is not a distant,
uncaring deity who watches suffering with detachment. He actively
delights in your shalom — your wholeness and flourishing. Let this
truth reshape your image of God.
