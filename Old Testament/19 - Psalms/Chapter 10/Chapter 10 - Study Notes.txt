Psalms - Chapter 10
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A lament over the arrogance of the wicked — they say 'God has forgotten,' they prey on the helpless, yet the psalmist affirms that the LORD sees, hears the desire of the afflicted, and will judge.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Individual/Communal Lament
Attributed Author: Anonymous (no superscription; continuation of
Psalm 9 acrostic — treated as one psalm in LXX)
Key Themes: God's apparent absence, the arrogance and cruelty of
the wicked, oppression of the poor, practical atheism, God as
defender of the fatherless

Historical Context: Psalm 10 continues the broken acrostic begun
in Psalm 9. It has no superscription because it is the second half
of that pair. The psalm provides the most detailed character study
of the wicked in the Psalter — describing the oppressor's
theology (vv.4, 11, 13: "God doesn't see/care"), strategy
(vv.8-10: ambush and predation), and attitude (vv.3-6: boasting,
self-sufficiency). The opening question — "Why standest thou afar
off?" — is the cry of all who suffer under injustice while God
seems silent. Yet the psalm ends with confident affirmation that
God DOES see, DOES hear, and WILL act for the fatherless and
oppressed.

Structure:
- The Cry: Where is God? (v.1)
- Portrait of the Wicked (vv.2-11)
- Appeal for God to Act (vv.12-15)
- Confident Affirmation (vv.16-18)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "Why standest thou afar off, O LORD? why hidest thou thyself
        in times of trouble?" (KJV) — The raw question of faith
        under suffering. Not atheistic doubt but believing protest.
        David ADDRESSES God while asking why God seems absent. The
        question assumes God's existence while lamenting His
        apparent inaction.

v.4  — "The wicked, through the pride of his countenance, will not
        seek after God: God is not in all his thoughts." (KJV) —
        Practical atheism: not intellectual denial of God's
        existence but functional exclusion of God from daily life.
        "All his thoughts are: There is no God" (ESV) — his
        operating assumption.

v.14 — "Thou hast seen it; for thou beholdest mischief and spite,
        to requite it with thy hand: the poor committeth himself
        unto thee; thou art the helper of the fatherless." (KJV) —
        The answer to v.1: God IS watching. The wicked says "God
        has forgotten" (v.11); the psalmist answers "Thou hast
        seen it" (v.14). God's silence is not blindness.

v.17-18 — "LORD, thou hast heard the desire of the humble: thou
          wilt prepare their heart, thou wilt cause thine ear to
          hear: To judge the fatherless and the oppressed" (KJV) —
          God hears, strengthens hearts, and brings justice. The
          psalm's resolution: God WILL act for the vulnerable.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.4 — KJV: "God is not in all his thoughts" / ESV: "all his
      thoughts are, 'There is no God'" — The Hebrew can be read
      both ways. KJV: God is not part of his thinking. ESV: his
      operating principle IS "no God." ESV is sharper — not just
      neglect of God but active exclusion.

v.5 — KJV: "His ways are always grievous" / ESV: "His ways prosper
      at all times" — Hebrew "yachilu" is debated. KJV reads as
      "grievous/painful" (causing grief to others); ESV reads as
      "firm/prosperous." ESV fits the context better — the problem
      is that the wicked SUCCEED, not that they suffer.

v.10 — KJV: "He croucheth, and humbleth himself, that the poor may
       fall by his strong ones" / ESV: "The helpless are crushed,
       sink down, and fall by his might" — Significantly different.
       KJV describes the predator crouching; ESV describes the
       victim collapsing. The Hebrew is difficult; both readings
       convey oppression.

v.17 — KJV: "thou wilt prepare their heart" / ESV: "you will
       strengthen their heart" — Hebrew "kun" = establish, make
       firm, prepare. God does not merely hear the humble — He
       strengthens their hearts to endure until justice comes.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.1 — ASV: "Why standest thou afar off, O Jehovah?" / WEB: "Why do you stand far off, Yahweh?" — Both preserve the divine covenant name (YHWH) while KJV renders "O LORD." NET paraphrases: "Why do you pay no attention during times of trouble?" — adding interpretive weight to "hide yourself."

- v.2 — NET: "The wicked arrogantly chase the oppressed; the oppressed are trapped by the schemes the wicked have dreamed up" — NET renders this as a declarative statement, while KJV/ASV read it as a wish/prayer: "let them be taken in the devices that they have conceived." The Hebrew allows both readings.

- v.4 — NET: "The wicked man is so arrogant he always thinks, 'God won't hold me accountable; he doesn't care'" — This is highly interpretive. KJV: "God is not in all his thoughts." ASV: "All his thoughts are, There is no God." NET expands the concept into a full internal monologue, making the practical atheism explicit.

- v.5 — ASV: "His ways are firm at all times" / WEB: "His ways are prosperous at all times" / NET: "He is secure at all times" — KJV reads "His ways are always grievous." The Hebrew "yachilu" is genuinely debated. ASV/WEB/NET all take the word as indicating the wicked man's success, while KJV reads it as causing grief to others.

- v.12 — WEB: "Arise, Yahweh! God, lift up your hand! Don't forget the helpless" — WEB uses "Yahweh" for YHWH and "helpless" where KJV reads "humble" (Hebrew "anav"). NET reads: "Rise up, Lord! O God, strike him down" — adding aggressive language not in the Hebrew "lift up your hand."

- v.14 — NET: "You have taken notice, for you always see one who inflicts pain and suffering. The unfortunate victim entrusts his cause to you" — NET greatly expands and interprets. KJV/ASV: "Thou hast seen it; for thou beholdest mischief and spite, to requite it with thy hand." NET's "unfortunate victim" replaces the Hebrew term for "helpless/poor."

- v.17 — ASV: "Jehovah, thou hast heard the desire of the meek" / WEB: "Yahweh, you have heard the desire of the humble" / NET: "Lord, you have heard the request of the oppressed" — Three different English terms for Hebrew "anavim": meek (ASV), humble (WEB/KJV), oppressed (NET). NET's choice emphasizes social condition over spiritual disposition.

- v.18 — NET: "You defend the fatherless and oppressed, so that mere mortals may no longer terrorize them" vs. KJV: "To judge the fatherless and the oppressed, that the man of the earth may no more oppress" — NET uses "defend" (positive protection) where Hebrew "shaphat" means "judge/execute justice for." Also, "mere mortals" for Hebrew "enosh min ha-aretz" (man of the earth) echoes Psalm 9:20.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Why?" is a prayer, not a sin (v.1). Asking God why He seems
   distant is not rebellion — it is faith in distress. The question
   is DIRECTED at God, which means it assumes God exists and is
   capable of answering. Honest questioning is better than
   polished silence.

2. Practical atheism is the most common form (v.4). Few explicitly
   deny God; many functionally exclude Him. The test is not "Do I
   believe God exists?" but "Is God in ALL my thoughts? In my
   finances, relationships, decisions, ambitions?"

3. God sees what the wicked think is hidden (v.14). The oppressor's
   assumption — "God has forgotten" (v.11) — is flatly
   contradicted: "Thou HAST seen it." Nothing is hidden from God.
   Every secret cruelty is observed and noted.

4. God is specifically the helper of the fatherless (v.14). Not
   generically kind but specifically oriented toward those with no
   human protector. The orphan, the widow, the refugee — those
   with no advocate find one in God Himself.

5. "The man of the earth" is put in his place (v.18). The final
   phrase: "that man who is of the earth may strike terror no
   more." Earthly oppressors are just that — earthly. Finite.
   Temporary. Their terror has an expiration date.
