Psalms - Chapter 62
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A psalm of quiet trust — 'My soul waiteth only upon God; from him cometh my salvation'; men are but a breath, trust not in extortion or riches; power and steadfast love belong to God alone.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Trust / Confidence Psalm
Attributed Author: David (directed to Jeduthun, a worship leader)
Key Themes: Waiting on God alone, soul silence before God, God as
rock and fortress, the vanity of human trust, power and mercy
belong to God

Historical Context: This psalm is directed "to Jeduthun" (one of
David's three chief musicians — 1 Chronicles 16:41-42). It may
date from Absalom's rebellion when David faced both political
conspiracy (v.4) and personal betrayal (v.4b — "they bless with
their mouth, but they curse inwardly"). The repeated word "only"
(Hebrew "ak") appears six times, making it the psalm's key word.
David insists that God ALONE is sufficient — not human alliances,
not wealth, not power. The psalm moves from personal testimony
(vv.1-7) to public exhortation (vv.8-12) and concludes with one
of Scripture's clearest statements about God's nature: power and
mercy belong to Him.

Structure:
- David's Soul Waits in Silence (vv.1-2)
- Rebuke of Enemies (vv.3-4)
- Renewed Declaration of Trust (vv.5-7)
- Exhortation to Trust God (v.8)
- The Vanity of Human Trust (vv.9-10)
- God's Final Word: Power and Mercy (vv.11-12)

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------

REFLECTION
------------------------------------
1. Silence before God is strength, not weakness (v.1). In a world
   that rewards noise and hustle, choosing stillness before God is
   radical trust. It says "I don't need to manipulate outcomes —
   God is enough."

2. You may be shaken but you won't be destroyed (v.2). "Not
   GREATLY moved" — faith doesn't promise zero turbulence. It
   promises you won't be overthrown. The rock sways in the storm
   but doesn't crumble.

3. Pour out your heart without editing (v.8). God doesn't need
   polished prayers. He wants raw honesty. Pour it out — the
   anger, the fear, the confusion, the grief. He is big enough to
   receive it all.

4. No human being can bear the weight of being your God (v.9).
   Don't put people on God's throne — they are lighter than breath.
   People will disappoint. Only God can bear the weight of your
   ultimate trust.

5. God is both powerful AND merciful (vv.11-12). This is the
   gospel in two words. If God were only powerful, we would be
   terrified. If only merciful, we would doubt His ability. He is
   both — and that changes everything.
