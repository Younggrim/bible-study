Psalms - Chapter 62
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A psalm of quiet trust — 'My soul waiteth only upon God; from him cometh my salvation'; men are but a breath, trust not in extortion or riches; power and steadfast love belong to God alone.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Trust / Confidence Psalm
Attributed Author: David (directed to Jeduthun, a worship leader)
Key Themes: Waiting on God alone, soul silence before God, God as
rock and fortress, the vanity of human trust, power and mercy
belong to God

Historical Context: This psalm is directed "to Jeduthun" (one of
David's three chief musicians — 1 Chronicles 16:41-42). It may
date from Absalom's rebellion when David faced both political
conspiracy (v.4) and personal betrayal (v.4b — "they bless with
their mouth, but they curse inwardly"). The repeated word "only"
(Hebrew "ak") appears six times, making it the psalm's key word.
David insists that God ALONE is sufficient — not human alliances,
not wealth, not power. The psalm moves from personal testimony
(vv.1-7) to public exhortation (vv.8-12) and concludes with one
of Scripture's clearest statements about God's nature: power and
mercy belong to Him.

Structure:
- David's Soul Waits in Silence (vv.1-2)
- Rebuke of Enemies (vv.3-4)
- Renewed Declaration of Trust (vv.5-7)
- Exhortation to Trust God (v.8)
- The Vanity of Human Trust (vv.9-10)
- God's Final Word: Power and Mercy (vv.11-12)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "Truly my soul waiteth upon God: from him cometh my
        salvation." (KJV) / ESV: "For God alone my soul waits in
        silence" — "Silence" (dumiyyah) = stillness, quiet trust.
        Not passive resignation but active, deliberate quieting of
        the soul. David chooses silence before God rather than
        anxious scrambling.

v.2  — "He only is my rock and my salvation; he is my defence; I
        shall not be greatly moved." (KJV) — Note "not GREATLY
        moved" — David is honest. He may be shaken, but not
        overthrown. The rock doesn't prevent all trembling but
        prevents collapse.

v.8  — "Trust in him at all times; ye people, pour out your heart
        before him: God is a refuge for us." (KJV) — The pastoral
        command: trust at ALL times (not just in emergencies), and
        POUR OUT your heart (not carefully worded prayers but raw
        honesty). God can handle your unfiltered emotions.

v.9  — "Surely men of low degree are vanity, and men of high
        degree are a lie: to be laid in the balance, they are
        altogether lighter than vanity." (KJV) — Both poor and
        rich, powerless and powerful, are lighter than breath on
        God's scales. No human being — regardless of status — can
        be the weight-bearing foundation of your life.

v.11-12 — "God hath spoken once; twice have I heard this; that
        power belongeth unto God. Also unto thee, O Lord,
        belongeth mercy." (KJV) — The psalm's theological
        climax. Power AND mercy — not one or the other. God is not
        merely powerful (that would be terrifying) or merely
        merciful (that would be impotent). He is both, perfectly.

v.12  — Revelation 22:12 — "My reward is with me, to give every
         man according as his work shall be."

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1 — KJV: "Truly my soul waiteth upon God" / ESV: "For God alone
      my soul waits in silence" — Hebrew "ak" = only, surely,
      indeed. ESV adds "in silence" from "dumiyyah" which KJV
      renders more loosely. The Hebrew emphasizes both exclusivity
      (God alone) and manner (in silence/stillness).

v.9 — KJV: "lighter than vanity" / ESV: "lighter than a breath" —
      Hebrew "hebel" (same word as Ecclesiastes' "vanity") = breath,
      vapor, mist. All humanity together, weighed against God's
      purposes, is lighter than a puff of air.

v.11 — KJV: "God hath spoken once; twice have I heard this" / ESV:
       "Once God has spoken; twice have I heard this" — A Hebrew
       numerical saying (like Proverbs 30) emphasizing certainty.
       "Once... twice" = definitively, with absolute clarity. What
       follows is beyond doubt.

v.1 — ASV: "in silence for God only: From" for KJV's "upon God: from"
       NET: "For the music director, Jeduthun; a psalm of David. For G..." for KJV's "Truly my soul waiteth upon God: from him cometh my salvat..."
       WEB: "rests in God alone. My salvation is" for KJV's "waiteth upon God:"
v.9 — ASV: adds "will go up; They"
       NET: "nothing but a mere breath; human beings" for KJV's "vanity, and men of high degree"
       WEB: adds "will go up. They"
v.11 — ASV: "once, Twice" for KJV's "once; twice"
       NET: "has declared one principle; two principles I" for KJV's "hath spoken once; twice"
       WEB: "belongs to" for KJV's "belongeth unto"

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Silence before God is strength, not weakness (v.1). In a world
   that rewards noise and hustle, choosing stillness before God is
   radical trust. It says "I don't need to manipulate outcomes —
   God is enough."

2. You may be shaken but you won't be destroyed (v.2). "Not
   GREATLY moved" — faith doesn't promise zero turbulence. It
   promises you won't be overthrown. The rock sways in the storm
   but doesn't crumble.

3. Pour out your heart without editing (v.8). God doesn't need
   polished prayers. He wants raw honesty. Pour it out — the
   anger, the fear, the confusion, the grief. He is big enough to
   receive it all.

4. No human being can bear the weight of being your God (v.9).
   Don't put people on God's throne — they are lighter than breath.
   People will disappoint. Only God can bear the weight of your
   ultimate trust.

5. God is both powerful AND merciful (vv.11-12). This is the
   gospel in two words. If God were only powerful, we would be
   terrified. If only merciful, we would doubt His ability. He is
   both — and that changes everything.
