Psalms - Chapter 20
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A royal psalm — a pre-battle prayer for the king: may the LORD answer you in the day of trouble, grant your heart's desire, and give victory; 'some trust in chariots, and some in horses: but we will remember the name of the LORD.'

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Royal/Community Prayer Psalm
Attributed Author: David — "To the chief Musician, A Psalm of
David"
Key Themes: Community praying for the king, trust in God's name
vs. military power, pre-battle liturgy, confidence in God's
anointed, salvation through divine intervention

Historical Context: Psalm 20 is a pre-battle liturgy — the people
pray for their king before he goes to war. It pairs with Psalm 21
(post-battle thanksgiving). The congregation addresses the king
(vv.1-5), then a single voice (prophet or king) declares
confidence (v.6), and the people affirm collective trust (vv.7-8).
The psalm's theology is explicitly anti-militaristic: "Some trust
in chariots, and some in horses: but we will remember the name of
the LORD" (v.7). Military success comes not from superior
equipment but from divine favor. This is the theology that sent
David against Goliath with stones instead of armor.

Structure:
- Congregational Prayer for the King (vv.1-5)
- Declaration of Confidence (v.6)
- Trust in God's Name vs. Military Power (vv.7-8)
- Final Prayer (v.9)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.5  — "We will rejoice in thy salvation, and in the name of our
        God we will set up our banners" (KJV) — The people
        anticipate victory and promise to credit God publicly.
        "Set up banners" = fly the victory flags in God's name,
        not the king's.

v.7  — "Some trust in chariots, and some in horses: but we will
        remember the name of the LORD our God." (KJV) — The
        psalm's theological center. Chariots and horses = the
        ancient world's ultimate military technology. Israel's
        trust is in a NAME — the revealed character of God. This
        is not anti-preparedness but anti-idolatry of human power.

v.8  — "They are brought down and fallen: but we are risen, and
        stand upright." (KJV) — The outcome of misplaced vs.
        rightly-placed trust. Those who trust technology collapse;
        those who trust God stand. The contrast is stark and
        absolute.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.1   — Psalm 46:1 — "God is our refuge and strength, a very
         present help in trouble." Same "day of trouble" refuge.
v.2   — 2 Chronicles 20:17 — "Stand ye still, and see the
         salvation of the LORD." Help from the sanctuary.
v.7   — Isaiah 31:1 — "Woe to them that go down to Egypt for help;
         and stay on horses." Same anti-militaristic theology.
v.7   — 1 Samuel 17:45 — "I come to thee in the name of the LORD
         of hosts." David vs. Goliath — the Name vs. weapons.
v.7   — Psalm 33:16-17 — "There is no king saved by the multitude
         of an host: a horse is a vain thing for safety." Same
         principle.
v.9   — Psalm 118:25 — "Save now, I beseech thee, O LORD."
         Similar cry for divine salvation.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1 — KJV: "The LORD hear thee" / ESV: "May the LORD answer you"
      — Both are jussive (wishes/prayers). ESV adds "may" to
      clarify the prayerful/wishful mood. The people are praying
      FOR their king, not describing what has happened.

v.3 — KJV: "accept thy burnt sacrifice" / ESV: "regard with favor
      your burnt sacrifices" — Hebrew "yedash'neha" = regard as
      fat/rich, accept favorably. Pre-battle sacrifice was
      standard practice; the prayer asks God to accept it.

v.7 — KJV: "we will remember the name" / ESV: "we trust in the
      name" — Hebrew "nazkir" = we will mention, invoke, remember,
      bring to mind. It implies both intellectual remembering and
      verbal invocation — calling on God's name as a battle cry.

v.9 — KJV: "Save, LORD: let the king hear us when we call" / ESV:
      "O LORD, save the king! May he answer us when we call" —
      Punctuation creates different readings. KJV: Save us, LORD;
      let the king be responsive. ESV: Save the king; may God
      answer us. The Hebrew can be divided either way; ESV's
      reading parallels the psalm's structure better.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.1 — ASV/WEB: "Jehovah"/"Yahweh" answer thee — Both use the divine name directly where KJV/ESV use "the LORD." ASV consistently renders YHWH as "Jehovah"; WEB uses the more linguistically accurate "Yahweh." This reveals what "the LORD" actually represents — God's covenant name.

- v.1 — NET: "May the Lord answer you when you are in trouble" — NET adds interpretive clarity with "when you are in trouble" rather than the more literal "in the day of trouble." The Hebrew "beyom tsarah" is literally "in the day of distress."

- v.5 — ASV/WEB: "We will triumph in thy salvation" / KJV: "We will rejoice in thy salvation" — Hebrew "narannenah" can mean to shout for joy, sing aloud, or triumph. ASV/WEB emphasize the triumphant shouting; NET renders it "shout for joy over your victory."

- v.6 — NET: "the Lord will deliver his chosen king" — NET interprets "his anointed" (mashiach) as "his chosen king," making the royal context explicit. KJV/ASV/WEB retain "his anointed," preserving the Messianic overtone of the Hebrew term.

- v.7 — WEB: "we trust the name of Yahweh our God" / ASV: "we will make mention of the name of Jehovah" / KJV: "we will remember the name of the LORD" — Three different renderings of Hebrew "nazkir." WEB interprets it as trust; ASV as verbal invocation ("make mention"); KJV as cognitive recall. The Hebrew encompasses all three — invoking, remembering, and trusting.

- v.8 — NET: "They will fall down, but we will stand firm" — NET simplifies and modernizes, losing the vivid contrast of the Hebrew. KJV's "brought down and fallen" vs. "risen and stand upright" better captures the bowing/rising imagery of the original.

- v.9 — NET: "The Lord will deliver the king; he will answer us when we call to him for help!" — NET completely restructures this verse as a confident declaration. KJV/ASV/WEB retain it as a prayer: "Save, LORD/Jehovah/Yahweh!" The different punctuation yields a different theology — prayer vs. confident assertion.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Pray for your leaders before the battle (vv.1-5). The
   congregation's role is intercession. Before the king faces
   danger, the people pray. Apply this to spiritual leadership,
   community leaders, and anyone facing a battle — pray before,
   not only during.

2. "The name of the LORD our God" is sufficient (v.7). Chariots =
   technology, resources, systems. Horses = speed, power,
   advantage. These are not wrong to have — but wrong to TRUST.
   The question is not "Do you have resources?" but "Where is your
   trust placed?"

3. The outcome proves the trust (v.8). Those who trust human power
   eventually collapse; those who trust God's name eventually
   stand. The timeline may vary, but the principle holds. Place
   your trust where the outcome confirms it.

4. Communal faith strengthens individual faith. This is a
   COLLECTIVE psalm — "we will remember," "we are risen." Faith
   is not only personal — it is corporate. Surround yourself with
   people who trust God's name rather than human chariots.

5. Credit God publicly for victories (v.5). "In the name of our
   God we will set up our banners" — when victory comes, fly
   God's flag. Public attribution prevents the pride that claims
   "my chariot delivered me."
