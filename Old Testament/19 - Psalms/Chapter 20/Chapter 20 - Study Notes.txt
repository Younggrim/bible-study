Psalms - Chapter 20
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A royal psalm — a pre-battle prayer for the king: may the LORD answer you in the day of trouble, grant your heart's desire, and give victory; 'some trust in chariots, and some in horses: but we will remember the name of the LORD.'

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Royal/Community Prayer Psalm
Attributed Author: David — "To the chief Musician, A Psalm of
David"
Key Themes: Community praying for the king, trust in God's name
vs. military power, pre-battle liturgy, confidence in God's
anointed, salvation through divine intervention

Historical Context: Psalm 20 is a pre-battle liturgy — the people
pray for their king before he goes to war. It pairs with Psalm 21
(post-battle thanksgiving). The congregation addresses the king
(vv.1-5), then a single voice (prophet or king) declares
confidence (v.6), and the people affirm collective trust (vv.7-8).
The psalm's theology is explicitly anti-militaristic: "Some trust
in chariots, and some in horses: but we will remember the name of
the LORD" (v.7). Military success comes not from superior
equipment but from divine favor. This is the theology that sent
David against Goliath with stones instead of armor.

Structure:
- Congregational Prayer for the King (vv.1-5)
- Declaration of Confidence (v.6)
- Trust in God's Name vs. Military Power (vv.7-8)
- Final Prayer (v.9)


CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.1   — Psalm 46:1 — "God is our refuge and strength, a very
         present help in trouble." Same "day of trouble" refuge.
v.2   — 2 Chronicles 20:17 — "Stand ye still, and see the
         salvation of the LORD." Help from the sanctuary.
v.7   — Isaiah 31:1 — "Woe to them that go down to Egypt for help;
         and stay on horses." Same anti-militaristic theology.
v.7   — 1 Samuel 17:45 — "I come to thee in the name of the LORD
         of hosts." David vs. Goliath — the Name vs. weapons.
v.7   — Psalm 33:16-17 — "There is no king saved by the multitude
         of an host: a horse is a vain thing for safety." Same
         principle.
v.9   — Psalm 118:25 — "Save now, I beseech thee, O LORD."
         Similar cry for divine salvation.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Pray for your leaders before the battle (vv.1-5). The
   congregation's role is intercession. Before the king faces
   danger, the people pray. Apply this to spiritual leadership,
   community leaders, and anyone facing a battle — pray before,
   not only during.

2. "The name of the LORD our God" is sufficient (v.7). Chariots =
   technology, resources, systems. Horses = speed, power,
   advantage. These are not wrong to have — but wrong to TRUST.
   The question is not "Do you have resources?" but "Where is your
   trust placed?"

3. The outcome proves the trust (v.8). Those who trust human power
   eventually collapse; those who trust God's name eventually
   stand. The timeline may vary, but the principle holds. Place
   your trust where the outcome confirms it.

4. Communal faith strengthens individual faith. This is a
   COLLECTIVE psalm — "we will remember," "we are risen." Faith
   is not only personal — it is corporate. Surround yourself with
   people who trust God's name rather than human chariots.

5. Credit God publicly for victories (v.5). "In the name of our
   God we will set up our banners" — when victory comes, fly
   God's flag. Public attribution prevents the pride that claims
   "my chariot delivered me."
