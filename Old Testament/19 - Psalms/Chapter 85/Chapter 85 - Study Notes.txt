Psalms - Chapter 85
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A communal prayer for revival — God formerly restored Jacob's fortunes; now the people plead for renewal: 'Wilt thou not revive us again?' Mercy and truth meet, righteousness and peace kiss each other.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Community Lament / Prophetic Psalm
Attributed Author: Sons of Korah
Key Themes: Past restoration recalled, present distress, prayer
for revival, mercy and truth meeting, righteousness and peace
kissing — the cross foreshadowed

Historical Context: This psalm likely dates to the post-exilic
period — after the return from Babylon. God HAD restored them
(vv.1-3), but the present reality is disappointing (vv.4-7). The
return was real but incomplete — the land was still troubled, the
people still struggling. The psalm asks for full revival. Its
theological climax (v.10) is one of the most Christological verses
in the OT: "Mercy and truth are met together; righteousness and
peace have kissed each other." In Christ, God's mercy (which wants
to forgive) and truth (which demands accountability) meet. His
righteousness (which requires judgment) and peace (which desires
reconciliation) kiss. The cross is where these attributes perfectly
unite.

Structure:
- Past Restoration Acknowledged (vv.1-3)
- Present Plea for Revival (vv.4-7)
- Prophetic Listening (v.8)
- The Vision: God's Attributes United (vv.9-13)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.6  — "Wilt thou not revive us again: that thy people may rejoice
        in thee?" (KJV) — The prayer for revival. "Revive" (chayah)
        = to make alive again, to restore to life. The purpose of
        revival: not excitement but REJOICING IN GOD. Revival is
        God-centered joy restored.

v.10 — "Mercy and truth are met together; righteousness and peace
        have kissed each other." (KJV) — Perhaps the most profound
        verse in Book III. Four divine attributes that seem to
        conflict find perfect unity. Mercy forgives; Truth accuses.
        Righteousness condemns; Peace reconciles. Only at the cross
        do all four perfectly meet and embrace.

v.11 — "Truth shall spring out of the earth; and righteousness
        shall look down from heaven." (KJV) — When God's restoration
        comes, truth sprouts from below (human faithfulness) and
        righteousness rains from above (divine blessing). Heaven
        and earth cooperate in redemption.

v.13 — "Righteousness shall go before him; and shall set us in the
        way of his steps." (KJV) — Righteousness becomes a
        pathfinder — going before God and preparing a road for His
        people to follow. Where God walks, righteousness has
        already paved the way.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.1   — Ezra 1:1-4 — Cyrus's decree releasing the Jews from
         Babylon. The restoration referenced.
v.6   — Habakkuk 3:2 — "O LORD, revive thy work in the midst of
         the years." Same revival prayer.
v.10  — Romans 3:25-26 — God is both "just and the justifier" —
         the NT explanation of how mercy and truth meet in Christ.

         the world unto himself." The kiss of righteousness and
         peace in action.

         of them that make peace."

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1 — KJV: "thou hast brought back the captivity of Jacob" / ESV:
      "you restored the fortunes of Jacob" — Same Hebrew phrase as
      in Psalm 53:6. "Restored fortunes" (ESV) is broader than
      "brought back captivity" and may better capture the full
      scope of restoration.

v.10 — KJV: "Mercy and truth" / ESV: "Steadfast love and
       faithfulness" — Hebrew "hesed" and "emeth." ESV's pairing
       is more precise: hesed = covenant loyalty/steadfast love;
       emeth = faithfulness/reliability. Both translations capture
       aspects of these rich Hebrew terms.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.7 — KJV: "Shew us thy mercy" / ASV: "Show us thy lovingkindness" / WEB: "Show us your loving kindness" / NET: "show us your loyal love" — The Hebrew חֶסֶד (chesed) appears here: KJV renders "mercy," ASV "lovingkindness," WEB "loving kindness," NET "loyal love." Each captures a different dimension of God's covenant faithfulness.

- v.8 — ASV: "I will hear what God Jehovah will speak" / WEB: "I will hear what God, Yahweh, will speak" / NET: "I will listen to what God the Lord says" — The full divine title "God YHWH" (El Yahweh): ASV/WEB retain the name; KJV: "God the LORD"; NET: "God the Lord." This is the psalmist pausing to listen for God's oracle.

- v.10 — NET: "Loyal love and faithfulness meet; deliverance and peace greet each other with a kiss" — NET renders חֶסֶד (chesed) as "loyal love" and צֶדֶק (tsedeq) as "deliverance" rather than "righteousness." This dramatically changes the theology — "deliverance" suggests rescue, while "righteousness" suggests moral perfection. The traditional reading (KJV/ASV/WEB) better preserves the cross-theology.

- v.6 — ASV: "Wilt thou not quicken us again" / KJV: "revive us" / NET: "revive us once more" / WEB: "revive us again" — ASV retains "quicken" (= make alive), an archaic form. NET adds "once more" for emphasis not in the Hebrew.

- v.12 — ASV: "Yea, Jehovah will give that which is good" / WEB: "Yes, Yahweh will give that which is good" / NET: "the Lord will bestow his good blessings" — NET adds "blessings" (not in Hebrew). KJV/ASV/WEB preserve the simple Hebrew: God gives "the good" (הַטּוֹב) — undefined, unlimited good.

- v.13 — NET: "Deliverance goes before him, and prepares a pathway for him" / KJV: "Righteousness shall go before him" — Again NET renders "righteousness" as "deliverance," losing the moral/ethical dimension. WEB: "Righteousness goes before him" stays literal.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Revival is needed when past blessing has faded (vv.1-6). The
   return from exile was real but incomplete. Spiritual
   experiences in the past don't guarantee vibrancy in the
   present. If your faith has cooled, pray for revival — God can
   make you alive again.

2. At the cross, mercy and truth meet (v.10). This is the gospel
   in one verse. How can God be merciful (forgiving sin) AND
   truthful (punishing sin)? Only through Christ — who bore
   truth's demand so mercy could flow. The cross is where the
   conflict resolves.

3. Righteousness and peace are not contradictions (v.10). They
   kiss — they are intimate allies, not enemies. True peace is
   built on righteousness, not on ignoring sin. And true
   righteousness leads to peace, not to perpetual conflict.

4. Revival means rejoicing IN GOD (v.6). Not just excitement,
   not just activity — rejoicing in GOD Himself. The measure of
   revival is how much your joy is centered on God rather than on
   experiences about God.
