Psalms - Chapter 130
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A Song of Ascents (penitential psalm) — 'Out of the depths have I cried unto thee, O LORD'; if He marked iniquities, none could stand; but there is forgiveness with Him; 'I wait for the LORD, my soul doth wait.'

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Song of Ascents / Penitential Psalm (De Profundis)
Attributed Author: Anonymous
Key Themes: Crying from the depths, God's forgiveness, waiting
on God, hope in God's word, plenteous redemption, the fear that
comes from forgiveness

Historical Context: Psalm 130 is the sixth of the seven
Penitential Psalms (6, 32, 38, 51, 102, 130, 143) and one of the
most profound in the Psalter. Known by its Latin name "De
Profundis" ("Out of the depths"), it has been prayed at deathbeds,
funerals, and in times of spiritual anguish for two millennia.
Martin Luther identified this as one of the "Pauline psalms"
(along with 32, 51, and 143) because of its emphasis on
forgiveness by grace, not merit. The psalm moves from the depths
(v.1) upward through forgiveness (vv.3-4), waiting (vv.5-6), to
confident hope for all Israel (vv.7-8). It is a complete gospel
in miniature: sin acknowledged, grace received, hope restored.

Structure:
- Cry from the Depths (vv.1-2)
- Forgiveness as the Basis of Fear (vv.3-4)
- Waiting on God's Word (vv.5-6)
- Hope for Complete Redemption (vv.7-8)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "Out of the depths have I cried unto thee, O LORD." (KJV)
        — "The depths" (Hebrew "ma'amaqqim") = the deep places,
        the abyss. Not a casual prayer but a cry from the bottom.
        Rock bottom is a valid prayer position. God hears from
        the depths.

v.3-4 — "If thou, LORD, shouldest mark iniquities, O Lord, who
        shall stand? But there is forgiveness with thee, that thou
        mayest be feared." (KJV) — Two revolutionary truths:
        (1) If God kept score, NO ONE could stand. Not the best
        person alive. (2) Forgiveness produces FEAR (reverence),
        not presumption. We fear God more BECAUSE He forgives, not
        less. Grace inspires awe.

v.5-6 — "I wait for the LORD, my soul doth wait, and in his word
        do I hope. My soul waiteth for the Lord more than they
        that watch for the morning." (KJV) — The intensity of
        waiting: more than night watchmen long for dawn. When
        you've been in darkness all night, you ACHE for morning.
        That's how the psalmist waits for God.

v.7  — "With the LORD there is mercy, and with him is plenteous
        redemption." (KJV) — Not scarce redemption, not reluctant
        mercy — PLENTEOUS. Abundant. Overflowing. Whatever your
        depth, His redemption is deeper still.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.3 — KJV: "mark iniquities" / ESV: "kept a record of sins" —
      Hebrew "shamar avonot" = to watch/guard/keep iniquities. If
      God maintained a permanent record of every sin, prosecution
      would be absolute and universal. But He doesn't. This is
      the gospel.

v.4 — "that thou mayest be feared" — Counterintuitive. Forgiveness
      leads to fear? Yes — the proper response to undeserved grace
      is not casual familiarity but profound reverence. Cheap grace
      produces presumption; true grace produces holy awe.

v.6 — "more than they that watch for the morning" — Night watchmen
      (soldiers, city guards) long desperately for dawn — for
      relief, for safety, for the end of darkness. The psalmist's
      longing for God exceeds even this.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.1 — KJV: "Out of the depths have I cried unto thee, O LORD" / ASV: "O Jehovah" / WEB: "Yahweh" / NET: "From the deep water I cry out to you, O Lord" — NET makes "depths" (Hebrew "ma'amaqqim") concrete as "deep water," losing the metaphorical ambiguity (depths of despair, Sheol, waters of chaos).
- v.3 — WEB: "If you, Yah, kept a record of sins" — WEB uniquely uses the shortened "Yah" here. NET: "If you, O Lord, were to keep track of sins" — adds "track" for emphasis.
- v.4 — KJV: "that thou mayest be feared" / WEB: "therefore you are feared" / NET: "so that you might be honored" — NET replaces "feared" with "honored" — a significant theological choice. Hebrew "yare" (fear) is central to the psalm's meaning: forgiveness produces reverence, not casual familiarity.
- v.5 — NET: "I rely on the Lord. I rely on him with my whole being; I wait for his assuring word" — Significantly expands KJV's "I wait for the LORD, my soul doth wait, and in his word do I hope." NET changes "wait" to "rely" and adds "assuring" to "word."
- v.7 — KJV: "with the LORD there is mercy" / ASV: "with Jehovah there is lovingkindness" / WEB: "with Yahweh there is loving kindness" / NET: "the Lord exhibits loyal love" — Classic chesed rendering. NET's "exhibits" adds the idea of active demonstration.
- v.7 — KJV: "plenteous redemption" / ASV: "plenteous redemption" / WEB: "abundant redemption" / NET: "is more than willing to deliver" — NET dramatically paraphrases, turning "plenteous redemption" (a noun) into an expression of God's willingness (attitude).

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Cry from where you are (v.1). You don't need to clean up
   before praying. The depths are a valid starting point. God
   doesn't require you to climb up first — He hears from the
   abyss.

2. If God kept score, no one would stand (v.3). This levels every
   person. The "good" person and the "bad" person are equally
   unable to stand before God's record-keeping. Only grace
   differentiates.

3. Forgiveness produces reverence (v.4). If you find yourself
   taking God's grace lightly, you haven't understood it yet.
   True forgiveness produces not carelessness but awe — "that
   thou mayest be FEARED."

4. Wait like a watchman for morning (v.6). If you're in a dark
   night of the soul, remember: morning WILL come. Wait for it
   with the certainty of dawn. God's faithfulness is as sure as
   sunrise.

5. Plenteous redemption (v.7). Whatever your sin, God's
   redemption is more. Not barely enough, not reluctantly given —
   PLENTEOUS. He will redeem Israel from ALL iniquities (v.8).
   All. Not some. All.
