Psalms - Chapter 103
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A hymn of praise — 'Bless the LORD, O my soul, and forget not all his benefits'; God forgives, heals, redeems, crowns with lovingkindness; 'as a father pitieth his children,' knowing we are dust; His mercy is from everlasting to everlasting.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Hymn of Praise / Thanksgiving Psalm
Attributed Author: David (superscription: "A Psalm of David")
Key Themes: God's compassion and forgiveness, the fatherhood of
God, removal of sin, human frailty and God's eternal love,
commanding one's soul to worship, God's sovereign rule over all

Historical Context: Psalm 103 is one of the most beloved psalms
in all of Scripture — a comprehensive celebration of God's
character, especially His compassion and forgiveness. It begins
and ends with "Bless the LORD, O my soul" — David commands
himself to praise, then expands that call to angels, hosts, all
creation, and back to himself. Unlike most praise psalms that
focus on what God HAS DONE (historical acts), Psalm 103 focuses
primarily on what God IS LIKE (His character). It contains no
petitions, no complaints, no enemies — pure praise from beginning
to end. The psalm echoes God's self-revelation in Exodus 34:6-7
(v.8) and applies it personally. Many consider this the greatest
psalm of praise in the Psalter.

Structure:
- Self-Exhortation to Praise (vv.1-2)
- Personal Benefits from God (vv.3-5)
- God's Character Revealed (vv.6-10)
- The Greatness of God's Love (vv.11-14)
- Human Frailty vs. Everlasting Love (vv.15-18)
- God's Universal Sovereignty (vv.19-22)


CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.1   — Psalm 104:1 — "Bless the LORD, O my soul." Companion
         psalm, same opening.
v.3   — Isaiah 53:5 — "With his stripes we are healed." The
         ultimate healing and forgiveness through Christ.
v.8   — Exodus 34:6 — God's self-proclamation to Moses —
         virtually identical wording.
v.10  — Ezra 9:13 — "Thou... hast punished us less than our
         iniquities deserve."
v.12  — Micah 7:19 — "Thou wilt cast all their sins into the
         depths of the sea."
v.14  — Genesis 2:7 — "The LORD God formed man of the dust of the
         ground." We are dust; He remembers.
v.15  — Isaiah 40:6-8 — "All flesh is grass... but the word of
         our God shall stand for ever."
v.19  — Daniel 4:34-35 — "His dominion is an everlasting
         dominion... he doeth according to his will."

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------

REFLECTION
------------------------------------
1. Command your soul to praise (vv.1-2). Don't wait until you
   feel like worshiping. Tell your soul what to do. Preach truth
   to yourself. "Forget not ALL his benefits" — make a list if
   you must, but do not let forgetfulness steal your praise.

2. Five benefits to remember (vv.3-5): He forgives ALL iniquity,
   heals ALL diseases, redeems from destruction, crowns with love,
   and satisfies with good. Not one or two benefits — a cascade
   of grace upon grace.

3. God doesn't treat you as your sins deserve (v.10). This is the
   gospel in one verse. If God gave what you earned, you'd be
   lost. But He doesn't. His mercy overwhelms His justice toward
   those who fear Him.

4. He knows you're dust (v.14). God is not disappointed in your
   limitations. He made you from dust and remembers it. His
   expectations are fatherly and compassionate, not impossible
   and cruel.

5. From self to all creation (vv.1-22). The psalm begins with
   "O my soul" and expands to angels, hosts, all works, all
   places — then returns to "O my soul." Personal praise
   becomes cosmic praise becomes personal again. You are part of
   the universal chorus.
