Psalms - Chapter 66
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A communal thanksgiving psalm — 'Make a joyful noise unto God, all ye lands'; the psalmist recounts God's mighty works (the Red Sea crossing), acknowledges testing like silver refined, and offers praise for answered prayer.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Hymn of Praise / Thanksgiving Psalm
Attributed Author: Anonymous (no named author in superscription)
Key Themes: Universal praise, God's awesome deeds, testing and
refining through trials, personal testimony of answered prayer,
the condition for being heard by God

Historical Context: This psalm has no named author and may
commemorate a specific national deliverance (possibly from the
Assyrian threat — 2 Kings 19). It uniquely combines community
praise (vv.1-12) with individual thanksgiving (vv.13-20). The
transition from "we" to "I" at v.13 is distinctive — the community
celebration of God's deliverance leads to one voice offering
personal testimony. The psalm may have been used liturgically when
an individual brought thank offerings to the temple, incorporating
the congregation's praise with personal vows. The "fire and water"
of v.12 likely refers to severe national trial, while the "wealthy
place" (or "place of abundance") marks God's deliverance.

Structure:
- Call to Universal Praise (vv.1-4)
- God's Awesome Deeds Recalled (vv.5-7)
- Praise for Preservation Through Testing (vv.8-12)
- Personal Vow and Offering (vv.13-15)
- Personal Testimony (vv.16-20)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.10 — "For thou, O God, hast proved us: thou hast tried us, as
        silver is tried." (KJV) — God's testing is purposeful —
        like a silversmith refining metal. The fire is not
        punishment but purification. God puts His people through
        trials to remove impurities and prove genuineness.

v.12 — "We went through fire and through water: but thou
        broughtest us out into a wealthy place." (KJV) — The
        pattern of biblical suffering: THROUGH fire, THROUGH
        water, but OUT into abundance. God doesn't promise to
        keep us FROM trials but to bring us THROUGH them to
        something better.

v.18 — "If I regard iniquity in my heart, the Lord will not hear
        me." (KJV) — A sobering condition for prayer. "Regard"
        (ra'ah) = to look upon with favor, to cherish. The issue
        is not sinlessness but harboring sin — clinging to known
        sin while asking God to listen.

v.20 — "Blessed be God, which hath not turned away my prayer, nor
        his mercy from me." (KJV) — The joyful conclusion: God
        DID hear, because the psalmist DID NOT cherish sin. The
        link between v.18 and v.20 is the key: clean conscience =
        heard prayer.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1 — KJV: "Make a joyful noise" / ESV: "Shout for joy" — Hebrew
      "hari'u" = shout, blast (like a trumpet). Both capture the
      exuberance; ESV's "shout for joy" is more natural in modern
      English.

v.3 — KJV: "How terrible art thou in thy works" / ESV: "How
      awesome are your deeds" — "Terrible" (nora) in 1611 meant
      "inspiring awe/terror." Modern "terrible" means "bad." ESV's
      "awesome" (in its original sense) is more accurate for
      modern readers.

v.12 — KJV: "a wealthy place" / ESV: "a place of abundance" —
       Hebrew "revayah" = abundance, overflow, saturation. ESV is
       clearer; the point is overflow and abundance, not merely
       material wealth.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.3 — NET: "How awesome are your deeds! Because of your great power your enemies cower in fear before you" — NET adds "cower in fear" (not in Hebrew "submit themselves"). KJV: "thine enemies submit themselves unto thee" — the Hebrew "kachash" actually means "feign obedience/cringe" suggesting forced/insincere submission.

- v.5 — NET: "Come and witness God's exploits! His acts on behalf of people are awesome" — NET uses "exploits" for "works" (ma'asim), adding adventure connotation. WEB: "Come, and see God's deeds— awesome work on behalf of the children of men."

- v.12 — NET: "you brought us out into a wide open place" / WEB: "you brought us to the place of abundance" vs. KJV: "thou broughtest us out into a wealthy place" — NET interprets "revayah" spatially (wide place = freedom); WEB interprets materially (abundance). Both are possible from Hebrew root meaning "overflow/saturation."

- v.18 — NET: "If I had harbored sin in my heart" / WEB: "If I cherished sin in my heart" vs. KJV: "If I regard iniquity in my heart" — NET's "harbored" and WEB's "cherished" both convey deliberate retention of known sin. These modern terms are more vivid than KJV's "regard."

- v.20 — ASV: "Nor his lovingkindness from me" / WEB: "nor his loving kindness from me" / NET: "or abandon his love for me" vs. KJV: "nor his mercy from me" — The "chesed" rendering: ASV "lovingkindness," WEB "loving kindness," NET "his love" (dropping the covenantal aspect). KJV "mercy."

- v.1 — NET: "Shout out praise to God, all the earth!" vs. KJV: "Make a joyful noise unto God, all ye lands" — NET modernizes "joyful noise" to "shout out praise," losing the exuberant spontaneity of noise-making. WEB: "Make a joyful shout to God, all the earth!"

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. God's testing is refining, not punishing (v.10). Silver is
   tried by fire not to destroy it but to purify it. When God
   allows trials, He is working toward a purer you, not a
   destroyed you.

2. The path to abundance goes THROUGH fire and water (v.12). There
   is no shortcut to the "wealthy place." The through-ness of
   trial is the pathway to blessing. Don't demand exemption from
   the process.

3. Cherished sin blocks prayer (v.18). The issue is not perfection
   but direction. If you are clinging to known sin — defending it,
   hiding it, refusing to release it — don't expect God to respond
   to your prayers. Let it go, and the line is clear again.

4. Testimony is powerful (v.16). "Come and hear... what he hath
   done for my soul." Personal testimony of God's faithfulness is
   one of the most convincing forms of witness. Tell your story.
