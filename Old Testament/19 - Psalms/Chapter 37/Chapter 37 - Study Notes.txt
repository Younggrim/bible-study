Psalms - Chapter 37
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
An acrostic wisdom psalm — 'fret not thyself because of evildoers'; trust in the LORD and do good, delight in Him and He will give you your heart's desires; the meek shall inherit the earth.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Wisdom Psalm / Acrostic (Alphabetic) Psalm
Attributed Author: David ("A Psalm of David")
Key Themes: "Fret not" (repeated), trust and delight in the LORD,
commit your way to God, the meek inheriting the earth, the
righteous not forsaken, the brevity of the wicked's prosperity

Historical Context: Psalm 37 is David's mature wisdom — likely
written in old age (v.25: "I have been young, and now am old").
It reads more like Proverbs than a typical psalm, offering
practical, experience-tested wisdom about the perennial question:
why do the wicked prosper while the righteous suffer?

This is an acrostic psalm with each strophe (roughly every other
verse) beginning with successive letters of the Hebrew alphabet.
The acrostic form gives the psalm a measured, reflective quality
— this is not passionate outburst but careful, deliberate
instruction built on a lifetime of observation.

The psalm's central concern is the temptation to "fret" (charah —
to burn with anxiety or anger) at the apparent prosperity of the
wicked. David's answer is not philosophical but practical: trust,
delight, commit, rest (vv.3-7). The theological foundation is
that God's timeline differs from ours. The wicked flourish
temporarily ("like a green bay tree," v.35) but perish utterly,
while the righteous enjoy permanent inheritance. Jesus draws from
this psalm in the Beatitudes: "Blessed are the meek: for they
shall inherit the earth" (Matthew 5:5, quoting Psalm 37:11).

Structure:
- Do Not Fret: Trust Instead (vv. 1-11)
- The Wicked Plot but God Laughs (vv. 12-15)
- The Righteous vs. the Wicked: Contrasts (vv. 16-22)
- God Orders the Steps of the Righteous (vv. 23-26)
- Practical Exhortation: Depart from Evil (vv. 27-31)
- The Wicked Watch but God Protects (vv. 32-36)
- Final Contrast: The End of Both (vv. 37-40)


CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.1 — Proverbs 24:19 — 20: "Fret not thyself because of evil men" — nearly identical wisdom, showing Psalm 37's connection to the Proverbs tradition.
v.4 — Psalm 16:11; Philippians 4:4: Delighting in God as the supreme pleasure — the Psalms' version of what Paul calls "rejoicing in the Lord always."
v.5 — Proverbs 16:3; 1 Peter 5:7: Rolling/committing our way to God and casting all our cares upon Him — the same principle from wisdom literature to apostolic instruction.
v.9,11,22,29,34 — Matthew 5:5: "The meek shall inherit the earth" — Jesus drew this Beatitude directly from Psalm 37's repeated promise about inheriting the land.
v.21 — Deuteronomy 15:6; Proverbs 22:7: The righteous as generous givers versus the wicked as debtors — financial conduct as a marker of spiritual condition.
v.23 — Proverbs 16:9; Proverbs 20:24; Jeremiah 10:23: Man's steps ordered by God — human agency within divine sovereignty.
v.25 — Psalm 34:10; Philippians 4:19: The righteous not forsaken or begging bread — God's faithful provision across generations.
v.35-36 — Psalm 73:17 — 20: The sudden disappearance of the wicked who once seemed permanently rooted — Asaph's extended meditation on the same truth David states here.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------

REFLECTION
------------------------------------
1. "Fret not" appears three times (vv.1,7,8) — indicating this is
a persistent temptation. Fretting is the anxiety of comparing your
situation to others' and concluding God is unfair. What specific
situations tempt you to fret — a colleague's promotion, an
unbeliever's health, an unjust person's success?

2. The four imperatives of verses 3-7 form a spiritual discipline:
Trust (lean your weight on God), Do good (act rightly regardless
of outcome), Delight (find your supreme pleasure in God), Commit
(roll your burden onto Him), Rest/Be still (cease striving).
Which of these is most difficult for you, and why?

3. "I have been young, and now am old; yet have I not seen the
righteous forsaken" (v.25). This is testimony from decades of
observation. While individual moments may feel like abandonment,
the long view reveals faithfulness. If you are young, trust the
testimony of the aged. If you are old, bear witness to God's
faithfulness to those coming behind you.

4. "Though he fall, he shall not be utterly cast down" (v.24).
Falling is not failing permanently. The righteous fall — David
certainly did — but the difference is that God's hand catches and
restores. Have you allowed a fall to define you rather than
experiencing God's restorative grip?

5. The wicked flourish "like a green bay tree" (v.35) — then
vanish completely (v.36). Envy is always based on a snapshot,
never the full story. What looks like permanent success is often
temporary display. Are you evaluating others' lives (and your own)
by snapshots or by endings?
