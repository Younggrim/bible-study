Psalms - Chapter 37
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
An acrostic wisdom psalm — 'fret not thyself because of evildoers'; trust in the LORD and do good, delight in Him and He will give you your heart's desires; the meek shall inherit the earth.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Wisdom Psalm / Acrostic (Alphabetic) Psalm
Attributed Author: David ("A Psalm of David")
Key Themes: "Fret not" (repeated), trust and delight in the LORD,
commit your way to God, the meek inheriting the earth, the
righteous not forsaken, the brevity of the wicked's prosperity

Historical Context: Psalm 37 is David's mature wisdom — likely
written in old age (v.25: "I have been young, and now am old").
It reads more like Proverbs than a typical psalm, offering
practical, experience-tested wisdom about the perennial question:
why do the wicked prosper while the righteous suffer?

This is an acrostic psalm with each strophe (roughly every other
verse) beginning with successive letters of the Hebrew alphabet.
The acrostic form gives the psalm a measured, reflective quality
— this is not passionate outburst but careful, deliberate
instruction built on a lifetime of observation.

The psalm's central concern is the temptation to "fret" (charah —
to burn with anxiety or anger) at the apparent prosperity of the
wicked. David's answer is not philosophical but practical: trust,
delight, commit, rest (vv.3-7). The theological foundation is
that God's timeline differs from ours. The wicked flourish
temporarily ("like a green bay tree," v.35) but perish utterly,
while the righteous enjoy permanent inheritance. Jesus draws from
this psalm in the Beatitudes: "Blessed are the meek: for they
shall inherit the earth" (Matthew 5:5, quoting Psalm 37:11).

Structure:
- Do Not Fret: Trust Instead (vv. 1-11)
- The Wicked Plot but God Laughs (vv. 12-15)
- The Righteous vs. the Wicked: Contrasts (vv. 16-22)
- God Orders the Steps of the Righteous (vv. 23-26)
- Practical Exhortation: Depart from Evil (vv. 27-31)
- The Wicked Watch but God Protects (vv. 32-36)
- Final Contrast: The End of Both (vv. 37-40)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
Verse 4 — "Delight thyself also in the LORD; and he shall give
thee the desires of thine heart." (KJV)
Often misread as a blank check for wishes. The key is the first
clause: when we genuinely delight in God Himself, our desires are
transformed to align with His will. The promise is not "get
whatever you want" but "God becomes what you want." Delighting in
the Giver shapes what we desire from Him.

Verse 5 — "Commit thy way unto the LORD; trust also in him; and
he shall bring it to pass." (KJV)
"Commit" (galal) literally means "roll" — roll your way onto the
LORD like rolling a heavy burden off your shoulders onto someone
stronger. The imagery is visceral: stop carrying what you were
never meant to bear. Trust (batach) means to lean your full weight
upon something.

Verse 11 — "But the meek shall inherit the earth; and shall
delight themselves in the abundance of peace." (KJV)
Quoted by Jesus in the third Beatitude (Matthew 5:5). The "meek"
(anav) are not weak but submitted — those who trust God's power
rather than asserting their own. The inheritance is both present
(contentment, peace) and eschatological (the new earth).

Verse 23-24 — "The steps of a good man are ordered by the LORD:
and he delighteth in his way. Though he fall, he shall not be
utterly cast down: for the LORD upholdeth him with his hand." (KJV)
Two truths: God directs every step (nothing is random or wasted),
and falling is not final. The righteous may stumble — this is
acknowledged honestly — but they are not destroyed because God's
hand catches them. The "good man" is not sinless but held.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.1 — Proverbs 24:19-20: "Fret not thyself because of evil men"
— nearly identical wisdom, showing Psalm 37's connection to the
Proverbs tradition.

v.4 — Psalm 16:11; Philippians 4:4: Delighting in God as the
supreme pleasure — the Psalms' version of what Paul calls
"rejoicing in the Lord always."

v.5 — Proverbs 16:3; 1 Peter 5:7: Rolling/committing our way to
God and casting all our cares upon Him — the same principle from
wisdom literature to apostolic instruction.

v.9,11,22,29,34 — Matthew 5:5: "The meek shall inherit the
earth" — Jesus drew this Beatitude directly from Psalm 37's
repeated promise about inheriting the land.

v.21 — Deuteronomy 15:6; Proverbs 22:7: The righteous as
generous givers versus the wicked as debtors — financial conduct
as a marker of spiritual condition.

v.23 — Proverbs 16:9; Proverbs 20:24; Jeremiah 10:23: Man's
steps ordered by God — human agency within divine sovereignty.

v.25 — Psalm 34:10; Philippians 4:19: The righteous not forsaken
or begging bread — God's faithful provision across generations.

v.35-36 — Psalm 73:17-20: The sudden disappearance of the wicked
who once seemed permanently rooted — Asaph's extended meditation
on the same truth David states here.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.3 — "verily thou shalt be fed" (KJV) / "befriend faithfulness"
(ESV): A significant translation difference. Hebrew re'eh emunah
can mean "feed on faithfulness" (KJV's reading — you will be
nourished/fed) or "tend faithfulness" (ESV's reading — cultivate
faithfulness as a companion). The Septuagint supports "be fed"
(pasture on faithfulness). Both readings are theologically rich.

v.7 — "Rest in the LORD" (KJV) / "Be still before the LORD"
(ESV): Hebrew dom means to be silent, still, quiet. KJV's "rest"
suggests cessation of anxiety; ESV's "be still" emphasizes
quieting oneself in God's presence. Both capture aspects of dom —
the inner posture of trusting silence before a sovereign God.

v.11 — "the meek shall inherit the earth" (KJV) / "the meek
shall inherit the land" (ESV): Hebrew eretz can mean either
"earth" (the whole world) or "land" (specifically the promised
land of Israel). In its original context, David likely meant
Canaan. Jesus universalized it to the whole earth in the
Beatitudes. Both readings are valid at different levels.

v.35 — "spreading himself like a green bay tree" (KJV) / "like a
green laurel tree" (ESV): Hebrew ezrach ra'anan — "a native tree,
luxuriant/green." KJV's "bay tree" is a traditional rendering; ESV
uses "laurel tree." Some Hebrew manuscripts read "the cedars of
Lebanon." The image is of a deeply rooted, flourishing native tree
that appears permanently established.

v.37 — "Mark the perfect man" (KJV) / "Mark the blameless" (ESV):
Hebrew tam means complete, whole, having integrity — not sinless
perfection. ESV's "blameless" better conveys the sense: a person
of integrity whose life is consistent and whole, not one who has
never sinned.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.3 — WEB: "Trust in Yahweh, and do good. Dwell in the land, and enjoy
  safe pasture" — "enjoy safe pasture" interprets "re'eh emunah" with
  pastoral imagery (sheep feeding safely). ASV: "feed on his faithfulness" —
  closest to KJV, making faithfulness the nourishment. NET: "Settle in the
  land and maintain your integrity" — most distant from the Hebrew, rendering
  "emunah" as personal integrity rather than God's faithfulness.
- v.4 — NET: "Then you will take delight in the Lord, and he will answer your
  prayers" — "answer your prayers" interprets "give thee the desires of thine
  heart" (KJV) as prayer-answering rather than desire-fulfilling. WEB/ASV:
  "he will give you the desires of your heart" — straightforward rendering.
- v.5 — NET: "Commit your future to the Lord" — "your future" interprets
  "thy way" (KJV/ESV/ASV/WEB) as forward-looking life-planning. Hebrew
  "derek" (way/path) encompasses all of life's direction. WEB: "Commit your
  way to Yahweh" — standard rendering with divine name.
- v.7 — NET: "Wait patiently for the Lord! Wait confidently for him!" — adds
  "confidently" (not in Hebrew) to characterize the quality of waiting. ASV:
  "Rest in Jehovah, and wait patiently for him" — matches KJV structure. WEB:
  "Rest in Yahweh" — same as ASV/KJV with divine name explicit.
- v.9,11 — NET: "possess the land" throughout / KJV: "inherit the earth" —
  Hebrew "yareshu eretz" — NET consistently uses "land" (local promise); KJV
  uses "earth" (universal scope). WEB: "inherit the land." ASV: "inherit the
  land." Jesus quotes v.11 in Matthew 5:5 ("the meek shall inherit the
  earth") — universalizing the promise. NET: "the oppressed will possess the
  land" — renders "anavim" (meek/humble) as "oppressed," emphasizing their
  social condition rather than their character.
- v.20 — NET: "the Lord's enemies will be incinerated—they will go up in
  smoke" — vivid and violent. KJV/ASV: "as the fat of lambs: they shall
  consume; into smoke shall they consume away" — sacrificial fat imagery
  (burning on the altar). WEB: "They will vanish—vanish like smoke" —
  emphasizes disappearance. The image is either sacrificial burning or simply
  dissolution into nothing.
- v.23 — NET: "The Lord grants success to the one whose behavior he finds
  commendable" — highly interpretive. KJV: "The steps of a good man are
  ordered by the LORD: and he delighteth in his way." ASV/WEB: "A man's
  goings are established by Jehovah/Yahweh. He delights in his way" — the
  ambiguity is whether God delights in the man's way, or the man delights in
  God's way. Most translations preserve this beautiful ambiguity.
- v.35 — NET: "I have seen ruthless, wicked people growing in influence, like
  a green tree grows in its native soil" — "growing in influence" interprets
  the tree metaphor socially. ASV/WEB: "spreading himself like a green tree
  in its native soil" — preserves the organic imagery. KJV: "like a green bay
  tree" — the specific "bay tree" identification.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Fret not" appears three times (vv.1,7,8) — indicating this is
a persistent temptation. Fretting is the anxiety of comparing your
situation to others' and concluding God is unfair. What specific
situations tempt you to fret — a colleague's promotion, an
unbeliever's health, an unjust person's success?

2. The four imperatives of verses 3-7 form a spiritual discipline:
Trust (lean your weight on God), Do good (act rightly regardless
of outcome), Delight (find your supreme pleasure in God), Commit
(roll your burden onto Him), Rest/Be still (cease striving).
Which of these is most difficult for you, and why?

3. "I have been young, and now am old; yet have I not seen the
righteous forsaken" (v.25). This is testimony from decades of
observation. While individual moments may feel like abandonment,
the long view reveals faithfulness. If you are young, trust the
testimony of the aged. If you are old, bear witness to God's
faithfulness to those coming behind you.

4. "Though he fall, he shall not be utterly cast down" (v.24).
Falling is not failing permanently. The righteous fall — David
certainly did — but the difference is that God's hand catches and
restores. Have you allowed a fall to define you rather than
experiencing God's restorative grip?

5. The wicked flourish "like a green bay tree" (v.35) — then
vanish completely (v.36). Envy is always based on a snapshot,
never the full story. What looks like permanent success is often
temporary display. Are you evaluating others' lives (and your own)
by snapshots or by endings?
