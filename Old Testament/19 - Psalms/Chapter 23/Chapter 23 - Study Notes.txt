Psalms - Chapter 23
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A psalm of trust — the most beloved psalm; 'The LORD is my shepherd, I shall not want.' Through green pastures, still waters, the valley of the shadow of death, and the prepared table — goodness and mercy follow all life's days.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Trust/Confidence Psalm
Attributed Author: David — "A Psalm of David"
Key Themes: God as Shepherd, complete provision, rest and
restoration, fearlessness in danger, abundance in God's presence,
lifelong goodness and mercy, eternal dwelling with God

Historical Context: The most beloved psalm in Scripture. David
writes from personal experience as a shepherd — he knows what a
good shepherd does because he WAS one (1 Sam 16:11; 17:34-36).
The psalm forms a trilogy with Psalms 22-24: Psalm 22 is the Good
Shepherd giving His life (John 10:11), Psalm 23 is the Great
Shepherd caring for His sheep (Heb 13:20), and Psalm 24 is the
Chief Shepherd returning in glory (1 Pet 5:4). The psalm uses two
metaphors: God as Shepherd (vv.1-4) and God as Host (vv.5-6).
Note the pronoun shift: vv.1-3 speak ABOUT God ("He"), vv.4-6
speak TO God ("Thou/You") — intimacy deepens in the valley.

Structure:
- The Shepherd Provides (vv.1-3)
- The Shepherd Protects in Darkness (v.4)
- The Host Prepares a Feast (v.5)
- Lifelong and Eternal Blessing (v.6)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "The LORD is my shepherd; I shall not want." (KJV) — Six
        words that contain an entire theology of provision. If
        Yahweh is your shepherd, every need is met. "Shall not
        want" is not "shall have no desires" but "shall lack
        nothing necessary." Contentment theology.

v.4  — "Yea, though I walk through the valley of the shadow of
        death, I will fear no evil: for thou art with me" (KJV) —
        The psalm does not promise absence of darkness but presence
        IN darkness. "THROUGH" — not "in" but "through" — the
        valley is traversed, not inhabited. And the basis for
        fearlessness: "thou art WITH me." Presence, not
        explanation, is the answer to fear.

v.5  — "Thou preparest a table before me in the presence of mine
        enemies" (KJV) — Not after the enemies are gone but IN
        THEIR PRESENCE. God's provision is not hidden or private —
        it is displayed before those who wish you harm. You feast
        while they watch.

v.6  — "Surely goodness and mercy shall follow me all the days of
        my life" (KJV) — "Follow" = Hebrew "radaph" = PURSUE,
        chase, hunt. Goodness and mercy are not passive — they
        actively PURSUE you. You are hunted by grace.

------------------------------------
v.1   — John 10:11 — "I am the good shepherd: the good shepherd
         giveth his life for the sheep." Christ fulfills this psalm.
v.1   — Isaiah 40:11 — "He shall feed his flock like a shepherd."
         Messianic shepherd prophecy.
v.2   — Revelation 7:17 — "The Lamb... shall lead them unto living
         fountains of waters." Eternal fulfillment of still waters.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1 — KJV/ESV identical: "The LORD is my shepherd; I shall not
      want." — The translations agree perfectly. Hebrew "lo
      echsar" = I will not lack. The negative is absolute — no
      deficiency, no gap, no unmet need.

v.2 — KJV: "He maketh me to lie down" / ESV: "He makes me lie
      down" — The shepherd causes rest. Sheep will not lie down
      unless free from fear, friction, flies, and hunger (Phillip
      Keller). God removes what prevents rest.

v.4 — KJV: "the valley of the shadow of death" / ESV: "the valley
      of the shadow of death" — Hebrew "tsalmaveth" — traditionally
      parsed as "shadow of death" (tsel + maveth). Some modern
      scholars read it as "deep darkness" (a single word). Both
      translations retain the traditional rendering. The image is
      of deepest possible darkness — whether death or not.

v.5 — KJV: "thou anointest my head with oil; my cup runneth over"
      / ESV: "you anoint my head with oil; my cup overflows" —
      Anointing = honor, healing, celebration. The overflowing cup
      = abundance beyond need. God does not merely meet minimum
      requirements — He provides extravagantly.

v.6 — KJV: "goodness and mercy shall follow me" / ESV: "goodness
      and mercy shall follow me" — Hebrew "radaph" is the word for
      pursuing/chasing (often used of enemies pursuing). God's
      goodness and mercy PURSUE you with the same intensity an
      enemy would. You cannot outrun grace.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.1 — WEB: "Yahweh is my shepherd" / ASV: "Jehovah is my shepherd" — both
  make the divine name explicit where KJV/ESV use "the LORD." The personal
  name emphasizes the intimate, covenantal relationship between Shepherd and
  sheep. NET: "I lack nothing" — more modern and immediate than "I shall not
  want" (KJV/ASV) which can confuse modern readers ("want" = lack, not desire).
- v.2 — NET: "He takes me to lush pastures, he leads me to refreshing water"
  — "lush" and "refreshing" are interpretive additions that make the sensory
  quality vivid. KJV/ESV/ASV/WEB all use "green pastures" and "still waters."
  NET's "refreshing water" loses the imagery of "still" (Hebrew "menuchot" =
  waters of rest/quietness) but captures the purpose.
- v.3 — NET: "He restores my strength" — renders Hebrew "nafshi" (my soul) as
  "my strength," interpreting the soul-restoration as physical/emotional
  renewal. NET also renders "for his name's sake" as "for the sake of his
  reputation" — a bold paraphrase that makes the Hebrew meaning (God acts to
  uphold His own character) explicit.
- v.3 — ASV: "He guideth me in the paths of righteousness" — "guideth"
  emphasizes ongoing, personal direction rather than general leading.
- v.4 — NET: "the darkest valley" — drops "shadow of death" entirely, reading
  Hebrew "tsalmaveth" as a single word meaning "deep darkness." This is the
  most significant divergence. KJV/ESV/ASV/WEB all retain "valley of the
  shadow of death." NET also uses "reassure me" instead of "comfort me" for
  the rod and staff — emphasizing confidence rather than emotional soothing.
- v.5 — NET: "You prepare a feast before me in plain sight of my enemies. You
  refresh my head with oil; my cup is completely full" — "feast" (vs. "table")
  heightens the abundance. "Refresh" (vs. "anoint") shifts from ritual honor
  to personal renewal. "Completely full" (vs. "runneth over") is slightly less
  vivid — KJV/WEB's "overflows" better captures the extravagance.
- v.6 — NET: "your goodness and faithfulness will pursue me" — renders Hebrew
  "chesed" as "faithfulness" rather than "mercy" (KJV/ESV) or "lovingkindness"
  (ASV) or "loving kindness" (WEB). Each rendering captures a facet: chesed =
  loyal love / covenant faithfulness / tender mercy. NET also adds "for the
  rest of my life" where others have "all the days of my life" and "forever."
- v.6 — ASV: "lovingkindness" — a distinctive ASV rendering of Hebrew "chesed"
  that combines love and kindness into a single compound word, attempting to
  capture the multifaceted meaning of this untranslatable Hebrew term.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "MY shepherd" — personal, possessive (v.1). Not "a shepherd" or
   "the shepherd" but MY shepherd. The psalm's power is in the
   personal pronoun. He is not shepherd in general — He is YOUR
   shepherd specifically.

2. Rest is provided, not achieved (v.2). "He MAKES me lie down."
   Rest is something the shepherd produces by creating the right
   conditions. If you cannot rest, ask what conditions the
   Shepherd needs to address — and let Him.

3. The valley is THROUGH, not permanent (v.4). You walk THROUGH
   it — it has an entrance and an exit. No valley lasts forever.
   And the shadow of death is just that — a SHADOW. Shadows cannot
   harm; only the reality casts shadows. Christ has defeated death;
   what remains is only its shadow.

4. God feasts you before your enemies (v.5). He does not wait
   until the conflict is over. His provision is demonstrative —
   visible to those who oppose you. Your abundance in their
   presence is testimony to His faithfulness.

5. Goodness and mercy PURSUE you (v.6). You are not chasing God's
   blessings — they are chasing YOU. The same relentless energy
   an enemy would use to hunt you is the energy goodness and mercy
   use to find you. You cannot escape grace.
