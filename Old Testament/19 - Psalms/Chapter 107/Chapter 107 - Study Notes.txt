Psalms - Chapter 107
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A thanksgiving psalm opening Book V — 'O give thanks unto the LORD, for he is good'; four groups in distress (wanderers, prisoners, fools, storm-tossed sailors) cry to the LORD and He delivers; 'let them give thanks for his steadfast love.'

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Thanksgiving Psalm / Wisdom Psalm
Attributed Author: Anonymous (opens Book V of the Psalter)
Key Themes: God's deliverance from various troubles, the
recurring pattern of distress and rescue, gratitude as duty,
God's sovereign control over nature and nations, wisdom in
recognizing God's hesed

Historical Context: Psalm 107 opens Book V (Psalms 107-150), the
final section of the Psalter. It is structured around four
vivid pictures of people in distress who cry to God and are
delivered: (1) lost travelers in the desert, (2) prisoners in
darkness, (3) the sick near death, (4) sailors in storms. After
each vignette, the same refrain calls for thanksgiving (vv.8, 15,
21, 31). The psalm likely celebrates return from Babylonian exile
(v.3: "gathered from the lands") but applies to all who have
experienced God's deliverance. The concluding wisdom section
(vv.33-43) shows God's sovereign power to reverse fortunes.

Structure:
- Call to Thanksgiving (vv.1-3)
- Vignette 1: Lost in the Desert (vv.4-9)
- Vignette 2: Prisoners in Darkness (vv.10-16)
- Vignette 3: Sick unto Death (vv.17-22)
- Vignette 4: Sailors in the Storm (vv.23-32)
- God's Sovereign Reversals (vv.33-42)
- Wisdom Conclusion (v.43)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "Let the redeemed of the LORD say so, whom he hath
        redeemed from the hand of the enemy." (KJV) — If you've
        been redeemed, SAY SO. Silence about God's deliverance is
        ingratitude. Testimony is commanded, not optional.

v.8  — "Oh that men would praise the LORD for his goodness, and
        for his wonderful works to the children of men!" (KJV) —
        The fourfold refrain (vv.8, 15, 21, 31). A longing cry:
        if only people would praise God for what He's done! The
        repetition shows the psalmist's frustration at human
        forgetfulness.

v.20 — "He sent his word, and healed them, and delivered them
        from their destructions." (KJV) — God heals by His WORD.
        Not medicine, not magic — His word. This anticipates
        Christ, the Word made flesh, who healed with a command.

v.43 — "Whoso is wise, and will observe these things, even they
        shall understand the lovingkindness of the LORD." (KJV) —
        The psalm ends as wisdom literature. The pattern of
        distress → cry → deliverance → thanksgiving is meant to
        be OBSERVED and UNDERSTOOD. The wise person sees God's
        hesed in every rescue.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.8 — KJV: "Oh that men would praise the LORD" / ESV: "Let them
      thank the LORD" — Hebrew "yodu la-YHVH" is a jussive (wish/
      command). KJV captures the longing; ESV captures the command.
      Both are present: "If only they would! They should!"

v.27 — KJV: "are at their wit's end" / ESV: "were at their wits'
       end" — Hebrew literally: "all their wisdom is swallowed up."
       This common English phrase ("at wit's end") comes directly
       from this verse in KJV.

v.40 — KJV: "He poureth contempt upon princes" / ESV: "he pours
       contempt on princes" — Echoes Job 12:21. God reverses
       human hierarchies: princes wander; the poor are exalted.
       No status is permanent apart from God.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.1 — KJV: "his mercy endureth for ever" / ASV: "his
  lovingkindness endureth for ever" / WEB: "his loving kindness
  endures forever" / NET: "his loyal love endures" — The same
  chesed variation as Psalm 106:1. This refrain opens both psalms
  with identical Hebrew but divergent English renderings.

- v.8 — KJV: "Oh that men would praise the LORD for his goodness"
  / ASV: "Oh that men would praise Jehovah for his lovingkindness"
  / WEB: "Let them praise Yahweh for his loving kindness" / NET:
  "Let them give thanks to the Lord for his loyal love" — The
  fourfold refrain shows the chesed difference starkly. KJV uses
  "goodness" here (not "mercy"), ASV/WEB use "lovingkindness,"
  NET uses "loyal love." Same Hebrew word, four different English
  expressions across the psalm's key structural refrain.

- v.20 — KJV: "He sent his word, and healed them" / NET: "He sent
  them an assuring word and healed them" — NET adds "assuring,"
  interpreting the bare Hebrew. WEB: "He sends his word, and heals
  them, and delivers them from their graves" — WEB's "graves"
  for Hebrew "shechitot" (destructions/pits) connects to Sheol
  imagery more directly than KJV's "destructions."

- v.27 — NET: "They swayed and staggered like drunks, and all
  their skill proved ineffective" — KJV/ASV: "are at their wits'
  end." NET loses the famous English idiom (which originated from
  this KJV verse) and replaces it with explanation. The Hebrew
  literally reads "all their wisdom is swallowed up."

- v.40 — NET: "He would pour contempt upon princes, and he made
  them wander in a wasteland with no road" — KJV: "in the
  wilderness, where there is no way." NET's "wasteland with no
  road" is interpretive but vivid. ASV: "in the waste, where there
  is no way."

- v.43 — KJV: "shall understand the lovingkindness of the LORD" /
  ASV: "will consider the lovingkindnesses of Jehovah" / WEB:
  "will consider the loving kindnesses of Yahweh" / NET: "Let them
  consider the Lord's acts of loyal love" — The psalm's conclusion
  brings the chesed theme full circle. NET again adds "acts of" to
  make the love concrete and active.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. If you've been redeemed, say so (v.2). Don't keep God's
   deliverance a secret. Your testimony of rescue is meant to
   be spoken — for your own remembrance and for others' faith.

2. The pattern never changes (vv.6, 13, 19, 28). Trouble → cry
   to God → deliverance. Four different situations, one solution:
   crying to the LORD. Whatever your distress — lostness,
   bondage, sickness, or chaos — the answer is the same: cry out.

3. Some suffering is self-inflicted (vv.11, 17). The prisoners
   rebelled; the sick were "fools through their transgression."
   God still delivers when they cry out. Self-inflicted suffering
   doesn't disqualify you from mercy.

4. He brings you to your desired haven (v.30). After the storm,
   God doesn't just calm the waters — He brings you to where you
   needed to go. Deliverance is not just rescue from danger but
   guidance to destination.

5. Wisdom sees the pattern (v.43). The wise person connects the
   dots across their life: every rescue reveals hesed. If you
   can't see God's lovingkindness, you're not observing closely
   enough.
