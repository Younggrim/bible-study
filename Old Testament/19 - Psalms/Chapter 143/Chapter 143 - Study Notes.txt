Psalms - Chapter 143
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A penitential lament — David's spirit is overwhelmed, the enemy has crushed him; he pleads for God's faithfulness (not strict justice, since none living is righteous), thirsts for God like parched land, and asks to be led on level ground.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Individual Lament / Penitential Psalm
Attributed Author: David (superscription: "A Psalm of David")
Key Themes: Pleading God's faithfulness, no one is righteous,
spiritual thirst, remembering past mercies, prayer for guidance,
the leading of God's Spirit

Historical Context: Psalm 143 is the seventh and last of the
Penitential Psalms (6, 32, 38, 51, 102, 130, 143). David is
crushed by enemies but knows he cannot claim innocence before God
("in thy sight shall no man living be justified," v.2). He
appeals instead to God's faithfulness and righteousness. The psalm
is especially notable for its plea for the Holy Spirit's guidance
(v.10) — one of the clearest OT prayers for the Spirit's leading.
Luther valued this psalm greatly for its gospel principle: no one
is justified by their own merit.

Structure:
- Plea Based on God's Faithfulness (vv.1-2)
- Description of Distress (vv.3-4)
- Remembering and Thirsting (vv.5-6)
- Urgent Pleas for Help (vv.7-10)
- Prayer for Deliverance (vv.11-12)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "Enter not into judgment with thy servant: for in thy sight
        shall no man living be justified." (KJV) — Foundational
        gospel truth. NO ONE can stand justified before God on
        their own merit. Not David, not any person living. This is
        why grace is necessary (cf. Rom 3:20, Gal 2:16).

v.6  — "I stretch forth my hands unto thee: my soul thirsteth
        after thee, as a thirsty land." (KJV) — The posture
        (outstretched hands) and the feeling (desperate thirst)
        combined. David's soul is parched earth reaching toward
        rain. Only God can satisfy this drought.

v.10 — "Teach me to do thy will; for thou art my God: thy spirit
        is good; lead me into the land of uprightness." (KJV) —
        "Thy Spirit is good" — one of the rare OT references to
        the Holy Spirit as personal guide. David asks for the
        Spirit's leading into righteous living.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.2 — KJV: "in thy sight shall no man living be justified" / NET: "no one alive is innocent before you" — NET replaces "justified" (legal/forensic) with "innocent" (moral state), a subtle but significant theological shift. Paul quotes this verse in Romans 3 using the justification framework.
- v.7 — KJV: "lest I be like unto them that go down into the pit" / NET: "or I will join those descending into the grave" — NET replaces "pit" (Hebrew "bor") with "grave," making the death reference explicit. ASV/WEB retain "pit."
- v.8 — KJV: "Cause me to hear thy lovingkindness in the morning" / ASV: "thy lovingkindness" / WEB: "your loving kindness" / NET: "May I hear about your loyal love in the morning" — Classic chesed pattern. NET adds "about" and changes the imperative to a wish ("May I hear").
- v.10 — KJV: "thy spirit is good" / ASV: "Thy Spirit is good" / WEB: "Your Spirit is good" / NET: "May your kind presence" — NET paraphrases "Spirit" (Hebrew "ruach") as "kind presence," removing the personal pronoun and pneumatological reference.
- v.11 — KJV: "Quicken me, O LORD" / ASV: "Quicken me, O Jehovah" / WEB: "Revive me, Yahweh" / NET: "O Lord...revive me" — Standard "quicken/revive" modernization pattern.
- v.12 — KJV: "of thy mercy cut off mine enemies" / ASV: "in thy lovingkindness cut off mine enemies" / WEB: "In your loving kindness, cut off my enemies" / NET: "As a demonstration of your loyal love, destroy my enemies" — NET adds "As a demonstration of," making God's chesed the motive for judgment. The connection between mercy and enemy destruction is theologically bold.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. No one is justified before God (v.2). This is not false
   humility — it's reality. Don't approach God on the basis of
   your performance. Approach on the basis of His faithfulness
   and mercy.

2. Thirst for God (v.6). Spiritual thirst is not weakness — it's
   health. A soul that doesn't thirst for God is a soul in
   danger. Stretch out your hands like parched earth toward rain.

3. Ask for the Spirit's leading (v.10). "Thy Spirit is good —
   lead me." This is one of the simplest and best prayers: Holy
   Spirit, lead me where I should go. Your Spirit is good;
   I trust Your direction.

4. Remember past mercies (v.5). In present crisis, recall past
   faithfulness. What God has done before, He can do again.
   Memory fuels faith.
