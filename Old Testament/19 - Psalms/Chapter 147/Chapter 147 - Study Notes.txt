Psalms - Chapter 147
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A Hallel psalm — God heals the brokenhearted and numbers the stars by name; His understanding is infinite; He delights not in military strength but in those who fear Him and hope in His steadfast love.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Hallelujah Psalm / Hymn of Praise
Attributed Author: Anonymous (post-exilic; references rebuilding
Jerusalem)
Key Themes: God rebuilds Jerusalem, He heals the brokenhearted,
He counts the stars yet cares for individuals, God's power in
nature, His delight in those who fear Him, His word running swiftly

Historical Context: Psalm 147 celebrates God's restoration of
Jerusalem after exile AND His ongoing cosmic power. The psalm
weaves together intimate care (healing broken hearts, v.3) with
cosmic grandeur (counting and naming every star, v.4). It likely
dates from the post-exilic period (Nehemiah's time) when
Jerusalem's walls were being rebuilt. The God who rebuilds walls
also rebuilds broken people. He gives the barley harvest AND gives
snow. He sends His word to melt ice AND sends His word (statutes)
to Israel. Creation and revelation flow from the same God.

Structure:
- God Rebuilds Jerusalem and Heals Hearts (vv.1-6)
- God Provides in Nature (vv.7-11)
- God Strengthens and Blesses Zion (vv.12-14)
- God's Powerful Word in Nature (vv.15-18)
- God's Unique Word to Israel (vv.19-20)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.3  — "He healeth the broken in heart, and bindeth up their
        wounds." (KJV) — The same God who builds Jerusalem's walls
        also repairs broken hearts. He is both cosmic contractor
        and personal physician. No heart is too shattered for His
        healing.

v.4  — "He telleth the number of the stars; he calleth them all
        by their names." (KJV) — Billions of stars — and God knows
        each one BY NAME. If He names stars, He certainly knows
        YOUR name. The God of the cosmos is the God of the
        individual.

v.5  — "Great is our Lord, and of great power: his understanding
        is infinite." (KJV) — Infinite understanding. Not great
        understanding — INFINITE. There is nothing He doesn't know,
        no problem He can't solve, no situation beyond His
        comprehension.

v.10-11 — "He delighteth not in the strength of the horse: he
        taketh not pleasure in the legs of a man. The LORD taketh
        pleasure in them that fear him, in those that hope in his
        mercy." (KJV) — What delights God? Not military power or
        human ability but those who FEAR Him and HOPE in His mercy.
        God is not impressed by your strength — He's delighted by
        your trust.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.1 — WEB: "Praise Yah" / ASV: "Praise ye Jehovah" / NET: "Praise the Lord" — The Hallelujah frame uses the short form in WEB ("Yah") and the full form in ASV ("Jehovah").
- v.5 — KJV: "Great is our Lord, and of great power: his understanding is infinite" / NET: "Our Lord is great and has awesome power; there is no limit to his wisdom" — NET replaces "understanding" with "wisdom" (Hebrew "tevunah" = understanding/discernment) and adds "awesome" not in the Hebrew.
- v.6 — KJV: "The LORD lifteth up the meek" / ASV: "Jehovah upholdeth the meek" / WEB: "Yahweh upholds the humble" / NET: "The Lord lifts up the oppressed" — KJV/WEB="meek/humble" (character trait); NET="oppressed" (social condition). Hebrew "anavim" encompasses both meanings.
- v.11 — KJV: "in those that hope in his mercy" / ASV: "in those that hope in his lovingkindness" / WEB: "in those who hope in his loving kindness" / NET: "in those who wait for his loyal love" — Classic chesed difference in the refrain. NET uses "wait" rather than "hope," emphasizing active expectation.
- v.14 — NET: "He abundantly provides for you the best grain" — Paraphrases KJV's "filleth thee with the finest of the wheat." NET adds "abundantly provides" and generalizes "wheat" to "grain."
- v.17 — KJV: "He casteth forth his ice like morsels" / WEB: "He hurls down his hail like pebbles" / NET: "He throws his hailstones like crumbs" — Three vivid images for the same action: KJV=morsels (food-size); WEB=pebbles (stone-size); NET=crumbs (tiny fragments). Hebrew "pittim" = morsels/fragments.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. He heals broken hearts AND counts stars (vv.3-4). God is not
   too big for your pain or too small for the universe. He does
   both simultaneously. The cosmic God is your personal healer.

2. God is not impressed by human power (v.10). Horses and strong
   legs don't move God. What delights Him: fear and hope. You
   don't need to be impressive — you need to be trusting.

3. His word runs swiftly (v.15). God's commands don't delay. When
   He speaks — to nature or to you — His word accomplishes
   instantly. His word is powerful and prompt.

4. Israel received something unique (vv.19-20). God gave His
   statutes to Israel as to no other nation. The possession of
   Scripture is a privilege and a responsibility.
