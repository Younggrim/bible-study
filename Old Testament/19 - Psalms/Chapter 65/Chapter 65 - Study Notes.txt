Psalms - Chapter 65
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A harvest thanksgiving psalm — praise awaits God in Zion; He forgives iniquity, answers prayer with awesome deeds, stills the roaring seas, and crowns the year with goodness as the pastures overflow.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Hymn of Praise / Thanksgiving / Creation Psalm
Attributed Author: David
Key Themes: Praise for answered prayer, atonement for sin, the
blessedness of God's chosen ones, God's sovereignty over nature,
abundant provision, the joy of creation

Historical Context: This is likely a harvest thanksgiving psalm,
celebrating God's provision of rain and agricultural abundance
after a period of need. It may have been composed for a specific
harvest festival or after a drought was broken. The psalm is
remarkable for its scope — it moves from the temple (vv.1-4) to
cosmic creation (vv.5-8) to the specific fields and valleys of
Israel (vv.9-13). It is one of the most joyful psalms in the
Psalter, with creation itself personified as shouting and singing
for joy. The progression from sin forgiven to creation rejoicing
shows the connection between spiritual and physical blessing in
Israel's theology.

Structure:
- Praise in Zion and Answered Prayer (vv.1-2)
- Atonement for Sin (v.3)
- Blessedness of the Chosen (v.4)
- God's Awesome Deeds in Creation (vv.5-8)
- God's Provision for the Earth (vv.9-13)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "O thou that hearest prayer, unto thee shall all flesh
        come." (KJV) — God's essential character: He HEARS prayer.
        And the scope: ALL flesh — not just Israel. A universalist
        vision of God's accessibility.

v.4  — "Blessed is the man whom thou choosest, and causest to
        approach unto thee, that he may dwell in thy courts."
        (KJV) — Election and access: God CHOOSES and CAUSES
        approach. Access to God is not a human achievement but a
        divine gift. We come because He drew us.

v.11 — "Thou crownest the year with thy goodness; and thy paths
        drop fatness." (KJV) — One of the Psalter's most beautiful
        agricultural images. God's goodness is a crown on the year.
        Wherever God travels (His "paths"), abundance overflows
        like oil dripping from wagon tracks.

v.13 — "The pastures are clothed with flocks; the valleys also are
        covered over with corn; they shout for joy, they also
        sing." (KJV) — Creation personified in celebration. Hills,
        meadows, and valleys are clothed in abundance and sing.
        Nature itself rejoices in God's provision. The whole earth
        is a worship service.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.2   — Isaiah 56:7 — "My house shall be called an house of
         prayer for all people." God's universal accessibility.
v.3   — 1 John 2:2 — "He is the propitiation for our sins: and
         not for ours only, but also for the sins of the whole
         world."
v.4   — Ephesians 1:4 — "He hath chosen us in him before the
         foundation of the world." Divine election.
v.4   — John 6:44 — "No man can come to me, except the Father
         which hath sent me draw him." God causing approach.
v.7   — Psalm 89:9 — "Thou rulest the raging of the sea: when the
         waves thereof arise, thou stillest them."
v.9   — Deuteronomy 11:11-12 — "A land which the LORD thy God
         careth for: the eyes of the LORD thy God are always upon
         it." God's agricultural care for Israel.
v.13  — Romans 8:19-22 — Creation groaning and waiting for
         redemption — the counterpart to creation singing.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1 — KJV: "Praise waiteth for thee" / ESV: "Praise is due to
      you" — Hebrew "dumiyyah" (silence/stillness) is difficult
      here. Some read "praise silently waits" (KJV sense); others
      emend to "tehillah" = "praise is fitting/due" (ESV sense).
      The idea may be that praise awaits God in quiet readiness.

v.5 — KJV: "By terrible things in righteousness wilt thou answer
      us" / ESV: "By awesome deeds you answer us with
      righteousness" — "Terrible" (KJV) = awe-inspiring, not
      frightening. ESV's "awesome" is clearer for modern readers.
      God's answers are dramatically powerful.

v.11 — KJV: "thy paths drop fatness" / ESV: "your wagon tracks
       overflow with abundance" — Hebrew "ma'gal" = track, rut,
       wagon path. ESV helpfully specifies: wherever God's chariot
       travels through the land, abundance flows in its tracks like
       spilled grain or oil.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.3 — ASV: "thou wilt forgive them" / WEB: "you atoned for our transgressions" / NET: "you forgive our acts of rebellion" vs. KJV: "thou shalt purge them away" — WEB uniquely uses "atoned" (Hebrew "kaphar" — to cover/atone), making the sacrificial action explicit. NET specifies "acts of rebellion" for "transgressions." KJV's "purge" emphasizes cleansing.

- v.5 — WEB: "By awesome deeds of righteousness, you answer us, God of our salvation. You who are the hope of all the ends of the earth" — WEB renders "confidence" (KJV) / "trust" as "hope" for Hebrew "mivtach." NET: "All the ends of the earth trust in you."

- v.8 — NET: "you cause those living in the east and west to praise you" — NET interprets "outgoings of the morning and evening" as geographic directions (east and west), while KJV preserves the temporal imagery: dawn and dusk themselves rejoice. WEB: "You call the morning's dawn and the evening with songs of joy."

- v.9 — NET: "God's streams are full of water; you provide grain for the people of the earth" — NET specifies "people of the earth" (not in Hebrew which just says "them"). WEB: "The river of God is full of water. You provide them grain" — stays closer to Hebrew. The "river of God" likely refers to heavenly waters/rain.

- v.11 — NET: "you leave abundance in your wake" / WEB: "Your carts overflow with abundance" vs. KJV: "thy paths drop fatness" — Three different images of the same Hebrew. WEB specifies "carts" (implied by wagon tracks); NET uses "wake" (like a ship); KJV preserves the more enigmatic "paths drop fatness."

- v.12 — NET: "The pastures in the wilderness glisten with moisture" — NET interprets "drop" (Hebrew "ra'aph") as moisture glistening, while KJV/ASV have "drop upon the pastures" (rain falling). WEB: "The wilderness grasslands overflow" — yet another reading.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. God hears prayer — that's His character (v.2). Prayer is not
   shouting into a void. The God of the universe is defined by
   hearing. And ALL flesh can come — no one is excluded from His
   audience.

2. Access to God is a gift, not an achievement (v.4). "Thou
   choosest and causest to approach." If you are drawn to God, it
   is because He drew you first. Proximity to God is grace, not
   merit.

3. God crowns the year with goodness (v.11). Look back over the
   past year — God's goodness is the crown. Even in hard years,
   His provision is present. His wagon tracks leave abundance in
   their wake.

4. All creation sings (v.13). The hills, valleys, and meadows
   worship. If nature sings to its Creator, how much more should
   those made in His image? Our silence is the anomaly, not our
   praise.
