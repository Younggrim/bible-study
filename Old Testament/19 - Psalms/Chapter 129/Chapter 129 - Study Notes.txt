Psalms - Chapter 129
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A Song of Ascents — Israel recounts lifelong affliction: 'Many a time have they afflicted me from my youth'; plowers plowed upon their back, yet the LORD cut the cords of the wicked; may Zion's enemies wither.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Song of Ascents / Communal Lament
Attributed Author: Anonymous (superscription: "A Song of degrees")
Key Themes: Israel's history of persecution, survival through
affliction, God cutting the cords of the wicked, judgment on
those who hate Zion, resilience of God's people

Historical Context: Psalm 129 is the tenth Song of Ascents — a
national testimony of survival. Israel has been afflicted "from
my youth" (from Egypt onward), yet the enemy has NOT prevailed.
The vivid image of plowers making long furrows on Israel's back
(v.3) captures centuries of oppression: Egypt, Assyria, Babylon,
and beyond. Yet the defiant refrain: "they have not prevailed
against me." The second half calls for judgment on Zion's enemies,
comparing them to grass on rooftops that withers before being
pulled — fruitless and temporary.

Structure:
- Afflicted but Not Defeated (vv.1-4)
- Prayer Against Zion's Enemies (vv.5-8)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "Many a time have they afflicted me from my youth: yet
        they have not prevailed against me." (KJV) — The testimony
        of survival. Yes, affliction came — repeatedly, severely,
        from the beginning. BUT: they have not prevailed. This is
        the story of God's people throughout history.

v.3  — "The plowers plowed upon my back: they made long their
        furrows." (KJV) — One of the most visceral images in the
        Psalms. Oppressors treated Israel's back like a field to
        be plowed — leaving deep, long wounds. Yet Israel survived.

v.4  — "The LORD is righteous: he hath cut asunder the cords of
        the wicked." (KJV) — God cut the ropes binding the plow
        to Israel's back. The righteous God liberates His people
        from their oppressors.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.1   — Hosea 11:1 — "When Israel was a child... I called my son
         out of Egypt." Affliction from youth.
v.3   — Isaiah 50:6 — "I gave my back to the smiters." Christ
         fulfilled this imagery.
v.4   — Nahum 1:13 — "I will break his yoke from off thee, and
         will burst thy bonds in sunder."
v.6   — Psalm 37:2 — "They shall soon be cut down like the
         grass, and wither as the green herb."

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
- v.1 — NET: "Since my youth they have often attacked me" — Paraphrases KJV's "Many a time have they afflicted me from my youth." NET uses "attacked" (active aggression) for "afflicted" (passive suffering), changing the tone.
- v.4 — KJV: "The LORD is righteous" / ASV: "Jehovah is righteous" / WEB: "Yahweh is righteous" / NET: "The Lord is just" — NET uses "just" rather than "righteous" for Hebrew "tsaddiq," emphasizing judicial fairness over moral character.
- v.4 — KJV: "he hath cut asunder the cords of the wicked" / NET: "he cut the ropes of the wicked" — NET simplifies "cut asunder" to "cut" and uses "ropes" for "cords," modernizing without significant theological change.
- v.5 — KJV: "confounded" / ASV: "put to shame" / WEB: "disappointed" / NET: "humiliated" — Four different renderings of Hebrew "bosh" (be ashamed). WEB's "disappointed" is notably weaker than the others.
- v.6 — KJV: "the grass upon the housetops, which withereth afore it groweth up" / NET: "the grass on the rooftops, which withers before one can even pull it up" — NET adds "one can even pull it up" to explain the imagery, making the speed of withering more vivid.
- v.7 — KJV: "the mower" / ASV/WEB: "the reaper" / NET: "the reaper" — KJV's "mower" (one who mows) is archaic; "reaper" is the standard rendering in modern translations.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Affliction does not mean defeat (v.2). Being attacked doesn't
   mean you've lost. God's people have been afflicted throughout
   history — and have NEVER been prevailed against. Survival is
   its own testimony.

2. God cuts the cords (v.4). Whatever has been plowing your back
   — oppression, abuse, injustice — God is righteous and He cuts
   those cords. Liberation comes from His hand.

3. The enemies of God's people are temporary (vv.5-8). Like
   rooftop grass — visible but fruitless, temporary, withering.
   Those who oppose God's people have no lasting harvest.
