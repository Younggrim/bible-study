Psalms - Chapter 76
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A Song of Zion celebrating God's victory — God is known in Judah, His dwelling is in Salem; He broke the weapons of war, stilled the enemy; 'surely the wrath of man shall praise thee.'

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Hymn of Praise / Zion Song
Attributed Author: Asaph
Key Themes: God's victory from Zion, divine warrior, the fear of
God, human wrath serving God's purposes, God as judge over all
the earth

Historical Context: This psalm celebrates a decisive military
victory — most likely the destruction of Sennacherib's Assyrian
army in 701 BC (2 Kings 19:35; Isaiah 37:36), when the angel of
the LORD struck down 185,000 soldiers overnight. The imagery of
warriors "sleeping their sleep" (v.5) and chariots cast into "dead
sleep" (v.6) perfectly fits an army destroyed in the night without
Israel lifting a sword. "Salem" (v.2) is an ancient name for
Jerusalem (Genesis 14:18 — Melchizedek was king of Salem). The
psalm declares God's terrifying power and the futility of human
military might against Him.

Structure:
- God Known in Zion (vv.1-3)
- God's Terrifying Victory (vv.4-6)
- God the Fearsome Judge (vv.7-9)
- Human Wrath Serves God's Glory (v.10)
- Call to Worship the Fearsome God (vv.11-12)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.3  — "There brake he the arrows of the bow, the shield, and the
        sword, and the battle." (KJV) — God disarms entire armies.
        Every weapon of war — offensive (arrows, sword) and
        defensive (shield) — is broken. The battle itself ceases.

v.7  — "Thou, even thou, art to be feared: and who may stand in
        thy sight when once thou art angry?" (KJV) — The rhetorical
        answer: no one. When God's anger is roused, no power in
        heaven or earth can resist. The double "thou, even thou"
        emphasizes exclusivity — God ALONE is to be feared.

v.10 — "Surely the wrath of man shall praise thee: the remainder
        of wrath shalt thou restrain." (KJV) — One of Scripture's
        most profound statements about sovereignty. Human rage,
        aimed against God, ultimately serves His glory. What it
        cannot serve, He simply restrains. Nothing escapes His
        control.

v.12 — "He shall cut off the spirit of princes: he is terrible to
        the kings of the earth." (KJV) — No earthly ruler is
        exempt from God's authority. The most powerful kings are
        nothing before the King of kings.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------

v.5   — 2 Kings 19:35 — 185,000 Assyrians dead overnight. The
         likely historical background.
v.7   — Nahum 1:6 — "Who can stand before his indignation? and

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.4 — KJV: "more glorious and excellent than the mountains of
      prey" / ESV: "more majestic than the mountains full of prey"
      — "Mountains of prey" may refer to enemy strongholds
      (mountains where predators hunt) or to the mountains of spoil
      (plunder). God is more majestic than any military stronghold.

v.10 — KJV: "the remainder of wrath shalt thou restrain" / ESV:
       "the remnant of wrath you will put on like a belt" — This is
       one of the most debated translations in the Psalms. KJV
       reads "restrain" (God limits what human wrath can do); ESV
       reads "put on like a belt" (God wears human wrath as an
       ornament/trophy). Both are theologically rich.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Human wrath ultimately serves God's praise (v.10). Every act of
   human opposition against God — from Pharaoh to Sennacherib to
   the cross — ends up magnifying His glory. What they intend for
   evil, God transforms into praise.

2. God is the only one to truly fear (v.7). If God is for you, no
   human enemy can ultimately harm you. But if God is against you,
   no human ally can save you. Fear God — and fear no one else.

3. Military might is nothing before God (vv.3-6). Arrows, shields,
   swords, chariots, horses — all rendered useless by divine
   intervention. Don't trust in technology or strategy — trust in
   the God who breaks weapons overnight.
