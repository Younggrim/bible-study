Psalms - Chapter 5
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A morning prayer psalm — David asks God to hear his voice at dawn, contrasts the righteous who take refuge in God with the wicked whose throats are open graves, and asks God to lead him in righteousness.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Individual Lament / Morning Prayer
Attributed Author: David — "To the chief Musician upon Nehiloth,
A Psalm of David"
Key Themes: Morning prayer, God's holiness vs. wickedness,
deceitful enemies, worship in reverence, divine protection as
shield and favor

Historical Context: This is a morning prayer psalm (v.3), forming
a liturgical sequence with Psalms 3 (morning) and 4 (evening).
"Nehiloth" likely refers to flute instruments. David faces enemies
characterized not by physical violence but by speech — flattery,
lies, and deceit. The psalm contrasts two approaches to God: the
wicked who cannot stand in His presence, and the righteous who
enter through mercy. David does not claim personal merit but "the
multitude of thy mercy" (v.7) as his access to worship. The psalm
anticipates Paul's quotation of v.9 in Romans 3:13 as evidence
of universal human depravity.

Structure:
- Morning Prayer and God's Attention (vv.1-3)
- God's Holiness Rejects Evil (vv.4-6)
- David's Approach Through Mercy (vv.7-8)
- The Wickedness of Enemies (vv.9-10)
- Joy and Protection for the Righteous (vv.11-12)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.3  — "My voice shalt thou hear in the morning, O LORD; in the
        morning will I direct my prayer unto thee, and will look
        up." (KJV) — A disciplined morning orientation toward God.
        "Direct" (arak) = to arrange in order, as a priest arranges
        sacrifice on an altar. Prayer is deliberate, ordered
        offering — not casual thought.

v.7  — "But as for me, I will come into thy house in the multitude
        of thy mercy" (KJV) — David's access to God is not by
        personal righteousness but by abundant mercy. Given that
        vv.4-6 describe who CANNOT approach God, David's "but as
        for me" acknowledges he enters only by grace.

v.9  — "Their throat is an open sepulchre; they flatter with their
        tongue." (KJV) — Quoted by Paul in Romans 3:13 to
        demonstrate universal depravity. The throat as open grave
        = speech that brings death and corruption wherever it goes.

v.12 — "For thou, LORD, wilt bless the righteous; with favour wilt
        thou compass him as with a shield." (KJV) — God's favor
        SURROUNDS the righteous like a large shield (tsinnah =
        full-body shield, not a small buckler). Protection is
        comprehensive, not partial.

------------------------------------
v.3   — Psalm 88:13 — "In the morning shall my prayer prevent
         thee." Morning prayer as consistent practice.
v.3   — Mark 1:35 — Jesus rose "a great while before day" to pray.
         Morning prayer modeled by Christ Himself.
v.4   — Habakkuk 1:13 — "Thou art of purer eyes than to behold
         evil." Same theology of divine holiness.
v.7   — Psalm 100:4 — "Enter into his gates with thanksgiving."

v.9   — Romans 3:13 — Paul quotes this verse directly as evidence
         of human depravity: "their throat is an open sepulchre."
v.11  — Psalm 32:11 — "Be glad in the LORD, and rejoice, ye
         righteous." Joy for those who trust.
v.12  — Psalm 3:3 — "Thou, O LORD, art a shield for me." Repeated
         shield imagery across David's psalms.

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1 — KJV: "consider my meditation" / ESV: "consider my groaning"
      — Hebrew "hagig" can mean meditation, musing, or groaning/
      sighing. ESV interprets as inarticulate groaning; KJV as
      thoughtful meditation. The context (v.2 "cry") supports ESV's
      reading — these are wordless sighs too deep for articulation.

v.3 — KJV: "I will direct my prayer unto thee, and will look up" /
      ESV: "I prepare a sacrifice for you and watch" — Hebrew
      "arak" = arrange/set in order (used of arranging wood on an
      altar). ESV makes the sacrificial metaphor explicit; KJV
      obscures it. "Watch/look up" = expectant waiting for God's
      response after presenting the offering.

v.5 — KJV: "The foolish" / ESV: "The boastful" — Hebrew
      "holelim" = boasters, arrogant ones (not intellectual fools
      but moral braggarts). ESV is more accurate.

v.10 — KJV: "Destroy thou them, O God" / ESV: "Make them bear
       their guilt, O God" — Significantly different. Hebrew
       "ashmem" = declare/make guilty. ESV preserves the legal/
       judicial sense — let them bear the consequences of their own
       guilt. KJV intensifies to outright destruction.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Morning prayer is arranged, not accidental (v.3). David
   "directs" his prayer like a priest arranging sacrifice. There
   is intentionality, order, and expectation. Then he WATCHES —
   looking for God's response throughout the day.

2. Access to God is by mercy, not merit (v.7). In a psalm that
   describes who CANNOT approach God (vv.4-6), David enters
   through "the multitude of thy mercy." This is Old Testament
   grace theology — no one approaches God on personal worthiness.

3. Speech reveals the heart (v.9). Flattering tongues, lying
   mouths, throats like open graves — the enemies' primary weapon
   is words. Guard what you receive (discern flattery) and what
   you speak (avoid becoming what you despise).

4. God's favor is comprehensive protection (v.12). The shield
   (tsinnah) is not a small buckler but a full-body covering.
   God's favor does not partially protect — it surrounds
   completely. You are compassed, not merely shielded from one
   direction.

5. Joy belongs to those who take refuge (v.11). The psalm ends not
   with the destruction of the wicked but with the celebration of
   the righteous. The focus moves from enemies to God's people
   shouting for joy under divine protection.
