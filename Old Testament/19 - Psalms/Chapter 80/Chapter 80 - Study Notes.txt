Psalms - Chapter 80
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A communal lament — 'Give ear, O Shepherd of Israel'; the vine God brought from Egypt is now burned and broken; three times the refrain pleads: 'Restore us, O God; let thy face shine, and we shall be saved.'

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Community Lament Psalm
Attributed Author: Asaph
Key Themes: God as Shepherd of Israel, the vine metaphor, national
devastation, "restore us" refrain, the shining face of God, prayer
for the "man of thy right hand"

Historical Context: Set to "Shoshannim Eduth" (Lilies of the
Testimony), this psalm addresses the northern tribes specifically
(Ephraim, Benjamin, Manasseh — Joseph's descendants, v.1-2). It
likely dates to the Assyrian crisis — either the fall of the
northern kingdom in 722 BC or the period immediately preceding it.
The vine metaphor (vv.8-16) tells Israel's story: planted by God
out of Egypt, grown to fill the land, then BROKEN — hedges down,
wild animals devouring it. The repeated refrain "Turn us again...
cause thy face to shine... we shall be saved" (vv.3,7,19) is the
psalm's heartbeat. The mysterious "man of thy right hand" (v.17)
may point to Benjamin (whose name means "son of the right hand"),
to the king, or Messianically to Christ.

Structure:
- Invocation: Shepherd, Shine Forth (vv.1-2)
- First Refrain: Restore Us (v.3)
- Lament: Tears and Mockery (vv.4-6)
- Second Refrain: Restore Us (v.7)
- The Vine Allegory (vv.8-16)
- Prayer for the Man of God's Right Hand (vv.17-18)
- Third Refrain: Restore Us (v.19)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "Give ear, O Shepherd of Israel, thou that leadest Joseph
        like a flock; thou that dwellest between the cherubims,
        shine forth." (KJV) — God as shepherd is tender; God
        enthroned on cherubim is terrifying. Both are addressed
        simultaneously. The plea: SHINE — let Your hidden glory
        become visible again.

v.8-9 — "Thou hast brought a vine out of Egypt: thou hast cast
        out the heathen, and planted it. Thou preparedst room
        before it, and didst cause it to take deep root, and it
        filled the land." (KJV) — The vine = Israel. Transplanted
        from Egypt, carefully planted in Canaan, supernaturally
        grown. Everything about this vine's success was GOD'S work.

v.12 — "Why hast thou then broken down her hedges?" (KJV) — The
        devastating question. God planted the vine AND broke its
        walls. The same God who gave success allowed destruction.
        This is not random — it is divine discipline.

v.17 — "Let thy hand be upon the man of thy right hand, upon the
        son of man whom thou madest strong for thyself." (KJV) —
        Messianic undertones. "Man of thy right hand" and "son of
        man" point beyond any immediate king to the ultimate
        Deliverer. Jesus called Himself "Son of Man."

v.19 — "Turn us again, O LORD God of hosts, cause thy face to
        shine; and we shall be saved." (KJV) — The climactic
        refrain. Each repetition (vv.3,7,19) adds a fuller divine
        title: God → God of hosts → LORD God of hosts. The prayer
        intensifies with each crying out.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.1   — Psalm 23:1 — "The LORD is my shepherd." Personal version
         of the corporate appeal here.
v.1   — Exodus 25:22 — God dwelling between the cherubim on the

v.17  — Daniel 7:13 — "One like the Son of man came with the

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.3 — KJV: "Turn us again" / ESV: "Restore us" — Hebrew "hashib"
      = cause to return, restore. The plea is for God to turn His
      people back to Himself — restoration is His work, not theirs.
      They cannot return on their own power.

v.5 — KJV: "the bread of tears" / ESV: "the bread of tears" —
      Same in both. A powerful image: tears have become their daily
      sustenance. What they eat and drink is weeping.

v.15 — KJV: "the branch that thou madest strong for thyself" / ESV:
       "the son whom you made strong for yourself" — Hebrew "ben"
       = son or branch. ESV reads it personally (a son); KJV reads
       it botanically (a branch of the vine). Both connect to the
       Messianic overtones of v.17.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.3 — NET: "Smile on us" / KJV: "cause thy face to shine" / ASV: "cause thy face to shine" / WEB: "Cause your face to shine" — NET renders the Hebrew "shine your face" as "Smile on us" — a dramatic interpretive choice that modernizes the ancient light-metaphor but loses the theophanic glory connotation.

- v.4 — ASV: "O Jehovah God of hosts" / WEB: "Yahweh God of Armies" / KJV: "O LORD God of hosts" / NET: "O Lord God of Heaven's Armies" — Four different renderings of יְהוָה אֱלֹהִים צְבָאוֹת. NET's "Heaven's Armies" is the most interpretive; WEB/ASV translate צְבָאוֹת literally; KJV retains the Hebrew loan-word "hosts."

- v.10 — ASV: "cedars of God" / WEB: "God's cedars" / KJV: "goodly cedars" / NET: "the highest cedars" — Hebrew "arzey-El" literally means "cedars of God" (a Hebrew superlative). ASV/WEB translate literally; KJV interprets as "goodly" (great); NET as "the highest" — showing how the divine-name superlative works.

- v.15 — NET: "the root your right hand planted, the shoot you made to grow" / KJV: "the vineyard which thy right hand hath planted, and the branch" — NET reads "root/shoot" (botanical); KJV reads "vineyard/branch." The Hebrew allows both, but NET's reading avoids the Messianic "branch" connection.

- v.18 — KJV: "quicken us" / ASV: "Quicken thou us" / NET: "Revive us" / WEB: "Revive us" — KJV/ASV retain the archaic "quicken" (= make alive); NET/WEB modernize to "revive." Both mean restoration to life/vitality from spiritual death.

- v.19 — ASV: "O Jehovah God of hosts" / WEB: "Yahweh God of Armies" — The climactic refrain uses the fullest divine title. The progression across vv.3, 7, 19 adds titles: "God" → "God of hosts" → "Yahweh/Jehovah God of hosts" — increasing urgency reflected in all translations.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. God is both tender Shepherd and enthroned King (v.1). He leads
   gently AND reigns absolutely. Approach Him with intimacy AND
   reverence — He is not one or the other but both.

2. God plants, grows, AND prunes (vv.8-12). The same God who
   transplanted you, rooted you, and grew you can also break down
   your hedges. Pruning is not abandonment — it is husbandry.
   Trust the Vinedresser even when He cuts.

3. "Restore us" is a prayer God loves to answer (vv.3,7,19).
   Restoration is God's specialty. He is the God of comebacks.
   Pray it repeatedly, pray it boldly — each time with increasing
   faith.

4. Salvation is tied to God's face shining (v.19). "Cause thy face
   to shine, and we shall be saved." Salvation comes from God's
   favorable attention. His turned face is judgment; His shining
   face is salvation. Seek His face above all.
