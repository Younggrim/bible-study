Psalms - Chapter 114
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A Hallel psalm celebrating the Exodus — when Israel went out of Egypt, the sea fled, Jordan turned back, mountains skipped; 'Tremble, thou earth, at the presence of the Lord' who turned the rock into a pool of water.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Hallel Psalm / Hymn of Praise
Attributed Author: Anonymous
Key Themes: The Exodus as cosmic event, God's presence causing
nature to flee, the power of God's presence, poetic personification
of creation, the God of Jacob as Lord of all nature

Historical Context: Psalm 114 is the second psalm in the Egyptian
Hallel (113-118), sung at Passover. It is one of the most
artistically brilliant poems in the Bible — only 8 verses, yet it
captures the entire Exodus in vivid poetic imagery. The sea, the
Jordan, the mountains, and the hills are personified as living
creatures who RESPOND to God's presence: the sea flees, Jordan
retreats, mountains skip like rams. The psalm asks creation
directly: "What ailed thee?" — as if the earth itself was
startled by God walking through. No divine name appears until v.7
("the Lord" / "the God of Jacob") — the whole poem builds toward
the revelation of who caused this cosmic disruption.

Structure:
- The Exodus Event (vv.1-2)
- Nature's Response to God's Presence (vv.3-4)
- Direct Address to Creation (vv.5-6)
- The Revelation: God of Jacob (vv.7-8)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.3  — "The sea saw it, and fled: Jordan was driven back." (KJV)
        — What did the sea "see"? God's presence. The Red Sea saw
        God coming and RAN. Forty years later, Jordan did the same.
        Nature recognizes its Master even when humans don't.

v.7  — "Tremble, thou earth, at the presence of the Lord, at the
        presence of the God of Jacob." (KJV) — The psalm's climax.
        If the sea flees and mountains skip, then ALL earth should
        tremble. And this awesome God is identified specifically as
        "the God of Jacob" — the God of one man, one family, one
        nation. The cosmic God is also the personal God.

v.8  — "Which turned the rock into a standing water, the flint
        into a fountain of waters." (KJV) — The final image: God
        doesn't just move water (sea, Jordan) — He CREATES water
        from rock. The God who can make the impossible happen from
        the hardest material.

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1 — "a people of strange language" — Hebrew "lo'ez" = foreign
      speech, barbarous tongue. Egypt's language was alien to the
      Hebrews — emphasizing that Israel was truly foreign there.

v.4 — "skipped like rams" — Hebrew "raqad" = to skip, dance, leap.
      The mountains DANCED at God's presence. This is playful,
      joyful imagery — creation celebrating its Creator's arrival.

v.7 — "the God of Jacob" — Not "the God of heaven" or "the
      Almighty" but specifically "of Jacob." The wrestler, the
      supplanter, the flawed man. This cosmic God binds Himself
      to ordinary people.

v.1 — NET: "foreign nation behind," for KJV's "people of strange language;"
v.4 — ASV: "The" for KJV's "and the"
v.7 — NET: "Lord— before" for KJV's "presence of the Lord, at the presence of"

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Creation recognizes God even when we don't (v.3). The sea saw
   and fled. Mountains leaped. Do you respond to God's presence
   with less awareness than rocks and water?

2. The God of the cosmos is the God of Jacob (v.7). The same
   power that split seas and shook mountains is personally
   committed to flawed individuals. He is both infinite and
   intimate.

3. God makes water from rock (v.8). Whatever impossible situation
   you face — flint, stone, hardness — God can bring forth living
   water from it. No material is too hard for His power.

4. The brevity and beauty of this psalm teaches: the greatest
   truths often need the fewest words. Eight verses capture
   an entire theology of divine power and presence.
