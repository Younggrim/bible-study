Psalms - Chapter 39
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A lament on life's brevity — David resolved to keep silent but could not contain his anguish; he asks God to show him the measure of his days, for man is but a breath and his wealth passes to another.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Individual Lament / Wisdom Reflection
Attributed Author: David ("To the chief Musician, even to
Jeduthun, A Psalm of David")
Key Themes: Guarding the tongue, the brevity and vanity of human
life, prayer for understanding life's shortness, pilgrimage and
sojourning, the discipline of God

Historical Context: The superscription assigns this psalm to
Jeduthun (also spelled Ethan), one of David's three chief
musicians alongside Asaph and Heman (1 Chronicles 25:1-6). The
phrase "to Jeduthun" likely means "for Jeduthun" to perform, or
"in the manner/style of Jeduthun." Jeduthun's name may derive
from yadah (to praise) — ironic given this psalm's somber tone.

Psalm 39 is one of the most introspective and philosophically
searching psalms in the Psalter. David wrestles with the
unbearable brevity of human existence and the seeming futility of
life under divine discipline. The psalm begins with attempted
silence (vv.1-2), breaks into speech when the inner fire becomes
unbearable (v.3), and then pours out a meditation on mortality
that is simultaneously faithful and anguished.

What makes this psalm remarkable and somewhat uncomfortable is its
ending. Unlike most lament psalms that resolve in confident praise,
Psalm 39 ends with the haunting request: "O spare me, that I may
recover strength, before I go hence, and be no more" (v.13). This
raw, unresolved quality gives the psalm its authenticity — not
every prayer ends with triumphant resolution. Sometimes faith means
simply bringing our unresolved grief to God. The psalm's theology
of human frailty ("every man at his best state is altogether
vanity," v.5) anticipates Ecclesiastes and connects to the broader
wisdom tradition.

Structure:
- Attempted Silence: Guarding the Tongue (vv. 1-3)
- Breaking the Silence: Prayer About Life's Brevity (vv. 4-6)
- Turning to God: Hope in the Midst of Vanity (vv. 7-8)
- Submission Under God's Discipline (vv. 9-11)
- Final Plea: A Sojourner's Prayer (vv. 12-13)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
Verse 4-5 — "LORD, make me to know mine end, and the measure of
my days, what it is; that I may know how frail I am. Behold, thou
hast made my days as an handbreadth; and mine age is as nothing
before thee: verily every man at his best state is altogether
vanity." (KJV)
David asks not "how long will I live?" but "help me grasp how
short life truly is." A "handbreadth" (tephach) is about four
inches — the width of four fingers. This is life measured against
eternity: microscopic. "At his best state" (nitsav) — even in
the prime of life, the apex of health and success — humanity is
hevel (vapor, breath, vanity).

Verse 6 — "Surely every man walketh in a vain shew: surely they
are disquieted in vain: he heapeth up riches, and knoweth not who
shall gather them." (KJV)
Human activity is a "vain shew" (tselem — shadow, image,
phantom). People bustle and worry (disquieted) for nothing. The
accumulation of wealth is exposed as absurd — you gather what
others will inherit, and you have no control over who receives it.
This verse powerfully anticipates Ecclesiastes 2:18-21.

Verse 7 — "And now, Lord, what wait I for? my hope is in thee."
(KJV)
The turning point. After exposing life's vanity, David does not
fall into nihilism. Instead, he redirects hope from temporal
things (which are all vapor) to the eternal God. If everything
under the sun is vanity, then only what is above the sun can bear
the weight of hope.

Verse 12 — "Hear my prayer, O LORD, and give ear unto my cry;
hold not thy peace at my tears: for I am a stranger with thee,
and a sojourner, as all my fathers were." (KJV)
David identifies himself as a "stranger" (ger) and "sojourner"
(toshav) — terms for resident aliens without permanent land
rights. Life itself is a pilgrimage; we are passing through.
This theology of pilgrimage shapes the entire biblical vision of
the Christian life (Hebrews 11:13, 1 Peter 2:11).

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.1 — James 1:26; James 3:2-10: Guarding the tongue — James'
extensive teaching on speech control echoes David's determined
(though ultimately unsuccessful) attempt at silence.

v.4-5 — Psalm 90:12: "Teach us to number our days, that we may
apply our hearts unto wisdom." Moses makes the same request —
understanding life's brevity leads to wisdom.

v.5 — James 4:14: "What is your life? It is even a vapour, that
appeareth for a little time, and then vanisheth away." James uses
the same imagery of life as vapor/breath.

v.5-6 — Ecclesiastes 1:2; 2:18-21: "Vanity of vanities" — the
Teacher's core theme is stated here in condensed form. The
connection between Psalm 39 and Ecclesiastes is profound.

v.7 — Psalm 62:5-6: "My soul, wait thou only upon God; for my
expectation is from him." The same redirection of hope from
temporal to eternal.

v.11 — Job 13:28; Isaiah 50:9; Isaiah 51:8: Life consumed like a
moth-eaten garment — the fragility of human existence under divine
correction.

v.12 — Hebrews 11:13; 1 Peter 2:11: Believers as strangers and
pilgrims — the New Testament explicitly uses this Old Testament
self-understanding for the church.

v.12 — Genesis 47:9; 1 Chronicles 29:15: Jacob before Pharaoh
and David before God both confess their days as few and
pilgrimage-like.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1 — "I will keep my mouth with a bridle" (KJV) / "I will guard
my mouth with a muzzle" (ESV): Hebrew machsom — a muzzle or
gag, used for animals. KJV's "bridle" (which controls) is softer
than ESV's "muzzle" (which silences completely). The Hebrew
suggests total suppression of speech — David tried to completely
silence himself.

v.5 — "every man at his best state is altogether vanity" (KJV) /
"Surely all mankind stands as a mere breath" (ESV): Hebrew kol-
hevel kol-adam nitsav — literally "all vanity, all of man
standing." The word hevel (vanity, breath, vapor) is the signature
word of Ecclesiastes. KJV's "at his best state" interprets
nitsav (standing firmly) as referring to man at his peak. ESV
reads it more generally.

v.6 — "in a vain shew" (KJV) / "as a shadow" (ESV): Hebrew
be-tselem — literally "in an image/shadow/phantom." KJV's "vain
shew" captures the theatrical sense — life as performance,
appearance without substance. ESV's "shadow" emphasizes
insubstantiality and transience.

v.11 — "thou makest his beauty to consume away like a moth" (KJV)
/ "you consume like a moth what is dear to him" (ESV): A
significant interpretive difference. KJV says God consumes the
person's beauty/desirability (chamudo — his desire/attractiveness).
ESV says God consumes what the person holds dear (his treasured
possessions). Hebrew chamud can mean either "what is desirable
about him" or "what he desires." Both readings describe divine
discipline stripping away what people value.

v.13 — "O spare me" (KJV) / "Look away from me" (ESV): Hebrew
hasha — meaning "look away, turn your gaze aside." ESV is more
literal. David asks God to relent from His intense scrutinizing
gaze — not to abandon him, but to give him breathing room before
death. This is similar to Job 7:19: "How long wilt thou not
depart from me?"

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.4 — ASV: "Jehovah, make me to know mine end" / WEB: "Yahweh, show me my end" / NET: "O Lord, help me understand my mortality and the brevity of life" — NET extensively paraphrases, adding "mortality" and "brevity of life" to interpret the Hebrew. The simple, stark prayer "make me to know mine end" (ASV/KJV) is more poignant — a direct request to understand one's death.

- v.5 — KJV: "every man at his best state is altogether vanity" / ASV: "Surely every man at his best estate is altogether vanity" / WEB: "Surely every man stands as a breath" / NET: "Surely all people, even those who seem secure, are nothing but vapor" — Hebrew "hevel" = breath, vapor, vanity. NET's "vapor" and WEB's "breath" emphasize transience; KJV/ASV's "vanity" (following Ecclesiastes tradition) emphasizes futility. NET adds "even those who seem secure" — interpreting "nitsav" (standing firmly).

- v.6 — KJV: "walketh in a vain shew" / ASV: "walketh in a vain show" / WEB: "walks like a shadow" / NET: "go through life as mere ghosts" — Hebrew "be-tselem" = in an image/shadow/phantom. NET's "ghosts" is evocative but perhaps too supernatural; WEB's "shadow" captures insubstantiality; KJV/ASV's "vain show" emphasizes empty appearance.

- v.11 — KJV: "thou makest his beauty to consume away like a moth" / ASV: "Thou makest his beauty to consume away like a moth" / WEB: "You consume his wealth like a moth" / NET: "like a moth you slowly devour their strength" — Hebrew "chamudo" is debated: beauty (KJV/ASV), wealth (WEB), or strength (NET)? Each translation interprets what God consumes through discipline. WEB's "wealth" and NET's "strength" are interpretive; KJV/ASV's "beauty" preserves the ambiguity.

- v.12 — KJV: "I am a stranger with thee, and a sojourner" / ASV: "I am a stranger with thee, A sojourner" / WEB: "I am a stranger with you, a foreigner" / NET: "I am a resident foreigner with you, a temporary settler" — NET elaborates with "resident foreigner" and "temporary settler," making the legal status explicit. Hebrew "ger" and "toshav" describe someone living in a land that isn't home — theological language for humanity's earthly existence.

- v.13 — KJV: "O spare me" / ASV: "Oh spare me" / WEB: "Oh spare me" / NET: "Turn your angry gaze away from me" — NET adds "angry gaze" — interpreting the Hebrew as God looking with anger. The Hebrew "hasha" means simply "look away" or "cease to gaze." NET makes God's disposition more hostile than the text warrants.

- v.5 — ASV: "my life-time is as nothing before thee" / WEB: "My lifetime is as nothing before you" / NET: "my life span is nothing from your perspective" — NET adds "from your perspective," framing the brevity as a matter of divine viewpoint rather than absolute reality. The Hebrew states it plainly: "my age is as nothing before you" — it IS nothing, not merely appears so.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. David's attempt to remain silent (vv.1-2) eventually failed
because the inner fire demanded expression (v.3). Suppressed
emotion does not disappear — it intensifies. Healthy spirituality
requires expression, whether in prayer, worship, journaling, or
honest conversation. What are you bottling up that needs to be
spoken before God?

2. "Make me to know mine end...that I may know how frail I am"
(v.4). This is not morbid fixation but wisdom. Numbering our days
produces urgency, gratitude, and proper priorities. If you truly
internalized that your life is a "handbreadth" — would your
priorities change? What would you stop doing? What would you
start?

3. "He heapeth up riches, and knoweth not who shall gather them"
(v.6). The futility of accumulation without purpose. Everything
you own will belong to someone else. This is not a call to poverty
but to generosity and proper perspective. Are you building a
legacy of character and faith, or merely a pile of stuff?

4. "My hope is in thee" (v.7) — when every temporal hope is
exposed as vapor, God alone remains. This is the essence of faith
in its purest form: not hoping in what God gives but in God
Himself. When circumstances strip away every secondary comfort,
is God enough?

5. "I am a stranger with thee, and a sojourner" (v.12). Christians
are resident aliens in this world. We belong elsewhere. This
pilgrim identity protects against both grasping attachment to
earthly things and despairing over earthly losses. You were never
meant to settle here permanently. How does "sojourner" identity
shape your relationship to possessions, reputation, and comfort?
