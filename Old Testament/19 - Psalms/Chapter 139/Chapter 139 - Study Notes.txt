Psalms - Chapter 139
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A psalm of God's omniscience and omnipresence — 'O LORD, thou hast searched me, and known me'; God knows every thought, is present in heaven, Sheol, and the uttermost sea; 'I am fearfully and wonderfully made.' David invites God's searching.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Hymn of Praise / Wisdom Psalm
Attributed Author: David (superscription: "To the chief Musician,
A Psalm of David")
Key Themes: God's omniscience (all-knowing), omnipresence
(everywhere), creation of human life in the womb, the
inescapability of God, being fully known and fully loved,
invitation for God to search the heart

Historical Context: Psalm 139 is one of the most theologically
rich and personally intimate psalms in the Bible. It addresses
three divine attributes — omniscience (vv.1-6), omnipresence
(vv.7-12), and sovereign creative power (vv.13-18) — not as
abstract theology but as intensely personal realities. David
doesn't discuss God's knowledge generally; he says "Thou hast
searched ME." This psalm has been called the most complete
expression of the individual's relationship to God in the Old
Testament. The imprecatory section (vv.19-22) has puzzled some,
but it flows logically: the God who knows David so intimately
also sees the wicked, and David aligns himself with God's
purposes. The psalm then returns full circle to the opening
theme: "Search ME" (v.23).

Structure:
- God's Omniscience: He Knows Me Completely (vv.1-6)
- God's Omnipresence: I Cannot Escape Him (vv.7-12)
- God's Creative Power: He Made Me (vv.13-18)
- Response: Against the Wicked (vv.19-22)
- Invitation: Search My Heart (vv.23-24)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "O LORD, thou hast searched me, and known me." (KJV) —
        The opening declaration: God has SEARCHED (Hebrew "chaqar"
        = thoroughly investigated, explored completely) and KNOWN
        (intimate, personal knowledge). You are completely
        transparent to God — and this is not threat but comfort.

v.7-10 — "Whither shall I go from thy spirit?... If I ascend up
        into heaven, thou art there: if I make my bed in hell,
        behold, thou art there... Even there shall thy hand lead
        me, and thy right hand shall hold me." (KJV) — No
        location in the universe is outside God's presence.
        Heaven, Sheol, the far side of the sea — HE IS THERE.
        And not just there — His hand LEADS and HOLDS even there.

v.14 — "I will praise thee; for I am fearfully and wonderfully
        made: marvellous are thy works; and that my soul knoweth
        right well." (KJV) — The declaration of human dignity.
        Every person is FEARFULLY (with reverent awe) and
        WONDERFULLY (with distinct, marvelous design) made. This
        is not self-esteem but GOD-esteem: your value is in your
        Maker's craftsmanship.

v.16 — "Thine eyes did see my substance, yet being unperfect; and
        in thy book all my members were written, which in
        continuance were fashioned, when as yet there was none of
        them." (KJV) — God saw you before you existed. Your days
        were written in His book before one of them came to be.
        Your life is not accidental but scripted by divine
        authorship.

v.23-24 — "Search me, O God, and know my heart: try me, and know
        my thoughts: And see if there be any wicked way in me, and
        lead me in the way everlasting." (KJV) — The psalm's
        conclusion: having declared that God knows everything, David
        INVITES further examination. Not hiding from God's gaze but
        welcoming it. "Lead me" — the result of being searched is
        not condemnation but guidance.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.13 — KJV: "thou hast possessed my reins" / ESV: "you formed my
       inward parts" — Hebrew "qanah" = to create, acquire,
       possess. "Reins" (kilayot) = kidneys, representing the
       inmost being/emotions. God created your deepest inner self.

v.14 — "fearfully and wonderfully made" — Hebrew "nora'ot
       niphleti" = with fear/awe I am set apart/distinguished.
       Each person is a unique, awe-inspiring creation.

v.15 — KJV: "curiously wrought in the lowest parts of the earth"
       / ESV: "intricately woven in the depths of the earth" —
       Hebrew "ruqqam" = embroidered, woven with skill. "Lowest
       parts of the earth" = poetic for the womb's hidden depths.
       You were EMBROIDERED by God in secret.

v.16 — KJV: "in thy book all my members were written" / ESV: "in
       your book were written, every one of them, the days that
       were formed for me" — The "book" contains God's plan for
       the person — either body parts (KJV reading) or days of
       life (ESV reading). Either way: God planned you before you
       existed.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.1 — WEB: "Yahweh, you have searched me" / ASV: "O Jehovah, thou hast
  searched me" — the divine name made explicit. NET: "O Lord, you examine me
  and know me" — "examine" adds intentionality beyond "searched" (KJV/ESV/
  ASV/WEB), suggesting God's knowing is active investigation, not passive
  awareness.
- v.2 — NET: "you understand my motives" — renders Hebrew "rea" (thought/
  purpose) as "motives," making explicit that God perceives not just what we
  think but WHY we think it. KJV/ASV: "my thought"; WEB: "my thoughts."
- v.5 — NET: "You squeeze me in from behind and in front" — vivid and
  physical rendering of Hebrew "tsur" (beset/hem in). KJV: "beset me"; ESV:
  "hem me in"; WEB: "hem me in." NET's "squeeze" captures the sense of being
  completely enclosed by God's presence — inescapable intimacy.
- v.13 — NET: "Certainly you made my mind and heart" — renders Hebrew
  "kilyotai" (kidneys/inward parts) as "mind and heart," interpreting the
  organ-metaphor as its meaning. WEB: "you formed my inmost being" — follows
  ESV's approach. ASV: "thou didst form my inward parts." KJV's "possessed
  my reins" is the most archaic ("reins" = kidneys as seat of emotions).
- v.13 — WEB: "You knit me together in my mother's womb" — uses "knit" where
  KJV/ASV have "covered" and ESV/NET use different verbs. "Knit" captures
  the careful, intricate assembly imagery of Hebrew "sakak" in this context.
- v.14 — NET: "your deeds are awesome and amazing. You knew me thoroughly" —
  diverges significantly from KJV/ESV/ASV/WEB's "I am fearfully and
  wonderfully made." NET shifts the wonder from the self to God's works and
  knowledge. The traditional rendering ("fearfully and wonderfully made")
  preserved by ASV/WEB focuses on the dignity of the created person.
- v.15 — WEB: "woven together in the depths of the earth" — "woven" captures
  the textile metaphor of Hebrew "ruqqam" (embroidered). NET: "sewed together
  in the depths of the earth" — also textile imagery but less elegant. ASV:
  "curiously wrought" — archaic but beautiful, meaning "intricately crafted."
- v.16 — NET: "Your eyes saw me when I was inside the womb. All the days
  ordained for me were recorded in your scroll before one of them came into
  existence" — "scroll" (vs. "book") evokes an ancient document. "Ordained"
  makes predestination explicit. WEB: "Your eyes saw my body. In your book
  they were all written, the days that were ordained for me" — similar to NET
  but retains "book."
- v.24 — NET: "See if there is any idolatrous way in me" — a bold
  interpretation of Hebrew "derech otseb" (way of pain/wickedness). KJV/ESV/
  ASV/WEB all have "wicked way." NET interprets the specific wickedness as
  idolatry — turning from God to false sources. This narrows the meaning but
  may reflect the psalm's context of total devotion to God alone.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. You are fully known (vv.1-6). There is nothing hidden from
   God — not a thought, not a word, not a movement. This could
   be terrifying... or it could be the deepest comfort imaginable.
   The One who knows EVERYTHING about you still loves you.

2. You cannot escape God (vv.7-12). Not in heaven, not in death,
   not in darkness, not at the ends of the earth. This means:
   wherever you are, God is there too. You are never truly alone.
   Never beyond His reach. Never lost to Him.

3. You are God's masterwork (vv.13-16). You were not an accident.
   You were embroidered, fashioned, written about BEFORE you
   existed. Your value is not earned — it's designed.

4. Welcome the search (vv.23-24). Don't hide from God's
   examination. INVITE it. "Search me" — let Him find what you
   can't see yourself. The goal is not condemnation but leading
   in "the way everlasting."

5. Knowledge of God should produce praise (v.14, 17). The proper
   response to being known by God is not fear but PRAISE: "I
   will praise thee." And His thoughts toward you are
   uncountable (v.18): more than the sand. Every grain on every
   beach — and more.
