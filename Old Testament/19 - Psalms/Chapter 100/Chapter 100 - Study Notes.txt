Psalms - Chapter 100
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A psalm of thanksgiving — 'Make a joyful noise unto the LORD, all ye lands'; know that the LORD is God who made us; enter His gates with thanksgiving; 'his mercy is everlasting and his truth endureth to all generations.'

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Hymn of Praise / Thanksgiving Psalm
Attributed Author: Anonymous (superscription: "A Psalm of praise/
thanksgiving")
Key Themes: Joyful worship, serving God with gladness, knowing
God as Creator and Shepherd, entering His presence with
thanksgiving, God's eternal goodness, mercy, and faithfulness

Historical Context: Psalm 100 is one of the most beloved and
frequently recited psalms in all of Scripture. Only 5 verses, yet
it contains the fullness of worship theology. The superscription
identifies it as "A Psalm for thanksgiving" (todah) — likely used
when bringing a todah (thank offering) to the temple. It serves as
the climactic conclusion to the enthronement psalm series (93-100),
summarizing the appropriate response to God's kingship: joyful
service, knowing Him rightly, entering His presence, and
acknowledging His character. The Jubilate Deo (Latin translation of
v.1) has been set to music thousands of times and used in
Christian liturgy for two millennia. This is the psalm that
captures the heartbeat of worship in five perfect verses.

Structure:
- Command: Joyful Noise and Service (vv.1-2)
- Foundation: Know Who God Is (v.3)
- Command: Enter with Thanksgiving and Praise (v.4)
- Foundation: Know What God Is Like (v.5)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "Serve the LORD with gladness: come before his presence
        with singing." (KJV) — Service and gladness are joined.
        Biblical service is not drudgery but delight. And the
        manner of approach: singing. Not fear, not reluctance —
        SINGING. Joy is the proper tone of worship.

v.3  — "Know ye that the LORD he is God: it is he that hath made
        us, and not we ourselves; we are his people, and the sheep
        of his pasture." (KJV) — Three truths to KNOW: (1) Yahweh
        is God (identity), (2) He made us, not we ourselves
        (origin), (3) we are His people/sheep (belonging). Worship
        flows from knowledge. Know WHO He is, WHERE you came from,
        and WHOSE you are.

v.4  — "Enter into his gates with thanksgiving, and into his
        courts with praise: be thankful unto him, and bless his
        name." (KJV) — The entry requirements for worship:
        thanksgiving and praise. Not perfection, not achievement,
        not even understanding — just gratitude and blessing. The
        door is open to anyone who comes thankful.

v.5  — "For the LORD is good; his mercy is everlasting; and his
        truth endureth to all generations." (KJV) — Three eternal
        attributes: (1) goodness — His nature, (2) mercy/hesed —
        His disposition toward His people, (3) truth/faithfulness —
        His reliability across all generations. This verse is the
        theological foundation of all the preceding commands.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.1   — Psalm 98:4 — "Make a joyful noise unto the LORD, all the
         earth." Same universal call.
v.2   — Philippians 4:4 — "Rejoice in the Lord alway: and again I
         say, Rejoice." Joy as command.
v.3   — Psalm 95:6-7 — "Let us kneel before the LORD our maker.
         For he is our God; and we are the people of his pasture."
         Nearly identical.
v.3   — Ephesians 2:10 — "We are his workmanship, created in
         Christ Jesus." He made us.
v.4   — Hebrews 13:15 — "Let us offer the sacrifice of praise to
         God continually, that is, the fruit of our lips giving
         thanks to his name."
v.5   — Psalm 136 — Every verse: "for his mercy endureth for
         ever." The fullest expansion of Ps 100:5.
v.5   — Lamentations 3:22-23 — "The LORD's mercies... are new
         every morning: great is thy faithfulness."

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1 — KJV: "all ye lands" / ESV: "all the earth" — Hebrew "kol
      ha-aretz" = all the earth/land. The scope is universal — not
      just Israel but every land, every nation.

v.3 — KJV: "it is he that hath made us, and not we ourselves" /
      ESV: "It is he who made us, and we are his" — The Hebrew
      has a textual variant: the written text (Ketiv) reads "lo"
      (not) = "we did not make ourselves." The read text (Qere)
      reads "lo" (to him) = "we are his." Both are theologically
      true. ESV follows the Qere; KJV follows the Ketiv.

v.5 — KJV: "his mercy is everlasting" / ESV: "his steadfast love
      endures forever" — Hebrew "hesed" again. KJV's "mercy" and
      ESV's "steadfast love" each capture one dimension of this
      rich term. It is covenant love that is faithful, loyal,
      generous, and enduring.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.1 — ASV: "Make a joyful noise unto Jehovah, all ye lands" / WEB: "Shout for joy to Yahweh, all you lands!" / NET: "Shout out praises to the Lord, all the earth!" — Divine name: ASV "Jehovah," WEB "Yahweh," NET "the Lord." NET interprets "joyful noise" (Hebrew "haru") as "shout out praises" — adding "praises" where the Hebrew simply means shout/cry out joyfully.

- v.2 — KJV: "Serve the LORD with gladness" / NET: "Worship the Lord with joy" — NET renders "serve" (Hebrew "ivdu") as "Worship" — a significant interpretive choice. The Hebrew "abad" means to serve/work/minister. NET narrows it to worship; the original is broader (all of life as service). NET also has "Enter his presence with joyful singing" rather than "come before his presence with singing" — adding "joyful" for emphasis.

- v.3 — KJV: "and not we ourselves" / ASV: "and we are his" / WEB: "and we are his" / NET: "and we belong to him" — This reflects the Hebrew Ketiv/Qere variant. KJV follows the Ketiv ("lo" = not): we did NOT make ourselves. ASV/WEB/NET follow the Qere ("lo" = to him): we are HIS. NET paraphrases further as "we belong to him" — interpretive but theologically accurate.

- v.3 — NET: "Acknowledge that the Lord is God" — KJV/ASV have "Know ye that the LORD/Jehovah, he is God." NET's "Acknowledge" implies public confession rather than mere intellectual knowledge. The Hebrew "da'u" = know (experientially/relationally), which NET interprets as active acknowledgment.

- v.5 — KJV: "his mercy is everlasting" / ASV: "his lovingkindness endureth for ever" / WEB: "His loving kindness endures forever" / NET: "His loyal love endures" — The Hebrew "hesed" rendered four ways: KJV "mercy," ASV "lovingkindness" (one word), WEB "loving kindness" (two words), NET "loyal love." Each captures a facet: covenant loyalty, tender affection, merciful disposition.

- v.5 — KJV: "his truth endureth to all generations" / ASV: "his faithfulness unto all generations" / WEB: "his faithfulness to all generations" / NET: "he is faithful through all generations" — KJV's "truth" (Hebrew "emunah") is rendered "faithfulness" by ASV/WEB/NET. NET again restructures from noun to adjective: not "his faithfulness" but "he IS faithful" — personalizing the attribute.

- v.4 — KJV: "be thankful unto him, and bless his name" / NET: "Give him thanks. Praise his name" / ASV: "Give thanks unto him, and bless his name" — NET replaces "bless his name" with "Praise his name." The Hebrew "bareku" specifically means to bless/kneel before. NET's "Praise" flattens the distinction between praise (hallel) and blessing (barak) — two different Hebrew concepts.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Serve with gladness (v.2). If your service to God feels like
   a burden, something is wrong — not with the service but with
   the heart. Gladness is the oxygen of true service. Serve
   because you WANT to, not because you HAVE to.

2. Know three things (v.3). WHO: the LORD is God. WHERE: He made
   you (you didn't make yourself). WHOSE: you belong to Him.
   These three truths resolve all identity crises. You are
   created, owned, and shepherded by the living God.

3. Enter with thanksgiving (v.4). Don't come to God listing
   complaints. Come first with gratitude. Thanksgiving is the
   gate; praise is the courtyard. Gratitude opens the door to
   everything else in worship.

4. God is good, loving, and faithful — always (v.5). Not
   sometimes. Not to some people. His goodness is His nature.
   His hesed is everlasting. His faithfulness spans ALL
   generations — including yours. These three truths never change.

5. This psalm is the summary of all worship. Five verses. The
   whole theology of praise distilled: who God is, who we are,
   how to come, what He's like. If you memorize one psalm about
   worship, let it be this one.
