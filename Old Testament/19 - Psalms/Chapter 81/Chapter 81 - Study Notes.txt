Psalms - Chapter 81
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A festival psalm — God calls for joyful praise, then speaks directly, recalling the Exodus deliverance and lamenting Israel's refusal to listen: 'Oh that my people had hearkened unto me!'

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Festival / Prophetic Psalm
Attributed Author: Asaph
Key Themes: Festival worship, God's voice from Sinai, divine
longing for obedience, the tragedy of "would not," God's
willingness to bless if only Israel would listen

Historical Context: Set to "upon Gittith" (a winepress song or
Gittite instrument), this psalm was likely composed for the Feast
of Tabernacles — the trumpet at the new moon (v.3) and harvest
imagery (v.16) support this. The psalm dramatically shifts at v.5
from human worship to GOD'S voice speaking directly. God recounts
His history with Israel (deliverance from Egypt, testing at
Meribah) and then laments Israel's refusal to listen. The pathos
in vv.13-16 is extraordinary — God says "Oh that my people had
hearkened!" This is divine longing, divine grief over what could
have been. Few passages reveal God's emotional investment in His
people so tenderly.

Structure:
- Call to Joyful Worship (vv.1-5a)
- God Speaks: His Past Deliverance (vv.5b-7)
- God's Demand: No Other Gods (vv.8-10)
- Israel's Refusal and God's Judgment (vv.11-12)
- God's Lament: "Oh That They Had Listened" (vv.13-16)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.10 — "I am the LORD thy God, which brought thee out of the land
        of Egypt: open thy mouth wide, and I will fill it." (KJV)
        — God's promise is extravagant: OPEN WIDE — don't limit
        what you ask for. The God who delivered from Egypt can fill
        any emptiness. The image is of a baby bird with mouth wide
        open for feeding.

v.11-12 — "But my people would not hearken to my voice; and Israel
        would none of me. So I gave them up unto their own hearts'
        lust." (KJV) — The most terrifying judgment is not fire
        from heaven but God giving you what you want. "Gave them
        up" = abandoned them to their own desires. Getting your own
        way IS the punishment.

v.13 — "Oh that my people had hearkened unto me, and Israel had
        walked in my ways!" (KJV) — Divine pathos. God WISHES His
        people had obeyed. This is not cold sovereignty but
        passionate desire. God wanted better for Israel than Israel
        wanted for itself.

v.16 — "He should have fed them also with the finest of the wheat:
        and with honey out of the rock should I have satisfied
        thee." (KJV) — What could have been: the finest wheat and
        honey from rock — supernatural abundance. Israel's
        disobedience cost them not just God's approval but God's
        extravagant provision.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.3   — Numbers 10:10 — "In the day of your gladness... blow the
         trumpets." Festival trumpets commanded.
v.7   — Exodus 17:1-7 — The testing at Meribah (waters of strife).
v.10  — Exodus 20:2 — "I am the LORD thy God, which have brought
         thee out of the land of Egypt." First Commandment.
v.12  — Romans 1:24,26,28 — "God gave them up..." Paul uses the
         same language for divine abandonment to sin.
v.13  — Isaiah 48:18 — "O that thou hadst hearkened to my
         commandments! then had thy peace been as a river." Same
         divine grief.
v.16  — Deuteronomy 32:13 — "He made him suck honey out of the
         rock." Same provision imagery.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.5 — KJV: "where I heard a language that I understood not" / ESV:
      "I hear a language I had not known" — The speaker shifts
      here from the psalmist to God (or to Israel hearing God's
      unfamiliar voice at Sinai). This transition is the psalm's
      dramatic hinge.

v.12 — KJV: "I gave them up unto their own hearts' lust" / ESV:
       "I gave them over to their stubborn hearts" — Hebrew
       "sherirut libbam" = stubbornness/hardness of their heart.
       ESV emphasizes stubbornness; KJV emphasizes desire. Both
       describe the same reality: self-will as divine punishment.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Open your mouth wide and I will fill it" (v.10). God's
   invitation: don't limit your requests to what seems reasonable.
   Ask largely. The God of the Exodus can exceed your widest
   expectations. Small asking dishonors a great God.

2. Being given over to your own desires is the worst judgment
   (v.12). Don't romanticize "getting what I want." Sometimes the
   most severe discipline is God saying "Fine — have it your way."
   Your own counsel, divorced from God's, leads to ruin.

3. God grieves over lost potential (v.13). "Oh that they had
   listened!" God is not indifferent to our disobedience — He
   grieves. He sees what could have been. Our refusal doesn't just
   hurt us — it grieves Him.

4. Obedience unlocks God's best provision (vv.14-16). What did
   Israel miss? Subdued enemies, finest wheat, honey from rock.
   Disobedience doesn't just invite punishment — it forfeits
   blessing that was ready and waiting.
