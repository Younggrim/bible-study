Psalms - Chapter 22
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A Messianic lament — 'My God, my God, why hast thou forsaken me?' describes suffering in extraordinary detail (pierced hands and feet, casting lots for garments), yet turns to worldwide praise as all nations worship the LORD.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Individual Lament / Messianic Psalm (supreme)
Attributed Author: David — "To the chief Musician upon Aijeleth
Shahar, A Psalm of David" ("Aijeleth Shahar" = "The Doe of the
Dawn")
Key Themes: Divine abandonment, extreme suffering, mockery, physical
torment describing crucifixion, pierced hands and feet, garments
divided, the pivot from lament to worldwide praise, universal
kingdom

Historical Context: Psalm 22 is the supreme Messianic psalm —
quoted by Jesus on the cross ("My God, my God, why hast thou
forsaken me?" — Matt 27:46) and fulfilled in detail by His
crucifixion. Written approximately 1000 years before Christ and
centuries before crucifixion was invented as an execution method,
it describes: pierced hands and feet (v.16), bones out of joint
(v.14), garments divided and lots cast (v.18), surrounded by
mockers using the exact words of Matthew 27:43 (v.8), thirst
(v.15), and visible bones (v.17). The psalm's structure moves
from deepest desolation (vv.1-21) to highest praise (vv.22-31),
paralleling Christ's journey from cross to resurrection. The final
phrase "he hath done this" (v.31) parallels Jesus' "It is finished"
(John 19:30).

Structure:
- The Cry of Forsakenness (vv.1-2)
- God's Past Faithfulness (vv.3-5)
- The Sufferer's Degradation (vv.6-8)
- Trust From Birth (vv.9-11)
- Enemies Surround (vv.12-13)
- Physical Suffering Described (vv.14-18)
- Final Plea for Deliverance (vv.19-21)
- The Pivot — Praise Declared (vv.22-26)
- Universal Worship Proclaimed (vv.27-31)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "My God, my God, why hast thou forsaken me?" (KJV) — Jesus'
        cry from the cross (Matt 27:46, Mark 15:34). The most
        mysterious moment in Scripture: the Son experiences
        separation from the Father while bearing the world's sin.
        David experienced perceived abandonment; Christ experienced
        actual judicial forsakenness.

v.16 — "They pierced my hands and my feet." (KJV) — Written ~1000
        BC, centuries before crucifixion existed (invented by
        Persians, adopted by Romans). The Hebrew is debated ("ka'ari"
        = "like a lion" or "they pierced"), but the LXX (pre-
        Christian Greek translation) renders it "pierced." The
        correspondence to crucifixion is unmistakable.

v.18 — "They part my garments among them, and cast lots upon my
        vesture." (KJV) — Fulfilled precisely in John 19:23-24.
        Soldiers divided Jesus' garments and cast lots for His
        seamless robe. John specifically notes this fulfilled Psalm
        22.

v.22 — "I will declare thy name unto my brethren: in the midst of
        the congregation will I praise thee." (KJV) — The pivot.
        Quoted in Hebrews 2:12 as Christ's words. After the
        suffering, He declares God's name to His "brothers" — the
        church. From cross to resurrection to proclamation.

v.31 — "He hath done this." (KJV) / "He has done it." (ESV) —
        Three words that parallel Jesus' "It is finished" (John
        19:30). The work is complete. What God purposed in
        suffering is accomplished.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.1   — Matthew 27:46 — Jesus quotes this verse from the cross.
v.7-8 — Matthew 27:39-43 — Mockers at the cross shake heads and
         say "He trusted in God; let him deliver him." Nearly
         word-for-word fulfillment.
v.14  — John 19:34 — "Blood and water" from Jesus' side —
         consistent with pericardial effusion ("heart like wax
         melted").
v.15  — John 19:28 — "I thirst." Fulfilled from this psalm.
v.16  — John 20:25-27 — Thomas sees the nail prints in Jesus'
         hands. Physical evidence of piercing.
v.18  — John 19:23-24 — John explicitly quotes Psalm 22:18 as
         fulfilled at the cross.
v.22  — Hebrews 2:12 — Quoted as Christ's words to His brothers
         (the church) after resurrection.
v.27  — Revelation 7:9 — "A great multitude, which no man could
         number, of all nations." Universal worship fulfilled.
v.31  — John 19:30 — "It is finished." The parallel declaration
         of accomplished work.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1 — KJV: "from the words of my roaring" / ESV: "from the words
      of my groaning" — Hebrew "she'agati" = my roaring/crying out.
      Not quiet prayer but visceral, loud anguish. On the cross,
      Jesus CRIED OUT with a loud voice (Mark 15:34).

v.16 — KJV: "they pierced my hands and my feet" / ESV: "they have
       pierced my hands and feet" — The Hebrew textual issue:
       Masoretic text reads "ka'ari" (like a lion — my hands and
       feet), which is grammatically difficult. The LXX, Dead Sea
       Scrolls fragment, and Syriac versions read "pierced." Most
       scholars accept "pierced" as the original reading.

v.21 — KJV: "thou hast heard me from the horns of the unicorns" /
       ESV: "You have rescued me from the horns of the wild oxen!"
       — Hebrew "re'emim" = wild oxen (now extinct aurochs), not
       unicorns. ESV corrects KJV's rendering. More importantly,
       this is the PIVOT — "thou hast HEARD me" — deliverance
       has come. Everything after this is praise.

v.31 — KJV: "he hath done this" / ESV: "he has done it" — The
       Hebrew is simply "asah" (he has done [it]). The parallel to
       "tetelestai" (It is finished) in John 19:30 is remarkable.
       Both declarations mark the completion of God's redemptive
       work through suffering.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.1 — NET: "why have you abandoned me? I groan in prayer, but help seems
  far away" — "abandoned" is more emotionally devastating than "forsaken"
  (KJV/ESV/ASV/WEB). NET also restructures the second clause interpretively.
  ASV/WEB: "why hast thou/have you forsaken me?" — retain the traditional
  rendering Jesus quoted on the cross (Matthew 27:46).
- v.3 — NET: "You are holy; you sit as king receiving the praises of Israel"
  — adds "sit as king" (interpretive). KJV/ASV: "thou that inhabitest the
  praises of Israel" — the remarkable image of God DWELLING in/on Israel's
  praise. WEB: "you who inhabit the praises of Israel" — same. Praise is
  God's address, His throne-room.
- v.8 — WEB: "He trusts in Yahweh. Let him deliver him" — divine name
  explicit in the mockers' taunt. This is precisely what the crowds said at
  the cross (Matthew 27:43). NET: "Commit yourself to the Lord! Let the Lord
  rescue him!" — "commit yourself" is less directly mocking than "He trusted"
  (KJV/WEB).
- v.14 — NET: "My strength drains away like water; all my bones are
  dislocated" — "drains away" captures the progressive weakening. "Dislocated"
  is medically precise for "out of joint" (KJV/ESV/ASV/WEB) — describing what
  happens during crucifixion as body weight pulls joints apart. WEB: "I am
  poured out like water. All my bones are out of joint."
- v.16 — NET: "like a lion they pin my hands and feet" — follows the Masoretic
  "ka'ari" reading (like a lion) rather than "pierced" (LXX/DSS). This is a
  significant textual choice. KJV/ESV/ASV/WEB all read "pierced" — the
  clearest Messianic prophecy of crucifixion. NET's footnotes acknowledge the
  textual debate but takes the more cautious Masoretic reading.
- v.20 — NET: "Save my life from the claws of the wild dogs" — adds "claws"
  (interpretive). KJV: "my darling from the power of the dog" / ASV: "My
  darling from the power of the dog" — Hebrew "yechidati" = my only one, my
  precious one, my solitary life. WEB: "my precious life from the power of
  the dog" — captures "yechidati" as preciousness/uniqueness.
- v.21 — KJV: "the horns of the unicorns" — Hebrew "re'emim" = wild oxen
  (aurochs). KJV followed earlier translations that didn't know this now-
  extinct animal. ASV/WEB/NET: "wild-oxen"/"wild oxen" — correct zoological
  identification.
- v.31 — NET: "they will tell a future generation what he has accomplished" —
  "accomplished" is more intentional than "done" (KJV/ESV). ASV: "that he
  hath done it." WEB: "for he has done it." The final words of the psalm echo
  forward to Christ's "It is finished" (John 19:30) — the work completed,
  the suffering ended, the victory won.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Jesus entered the depths of forsakenness for you (v.1). The
   sinless Son experienced what no human has — full divine
   abandonment — so that you never would. His "Why?" was answered
   on Easter morning. If you feel forsaken, know that Christ
   endured this so that you would never be truly abandoned.

2. Past faithfulness anchors present suffering (vv.3-5). Even in
   agony, the sufferer recalls: "Our fathers trusted... thou didst
   deliver them." When you cannot feel God's presence, remember
   His track record. He has been faithful before; He will be again.

3. Suffering does not negate God's holiness (v.3). "BUT thou art
   holy." In the middle of feeling forsaken, the sufferer
   acknowledges: God is still holy. Suffering does not mean God
   has failed or is unjust. Hold both truths: I feel abandoned AND
   God is holy.

4. The psalm MOVES from suffering to worship (vv.21→22). The
   pivot is the key. Suffering is real — 21 verses of it. But it
   is not final. Praise erupts from the very one who suffered
   most. The cross leads to the empty tomb. Friday leads to
   Sunday.

5. "He has done it" (v.31). The final word is accomplishment, not
   suffering. What God intended through the Messiah's agony is
   COMPLETE. Salvation is not in progress — it is done. Rest in
   the finished work.
