Psalms - Chapter 28
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A lament turning to praise — David cries out lest God's silence mean death, asks not to be dragged away with the wicked, then suddenly rejoices because the LORD has heard and become his strength and shield.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Individual Lament / Thanksgiving
Attributed Author: David — "A Psalm of David"
Key Themes: Fear of God's silence, separation from the wicked's
fate, God as rock and shield, answered prayer producing praise,
shepherd-king theology

Historical Context: David fears that if God remains silent, he
will die ("become like them that go down into the pit"). He prays
toward the "holy oracle" (debir = the inner sanctuary, Holy of
Holies) — the symbolic location of God's presence and speech. The
psalm's structure shows the pivot from desperate plea (vv.1-5) to
joyful thanksgiving (vv.6-9). The key wicked characteristic: they
"speak peace to their neighbours, but mischief is in their hearts"
(v.3) — peaceful words concealing violent intentions. V.9's prayer
to "feed them" and "lift them up forever" echoes Psalm 23's
shepherd imagery — David as king asks God to shepherd His people.

Structure:
- Plea for God's Voice (vv.1-2)
- Separation from the Wicked (vv.3-5)
- Thanksgiving for Answered Prayer (vv.6-7)
- Prayer for God's People (vv.8-9)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "Unto thee will I cry, O LORD my rock; be not silent to me:
        lest, if thou be silent to me, I become like them that go
        down into the pit." (KJV) — God's silence feels like death.
        If God does not respond, David is as good as dead. The
        urgency: silence = abandonment = death.

v.7  — "The LORD is my strength and my shield; my heart trusted in
        him, and I am helped: therefore my heart greatly rejoiceth;
        and with my song will I praise him." (KJV) — The complete
        cycle: trust → help → rejoicing → praise. Trust precedes
        experience of help, which produces joy, which overflows in
        song.

v.9  — "Save thy people, and bless thine inheritance: feed them
        also, and lift them up for ever." (KJV) — David prays not
        just for himself but for God's people. "Feed them" =
        shepherd them. "Lift them up" = carry them. The king asks
        God to be the ultimate Shepherd-King.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.2 — KJV: "toward thy holy oracle" / ESV: "toward your most holy
      sanctuary" — Hebrew "debir" = the innermost room of the
      temple (Holy of Holies). The "oracle" because God spoke from
      between the cherubim. KJV captures the speech-dimension;
      ESV captures the spatial dimension.

v.5 — KJV: "he shall destroy them, and not build them up" / ESV:
      "he will tear them down and build them up no more" — The
      imagery is architectural: God tears down what the wicked
      build, and refuses to rebuild them. Permanent deconstruction.

v.9 — KJV: "feed them also, and lift them up for ever" / ESV: "Be
      their shepherd and carry them forever" — ESV makes the
      shepherd metaphor explicit. "Carry" (nasa = lift, bear) —
      God carries His people like a shepherd carries lambs.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.1 — ASV: "O Jehovah" / WEB: "Yahweh" / NET: "O Lord" / KJV: "O LORD my rock" — NET renders the divine name as "Lord" and replaces "rock" with "My Protector" — an interpretation of the metaphor rather than the metaphor itself. ASV/WEB preserve "My rock" with the divine name.

- v.1 — NET: "I will join those who are descending into the grave" — NET interprets "the pit" (bor) as "the grave," making the death-reference explicit. KJV/ASV/WEB retain "pit" — which can mean cistern, dungeon, or grave/Sheol, preserving the Hebrew ambiguity.

- v.2 — KJV: "thy holy oracle" / ASV: "thy holy oracle" / WEB: "your Most Holy Place" / NET: "your holy temple" — Three different renderings of Hebrew "debir." WEB capitalizes "Most Holy Place" (the inner sanctuary). NET generalizes to "temple." The debir was specifically the innermost shrine where God's presence dwelt.

- v.7 — ASV: "Jehovah is my strength and my shield" / WEB: "Yahweh is my strength and my shield" / NET: "The Lord strengthens and protects me" — NET converts nouns ("strength," "shield") into verbs ("strengthens," "protects"), losing the metaphorical power. God is not merely an actor who strengthens — He IS the strength itself.

- v.8 — KJV: "the saving strength of his anointed" / ASV: "a stronghold of salvation to his anointed" / NET: "he protects and delivers his chosen king" — NET interprets "anointed" (mashiach) as "chosen king," making the royal context explicit but removing the Messianic resonance. ASV's "stronghold of salvation" is a powerful compound metaphor.

- v.9 — KJV: "feed them also, and lift them up" / ASV: "Be their shepherd also, and bear them up" / WEB: "Be their shepherd also, and bear them up" / NET: "Care for them like a shepherd and carry them in your arms at all times!" — NET adds "in your arms" and "at all times" — neither in the Hebrew but creating a tender, expanded image. ASV/WEB's "bear them up" is closer to the Hebrew "nasa" (to lift, carry).

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. God's silence feels like death (v.1). When God seems quiet, the
   spiritual danger is real — not physical death but the death of
   hope. In silence, keep crying out. Persistence in prayer during
   divine silence is the test of faith.

2. Beware peaceful words with mischievous hearts (v.3). The most
   dangerous enemies speak kindly while plotting harm. Discernment
   requires looking beyond words to patterns, fruit, and
   consistency.

3. Trust produces the sequence: help → joy → praise (v.7). The
   chain begins with trust, not with favorable circumstances.
   Trust is the first domino. Set it in motion and the sequence
   follows.

4. Pray beyond yourself (v.9). David moves from personal crisis to
   communal intercession. True spiritual maturity expands outward —
   from "save ME" to "save YOUR PEOPLE." Let your personal
   deliverance produce intercession for others.

5. God carries His people forever (v.9). Not temporarily, not
   conditionally — forever. The Shepherd does not set down His
   lambs. He carries them through every wilderness.
