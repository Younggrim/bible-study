Psalms - Chapter 64
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A lament — David prays for protection from the secret plots of the wicked whose tongues are sharp swords; God will suddenly shoot His arrow at them, and all mankind will fear and declare His work.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Individual Lament Psalm
Attributed Author: David
Key Themes: Secret plots of enemies, the tongue as weapon, God's
sudden counter-attack, divine poetic justice, the righteous
rejoicing in God's judgment

Historical Context: No specific historical event is named in the
superscription, but the psalm describes a situation where David
faces secret conspiracies and verbal attacks from those who plot in
hidden. This could fit many periods in David's life — Saul's
courtiers, Absalom's conspiracy, or any number of political
intrigues. The psalm is notable for its "boomerang" structure: the
arrows the wicked aim at the righteous (vv.3-4) become God's arrow
aimed at them (v.7). Their own tongue brings them down (v.8). The
theme of hidden plots met by sudden divine intervention runs
throughout.

Structure:
- Prayer for Protection (vv.1-2)
- The Enemy's Secret Attacks (vv.3-6)
- God's Sudden Counter-Strike (vv.7-8)
- Universal Recognition of God's Justice (vv.9-10)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.3  — "Who whet their tongue like a sword, and bend their bows
        to shoot their arrows, even bitter words." (KJV) — Words
        ARE weapons. The tongue is deliberately sharpened (whet)
        for maximum damage. Bitter words are arrows aimed from
        concealment.

v.5  — "They encourage themselves in an evil matter: they commune
        of laying snares privily; they say, Who shall see them?"
        (KJV) — The psychology of conspiracy: mutual reinforcement
        in evil ("encourage themselves"), secret planning ("laying
        snares privily"), and assumed invisibility ("who shall see
        them?"). They forget God sees everything.

v.7  — "But God shall shoot at them with an arrow; suddenly shall
        they be wounded." (KJV) — The divine reversal. They shot
        arrows (v.3); now God shoots back. They attacked "suddenly"
        (v.4); now God strikes "suddenly." Perfect poetic justice —
        the punishment mirrors the crime.

v.9  — "And all men shall fear, and shall declare the work of God;
        for they shall wisely consider of his doing." (KJV) — The
        purpose of divine judgment: universal recognition. When God
        acts, people stop, observe, and acknowledge His hand. His
        justice is visible and instructive.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1 — KJV: "preserve my life from fear of the enemy" / ESV:
      "preserve my life from dread of the enemy" — Both translate
      "pachad" (fear/dread). The prayer is not just for physical
      preservation but for freedom from the paralyzing fear that
      enemies create.

v.6 — KJV: "They search out iniquities; they accomplish a diligent
      search" / ESV: "They search out injustice, saying, 'We have
      accomplished a diligent search'" — The wicked are thorough in
      their evil planning. They "research" their wickedness with
      the same diligence a scholar would apply to study. But v.6b
      adds: "the inward thought of every one of them, and the
      heart, is deep" — human wickedness is deeper than even their
      own investigations reveal.

v.8 — KJV: "So they shall make their own tongue to fall upon
      themselves" / ESV: "with their own tongues turned against
      them" — The tongue that was their weapon becomes their
      downfall. Their own words condemn them.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.1 — NET: "Listen to me, O God, as I offer my lament! Protect my life from the enemy's terrifying attacks" — NET adds "terrifying attacks" (not in Hebrew "pachad" which is simply "fear/dread of the enemy"). WEB stays with KJV: "Preserve my life from fear of the enemy."

- v.2 — NET: "from the crowd of evildoers" vs. KJV: "from the insurrection of the workers of iniquity" — NET's "crowd" is weaker than KJV's "insurrection" (Hebrew "rigshah" — tumult, conspiracy, uprising). WEB: "from the noisy crowd of the ones doing evil" — blends both ideas.

- v.5 — NET: "They encourage one another to carry out their evil deed" / WEB: "They encourage themselves in evil plans" — NET makes it collaborative ("one another"); KJV/ASV/WEB make it self-reinforcing ("encourage themselves"). Both readings reveal how evil gains momentum.

- v.7 — NET: "suddenly they will be wounded by an arrow" / WEB: "They will be suddenly struck down with an arrow" — Both preserve the dramatic reversal (the arrow-shooters of v.3-4 are now shot). ASV: "With an arrow suddenly shall they be wounded."

- v.10 — ASV: "The righteous shall be glad in Jehovah" / WEB: "The righteous shall be glad in Yahweh" vs. KJV: "The righteous shall be glad in the LORD" — The covenant name explicit in ASV/WEB. NET: "The godly will rejoice in the Lord" — uses "godly" rather than "righteous."

- v.10 — NET: "All the morally upright will boast" vs. KJV: "all the upright in heart shall glory" — NET adds "morally" (not in Hebrew) and uses "boast" for "glory" (halal). WEB: "All the upright in heart shall praise him" — renders "halal" as "praise" (its most common meaning).

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Words aimed in secret are still seen by God (v.5). "Who shall
   see them?" — God does. Every whispered slander, every secret
   plot, every hidden cruelty is fully visible to the One who
   sees all. Privacy from God is an illusion.

2. God's justice often mirrors the crime (v.7). Arrows shot at the
   righteous become God's arrow at the wicked. What you launch
   against others often returns to you. This is not karma — it is
   divine justice with poetic precision.

3. God's timing is sudden (v.7). The wicked assume they have
   endless time. Then suddenly — God acts. Don't mistake God's
   patience for God's absence. His intervention comes swiftly when
   it comes.

4. The purpose of judgment is declaration (v.9). God judges not
   merely to punish but to teach. His acts of justice declare His
   character to all who observe. When God acts, wisdom responds by
   "wisely considering" what He has done.
