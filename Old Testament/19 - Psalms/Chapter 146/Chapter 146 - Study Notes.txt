Psalms - Chapter 146
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A Hallel psalm opening the final five — 'Praise ye the LORD; praise the LORD, O my soul'; trust not in princes who perish; happy is he whose help is the God of Jacob who executes justice, feeds the hungry, and lifts up the fallen.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Hallelujah Psalm (begins the Final Five)
Attributed Author: Anonymous
Key Themes: Lifelong praise, trusting God over princes, God as
helper of the helpless, God who executes justice for the
oppressed, the Creator who cares for the marginalized

Historical Context: Psalm 146 begins the final five "Hallelujah
Psalms" (146-150) that close the Psalter. Each begins and ends
with "Praise the LORD" (Hallelujah). These five psalms form a
crescendo of praise that builds to the universal climax of Psalm
150. Psalm 146 focuses on WHY God should be praised: He is the
reliable helper (unlike human princes who die), the Creator who
keeps faith forever, the champion of the oppressed, hungry,
prisoners, blind, bowed down, strangers, widows, and fatherless.
This psalm heavily influenced Mary's Magnificat (Luke 1:46-55).

Structure:
- Personal Commitment to Lifelong Praise (vv.1-2)
- Warning Against Trusting Humans (vv.3-4)
- The Blessed Trust in God (v.5)
- God's Character: Creator and Keeper (v.6)
- God's Actions: Justice and Mercy (vv.7-9)
- God's Eternal Reign (v.10)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.3-4 — "Put not your trust in princes, nor in the son of man,
        in whom there is no help. His breath goeth forth, he
        returneth to his earth; in that very day his thoughts
        perish." (KJV) — The bluntest political theology in
        Scripture. Don't trust princes (politicians, leaders,
        powerful people). WHY? Because they DIE. Their plans
        perish with them. Only God's plans endure.

v.5  — "Happy is he that hath the God of Jacob for his help,
        whose hope is in the LORD his God." (KJV) — True happiness
        = having God as your help. Not princes but the God of
        JACOB — the God who wrestles with flawed people.

v.7-9 — "Which executeth judgment for the oppressed: which giveth
        food to the hungry. The LORD looseth the prisoners: The
        LORD openeth the eyes of the blind: the LORD raiseth them
        that are bowed down: the LORD loveth the righteous: The
        LORD preserveth the strangers; he relieveth the fatherless
        and widow." (KJV) — Nine actions of God toward the
        vulnerable: justice for oppressed, food for hungry, freedom
        for prisoners, sight for blind, lifting the bowed, loving
        the righteous, preserving strangers, relieving orphans and
        widows. This is who God IS.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.3 — KJV: "nor in the son of man, in whom there is no help" / NET: "or in human beings, who cannot deliver" — NET replaces "son of man" (Hebrew "ben-adam") with generic "human beings" and "no help" with "cannot deliver," making the limitation about ability rather than absence.
- v.4 — KJV: "His breath goeth forth" / ASV: "His breath goeth forth" / WEB: "His spirit departs" / NET: "Their life's breath departs" — Hebrew "ruach" can be breath/spirit/wind. KJV/ASV=breath; WEB=spirit; NET adds "life's" for clarity.
- v.7 — KJV: "The LORD looseth the prisoners" / ASV: "Jehovah looseth the prisoners" / WEB: "Yahweh frees the prisoners" / NET: "The Lord releases the imprisoned" — Four verbs for the same liberation: looseth/frees/releases. All convey the same truth with different register.
- v.9 — KJV: "The LORD preserveth the strangers" / ASV: "Jehovah preserveth the sojourners" / WEB: "Yahweh preserves the foreigners" / NET: "The Lord protects the resident foreigner" — NET's "resident foreigner" precisely renders Hebrew "ger" (sojourner/resident alien), specifying legal status.
- v.9 — KJV: "he relieveth the fatherless and widow" / NET: "He lifts up the fatherless and the widow" — NET uses "lifts up" (same Hebrew word used in v.8) while KJV uses "relieves," showing different aspects of God's care.
- v.9 — KJV: "the way of the wicked he turneth upside down" / WEB: same / NET: "he opposes the wicked" — NET simplifies the vivid Hebrew image of path-overturning into simple "opposes," losing the dramatic imagery.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Don't put ultimate trust in leaders (vv.3-4). Vote, engage,
   participate — but don't TRUST princes with your ultimate hope.
   They are mortal. Their plans perish. Only God's plans endure.

2. God champions the marginalized (vv.7-9). If you want to be
   like God, care for who He cares for: the oppressed, hungry,
   imprisoned, blind, bowed down, strangers, orphans, widows.
   This is God's heart.

3. Praise for a lifetime (v.2). "While I live will I praise the
   LORD: I will sing praises unto my God while I have any being."
   Praise is not seasonal — it's lifelong. As long as you have
   being, you have reason to praise.

4. God reigns forever (v.10). "The LORD shall reign for ever."
   Every other ruler perishes. This King endures. Anchor yourself
   to the eternal throne.
