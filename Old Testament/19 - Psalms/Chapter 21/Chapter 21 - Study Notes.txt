Psalms - Chapter 21
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A royal psalm — thanksgiving for the king's victory and God's blessings upon him; the king trusted in the LORD, and through the Most High's steadfast love he shall not be moved.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Royal Thanksgiving Psalm
Attributed Author: David — "To the chief Musician, A Psalm of
David"
Key Themes: King's joy in divine strength, answered prayer,
blessings received, trust in God's steadfast love, judgment on
enemies, divine exaltation

Historical Context: Psalm 21 is the post-battle companion to
Psalm 20 (pre-battle prayer). What the people prayed for in Psalm
20, they celebrate in Psalm 21. The king has been victorious, and
the congregation acknowledges that the victory came from God, not
military might. The language is both Davidic and Messianic — "life"
and "length of days forever and ever" (v.4) exceeds what any
earthly king receives and points to the eternal Messianic King.
The structure mirrors Psalm 20: the people speak about the king
(vv.1-7), address God about judgment on enemies (vv.8-12), and
close with praise (v.13).

Structure:
- The King's Blessings Celebrated (vv.1-7)
- God's Judgment on Enemies (vv.8-12)
- Closing Praise (v.13)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.4  — "He asked life of thee, and thou gavest it him, even length
        of days for ever and ever." (KJV) — Exceeds mere human
        lifespan — "forever and ever" points beyond David to the
        Messianic King whose reign is eternal. Christ asked for
        life and received resurrection — life without end.

v.6  — "Thou hast made him exceeding glad with thy countenance."
        (KJV) — The king's highest joy is not military victory but
        God's face (countenance). The blessing of God's presence
        exceeds the blessing of God's gifts.

v.7  — "For the king trusteth in the LORD, and through the mercy
        of the most High he shall not be moved." (KJV) — The reason
        for the king's stability: trust in God and the steadfast
        love (chesed) of the Most High. Not strategy, not army,
        not alliances — trust and mercy.

v.13 — "Be thou exalted, LORD, in thine own strength: so will we
        sing and praise thy power." (KJV) — The psalm ends by
        crediting God entirely. The victory was divine strength;
        the praise returns to God.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.1   — Psalm 20:5 — "We will rejoice in thy salvation." Psalm 20
         anticipated; Psalm 21 fulfills.
v.3   — Psalm 20:4 — "Grant thee according to thine own heart."
         Prayer answered in Psalm 21:2.
v.4   — 2 Samuel 7:16 — "Thy throne shall be established for
         ever." Davidic covenant — eternal kingdom.
v.6   — Psalm 16:11 — "In thy presence is fulness of joy."
         Same countenance-joy theology.
v.7   — Psalm 16:8 — "I shall not be moved." Same stability
         from God's presence.
v.9   — Malachi 4:1 — "The day cometh, that shall burn as an
         oven." Same fiery oven judgment imagery.
v.13  — Psalm 57:5 — "Be thou exalted, O God, above the heavens."
         Same closing exaltation.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.3 — KJV: "thou preventest him with the blessings of goodness" /
      ESV: "you meet him with rich blessings" — KJV "prevent" is
      archaic English meaning "go before/precede" (Latin praevenio).
      ESV clarifies: God MEETS the king — His blessings arrive
      before the king even asks.

v.4 — KJV/ESV both: "length of days forever and ever" — This
      exceeds any earthly king's lifespan. Messianic interpreters
      see this pointing to Christ's eternal reign. Davidic
      interpreters see hyperbolic court language or dynastic
      perpetuity.

v.9 — KJV: "a fiery oven" / ESV: "a blazing oven" — The image of
      judgment as a furnace. Those who opposed God's king are
      consumed like fuel in an oven. Intense, thorough, complete
      destruction.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Answered prayer deserves public celebration (vv.1-5). What you
   prayed for in private (Psalm 20), celebrate publicly when
   granted (Psalm 21). Do not forget to return thanks for prayers
   answered.

2. God's blessings often precede our asking (v.3). He "meets" us
   with blessings — arrives ahead of us. Grace is not always
   reactive to our requests; it is often proactive, preparing
   goodness before we know to ask.

3. The king's joy is in God's strength, not his own (v.1). True
   leaders rejoice not in their own power but in God's. If your
   joy is in your own strength, every loss diminishes you. If your
   joy is in GOD'S strength, you are never diminished.

4. Trust + steadfast love = stability (v.7). The equation for being
   unmoved: the king trusts; God is faithful. Your trust meeting
   God's chesed produces permanence nothing can shake.

5. All glory returns to God (v.13). The psalm's final word is
   worship — not of the victorious king but of the God who gave
   the victory. Every human triumph finds its proper ending in
   divine praise.
