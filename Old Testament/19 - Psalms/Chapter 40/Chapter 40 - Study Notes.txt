Psalms - Chapter 40
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A thanksgiving and lament psalm — David recalls God lifting him from the pit and putting a new song in his mouth; sacrifice is not God's desire but obedience ('in the volume of the book it is written of me'); yet troubles surround him.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Thanksgiving Psalm / Messianic Psalm
Attributed Author: David ("To the chief Musician, A Psalm of
David")
Key Themes: Patient waiting for God, deliverance from the pit,
the new song, sacrifice versus obedience, Messianic prophecy
(Christ's incarnation and will), delight in God's will

Historical Context: Psalm 40 is one of the most significant
Messianic psalms in the Psalter. While it begins as David's
personal testimony of deliverance, verses 6-8 transcend David's
experience and are directly applied to Christ in Hebrews 10:5-7,
where the author identifies these words as spoken by the Son of
God at His incarnation: "Lo, I come...to do thy will, O God."

The psalm has a two-part structure that puzzled ancient
interpreters. The first half (vv.1-10) is confident thanksgiving
and testimony, while the second half (vv.11-17) is urgent lament
and plea for help. This has led some scholars to suggest two
originally separate psalms were joined (note that vv.13-17 appear
independently as Psalm 70). However, the structure also reflects
authentic spiritual experience: past deliverance does not prevent
present crisis, and the memory of God's faithfulness becomes the
foundation for new prayers.

The Messianic content is remarkable. The phrase "mine ears hast
thou opened" (v.6, KJV) is rendered in the Septuagint (and
Hebrews 10:5) as "a body hast thou prepared me" — pointing to
the incarnation. "In the volume of the book it is written of me"
(v.7) suggests all Scripture testifies to Christ. "I delight to
do thy will" (v.8) describes perfect obedience that no mere human
has achieved — fulfilled only in Christ.

Structure:
- Testimony of Patient Waiting and Deliverance (vv. 1-3)
- The Blessedness of Trust (vv. 4-5)
- Sacrifice and Obedience: The Messianic Declaration (vv. 6-8)
- Proclamation in the Great Congregation (vv. 9-10)
- Plea for Continued Mercy (v. 11)
- Present Distress and Urgent Cry (vv. 12-17)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
Verse 1-2 — "I waited patiently for the LORD; and he inclined
unto me, and heard my cry. He brought me up also out of an
horrible pit, out of the miry clay, and set my feet upon a rock,
and established my goings." (KJV)
"Waited patiently" is literally "waiting, I waited" (qavoh
qivviti) — an intensified Hebrew construction emphasizing
prolonged, persistent waiting. The "horrible pit" (bor sha'on —
pit of destruction/roaring) and "miry clay" (tit hayaven —
miry/sinking mud) portray desperate helplessness. God's response:
solid rock, sure footing, established steps. The transformation
is total.

Verse 6-8 — "Sacrifice and offering thou didst not desire; mine
ears hast thou opened: burnt offering and sin offering hast thou
not required. Then said I, Lo, I come: in the volume of the book
it is written of me, I delight to do thy will, O my God: yea, thy
law is within my heart." (KJV)
The theological center of the psalm. God does not ultimately
desire the sacrificial system — He desires obedience from the
heart. "Mine ears hast thou opened" literally means "ears thou
hast dug/pierced for me" — possibly alluding to the ear-piercing
of a willing servant (Exodus 21:6). Hebrews 10:5-7 applies this
to Christ's incarnation.

Verse 3 — "And he hath put a new song in my mouth, even praise
unto our God: many shall see it, and fear, and shall trust in the
LORD." (KJV)
Deliverance produces a "new song" — not merely repetition of old
praise but fresh expression born from fresh experience. Moreover,
David's testimony becomes evangelistic: "many shall see...and
trust." One person's deliverance becomes another person's
invitation to faith.

Verse 17 — "But I am poor and needy; yet the Lord thinketh upon
me: thou art my help and my deliverer; make no tarrying, O my
God." (KJV)
The psalm's final verse perfectly balances human weakness ("poor
and needy") with divine attentiveness ("the Lord thinketh upon
me"). Despite his lowliness, David is not forgotten. God actively
considers, plans for, and moves toward His afflicted servant.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.1-2 — Psalm 27:14; Psalm 130:5-6: The theology of waiting —
God's timing requires patient endurance, but His response is
certain.

v.2 — Psalm 69:2,14: Deliverance from miry clay/deep waters —
Psalm 69 uses the same imagery from a position still in the pit,
while Psalm 40 testifies from solid ground.

v.3 — Psalm 33:3; Psalm 96:1; Revelation 5:9: The "new song"
motif — fresh praise arising from fresh deliverance, culminating
in the new song of the redeemed in heaven.

v.6-8 — Hebrews 10:5-10: The most important cross-reference.
The author of Hebrews directly attributes these words to Christ
at His incarnation, interpreting them as explaining why animal
sacrifices are superseded by Christ's perfect obedience.

v.6 — 1 Samuel 15:22; Hosea 6:6; Micah 6:6-8: The prophetic
tradition of obedience over sacrifice — God desires heart
compliance, not ritual performance.

v.6 — Exodus 21:5-6: The willing servant whose ear is pierced —
a possible background to "mine ears hast thou opened," suggesting
voluntary, permanent, joyful servitude.

v.7 — Luke 24:27,44; John 5:39: "In the volume of the book it is
written of me" — Christ's own claim that all Scripture testifies
about Him.

v.13-17 — Psalm 70:1-5: These verses appear as an independent
psalm (Psalm 70), suggesting they circulated separately as an
urgent prayer for help.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.2 — "an horrible pit" (KJV) / "the pit of destruction" (ESV):
Hebrew bor sha'on — bor means pit, cistern, or dungeon; sha'on
means destruction, roaring, or tumult. KJV's "horrible" captures
the terror; ESV's "destruction" identifies the pit's nature. The
combination suggests a cistern of chaos — a place of drowning
destruction.

v.6 — "mine ears hast thou opened" (KJV) / "you have given me an
open ear" (ESV): Hebrew oznayim karita li — literally "ears you
have dug/pierced for me." This is the most theologically
significant translation issue in the psalm. The Septuagint renders
this as "a body you have prepared for me" (soma katartiso moi),
which is the version quoted in Hebrews 10:5. The Hebrew likely
refers to ears being "dug out" (created/opened) for hearing and
obedience. The Septuagint's broader "body" may be an
interpretive expansion — ears as representative of the whole body
prepared for obedient service.

v.7 — "in the volume of the book" (KJV) / "in the scroll of the
book" (ESV): Hebrew bimgillat-sepher — a scroll (megillah) of a
book/document. This refers to what is written about the speaker
in God's authoritative record — whether the Torah, the broader
Scripture, or the heavenly book of God's decree. Christ claimed
all Scripture testified of Him.

v.12 — "mine iniquities have taken hold upon me, so that I am not
able to look up" (KJV) / "my iniquities have overtaken me, and I
cannot see" (ESV): A puzzle in Messianic interpretation. How can
Christ, who is sinless, speak of "mine iniquities"? The answer
lies in substitutionary atonement — Christ bore our sins as His
own (2 Corinthians 5:21, Isaiah 53:6). The shift from v.8's
perfect obedience to v.12's sin-bearing mirrors the movement
from incarnation to crucifixion.

v.17 — "the Lord thinketh upon me" (KJV) / "the Lord takes
thought for me" (ESV): Hebrew yachashav li — "He thinks/plans
toward me." The same verb (chashab) used for "imputing" sin in
Psalm 32:2 is here used positively — God "reckons" the poor and
needy as worthy of attention. He actively plans on their behalf.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.1 — ASV: "I waited patiently for Jehovah" / WEB: "I waited patiently for Yahweh" / NET: "I relied completely on the Lord" — NET changes "waited patiently" to "relied completely" — a significant shift. Hebrew "qavvoh qivviti" is literally "waiting I waited" (emphatic doubling). The concept is patient, expectant waiting — not mere reliance. NET loses the temporal/patience dimension.

- v.2 — NET: "He lifted me out of the watery pit, out of the slimy mud" — NET adds "watery" to clarify "horrible pit" (bor sha'on). KJV's "horrible pit" and ASV's "horrible pit" preserve the terror; NET's "watery pit" specifies but domesticates the image.

- v.6 — KJV: "mine ears hast thou opened" / ASV: "Mine ears hast thou opened" / WEB: "You have opened my ears" / NET: "You make that quite clear to me" — NET completely removes the "ears opened" phrase, paraphrasing the entire verse. This eliminates the crucial Hebrew "oznayim karita li" (ears you have dug/pierced for me) — the very phrase the LXX rendered as "a body you prepared for me" (quoted in Hebrews 10:5). A major loss.

- v.10 — ASV: "I have not concealed thy lovingkindness and thy truth" / WEB: "I have not concealed your loving kindness and your truth" / NET: "I have not neglected to tell the great assembly about your loyal love and faithfulness" — NET's "loyal love" for chesed and "faithfulness" for emet. ASV/WEB preserve "lovingkindness and truth" — the traditional rendering of the great Hebrew word-pair "chesed v'emet."

- v.11 — ASV: "Let thy lovingkindness and thy truth continually preserve me" / WEB: "Let your loving kindness and your truth continually preserve me" / NET: "May your loyal love and faithfulness continually protect me" — Again NET's "loyal love" vs. ASV/WEB's "lovingkindness." NET's "protect" replaces "preserve" (natsar = guard, watch over, preserve) — a subtle but important difference between protection from attack and preservation of existence.

- v.16 — ASV: "Jehovah be magnified" / WEB: "Let Yahweh be exalted" / NET: "May the Lord be praised" — Three different renderings: magnified (made great), exalted (lifted up), praised (thanked). Hebrew "yigdal YHWH" = let YHWH be great/magnified. ASV is most literal; NET's "praised" is weakest — magnification implies God's greatness being recognized, not merely verbal praise.

- v.17 — KJV: "yet the Lord thinketh upon me" / ASV: "Yet the Lord thinketh upon me" / WEB: "May the Lord think about me" / NET: "May the Lord pay attention to me" — NET's "pay attention" is dismissive compared to the Hebrew "chashab" (to think, plan, reckon toward). God doesn't merely "pay attention" — He actively plans and purposes good for the needy.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "I waited patiently for the LORD" (v.1) — the Hebrew doubles
the verb for emphasis. Waiting is not passive idleness but active,
expectant endurance. David's waiting was rewarded with complete
transformation: from pit to rock, from sinking to standing. What
are you currently waiting for? Can you wait with expectation
rather than resignation?

2. Your testimony of deliverance becomes others' invitation to
faith (v.3: "many shall see it, and fear, and shall trust"). Your
story is not just for you. The pit God pulled you from and the
rock He placed you on is meant to be proclaimed. Are you sharing
your "new song" with others, or keeping your testimony private?

3. Verses 6-8 reveal that God's ultimate desire is not religious
activity but heart obedience. You can perform every spiritual
discipline and still miss what God wants most: willing, delighted
submission to His will. Is your relationship with God
characterized by duty or delight? Is His law "within your heart"
(v.8) or merely on your schedule?

4. The psalm's two halves (thanksgiving in vv.1-10, lament in
vv.11-17) reflect real spiritual life. Past deliverance does not
immunize against future crisis. The memory of God's faithfulness
does not prevent new problems, but it provides a foundation for
new faith. Can you hold gratitude for past rescue and honesty
about present struggle simultaneously?

5. "I am poor and needy; yet the Lord thinketh upon me" (v.17).
You do not need to be impressive, self-sufficient, or spiritually
advanced for God to attend to you. He thinks upon the poor and
needy — those who know they have nothing to bring. Your
insufficiency is not a barrier to God's attention; it is the
very thing that draws it.
