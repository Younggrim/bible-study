Psalms - Chapter 119
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
The longest psalm and chapter in the Bible — an acrostic meditation on God's Word (Torah) in 176 verses across 22 stanzas, celebrating the law as a lamp, delight, comfort, and source of life through every circumstance.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Acrostic Wisdom Psalm / Torah Psalm
Attributed Author: Anonymous (variously attributed to David,
Ezra, or Daniel; most likely a post-exilic composition)
Key Themes: The supremacy and sufficiency of God's Word, love for
God's law, obedience as delight, Scripture as guide/lamp/comfort/
life/treasure, persecution for righteousness, longing for God
through His Word, the Word as source of wisdom/purity/freedom

Historical Context: Psalm 119 is the longest chapter in the Bible
— 176 verses divided into 22 stanzas of 8 verses each, one for
each letter of the Hebrew alphabet. Within each stanza, every
verse begins with that Hebrew letter. It is the most elaborate
acrostic in Scripture — an A-to-Z meditation on God's Word.
Nearly every verse contains one of eight Hebrew words for God's
revelation: torah (law), dabar (word), mishpatim (judgments),
edot (testimonies), mitzvot (commandments), chuqqim (statutes),
piqqudim (precepts), and imrah (saying/promise). The psalm is not
a legal document about rules — it is a LOVE LETTER to the Word of
God. The psalmist is clearly under persecution (vv.23, 51, 61, 69,
78, 84-87, 95, 110, 121, 134, 157, 161) yet clings to God's Word
as his life, comfort, delight, and counsel. This is perhaps the
most sustained meditation on Scripture in all of Scripture.

Structure:
- Aleph (vv.1-8): The blessedness of the obedient
- Beth (vv.9-16): Cleansing through the Word
- Gimel (vv.17-24): A stranger seeking God's commands
- Daleth (vv.25-32): Reviving through the Word
- He (vv.33-40): Prayer for understanding
- Vav (vv.41-48): Freedom through obedience
- Zayin (vv.49-56): Comfort in affliction
- Cheth (vv.57-64): God as portion
- Teth (vv.65-72): Good affliction teaches
- Yod (vv.73-80): Created for understanding
- Kaph (vv.81-88): Fainting yet faithful
- Lamed (vv.89-96): The Word settled in heaven
- Mem (vv.97-104): Wiser through the Word
- Nun (vv.105-112): A lamp to my feet
- Samekh (vv.113-120): God as hiding place
- Ayin (vv.121-128): Time for God to work
- Pe (vv.129-136): Light from the Word
- Tsade (vv.137-144): Righteous testimonies

- Resh (vv.153-160): Plead my cause
- Shin (vv.161-168): Great peace
- Tav (vv.169-176): Final cry and praise

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------

REFLECTION
------------------------------------
1. Hide the Word in your heart (v.11). Memorize Scripture. Not
   as academic exercise but as sin-prevention. What is stored
   in the heart becomes available in the moment of temptation.

2. Affliction teaches (vv.67, 71). If you're suffering, you may
   be in school. "It is good for me that I have been afflicted,
   that I might learn thy statutes." Pain is not purposeless
   when it drives you to the Word.

3. The Word is settled (v.89). Don't let cultural pressure move
   you from Scripture's authority. God's Word is fixed in heaven
   — not subject to earthly revision.

4. One step at a time (v.105). The Word doesn't show you the
   whole journey — it shows you the next step. Walk by faith in
   the light you have, and more light will come.

5. Love the law and find peace (v.165). Peace comes not from
   escaping difficulty but from loving God's Word so deeply that
   nothing can shake you. Those who love His law have a peace
   the world cannot give or take.

6. End with honesty (v.176). After the most extensive devotion
   to God's Word in Scripture, the psalmist confesses: "I have
   gone astray." Even the most devoted still need the Shepherd.
   Love for God's Word coexists with awareness of weakness.
