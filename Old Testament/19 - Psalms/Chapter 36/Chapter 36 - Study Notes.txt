Psalms - Chapter 36
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A psalm contrasting wickedness and God's love — the wicked have no fear of God, but God's steadfast love reaches the heavens, His faithfulness the clouds; in His light we see light.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Wisdom Psalm / Hymn of Praise (combination)
Attributed Author: David ("To the chief Musician, A Psalm of David
the servant of the LORD")
Key Themes: The wickedness of the wicked (no fear of God), God's
steadfast love reaching to the heavens, the river of God's
delights, the fountain of life, seeing light in God's light

Historical Context: Psalm 36 is unique in combining a wisdom
meditation on wickedness (vv.1-4) with a soaring hymn to God's
lovingkindness (vv.5-9), followed by a brief prayer (vv.10-12).
The superscription identifies David as "the servant of the LORD"
— a title of special honor (also given to Moses, Joshua, and the
prophets), emphasizing David's role as God's covenant servant.

The psalm's structure creates a powerful contrast. The first
section (vv.1-4) is a clinical, almost psychological portrait of
wickedness from the inside — beginning with the root cause ("no
fear of God before his eyes") and tracing its effects through
self-flattery, corrupt speech, the abandonment of wisdom, and
deliberate plotting of evil. Then, with stunning rhetorical
effect, David turns from the cramped, dark world of the wicked to
the limitless expanse of God's attributes in verses 5-9.

Verses 5-9 contain some of the most beautiful theology in the
Psalter. God's love reaches to the heavens, His faithfulness to
the clouds, His righteousness stands like mountains, His judgments
are unfathomable as the deep ocean. The imagery builds to the
magnificent declaration: "For with thee is the fountain of life:
in thy light shall we see light" (v.9) — a verse that has
profoundly shaped Christian theology about the nature of God as
the source of all life and all knowledge.

Structure:
- Portrait of the Wicked: No Fear of God (vv. 1-4)
- The Limitless Attributes of God (vv. 5-9)
- Prayer for Continued Lovingkindness (vv. 10-12)

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------

REFLECTION
------------------------------------
1. The portrait of wickedness begins with "no fear of God before
his eyes" (v.1). The fear of the LORD is not merely one virtue
among many — it is the foundation. When fear of God erodes,
everything else follows: self-deception (v.2), corrupt speech
(v.3), abandonment of wisdom (v.3), and deliberate evil (v.4).
How actively do you cultivate the fear of God?

2. Verses 5-6 measure God's attributes by creation's scale. His
love is not small, not limited, not conditional — it reaches to
the heavens. His faithfulness extends to the clouds. When you feel
God's love is inadequate for your situation, remember: you are
trying to outrun something as vast as the sky.

3. "The river of thy pleasures" (v.8) — the word for "pleasures"
shares its root with "Eden." God Himself is paradise. The deepest
human longing for joy, beauty, and satisfaction is ultimately a
longing for God. Are you seeking Eden in created things, or
drinking from the Source directly?

4. "In thy light shall we see light" (v.9) has profound
epistemological implications: God is the necessary precondition
for all true knowledge. Apart from His revelation, we cannot
rightly interpret reality. How does this shape your approach to
wisdom, education, and decision-making?

5. The psalm contrasts two worlds: the cramped, dark world of the
wicked (vv.1-4) who see only themselves, and the vast, luminous
world of those who know God (vv.5-9) who see infinite love,
boundless faithfulness, and eternal light. Which world do you
inhabit moment by moment?
