Psalms - Chapter 36
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A psalm contrasting wickedness and God's love — the wicked have no fear of God, but God's steadfast love reaches the heavens, His faithfulness the clouds; in His light we see light.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Wisdom Psalm / Hymn of Praise (combination)
Attributed Author: David ("To the chief Musician, A Psalm of David
the servant of the LORD")
Key Themes: The wickedness of the wicked (no fear of God), God's
steadfast love reaching to the heavens, the river of God's
delights, the fountain of life, seeing light in God's light

Historical Context: Psalm 36 is unique in combining a wisdom
meditation on wickedness (vv.1-4) with a soaring hymn to God's
lovingkindness (vv.5-9), followed by a brief prayer (vv.10-12).
The superscription identifies David as "the servant of the LORD"
— a title of special honor (also given to Moses, Joshua, and the
prophets), emphasizing David's role as God's covenant servant.

The psalm's structure creates a powerful contrast. The first
section (vv.1-4) is a clinical, almost psychological portrait of
wickedness from the inside — beginning with the root cause ("no
fear of God before his eyes") and tracing its effects through
self-flattery, corrupt speech, the abandonment of wisdom, and
deliberate plotting of evil. Then, with stunning rhetorical
effect, David turns from the cramped, dark world of the wicked to
the limitless expanse of God's attributes in verses 5-9.

Verses 5-9 contain some of the most beautiful theology in the
Psalter. God's love reaches to the heavens, His faithfulness to
the clouds, His righteousness stands like mountains, His judgments
are unfathomable as the deep ocean. The imagery builds to the
magnificent declaration: "For with thee is the fountain of life:
in thy light shall we see light" (v.9) — a verse that has
profoundly shaped Christian theology about the nature of God as
the source of all life and all knowledge.

Structure:
- Portrait of the Wicked: No Fear of God (vv. 1-4)
- The Limitless Attributes of God (vv. 5-9)
- Prayer for Continued Lovingkindness (vv. 10-12)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
Verse 1 — "The transgression of the wicked saith within my heart,
that there is no fear of God before his eyes." (KJV)
The root of all wickedness is identified: the absence of the fear
of God. Sin itself "speaks" as an oracle (ne'um — the same word
used for prophetic utterance). Where God's prophets declare truth,
sin declares its own seductive message to the wicked heart.
Romans 3:18 quotes this as the summary of human depravity.

Verse 5-6 — "Thy mercy, O LORD, is in the heavens; and thy
faithfulness reacheth unto the clouds. Thy righteousness is like
the great mountains; thy judgments are a great deep." (KJV)
Four divine attributes measured against creation's vastness: love
(heavens), faithfulness (clouds), righteousness (mountains),
judgments (ocean deeps). Each comparison suggests boundlessness —
God's character is immeasurable, inexhaustible, and unfathomable.

Verse 8 — "They shall be abundantly satisfied with the fatness of
thy house; and thou shalt make them drink of the river of thy
pleasures." (KJV)
"Fatness" (deshen) suggests rich, abundant provision — a feast of
the finest portions. "River of thy pleasures" (nachal adaneycha)
— the word for "pleasures" (Eden) connects to the Garden of Eden.
God's presence is paradise itself; those who dwell with Him drink
from Eden's river.

Verse 9 — "For with thee is the fountain of life: in thy light
shall we see light." (KJV)
One of the most profound verses in Scripture. God is the meqor
(fountain, source, spring) of all life — not merely a sustainer
but the origin from which all existence flows. "In thy light shall
we see light" — only by God's illumination can we perceive truth.
Knowledge of God is the prerequisite for all other knowledge.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.1 — Romans 3:18: Paul quotes this verse as the culmination of
his indictment of universal human sinfulness — "There is no fear
of God before their eyes."

v.5 — Psalm 57:10; Psalm 108:4: God's mercy/love reaching to the
heavens — repeated imagery of divine love exceeding all created
measurement.

v.7 — Psalm 17:8; Psalm 57:1; Ruth 2:12: The shadow of God's
wings — protective imagery combining the mother bird (maternal
care) with the wings of the cherubim over the ark (divine
presence).

v.8 — Psalm 46:4; Revelation 22:1-2: The river of God — from
Eden's river (Gen. 2:10), to the river in God's city (Ps. 46:4),
to the river of life flowing from God's throne (Rev. 22:1).

v.9 — John 1:4-9; John 8:12: "In him was life; and the life was
the light of men." Jesus identifies Himself as this fountain of
life and light of the world — the New Testament fulfillment of
Psalm 36:9.

v.9 — Jeremiah 2:13; John 4:14: God as the fountain of living
waters — contrasted with the broken cisterns of human-made
religion and the living water Christ offers.

v.9 — 1 John 1:5: "God is light, and in him is no darkness at
all." The epistle's theology of God as light flows directly from
this Old Testament foundation.

v.12 — Psalm 1:6; Psalm 37:36: The fall of the wicked is certain
and final — "unable to rise."

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1 — "The transgression of the wicked saith within my heart"
(KJV) / "Transgression speaks to the wicked deep in his heart"
(ESV): A significant textual issue. The Hebrew reads "in my
heart" (libbi), but many manuscripts, the Septuagint, and the
Syriac read "in his heart" (libbo). KJV follows the Hebrew
Masoretic text; ESV follows the emended reading. The difference
changes whether David is reporting what sin reveals to him about
the wicked, or what sin speaks directly to the wicked person's
own heart.

v.5 — "Thy mercy" (KJV) / "Your steadfast love" (ESV): Hebrew
chesed — God's covenant loyalty and love. KJV's "mercy" emphasizes
compassion; ESV's "steadfast love" emphasizes commitment and
constancy. Both aspects are present in chesed.

v.7 — "How excellent is thy lovingkindness" (KJV) / "How precious
is your steadfast love" (ESV): Hebrew mah-yaqar — literally "how
precious, costly, valuable." ESV's "precious" captures the sense
of rare value better than KJV's "excellent," which in modern
English suggests mere quality rather than costly preciousness.

v.8 — "the fatness of thy house" (KJV) / "the abundance of your
house" (ESV): Hebrew deshen means fatness, richness — the choicest
portions of sacrificial meals. KJV preserves the vivid culinary
metaphor; ESV generalizes to "abundance." In ancient Near Eastern
culture, fat was the richest, most prized part of food.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.1 — NET: "An evil man is rebellious to the core. He does not fear God" — NET restructures the difficult Hebrew of v.1 entirely. Where KJV/ASV have "The transgression of the wicked saith within my heart," NET removes the mysterious "oracle of transgression" speaking and simply describes the wicked man. This eliminates a significant textual and theological puzzle.

- v.5 — KJV: "Thy mercy, O LORD" / ASV: "Thy lovingkindness, O Jehovah" / WEB: "Your loving kindness, Yahweh" / NET: "O Lord, your loyal love" — Hebrew chesed rendered four ways. NET's "loyal love" is distinctive — emphasizing the loyalty component of chesed. The cosmic imagery ("reaches to the sky/heavens") is preserved across all translations.

- v.6 — KJV: "Thy righteousness is like the great mountains" / ASV: "like the mountains of God" / WEB: "like the mountains of God" / NET: "like the highest mountains" — Hebrew "harere-El" = literally "mountains of God" — a superlative idiom meaning the greatest mountains. ASV/WEB retain the literal reading; KJV/NET interpret the superlative.

- v.7 — KJV: "How excellent is thy lovingkindness" / ASV: "How precious is thy lovingkindness" / WEB: "How precious is your loving kindness" / NET: "How precious is your loyal love" — Hebrew "mah-yaqar" = how precious/costly. ASV/WEB/NET all agree on "precious" over KJV's "excellent." Again NET uses "loyal love" for chesed.

- v.9 — WEB: "For with you is the spring of life" / ASV/KJV: "fountain of life" / NET: "fountain of life" — WEB uses "spring" instead of "fountain" for Hebrew "maqor" — a natural spring bubbling up from the earth, emphasizing the perpetual, self-renewing nature of divine life.

- v.10 — ASV: "Oh continue thy lovingkindness to them that know thee" / WEB: "Oh continue your loving kindness to those who know you" / NET: "Extend your loyal love to your faithful followers" — NET's "faithful followers" interprets "those who know thee" — Hebrew "yodeka" (those knowing You). Knowledge of God in Hebrew implies intimate relational experience, not mere intellectual awareness. NET's "followers" flattens this relational knowing.

- v.11 — NET: "Do not let arrogant men overtake me, or let evil men make me homeless" — NET's "make me homeless" interprets Hebrew "nudah" (drive away, make wander). KJV's "remove me" and ASV's "drive me away" are more literal. NET adds concrete social imagery (homelessness) to an abstract spiritual displacement.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. The portrait of wickedness begins with "no fear of God before
his eyes" (v.1). The fear of the LORD is not merely one virtue
among many — it is the foundation. When fear of God erodes,
everything else follows: self-deception (v.2), corrupt speech
(v.3), abandonment of wisdom (v.3), and deliberate evil (v.4).
How actively do you cultivate the fear of God?

2. Verses 5-6 measure God's attributes by creation's scale. His
love is not small, not limited, not conditional — it reaches to
the heavens. His faithfulness extends to the clouds. When you feel
God's love is inadequate for your situation, remember: you are
trying to outrun something as vast as the sky.

3. "The river of thy pleasures" (v.8) — the word for "pleasures"
shares its root with "Eden." God Himself is paradise. The deepest
human longing for joy, beauty, and satisfaction is ultimately a
longing for God. Are you seeking Eden in created things, or
drinking from the Source directly?

4. "In thy light shall we see light" (v.9) has profound
epistemological implications: God is the necessary precondition
for all true knowledge. Apart from His revelation, we cannot
rightly interpret reality. How does this shape your approach to
wisdom, education, and decision-making?

5. The psalm contrasts two worlds: the cramped, dark world of the
wicked (vv.1-4) who see only themselves, and the vast, luminous
world of those who know God (vv.5-9) who see infinite love,
boundless faithfulness, and eternal light. Which world do you
inhabit moment by moment?
