Psalms - Chapter 3
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A lament psalm of David fleeing Absalom — surrounded by enemies who say God will not help him, yet David declares the LORD is his shield and sustainer; he slept and woke because the LORD sustained him.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Individual Lament / Trust Psalm
Attributed Author: David — "A Psalm of David, when he fled from
Absalom his son" (superscription)
Key Themes: Enemies surrounding, trust in God as shield, prayer
answered, fearless sleep, salvation belongs to God

Historical Context: This is the first psalm with a superscription
and the first attributed to David. The historical context is
2 Samuel 15-17 — Absalom's rebellion. David fled Jerusalem with a
small band while his own son pursued him with an army. The
emotional weight is enormous: this is not a foreign enemy but his
own child. The taunt "there is no help for him in God" (v.2) may
reflect Shimei's cursing (2 Sam 16:5-8) or the general perception
that God had abandoned David because of his sin with Bathsheba.
Yet David slept peacefully that night — an astonishing act of
trust. This is traditionally understood as a "morning psalm"
(paired with Psalm 4 as an "evening psalm").

Structure:
- The Crisis Described (vv.1-2)
- Confidence in God (vv.3-4)
- Fearless Rest (vv.5-6)
- Prayer and Praise (vv.7-8)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.3  — "But thou, O LORD, art a shield for me; my glory, and the
        lifter up of mine head." (KJV) — The great "BUT" — the
        pivot from crisis to confidence. Enemies are many, taunts
        are loud, but GOD is shield, glory, and head-lifter. David
        fled with his head covered in shame (2 Sam 15:30); God
        lifts it again.

v.5  — "I laid me down and slept; I awaked; for the LORD sustained
        me." (KJV) — Astonishing in context. A king fleeing his
        own son with an army in pursuit — and he SLEPT. This is
        not denial but trust. Sleep is the ultimate indicator of
        inner peace (cf. Mark 4:38, Jesus sleeping in the storm).

v.8  — "Salvation belongeth unto the LORD: thy blessing is upon thy
        people. Selah." (KJV) — The psalm's theological summary.
        Salvation is God's domain — not David's strategy, army, or
        political maneuvering. And it extends beyond David to "thy
        people" — communal blessing from personal trust.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.1   — 2 Samuel 15:12-13 — "The conspiracy was strong; for the
         people increased continually with Absalom." The historical
         backdrop — David's supporters defecting.
v.2   — 2 Samuel 16:7-8 — Shimei's curse: "Come out, come out,
         thou bloody man." The taunt that God abandoned David.
v.3   — Genesis 15:1 — "Fear not, Abram: I am thy shield." God as
         shield — Abrahamic promise.
v.3   — Psalm 27:6 — "Now shall mine head be lifted up above mine
         enemies." Same head-lifting imagery.
v.5   — Psalm 4:8 — "I will both lay me down in peace, and sleep."
         The evening companion psalm echoes this trust.
v.5   — Mark 4:38 — Jesus asleep in the storm — the same radical
         trust expressed in rest.
v.7   — Psalm 58:6 — "Break their teeth, O God, in their mouth."
         Similar imprecatory language — breaking teeth = removing
         power to harm.
v.8   — Jonah 2:9 — "Salvation is of the LORD." Same theological
         declaration from another desperate situation.

------------------------------------

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.2 — KJV: "There is no help for him in God" / ESV: "There is no
      salvation for him in God" — Hebrew "yeshuah" = salvation/
      deliverance. ESV's "salvation" is more precise. The taunt is
      theological: God cannot or will not save him. This is the
      deepest cut — not just physical danger but spiritual
      abandonment.

v.3 — KJV: "a shield for me" / ESV: "a shield about me" — Hebrew
      "ba'adi" could mean "for me" or "around me." ESV suggests
      surrounding protection; KJV suggests advocacy. Both are
      legitimate readings.

v.4 — KJV: "he heard me" / ESV: "he answered me" — Hebrew "anah"
      = answered/responded. ESV is more precise — God did not
      merely hear passively but actively responded. The answer came
      "from his holy hill" — Zion, which David has just LEFT. God
      is not confined to the geographic location David departed.

v.7 — KJV: "thou hast smitten all mine enemies upon the cheek
      bone" / ESV: "you strike all my enemies on the cheek" —
      Striking the cheek = shaming and defanging. Breaking teeth =
      removing the power to devour. The imagery is of a predator
      rendered harmless.

Selah — Appears three times (vv.2, 4, 8). Meaning uncertain —
likely a musical or liturgical pause. Functionally: stop and
consider what was just said.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. The world will tell you God cannot help (v.2). "There is no
   salvation for him in God" — this is the taunt of every
   circumstance that seems too large, every failure that seems
   disqualifying, every enemy that seems overwhelming. The taunt
   is always a lie.

2. The word "BUT" changes everything (v.3). Enemies are real,
   danger is real, taunts are real — BUT God is shield, glory,
   head-lifter. Faith does not deny the crisis; it introduces a
   greater reality into the crisis.

3. You can sleep when God is your shield (v.5). David's sleep is
   not escapism — it is the most practical demonstration of trust.
   If you truly believe God is your protector, you can close your
   eyes in the presence of ten thousand enemies. Insomnia often
   reveals where trust has eroded.

4. Personal trust becomes communal blessing (v.8). "Thy blessing
   is upon thy people." David's faith did not serve only David. A
   leader who trusts God becomes a channel of blessing to everyone
   under their care.

5. Salvation BELONGS to the Lord (v.8). It is His possession, His
   prerogative, His gift. You cannot manufacture it, earn it, or
   lose it through human failure. It belongs to Him — and He gives
   it freely to those who cry out (v.4).
