Psalms - Chapter 27
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A psalm of trust and longing — 'The LORD is my light and my salvation; whom shall I fear?' David's one desire is to dwell in God's house; though father and mother forsake him, the LORD will take him up. 'Wait on the LORD.'

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Trust/Confidence Psalm with Lament
Attributed Author: David — "A Psalm of David"
Key Themes: God as light and salvation, fearlessness, one supreme
desire (God's presence), divine shelter, seeking God's face,
abandonment by parents but not God, waiting on the LORD

Historical Context: Psalm 27 has two dramatically different halves:
vv.1-6 are radiant confidence; vv.7-14 are urgent pleading. Some
scholars suggest two psalms combined, but the contrast teaches that
confidence and plea coexist in real faith. David faces enemies,
false witnesses, and even parental abandonment (v.10), yet his
central desire remains singular: to dwell in God's presence and
behold His beauty (v.4). This "one thing" theology reduces life
to its essential priority. The psalm ends with the memorable
command to "wait on the LORD" — repeated twice for emphasis.

Structure:
- Bold Confidence: Light and Salvation (vv.1-6)
- Urgent Plea: Hear and Answer (vv.7-12)
- Concluding Faith: Wait on the LORD (vv.13-14)


CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.1 — John 8:12 — Christ as the fulfillment of God as "light."
v.1 — Micah 7:8 — Same light-in-darkness theology.
v.4 — Luke 10:42 — Mary chose the same "one thing" — presence with Jesus.
v.4 — Philippians 3:13 — Paul's singular focus echoes David's.
v.8 — 2 Chronicles 7:14 — God's invitation to face-seeking.
v.10 — Isaiah 49:15 — God surpasses even maternal faithfulness.
v.14 — Isaiah 40:31 — Same waiting-and-strength theology.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------

REFLECTION
------------------------------------
1. If God is your light, fear is irrational (v.1). The logic is
   complete: if the LORD handles darkness, danger, and attack —
   what is left to fear? Fear persists when we forget who God is.
   Remind yourself of His identity, and fear loses its basis.

2. Reduce life to "one thing" (v.4). David had many needs, many
   enemies, many responsibilities — but ONE desire dominated: God's
   presence. Simplify. What is the one thing? Everything else
   flows from or is secondary to this.

3. God adopts the abandoned (v.10). Even if every human
   relationship fails — parents, spouse, friends — God receives
   you. You cannot be orphaned by God. He gathers what others
   scatter.

4. Believe you will see goodness IN THIS LIFE (v.13). Not only
   in heaven. David expected to see God's goodness "in the land
   of the living" — this side of eternity. Hope is not only
   eschatological — God is good NOW.

5. Wait — and then wait again (v.14). Repeated: "wait on the
   LORD... wait, I say, on the LORD." The repetition acknowledges
   that waiting is hard enough to need reinforcement. Be strong.
   Take courage. And wait.
