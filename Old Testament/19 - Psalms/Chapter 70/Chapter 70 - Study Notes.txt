Psalms - Chapter 70
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A brief urgent lament (parallel to Psalm 40:13-17) — David pleads 'Make haste, O God, to deliver me'; let the seekers of his life be shamed, but let all who seek God rejoice.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Individual Lament Psalm
Attributed Author: David
Key Themes: Urgency of prayer, pleading for speed, shame on
enemies, joy for God-seekers, poverty and neediness before God

Historical Context: Psalm 70 is nearly identical to Psalm 40:13-17
and appears to be extracted from that longer psalm for independent
liturgical use. The superscription says "to bring to remembrance"
(lehazkir), suggesting it was used to call God's attention to the
psalmist's situation — possibly accompanying a memorial offering
(Leviticus 2:2). The psalm is only 5 verses but captures the
essence of urgent prayer. David is in immediate danger and has no
time for lengthy meditation — he needs God NOW. It serves as a
model for crisis prayer: brief, direct, honest, and God-centered
despite personal desperation. It closes Book II of the Psalter
alongside Psalm 72.

Structure:
- Urgent Cry for Help (v.1)
- Imprecation Against Enemies (vv.2-3)
- Joy for God-Seekers (v.4)
- Final Plea from Poverty (v.5)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "Make haste, O God, to deliver me; make haste to help me,
        O LORD." (KJV) — The double "make haste" opens with raw
        urgency. No preamble, no formal address — just desperate
        need. Sometimes prayer needs no introduction — just the
        cry: "HURRY, God!"

v.4  — "Let all those that seek thee rejoice and be glad in thee:
        and let such as love thy salvation say continually, Let God
        be magnified." (KJV) — Even in urgency, David's vision
        extends beyond himself. He prays for ALL God-seekers, not
        just for his own rescue. And their song: "Let God be
        magnified" — making God bigger in perception.

v.5  — "But I am poor and needy: make haste unto me, O God: thou
        art my help and my deliverer; O LORD, make no tarrying."
        (KJV) — David's self-assessment: poor and needy. His
        identity before God is not warrior-king but dependent
        beggar. The psalm ends as it began — "make haste... make
        no tarrying." Urgency frames everything.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.1   — Psalm 40:13-17 — The parallel/source passage from which
         this psalm was extracted.
v.1   — Psalm 71:12 — "O God, be not far from me: O my God, make
         haste for my help." Same urgency continues into Ps 71.
v.2   — Psalm 35:4 — "Let them be confounded and put to shame
         that seek after my soul." Same imprecation.
v.4   — Psalm 40:16 — Nearly identical verse in the parent psalm.
v.5   — Psalm 86:1 — "Bow down thine ear, O LORD, hear me: for I
         am poor and needy."
v.5   — 2 Corinthians 12:9-10 — "When I am weak, then am I
         strong." Strength through acknowledged weakness.

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1 — KJV: "Make haste" / ESV: "Make haste" — Both retain the
      Hebrew "chush" = to hurry, make haste. The urgency is
      unmistakable and both translations preserve it faithfully.

v.3 — KJV: "Let them be turned back for a reward of their shame
      that say, Aha, aha" / ESV: "Let them turn back because of
      their shame who say, 'Aha, Aha!'" — "Aha, aha" (he'ach,
      he'ach) = a mocking exclamation of glee at another's
      misfortune. David prays that their mockery becomes their
      own shame.

v.5 — KJV: "make no tarrying" / ESV: "do not delay" — Same
      Hebrew "achar" = to delay, linger, be late. The psalm's
      final word is about TIME — David has no time to spare and
      begs God not to linger.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Urgent prayer needs no formality (v.1). When the house is on
   fire, you don't compose a letter — you shout. God receives
   raw, urgent, unpolished cries as readily as formal petitions.
   "Make haste" is a complete prayer.

2. Acknowledging poverty before God is the position of strength
   (v.5). "I am poor and needy." This is not self-pity but
   spiritual accuracy. The one who knows their emptiness is
   positioned to receive God's fullness. Self-sufficiency is
   the real poverty.

3. Even in personal crisis, pray for the community (v.4). David
   is desperate, yet he lifts his eyes to "all those that seek
   thee." Personal urgency need not produce selfishness. Even
   your emergency prayers can include others.

4. "Make no tarrying" is a valid prayer (v.5). It is not
   irreverent to ask God to hurry. It is faith expressing
   dependence. You can pray with both trust in God's timing and
   honest acknowledgment of your need for speed.
