Psalms - Chapter 137
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A lament of exile — 'By the rivers of Babylon, there we sat down, yea, we wept, when we remembered Zion'; the exiles cannot sing the LORD's song in a foreign land; ends with fierce imprecation against Babylon.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Communal Lament / Imprecatory Psalm
Attributed Author: Anonymous (clearly post-exilic, written during
or after the Babylonian captivity)
Key Themes: The pain of exile, inability to sing in a foreign
land, memory of Jerusalem, longing for homeland, the raw emotion
of loss, imprecation against Babylon and Edom

Historical Context: Psalm 137 is perhaps the most emotionally raw
psalm in the Psalter — a lament from Babylonian exile (586-538
BC). The exiles sit by Babylon's rivers weeping, harps hung on
willows, while their captors mock them: "Sing us one of your
songs!" But how can you sing the LORD's song in a foreign land?
The psalm ends with one of the most disturbing verses in Scripture
(v.9) — a cry for violent retribution against Babylon. This must
be read as the raw expression of a people who watched their
children murdered during Jerusalem's fall (2 Kings 25), not as
moral prescription. The psalm is theologically honest about the
human experience of trauma and desire for justice.

Structure:
- Weeping by the Rivers (vv.1-4)
- Vow to Remember Jerusalem (vv.5-6)
- Imprecation Against Edom and Babylon (vv.7-9)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "By the rivers of Babylon, there we sat down, yea, we
        wept, when we remembered Zion." (KJV) — One of the most
        iconic opening lines in all poetry. They SAT (defeat),
        they WEPT (grief), they REMEMBERED (longing). Exile is
        sitting, weeping, and remembering what was lost.

v.4  — "How shall we sing the LORD'S song in a strange land?"
        (KJV) — The impossible question of exile: How do you
        worship when everything is wrong? How do you sing when
        your world is destroyed? This is the question of every
        displaced, traumatized, grieving person.

v.5-6 — "If I forget thee, O Jerusalem, let my right hand forget
        her cunning. If I do not remember thee, let my tongue
        cleave to the roof of my mouth; if I prefer not Jerusalem
        above my chief joy." (KJV) — The vow of remembrance. I
        would rather lose my ability to play music and speak than
        forget you, Jerusalem. Memory is loyalty.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.1   — Ezekiel 1:1-3 — Ezekiel by the river Chebar in Babylon.
v.4   — Psalm 126:1 — "When the LORD turned again the captivity
         of Zion" — the answer to this psalm's grief.
v.7   — Obadiah 10-14 — Edom's treachery during Jerusalem's fall.
v.8   — Isaiah 13:16 — Prophecy of Babylon's destruction: "Their
         children also shall be dashed to pieces."
v.8   — Revelation 18:2 — "Babylon the great is fallen."

COMMENTARY REFERENCES

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.2 — "We hanged our harps upon the willows" — The willows
      (poplars) of Babylon's riverbanks. Hanging the harp means
      silence — refusal or inability to play. Worship instruments
      stored, not destroyed. Hope of return remains.

v.9 — "Happy shall he be, that taketh and dasheth thy little ones
      against the stones" — This reflects the horror of ancient
      siege warfare that Israel EXPERIENCED (from Babylon). The
      psalmist prays for reciprocal justice — not initiating
      violence but crying for the same treatment Babylon inflicted.
      Deeply human, deeply troubling, deeply honest.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.2 — KJV: "the willows" / WEB: "the willows" / ASV: "the willows" / NET: "the poplars" — NET alone uses "poplars" (Hebrew "'arabim" may refer to either willows or poplars), departing from the iconic "willows" of Babylon.
- v.3 — KJV: "they that wasted us" / ASV: "they that wasted us" / WEB: "Those who tormented us" / NET: "those who mock us" — Three renderings of Hebrew "tolaleynu": KJV/ASV=wasted/devastated; WEB=tormented; NET=mocked. NET is weakest, reducing physical devastation to verbal mockery.
- v.4 — KJV: "in a strange land" / ASV: "In a foreign land" / WEB: "in a foreign land" / NET: "in a foreign land" — KJV's "strange" is archaic for "foreign." All modern versions use "foreign."
- v.5 — KJV: "let my right hand forget her cunning" / ASV: "Let my right hand forget her skill" / WEB: "let my right hand forget its skill" / NET: "may my right hand be crippled" — NET radically departs: instead of "forgetting skill" (musical ability), NET says "be crippled" — interpreting the consequence as physical disability rather than loss of ability.
- v.7 — KJV: "Rase it, rase it" / ASV: "Rase it, rase it" / WEB: "Raze it! Raze it!" / NET: "Tear it down, tear it down" — WEB updates spelling; NET paraphrases the archaic "raze" (demolish) to "tear it down."
- v.8 — NET: "how blessed will be the one who repays you for what you dished out to us" — Colloquial "dished out" replaces KJV's "as thou hast served us." NET also uses "how blessed" (Heb. "ashre") rather than KJV's "happy."
- v.9 — NET: "grabs your babies and smashes them on a rock" — More viscerally graphic than KJV's "taketh and dasheth thy little ones against the stones." NET makes the violence impossible to soften.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Grief is valid worship (v.1). Sitting, weeping, remembering —
   these are not failures of faith. They are the honest response
   to devastating loss. God receives lament as worship.

2. "How shall we sing?" (v.4). Sometimes you can't. Sometimes
   worship is impossible because the pain is too fresh. The psalm
   doesn't answer the question — it just asks it honestly.

3. Never forget where you belong (vv.5-6). No matter how
   comfortable exile becomes, remember Jerusalem. Remember where
   your true home is. Don't let a foreign land become your
   identity.

4. Scripture includes raw human emotion (v.9). This verse is
   not prescriptive but descriptive — the cry of trauma. The
   Bible includes it because God can handle our worst thoughts.
   Bring even THESE to Him rather than acting on them.
