Psalms - Chapter 123
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A Song of Ascents — the psalmist lifts his eyes to God in heaven as a servant looks to the hand of his master, pleading for mercy because the people are filled with the contempt of the proud.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Song of Ascents / Communal Lament
Attributed Author: Anonymous (superscription: "A Song of degrees")
Key Themes: Looking to God for mercy, patient waiting as a
servant, contempt from the proud, humble dependence on God

Historical Context: Psalm 123 is the fourth Song of Ascents — a
short prayer of four verses from a people experiencing contempt
and scorn from those "at ease" (the arrogant wealthy). The central
image is stunning: eyes fixed on God's hand like a servant
watching a master for the slightest signal. The psalm likely
reflects post-exilic Israel, despised by surrounding nations.
It teaches that when scorned by the world, the proper response is
not retaliation but looking upward — fixing your gaze on God's
hand, waiting for His mercy to act.

Structure:
- Eyes Lifted to God (v.1)
- The Servant's Gaze (v.2)
- Plea for Mercy (v.3)
- The Weight of Contempt (v.4)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "Behold, as the eyes of servants look unto the hand of
        their masters, and as the eyes of a maiden unto the hand
        of her mistress; so our eyes wait upon the LORD our God,
        until that he have mercy upon us." (KJV) — The servant
        watches the master's hand for direction, provision, and
        protection. The key word: UNTIL. Keep watching until mercy
        comes. Don't look away.

v.3  — "Have mercy upon us, O LORD, have mercy upon us: for we
        are exceedingly filled with contempt." (KJV) — The
        repeated plea reveals desperation. They are FULL of
        contempt — filled to overflowing with scorn from others.
        The only relief: God's mercy.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.1   — Psalm 121:1 — "I will lift up mine eyes." Same upward
         gaze.
v.2   — Psalm 25:15 — "Mine eyes are ever toward the LORD."
v.3   — Nehemiah 4:4 — Nehemiah's prayer when scorned.
v.4   — 1 Corinthians 4:13 — "We are made as the filth of the
         world... the offscouring of all things."

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.2 — "the hand of their masters" — In ancient households,
      masters communicated through hand gestures. Servants watched
      constantly for signals. This is the posture of faith:
      attentive, expectant, ready to respond.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.1 — KJV: "O thou that dwellest in the heavens" / ASV: "O thou that sittest in the heavens" / WEB: "you who sit in the heavens" / NET: "the one enthroned in heaven" — Four renderings of Hebrew "yoshev" (sits/dwells). NET's "enthroned" adds royal imagery; KJV's "dwellest" is more residential.
- v.2 — KJV: "until that he have mercy upon us" / ASV: "Until he have mercy upon us" / WEB: "until he has mercy on us" / NET: "until he shows us favor" — NET replaces "mercy" (Hebrew "chanan") with "shows us favor," emphasizing the active demonstration rather than the quality.
- v.2 — KJV: "a maiden" / NET: "a female servant" — NET avoids "maiden" (which implies youth/virginity) for the more precise "female servant" (Hebrew "shiphchah"), keeping the focus on the servant-master relationship rather than age.
- v.3 — NET: "Show us favor, O Lord, show us favor! For we have had our fill of humiliation, and then some" — The colloquial "and then some" is strikingly informal for NET, paraphrasing KJV's "we are exceedingly filled with contempt."
- v.4 — NET: "the taunts of the self-assured" — Interprets KJV's "scorning of those that are at ease." Hebrew "sha'anan" (at ease/complacent) becomes "self-assured," adding a psychological characterization.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Fix your eyes on God's hand (v.2). When you face contempt or
   scorn, don't fixate on the scorners. Look at God's hand.
   Watch for His signal, His provision, His timing.

2. "Until" (v.2). Keep looking until mercy comes. Patience is not
   passive — it's active watching. Don't give up before God acts.

3. Being filled with contempt is painful (vv.3-4). Scripture
   acknowledges this pain without minimizing it. Being despised
   by the comfortable and proud is real suffering. Bring it to
   God.
