Psalms - Chapter 84
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A pilgrimage psalm of the Sons of Korah — 'How amiable are thy tabernacles, O LORD'; the psalmist longs for God's courts, envies the sparrow nesting near the altar; 'a day in thy courts is better than a thousand elsewhere.'

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Zion Song / Pilgrimage Psalm
Attributed Author: Sons of Korah
Key Themes: Longing for God's house, the blessedness of dwelling
in God's presence, pilgrimage faith, God as sun and shield,
preferring God's courts to worldly comforts

Historical Context: A psalm of the Sons of Korah, set to "upon
Gittith." This is one of the most beloved psalms of longing for
God's presence. It was likely sung by pilgrims traveling to
Jerusalem for the annual festivals. The psalmist is separated from
the temple and aches for return. The "Valley of Baca" (v.6) —
meaning "valley of weeping" or "valley of balsam trees" — may be
a literal dry valley on the pilgrimage route or a metaphor for any
place of tears that God transforms into springs. The Sons of Korah
were Levitical temple servants, which explains their particular
attachment to God's house. Their ancestor Korah perished in
rebellion (Numbers 16), but his descendants were preserved — giving
them special reason to treasure God's presence.

Structure:
- Longing for God's Courts (vv.1-4)
- The Blessed Pilgrims (vv.5-7)
- Prayer for the King (vv.8-9)
- One Day in God's Courts (vv.10-12)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "My soul longeth, yea, even fainteth for the courts of the
        LORD: my heart and my flesh crieth out for the living God."
        (KJV) — Total-person desire: soul, heart, flesh. Not
        intellectual interest but visceral longing. The God they
        long for is "living" — not an idol but the God who is
        actively alive and present.

v.6  — "Who passing through the valley of Baca make it a well; the
        rain also filleth the pools." (KJV) — Pilgrims of faith
        transform dry places into springs. The journey itself
        creates blessing. Tears become wells. God turns the path
        of difficulty into a source of refreshment.

v.10 — "For a day in thy courts is better than a thousand. I had
        rather be a doorkeeper in the house of my God, than to
        dwell in the tents of wickedness." (KJV) — The ultimate
        valuation: one day with God > 1000 days elsewhere. The
        lowest position in God's house > the highest position
        outside it. Proximity to God outweighs everything else.

v.11 — "For the LORD God is a sun and shield: the LORD will give
        grace and glory: no good thing will he withhold from them
        that walk uprightly." (KJV) — God is BOTH sun (source of
        life, warmth, growth) AND shield (protection, defense).
        He gives grace (for today) AND glory (for eternity). He
        withholds NO good thing from the upright.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.1   — Psalm 27:4 — "One thing have I desired of the LORD... to
         dwell in the house of the LORD." Same singular longing.
v.2   — Psalm 42:1-2 — "As the hart panteth after the water
         brooks, so panteth my soul after thee, O God." Same
         desperate thirst.
v.6   — 2 Corinthians 4:17 — "Our light affliction... worketh for
         us a far more exceeding and eternal weight of glory."
         Tears producing wells.
v.10  — Psalm 63:3 — "Thy lovingkindness is better than life."
         Same radical valuation of God over everything.
v.11  — James 1:17 — "Every good gift and every perfect gift is
         from above." God withholding no good thing.
v.11  — Romans 8:32 — "He that spared not his own Son... shall he
         not with him also freely give us all things?"

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1 — KJV: "How amiable are thy tabernacles" / ESV: "How lovely
      is your dwelling place" — Hebrew "yedidot" = beloved, lovely,
      desirable. "Amiable" (KJV) is archaic; ESV's "lovely" better
      conveys the warmth of the Hebrew.

v.6 — KJV: "the valley of Baca" / ESV: "the Valley of Baca" —
      "Baca" may mean weeping (from bakah) or balsam trees (which
      grow in arid places). Either way, it represents a dry,
      difficult stretch on the pilgrimage route that faith
      transforms.

v.7 — KJV: "They go from strength to strength" / ESV: same —
      Unlike worldly journeys that deplete, this pilgrimage
      INCREASES strength. Each stage of the journey adds vigor
      rather than subtracting it. Spiritual pilgrimage energizes.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.1 — ASV: "O Jehovah of hosts" / WEB: "Yahweh of Armies" / KJV: "O LORD of hosts" / NET: "O Lord of Heaven's Armies" — The divine name + hosts/armies. NET's "Heaven's Armies" is the most expansive interpretation; WEB translates צְבָאוֹת directly; ASV combines "Jehovah" with "hosts."

- v.2 — NET: "I desperately want to be in the courts of the Lord's temple" — NET intensifies with "desperately" and adds "temple" (not in Hebrew). KJV/ASV/WEB's "longeth, yea, even fainteth" preserves the poetic crescendo of desire that NET flattens.

- v.6 — ASV/WEB: "the valley of Weeping" / KJV: "the valley of Baca" / NET: "the Baca Valley" — ASV and WEB translate the Hebrew בָּכָא as "Weeping," making the metaphorical meaning explicit. KJV/NET retain the Hebrew name "Baca" untranslated.

- v.9 — NET: "Show concern for your chosen king" / KJV: "look upon the face of thine anointed" / ASV: "look upon the face of thine anointed" — NET paraphrases "the face of thine anointed" as "your chosen king," interpreting the "anointed" specifically as the king. KJV/ASV retain the more open Hebrew that allows Messianic reading.

- v.10 — NET: "I would rather stand at the entrance to the temple of my God than live in the tents of the wicked" — NET replaces the beloved "doorkeeper" with the blander "stand at the entrance." KJV/ASV/WEB all retain "doorkeeper" — the humble, servant position that still places one in God's house.

- v.11 — ASV: "For Jehovah God is a sun and a shield: Jehovah will give grace and glory" / WEB: "For Yahweh God is a sun and a shield. Yahweh will give grace and glory" / NET: "The Lord bestows favor and honor" — NET replaces "grace and glory" with "favor and honor," losing the theological richness of "grace" (חֵן, chen) and "glory" (כָּבוֹד, kavod).

- v.11 — WEB: "those who walk blamelessly" / KJV: "them that walk uprightly" / NET: "those who have integrity" — Three different English renderings of the same Hebrew concept of walking in wholeness/completeness before God.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Long for God's presence with your whole being (v.2). Soul,
   heart, flesh — let every part of you reach for God. Worship is
   not just spiritual — your body, your emotions, your will should
   all strain toward Him.

2. Faith transforms deserts into springs (v.6). Whatever "Valley
   of Baca" you're passing through, faith can make it a well.
   Tears can become the water that refreshes you and others. Don't
   waste your valleys.

3. One day with God outweighs everything (v.10). Recalibrate your
   values. The lowest place in God's presence is better than the
   highest place outside it. Choose proximity over promotion.

4. God withholds no good thing from the upright (v.11). If He
   hasn't given it to you, either it's not good, it's not time,
   or your walk needs adjustment. Trust His withholding as much
   as His giving — both are love.

5. The journey goes from strength to strength (v.7). God's path
   doesn't exhaust you — it empowers you. If your spiritual life
   feels depleting, check your direction. The right path adds
   strength at every stage.
