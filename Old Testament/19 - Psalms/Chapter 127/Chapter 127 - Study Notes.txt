Psalms - Chapter 127
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A Song of Ascents of Solomon — a wisdom psalm: 'Except the LORD build the house, they labour in vain'; God gives sleep to His beloved; children are a heritage from the LORD, arrows in a warrior's hand.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Song of Ascents / Wisdom Psalm
Attributed Author: Solomon (superscription: "A Song of degrees
for Solomon")
Key Themes: The futility of human effort without God, trust vs.
anxiety, children as God's blessing, the family as divine gift,
rest in God's sovereignty

Historical Context: Psalm 127 is one of only two psalms attributed
to Solomon in the Songs of Ascents (the other being possibly 72,
which is in Book II). As the builder of the temple, Solomon is
uniquely qualified to declare that building without God is useless.
The irony is powerful: the greatest builder in Israel's history
says, "Unless the LORD builds the house, you labor in vain." The
psalm has two halves: vv.1-2 address anxious self-reliance in
work/security; vv.3-5 address the blessing of children. Both
halves make the same point: what matters most comes from God, not
from human striving. This psalm directly challenges the
workaholic, the anxious, and the self-made person.

Structure:
- Without God, Labor is Vain (v.1)
- Anxious Toil vs. God's Gift of Rest (v.2)
- Children as God's Heritage (vv.3-5)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "Except the LORD build the house, they labour in vain that
        build it: except the LORD keep the city, the watchman
        waketh but in vain." (KJV) — The word "vain" (Hebrew
        "shav") appears three times in vv.1-2. Without God:
        building is vain, watching is vain, striving is vain. Not
        that effort is wrong — but effort WITHOUT GOD accomplishes
        nothing of lasting value.

v.2  — "It is vain for you to rise up early, to sit up late, to
        eat the bread of sorrows: for so he giveth his beloved
        sleep." (KJV) — Anxious overwork ("bread of sorrows" =
        bread of painful toil) is futile. God gives His beloved
        REST. Sleep is a gift — receiving it requires trust that
        God works while you rest.

v.3  — "Lo, children are an heritage of the LORD: and the fruit
        of the womb is his reward." (KJV) — Children are not
        accidents, burdens, or commodities. They are GOD'S GIFT
        — His heritage, His reward. The language is of inheritance
        and divine generosity.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.1   — Psalm 121:4 — "He that keepeth Israel shall neither
         slumber nor sleep." God is the true keeper.
v.1   — 2 Chronicles 2:1 — Solomon building the temple — yet
         acknowledging God must build it.
v.2   — Matthew 6:25-34 — "Take no thought for the morrow."
         Jesus against anxiety.
v.2   — Psalm 4:8 — "I will both lay me down in peace, and

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.2 — KJV: "he giveth his beloved sleep" / ESV: "he gives to his
      beloved sleep" — Some translate: "he gives to his beloved
      WHILE THEY SLEEP" (i.e., God works for you even while you
      rest). Both readings are powerful: God either gives you the
      gift of peaceful sleep OR gives blessings while you sleep.

v.4 — "arrows in the hand of a mighty man" — Children are
      compared to ARROWS: pointed, purposeful, launched forward
      into the future. Parents are the bow; children are the
      arrows sent where parents cannot go themselves.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.1 — KJV: "Except the LORD build the house" / ASV: "Except Jehovah build the house" / WEB: "Unless Yahweh builds the house" / NET: "If the Lord does not build a house" — NET restructures from proverbial "except/unless" format to conditional "if...not," losing the proverbial tone.
- v.2 — KJV: "to eat the bread of sorrows" / ASV: "To eat the bread of toil" / WEB: "eating the bread of toil" / NET: "and work so hard for your food" — Hebrew "etsev" = toil/pain/sorrow. KJV emphasizes grief; ASV/WEB emphasize hard labor; NET paraphrases entirely, removing the "bread of" metaphor.
- v.2 — KJV: "so he giveth his beloved sleep" / ASV: "so he giveth unto his beloved sleep" / WEB: "for he gives sleep to his loved ones" / NET: "Yes, he provides for those whom he loves even when they sleep" — NET radically reinterprets: instead of God GIVING sleep (rest), God provides FOR them while they sleep. This is a significant theological difference.
- v.3 — KJV: "children are an heritage of the LORD" / ASV: "a heritage of Jehovah" / WEB: "a heritage of Yahweh" / NET: "sons are a gift from the Lord" — NET uses "gift" for "heritage/inheritance" (Hebrew "nachalah"), shifting from covenant inheritance language to general blessing.
- v.4 — NET: "Sons born during one's youth are like arrows in a warrior's hand" — Expands KJV's compact "As arrows are in the hand of a mighty man; so are children of the youth" into an explanatory sentence.
- v.5 — KJV: "Happy is the man that hath his quiver full of them" / NET: "How blessed is the man who fills his quiver with them" — NET's "How blessed" (vs. "Happy") aligns with Hebrew "ashre" which denotes blessedness beyond mere happiness.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. God must build it (v.1). Whatever you're building — career,
   ministry, family, business — unless God is building it with
   you, it's empty. Acknowledge dependence BEFORE effort.

2. Stop eating the bread of sorrows (v.2). Anxious overwork is
   not virtue — it's unbelief. God gives His beloved sleep.
   Can you rest? Can you stop? Trust is demonstrated by what
   you're willing to leave undone.

3. Children are heritage, not hassle (v.3). In a culture that
   often views children as inconvenience, Scripture calls them
   GOD'S REWARD. Reframe your view: children are divine gift.

4. Sleep is a spiritual discipline (v.2). Going to bed is an act
   of faith — trusting that God works while you rest. The
   inability to sleep often reveals the inability to trust.
