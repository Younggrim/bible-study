Psalms - Chapter 72
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A royal/Messianic psalm closing Book II — a prayer for Solomon's reign: may the king judge with righteousness, deliver the poor, rule from sea to sea; all nations shall serve him and his name endure forever.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Royal / Messianic Psalm
Attributed Author: Solomon (or "for Solomon" — by David for his
son)
Key Themes: The ideal king, justice for the poor, universal
dominion, prosperity under righteous rule, the everlasting
kingdom, all nations blessed

Historical Context: The superscription says "Of Solomon" or "For
Solomon." This may be Solomon's own prayer upon ascending the throne
or David's prayer for his son (supported by v.20: "The prayers of
David the son of Jesse are ended"). This psalm closes Book II of
the Psalter. Its vision far exceeds anything Solomon achieved — sea
to sea dominion, eternal name, all nations blessed. It is deeply
Messianic, describing the perfect King whose reign brings universal
justice, peace, and blessing. The language echoes God's promise to
Abraham (Gen 12:3 — "all nations shall be blessed in him," v.17)
and the Davidic covenant (2 Sam 7). No earthly king fulfilled
these words; they await Christ's kingdom.

Structure:
- Prayer for Royal Justice (vv.1-4)
- Universal and Eternal Dominion (vv.5-11)
- The King's Care for the Poor (vv.12-14)
- Prayer for the King's Prosperity (vv.15-17)
- Doxology Closing Book II (vv.18-20)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.6  — "He shall come down like rain upon the mown grass: as
        showers that water the earth." (KJV) — The king's reign
        as gentle, life-giving rain. Not oppressive but refreshing.
        Mown grass (recently cut) needs rain to recover — and the
        ideal king provides exactly what the vulnerable need.

v.8  — "He shall have dominion also from sea to sea, and from the
        river unto the ends of the earth." (KJV) — The extent of
        Messiah's kingdom: total. "Sea to sea" and "ends of the
        earth" — no corner of creation lies outside His authority.
        This exceeds Solomon's actual territory by infinite measure.

v.12-13 — "For he shall deliver the needy when he crieth; the
        poor also, and him that hath no helper. He shall spare the
        poor and needy, and shall save the souls of the needy."
        (KJV) — The Messianic king's distinguishing mark: advocacy
        for the helpless. Not power for power's sake but power for
        protection of the vulnerable.

v.17 — "His name shall endure for ever... and men shall be blessed
        in him: all nations shall call him blessed." (KJV) — The
        Abrahamic promise fulfilled through the Messianic king. All
        nations blessed — not conquered, not subjugated, but
        BLESSED through Him. This is Christ.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1 — KJV: "Give the king thy judgments" / ESV: "Give the king
      your justice" — Hebrew "mishpat" = justice, judgment,
      ordinance. ESV's "justice" emphasizes righteous outcomes;
      KJV's "judgments" emphasizes the capacity to adjudicate
      rightly.

v.6 — KJV: "rain upon the mown grass" / ESV: "rain that falls on
      the mown grass" — "Mown grass" (gez) = freshly cut grass,
      shorn meadow. The image is of something that has been cut
      back and needs gentle restoration — not pounding storms but
      nurturing showers.

v.20 — KJV: "The prayers of David the son of Jesse are ended" /
       ESV: Same — This editorial note marks the end of Book II
       of the Psalter. It doesn't mean David wrote no other psalms
       after this but that a Davidic collection concludes here.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.1 — NET: "O God, grant the king the ability to make just decisions" vs. KJV: "Give the king thy judgments, O God" — NET heavily paraphrases, adding "ability to make just decisions" for the simple "thy judgments" (mishpatim). WEB: "God, give the king your justice" — closer to the Hebrew.

- v.7 — NET: "During his days the godly will flourish" vs. KJV: "In his days shall the righteous flourish" — NET changes "righteous" (tsaddiq) to "godly," softening the moral precision. ASV/WEB preserve "righteous." NET also uses "peace will prevail" for KJV's "abundance of peace."

- v.14 — NET: "From harm and violence he will defend them; he will value their lives" vs. KJV: "He shall redeem their soul from deceit and violence: and precious shall their blood be in his sight" — NET drops "redeem" (ga'al — a kinsman-redeemer term) and substitutes "defend." Also loses "precious shall their blood be" for the weaker "value their lives."

- v.17 — NET: "May they use his name when they formulate their blessings. May all nations consider him to be favored by God" vs. KJV: "men shall be blessed in him: all nations shall call him blessed" — NET weakens this Messianic verse dramatically. KJV echoes the Abrahamic promise (Genesis 12:3); NET reduces it to mere reputation.

- v.18 — ASV: "Blessed be Jehovah God, the God of Israel" / WEB: "Praise be to Yahweh God, the God of Israel" vs. KJV: "Blessed be the LORD God, the God of Israel" — The covenant name is explicit in ASV/WEB at this crucial doxology closing Book II of the Psalter.

- v.19 — NET: "May his majestic splendor fill the whole earth. We agree! We agree!" vs. KJV: "let the whole earth be filled with his glory; Amen, and Amen" — NET renders the Hebrew "Amen" as "We agree!" losing the liturgical solemnity. WEB/ASV preserve "Amen and Amen."

- v.3 — WEB: "The mountains shall bring prosperity to the people" vs. KJV: "The mountains shall bring peace to the people" — WEB interprets "shalom" as "prosperity" rather than "peace." NET: "news of peace." The Hebrew "shalom" encompasses peace, wholeness, prosperity, and well-being.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. The ideal king prioritizes the poor and needy (vv.12-14). Jesus
   fulfilled this — His ministry focused on the marginalized, the
   sick, the outcast. Biblical leadership is measured by how it
   treats the weakest, not the strongest.

2. Righteous rule is like gentle rain, not oppressive storms (v.6).
   True authority refreshes and restores rather than dominating and
   exploiting. Whether in church, family, or workplace — lead like
   rain on mown grass.

3. Only Christ's kingdom fulfills these promises. Solomon was
   impressive but fell far short. Every human leader disappoints.
   But there is a King coming whose dominion truly is sea to sea
   and whose name endures forever. Fix your hope there.

4. "All nations shall call him blessed" (v.17). The trajectory of
   history leads to universal acknowledgment of Christ. Every
   nation, every tongue — all will recognize the goodness of His
   reign.
