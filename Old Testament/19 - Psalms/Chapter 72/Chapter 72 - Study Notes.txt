Psalms - Chapter 72
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A royal/Messianic psalm closing Book II — a prayer for Solomon's reign: may the king judge with righteousness, deliver the poor, rule from sea to sea; all nations shall serve him and his name endure forever.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Royal / Messianic Psalm
Attributed Author: Solomon (or "for Solomon" — by David for his
son)
Key Themes: The ideal king, justice for the poor, universal
dominion, prosperity under righteous rule, the everlasting
kingdom, all nations blessed

Historical Context: The superscription says "Of Solomon" or "For
Solomon." This may be Solomon's own prayer upon ascending the throne
or David's prayer for his son (supported by v.20: "The prayers of
David the son of Jesse are ended"). This psalm closes Book II of
the Psalter. Its vision far exceeds anything Solomon achieved — sea
to sea dominion, eternal name, all nations blessed. It is deeply
Messianic, describing the perfect King whose reign brings universal
justice, peace, and blessing. The language echoes God's promise to
Abraham (Gen 12:3 — "all nations shall be blessed in him," v.17)
and the Davidic covenant (2 Sam 7). No earthly king fulfilled
these words; they await Christ's kingdom.

Structure:
- Prayer for Royal Justice (vv.1-4)
- Universal and Eternal Dominion (vv.5-11)
- The King's Care for the Poor (vv.12-14)
- Prayer for the King's Prosperity (vv.15-17)
- Doxology Closing Book II (vv.18-20)

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Tarshish (v.10): Distant port city (possibly Spain); represents the farthest reaches of the known world.
- Lebanon (v.16): Region north of Israel known for its cedar forests.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------

REFLECTION
------------------------------------
1. The ideal king prioritizes the poor and needy (vv.12-14). Jesus
   fulfilled this — His ministry focused on the marginalized, the
   sick, the outcast. Biblical leadership is measured by how it
   treats the weakest, not the strongest.

2. Righteous rule is like gentle rain, not oppressive storms (v.6).
   True authority refreshes and restores rather than dominating and
   exploiting. Whether in church, family, or workplace — lead like
   rain on mown grass.

3. Only Christ's kingdom fulfills these promises. Solomon was
   impressive but fell far short. Every human leader disappoints.
   But there is a King coming whose dominion truly is sea to sea
   and whose name endures forever. Fix your hope there.

4. "All nations shall call him blessed" (v.17). The trajectory of
   history leads to universal acknowledgment of Christ. Every
   nation, every tongue — all will recognize the goodness of His
   reign.
