Psalms - Chapter 8
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A creation hymn of praise — marveling at God's majesty in the heavens yet His attention to frail humanity: 'What is man that thou art mindful of him?' Man is crowned with glory and given dominion over creation.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Hymn of Praise / Creation Psalm
Attributed Author: David — "To the chief Musician upon Gittith,
A Psalm of David"
Key Themes: God's majesty in creation, human dignity and purpose,
dominion mandate, the paradox of human smallness and greatness,
Messianic fulfillment

Historical Context: "Gittith" may refer to a Gittite instrument
or a tune associated with wine-pressing (gat = winepress) — perhaps
a harvest celebration song. This is the first pure hymn of praise
in the Psalter and a creation theology psalm. David contemplates
the night sky (moon and stars — v.3, suggesting composition under
a night sky, possibly as a shepherd) and is overwhelmed by the
paradox: the God of infinite heavens is mindful of finite humanity.
The NT applies vv.4-6 christologically — Hebrews 2:6-9 sees the
ultimate "man" crowned with glory as Jesus, and 1 Corinthians
15:27 applies "all things under his feet" to Christ's lordship.
The psalm forms an inclusio (envelope structure) with v.1 and v.9
as identical frames.

Structure:
- God's Majestic Name (v.1)
- Strength from Weakness (v.2)
- Human Smallness Before Creation (vv.3-4)
- Human Dignity and Dominion (vv.5-8)
- God's Majestic Name (v.9)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "O LORD our Lord, how excellent is thy name in all the
        earth!" (KJV) — Two different Hebrew words: "Yahweh" (LORD,
        the covenant name) and "Adonai" (Lord, sovereign master).
        The psalm addresses the covenant God who is also universal
        sovereign. His "name" = His character, reputation, and
        self-revelation.

v.4  — "What is man, that thou art mindful of him? and the son of
        man, that thou visitest him?" (KJV) — The central question
        of theological anthropology. "Man" (enosh = frail mortal)
        and "son of man" (ben-adam = child of dust). Against the
        vastness of creation, humanity is impossibly small — yet
        God is "mindful" (remembers, pays attention) and "visits"
        (attends to, cares for).

v.5  — "For thou hast made him a little lower than the angels, and
        hast crowned him with glory and honour." (KJV) — "Angels"
        = Hebrew "elohim" (God/gods/divine beings). KJV follows
        the LXX ("angels"); the Hebrew says "God" — humanity is
        made just barely below the divine. Either way, the point
        is staggering: humans are crowned with glory like royalty.

v.6  — "Thou madest him to have dominion over the works of thy
        hands; thou hast put all things under his feet" (KJV) —
        The Genesis 1:26-28 dominion mandate in poetic form. "All
        things under his feet" is applied to Christ in 1 Cor 15:27
        and Eph 1:22.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.1   — Psalm 148:13 — "His name alone is excellent; his glory is
         above the earth and heaven." Same exaltation of the Name.
v.2   — Matthew 21:16 — Jesus quotes this verse when children cry
         "Hosanna" in the temple: "Out of the mouth of babes..."
         The religious leaders are silenced by children's praise.
v.4   — Hebrews 2:6-9 — Directly quotes vv.4-6 and applies them
         to Jesus, who was "made a little lower than the angels"
         in incarnation and is now "crowned with glory."
v.5   — Genesis 1:26-27 — "Let us make man in our image." The
         theological basis for human dignity and near-divine status.
v.6   — Genesis 1:28 — "Have dominion over the fish of the sea,
         and over the fowl of the air." Psalm 8 is Genesis 1:28
         set to music.
v.6   — 1 Corinthians 15:27 — "He hath put all things under his
         feet." Applied to Christ's eschatological lordship.
v.6   — Ephesians 1:22 — "Hath put all things under his feet."
         Christ as the true Adam exercising full dominion.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1 — KJV: "how excellent is thy name" / ESV: "how majestic is
      your name" — Hebrew "addir" = majestic, glorious, mighty.
      ESV's "majestic" is more precise than KJV's general
      "excellent."

v.2 — KJV: "ordained strength" / ESV: "established strength" —
      Both translate "yissadta oz." The paradox: God establishes
      strength from infants' mouths. The weakest source produces
      the most effective weapon against God's enemies.

v.5 — KJV: "a little lower than the angels" / ESV: "a little lower
      than the heavenly beings" — Hebrew "elohim" can mean God,
      gods, divine beings, or angels. The LXX (Greek OT) translated
      as "angels," and the KJV follows this. ESV hedges with
      "heavenly beings." The Hebrew literally says "God" — humanity
      is barely below the divine in status. This is either the
      highest possible view of humanity or a reference to angelic
      beings.

v.5 — KJV: "crowned him with glory and honour" / ESV: "crowned him
      with glory and honor" — Same translation. The metaphor is
      royal coronation — God places a crown on humanity. Humans are
      not servants merely but royalty in creation.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Start and end with worship (vv.1, 9). The psalm's identical
   opening and closing teach that true reflection on creation
   begins and ends with worship. Knowledge that does not produce
   praise has missed the point.

2. You are impossibly small and incredibly valued (vv.3-5).
   Hold both truths simultaneously. Against the universe you are
   nothing; in God's eyes you are crowned royalty. Neither truth
   cancels the other. Humility and dignity coexist.

3. Dominion is responsibility, not exploitation (v.6). "Under his
   feet" is stewardship language, not domination language. The
   crown of v.5 comes with the responsibility of v.6. Human
   dignity implies human accountability for creation.

4. God's strength works through weakness (v.2). Babies silence the
   enemy. God's pattern is to use the least likely instruments for
   His greatest works — infants, shepherds, crucified Messiahs.
   Do not despise weakness; God often chooses it as His vehicle.

5. Christ is the true fulfillment (vv.4-6, via Hebrews 2). We see
   "not yet all things put under" human feet (Heb 2:8) — the
   dominion mandate is not fully realized in fallen humanity. But
   we see JESUS, crowned with glory, exercising the dominion Adam
   lost. Psalm 8 is ultimately about Christ.
