Psalms - Chapter 7
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
An individual lament — David appeals to God as righteous judge against the slander of Cush the Benjaminite, affirming that God tests hearts and that the wicked fall into the pit they dig for others.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Individual Lament / Protestation of Innocence
Attributed Author: David — "Shiggaion of David, which he sang unto
the LORD, concerning the words of Cush the Benjamite"
Key Themes: False accusation, appeal to divine justice, self-
imprecation (inviting judgment on self if guilty), the wicked
trapped by their own schemes, God as righteous judge

Historical Context: The "Shiggaion" (probably "wandering song" —
irregular/passionate meter) and the reference to "Cush the
Benjamite" place this in a specific but uncertain context. Cush may
be a member of Saul's tribe who slandered David (since Saul was
Benjamite). David is falsely accused and responds with a remarkable
oath: if I have done what they accuse me of, let my enemies have
me (vv.3-5). This is a courtroom psalm — David appeals to God as
judge, presents his innocence, and asks for vindication. The
principle of self-destruction (vv.14-16) — the wicked falling into
their own pit — is one of Scripture's most common justice themes.

Structure:
- Cry for Refuge (vv.1-2)
- Oath of Innocence (vv.3-5)
- Appeal to the Divine Judge (vv.6-9)
- God as Defender and Judge (vv.10-13)
- The Self-Destruction of Evil (vv.14-16)
- Praise for God's Righteousness (v.17)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.8  — "Judge me, O LORD, according to my righteousness, and
        according to mine integrity that is in me." (KJV) — David
        does not claim sinlessness but innocence of the specific
        charge. He can ask for judicial examination because the
        accusation is false. This is not arrogance but confidence
        in a clean conscience regarding the matter at hand.

v.11 — "God judgeth the righteous, and God is angry with the wicked
        every day." (KJV) — God's anger at wickedness is not
        occasional but continuous. This is not capricious rage but
        settled moral opposition to evil. The righteous Judge does
        not ignore injustice.

v.15 — "He made a pit, and digged it, and is fallen into the ditch
        which he made." (KJV) — The "boomerang principle" of divine
        justice: evil recoils on the evildoer. The schemer is
        caught in his own scheme. This is poetic justice — not
        arbitrary punishment but the natural consequence of evil
        turning on itself.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.2   — 1 Peter 5:8 — "Your adversary the devil, as a roaring
         lion, walketh about, seeking whom he may devour." The
         lion-enemy imagery.
v.8   — Psalm 26:1 — "Judge me, O LORD; for I have walked in mine
         integrity." Same appeal for vindication.
v.9   — 1 Samuel 16:7 — "The LORD looketh on the heart." God tests
         hearts and minds.
v.9   — Jeremiah 17:10 — "I the LORD search the heart, I try the
         reins." God's examination of inner motives.
v.11  — Nahum 1:2 — "God is jealous, and the LORD revengeth." God's
         settled opposition to evil.
v.14  — James 1:15 — "When lust hath conceived, it bringeth forth
         sin: and sin, when it is finished, bringeth forth death."
         Same birth imagery for evil's progression.
v.15  — Proverbs 26:27 — "Whoso diggeth a pit shall fall therein."
         The proverbial statement of this principle.
v.15  — Esther 7:10 — "So they hanged Haman on the gallows that he
         had prepared for Mordecai." Historical fulfillment.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.4 — KJV: "yea, I have delivered him that without cause is mine
      enemy" / ESV: "or plundered my enemy without cause" — The
      Hebrew is debated. KJV reads David as SAVING his enemy
      (proving his innocence — he helped even his attacker). ESV
      reads David denying he plundered his enemy. Both support
      innocence, but from different angles.

v.11 — KJV: "God judgeth the righteous, and God is angry with the
       wicked every day" / ESV: "God is a righteous judge, and a
       God who feels indignation every day" — ESV makes God the
       "righteous judge" (adjective); KJV has God "judging the
       righteous" (verb) and separately being angry at the wicked.
       The Hebrew supports both readings.

v.12 — KJV: "If he turn not, he will whet his sword" / ESV: "If
       a man does not repent, God will whet his sword" — KJV's
       subject is ambiguous (God? the wicked?). ESV clarifies:
       "if a man does not repent" = the wicked man; "God will
       whet his sword" = divine judgment follows. The warning is
       conditional — repentance averts judgment.

v.14 — KJV: "he travaileth with iniquity, and hath conceived
       mischief, and brought forth falsehood" / ESV: "the wicked
       man conceives evil and is pregnant with mischief and gives
       birth to lies" — Both capture the birth metaphor. Evil is
       conceived (desire), gestated (planning), and born (action).
       The sequence mirrors James 1:15.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.1 — WEB: "Yahweh, my God, I take refuge in you" — WEB uses "Yahweh" where KJV has "LORD," placing the personal covenant name at the psalm's opening cry. "Take refuge" (also ASV) is more precise than KJV's "put my trust" — Hebrew "chasah" means to flee for shelter, implying danger and urgency.
- v.1 — ASV: "O Jehovah my God, in thee do I take refuge" — ASV's "Jehovah" likewise preserves the divine name. The combination "Jehovah my God" (YHWH Elohay) invokes both the covenant name and the personal possessive — "my God" — maximum intimacy in crisis.
- v.8 — NET: "Vindicate me, Lord, because I am innocent, because I am blameless, O Exalted One" — NET replaces KJV's "Judge me, O LORD, according to my righteousness" with "Vindicate me...because I am innocent." This is more interpretive — it declares innocence outright rather than asking God to evaluate. "O Exalted One" for Hebrew "al" (Most High/over) is a bold paraphrase.
- v.9 — ASV: "the righteous God trieth the minds and hearts" — ASV renders Hebrew "bochen libboth u-kelayoth" (tests hearts and kidneys/reins) as "minds and hearts." KJV has "hearts and reins." The Hebrew "kelayoth" (kidneys) was considered the seat of deep emotions — equivalent to our "gut feelings."
- v.11 — NET: "God is a just judge; he is angry throughout the day" — NET's "throughout the day" differs from KJV's "every day." Both translate Hebrew "bekhol-yom," but NET implies continuous anger within each day, while KJV implies daily recurrence. Both convey God's unrelenting moral opposition to wickedness.
- v.12 — NET: "If a person does not repent, God will wield his sword" — NET makes both subject and object explicit where KJV is ambiguous ("If he turn not, he will whet his sword" — who is "he"?). NET clarifies: the person must repent; God is the one who acts in judgment. This resolves a genuine interpretive difficulty.
- v.13 — WEB: "He makes ready his flaming arrows" — WEB's "flaming arrows" and ASV's "fiery shafts" translate Hebrew "dolqim" (burning). KJV has "ordaineth his arrows against the persecutors" — missing the fire imagery entirely. The burning arrows depict divine judgment as inescapable and consuming.
- v.17 — WEB: "I will give thanks to Yahweh according to his righteousness, and will sing praise to the name of Yahweh Most High" — WEB closes with "Yahweh Most High" (YHWH Elyon), combining the covenant name with the title of supreme sovereignty. This full naming — personal yet exalted — is David's final word: the God he knows by name rules above all.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. You can appeal to God when falsely accused (vv.3-5). David does
   not defend himself before men — he appeals to the divine court.
   When human justice fails or humans slander, the righteous Judge
   remains. Present your case to Him.

2. Evil is self-destructive (vv.14-16). The pit-digger falls into
   his own pit. This is not mere poetic justice — it is the nature
   of evil to consume its practitioner. You do not always need to
   fight your enemies; often their own schemes will undo them.

3. God's anger is moral, not capricious (v.11). God is angry with
   wickedness EVERY DAY — this is not mood swings but moral
   consistency. A judge who never gets angry at injustice is not a
   good judge. Divine anger is the proper response of perfect love
   to moral evil.

4. Repentance is always available (v.12). "If he turn not" — the
   conditional implies that turning IS possible. Even with the
   sword being sharpened, the door of repentance remains open.
   Judgment is what happens when mercy is refused.

5. End with praise, not bitterness (v.17). Despite false
   accusation and danger, David ends by praising God's
   righteousness. The falsely accused can praise because God's
   character — not current circumstances — is the basis of joy.
