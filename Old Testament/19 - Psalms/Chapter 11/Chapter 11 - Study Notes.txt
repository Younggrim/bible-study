Psalms - Chapter 11
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A psalm of trust — when counseled to flee like a bird, David refuses: 'In the LORD put I my trust.' The LORD is in His holy temple testing the righteous and wicked; He loves righteousness.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Trust/Confidence Psalm
Attributed Author: David — "To the chief Musician, A Psalm of
David"
Key Themes: Refusing fear-based flight, God's sovereign
observation from heaven, testing of the righteous, judgment on the
wicked, beholding God's face

Historical Context: This short, powerful psalm likely dates from
David's time fleeing Saul. Well-meaning advisors urge him to flee
"like a bird to your mountain" — to abandon his post and run.
David's response is not recklessness but theology: the LORD is in
His temple, His throne is in heaven, His eyes see everything.
Why flee when the sovereign God is watching? The psalm addresses
the temptation to abandon faith-based action for fear-based
escape. The question "If the foundations be destroyed, what can
the righteous do?" (v.3) is one of Scripture's most profound
questions about societal collapse.

Structure:
- Rejection of Fearful Advice (vv.1-3)
- God's Sovereign Observation (vv.4-5)
- Judgment on the Wicked (v.6)
- The Reward of the Upright (v.7)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "In the LORD put I my trust: How say ye to my soul, Flee
        as a bird to your mountain?" (KJV) — David's opening is
        his answer: I already HAVE my refuge (the LORD), so why
        would I flee to a mountain? The advice to run is
        contradicted by the reality of where David's trust lies.

v.3  — "If the foundations be destroyed, what can the righteous
        do?" (KJV) — The profound question: when society's moral
        foundations collapse, what options remain? The answer is v.4
        — the LORD's throne in heaven is unshaken. Earthly
        foundations may fail; heavenly ones cannot.

v.4  — "The LORD is in his holy temple, the LORD'S throne is in
        heaven: his eyes behold, his eyelids try, the children of
        men." (KJV) — God sees from His throne. "Eyelids try" =
        the narrowing of eyes in careful examination. God is not
        distant or unaware — He examines closely.

v.7  — "For the righteous LORD loveth righteousness; his
        countenance doth behold the upright." (KJV) — The reward:
        the upright will see God's face. In contrast to the
        wicked's cup of fire (v.6), the righteous receive God's
        presence — the beatific vision.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.1   — 1 Samuel 23:14 — David in the wilderness, trusting God
         rather than fleeing permanently.
v.3   — Isaiah 19:10 — When foundations are destroyed, only God
         remains the foundation (Isa 28:16).
v.4   — Habakkuk 2:20 — "The LORD is in his holy temple: let all
         the earth keep silence." Same truth — God is sovereign.
v.5   — 1 Peter 1:7 — "The trial of your faith, being much more
         precious than gold." God tests the righteous through fire.
v.6   — Genesis 19:24 — "The LORD rained upon Sodom and upon
         Gomorrah brimstone and fire." The historical precedent
         for v.6's imagery.
v.7   — Matthew 5:8 — "Blessed are the pure in heart: for they
         shall see God." NT fulfillment of beholding His face.
v.7   — Revelation 22:4 — "And they shall see his face." The
         ultimate fulfillment.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.2 — KJV: "privily shoot" / ESV: "shoot in the dark" — Hebrew
      "b'mo ophel" = in darkness/gloom. The wicked attack secretly,
      from concealment. ESV is more literal.

v.5 — KJV: "The LORD trieth the righteous" / ESV: "The LORD tests
      the righteous" — Hebrew "bachan" = test/examine/prove. Both
      translations convey the same idea: God purifies the righteous
      through testing, while His soul hates (strong rejection) the
      violent wicked.

v.6 — KJV: "rain snares, fire and brimstone" / ESV: "rain coals
      on the wicked; fire and sulfur" — Hebrew "pachim" is
      debated — "snares" (KJV) or "coals" (ESV). The Sodom-and-
      Gomorrah imagery is unmistakable either way.

v.7 — KJV: "his countenance doth behold the upright" / ESV: "the
      upright shall behold his face" — These are OPPOSITE subjects.
      KJV: God looks at the upright (divine attention). ESV: The
      upright look at God (beatific vision). Hebrew can support
      both — "yashar yechezu panemo." ESV's reading connects to
      the great biblical hope of seeing God face-to-face.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.1 — ASV: "In Jehovah do I take refuge" / WEB: "In Yahweh, I take refuge" / NET: "In the Lord I have taken shelter" — KJV: "In the LORD put I my trust." NET uses "shelter" (a physical metaphor) where KJV uses the abstract "trust." ASV/WEB preserve "take refuge" (Hebrew "chasah"), which carries imagery of fleeing to safety — ironic given the advice to "flee like a bird."

- v.1 — NET: "How can you say to me, 'Flee to a mountain like a bird'" — NET adds "like a bird" to interpret the Hebrew, while KJV has "Flee as a bird to your mountain." The bird-and-mountain imagery is present in all versions but NET rephrases the comparison.

- v.4 — ASV: "Jehovah is in his holy temple; Jehovah, his throne is in heaven" — ASV uses "Jehovah" twice, emphasizing the repeated divine name in the Hebrew. WEB similarly doubles "Yahweh." NET: "The Lord is in his holy temple; the Lord's throne is in heaven" — using the standard title.

- v.5 — NET: "The Lord approves of the godly, but he hates the wicked" — NET interprets "bachan" (test/examine/prove) as "approves," which loses the refining/testing imagery. KJV/ASV: "The LORD trieth the righteous" — preserving the idea that God puts the righteous THROUGH trials, not merely approving them.

- v.6 — WEB: "blazing coals; fire, sulfur, and scorching wind" vs. KJV: "rain snares, fire and brimstone, and an horrible tempest" — Hebrew "pachim" is debated (snares or coals). WEB reads "blazing coals," KJV reads "snares." NET: "May he rain down burning coals and brimstone" — making it optative (a wish/prayer) rather than declarative.

- v.7 — ASV: "For Jehovah is righteous; he loveth righteousness" / NET: "Certainly the Lord is just; he rewards godly deeds" — NET adds "rewards" and "deeds," interpreting "loves righteousness" as active recompense. This is more interpretive than the straightforward "loves righteousness" of ASV/WEB.

- v.7 — NET: "The upright will experience his favor" vs. KJV: "his countenance doth behold the upright" vs. ESV: "the upright shall behold his face" — Three radically different readings. NET avoids the face-of-God theology entirely. ASV follows ESV: "The upright shall behold his face" — the beatific vision reading. KJV reverses the subject: God looks at them.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Don't let fear-counsel override faith-conviction (v.1). Others
   may advise flight when God calls you to stand. Test all advice
   — even well-meaning advice — against your trust in God.

2. When foundations collapse, look up (vv.3-4). The answer to
   "what can the righteous do?" is not panic but perspective.
   God's throne is undisturbed by earthly chaos. Heavenly reality
   remains stable when earthly reality crumbles.

3. Testing is not punishment (v.5). God tests the righteous — not
   to destroy but to refine. The same God who hates the violent
   tests the faithful. Distinguish between divine discipline
   (refining) and divine judgment (punishing).

4. The wicked receive their "cup" (v.6). The cup metaphor appears
   throughout Scripture for divine judgment (Jer 25:15; Rev 14:10).
   The wicked's portion is not arbitrary — it is the logical
   consequence of loving violence.

5. The ultimate reward is God's face (v.7). Not prosperity, not
   ease, not vindication before enemies — but seeing God. The
   highest good is not what God gives but who God IS. The upright
   will behold Him.
