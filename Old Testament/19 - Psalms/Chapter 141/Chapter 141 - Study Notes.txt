Psalms - Chapter 141
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A lament — David prays his prayer would rise as incense before God; he asks for a guard over his mouth, willingness to receive righteous rebuke, and deliverance from the snares of evildoers.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Individual Lament / Evening Prayer
Attributed Author: David (superscription: "A Psalm of David")
Key Themes: Prayer as incense, guarding the mouth, welcoming
righteous correction, refusing the enticements of the wicked,
evening worship

Historical Context: Psalm 141 is David's evening prayer (paired
with Psalm 5, his morning prayer). The imagery of prayer rising
like incense and lifted hands like the evening sacrifice (v.2) has
made this one of the most important psalms in Christian liturgy
for vespers (evening worship). The psalm reveals David's awareness
of two dangers: (1) his own tongue needing a guard (v.3), and
(2) the temptation to join the wicked (v.4). He asks for both
external protection and internal discipline.

Structure:
- Urgent Prayer as Incense (vv.1-2)
- Prayer for Self-Discipline (vv.3-4)
- Welcoming Righteous Correction (v.5)
- Imprecation and Trust (vv.6-10)

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Prayer is incense (v.2). Your prayers rise to God as fragrant
   offering. Even simple prayers are received like sacred incense
   in His presence. Never underestimate the weight of prayer.

2. Ask God to guard your mouth (v.3). If David — the man after
   God's heart — needed divine help with his tongue, so do you.
   Pray this verse daily: "Set a watch, O LORD, before my mouth."

3. Welcome correction (v.5). Rebuke from the righteous is not
   attack — it's anointing oil. The person who corrects you in
   love is doing you a kindness. Receive it.

4. Don't be enticed by the wicked (v.4). "Let me not eat of their
   dainties." The wicked offer attractive enticements. David
   prays for the will to refuse what looks good but leads to evil.
