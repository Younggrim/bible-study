Psalms - Chapter 67
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A missionary psalm — a prayer for God's blessing upon Israel so that His way may be known among all nations and all peoples may praise Him; the earth shall yield her increase.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Hymn of Praise / Missionary Psalm
Attributed Author: Anonymous
Key Themes: God's blessing for global mission, all nations
praising God, God's righteous governance, harvest blessing, the
Aaronic benediction extended to the world

Historical Context: This brief, beautiful psalm (7 verses) is
often called the "Old Testament Lord's Prayer" or the "Missionary
Psalm." It opens by echoing the Aaronic Benediction (Numbers
6:24-26) but with a PURPOSE: "that thy way may be known upon
earth" (v.2). Israel's blessing was never meant to be hoarded —
it was intended to be a conduit of God's self-revelation to ALL
nations. The psalm has a concentric structure with the refrain
"Let the peoples praise thee" (vv.3,5) framing the center (v.4).
It was likely used at harvest festivals (v.6 — "the earth has
yielded her increase") to remind Israel that their agricultural
blessing was connected to their calling to be a light to the
nations.

Structure:
- Prayer for Blessing with Purpose (vv.1-2)
- Refrain: Let the Peoples Praise God (v.3)
- Nations Rejoicing in God's Justice (v.4)
- Refrain: Let the Peoples Praise God (v.5)
- Harvest Blessing and Universal Fear (vv.6-7)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1-2 — "God be merciful unto us, and bless us; and cause his
        face to shine upon us; THAT thy way may be known upon
        earth." (KJV) — The critical word is "THAT." Blessing has
        a purpose — it is not an end in itself. God blesses Israel
        SO THAT the world will know Him. Blessing received is
        blessing meant to be transmitted.

v.4  — "O let the nations be glad and sing for joy: for thou
        shalt judge the people righteously, and govern the nations
        upon earth." (KJV) — The nations rejoice specifically
        because God judges RIGHTEOUSLY. Just governance is cause
        for celebration. Under God's rule, equity reigns.

v.7  — "God shall bless us; and all the ends of the earth shall
        fear him." (KJV) — The psalm's trajectory: from "us" to
        "all the ends of the earth." The circle expands from Israel
        outward until the entire earth fears (reveres) God.
        Blessing always flows outward.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.1   — Numbers 6:24-26 — The Aaronic Benediction: "The LORD bless
         thee, and keep thee: The LORD make his face shine upon
         thee." Psalm 67 applies this blessing missionally.
v.2   — Genesis 12:2-3 — "I will bless thee... and thou shalt be
         a blessing... in thee shall all families of the earth be
         blessed." The Abrahamic mission statement.
v.4   — Revelation 15:4 — "Who shall not fear thee, O Lord, and
         glorify thy name? for thou only art holy: for all nations
         shall come and worship before thee."
v.6   — Leviticus 26:4 — "Then I will give you rain in due season,
         and the land shall yield her increase." Harvest as sign
         of covenant faithfulness.
v.7   — Psalm 22:27 — "All the ends of the world shall remember
         and turn unto the LORD: and all the kindreds of the
         nations shall worship before thee."

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1 — KJV: "God be merciful unto us" / ESV: "May God be gracious
      to us" — Hebrew "chanan" = to be gracious, show favor. ESV's
      "gracious" is closer to the Hebrew; KJV's "merciful"
      (racham) is a related but different concept.

v.2 — KJV: "thy saving health" / ESV: "your saving power" —
      Hebrew "yeshuah" = salvation, deliverance. KJV's "saving
      health" is archaic; "salvation" or "saving power" captures
      the comprehensive deliverance God offers all nations.

v.4 — KJV: "govern the nations" / ESV: "guide the nations" —
      Hebrew "nachah" = lead, guide. ESV's "guide" is more
      precise; the image is of a shepherd leading his flock, not
      a king issuing decrees from a throne.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.1 — NET: "May God show us his favor and bless us. May he smile on us" — NET paraphrases "cause his face to shine upon us" as "smile on us," a vivid modern metaphor but losing the Hebrew imagery of radiant divine presence (connected to the Aaronic blessing of Numbers 6:25).

- v.2 — ASV: "Thy salvation among all nations" / NET: "all nations will know how you deliver your people" vs. KJV: "thy saving health among all nations" — ASV uses direct "salvation" (yeshuah). NET adds "your people" (not in Hebrew) and paraphrases "salvation" as "how you deliver." WEB: "your salvation among all nations."

- v.4 — NET: "For you execute justice among the nations" vs. KJV: "thou shalt judge the people righteously" — NET uses "execute justice" for "judge righteously" (shephat mishor), slightly changing the nuance from judicial fairness to active justice enforcement. ASV: "judge the peoples with equity."

- v.4 — NET: "Let foreigners rejoice and celebrate" vs. KJV: "O let the nations be glad and sing for joy" — NET renders "nations" (goyim) as "foreigners," shifting from a universal category to an outsider perspective. ASV/WEB keep "nations."

- v.7 — NET: "Then all the ends of the earth will give him the honor he deserves" vs. KJV: "all the ends of the earth shall fear him" — NET interprets "fear" (yira) as "give honor," softening the reverential dread to mere respect. ASV/WEB preserve "fear."

- v.6 — NET: "The earth yields its crops. May God, our God, bless us" — NET uses "crops" for "increase" (yevulah) and changes the tense from past/prophetic to present. ASV/WEB: "The earth hath/has yielded its increase."

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Blessing has a missionary purpose (vv.1-2). God blesses you
   not so you can accumulate but so you can transmit. Every
   blessing received is a blessing meant to flow through you to
   others. The question is always: "That WHAT?"

2. God's just rule brings joy, not fear (v.4). Nations should SING
   FOR JOY because God judges righteously. Under human rulers,
   judgment is often corrupt. Under God's governance, everyone
   gets equity. That is cause for celebration.

3. The harvest is evidence of God's faithfulness (v.6). Physical
   provision points to spiritual reality. When God blesses
   materially, receive it as a sign of His covenant care — and
   share it as a witness.

4. The trajectory of faith is always outward (v.7). From "us" to
   "all the ends of the earth." A faith that stays turned inward
   has missed its purpose. Biblical blessing always pushes
   outward toward those who have not yet known God.
