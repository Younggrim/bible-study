Psalms - Chapter 58
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
An imprecatory psalm — David confronts unjust rulers who speak not righteousness, praying God will break their teeth and make them vanish; the righteous will rejoice when they see vengeance, for God judges the earth.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Imprecatory Psalm
Attributed Author: David
Key Themes: Corrupt judges, injustice, innate human wickedness,
divine vengeance, God as righteous Judge, vindication of the
righteous

Historical Context: This Miktam of David is set to "Do Not Destroy"
(Al Tashheth) and addresses corrupt rulers or judges ("gods" /
"congregation" in v.1) who pervert justice. The Hebrew "elem" in
v.1 is debated — it could mean "silence" (are you silent about
justice?), "gods" (divine beings/rulers), or "congregation." The
psalm is one of the strongest imprecatory psalms, using vivid
imagery to call for God's judgment on the unjust. It may reflect
David's experience under Saul's corrupt administration or the
general oppression of the powerless by wicked authorities. The
psalm's harsh imagery (broken teeth, melting snails, bloody feet)
reflects the seriousness with which Scripture treats injustice.

Structure:
- Accusation Against Corrupt Judges (vv.1-2)
- The Nature of the Wicked from Birth (vv.3-5)
- Imprecations Against the Wicked (vv.6-9)
- The Righteous Vindicated (vv.10-11)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "Do ye indeed speak righteousness, O congregation? do ye
        judge uprightly, O ye sons of men?" (KJV) — A rhetorical
        challenge to those in authority. The expected answer is no.
        Those entrusted with justice have perverted it. ESV renders
        "you gods" — viewing rulers as divine representatives who
        have failed their mandate.

v.3  — "The wicked are estranged from the womb: they go astray as
        soon as they be born, speaking lies." (KJV) — Like Psalm
        51:5, this affirms innate sinfulness. Wickedness is not
        merely learned behavior but a condition present from birth.
        The propensity to lie is natural to fallen humanity.

v.6  — "Break their teeth, O God, in their mouth: break out the
        great teeth of the young lions, O LORD." (KJV) — The
        wicked are compared to predatory beasts. Breaking their
        teeth = removing their power to devour the weak. This is
        not personal vindictiveness but a cry for the protection
        of victims.

v.11 — "So that a man shall say, Verily there is a reward for the
        righteous: verily he is a God that judgeth in the earth."
        (KJV) — The ultimate purpose of divine judgment: to
        demonstrate that righteousness is rewarded and that God
        actively governs. Justice is not an illusion.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.1   — Psalm 82:1-2 — "God standeth in the congregation of the
         mighty; he judgeth among the gods." Same address to unjust
         rulers as "gods."
v.3   — Psalm 51:5 — "In sin did my mother conceive me." Same
         doctrine of innate sinfulness.
v.4   — Romans 3:13 — "The poison of asps is under their lips."
         Paul uses this serpent imagery for universal sin.
v.6   — Psalm 3:7 — "Thou hast broken the teeth of the ungodly."
         Same imagery of disarming the wicked.
v.10  — Revelation 19:2 — "True and righteous are his judgments...
         he hath avenged the blood of his servants." Ultimate
         vindication.
v.11  — Psalm 9:16 — "The LORD is known by the judgment which he
         executeth." God revealed through justice.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1 — KJV: "O congregation" / ESV: "you gods" — The Hebrew "elem"
      is highly debated. It could be: (1) a scribal variant of
      "elim" (gods/mighty ones = rulers); (2) related to "elem"
      (silence); or (3) a form of "eylam" (congregation). ESV
      follows the interpretation of rulers as God's representatives.

v.8 — KJV: "As a snail which melteth" / ESV: "like the snail that
      dissolves into slime" — The ancient understanding was that
      snails melted as they moved (their trail being their
      substance disappearing). The image is of gradual dissolution
      and disappearance.

v.9 — KJV: "Before your pots can feel the thorns" / ESV: "Sooner
      than your pots can feel the heat of thorns" — An image of
      swiftness: before a cooking fire of thorns can even heat the
      pot, God's judgment comes. Thorns burn quickly but even that
      speed is too slow for God's justice.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Those in authority will answer for how they use their power
   (vv.1-2). Position carries responsibility. Every judge, ruler,
   and leader will give account for whether they used their
   authority for justice or for personal gain.

2. Human wickedness is innate, not merely environmental (v.3).
   This is uncomfortable but biblical. We are not born neutral and
   corrupted by society — we are born with a bent toward sin.
   This makes grace necessary, not optional.

3. It is right to pray for justice against oppressors (v.6). The
   imprecatory psalms are not about personal revenge but about
   God's protection of the vulnerable. When the powerful crush the
   weak, it is righteous to cry out for God to intervene.

4. God's judgment vindicates His character (v.11). When justice
   finally comes, it proves that God is real, that He sees, and
   that righteousness matters. Delayed justice is not denied
   justice — God WILL judge the earth.
