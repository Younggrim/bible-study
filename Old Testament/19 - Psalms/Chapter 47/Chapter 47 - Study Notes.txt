Psalms - Chapter 47
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
An enthronement psalm — 'Clap your hands, all ye people'; God has gone up with a shout, the LORD with the sound of a trumpet; He reigns over the nations and sits upon His holy throne.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Hymn of Praise / Enthronement Psalm
Attributed Author: Sons of Korah (To the chief Musician, A Psalm
for the sons of Korah)
Key Themes: Universal kingship of God, exuberant praise, God's
ascension, nations gathered to God, joyful worship

Historical Context: Psalm 47 is one of the "Enthronement Psalms"
(alongside Psalms 93, 95-99) celebrating God's kingship over all
the earth. It continues the triumphant tone of Psalm 46 and forms
part of a cluster of praise psalms by the Sons of Korah (46-48).
While some scholars connect it to the Ark's processional entry into
Jerusalem (2 Samuel 6) or to a New Year's celebration of God's
sovereignty, its language transcends any single historical occasion.

The psalm is remarkable for its universal scope — "all ye people"
(v. 1), "all the earth" (v. 2, 7), "the heathen/nations" (v. 8),
"the people of the God of Abraham" (v. 9). This is not merely
Israelite nationalism but a vision of God reigning over every
nation. The mention of "the God of Abraham" (v. 9) rather than "the
God of Moses" points to the Abrahamic promise: "in thee shall all
families of the earth be blessed" (Genesis 12:3).

Verse 5 — "God is gone up with a shout, the LORD with the sound of
a trumpet" — has been traditionally associated with Christ's
Ascension. The early church connected this psalm to Ascension Day
liturgy, understanding God's "going up" as fulfilled in Christ's
ascent to the Father's right hand (Acts 1:9-11). The psalm thus
looks forward to the day when every knee shall bow and the nations
gather under the rule of the ascended King.

Structure:
- Call to Exuberant Praise (vv. 1-4)
- God's Ascension with Shout and Trumpet (vv. 5-7)
- God's Universal Reign Over All Nations (vv. 8-9)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
Verse 1 — "O clap your hands, all ye people; shout unto God with
the voice of triumph."
The psalm commands physical, exuberant worship — clapping and
shouting. This is not dignified silence but jubilant celebration.
"All ye people" (kol ha'ammim — all peoples/nations) extends the
invitation beyond Israel to every human being. God's kingship
demands universal acknowledgment.

Verse 5 — "God is gone up with a shout, the LORD with the sound of
a trumpet."
The central verse of the psalm, traditionally connected to Christ's
Ascension (Acts 1:9). The imagery may originally refer to the Ark
of the Covenant being carried up to Zion (2 Samuel 6:15: "David
and all the house of Israel brought up the ark of the LORD with
shouting, and with the sound of the trumpet"). In its fullest
Messianic sense, it celebrates Christ's triumphant return to
heaven after completing His redemptive work.

Verse 7 — "For God is the King of all the earth: sing ye praises
with understanding."
God's kingship is universal — not merely over Israel but over "all
the earth." The command to sing "with understanding" (Hebrew:
"maskil") indicates thoughtful, intelligent worship. True praise
engages the mind as well as the emotions. We are to understand
what we praise and whom we praise.

Verse 9 — "The princes of the people are gathered together, even
the people of the God of Abraham: for the shields of the earth
belong unto God: he is greatly exalted."
A stunning eschatological vision: the princes/rulers of all nations
gathered as "the people of the God of Abraham." This anticipates
Gentile inclusion in God's family — fulfilled in Christ where
"there is neither Jew nor Greek" (Galatians 3:28-29). The "shields
of the earth" (rulers/protectors of nations) all belong to God.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v. 1 — Isaiah 55:12 — "Ye shall go out with joy... the mountains
and the hills shall break forth before you into singing, and all
the trees of the field shall clap their hands." Creation itself
joins the applause.

v. 2 — Psalm 95:3 — "For the LORD is a great God, and a great King
above all gods." God's supremacy over all other claimed
authorities.

v. 5 — Acts 1:9-11 — "While they beheld, he was taken up; and a
cloud received him out of their sight." Christ's ascension fulfills
the imagery of God going up with a shout.

v. 5 — Ephesians 4:8 — "When he ascended up on high, he led
captivity captive, and gave gifts unto men." Paul connects
ascension to triumph and generosity.

v. 7 — 1 Corinthians 14:15 — "I will sing with the spirit, and I
will sing with the understanding also." Worship that engages the
mind, not just emotion.

v. 8 — Revelation 11:15 — "The kingdoms of this world are become
the kingdoms of our Lord, and of his Christ; and he shall reign for
ever and ever." The ultimate fulfillment of God's universal reign.

v. 9 — Galatians 3:28-29 — "If ye be Christ's, then are ye
Abraham's seed, and heirs according to the promise." All nations
gathered as Abraham's children through faith.

v. 9 — Genesis 12:3 — "In thee shall all families of the earth be
blessed." The Abrahamic promise of universal blessing fulfilled in
the gathering of all peoples to God.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Worship should be exuberant. This psalm commands clapping,
   shouting, and singing — physical expressions of joy. While
   reverence has its place, so does jubilation. If God is truly
   King of all the earth, our response should sometimes overflow
   in visible, audible celebration. Subdued worship is not always
   more spiritual.

2. God's reign is universal, not tribal. "King of all the earth"
   (v. 7) — not merely King of our denomination, nation, or
   tradition. His sovereignty extends over every people, culture,
   and government. This expands our prayer life, our missions
   vision, and our understanding of God's purposes.

3. Christ ascended in triumph. Verse 5 reminds us that Jesus did
   not merely depart — He ascended as conquering King, enthroned
   at the Father's right hand with all authority. His current
   position is one of supreme authority, and He awaits the day
   when every knee will bow.

4. Worship should engage the mind. "Sing with understanding" (v. 7)
   calls us beyond emotional experience to theological
   comprehension. We should know what we're singing, why we're
   singing it, and whom we're singing to. Mindless worship
   dishonors the God who gave us minds.

5. All nations will be gathered. The vision of verse 9 — princes of
   every people assembled as God's own — fuels hope for global
   redemption. Every tongue, tribe, and nation has representatives
   destined for God's kingdom. This is not wishful thinking but
   prophetic certainty.
