Psalms - Chapter 126
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A Song of Ascents — 'When the LORD turned again the captivity of Zion, we were like them that dream'; a joyful remembrance of restoration and prayer for more: 'They that sow in tears shall reap in joy.'

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Song of Ascents / Communal Lament and Hope
Attributed Author: Anonymous (superscription: "A Song of degrees")
Key Themes: The joy of restoration, laughter and singing at
God's deliverance, sowing in tears and reaping in joy, the
dream-like quality of answered prayer

Historical Context: Psalm 126 likely celebrates the return from
Babylonian exile (538 BC) — the restoration of Zion that seemed
like a dream when it happened. The first half recalls past
deliverance with overwhelming joy; the second half prays for
continued/future restoration using agricultural imagery. "Sowing
in tears" and "reaping in joy" has become proverbial. The Negev
streams (v.4) are wadis that are bone-dry in summer but suddenly
rush with water after rain — an image of sudden, dramatic
restoration after long drought.

Structure:
- Remembering Past Restoration (vv.1-3)
- Prayer for Present Restoration (v.4)
- The Principle: Tears to Joy (vv.5-6)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "When the LORD turned again the captivity of Zion, we were
        like them that dream." (KJV) — So unexpected, so good, so
        overwhelming that it seemed unreal. When God moves, it can
        feel like a dream — too good to be true. But it IS true.

v.3  — "The LORD hath done great things for us; whereof we are
        glad." (KJV) — Even the nations said it (v.2). When God
        does something unmistakable, even outsiders acknowledge it.
        The proper response: gladness.

v.5-6 — "They that sow in tears shall reap in joy. He that goeth
        forth and weepeth, bearing precious seed, shall doubtless
        come again with rejoicing, bringing his sheaves with him."
        (KJV) — The agricultural principle of faith: tears are
        seed. Weeping is sowing. The harvest is CERTAIN: "shall
        DOUBTLESS come again." Joy is guaranteed to the faithful
        sower.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1 — "turned the captivity" — Hebrew "shub shebut" can also mean
      "restored the fortunes." Not just ending exile but restoring
      what was lost.

v.4 — "streams in the south" / "watercourses in the Negev" —
      The Negev (dry southland) has wadis that suddenly fill with
      rushing water. The image: sudden, abundant, dramatic
      restoration after extended dryness.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.1 — KJV: "turned again the captivity of Zion" / ASV: "brought back those that returned to Zion" / WEB: "brought back those who returned to Zion" / NET: "restored the well-being of Zion" — Significant interpretive divergence. KJV/ASV/WEB read Hebrew "shivat" as return from captivity; NET reads "shevut" as restoration of fortunes/well-being, not necessarily exile.
- v.2 — KJV: "the heathen" / ASV/WEB: "the nations" / NET: "the nations" — KJV's "heathen" carries pejorative connotation absent from the neutral Hebrew "goyim."
- v.4 — KJV: "as the streams in the south" / ASV: "the streams in the South" / WEB: "like the streams in the Negev" / NET: "just as the streams in the arid south are replenished" — WEB uses the actual Hebrew place name "Negev"; NET adds "arid" and "replenished" to explain the geography and the metaphor.
- v.5 — NET: "Those who shed tears as they plant will shout for joy when they reap the harvest" — More interpretive than KJV's "They that sow in tears shall reap in joy." NET adds "shed," "plant" (for sow), and "the harvest" to make the agricultural metaphor explicit.
- v.6 — KJV: "bearing precious seed" / ASV: "bearing seed for sowing" / WEB: "carrying seed for sowing" / NET: "carrying his bag of seed" — Different interpretations of Hebrew "meshek." KJV emphasizes value ("precious"); ASV/WEB emphasize purpose ("for sowing"); NET adds container ("bag").
- v.1 — ASV: "Jehovah" / WEB: "Yahweh" / NET: "the Lord" — The divine name appears three times in six verses.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Restoration feels like a dream (v.1). When God finally moves
   after long waiting, it's almost unbelievable. Trust that
   God-sized answers will come — even if they seem too good to
   be true.

2. Sow in tears (v.5). Your pain is not wasted if you're sowing.
   Tears are seed. Every faithful act done through suffering is
   investment in future harvest. Keep sowing, even when weeping.

3. "Shall doubtless come again with rejoicing" (v.6). The harvest
   is GUARANTEED. Not "might" — SHALL. Not "possibly" — DOUBTLESS.
   If you sow faithfully, you WILL reap joyfully. The only
   question is timing.

4. Turn your captivities into testimonies (vv.1-3). When God
   delivers, don't keep it quiet. Even the nations noticed
   Israel's restoration. Let your testimony be heard.
