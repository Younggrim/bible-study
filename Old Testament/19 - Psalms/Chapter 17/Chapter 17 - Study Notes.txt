Psalms - Chapter 17
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
An individual lament — David appeals to God's justice, professing his integrity under testing, and prays for deliverance from the wicked; he will be satisfied when he awakes with God's likeness.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Individual Lament / Prayer for Vindication
Attributed Author: David — "A Prayer of David"
Key Themes: Innocence before God, divine testing and approval,
enemies like lions, apple of God's eye, shadow of God's wings,
satisfaction in God's presence vs. worldly satisfaction

Historical Context: Labeled simply "A Prayer" — one of only five
psalms so designated (17, 86, 90, 102, 142). David protests his
innocence (vv.1-5) in language similar to Psalm 7, appeals for
protection using intimate imagery (apple of the eye, shadow of
wings), and contrasts two ultimate satisfactions: the worldly
person satisfied with "this life" (v.14) vs. David satisfied with
God's face at resurrection (v.15). The enemies are described as
predatory lions (v.12), suggesting a period when David was hunted.
The final verse (v.15) is one of the most remarkable statements of
afterlife hope in the Psalter.

Structure:
- Appeal Based on Innocence (vv.1-5)
- Petition for Protection (vv.6-9)
- Description of Enemies (vv.10-12)
- Prayer for Deliverance (vv.13-14)
- David's Ultimate Satisfaction (v.15)

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------

REFLECTION
------------------------------------
1. You are the apple of God's eye (v.8). The most instinctively
   protected part of the body is the pupil. God guards you with
   that same reflexive, immediate, total protection. You are not
   peripheral to Him — you are central.

2. Two satisfactions, two destinies (vv.14-15). The worldly person
   is full NOW — children, wealth, inheritance. David's
   satisfaction comes LATER — at awakening, in God's likeness.
   Which satisfaction are you pursuing? One ends at death; the
   other begins at resurrection.

3. God tests in the night (v.3). Your true character is revealed
   when no one is watching — "in the night seasons." What you do
   in private, what you think when alone, how you behave
   unsupervised — these are the divine tests.

4. "I shall be satisfied with thy likeness" (v.15). The ultimate
   human satisfaction is not comfort, achievement, or pleasure
   but LIKENESS TO GOD. We were made in His image; we will be
   restored to it fully. That restoration is what finally
   satisfies the human soul.

5. The shadow of His wings (v.8). In danger, run TO God, not FROM
   danger. The chick does not fight the hawk — it runs to its
   mother's wings. God's wings are both refuge and warmth. They
   protect AND comfort simultaneously.
