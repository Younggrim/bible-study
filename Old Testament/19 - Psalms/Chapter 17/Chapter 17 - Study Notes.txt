Psalms - Chapter 17
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
An individual lament — David appeals to God's justice, professing his integrity under testing, and prays for deliverance from the wicked; he will be satisfied when he awakes with God's likeness.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Individual Lament / Prayer for Vindication
Attributed Author: David — "A Prayer of David"
Key Themes: Innocence before God, divine testing and approval,
enemies like lions, apple of God's eye, shadow of God's wings,
satisfaction in God's presence vs. worldly satisfaction

Historical Context: Labeled simply "A Prayer" — one of only five
psalms so designated (17, 86, 90, 102, 142). David protests his
innocence (vv.1-5) in language similar to Psalm 7, appeals for
protection using intimate imagery (apple of the eye, shadow of
wings), and contrasts two ultimate satisfactions: the worldly
person satisfied with "this life" (v.14) vs. David satisfied with
God's face at resurrection (v.15). The enemies are described as
predatory lions (v.12), suggesting a period when David was hunted.
The final verse (v.15) is one of the most remarkable statements of
afterlife hope in the Psalter.

Structure:
- Appeal Based on Innocence (vv.1-5)
- Petition for Protection (vv.6-9)
- Description of Enemies (vv.10-12)
- Prayer for Deliverance (vv.13-14)
- David's Ultimate Satisfaction (v.15)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.8  — "Keep me as the apple of the eye, hide me under the shadow
        of thy wings" (KJV) — Two of Scripture's most intimate
        images of God's protection. "Apple of the eye" (literally
        "pupil-daughter of the eye") = that which is most precious
        and instinctively guarded. "Shadow of wings" = a mother
        bird sheltering her chicks.

v.14 — "From men of the world, which have their portion in this
        life" (KJV) — The worldly person's limitation: their
        portion is IN THIS LIFE only. They are full, satisfied,
        leave abundance to their children — but it all ends at
        death. Contrast v.15.

v.15 — "As for me, I will behold thy face in righteousness: I
        shall be satisfied, when I awake, with thy likeness."
        (KJV) — David's contrasting hope: not satisfaction in this
        life but satisfaction at AWAKENING — resurrection morning.
        Seeing God's face and bearing His likeness. This is one of
        the OT's clearest personal resurrection hopes.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.8   — Deuteronomy 32:10 — "He kept him as the apple of his eye."
         God's care for Israel in the wilderness.
v.8   — Ruth 2:12 — "Under whose wings thou art come to trust."
         Same protective wing imagery.
v.8   — Matthew 23:37 — "As a hen gathereth her chickens under her
         wings." Jesus uses the same image of protective love.
v.14  — Luke 12:19-20 — "Soul, thou hast much goods... Thou fool."
         The rich man whose portion was only in this life.
v.15  — 1 John 3:2 — "We shall be like him; for we shall see him
         as he is." NT fulfillment: seeing God, becoming like Him.
v.15  — Psalm 16:11 — "In thy presence is fulness of joy." Parallel
         hope of God's presence as ultimate satisfaction.
v.15  — 2 Corinthians 3:18 — "Changed into the same image from
         glory to glory." Transformation into God's likeness.

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1 — KJV: "Hear the right, O LORD" / ESV: "Hear a just cause, O
      LORD" — Hebrew "tsedeq" = righteousness/just cause. David
      presents his case as legally sound — he has a just cause to
      bring before the divine court.

v.3 — KJV: "thou hast proved mine heart; thou hast visited me in
      the night" / ESV: "You have tried my heart, you have visited
      me by night" — God's testing happens "in the night" — in
      hidden moments when no one else sees. David passes the
      nighttime test.

v.10 — KJV: "They are inclosed in their own fat" / ESV: "They
       close their hearts to pity" — Hebrew "chelbamo sagru" =
       "they have closed their fat." KJV reads anatomically (fat
       closes around them = self-indulgent, insensitive). ESV
       interprets: they have shut their compassion. The image is
       a hardened, insensitive heart.

v.15 — KJV: "when I awake" / ESV: "when I awake" — This could
       mean awakening from sleep (seeing God in the morning) or
       awakening from death (resurrection). The contrast with v.14
       (whose portion is THIS life) strongly supports the
       resurrection reading: David's satisfaction comes BEYOND
       this life.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. You are the apple of God's eye (v.8). The most instinctively
   protected part of the body is the pupil. God guards you with
   that same reflexive, immediate, total protection. You are not
   peripheral to Him — you are central.

2. Two satisfactions, two destinies (vv.14-15). The worldly person
   is full NOW — children, wealth, inheritance. David's
   satisfaction comes LATER — at awakening, in God's likeness.
   Which satisfaction are you pursuing? One ends at death; the
   other begins at resurrection.

3. God tests in the night (v.3). Your true character is revealed
   when no one is watching — "in the night seasons." What you do
   in private, what you think when alone, how you behave
   unsupervised — these are the divine tests.

4. "I shall be satisfied with thy likeness" (v.15). The ultimate
   human satisfaction is not comfort, achievement, or pleasure
   but LIKENESS TO GOD. We were made in His image; we will be
   restored to it fully. That restoration is what finally
   satisfies the human soul.

5. The shadow of His wings (v.8). In danger, run TO God, not FROM
   danger. The chick does not fight the hawk — it runs to its
   mother's wings. God's wings are both refuge and warmth. They
   protect AND comfort simultaneously.
