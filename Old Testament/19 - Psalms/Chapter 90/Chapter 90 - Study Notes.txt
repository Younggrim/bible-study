Psalms - Chapter 90
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A psalm of Moses opening Book IV — 'LORD, thou hast been our dwelling place in all generations'; God is eternal, man is dust; 'teach us to number our days, that we may apply our hearts unto wisdom.'

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Community Lament / Wisdom Psalm
Attributed Author: Moses (the only psalm attributed to Moses)
Key Themes: God's eternity vs. human brevity, the brevity of life,
sin and divine wrath, numbering our days, prayer for God's mercy
and the establishment of human work

Historical Context: Psalm 90 opens Book IV of the Psalter (90-106)
and is the oldest psalm in the collection — attributed to Moses,
making it potentially 3400+ years old. As the man who spent 40
years watching a generation die in the wilderness (Numbers
14:29-35), Moses had unparalleled experience with human mortality.
The psalm's meditation on the brevity of life (70-80 years, v.10)
and God's wrath consuming sinful humanity may directly reflect the
wilderness generation's slow death. Yet it is not despairing —
it moves from sober recognition of mortality to prayer for wisdom,
mercy, gladness, and the establishment of human work. The phrase
"from everlasting to everlasting, thou art God" (v.2) is one of
Scripture's grandest theological declarations.

Structure:
- God as Eternal Dwelling Place (vv.1-2)
- Human Mortality Under God's Authority (vv.3-6)
- Sin and Divine Wrath (vv.7-11)
- Prayer for Wisdom (v.12)
- Prayer for Mercy, Gladness, and Purpose (vv.13-17)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "Before the mountains were brought forth, or ever thou
        hadst formed the earth and the world, even from everlasting
        to everlasting, thou art God." (KJV) — God's eternality
        stated absolutely. Before creation, beyond creation — He IS.
        "From everlasting to everlasting" — no beginning, no end.
        Mountains are ancient; God predates them.

v.10 — "The days of our years are threescore years and ten; and if
        by reason of strength they be fourscore years, yet is their
        strength labour and sorrow; for it is soon cut off, and we
        fly away." (KJV) — The most famous statement on human
        lifespan in Scripture. 70-80 years — even the strong years
        are marked by toil and sorrow. And then: "we fly away."
        Life is a vapor.

v.12 — "So teach us to number our days, that we may apply our
        hearts unto wisdom." (KJV) — The psalm's supreme prayer.
        NUMBERING days = recognizing their limit. The result:
        wisdom of heart, not head. Knowing life is brief produces
        urgency, priority, and perspective.

v.17 — "And let the beauty of the LORD our God be upon us: and
        establish thou the work of our hands upon us; yea, the
        work of our hands establish thou it." (KJV) — The final
        prayer: that brief human life will produce LASTING work.
        We are temporary; but by God's grace, our work can outlast
        us. "Establish" — make permanent what is impermanent.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.1   — Deuteronomy 33:27 — "The eternal God is thy refuge, and
         underneath are the everlasting arms." Moses' final words.
v.2   — 2 Peter 3:8 — "One day is with the Lord as a thousand
         years, and a thousand years as one day." Echoing v.4.
v.3   — Genesis 3:19 — "Dust thou art, and unto dust shalt thou
         return." Moses connects to his own writing.
v.4   — 2 Peter 3:8 — Peter directly cites this verse regarding
         God's perspective on time.
v.10  — James 4:14 — "For what is your life? It is even a vapour,
         that appeareth for a little time, and then vanisheth away."
v.12  — Ephesians 5:15-16 — "Redeeming the time, because the days
         are evil." The NT application of numbering days.
v.17  — 1 Corinthians 15:58 — "Your labour is not in vain in the
         Lord." The NT promise that work established by God lasts.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.3 — KJV: "Thou turnest man to destruction" / ESV: "You return
      man to dust" — Hebrew "dakka" = dust, crushed substance. ESV
      is more literal and connects directly to Genesis 3:19. God
      returns humanity to its origin — dust.

v.9 — KJV: "we spend our years as a tale that is told" / ESV: "we
      bring our years to an end like a sigh" — Hebrew "hegeh" =
      a moan, a murmur, a sigh. ESV's "sigh" captures the
      brevity and sadness better. Life ends with a whisper, not
      a bang.

v.10 — KJV: "threescore years and ten" / ESV: "seventy" — Same
       number, different expression. KJV preserves the archaic
       beauty; ESV is plain but clear. The observation: even in
       Moses' time, human lifespan was recognizably brief.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.1 — NET: "you have been our protector through all generations" — renders
  "ma'on" (dwelling place) as "protector," interpreting the shelter-metaphor.
  KJV/ESV/ASV/WEB all retain "dwelling place" — God as HOME, not just shield.
  The Hebrew evokes a permanent residence, not temporary help.
- v.2 — NET: "you were the eternal God" — simplified from the majestic "from
  everlasting to everlasting, thou art God" (KJV/ASV/WEB). NET loses the
  double-infinity ("everlasting to everlasting") that emphasizes God's
  existence extending infinitely in both directions — before time and beyond it.
- v.3 — NET: "You make mankind return to the dust" — "dust" connects to
  Genesis 3:19. WEB: "You turn man to destruction" — matches KJV with Hebrew
  "dakka." ASV: "Thou turnest man to destruction" — same as KJV.
- v.5 — NET: "You bring their lives to an end and they 'fall asleep'" —
  "fall asleep" is a death euphemism. KJV/ASV: "Thou carriest them away as
  with a flood; they are as a sleep" — the flood imagery is vivid: life swept
  away suddenly. WEB: "You sweep them away as they sleep" — combines both
  images (sweeping away + sleep).
- v.9 — NET: "the years of our lives pass quickly, like a sigh" — matches
  ESV. WEB/ASV: "We bring our years to an end as a sigh" — same rendering.
  KJV stands alone with "a tale that is told" — a traditional but less
  accurate rendering of Hebrew "hegeh" (murmur/sigh). The image is not a
  long tale but a brief exhale.
- v.10 — NET: "But even one's best years are marred by trouble and
  oppression. Yes, they pass quickly and we fly away" — "marred by trouble"
  interprets "their pride/strength is labour and sorrow" (KJV). WEB: "their
  pride is but labor and sorrow, for it passes quickly, and we fly away" —
  preserves "pride" (Hebrew "rohab" = strength/pride). The pathos: even the
  best years are heavy with toil.
- v.12 — NET: "So teach us to consider our mortality, so that we might live
  wisely" — highly interpretive paraphrase of the famous "teach us to number
  our days, that we may apply our hearts unto wisdom" (KJV). ASV: "teach us
  to number our days, That we may get us a heart of wisdom." WEB: "teach us
  to count our days, that we may gain a heart of wisdom." NET's "consider our
  mortality" explains what "numbering days" means, but loses the active,
  mathematical metaphor (counting each day as precious and finite).
- v.13 — WEB: "Relent, Yahweh! How long? Have compassion on your servants!"
  — "Relent" captures Hebrew "nacham" (to be sorry, change course) well.
  ASV: "Return, O Jehovah; how long? And let it repent thee" — divine name
  explicit. NET: "Turn back toward us, O Lord. How long must this suffering
  last?" — adds "suffering" (interpretive).
- v.14 — NET: "Satisfy us in the morning with your loyal love" — "loyal love"
  for "chesed." ASV: "lovingkindness." WEB: "loving kindness." KJV: "mercy."
  Each captures a facet of the untranslatable "chesed."
- v.17 — NET: "May our Sovereign God extend his favor to us. Make our
  endeavors successful" — "Sovereign God" for "Lord our God"; "endeavors" for
  "work of our hands." KJV: "the beauty of the LORD our God be upon us" —
  Hebrew "no'am" = pleasantness, beauty, favor. WEB/ASV: "the favor of the
  Lord our God" — middle ground between KJV's "beauty" and NET's "favor."

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. God is your dwelling place across generations (v.1). Before you
   were born, God was home. After you die, God remains. Your true
   address is not geographical but theological — God Himself is
   where you live.

2. Life is breathtakingly brief (v.10). 70-80 years. Even the
   strong ones are toil. This is not morbid but urgent — if life
   is short, what will you do with it? Brevity produces focus.

3. Number your days (v.12). The one who knows life's limit lives
   with wisdom. Count backward from the end. What matters? What
   doesn't? Let mortality teach you priority.

4. Ask God to establish your work (v.17). Human effort without
   God is temporary. But work committed to God, established by
   God, can outlast the worker. Pray this daily: "God, make my
   work last beyond my life."

5. Even Moses needed morning mercy (v.14). "Satisfy us EARLY with
   thy mercy." Every day begins with a need for fresh grace. No
   one outgrows the need for daily mercy — not even the man who
   spoke with God face to face.
