Psalms - Chapter 90
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A psalm of Moses opening Book IV — 'LORD, thou hast been our dwelling place in all generations'; God is eternal, man is dust; 'teach us to number our days, that we may apply our hearts unto wisdom.'

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Community Lament / Wisdom Psalm
Attributed Author: Moses (the only psalm attributed to Moses)
Key Themes: God's eternity vs. human brevity, the brevity of life,
sin and divine wrath, numbering our days, prayer for God's mercy
and the establishment of human work

Historical Context: Psalm 90 opens Book IV of the Psalter (90-106)
and is the oldest psalm in the collection — attributed to Moses,
making it potentially 3400+ years old. As the man who spent 40
years watching a generation die in the wilderness (Numbers
14:29-35), Moses had unparalleled experience with human mortality.
The psalm's meditation on the brevity of life (70-80 years, v.10)
and God's wrath consuming sinful humanity may directly reflect the
wilderness generation's slow death. Yet it is not despairing —
it moves from sober recognition of mortality to prayer for wisdom,
mercy, gladness, and the establishment of human work. The phrase
"from everlasting to everlasting, thou art God" (v.2) is one of
Scripture's grandest theological declarations.

Structure:
- God as Eternal Dwelling Place (vv.1-2)
- Human Mortality Under God's Authority (vv.3-6)
- Sin and Divine Wrath (vv.7-11)
- Prayer for Wisdom (v.12)
- Prayer for Mercy, Gladness, and Purpose (vv.13-17)

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------

REFLECTION
------------------------------------
1. God is your dwelling place across generations (v.1). Before you
   were born, God was home. After you die, God remains. Your true
   address is not geographical but theological — God Himself is
   where you live.

2. Life is breathtakingly brief (v.10). 70-80 years. Even the
   strong ones are toil. This is not morbid but urgent — if life
   is short, what will you do with it? Brevity produces focus.

3. Number your days (v.12). The one who knows life's limit lives
   with wisdom. Count backward from the end. What matters? What
   doesn't? Let mortality teach you priority.

4. Ask God to establish your work (v.17). Human effort without
   God is temporary. But work committed to God, established by
   God, can outlast the worker. Pray this daily: "God, make my
   work last beyond my life."

5. Even Moses needed morning mercy (v.14). "Satisfy us EARLY with
   thy mercy." Every day begins with a need for fresh grace. No
   one outgrows the need for daily mercy — not even the man who
   spoke with God face to face.
