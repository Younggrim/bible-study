Psalms - Chapter 140
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A lament — David prays for deliverance from violent men who sharpen their tongues like serpents; he trusts God will maintain the cause of the afflicted and let the wicked fall into their own pits.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Individual Lament / Imprecatory Psalm
Attributed Author: David (superscription: "To the chief Musician,
A Psalm of David")
Key Themes: Protection from violent and deceitful enemies, the
tongue as a weapon, God as defender of the afflicted, imprecation
against the wicked, confidence in God's justice

Historical Context: Psalm 140 begins a cluster of five Davidic
laments (140-144) that share common themes: enemies, danger,
prayer for protection, and confidence in God's deliverance. These
may reflect David's years fleeing Saul or later court intrigues.
The enemies here are specifically verbal: tongues sharp as
serpents, lips hiding adder's poison (v.3). The psalm moves from
desperate plea through imprecation to confident declaration that
God will maintain the cause of the afflicted.

Structure:
- Plea for Deliverance from Violent Men (vv.1-3)
- Prayer for Protection from Traps (vv.4-5)
- Appeal to God as Strength (vv.6-7)
- Imprecation on the Wicked (vv.8-11)
- Confidence in God's Justice (vv.12-13)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.3  — "They have sharpened their tongues like a serpent; adders'
        poison is under their lips." (KJV) — Quoted by Paul in
        Romans 3:13 to prove universal sinfulness. The tongue is
        the weapon — sharp, poisonous, deadly.

v.7  — "O GOD the Lord, the strength of my salvation, thou hast
        covered my head in the day of battle." (KJV) — God as
        helmet — covering the head, the most vulnerable part.
        In the day of battle, God's protection is specific and
        personal.

v.12 — "I know that the LORD will maintain the cause of the
        afflicted, and the right of the poor." (KJV) — Confident
        knowledge: God WILL act for the afflicted. Not hope, not
        wish — "I KNOW."

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.3   — Romans 3:13 — Paul quotes directly: "The poison of asps
         is under their lips."
v.5   — Psalm 141:9 — "Keep me from the snares which they have
         laid for me."
v.7   — Ephesians 6:17 — "Take the helmet of salvation."
v.12  — Psalm 9:18 — "The needy shall not alway be forgotten."

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.1 — ASV: "Jehovah" / WEB: "Yahweh" / NET: "O Lord" — Standard divine name pattern. The name appears seven times in this psalm.
- v.3 — NET: "Their tongues wound like a serpent; a viper's venom is behind their lips" — Paraphrases KJV's "They have sharpened their tongues like a serpent; adders' poison is under their lips." NET adds "wound" and changes "under" to "behind," making the danger more immediate.
- v.4 — KJV: "who have purposed to overthrow my goings" / ASV: "to thrust aside my steps" / WEB: "who have determined to trip my feet" / NET: "who plan to knock me over" — Four renderings of the same concept: KJV=overthrow goings; ASV=thrust aside steps; WEB=trip feet; NET=knock over. NET is most colloquial.
- v.7 — KJV: "O GOD the Lord, the strength of my salvation" / ASV: "O Jehovah the Lord" / WEB: "Yahweh, the Lord" / NET: "O Sovereign Lord, my strong deliverer" — NET uses "Sovereign Lord" for "Adonai YHWH" (the doubled divine title) and paraphrases "strength of salvation" as "strong deliverer."
- v.11 — KJV: "Let not an evil speaker be established in the earth" / NET: "A slanderer will not endure on the earth" — NET specifies "slanderer" (one type of evil speaker) and changes petition ("Let not") to declaration ("will not").
- v.12 — KJV: "the right of the poor" / ASV: "justice for the needy" / NET: "vindicates the poor" — NET uses "vindicates" (active verb) rather than "maintains the right/justice" (noun phrase), strengthening God's advocate role.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Verbal attacks are as real as physical ones (v.3). Slander,
   gossip, and lies are poisonous weapons. If you're under verbal
   attack, bring it to God — He takes words seriously.

2. God covers your head in battle (v.7). The most vulnerable part
   of you is protected. God doesn't leave you exposed where it
   matters most.

3. God maintains the cause of the afflicted (v.12). If you're
   afflicted and poor, God is your advocate. He will not forget
   your case. Justice will come.
