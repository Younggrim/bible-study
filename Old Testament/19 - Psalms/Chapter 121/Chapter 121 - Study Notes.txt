Psalms - Chapter 121
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A Song of Ascents (pilgrimage psalm of trust) — 'I will lift up mine eyes unto the hills; from whence cometh my help? My help cometh from the LORD'; He who keeps Israel neither slumbers nor sleeps.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Song of Ascents / Psalm of Trust
Attributed Author: Anonymous (superscription: "A Song of
degrees/ascents")
Key Themes: God as keeper/protector, divine watchfulness that
never sleeps, help from the Creator, protection from all evil,
preservation in all circumstances, total security in God

Historical Context: Psalm 121 is the second Song of Ascents and
one of the most beloved psalms in all of Scripture. It was sung
by pilgrims approaching Jerusalem, looking up at the hills ahead.
The "hills" likely refer to the mountains surrounding Jerusalem
(or possibly the hills where pagan shrines were located — in which
case the answer is: help comes NOT from the hills/shrines but from
the LORD). The psalm uses the word "keep/preserve" (Hebrew
"shamar") six times in eight verses — the most concentrated
expression of divine protection in the Psalter. It likely
functioned as a blessing pronounced by a priest over departing
pilgrims — a divine guarantee for the journey home.

Structure:
- The Pilgrim's Question (v.1)
- The Pilgrim's Answer (v.2)
- The Keeper Who Never Sleeps (vv.3-4)
- Complete Protection (vv.5-6)
- Preservation Always (vv.7-8)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1-2 — "I will lift up mine eyes unto the hills, from whence
        cometh my help. My help cometh from the LORD, which made
        heaven and earth." (KJV) — The question and answer of
        faith. Where does help come from? Not the hills themselves
        but the God who MADE them. If He made heaven and earth, He
        can certainly help you.

v.3-4 — "He will not suffer thy foot to be moved: he that keepeth
        thee will not slumber. Behold, he that keepeth Israel
        shall neither slumber nor sleep." (KJV) — Unlike pagan
        gods who might sleep (cf. 1 Kings 18:27 — Elijah mocking
        Baal), the true God NEVER sleeps. Not slumber (light
        dozing), not sleep (deep rest) — NEVER. His watchfulness
        is perpetual, 24/7, without interruption.

v.7  — "The LORD shall preserve thee from all evil: he shall
        preserve thy soul." (KJV) — From ALL evil. Not some, not
        most — ALL. This doesn't promise no suffering, but that
        no evil ultimately prevails. And specifically: He preserves
        your SOUL — the deepest part of you is always safe.

v.8  — "The LORD shall preserve thy going out and thy coming in
        from this time forth, and even for evermore." (KJV) — Every
        departure and return. Every venture and homecoming. From
        NOW until FOREVER. No gaps in coverage, no expiration date
        on protection.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.1   — Psalm 87:1 — "His foundation is in the holy mountains."
v.2   — Psalm 124:8 — "Our help is in the name of the LORD, who
         made heaven and earth."

         comest in, and blessed shalt thou be when thou goest out."

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1 — The Hebrew can be read as a statement ("from whence my help
      comes") or a question ("from where does my help come?"). If
      a question, it heightens the drama: the pilgrim asks, then
      answers. ESV reads it as a question: "from where does my
      help come?"

v.3 — "keeper" (shamar) = to guard, watch over, keep safe. Used
      six times in this psalm (vv.3, 4, 5, 7[x2], 8). Massive
      emphasis on God's guardian role. He is THE keeper.

v.6 — "sun shall not smite thee by day, nor the moon by night" —
      Protection from heatstroke by day and harmful night exposure.
      Also possibly spiritual: protection from all celestial
      influences, both seen and unseen dangers.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.1 — NET: "I look up toward the hills. From where does my help come?" —
  renders as present tense ("I look up") and makes the question explicit. KJV
  reads as a statement: "from whence cometh my help" (no question mark in the
  original 1611). ASV: "I will lift up mine eyes unto the mountains: From
  whence shall my help come?" — both future tense and question. WEB: "Where
  does my help come from?" — simplest question form.
- v.2 — NET: "the Creator of heaven and earth" — adds "Creator" (interpretive;
  Hebrew simply says "maker"). Makes the creation theology more explicit.
  ASV: "Jehovah, Who made heaven and earth." WEB: "Yahweh, who made heaven
  and earth" — divine name explicit in both.
- v.3 — NET: "May he not allow your foot to slip. May your Protector not
  sleep" — renders as wishes/prayers (jussive mood), suggesting the psalmist
  is praying these things, not just stating them. KJV/ASV/WEB use declarative
  statements: "He will not suffer thy foot to be moved." NET also capitalizes
  "Protector" as a divine title.
- v.5 — ASV: "Jehovah is thy keeper: Jehovah is thy shade upon thy right
  hand" — the double "Jehovah" emphasizes personal covenant keeping. WEB
  similarly: "Yahweh is your keeper. Yahweh is your shade on your right hand."
  NET: "The Lord is your protector; the Lord is the shade at your right hand"
  — "protector" replaces "keeper" throughout, losing the Hebrew word-play
  where "shamar" (keep/guard) is repeated six times.
- v.7 — NET: "The Lord will protect you from all harm; he will protect your
  life" — "protect" (used consistently) vs. KJV: "preserve" and ASV/WEB:
  "keep." WEB: "Yahweh will keep you from all evil. He will keep your soul"
  — "keep your soul" (Hebrew "shamar et-nafshekha") means guarding your very
  life/being, not just physical safety.
- v.8 — NET: "The Lord will protect you in all you do, now and forevermore" —
  simplifies "thy going out and thy coming in" (KJV) to "all you do." The
  Hebrew "going out and coming in" is a merism — encompassing all of life's
  activities by naming its extremes (departure and return, beginning and end).
  ASV/WEB preserve this: "going out and coming in." The temporal scope is
  staggering: "from this time forth and for evermore" — protection without
  end.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Lift your eyes (v.1). When overwhelmed, look UP. Not at the
   problem, not at the obstacle — up to the hills where God
   dwells. Direction of gaze determines direction of hope.

2. Your help is from the MAKER (v.2). The One who made heaven
   and earth is your helper. He is not inadequate to your
   problem. Whatever you face, He made something bigger.

3. He never sleeps (vv.3-4). When you're awake at 3 AM with
   anxiety, God is awake too — not anxious but watching, keeping,
   guarding. You can sleep BECAUSE He doesn't.

4. All evil, all time (vv.7-8). Every exit and entrance. Every
   journey and return. From now until forever. There is no gap
   in God's protective care. No situation where He is absent.

5. This psalm is the ultimate travel blessing. Whether you're
   walking to Jerusalem or walking through life — the Keeper of
   Israel watches over your every step.
