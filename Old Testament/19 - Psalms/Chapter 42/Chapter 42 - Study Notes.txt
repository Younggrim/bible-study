Psalms - Chapter 42
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A lament of the Sons of Korah — 'As the hart panteth after the water brooks, so panteth my soul after thee, O God'; the psalmist, exiled from the temple, combats despair with the refrain: 'Why art thou cast down, O my soul? Hope thou in God.'

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Individual Lament
Attributed Author: Sons of Korah (To the chief Musician, Maschil,
for the sons of Korah)
Key Themes: Spiritual thirst, longing for God's presence, despair
and hope, memory of worship, self-counsel

Historical Context: Psalm 42 opens Book II of the Psalter (Psalms
42-72) and is the first psalm attributed to the Sons of Korah — a
Levitical family of temple musicians descended from the rebel Korah
(Numbers 16), whose sons were spared by God's grace. This psalm is
universally recognized as paired with Psalm 43, likely originally
a single composition sharing the same refrain (42:5, 42:11, 43:5).

The psalmist is geographically distant from the temple, apparently
in the region of the Jordan headwaters, near Mount Hermon and
"the hill Mizar" (v. 6) — far north of Jerusalem. He remembers
leading worship processions to the house of God and now weeps in
exile, taunted by enemies who mock, "Where is thy God?" The
specific mention of geographic locations suggests a historical
exile — possibly during David's flight from Absalom (2 Samuel
15-17) or during the northern exile of Levitical worshipers.

This psalm is among the most emotionally transparent in Scripture.
It models how a believer wrestles with depression while refusing to
abandon faith. The psalmist speaks to his own soul, commanding it
to hope in God even when feelings fail. The imagery of the
thirsting deer has become one of the most beloved metaphors in
Christian worship and hymnody.

Structure:
- Desperate Longing for God (vv. 1-2)
- Tears and Taunting (v. 3)
- Memory of Past Worship (v. 4)
- First Refrain: Self-Counsel to Hope (v. 5)
- Cast Down but Remembering God (vv. 6-7)
- God's Lovingkindness and Prayer (v. 8)
- Complaint: Why Have You Forgotten Me? (vv. 9-10)
- Second Refrain: Self-Counsel to Hope (v. 11)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
Verse 1 — "As the hart panteth after the water brooks, so panteth
my soul after thee, O God."
One of Scripture's most beautiful metaphors for spiritual desire.
The hart (deer) in a dry land is desperate, not casually thirsty.
This is existential longing — the soul's very survival depends on
finding God. The image suggests that spiritual dryness, rightly
understood, reveals how essential God is to us.

Verse 5 — "Why art thou cast down, O my soul? and why art thou
disquieted in me? hope thou in God: for I shall yet praise him for
the help of his countenance."
The pivotal refrain repeated three times (42:5, 42:11, 43:5). The
psalmist preaches to himself rather than listening to his feelings.
This is a model for fighting despair: acknowledging the downcast
state honestly, then commanding the soul toward hope based on God's
character, not present circumstances.

Verse 7 — "Deep calleth unto deep at the noise of thy waterspouts:
all thy waves and thy billows are gone over me."
A stunning image of overwhelming sorrow — trouble upon trouble,
like ocean depths calling to each other as waves crash over him.
Yet even this watery chaos belongs to God ("thy waves and thy
billows"), suggesting that even suffering is under divine
sovereignty.

Verse 8 — "Yet the LORD will command his lovingkindness in the
daytime, and in the night his song shall be with me, and my prayer
unto the God of my life."
In the midst of despair, the psalmist affirms that God actively
commands (dispatches) His covenant love. Even the darkest night
cannot silence the song God gives. "The God of my life" — not
merely a theological concept but the source of all vitality.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v. 1 — Psalm 63:1 — "O God, thou art my God; early will I seek
thee: my soul thirsteth for thee, my flesh longeth for thee in a
dry and thirsty land." David's similar cry of spiritual thirst.

v. 1 — John 7:37 — "If any man thirst, let him come unto me, and
drink." Jesus offers Himself as the fulfillment of spiritual
thirst.

v. 3 — Psalm 80:5 — "Thou feedest them with the bread of tears;
and givest them tears to drink in great measure." Tears as
sustenance during sorrow.

v. 5 — Psalm 43:5 — The identical refrain, confirming these psalms
were composed as one unit.

v. 7 — Jonah 2:3 — "All thy billows and thy waves passed over me."
Jonah uses nearly identical language from the belly of the great
fish — the experience of being overwhelmed by God's waves.

v. 7 — Psalm 69:1-2 — "Save me, O God; for the waters are come in
unto my soul." The imagery of drowning in sorrow recurs throughout
the Psalms.

v. 8 — Psalm 32:7 — "Thou shalt compass me about with songs of
deliverance." God surrounds the afflicted with His song even in
trouble.

v. 10 — Psalm 22:1 — "My God, my God, why hast thou forsaken me?"
The pattern of crying out to God in perceived abandonment — most
fully expressed by Christ on the cross.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v. 1 — KJV "hart" vs. ESV "deer" — The Hebrew "ayyal" (אַיָּל)
refers specifically to a male deer or stag. KJV's archaic "hart"
preserves this masculine specificity, while ESV uses the more
general modern term. The intensity of panting (arag) suggests the
animal is in extremity.

v. 4 — KJV "I had gone with the multitude" vs. ESV "how I would go
with the throng" — The Hebrew is difficult here. KJV renders it as
a straightforward past tense memory. ESV captures the habitual
aspect ("I would go") suggesting a repeated pattern of joyful
worship now lost.

v. 5 — KJV "the help of his countenance" vs. ESV "my salvation" —
The Hebrew is debated. KJV follows the Masoretic text more
literally: "the salvations of his face/countenance." ESV simplifies
for readability. The Hebrew connects salvation directly to seeing
God's face — being in His presence is itself the deliverance.

v. 7 — KJV "waterspouts" vs. ESV "waterfalls" — The Hebrew
"tsinnor" is rare and debated. It may refer to waterfalls,
cataracts, water channels, or conduits. Given the northern
geographic setting near Hermon's streams, "waterfalls" (ESV) fits
the landscape, while "waterspouts" (KJV) evokes more violent water
imagery.

v. 11 — KJV "the health of my countenance" vs. ESV "my salvation
and my God" — KJV's rendering captures the Hebrew idea that God's
saving presence literally shows on one's face — He is the health
(healing, wholeness) that restores the downcast countenance.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.1 — NET: "As a deer longs for streams of water, so I long for you, O
  God!" — "longs" instead of "panteth" (KJV/ASV). Loses the physical
  desperation imagery of panting but is more immediately understood. WEB: "As
  the deer pants for the water brooks, so my soul pants after you" — modern
  language with "pants" retained for the physical urgency. ASV: "As the hart
  panteth after the water brooks" — retains "hart" (male deer) with KJV.
- v.3 — NET: "I cannot eat; I weep day and night" — interprets "tears have
  been my food" (KJV/ASV/WEB) as inability to eat. KJV: "My tears have been
  my meat day and night" — "meat" in 1611 English meant food generally. The
  Hebrew metaphor: tears are so constant they replace food.
- v.5 — NET: "Why are you depressed, O my soul? Why are you upset? Wait for
  God!" — "depressed" and "upset" are modern psychological terms. KJV/ASV:
  "cast down...disquieted"; WEB: "in despair...disturbed"; ESV: "cast down...
  in turmoil." NET also renders "hope" as "Wait" — the Hebrew "yachal" means
  both to wait and to hope, combining patience with expectation.
- v.5 — NET: "his saving intervention" — where KJV has "the help of his
  countenance" and ASV has "the help of his countenance." NET interprets
  salvation as God actively intervening rather than simply being present.
  WEB: "the saving help of his presence" — combines intervention with
  presence.
- v.7 — NET: "One deep stream calls out to another at the sound of your
  waterfalls; all your billows and waves overwhelm me" — "overwhelm" captures
  the emotional force better than "gone over me" (KJV/ASV). WEB: "Deep calls
  to deep at the noise of your waterfalls. All your waves and your billows
  have swept over me" — "swept over" is vivid. The famous "Deep calleth unto
  deep" (KJV/ASV) is one of the most poetic lines in the Psalter.
- v.8 — ASV: "Jehovah will command his lovingkindness in the day-time" / WEB:
  "Yahweh will command his loving kindness in the daytime" — the divine name
  explicit. NET: "By day the Lord decrees his loyal love" — "decrees" makes
  God's love an authoritative command, not mere sentiment. "Loyal love" for
  "chesed" captures the covenant dimension.
- v.9 — NET: "I will pray to God, my high ridge" — renders Hebrew "sela"
  (rock) as "high ridge," emphasizing the elevated fortress idea. KJV/ASV/WEB:
  "God my rock." NET also: "Why do you ignore me?" — sharper than "Why hast
  thou forgotten me?" (KJV/ASV/WEB), making the accusation more pointed.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Spiritual thirst is not a sign of failure but of life. Only
   living souls thirst. The psalmist's desperate longing for God
   reveals a heart that knows what it needs. When we feel spiritual
   dryness, it may be evidence of genuine faith recognizing its
   need.

2. Preaching to yourself is a spiritual discipline. The refrain
   teaches us to speak truth to our own souls rather than passively
   listening to our emotions. When cast down, we can ask "Why?"
   and then command ourselves toward hope — not denying the pain
   but redirecting the soul toward God's faithfulness.

3. Remembering past faithfulness sustains present faith. The
   psalmist recalls leading worship processions (v. 4) and uses
   those memories as anchors. Keeping a record of God's past
   dealings with us provides fuel for hope in present darkness.

4. Tears and faith coexist. The psalmist weeps day and night (v. 3)
   while simultaneously affirming God's lovingkindness (v. 8).
   Biblical faith does not suppress grief — it grieves honestly
   while holding to God's character.

5. Community matters. Part of the psalmist's anguish is isolation
   from corporate worship (v. 4). We were not designed to walk
   through spiritual valleys alone. The absence of gathered worship
   deepens spiritual pain; its restoration brings healing.
