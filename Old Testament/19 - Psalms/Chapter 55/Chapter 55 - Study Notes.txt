Psalms - Chapter 55
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A lament over betrayal — David wishes for wings like a dove to fly away; the pain is sharpest because the betrayer is his close companion; he casts his burden on the LORD, who will sustain him.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Individual Lament Psalm
Attributed Author: David
Key Themes: Betrayal by a close friend, desire to escape, the
pain of broken trust, casting burdens on the LORD, God's
faithfulness amid human treachery

Historical Context: This psalm likely arises from Absalom's
rebellion and particularly the betrayal of Ahithophel, David's
trusted counselor who defected to Absalom (2 Samuel 15-17). The
description of the betrayer as "a man mine equal, my guide, and
mine acquaintance" (v.13) with whom David "took sweet counsel
together" (v.14) fits Ahithophel perfectly. The psalm captures
the unique agony of being betrayed not by an enemy (which could
be endured) but by an intimate friend. The city filled with
violence and strife (vv.9-11) may describe Jerusalem under
Absalom's corrupt influence. This is one of the most emotionally
raw psalms, moving from terror to flight-fantasy to imprecation
to trust.

Structure:
- Desperate Cry for Help (vv.1-3)
- Terror and Desire to Flee (vv.4-8)
- Prayer Against Enemies in the City (vv.9-11)
- The Pain of a Friend's Betrayal (vv.12-14)
- Imprecation Against the Wicked (v.15)
- Confidence in God's Deliverance (vv.16-19)
- Description of the Betrayer (vv.20-21)
- Exhortation to Trust God (vv.22-23)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.6  — "And I said, Oh that I had wings like a dove! for then
        would I fly away, and be at rest." (KJV) — One of the
        most human moments in all the Psalms. David the warrior-
        king wishes he could simply escape. The dove represents
        peaceful flight — not aggressive departure but gentle
        withdrawal. Even the strongest believers sometimes wish
        they could just fly away from their problems.

v.12-13 — "For it was not an enemy that reproached me; then I
        could have borne it... But it was thou, a man mine equal,
        my guide, and mine acquaintance." (KJV) — The deepest
        wounds come from the closest people. Enemy opposition is
        expected and manageable. Friend betrayal is devastating
        because it violates trust, intimacy, and shared history.

v.21 — "The words of his mouth were smoother than butter, but war
        was in his heart: his words were softer than oil, yet were
        they drawn swords." (KJV) — The anatomy of betrayal:
        smooth words concealing violent intentions. The contrast
        between external speech and internal reality is the
        essence of treachery.

v.22 — "Cast thy burden upon the LORD, and he shall sustain thee:
        he shall never suffer the righteous to be moved." (KJV) —
        The great command and promise of this psalm. "Cast"
        (hashlekh) = throw, hurl. Not gently set down but
        aggressively throw your burden onto God. He can bear what
        you cannot.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.6   — Jeremiah 9:2 — "Oh that I had in the wilderness a lodging
         place of wayfaring men; that I might leave my people."
         Jeremiah echoes the same desire to flee.
v.12-14 — 2 Samuel 15:12,31 — Ahithophel's betrayal of David,
         the likely historical referent.
v.13  — Matthew 26:50 — Jesus to Judas: "Friend, wherefore art
         thou come?" Jesus experienced this same betrayal.
v.15  — Numbers 16:31-33 — Korah's company going down alive into
         the pit — the precedent for David's imprecation.
v.17  — Daniel 6:10 — Daniel praying three times daily, the same
         pattern David describes.
v.22  — 1 Peter 5:7 — "Casting all your care upon him; for he
         careth for you." Peter quotes/echoes this verse directly.
v.22  — Matthew 11:28 — "Come unto me, all ye that labour and are
         heavy laden, and I will give you rest."

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.2 — KJV: "I mourn in my complaint, and make a noise" / ESV: "I
      am restless in my complaint and I moan" — Hebrew "arid" =
      restless, tossing about. The image is of someone who cannot
      be still because of anxiety. ESV's "restless" captures this
      better than KJV's "mourn."

v.15 — KJV: "let them go down quick into hell" / ESV: "let them
       go down to Sheol alive" — "Hell" (KJV) is misleading; the
       Hebrew is "Sheol" = the realm of the dead, not the place of
       final punishment. ESV is more accurate. "Quick" = alive
       (archaic English).

v.19 — KJV: "Because they have no changes, therefore they fear not
       God" / ESV: "because they do not change and do not fear God"
       — "No changes" = no reversals, no suffering that would
       humble them. Uninterrupted prosperity breeds arrogance and
       godlessness.

v.22 — KJV: "Cast thy burden" / ESV: "Cast your burden" — Hebrew
       "yehab" appears only here in Psalms. It may mean "what is
       given to you" (your lot, your portion). Cast your God-given
       portion — whatever He has assigned you — back onto Him for
       sustaining.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.9 — WEB: "Confuse them, Lord, and confound their language" vs. KJV: "Destroy, O Lord, and divide their tongues" — WEB interprets "divide their tongues" as "confound their language," explicitly connecting to the Babel narrative (Genesis 11). NET: "Confuse them, O Lord. Frustrate their plans" — drops the tongue/language imagery entirely.

- v.15 — ASV/WEB/NET: "go down alive into Sheol" vs. KJV: "go down quick into hell" — All three modern translations correctly render "Sheol" rather than KJV's misleading "hell." They also update archaic "quick" (alive) to "alive." This recalls Korah's fate (Numbers 16:30-33).

- v.16 — ASV: "Jehovah will save me" / WEB: "Yahweh will save me" vs. KJV: "the LORD shall save me" — The divine covenant name rendered explicitly. NET uses "the Lord will deliver me."

- v.19 — NET: "God, the one who has reigned as king from long ago, will hear and humiliate them" vs. KJV: "God shall hear, and afflict them, even he that abideth of old" — NET interprets "abideth of old" as eternal kingship and "afflict" as "humiliate." WEB: "God, who is enthroned forever."

- v.22 — ASV: "Cast thy burden upon Jehovah" / WEB: "Cast your burden on Yahweh" vs. KJV: "Cast thy burden upon the LORD" — The personal covenant name is explicit in ASV/WEB. NET: "Throw your burden upon the Lord" — uses more forceful "throw" for the Hebrew.

- v.22 — NET: "He will never allow the godly to be shaken" vs. KJV: "He shall never suffer the righteous to be moved" — NET uses "godly" for "righteous" (Hebrew "tsaddiq") and "shaken" for "moved." The difference matters: "righteous" emphasizes moral standing; "godly" emphasizes relationship with God.

- v.23 — NET: "Violent and deceitful people will not live even half a normal life-span" vs. KJV: "bloody and deceitful men shall not live out half their days" — NET adds "normal" (not in Hebrew) to specify what "half their days" means — half the expected lifespan. ASV: "Bloodthirsty and deceitful men."

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. It is not weakness to wish for escape (v.6). Even David wished
   for wings. The desire to flee is human and honest. God does not
   rebuke this desire — He simply redirects it toward Himself
   rather than toward physical flight.

2. Betrayal by a friend wounds deeper than attack by an enemy
   (vv.12-14). The closer the relationship, the deeper the wound.
   If you have experienced this, know that David — and ultimately
   Jesus — understands this particular agony completely.

3. God sustains the burden-caster (v.22). The command is not
   "figure it out" but "throw it on God." The burden doesn't
   disappear — but it transfers to One who can carry it. Your job
   is to cast; God's job is to sustain.

4. Smooth words can conceal violent hearts (v.21). Not everyone
   who speaks kindly is kind. Discernment requires looking beyond
   words to patterns, actions, and fruit. Words are cheap;
   character is costly.

5. Three-times-daily prayer sustains through crisis (v.17).
   Evening, morning, and noon — a rhythm of continual dependence.
   When life is overwhelming, structure your prayer. Don't wait
   until you "feel like it" — pray on schedule.
