Psalms - Chapter 14
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A wisdom psalm on universal depravity — 'The fool hath said in his heart, There is no God'; all have turned aside, none does good; yet the LORD is refuge for the poor, and salvation will come from Zion.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Wisdom/Prophetic Lament
Attributed Author: David — "To the chief Musician, A Psalm of
David"
Key Themes: Practical atheism ("no God"), universal human
corruption, God's search for the righteous, none found good, God
as refuge for the poor, longing for Zion's salvation

Historical Context: Psalm 14 is nearly identical to Psalm 53 (an
Elohistic variant in Book II). Paul quotes vv.1-3 extensively in
Romans 3:10-12 to establish universal human sinfulness — "there is
none righteous, no, not one." The "fool" (nabal) is not
intellectually deficient but morally corrupt — the same word used
for Nabal in 1 Samuel 25. This psalm presents the divine
perspective: God looks down from heaven searching for anyone who
understands or seeks Him — and finds no one. The picture is
devastating: universal departure from God, universal corruption,
universal moral failure. Only divine salvation (v.7) can remedy
human depravity.

Structure:
- The Fool's Declaration and Its Consequences (v.1)
- God's Search — None Found Righteous (vv.2-3)
- Indictment of Oppressors (vv.4-6)
- Longing for God's Salvation (v.7)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "The fool hath said in his heart, There is no God. They are
        corrupt, they have done abominable works, there is none that
        doeth good." (KJV) — "In his heart" — not an intellectual
        conclusion but a heart-posture. The fool's atheism is moral,
        not philosophical. He says "no God" because he WANTS no God
        — no accountability, no authority over him.

v.2-3 — "The LORD looked down from heaven upon the children of
         men, to see if there were any that did understand... They
         are all gone aside... there is none that doeth good, no,
         not one." (KJV) — The divine investigation yields a
         devastating verdict: universal failure. No exceptions. Paul
         uses this in Romans 3 as the definitive statement of human
         depravity and the need for grace.

v.7  — "Oh that the salvation of Israel were come out of Zion!"
        (KJV) — After establishing that none is righteous, the
        only hope is divine intervention. Salvation must come FROM
        God (out of Zion) because it cannot come from humanity.
        Ultimately fulfilled in Christ.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.1   — Psalm 53:1 — Nearly identical psalm (Elohistic version).
v.1   — 1 Samuel 25:25 — Nabal: "for as his name is, so is he;
         Nabal is his name, and folly is with him." The moral
         "fool."
v.1-3 — Romans 3:10-12 — Paul quotes this passage directly:
         "There is none righteous, no, not one."
v.2   — Genesis 11:5 — "The LORD came down to see." Same divine
         investigation imagery.
v.2   — 2 Chronicles 16:9 — "The eyes of the LORD run to and fro
         throughout the whole earth." God searching for faithful.
v.4   — Micah 3:3 — Leaders who "eat the flesh of my people."
         Same devouring-the-poor imagery.
v.6   — Psalm 9:9 — "The LORD also will be a refuge for the
         oppressed." God as refuge for the poor.
v.7   — Romans 11:26 — "There shall come out of Sion the
         Deliverer." NT fulfillment of David's longing.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1 — KJV: "The fool" / ESV: "The fool" — Hebrew "nabal" =
      morally senseless, brutish, godless in practice. Not
      intellectual limitation but chosen moral corruption. A
      "nabal" rejects divine authority.

v.3 — KJV: "become filthy" / ESV: "become corrupt" — Hebrew
      "ne'elachu" = gone sour, become rotten (like spoiled food).
      The image is of moral putrefaction — humanity has "gone off"
      like spoiled milk.

v.5 — KJV: "There were they in great fear" / ESV: "There they are
      in great terror" — Sudden, unexpected fear. The wicked
      thought they were safe — then God's presence among the
      righteous terrifies them. The shift from complacency to
      terror is abrupt.

v.7 — KJV: "when the LORD bringeth back the captivity" / ESV:
      "when the LORD restores the fortunes" — Hebrew "shuvt shevut"
      can mean "restore captivity" (return from exile) or "restore
      fortunes" (reverse circumstances). Both point to
      comprehensive divine restoration.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.1 — NET: "Fools say to themselves, 'There is no God.' They sin and commit evil deeds; none of them does what is right" — NET makes this plural ("Fools say") where KJV/ASV/WEB use singular ("The fool hath said"). NET also replaces "corrupt... abominable works" with the softer "sin and commit evil deeds." The Hebrew "hit'ivu alilah" (made abominable their deeds) is stronger than NET suggests.

- v.2 — ASV: "Jehovah looked down from heaven" / WEB: "Yahweh looked down from heaven" / NET: "The Lord looks down from heaven at the human race" — NET adds "at the human race" (not in Hebrew) and shifts to present tense ("looks") where KJV/ASV use past tense ("looked"). The divine name rendering remains the key distinction: Jehovah (ASV), Yahweh (WEB), LORD (KJV), Lord (NET).

- v.3 — NET: "Everyone rejects God; they are all morally corrupt" — NET interprets "sar" (turned aside) as "rejects God" and "ne'elachu" (become filthy/rotten) as "morally corrupt." KJV: "become filthy." ASV: "become filthy." WEB: "become corrupt." The Hebrew image is of spoiling/going rancid (like food), which only KJV/ASV preserve with "filthy."

- v.4 — ASV: "Who eat up my people as they eat bread, And call not upon Jehovah?" / NET: "those who devour my people as if they were eating bread and do not call out to the Lord" — NET's "devour" and "as if they were eating bread" makes the metaphor more explicit. The casualness of oppression — consuming the vulnerable as routinely as eating bread — is powerful in all versions.

- v.5 — NET: "They are absolutely terrified, for God defends the godly" — Significantly paraphrased. KJV: "There were they in great fear: for God is in the generation of the righteous." ASV: "There were they in great fear; For God is in the generation of the righteous." NET adds "defends" — the Hebrew simply says God "is in/among" the generation of the righteous. His presence, not His action, is what terrifies.

- v.6 — NET: "You want to humiliate the oppressed, even though the Lord is their shelter" vs. KJV: "Ye have shamed the counsel of the poor, because the LORD is his refuge" — NET makes this an accusation with "You want to humiliate." WEB: "You frustrate the plan of the poor, because Yahweh is his refuge." The irony in all versions: the wicked attack the poor precisely because the poor trust God rather than human power.

- v.7 — ASV: "When Jehovah bringeth back the captivity of his people" / WEB: "When Yahweh restores the fortunes of his people" / NET: "When the Lord restores the well-being of his people" — Hebrew "shuvt shevut" is debated: "restore captivity" (ASV, literal) or "restore fortunes" (WEB/NET). NET's "well-being" is the most interpretive, moving from the concrete (captivity/fortunes) to the abstract.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Practical atheism is more dangerous than intellectual atheism
   (v.1). Most "atheism" is not philosophical but functional —
   living AS IF God does not exist. The fool denies God not from
   evidence but from desire — he wants no accountability.

2. Human corruption is universal (vv.2-3). Paul's use of this
   passage in Romans 3 is devastating: "There is none that doeth
   good, no, not one." This is not pessimism — it is diagnosis.
   Without grace, the human condition is uniformly corrupt. This
   makes salvation entirely of God.

3. The oppression of God's people is personal to God (v.4). Those
   who "eat up my people as they eat bread" — casual, daily
   consumption of the vulnerable — will face sudden terror (v.5)
   when they realize God is with those they oppress.

4. Salvation must come from outside humanity (v.7). Since none is
   righteous, salvation cannot arise from human effort. It must
   "come out of Zion" — from God Himself. This is the gospel in
   embryonic form: divine rescue for a humanity incapable of self-
   rescue.

5. The psalm begins with human depravity and ends with divine hope
   (v.1→v.7). The trajectory is crucial: honest diagnosis THEN
   hopeful longing. You cannot appreciate salvation until you
   accept the diagnosis. But the diagnosis is never the last word.
