Psalms - Chapter 16
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A psalm of trust and Messianic prophecy — David declares God as his portion, the boundary lines have fallen in pleasant places; 'thou wilt not leave my soul in hell' — quoted by Peter at Pentecost as fulfilled in Christ's resurrection.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Trust Psalm / Messianic Psalm
Attributed Author: David — "Michtam of David"
Key Themes: God as refuge and portion, satisfaction in God alone,
rejection of idolatry, counsel and guidance, resurrection hope,
fullness of joy in God's presence

Historical Context: "Michtam" is an uncertain term — possibly
"golden psalm" or "inscription/engraved poem." This is one of the
most significant Messianic psalms — Peter quotes vv.8-11 in his
Pentecost sermon (Acts 2:25-28) to prove Christ's resurrection,
and Paul cites it in Acts 13:35. Peter's argument: David died and
his body DID see corruption; therefore David spoke prophetically
about another — the Messiah — whose body would NOT remain in the
grave. The psalm is profoundly personal — David expressing
complete satisfaction in God as his inheritance, portion, and
source of joy. It contains no complaint, no enemies, no crisis —
only deep contentment in God.

Structure:
- Prayer for Preservation and Declaration of Trust (vv.1-2)
- Delight in the Saints, Rejection of Idols (vv.3-4)
- God as Inheritance and Portion (vv.5-6)
- God as Counselor and Presence (vv.7-8)
- Resurrection Hope and Eternal Joy (vv.9-11)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.5  — "The LORD is the portion of mine inheritance and of my cup:
        thou maintainest my lot." (KJV) — God Himself is David's
        inheritance — not land, wealth, or power. As the Levites
        had no land inheritance because "the LORD is their
        inheritance" (Deut 18:2), David claims the same: God alone
        is enough.

v.8  — "I have set the LORD always before me: because he is at my
        right hand, I shall not be moved." (KJV) — Quoted by Peter
        at Pentecost (Acts 2:25). The discipline of keeping God
        perpetually in view. "At my right hand" = the position of
        defense and strength. With God there, nothing shakes you.

v.10 — "For thou wilt not leave my soul in hell; neither wilt thou
        suffer thine Holy One to see corruption." (KJV) — The
        resurrection verse. Peter argues: David DID see corruption;
        therefore he spoke of Christ who did NOT remain in the
        grave. This is the OT's clearest prophecy of bodily
        resurrection.

v.11 — "In thy presence is fulness of joy; at thy right hand there
        are pleasures for evermore." (KJV) — The ultimate promise:
        complete joy and eternal pleasures — found not in
        circumstances but in God's presence. This is the telos
        (goal) of all human longing.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.5   — Numbers 18:20 — "I am thy part and thine inheritance." God
         as Levitical inheritance — David claims the same privilege.
v.5   — Lamentations 3:24 — "The LORD is my portion, saith my
         soul; therefore will I hope in him." Same inheritance
         theology.
v.8   — Acts 2:25-28 — Peter quotes vv.8-11 to prove Christ's
         resurrection from the dead.
v.10  — Acts 2:29-31 — Peter's argument: David died and decayed;
         he spoke of Christ who did not.
v.10  — Acts 13:35-37 — Paul uses the same verse for the same
         argument in Antioch.
v.11  — Psalm 17:15 — "I shall be satisfied, when I awake, with
         thy likeness." Parallel afterlife hope.
v.11  — Revelation 21:3-4 — "God himself shall be with them." The
         eternal presence that is fullness of joy.

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.2 — KJV: "my goodness extendeth not to thee" / ESV: "I have no
      good apart from you" — Significantly different. KJV: my good
      does not reach/benefit God. ESV: I have no good outside of
      God. The Hebrew is notoriously difficult ("tovati bal
      aleyka"). ESV's reading makes better theological sense in
      context.

v.7 — KJV: "my reins also instruct me" / ESV: "my heart instructs
      me" — Hebrew "kilyot" = kidneys, considered the seat of
      emotions/conscience in Hebrew thought. KJV "reins" (kidneys)
      is literal but archaic; ESV "heart" modernizes the metaphor.

v.9 — KJV: "my glory rejoiceth" / ESV: "my whole being rejoices"
      — Hebrew "kevodi" = my glory/honor (possibly "my liver" or
      "my inner self"). KJV is literal; ESV interprets as the whole
      person. Some see "glory" as a reference to the soul/spirit
      as the glorious part of a person.

v.10 — KJV: "leave my soul in hell" / ESV: "abandon my soul to
       Sheol" — Hebrew "Sheol" = the realm of the dead (not NT
       "hell" as place of punishment). ESV preserves the Hebrew
       concept. The promise: God will not leave His Holy One in the
       state/place of death. "Corruption" (shachath) = physical
       decay of the body.

v.11 — KJV: "pleasures for evermore" / ESV: "pleasures forevermore"
       — Hebrew "ne'imot netsach" = delights/sweetnesses of
       perpetuity. The pleasures at God's right hand are not
       temporary — they are eternal. No fading, no diminishing,
       no end.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. God Himself is the inheritance (v.5). Not His gifts, not His
   blessings, not His services — God HIMSELF. When He is your
   portion, every other loss becomes survivable. The question is
   not "What has God given me?" but "Do I have GOD?"

2. Contentment is a choice and a practice (v.6). "The lines have
   fallen for me in pleasant places" — David chose to see his
   allotment as beautiful. Contentment is not passive acceptance
   but active recognition of goodness already present.

3. Keep the LORD always before you (v.8). This is a spiritual
   discipline — "I have SET the LORD." It is deliberate,
   continuous, and produces unshakable stability. When God is
   your focal point, you cannot be disoriented.

4. Physical death is not the end (v.10). The resurrection hope is
   not merely NT theology — it is rooted in the Psalms. David
   believed (and the Spirit confirmed through Peter) that death
   would not have the final word for God's Holy One.

5. Fullness of joy is in PRESENCE, not in presents (v.11). The
   ultimate pleasure is not heaven's streets of gold but heaven's
   face-to-face encounter with God. Joy is relational, not
   material. It is found "in thy presence" — nowhere else provides
   FULLNESS.
