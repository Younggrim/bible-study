Psalms - Chapter 56
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A lament of trust — David, seized by the Philistines in Gath, declares 'When I am afraid, I will trust in thee'; God has collected his tears in a bottle; 'in God I have put my trust; I will not fear what flesh can do unto me.'

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Individual Lament / Trust Psalm
Attributed Author: David
Key Themes: Trust in the midst of fear, God's Word as anchor,
divine record of suffering, tears stored by God, confidence over
fear of man

Historical Context: The superscription places this psalm when the
Philistines seized David in Gath (1 Samuel 21:10-15). David had
fled from Saul to the Philistine city of Gath, where he was
recognized as the one who had killed Goliath. In mortal danger, he
feigned madness to escape. The tune name "Jonath elem rehokim"
means "The Dove on Distant Oaks" or "The Silent Dove of Those Far
Away" — possibly describing David as a vulnerable dove far from
home. This psalm is remarkable for its honest admission of fear
(v.3) paired with resolute trust. The refrain "I will not fear
what flesh can do unto me" (vv.4,11) is one of Scripture's great
declarations of faith amid danger.

Structure:
- Cry for Mercy Under Persecution (vv.1-2)
- Declaration of Trust Over Fear (vv.3-4)
- Description of Enemy Activity (vv.5-7)
- God's Intimate Knowledge of Suffering (v.8)
- Confidence That God Is For Me (vv.9-11)
- Vow of Praise (vv.12-13)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.3  — "What time I am afraid, I will trust in thee." (KJV) —
        Not "I will never be afraid" but "WHEN I am afraid, I
        will trust." Faith is not the absence of fear but the
        decision to trust despite fear. This is one of the most
        honest and practical verses on faith in all of Scripture.

v.4  — "In God I will praise his word, in God I have put my
        trust; I will not fear what flesh can do unto me." (KJV) —
        The refrain of the psalm. God's Word is the object of
        praise and the basis of trust. "Flesh" (basar) = mere
        mortality. What can mortal weakness do against the
        immortal God?

v.8  — "Thou tellest my wanderings: put thou my tears into thy
        bottle: are they not in thy book?" (KJV) — One of the
        most tender verses in the Psalter. God counts David's
        restless wanderings, collects his tears in a bottle (a
        wineskin or tear-flask), and records them in His book.
        Nothing is lost or unnoticed.

v.9  — "This I know; for God is for me." (KJV) — Echoed by Paul
        in Romans 8:31: "If God be for us, who can be against us?"
        David moves from petition to certainty — he KNOWS God is
        on his side.

v.4   — Romans 8:31 — "If God be for us, who can be against us?"
         The New Testament expansion of David's confidence.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1 — KJV: "man would swallow me up" / ESV: "man tramples on me"
      — Hebrew "sha'aph" can mean to trample, crush, or swallow.
      Both translations capture the sense of being overwhelmed by
      human opposition.

v.8 — KJV: "Thou tellest my wanderings" / ESV: "You have kept
      count of my tossings" — Hebrew "nod" = wandering, tossing,
      homelessness. KJV emphasizes David's fugitive travels; ESV
      emphasizes sleepless nights. Both are valid readings of
      David's restless suffering.

v.8 — KJV: "put thou my tears into thy bottle" / ESV: "put my
      tears in your bottle" — The Hebrew "nod" (bottle/wineskin)
      creates a wordplay with "nod" (wandering) in the same verse.
      Some scholars connect this to ancient Near Eastern practices
      of collecting tears in small flasks.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.4 — NET: "In God—I boast in his promise" vs. KJV: "In God I will praise his word" — NET interprets "praise his word" as "boast in his promise," shifting from worship language to confidence language. ASV/WEB keep KJV's "praise his word."

- v.8 — NET: "You keep track of my misery. Put my tears in your leather container" — NET renders "nod" (wanderings) as "misery" (losing the fugitive imagery) and "bottle" as "leather container" (making the vessel explicit). WEB: "You count my wanderings. You put my tears into your bottle" — stays with KJV.

- v.9 — NET: "I know that God is on my side" vs. KJV: "this I know; for God is for me" — NET's "on my side" is a modern idiom replacing the more theological "for me." The Hebrew simply says "God is for me" — ASV/WEB preserve this directness.

- v.10 — ASV: "In Jehovah (I will praise his word)" / WEB: "In Yahweh, I will praise his word" vs. KJV: "in the LORD will I praise his word" — The divine name is explicit in ASV/WEB. The psalm pairs "God" (Elohim) with "Yahweh/Jehovah/LORD" in parallel — the general name with the covenant name.

- v.12 — NET: "I am obligated to fulfill the vows I made to you, O God; I will give you the thank offerings you deserve" — NET heavily interprets, adding "obligated" and "you deserve" (neither in Hebrew). KJV/ASV/WEB simply state: "Thy vows are upon me... I will render praises/thank-offerings."

- v.13 — NET: "so that I might serve God as I enjoy life" vs. KJV: "that I may walk before God in the light of the living" — NET loses the beautiful Hebrew idiom "walk before God in the light of the living" and substitutes a flat paraphrase about serving God while alive. WEB preserves: "that I may walk before God in the light of the living."

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Fear and faith can coexist (v.3). "When I am afraid" — David
   admits the fear before declaring the trust. You don't need to
   pretend you're not afraid. Faith is the decision you make AFTER
   acknowledging the fear.

2. God's Word overcomes human threats (v.4). "What can flesh do to
   me?" — When you have God's Word, you have God's promise. Mortal
   threats cannot overpower immortal promises.

3. God notices every tear (v.8). Your suffering is not invisible.
   God counts your sleepless nights, collects your tears, records
   your pain. Nothing you endure is unobserved or wasted.

4. "God is for me" is a settled fact, not a feeling (v.9). "This
   I KNOW." Not "I hope" or "I wish" — I know. Base your
   confidence on God's character, not your circumstances.

5. Deliverance has a purpose: to walk in God's light (v.13). God
   doesn't save us FROM danger merely to leave us idle. He saves
   us FOR something — to walk before Him in the light of life.
