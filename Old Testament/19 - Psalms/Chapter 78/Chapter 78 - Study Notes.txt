Psalms - Chapter 78
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A historical psalm of Asaph — a lengthy retelling of Israel's history from the Exodus through the wilderness rebellion, the conquest, and God's choice of David and Zion; a warning to future generations not to repeat their fathers' unfaithfulness.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Historical / Wisdom / Didactic Psalm
Attributed Author: Asaph
Key Themes: Teaching the next generation, Israel's cycle of
rebellion and grace, God's patience and compassion, the Exodus
retold, God's choice of David and Judah over Ephraim

Historical Context: Psalm 78 is the longest Asaphite psalm and the
second-longest in the Psalter (after 119). It is a Maskil
(instructive psalm) that retells Israel's history as a cautionary
tale. Jesus cites v.2 in Matthew 13:35 ("I will open my mouth in
parables"), applying it Messianically. The psalm covers: the
Exodus plagues, the Red Sea crossing, wilderness provision and
rebellion, the loss of the Ark at Shiloh (1 Samuel 4), and God's
ultimate choice of Judah and David over Ephraim. Its purpose is
explicitly stated: so the next generation will trust God and avoid
their fathers' rebellion (vv.5-8). The psalm's climax is not
judgment but grace — God choosing David the shepherd to shepherd
His people (vv.70-72).

Structure:
- Call to Intergenerational Teaching (vv.1-8)
- Ephraim's Failure (vv.9-11)
- The Exodus Recalled (vv.12-16)
- Israel's Rebellion and God's Provision (vv.17-31)
- Continued Sin and God's Compassion (vv.32-39)
- The Plagues of Egypt Recalled (vv.40-55)
- Rebellion in the Land and Loss of Shiloh (vv.56-64)
- God's Awakening: Choice of Judah and David (vv.65-72)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.4  — "We will not hide them from their children, shewing to the
        generation to come the praises of the LORD, and his
        strength, and his wonderful works." (KJV) — The
        foundational command of Israelite faith: TELL THE NEXT
        GENERATION. Faith is not genetic — it must be taught,
        narrated, and passed down.

v.38 — "But he, being full of compassion, forgave their iniquity,
        and destroyed them not: yea, many a time turned he his
        anger away, and did not stir up all his wrath." (KJV) —
        The theological center: God's response to persistent
        rebellion is persistent compassion. He forgave MANY TIMES.
        He turned anger away OFTEN. He did NOT stir up ALL His
        wrath. Grace exceeds judgment.

v.39 — "For he remembered that they were but flesh; a wind that
        passeth away, and cometh not again." (KJV) — God tempers
        His justice with knowledge of human frailty. He knows we
        are dust (Ps 103:14). Our brevity moves Him to compassion,
        not contempt.

v.70-72 — "He chose David also his servant, and took him from the
        sheepfolds... So he fed them according to the integrity of
        his heart; and guided them by the skilfulness of his
        hands." (KJV) — The psalm's triumphant conclusion: God
        chose a shepherd-boy to shepherd His people. David's
        qualification: integrity of heart and skillful hands. Both
        character AND competence.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.2 — KJV: "dark sayings of old" / ESV: "dark sayings from of
      old" — Hebrew "chidoth" = riddles, enigmatic things. Not that
      the content is unclear but that it contains deep, layered
      meaning requiring meditation to understand.

v.25 — KJV: "Man did eat angels' food" / ESV: "Man ate of the
       bread of the angels" — Hebrew "lechem abbirim" = bread of
       the mighty/angels. Manna is described as heavenly food —
       prepared in God's realm and sent down to earth.

v.57 — KJV: "they were turned aside like a deceitful bow" / ESV:
       "they twisted like a deceitful bow" — A bow that doesn't
       shoot straight. Israel appeared aimed at God but always
       veered off target. Unreliable, inconsistent, misleading.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.4 — ASV: "the praises of Jehovah" / WEB: "the praises of Yahweh" / KJV: "the praises of the LORD" / NET: "the Lord's praiseworthy acts" — The divine name YHWH in its characteristic renderings. NET paraphrases "praises" into "praiseworthy acts," shifting from worship-language to descriptive language.

- v.21 — ASV: "Therefore Jehovah heard" / WEB: "Therefore Yahweh heard" / KJV: "Therefore the LORD heard" — Again the divine name pattern. This verse is significant because it shows God's anger at unbelief specifically.

- v.25 — KJV: "Man did eat angels' food" / ASV: "the bread of the mighty" / NET: "the food of the mighty ones" / WEB: "the bread of angels" — The Hebrew אַבִּירִים (abbirim) literally means "mighty ones." KJV/WEB interpret this as "angels"; ASV/NET keep it ambiguous as "mighty/mighty ones."

- v.41 — KJV: "limited the Holy One of Israel" / ASV: "provoked the Holy One of Israel" / NET: "offended the Holy One of Israel" / WEB: "provoked the Holy One of Israel" — KJV's "limited" is distinctive and theologically provocative — suggesting human unbelief places limitations on God's activity. Other translations use "provoked/offended."

- v.49 — KJV: "by sending evil angels among them" / ASV: "A band of angels of evil" / NET: "messengers who bring disaster" / WEB: "a band of angels of evil" — NET demythologizes "evil angels" into "messengers who bring disaster." The Hebrew מַלְאֲכֵי רָעִים can mean either angels/messengers of calamity.

- v.57 — NET: "they were as unreliable as a malfunctioning bow" / WEB: "They were twisted like a deceitful bow" / KJV: "like a deceitful bow" — NET modernizes the ancient metaphor with "malfunctioning," losing the moral dimension of "deceitful" but gaining modern clarity.

- v.65 — NET: "he was like a warrior in a drunken rage" / KJV: "like a mighty man that shouteth by reason of wine" — NET intensifies with "drunken rage" (quite bold for a Bible translation). The anthropomorphism pictures God rousing Himself with warrior fury. Both ASV and WEB stay closer to KJV's phrasing.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Tell the next generation (v.4). History not taught is history
   repeated. Every generation is responsible to pass on God's works
   — not just Bible facts but personal experience of His
   faithfulness. If you don't tell them, who will?

2. God's compassion outlasts human rebellion (v.38). Israel sinned
   REPEATEDLY. God forgave REPEATEDLY. His patience is not
   weakness — it is the strength of love that refuses to abandon.

3. Forgetfulness is the root of rebellion (v.42). "They remembered
   not his hand." Every time Israel forgot what God had done, they
   fell into sin. Memory is a spiritual discipline — rehearse
   God's works or lose your footing.

4. God chooses unlikely instruments (vv.70-71). A shepherd boy.
   Not from Ephraim (the expected tribe) but from Judah. Not from
   the palace but from the sheepfold. God's choices confound human
   expectations.
