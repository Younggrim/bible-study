Psalms - Chapter 24
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A processional/enthronement psalm — 'The earth is the LORD's'; who may ascend His holy hill? The one with clean hands and a pure heart. 'Lift up your heads, O ye gates — the King of glory shall come in!'

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Entrance Liturgy / Hymn of Praise / Messianic
Attributed Author: David — "A Psalm of David"
Key Themes: God's universal ownership, entrance requirements for
worship, the King of Glory entering His gates, God as warrior-king,
the LORD of hosts

Historical Context: Psalm 24 completes the trilogy of Psalms 22-24
(Cross, Shepherd, Crown). It may have been composed for the Ark's
procession into Jerusalem (2 Sam 6:12-15) — the dramatic moment
when the Ark approached the gates of Zion. The psalm has a
responsive/antiphonal structure suggesting liturgical use: one
group asks "Who shall ascend?" (v.3); another answers (vv.4-5); a
processional voice cries "Lift up your heads, O gates!" (v.7);
gatekeepers respond "Who is this King of glory?" (v.8); and the
answer thunders: "The LORD strong and mighty!" This is also
prophetically applied to Christ's ascension and His future return
in glory.

Structure:
- God's Ownership of All Creation (vv.1-2)
- Who May Approach God? (vv.3-6)
- The King of Glory Enters (vv.7-10)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "The earth is the LORD'S, and the fulness thereof; the
        world, and they that dwell therein." (KJV) — Universal
        divine ownership. Everything and everyone belongs to God
        by right of creation. Quoted by Paul in 1 Cor 10:26
        regarding Christian freedom.

v.3-4 — "Who shall ascend into the hill of the LORD?... He that
         hath clean hands, and a pure heart" (KJV) — The second
         entrance liturgy in the Psalter (cf. Psalm 15). Access to
         God requires both external conduct (clean hands) and
         internal purity (pure heart). Neither alone suffices.

v.7  — "Lift up your heads, O ye gates; and be ye lift up, ye
        everlasting doors; and the King of glory shall come in."
        (KJV) — The processional cry. Gates are personified — told
        to lift their "heads" (lintels) to admit a King too great
        for ordinary doors. Applied to: (1) the Ark entering
        Jerusalem, (2) Christ ascending to heaven, (3) Christ
        returning in glory.

v.10 — "The LORD of hosts, he is the King of glory." (KJV) — The
        final answer to "Who is this King?" — Yahweh Tsebaoth, the
        LORD of angel-armies. The King of glory is the God of all
        heavenly forces.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.1   — 1 Corinthians 10:26 — Paul quotes this: "The earth is the
         Lord's, and the fulness thereof." Christian freedom in
         eating.
v.1   — Exodus 19:5 — "All the earth is mine." Same ownership
         claim at Sinai.
v.3-4 — Psalm 15:1-5 — Parallel entrance liturgy with expanded
         ethical requirements.
v.3-4 — Isaiah 33:14-16 — "Who among us shall dwell with the
         devouring fire?" Third entrance liturgy.
v.7   — 2 Samuel 6:12-15 — The Ark's entry into Jerusalem — the
         likely historical occasion.
v.7   — Acts 1:9-11 — Christ's ascension — entering heaven's
         gates as the King of glory.
v.10  — Isaiah 6:3 — "The whole earth is full of his glory." The
         LORD of hosts revealed.
v.10  — Revelation 19:11-16 — Christ returns as King of kings —
         the ultimate "King of glory" entrance.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.2 — KJV: "founded it upon the seas" / ESV: "founded it upon the
      seas" — Ancient cosmology: the earth was understood as
      established over primordial waters. The theological point is
      not cosmology but sovereignty — God established the world
      over chaos and maintains it.

v.4 — KJV: "lifted up his soul unto vanity" / ESV: "does not lift
      up his soul to what is false" — Hebrew "shav'" = emptiness,
      falsehood, worthlessness. ESV clarifies: the pure-hearted
      person does not orient their life toward what is empty/false
      (idols, lies, worthless things).

v.6 — KJV: "that seek thy face, O Jacob" / ESV: "who seek the
      face of the God of Jacob" — The Hebrew is unusual. KJV reads
      as addressing Jacob/Israel. ESV (following some manuscripts)
      reads "the God of Jacob." ESV makes more theological sense —
      they seek GOD'S face, not Jacob's.

v.7 — KJV: "everlasting doors" / ESV: "ancient doors" — Hebrew
      "pithchey olam" = doors of eternity/antiquity. Both
      translations work — these gates have stood since ancient
      times and will endure forever. They must expand to receive
      the infinitely glorious King.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Everything belongs to God (v.1). You own nothing ultimately.
   Your possessions, your body, your time, your relationships —
   all belong to the Creator. This is not restrictive but
   liberating: if He owns it all, He is responsible for it all.

2. Access to God requires integrity inside AND out (v.4). Clean
   hands (behavior) without a pure heart (motive) is hypocrisy.
   A pure heart without clean hands is self-deception. God
   requires both. Begin with the heart; the hands will follow.

3. The King of glory requires expanded gates (v.7). He is too
   great for the doors as they are. Our conceptions of God must
   be continually expanded — "lift up your heads." Our categories
   are always too small for the God who comes.

4. Three applications of the King's entry: (1) Historical — the
   Ark entering Jerusalem. (2) Heavenly — Christ ascending after
   resurrection. (3) Future — Christ returning in glory. In each
   case, the King comes to reclaim what is His.

5. "Who is this King of glory?" (vv.8, 10). The question is
   answered with God's most martial title: LORD of hosts, mighty
   in battle. The King who comes is not a passive figurehead but
   a warrior who has won the decisive battle. He enters as
   conqueror.
