Psalms - Chapter 15
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
An entrance liturgy — 'LORD, who shall abide in thy tabernacle?' The answer describes the person of integrity: truthful speech, no slander, keeps oaths even when it hurts, and does not take bribes.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Torah/Entrance Liturgy Psalm
Attributed Author: David — "A Psalm of David"
Key Themes: Requirements for dwelling with God, ethical character,
truthful speech, integrity in relationships, financial justice,
keeping vows

Historical Context: Psalm 15 is an "entrance liturgy" — likely
used at the gates of the tabernacle/temple when worshippers asked
who may enter God's presence. The question (v.1) receives a
ten-point ethical answer (vv.2-5) rather than a ritual one.
Notably, NONE of the qualifications are ceremonial — they are all
moral and relational. This is remarkable: access to God is
determined by character, not ritual compliance. This may have been
composed when David brought the Ark to Jerusalem (2 Sam 6) and
established tabernacle worship there. Compare Isaiah 33:14-16 and
Psalm 24:3-5 for parallel entrance liturgies.

Structure:
- The Question: Who May Dwell With God? (v.1)
- The Answer: Ten Ethical Qualifications (vv.2-5a)
- The Promise: Unshakable Security (v.5b)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "LORD, who shall abide in thy tabernacle? who shall dwell
        in thy holy hill?" (KJV) — The foundational question of
        worship: who has access to God? The answer is character-
        based, not ritual-based. This reframes worship as an
        ethical commitment, not merely a ceremonial act.

v.2  — "He that walketh uprightly, and worketh righteousness, and
        speaketh the truth in his heart." (KJV) — Three spheres:
        conduct (walketh), action (worketh), and speech/thought
        (speaketh truth IN HIS HEART). Integrity begins internally
        — truth in the heart before truth on the lips.

v.4  — "He that sweareth to his own hurt, and changeth not." (KJV)
        — One of Scripture's most challenging ethical standards:
        keeping your word even when it costs you. A promise made is
        binding regardless of changed circumstances or personal
        disadvantage.

v.5b — "He that doeth these things shall never be moved." (KJV) —
        The promise: stability, permanence, security. The ethical
        life produces the unshakable life. Character is foundation.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.1   — Psalm 24:3-4 — "Who shall ascend into the hill of the
         LORD?" Same entrance-liturgy question with parallel
         answer.
v.1   — Isaiah 33:14-16 — "Who among us shall dwell with the
         devouring fire?" Same question in prophetic context.
v.2   — Micah 6:8 — "What doth the LORD require of thee? To do
         justly, love mercy, walk humbly." Same ethical summary.
v.3   — James 3:5-8 — "The tongue is a fire." Speech control as
         character evidence.
v.4   — Matthew 5:37 — "Let your yes be yes and your no be no."
         Jesus' ethic of keeping vows.
v.4   — Joshua 9:18-19 — Israel kept its oath to the Gibeonites
         despite being deceived. Swearing to one's hurt and not
         changing.
v.5   — Exodus 22:25 — "If thou lend money to any of my people
         that is poor... thou shalt not be to him as an usurer."
         OT prohibition on exploitative lending.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1 — KJV: "abide in thy tabernacle" / ESV: "sojourn in your
      tent" — Hebrew "gur" = sojourn/dwell as a guest. "Ohel" =
      tent. The imagery is of being a guest in God's own dwelling.
      Who is welcome in God's house?

v.3 — KJV: "backbiteth not" / ESV: "does not slander" — Hebrew
      "ragal" literally = to go about as a spy/foot-traveler,
      hence "going about gossiping." Both translations capture the
      sense of spreading harmful speech about others.

v.4 — KJV: "a vile person is contemned" / ESV: "a vile person is
      despised" — The righteous person makes moral distinctions.
      This is not social snobbery but moral discernment — refusing
      to honor what God rejects while honoring what God values
      (those who fear the LORD).

v.5 — KJV: "putteth not out his money to usury" / ESV: "does not
      put out his money at interest" — Hebrew "neshek" = interest/
      usury. In the OT context, lending at interest to fellow
      Israelites (especially the poor) was prohibited. This is
      about exploitation of the vulnerable, not all financial
      transactions.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.1 — ASV: "Jehovah, who shall sojourn in thy tabernacle?" / WEB: "Yahweh, who shall dwell in your sanctuary?" / NET: "Lord, who may be a guest in your home?" — Three different approaches to the entrance question. ASV preserves "sojourn" (gur = dwell as guest) and "tabernacle." WEB uses "sanctuary." NET domesticates it entirely to "your home" — losing the sacred-space theology of temple/tabernacle worship.

- v.1 — NET: "Who may live on your holy hill?" — All versions preserve "holy hill" (har qodsheka), but NET's "live" is less formal than ASV's "dwell" or KJV's "dwell." The question is about permanent residency with God, not casual visiting.

- v.3 — NET: "He does not slander, or do harm to others, or insult his neighbor" — NET breaks this into three clauses. KJV: "He that backbiteth not with his tongue, nor doeth evil to his neighbour, nor taketh up a reproach against his neighbour." ASV uses "slandereth" for Hebrew "ragal" (to spy/go about gossiping). WEB: "doesn't slander with his tongue" — the tongue-connection is important as it links to the speech theme of v.2.

- v.4 — ASV: "He that sweareth to his own hurt, and changeth not" / WEB: "he who keeps an oath even when it hurts, and doesn't change" / NET: "He makes firm commitments and does not renege on his promise" — NET loses the "to his own hurt" dimension entirely, replacing it with the bland "firm commitments." The Hebrew specifically says this person swears even when it damages him — integrity over self-interest.

- v.4 — NET: "he despises a reprobate, but honors the Lord's loyal followers" — NET uses "loyal followers" for Hebrew "yir'ey YHWH" (those who fear the LORD/Yahweh). This is a significant theological shift — "fearing God" carries awe, reverence, and trembling that "loyal followers" does not convey.

- v.5 — ASV: "He that putteth not out his money to interest" / WEB: "he who doesn't lend out his money for usury" / NET: "He does not charge interest when he lends his money" — KJV uses "usury" (neshek = a bite, i.e., interest that bites/devours). WEB retains "usury." NET neutralizes it to "charge interest." In the OT context, lending at interest to fellow Israelites (especially the poor) was exploitative.

- v.5 — NET: "He does not take bribes to testify against the innocent" — NET adds "to testify" (not explicit in Hebrew). KJV/ASV: "nor taketh reward against the innocent" — Hebrew "shochad" = bribe. The broader Hebrew includes any payment that perverts justice, not only courtroom testimony.

- v.5 — WEB: "He who does these things shall never be shaken" vs. KJV: "shall never be moved" — Both translate Hebrew "mot" (to totter/shake). NET: "will never be shaken." ASV: "shall never be moved." The promise of unshakable stability for the person of integrity is consistent across all versions.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Access to God is ethical, not merely ceremonial (v.1). God does
   not ask "Did you perform the right ritual?" but "What is your
   character?" Worship without integrity is empty. Character IS the
   qualification for God's presence.

2. Truth must be internal before it is external (v.2). "Speaketh
   the truth IN HIS HEART" — not just truth on the lips but truth
   in the mind. Self-deception disqualifies as much as lying to
   others. Are you honest with yourself?

3. Keep your word even when it costs (v.4). This is perhaps the
   most practical and painful requirement. A promise made in
   optimism must be honored in adversity. Your word is your bond
   even when "the deal" turns against you.

4. Financial ethics matter to God (v.5). How you use money reveals
   character. Exploiting the vulnerable through lending or taking
   bribes against the innocent — these are worship disqualifiers.
   Financial integrity is spiritual integrity.

5. Character produces stability (v.5b). "Shall never be moved" is
   the reward of ethical living. The person of integrity has a
   foundation that circumstances cannot shake. Compromise creates
   instability; integrity creates permanence.
