Psalms - Chapter 101
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A royal psalm of David — a king's pledge of integrity: 'I will walk within my house with a perfect heart'; he will cut off the slanderer, the proud, the deceitful, and surround himself with faithful servants.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Royal Psalm / Psalm of Personal Commitment
Attributed Author: David (superscription: "A Psalm of David")
Key Themes: Integrity of leadership, personal holiness, justice
in governance, choosing righteous company, rejecting wickedness,
the king's moral commitments

Historical Context: Psalm 101 is David's "code of conduct" for
his reign — essentially a royal manifesto of integrity. It reads
like a coronation oath or a king's pledge to God about how he will
govern. David makes a series of "I will" declarations covering
both his private life (within his house) and public duties
(governing the city of the LORD). This psalm likely dates from
early in David's reign, perhaps when he established Jerusalem as
capital and brought the ark there. It has been called "The Ruler's
Psalm" and "The Prince's Psalm" — a model for any leader who
desires to honor God in their position of authority. Augustine
reportedly said he sang this psalm daily.

Structure:
- Declaration of Praise for Mercy and Justice (v.1)
- Personal Commitment to Integrity (vv.2-4)
- Commitment to Justice Among Associates (vv.5-7)
- Daily Commitment to Purge Wickedness (v.8)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "I will behave myself wisely in a perfect way. O when wilt
        thou come unto me? I will walk within my house with a
        perfect heart." (KJV) — The commitment to integrity starts
        at HOME. Not in public, not before the crowd — within his
        house. Private character before public performance. The
        interjection "O when wilt thou come unto me?" reveals
        David's longing for God's presence to enable this standard.

v.3  — "I will set no wicked thing before mine eyes: I hate the
        work of them that turn aside; it shall not cleave to me."
        (KJV) — What you put before your eyes shapes your heart.
        David guards his gaze. "Turn aside" = apostasy, departure
        from God's way. He doesn't merely avoid evil — he HATES
        the work of it. And his commitment: it shall not cleave
        (cling) to me.

v.5  — "Whoso privily slandereth his neighbour, him will I cut
        off: him that hath an high look and a proud heart will not
        I suffer." (KJV) — Two types David will not tolerate:
        (1) the secret slanderer — attacking reputation covertly,
        (2) the proud — haughty eyes and arrogant heart. A leader
        must confront both gossip and pride in his circle.

v.6  — "Mine eyes shall be upon the faithful of the land, that
        they may dwell with me: he that walketh in a perfect way,
        he shall serve me." (KJV) — The positive counterpart to
        cutting off the wicked: actively seeking and elevating the
        faithful. You become who you surround yourself with. David
        chose advisors by character, not mere competence.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.1   — Micah 6:8 — "To do justly, and to love mercy" — mercy
         and justice are God's twin attributes David sings of.
v.2   — 1 Timothy 3:4-5 — An elder must manage his own household
         well. Private integrity qualifies public leadership.
v.3   — Job 31:1 — "I made a covenant with mine eyes." Same
         commitment to guarding what enters through the eyes.
v.5   — Proverbs 6:16-17 — "A proud look" is among the things
         God hates.
v.6   — Proverbs 13:20 — "He that walketh with wise men shall be
         wise." Choosing righteous company.
v.8   — Jeremiah 21:12 — "Execute judgment in the morning" — same
         concept of daily, prompt justice.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1 — KJV: "mercy and judgment" / ESV: "steadfast love and
      justice" — Hebrew "hesed u-mishpat." These are the twin
      pillars of God's character and of righteous rule. Hesed
      (mercy/love) and mishpat (justice/judgment) must go together.

v.2 — KJV: "I will behave myself wisely in a perfect way" / ESV:
      "I will ponder the way that is blameless" — Hebrew "askil"
      (give attention to, act wisely) + "derek tamim" (blameless
      way). KJV emphasizes wise conduct; ESV emphasizes thoughtful
      consideration. Both capture the intentionality required.

v.4 — KJV: "A froward heart" / ESV: "A perverse heart" — Hebrew
      "iqqesh" = twisted, crooked. Not merely sinful but
      deliberately distorted — a heart that bends truth.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.1 — KJV: "I will sing of mercy and judgment" / ASV: "I will sing of lovingkindness and justice" / WEB: "I will sing of loving kindness and justice" / NET: "I will sing about loyalty and justice" — Hebrew "hesed u-mishpat" rendered four ways: KJV "mercy/judgment," ASV "lovingkindness/justice," WEB "loving kindness/justice," NET "loyalty/justice." NET's "loyalty" emphasizes the covenantal fidelity dimension of hesed.

- v.1 — ASV: "Unto thee, O Jehovah, will I sing praises" / WEB: "To you, Yahweh, I will sing praises" / NET: "To you, O Lord, I will sing praises" — Divine name: ASV "Jehovah," WEB "Yahweh," NET "Lord." All three make "sing praises" (Hebrew "azammera") explicit, where KJV simply has "unto thee, O LORD, will I sing."

- v.2 — NET: "I will walk in the way of integrity. When will you come to me? I will conduct my business with integrity in the midst of my palace" — NET's "conduct my business" and "palace" are interpretive expansions. KJV/ASV have "walk within my house with a perfect heart." NET assumes the Davidic royal context (house = palace) and adds "conduct my business" — shifting from personal character to administrative conduct.

- v.3 — KJV: "I will set no wicked thing before mine eyes" / ASV: "I will set no base thing before mine eyes" / WEB: "I will set no vile thing before my eyes" / NET: "I will not even consider doing what is dishonest" — ASV uses "base" (worthless), WEB uses "vile." NET completely restructures: from guarding what one SEES to refusing to CONSIDER dishonesty — an interpretive leap from visual temptation to mental deliberation.

- v.4 — KJV: "A froward heart shall depart from me" / ASV: "A perverse heart shall depart from me" / WEB: "A perverse heart will be far from me" / NET: "I will have nothing to do with a perverse person" — KJV's archaic "froward" = perverse/crooked (Hebrew "iqqesh"). NET changes from abstract ("perverse heart departing") to concrete personal action ("I will have nothing to do with") — a significant interpretive shift.

- v.5 — KJV: "him will I cut off" / ASV: "him will I destroy" / WEB: "I will silence" / NET: "I will destroy" — Hebrew "atsmit" = cut off/silence/destroy. Different translations emphasize different aspects: KJV's "cut off" (from community/court), WEB's "silence" (stop the slander), ASV/NET's "destroy" (stronger punitive action).

- v.8 — KJV: "I will early destroy all the wicked" / ASV: "Morning by morning will I destroy all the wicked of the land" / WEB: "Morning by morning, I will destroy all the wicked of the land" / NET: "Each morning I will destroy all the wicked people in the land" — KJV's "early" is clarified by ASV/WEB/NET as "morning by morning" (Hebrew "labbeqarim"). Also: KJV "city of the LORD" / ASV/WEB "city of Jehovah/Yahweh" — again the divine name distinction.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Integrity starts at home (v.2). David's first commitment is
   how he walks "within my house." If your character doesn't hold
   up at home — where no one is watching — it won't hold up
   anywhere. Private life IS public qualification.

2. Guard your eyes (v.3). David's ancient commitment anticipates
   our modern struggle perfectly. What you set before your eyes
   shapes your desires, your thoughts, your actions. Be
   intentional about what you consume.

3. Choose your circle wisely (vv.5-7). A leader is only as strong
   as who they allow close. David committed to removing deceivers,
   slanderers, and the proud from his inner circle while actively
   seeking the faithful. Who you tolerate, you become.

4. Justice must be daily (v.8). "Morning by morning" — not
   occasionally, not when convenient. Consistent, daily pursuit of
   righteousness in your sphere of influence.

5. This psalm challenges every person in leadership: Will you
   write your own "Psalm 101"? What are YOUR "I will" commitments
   for your home, your work, your influence?
