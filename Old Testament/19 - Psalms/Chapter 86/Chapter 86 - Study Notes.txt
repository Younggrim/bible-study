Psalms - Chapter 86
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
An individual lament of David — 'Bow down thine ear, O LORD, for I am poor and needy'; God is merciful, great in steadfast love, ready to forgive all who call; 'teach me thy way, O LORD; unite my heart to fear thy name.'

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Individual Lament / Prayer Psalm
Attributed Author: David (the only Davidic psalm in Book III)
Key Themes: Poverty and neediness before God, God's character as
the basis of prayer, undivided heart, the incomparability of God,
all nations worshiping, divine compassion

Historical Context: This is the only psalm in Book III (73-89)
attributed to David, making it distinctive in an otherwise Asaph/
Korah section. It is a "Prayer of David" — intensely personal,
weaving together phrases from other psalms and from Exodus 34:6
(the divine self-revelation to Moses). David's prayer is saturated
with Scripture — nearly every verse echoes earlier biblical
language. This makes it a model of how to pray: using God's own
revealed words back to Him. The psalm moves between petition and
praise, with v.11 ("unite my heart to fear thy name") as its
theological center.

Structure:
- Cry for Help Based on Need (vv.1-5)
- Confidence in God's Responsiveness (vv.6-7)
- God's Incomparability Among the Gods (vv.8-10)
- Prayer for an Undivided Heart (v.11)
- Praise for Past Deliverance (vv.12-13)
- Description of Enemies (v.14)
- God's Character Declared (v.15)
- Final Petition (vv.16-17)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.5  — "For thou, Lord, art good, and ready to forgive; and
        plenteous in mercy unto all them that call upon thee." (KJV)
        — God's readiness to forgive. He is not reluctant but
        "ready" — eager, willing, predisposed to mercy. And the
        scope: ALL who call. No one who calls is excluded.

v.11 — "Teach me thy way, O LORD; I will walk in thy truth: unite
        my heart to fear thy name." (KJV) — The psalm's great
        prayer. "Unite my heart" — David knows his heart is
        divided, pulled in multiple directions. He asks God to
        gather his scattered affections into a single focus: the
        fear of God's name.

v.15 — "But thou, O Lord, art a God full of compassion, and
        gracious, longsuffering, and plenteous in mercy and truth."
        (KJV) — Direct echo of Exodus 34:6 — God's self-revelation
        to Moses. David prays God's own character description back
        to Him. This is the foundation of all prayer: God IS
        compassionate, gracious, patient, abounding in love.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.5   — Exodus 34:6-7 — The divine self-revelation on Sinai.
         David quotes this throughout the psalm.
v.9   — Revelation 15:4 — "All nations shall come and worship
         before thee." Fulfillment of David's prophetic vision.
v.11  — Jeremiah 32:39 — "I will give them one heart." God
         promising to do what David prays for.
v.11  — Ezekiel 11:19 — "I will give them one heart, and I will
         put a new spirit within you." Same undivided heart.
v.13  — Psalm 116:8 — "Thou hast delivered my soul from death."
v.15  — Joel 2:13 — "He is gracious and merciful, slow to anger."
         Same divine character.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.2 — KJV: "for I am holy" / ESV: "for I am godly" — Hebrew
      "chasid" = one who is devoted, loyal, godly. David is not
      claiming sinlessness but covenant loyalty — he is God's
      faithful one who trusts in Him.

v.11 — KJV: "unite my heart" / ESV: "unite my heart" — Hebrew
       "yached" = to make one, to unite. The divided heart is the
       universal human problem: wanting God AND wanting other
       things. David asks for integration — one heart, one focus.

v.13 — KJV: "the lowest hell" / ESV: "the depths of Sheol" —
       Hebrew "Sheol tachtiyyah" = the lowest Sheol. ESV is more
       precise. David speaks of being rescued from the very brink
       of death.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.1 — ASV: "Bow down thine ear, O Jehovah" / WEB: "Hear, Yahweh" / KJV: "Bow down thine ear, O LORD" — The divine name opens this prayer. WEB simplifies "bow down thine ear" to just "hear," losing the vivid anthropomorphism of God bending down to listen.

- v.5 — KJV: "plenteous in mercy" / ASV: "abundant in lovingkindness" / WEB: "abundant in loving kindness" / NET: "show great faithfulness" — The Hebrew חֶסֶד (chesed) again: KJV "mercy," ASV/WEB "lovingkindness/loving kindness," NET "faithfulness." NET's rendering here is unusual — chesed typically isn't "faithfulness" (that's usually אֱמֶת, emeth).

- v.11 — ASV: "Teach me thy way, O Jehovah" / WEB: "Teach me your way, Yahweh" / NET: "O Lord, teach me how you want me to live" — NET dramatically paraphrases "teach me thy way" into "teach me how you want me to live." While capturing the sense, it loses the concision and poetry of the Hebrew.

- v.13 — KJV: "the lowest hell" / ASV: "the lowest Sheol" / WEB: "the lowest Sheol" / NET: "the depths of Sheol" — KJV translates שְׁאוֹל (Sheol) as "hell," while ASV/WEB/NET retain "Sheol" — the Hebrew concept of the realm of the dead, which is not equivalent to the Christian "hell."

- v.15 — KJV: "plenteous in mercy and truth" / ASV: "abundant in lovingkindness and truth" / WEB: "abundant in loving kindness and truth" / NET: "great loyal love and faithfulness" — The classic pair חֶסֶד וֶאֱמֶת (chesed ve-emeth) from Exodus 34:6. NET's "loyal love and faithfulness" captures the covenant dimension that "mercy and truth" (KJV) may miss.

- v.11 — WEB: "Make my heart undivided to fear your name" / KJV: "unite my heart to fear thy name" / NET: "Make me wholeheartedly committed to you" — NET loses "fear thy name" entirely, replacing it with a generic commitment phrase. The Hebrew specifically asks for a heart united in reverent fear of God's name — a richer concept than mere "commitment."

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. God is READY to forgive (v.5). You don't need to convince Him.
   He is not grudging or reluctant. His disposition toward those
   who call on Him is generous mercy. Come boldly.

2. Pray for an undivided heart (v.11). The greatest spiritual
   danger is not atheism but divided loyalty. Ask God to unite
   your scattered desires into singular devotion. A united heart
   is the foundation of all spiritual growth.

3. Pray Scripture back to God (v.15). David uses God's own words
   (Exodus 34:6) as the basis of his prayer. This is how to pray
   powerfully: remind God of who He has revealed Himself to be.
   His character is your strongest argument.

4. "Teach me thy way" precedes "I will walk in thy truth" (v.11).
   Knowledge before obedience. You can't walk a path you haven't
   learned. Ask to be taught FIRST — then commit to walking.
