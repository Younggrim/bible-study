Psalms - Chapter 136
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
The Great Hallel — a litany of thanksgiving with the refrain 'for his mercy endureth for ever' repeated in all 26 verses, recounting creation, the Exodus, wilderness provision, and the conquest.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Historical Hymn / Responsive Liturgical Psalm
(The Great Hallel)
Attributed Author: Anonymous (known as "The Great Hallel" in
Jewish tradition)
Key Themes: God's mercy (hesed) enduring forever — repeated 26
times, God as Creator, God as Redeemer from Egypt, God as Provider,
the connection between mercy and every divine act

Historical Context: Psalm 136 is unique in the Psalter — every
single verse (all 26) ends with the same refrain: "for his mercy
endureth for ever" (Hebrew: "ki le-olam hasdo"). This was sung
antiphonally: a leader sang the first half, and the congregation
responded with the refrain. It was sung at temple dedications
(2 Chr 7:3), before battles (2 Chr 20:21), and at Passover. Known
in Jewish tradition as "The Great Hallel," it surveys all of
salvation history — creation (vv.4-9), Exodus (vv.10-22), and
ongoing providence (vv.23-25) — declaring that God's hesed
(covenant love/mercy) underlies EVERY act. The relentless
repetition is not laziness but theology: mercy is not occasional
but CONSTANT in everything God does.

Structure:
- Call to Thanksgiving (vv.1-3)
- God's Mercy in Creation (vv.4-9)
- God's Mercy in the Exodus (vv.10-22)
- God's Mercy in Providence (vv.23-26)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "O give thanks unto the LORD; for he is good: for his
        mercy endureth for ever." (KJV) — The thesis statement.
        WHY give thanks? Because He is good. And specifically:
        His mercy ENDURES — not expires, not fluctuates, not ends.
        FOREVER.

v.23 — "Who remembered us in our low estate: for his mercy
        endureth for ever." (KJV) — After creation and Exodus, the
        psalm becomes personal: "remembered US in OUR low estate."
        This isn't just ancient history — it's your story. God
        remembered YOU when you were at your lowest.

v.25 — "Who giveth food to all flesh: for his mercy endureth for
        ever." (KJV) — Even daily bread is mercy. God feeds ALL
        FLESH — not just Israel, not just believers. Universal
        provision flows from everlasting mercy.

v.26 — "O give thanks unto the God of heaven: for his mercy
        endureth for ever." (KJV) — The psalm ends where it began:
        thanksgiving. After 26 declarations of eternal mercy, the
        only response is gratitude.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
"his mercy endureth for ever" — Hebrew "ki le-olam hasdo" =
literally "for to eternity [is] His hesed." Hesed encompasses
mercy, lovingkindness, steadfast love, covenant faithfulness,
loyalty, and grace. No single English word captures it. The KJV's
"mercy" and ESV's "steadfast love" each catch one facet of this
diamond.

The 26-fold repetition: In Jewish numerology, 26 is the numerical
value of God's name YHVH (Yod=10, He=5, Vav=6, He=5). Whether
intentional or not, the number of repetitions matches the number
of God's Name.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.1 — KJV: "his mercy endureth for ever" (×26) / ASV: "his lovingkindness endureth for ever" / WEB: "his loving kindness endures forever" / NET: "his loyal love endures" — The psalm's refrain appears 26 times, making the chesed translation choice the most conspicuous feature. KJV's "mercy" vs. ASV's "lovingkindness" vs. NET's "loyal love" shapes the entire psalm's theology.
- v.1 — ASV: "Jehovah" / WEB: "Yahweh" / NET: "the Lord" — Only v.1 uses the personal divine name; the rest use "God of gods," "Lord of lords," and "him who..."
- v.4 — NET: "To the one who performs magnificent, amazing deeds all by himself" — Significantly expands KJV's "To him who alone doeth great wonders." NET adds "magnificent, amazing" and "all by himself," making God's solitary action emphatic.
- v.5 — KJV: "by wisdom" / ASV/WEB: "by understanding" / NET: "used wisdom" — Hebrew "tevunah" means understanding/intelligence. KJV chooses "wisdom"; ASV/WEB stay closer with "understanding."
- v.15 — NET: "tossed Pharaoh and his army into the Red Sea" — Vividly paraphrases KJV's "overthrew Pharaoh and his host in the Red sea." NET's "tossed" is more physical and violent than "overthrew."
- v.23 — NET: "remembered us when we were down" — Colloquial paraphrase of KJV's "remembered us in our low estate." NET trades dignity for accessibility.
- v.24 — KJV: "redeemed us from our enemies" / ASV: "delivered us from our adversaries" / NET: "snatched us away from our enemies" — NET's "snatched us away" adds urgency and violence to the rescue imagery.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. His mercy is in EVERYTHING (all 26 verses). Creation — mercy.
   Judgment on Egypt — mercy. Wilderness leading — mercy. Daily
   food — mercy. There is no act of God that is divorced from
   His hesed. Look for mercy in everything He does.

2. Repetition is not laziness but emphasis. Saying it once is a
   statement. Saying it 26 times is a conviction, a theology,
   a worldview. Let "His mercy endures forever" become your
   refrain — the lens through which you see everything.

3. Even in "low estate" — mercy (v.23). Your lowest point is not
   outside God's mercy. He REMEMBERS you there. Low estate is
   where mercy meets you.

4. Sing this psalm antiphonally. This psalm was meant to be
   ANSWERED — leader and congregation. Find someone and practice
   responsive worship: they sing God's acts, you respond with
   "His mercy endures forever."

5. All flesh receives His provision (v.25). God's mercy is not
   exclusive. He feeds everyone. Common grace flows to all
   creation from His everlasting love.
