Psalms - Chapter 2
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A royal/Messianic psalm — the nations rage against the LORD and His Anointed, but God laughs from heaven, installs His King on Zion, and declares 'Thou art my Son'; blessed are all who take refuge in Him.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Royal/Messianic Psalm
Attributed Author: David (attributed in Acts 4:25)
Key Themes: God's sovereign rule, rebellion of nations, the
Anointed King (Messiah), divine sonship, judgment, refuge in God

Historical Context: Psalm 2 is the second "gateway" psalm,
paired with Psalm 1 as a dual introduction to the Psalter. While
Psalm 1 addresses individual righteousness, Psalm 2 addresses
cosmic sovereignty. Though no superscription appears in the Hebrew
text, Acts 4:25 attributes it to David. Its immediate context may
be a coronation liturgy for Davidic kings, but its ultimate
referent is Messianic — the NT quotes it more than any other psalm.
The "decree" of v.7 was applied at each king's enthronement but
finds its ultimate fulfillment in Christ. Four speakers alternate:
the nations (v.3), God the Father (vv.6-9), the Son (v.7), and
the psalmist/wisdom voice (vv.10-12).

Structure:
- The Nations Rebel (vv.1-3)
- God Laughs and Responds (vv.4-6)
- The Son Declares the Decree (vv.7-9)
- The Warning and Invitation (vv.10-12)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "The kings of the earth set themselves, and the rulers take
        counsel together, against the LORD, and against his
        anointed" (KJV) — "Anointed" = Hebrew "mashiach" (Messiah).
        Opposition to God and to His Christ are one and the same
        rebellion. Applied to Jesus in Acts 4:25-27.

v.4  — "He that sitteth in the heavens shall laugh: the Lord shall
        have them in derision." (KJV) — God's response to human
        rebellion is not anxiety but laughter. Not cruel mockery
        but the response of absolute sovereignty to absurd
        pretension. The nations' conspiracy is laughable to the
        One who sits above all.

v.7  — "The LORD hath said unto me, Thou art my Son; this day have
        I begotten thee." (KJV) — The most quoted OT verse in the
        NT. Applied to Christ's resurrection (Acts 13:33), His
        superiority to angels (Hebrews 1:5), and His priestly
        appointment (Hebrews 5:5). "Today I have begotten you"
        refers to enthronement/coronation, not origin.

v.12 — "Kiss the Son, lest he be angry, and ye perish from the
        way, when his wrath is kindled but a little. Blessed are
        all they that put their trust in him." (KJV) — "Kiss" =
        submission/homage. The psalm ends as Psalm 1 began: with
        "blessed." The two psalms form a frame: blessed is the one
        who delights in Torah (Ps 1) and takes refuge in the Son
        (Ps 2).

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.1-2 — Acts 4:25-28 — The early church explicitly identifies
         Herod, Pilate, Gentiles, and Israel as the "kings and
         rulers" who conspired against Jesus.
v.6   — Psalm 110:1-2 — "The LORD said unto my Lord, Sit thou at
         my right hand." Another Messianic enthronement psalm.
v.7   — Acts 13:33 — Paul applies "today I have begotten you" to
         Christ's resurrection — the moment of public declaration.
v.7   — Hebrews 1:5 — Applied to demonstrate Christ's superiority
         over angels.
v.7   — Hebrews 5:5 — Applied to Christ's priestly appointment.
v.8   — Philippians 2:9-11 — "God also hath highly exalted him,
         and given him a name which is above every name." The
         universal inheritance fulfilled.
v.9   — Revelation 2:27 — "He shall rule them with a rod of iron;
         as the vessels of a potter shall they be broken." Applied
         to the glorified church sharing Christ's authority.
v.9   — Revelation 19:15 — Christ returns with "a rod of iron" to
         smite the nations.
v.12  — Psalm 34:8 — "O taste and see that the LORD is good:
         blessed is the man that trusteth in him." Same refuge
         theology.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1 — KJV: "Why do the heathen rage" / ESV: "Why do the nations
      rage" — Hebrew "goyim" simply means nations/peoples. KJV
      "heathen" carries a pejorative connotation not present in
      the Hebrew. ESV "nations" is more accurate.

v.1 — KJV: "imagine a vain thing" / ESV: "plot in vain" — Hebrew
      "hagah" (same word as "meditate" in 1:2!) — here used
      negatively: the nations "mutter/plot" emptiness, just as the
      righteous "mutter/meditate" on Torah. Same activity, opposite
      content.

v.6 — KJV: "Yet have I set my king upon my holy hill of Zion" /
      ESV: "As for me, I have set my King on Zion, my holy hill"
      — ESV's "As for me" emphasizes the contrast: the nations
      plot, but AS FOR ME (God) — I have already installed my King.
      The rebellion is futile because the enthronement is past
      tense — already accomplished.

v.12 — KJV: "Kiss the Son" / ESV: "Kiss the Son" — Hebrew "bar"
       (Aramaic for "son") rather than the expected Hebrew "ben."
       Some scholars see this as evidence of early date or
       deliberate word choice. "Kiss" = an act of homage and
       submission to a ruler.

v.12 — KJV: "put their trust in him" / ESV: "take refuge in him"
       — ESV's "take refuge" captures the Hebrew "chasah" better
       — it implies running TO someone for shelter, not merely
       intellectual belief. Trust as active dependence.

v.1 — ASV: "peoples meditate" for KJV's "people imagine"
       NET: "countries devising plots that will fail?" for KJV's "people imagine a vain thing?"
       WEB: "peoples plot" for KJV's "people imagine"
v.6 — NET: "installed" for KJV's "I set"
       WEB: "King on" for KJV's "king upon"
v.12 — ASV: adds "Psalm 3 A Psalm of David, when he fled from Absalom his son."
       NET: "you will die because of your behavior," for KJV's "ye perish from the way,"
       WEB: "will soon be kindled." for KJV's "is kindled but a little."

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Human rebellion against God is absurd (vv.1-4). The Creator of
   the universe does not tremble at human conspiracy. He laughs.
   Not because suffering is trivial, but because the outcome was
   never in doubt. When the world seems chaotic, remember: God is
   not worried.

2. God's plan cannot be thwarted (v.6). "I HAVE set my King" —
   past tense, accomplished. The enthronement of Christ is not
   pending human permission. It is done. Opposition does not delay
   God's purposes.

3. "Kiss the Son" — submission is wisdom (vv.10-12). The psalm
   does not end with wrath but with invitation. Kings are warned:
   be wise, submit, serve with reverent joy. The alternative is
   not punishment imposed but natural consequence — you "perish
   from the way" by choosing to oppose the way.

4. The psalm ends with "blessed" (v.12b). This connects back to
   Psalm 1:1. The double blessing frames the introduction to the
   Psalter: blessed is the one who walks in Torah AND takes refuge
   in God's Anointed. Both are needed — obedience and trust.

5. Refuge is the right response to divine power (v.12). The
   appropriate reaction to a God this powerful is not terror but
   refuge. His power becomes your protection when you run TO Him
   rather than FROM Him.
