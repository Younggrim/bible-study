Psalms - Chapter 118
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A Hallel thanksgiving psalm — processional liturgy: 'This is the day which the LORD hath made'; 'the stone which the builders refused is become the head stone of the corner'; 'Blessed is he that cometh in the name of the LORD.'

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Hallel Psalm / Messianic Thanksgiving
Attributed Author: Anonymous (possibly Davidic; final psalm of
the Egyptian Hallel)
Key Themes: God's enduring mercy, trust over human confidence,
the rejected stone becoming the cornerstone, the day the LORD
has made, Messianic triumph through rejection, processional
worship

Historical Context: Psalm 118 concludes the Egyptian Hallel
(113-118) and is the last psalm Jesus sang before Gethsemane
(Matt 26:30). It is heavily Messianic — v.22 (the rejected stone)
is quoted by Jesus (Matt 21:42), Peter (Acts 4:11; 1 Pet 2:7),
and Paul (Eph 2:20). Verse 26 ("Blessed is he who comes in the
name of the LORD") was shouted at Jesus' Triumphal Entry (Matt
21:9) and referenced in His lament over Jerusalem (Matt 23:39).
Martin Luther called this psalm his favorite: "This is my psalm,
my chosen psalm. I love them all... but this one is nearest my
heart." The psalm moves from personal testimony through threat
and deliverance to the temple gates, culminating in Messianic
prophecy.

Structure:
- Call to Thanksgiving (vv.1-4)
- Personal Testimony of Deliverance (vv.5-18)
- The Gate of Righteousness (vv.19-21)
- The Rejected Cornerstone (vv.22-24)
- Hosanna and Blessing (vv.25-27)
- Personal Praise and Closing (vv.28-29)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.6  — "The LORD is on my side; I will not fear: what can man do
        unto me?" (KJV) — If God is FOR you, human opposition is
        irrelevant. Not that men can't harm you — but that their
        harm cannot ultimately prevail against God's purpose.

v.8  — "It is better to trust in the LORD than to put confidence
        in man." (KJV) — One of Scripture's most direct comparative
        statements. Human reliability vs. divine reliability is no
        contest. Every disappointment in human trust confirms this.

v.22 — "The stone which the builders refused is become the head
        stone of the corner." (KJV) — Profoundly Messianic. Jesus
        was rejected by Israel's leaders (the builders) but became
        the cornerstone of God's building. What experts discard,
        God makes foundational. Rejection is not the end —
        vindication follows.

v.24 — "This is the day which the LORD hath made; we will rejoice
        and be glad in it." (KJV) — Often quoted generically, but
        in context "this day" refers to the day of God's dramatic
        reversal — the rejected stone becoming the cornerstone.
        Ultimately: the resurrection day. THAT is the day the LORD
        has made.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.22 — "head stone of the corner" / "cornerstone" — Hebrew "rosh
       pinnah" = head of the corner. The most important stone in
       a building — either the foundation stone that determines all
       angles, or the capstone that crowns the structure. Either
       way: the supreme position.

v.25 — "Save now, I beseech thee" — Hebrew "hoshi'a na" =
       HOSANNA. The Palm Sunday cry comes directly from this verse.
       "Hosanna" is not merely an exclamation of praise but a
       prayer: "Save us NOW!"

v.27 — KJV: "bind the sacrifice with cords, even unto the horns
       of the altar" — Imagery of the festal sacrifice being bound
       to the altar. Prophetically: Christ, the sacrifice, bound
       to the cross-altar.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.1 — KJV: "his mercy endureth for ever" / ASV: "his lovingkindness endureth for ever" / WEB: "his loving kindness endures forever" / NET: "his loyal love endures" — The psalm's refrain repeats this chesed formula four times (vv.1-4). Each translation applies its standard rendering consistently.
- v.5 — WEB: "Out of my distress, I called on Yah. Yah answered me with freedom" — WEB uses the shortened divine name "Yah" and renders "large place" (KJV) as "freedom," interpreting spatial metaphor as liberation.
- v.8-9 — ASV/WEB: "take refuge in Jehovah/Yahweh" vs. KJV: "trust in the LORD" vs. NET: "take shelter in the Lord" — Hebrew "chasah" means to seek refuge/shelter, not merely trust. KJV softens the imagery; ASV/WEB/NET preserve the protective metaphor.
- v.10-12 — KJV: "I will destroy them" / ASV/WEB: "I will cut them off" / NET: "I pushed them away" — NET notably softens the violence, using past tense "pushed away" where KJV declares future destruction. Hebrew "amil" = circumcise/cut off.
- v.14 — WEB: "Yah is my strength and song" — Uses the short form "Yah" as in the Song of Moses (Ex 15:2). NET paraphrases: "The Lord gives me strength and protects me."
- v.15 — KJV: "tabernacles" / ASV/WEB: "tents" / NET: "tents" — KJV's "tabernacles" evokes worship; "tents" (literal Hebrew "ohel") is more accurate for dwelling places of the righteous.
- v.22 — KJV: "The stone which the builders refused" / ASV/WEB: "rejected" / NET: "discarded" — NET's "discarded" implies the stone was thrown away, stronger than "refused/rejected." This Messianic verse (quoted in Matt 21:42) carries different weight depending on translation choice.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "What can man do unto me?" (v.6). Fear of man is neutralized
   by confidence in God. When God is on your side, no human
   opposition is ultimate. This doesn't promise comfort — it
   promises victory.

2. Trust God over princes (vv.8-9). Every human institution will
   eventually fail you. Politicians, leaders, systems — all
   are limited. Only God's faithfulness is absolute.

3. The rejected stone wins (v.22). If you've been rejected,
   overlooked, discarded by "builders" — remember: God specializes
   in making rejected things foundational. What humans refuse,
   God exalts.

4. "This is the day" (v.24). Not just any day — the day of God's
   reversal, the day of resurrection hope. Rejoice in it
   specifically because GOD MADE IT HAPPEN, not because
   circumstances are easy.

5. "Hosanna — save now!" (v.25). This is not a casual song. It's
   a desperate prayer: SAVE US NOW, LORD. The Triumphal Entry
   crowd was praying Psalm 118 — and the Answer was riding on
   a donkey before them.
