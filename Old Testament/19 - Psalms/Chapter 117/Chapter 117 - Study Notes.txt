Psalms - Chapter 117
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
The shortest psalm and chapter in the Bible — a two-verse call for all nations to praise the LORD for His steadfast love and faithfulness that endure forever.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Hymn of Praise / Hallel Psalm
Attributed Author: Anonymous
Key Themes: Universal praise from all nations, God's mercy
(hesed) toward His people, God's eternal truth, the brevity
and completeness of praise

Historical Context: Psalm 117 is the shortest chapter in the
Bible — only 2 verses, 33 words in Hebrew. Yet it carries
enormous theological weight. It is also the middle chapter of the
Bible (with 594 chapters before it and 594 after). It calls ALL
NATIONS and ALL PEOPLES to praise Israel's God — making it one
of the most universalist statements in the Old Testament. Paul
quotes this psalm in Romans 15:11 as proof that God always
intended to include the Gentiles in His redemptive plan. Despite
its brevity, it contains both reasons for praise: God's hesed
(merciful kindness/steadfast love) and His emet (truth/
faithfulness). Small psalm, infinite theology.

Structure:
- Universal Call to Praise (v.1)
- Reason for Praise: Hesed and Emet (v.2a)
- Closing Hallelujah (v.2b)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "O praise the LORD, all ye nations: praise him, all ye
        people." (KJV) — Not just Israel — ALL nations, ALL
        peoples. The scope of praise is universal. God's plan was
        never exclusively ethnic — He always intended all nations
        to know and praise Him.

v.2  — "For his merciful kindness is great toward us: and the
        truth of the LORD endureth for ever." (KJV) — Two eternal
        attributes: (1) His hesed (merciful kindness/steadfast
        love) is GREAT — literally "mighty, prevailing" over us.
        (2) His truth endures FOREVER. Love and truth — the
        complete character of God in one verse.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.1   — Romans 15:11 — Paul quotes this verse directly: "Praise
         the Lord, all ye Gentiles; and laud him, all ye people."
v.1   — Revelation 7:9 — "A great multitude, which no man could
         number, of all nations" praising God.
v.2   — Psalm 100:5 — "His mercy is everlasting; and his truth
         endureth to all generations."
v.2   — John 1:14 — "Full of grace and truth" — Christ embodies
         hesed and emet.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.2 — KJV: "his merciful kindness is great toward us" / ESV: "his
      steadfast love is great toward us" — Hebrew "gavar" = mighty,
      prevailing. God's hesed doesn't just exist — it PREVAILS over
      us, conquers us, overwhelms us. It is actively powerful.

v.2 — "truth" (emet) = firmness, reliability, faithfulness. God's
      truth is not merely factual accuracy — it is His unwavering
      reliability and constancy.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.1 — ASV: "Jehovah" / WEB: "Yahweh" / NET: "the Lord" — Even in this shortest Bible chapter (2 verses), the divine name translation choice is visible, appearing twice.
- v.1 — KJV: "all ye people" / ASV: "all ye peoples" / NET: "all you foreigners" / WEB: "all you peoples" — NET's "foreigners" interprets Hebrew "ummim" (peoples/nations) to emphasize the outsider perspective. The plural "peoples" in ASV/WEB is more precise than KJV's singular.
- v.2 — KJV: "his merciful kindness is great toward us" / ASV: "his lovingkindness is great toward us" / WEB: "his loving kindness is great toward us" / NET: "his loyal love towers over us" — Classic chesed difference. NET's "towers over" adds vertical imagery (Hebrew "gabar" = be strong/prevail) absent from other translations.
- v.2 — KJV: "the truth of the LORD endureth for ever" / ASV: "the truth of Jehovah" / WEB: "Yahweh's faithfulness" / NET: "the Lord's faithfulness" — Hebrew "emet" rendered as "truth" (KJV/ASV) vs. "faithfulness" (WEB/NET). Both are valid; "faithfulness" emphasizes relational constancy.
- v.1 — NET: "Applaud him" / ASV: "Laud him" / WEB: "Extol him" / KJV: "praise him" — Four different renderings of Hebrew "shabach" (to praise/commend). NET's "applaud" is strikingly modern and physical.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. The shortest chapter contains the widest scope. Two verses
   encompass all nations, all peoples, infinite love, and eternal
   truth. Sometimes the most profound truths need the fewest
   words.

2. God's love prevails (v.2). His hesed doesn't merely reach you
   — it OVERPOWERS you, conquers your resistance, overwhelms
   your unworthiness. You cannot out-sin His love.

3. All nations will praise (v.1). This is prophetic. One day every
   tongue will confess. The church's mission to all peoples is
   fulfilling what this tiny psalm declares.

4. Truth endures forever (v.2). In an age of relativism and
   shifting "truths," God's truth stands forever. Unshakeable,
   unchanging, enduring through every generation.
