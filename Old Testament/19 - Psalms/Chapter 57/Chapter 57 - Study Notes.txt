Psalms - Chapter 57
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A lament turning to praise — David hides in a cave from Saul, taking refuge under God's wings until destruction passes; his heart is steadfast: 'Awake, my glory! I will praise thee among the nations.'

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Individual Lament / Confidence Psalm
Attributed Author: David
Key Themes: Refuge under God's wings, steadfast heart, praise in
the midst of danger, God's glory above all, faithfulness and mercy
reaching to the heavens

Historical Context: The superscription places this psalm when David
fled from Saul into a cave (1 Samuel 22:1 — Cave of Adullam, or
1 Samuel 24:1-3 — Cave of En Gedi). Set to "Do Not Destroy" (Al
Tashheth), which may reference David's refusal to destroy Saul when
he had the opportunity. In the cave — literally underground, hiding
for his life — David's heart is "fixed" (steadfast) and he resolves
to praise. This Miktam (possibly "golden psalm" or "inscription
psalm") moves from desperate refuge-seeking to exuberant worship,
demonstrating how faith transforms the darkest circumstances into
occasions for praise.

Structure:
- Cry for Mercy and Refuge (vv.1-3)
- Description of Enemies (v.4)
- First Refrain: Be Exalted, O God (v.5)
- Enemy Trap Reversed (v.6)
- Steadfast Heart and Praise (vv.7-10)
- Second Refrain: Be Exalted, O God (v.11)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "Be merciful unto me, O God, be merciful unto me: for my
        soul trusteth in thee: yea, in the shadow of thy wings
        will I make my refuge, until these calamities be overpast."
        (KJV) — The double "be merciful" expresses urgency. "Shadow
        of thy wings" — like a mother bird sheltering her young.
        "Until these calamities be overpast" — storms are temporal;
        God's refuge is permanent. David expects the trial to end.

v.5  — "Be thou exalted, O God, above the heavens; let thy glory
        be above all the earth." (KJV) — The refrain (repeated in
        v.11). Even in a cave, David's vision is cosmic. His
        prayer extends beyond personal deliverance to God's
        universal glory. True worship looks beyond self.

v.7  — "My heart is fixed, O God, my heart is fixed: I will sing
        and give praise." (KJV) — "Fixed" (nachon) = established,
        prepared, steadfast. The double declaration is emphatic.
        David's circumstances are chaotic, but his heart is
        settled. A fixed heart can sing in any situation.

v.10 — "For thy mercy is great unto the heavens, and thy truth
        unto the clouds." (KJV) — God's mercy (hesed) and truth
        (emeth) are immeasurable — they reach from earth to heaven.
        No situation is beyond the reach of God's covenant love.

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1 — KJV: "until these calamities be overpast" / ESV: "till the
      storms of destruction pass by" — Hebrew "havvot" = destruction,
      calamity, ruin. ESV's "storms" adds vivid meteorological
      imagery not explicitly in the Hebrew but captures the sense
      of passing turbulence.

v.7 — KJV: "My heart is fixed" / ESV: "My heart is steadfast" —
      Hebrew "nachon" = firm, established, prepared. Both work well.
      "Steadfast" (ESV) emphasizes endurance; "fixed" (KJV)
      emphasizes immovability.

v.8 — KJV: "Awake up, my glory" / ESV: "Awake, my glory!" —
      "Glory" here likely means David's soul or his tongue (the
      instrument of praise). He commands his whole being to rise
      and worship. "I myself will awake early" / "I will awake the
      dawn" — David's praise is so eager it precedes sunrise.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. God's wings are a refuge until storms pass (v.1). The storms
   WILL pass. This is temporary. God's protection is the shelter
   during the storm, not necessarily the removal of the storm.
   Wait it out under His wings.

2. A fixed heart can praise in any circumstance (v.7). David is
   in a cave, hunted, uncertain of his future — and his heart is
   "fixed." Stability of heart is not a product of stable
   circumstances but of settled trust.

3. Enemies' traps become their own judgment (v.6). Those who dig
   pits for the righteous fall into them. God turns evil
   strategies against their authors. You don't need to avenge
   yourself — wickedness is self-defeating.

4. Worship should be cosmic in scope (v.5). Even personal prayers
   should lift our eyes to God's universal glory. Our problems are
   real, but God is bigger than our problems. Let worship expand
   your vision beyond your cave.

5. Wake up early to praise (v.8). "I will awake the dawn" —
   don't let the day get ahead of your worship. The first voice
   of your morning should be praise, not anxiety.
