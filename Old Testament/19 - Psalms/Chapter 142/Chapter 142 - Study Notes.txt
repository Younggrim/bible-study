Psalms - Chapter 142
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A lament — David's prayer from the cave (of Adullam or En-gedi): 'I cried unto the LORD; no man cared for my soul'; God is his refuge and portion in the land of the living; he pleads for deliverance from his prison.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Individual Lament / Prayer from the Cave
Attributed Author: David (superscription: "Maschil of David; A
Prayer when he was in the cave")
Key Themes: Complete isolation, God as sole refuge, pouring out
complaints before God, no human help available, prayer from
confinement, hope for future praise

Historical Context: Psalm 142 comes from David in a cave —
either Adullam (1 Sam 22) or En-gedi (1 Sam 24) while fleeing
Saul. It is one of Scripture's most honest prayers of isolation:
"No man cared for my soul" (v.4). David has no one — no allies,
no escape, no human help. In this total abandonment, God becomes
his ONLY refuge. The psalm is a model for prayer when you are
completely alone and utterly overwhelmed.

Structure:
- Vocal Prayer and Complaint (vv.1-2)
- God Knows When I'm Overwhelmed (v.3)
- Total Isolation (v.4)
- God as Only Refuge (v.5)
- Plea for Deliverance (vv.6-7)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.3  — "When my spirit was overwhelmed within me, then thou
        knewest my path." (KJV) — When you can't see the way,
        GOD KNOWS your path. Your overwhelm doesn't obscure God's
        vision. He sees clearly what you cannot.

v.4  — "I looked on my right hand, and beheld, but there was no
        man that would know me: refuge failed me; no man cared for
        my soul." (KJV) — Total isolation. No ally on the right
        (the position of defender). No refuge. No one who cared.
        This is the loneliest verse in the Psalms.

v.5  — "I cried unto thee, O LORD: I said, Thou art my refuge
        and my portion in the land of the living." (KJV) — When
        no one else is there, GOD is refuge and portion. In the
        land of the living — not just in eternity but HERE, NOW.

v.7  — "Bring my soul out of prison, that I may praise thy name."
        (KJV) — The purpose of deliverance: PRAISE. David doesn't
        want freedom for comfort — he wants it to praise God
        publicly. Future praise motivates present prayer.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.1   — 1 Samuel 22:1; 24:3 — David in the cave.
v.3   — Psalm 77:3 — "My spirit was overwhelmed."
v.4   — 2 Timothy 4:16 — "No man stood with me." Paul

v.5   — Psalm 16:5 — "The LORD is the portion of mine

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.3 — KJV: "When my spirit was overwhelmed within me, then thou knewest my path" / NET: "Even when my strength leaves me, you watch my footsteps" — NET interprets "spirit overwhelmed" as loss of strength and "knewest my path" as "watch my footsteps," adding protective attentiveness.
- v.4 — KJV: "there was no man that would know me: refuge failed me; no man cared for my soul" / NET: "No one cares about me. I have nowhere to run; no one is concerned about my life" — NET modernizes the threefold complaint. "Refuge failed" becomes "nowhere to run," emphasizing isolation.
- v.5 — KJV: "my refuge and my portion" / ASV: "my refuge, My portion" / WEB: "my refuge, my portion" / NET: "my shelter, my security" — NET replaces biblical "portion" (inheritance language) with "security," losing the covenant allotment imagery.
- v.6 — KJV: "I am brought very low" / WEB: "I am in desperate need" / NET: "I am in serious trouble" — Hebrew "dalal" = brought low/impoverished. WEB and NET interpret the condition rather than translating the metaphor.
- v.7 — KJV: "Bring my soul out of prison" / NET: "Free me from prison" — Both preserve the prison imagery. David wrote this from the cave of Adullam, where the cave became metaphorical prison.
- v.7 — KJV: "the righteous shall compass me about" / WEB: "The righteous will surround me" / NET: "Because of me the godly will assemble" — NET adds causation ("because of me") and changes "surround" to "assemble," implying celebration rather than encircling fellowship.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. God knows your path when you don't (v.3). Being overwhelmed
   doesn't mean being lost. Your confusion is not God's confusion.
   He sees clearly even when you're in the cave.

2. "No man cared for my soul" (v.4). If you've felt this — know
   David felt it too. And the answer is v.5: GOD is your refuge.
   Human abandonment drives you to divine sufficiency.

3. Pray FROM the cave (v.1). Don't wait until you're out to pray.
   The cave, the prison, the lowest place — that's where prayer
   happens most honestly.

4. Freedom is for praise (v.7). The purpose of your deliverance
   is not just personal relief — it's that others will gather
   around and hear your testimony. Get free so you can praise.
