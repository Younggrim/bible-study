Psalms - Chapter 60
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A communal lament after military defeat — God has rejected Israel and broken them, yet David cries for help: 'Give us aid against the enemy, for the help of man is vain.' God declares victory over surrounding nations.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Community Lament / Royal Psalm
Attributed Author: David
Key Themes: National defeat and divine discipline, God's
sovereignty over all nations, hope after setback, the futility of
human help, confidence in God's ultimate victory

Historical Context: The superscription places this psalm during
David's wars in the northeast (Aram-naharaim and Aram-zobah) when
Joab returned and struck 12,000 of Edom in the Valley of Salt
(2 Samuel 8:13; 1 Chronicles 18:12). While David fought in the
north, Edom attacked from the south, causing an initial defeat
that felt like divine rejection. The psalm is a Miktam set to
"Shushan Eduth" (Lily of the Testimony) and is marked "for
teaching." It moves from lament over defeat to a divine oracle of
sovereignty to renewed confidence. Verses 6-12 reappear nearly
verbatim in Psalm 108:7-13.

Structure:
- Lament Over National Defeat (vv.1-3)
- A Banner of Hope (vv.4-5)
- God's Oracle of Sovereignty (vv.6-8)
- Question of Faith (vv.9-10)
- Confidence in God Alone (vv.11-12)


CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.1   — 2 Samuel 8:13 — Joab's victory over Edom in the Valley
         of Salt, the historical context.
v.4   — Isaiah 11:10 — "There shall be a root of Jesse, which
         shall stand for an ensign of the people." Christ as the
         ultimate banner.
v.6-8 — Psalm 108:7-13 — Nearly identical repetition of these
         verses in a later psalm.
v.8   — Genesis 25:23 — "The elder shall serve the younger." God's
         sovereignty over Edom (Esau) fulfilled here.
v.11  — Psalm 146:3 — "Put not your trust in princes." Same
         warning against human reliance.
v.12  — Philippians 4:13 — "I can do all things through Christ
         which strengtheneth me." Same principle of divine
         empowerment.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------

REFLECTION
------------------------------------
1. God can use defeat to teach dependence (vv.1-3). Israel's
   initial loss drove them back to God. Sometimes God allows
   setbacks to break our self-sufficiency and remind us that
   victory comes from Him alone.

2. God is sovereign over nations we consider enemies (vv.6-8).
   No nation, no opposition, no human power is outside God's
   control. Moab, Edom, Philistia — all are His to command. Your
   "enemies" are already under His authority.

3. Human help is vain without God (v.11). Alliances, strategies,
   resources — all empty without divine backing. This doesn't mean
   don't plan; it means don't trust in planning AS God.

4. Through God, we do valiantly (v.12). The partnership: God
   empowers, we act. Neither passive waiting nor self-reliant
   striving — but God-dependent, courageous action.
