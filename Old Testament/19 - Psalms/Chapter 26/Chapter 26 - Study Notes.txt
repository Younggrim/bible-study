Psalms - Chapter 26
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A psalm of integrity — David asks God to examine and test him, professing he has walked in faithfulness, avoided the wicked, and loves the house where God's glory dwells.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Protestation of Innocence / Individual Lament
Attributed Author: David — "A Psalm of David"
Key Themes: Integrity, self-examination before God, separation
from evildoers, love for God's house, worship at the altar,
standing on level ground

Historical Context: Like Psalms 7 and 17, David protests his
innocence and invites divine examination. He has been accused or
associated with wrongdoers and asks God to vindicate his integrity.
The psalm emphasizes David's CHOICES: who he does and does not
associate with (vv.4-5), his love for the temple (v.8), and his
determination to walk in integrity (v.11). It connects closely to
Psalm 15's entrance requirements — David claims to meet those
qualifications. The "washing hands" (v.6) likely refers to the
ritual washing before approaching the altar, but metaphorically
to moral preparation for worship.

Structure:
- Appeal for Vindication (vv.1-3)
- Separation from Evil Associates (vv.4-5)
- Approach to God's Altar (vv.6-8)
- Prayer Not to Share Sinners' Fate (vv.9-10)
- Renewed Commitment to Integrity (vv.11-12)


CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.1   — Psalm 7:8 — "Judge me, O LORD, according to my
         righteousness." Same appeal for vindication.
v.2   — Psalm 139:23-24 — "Search me, O God, and know my heart."
         Same invitation for divine examination.
v.4   — Psalm 1:1 — "Blessed is the man that walketh not in the
         counsel of the ungodly." Same separation principle.
v.6   — Psalm 73:13 — "I have cleansed my heart in vain, and
         washed my hands in innocency." Asaph echoes David's words
         in a crisis of faith.
v.8   — Psalm 27:4 — "One thing have I desired of the LORD...
         to behold the beauty of the LORD." Same temple-love.
v.8   — Psalm 84:1-2 — "How amiable are thy tabernacles!" Same
         longing for God's house.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------

REFLECTION
------------------------------------
1. Invite God's examination (v.2). If you are innocent, welcome
   scrutiny. If you cannot invite God to test your heart, that
   itself is diagnostic. Confidence before God is not arrogance —
   it is the fruit of integrity.

2. Who you sit with matters (vv.4-5). David is defined partly by
   who he refuses to associate with. Not isolation but
   discernment. You become like your company. Choose your
   companions with the same care you choose your commitments.

3. Love God's house (v.8). Not dutiful attendance but genuine
   affection. When you truly love the place where God is
   worshipped, attendance is not obligation but delight. Examine
   whether your worship attendance flows from love or duty.

4. Worship requires preparation (v.6). "Washing hands" before
   approaching the altar — worship is not casual. There is
   something to be PUT RIGHT before we come to God. Not
   perfection, but intentional preparation.

5. Stand on level ground (v.12). After examination, after
   separation from evil, after worship — David stands on stable
   ground. Integrity produces stability. When your life is honest,
   your footing is sure.
