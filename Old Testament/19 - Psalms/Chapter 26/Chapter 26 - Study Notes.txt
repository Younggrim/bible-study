Psalms - Chapter 26
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A psalm of integrity — David asks God to examine and test him, professing he has walked in faithfulness, avoided the wicked, and loves the house where God's glory dwells.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Protestation of Innocence / Individual Lament
Attributed Author: David — "A Psalm of David"
Key Themes: Integrity, self-examination before God, separation
from evildoers, love for God's house, worship at the altar,
standing on level ground

Historical Context: Like Psalms 7 and 17, David protests his
innocence and invites divine examination. He has been accused or
associated with wrongdoers and asks God to vindicate his integrity.
The psalm emphasizes David's CHOICES: who he does and does not
associate with (vv.4-5), his love for the temple (v.8), and his
determination to walk in integrity (v.11). It connects closely to
Psalm 15's entrance requirements — David claims to meet those
qualifications. The "washing hands" (v.6) likely refers to the
ritual washing before approaching the altar, but metaphorically
to moral preparation for worship.

Structure:
- Appeal for Vindication (vv.1-3)
- Separation from Evil Associates (vv.4-5)
- Approach to God's Altar (vv.6-8)
- Prayer Not to Share Sinners' Fate (vv.9-10)
- Renewed Commitment to Integrity (vv.11-12)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "Examine me, O LORD, and prove me; try my reins and my
        heart." (KJV) — An invitation for divine investigation.
        David is confident enough in his integrity to ask God to
        test his innermost being. This is not arrogance — it is the
        confidence of a clean conscience on the specific matter.

v.8  — "LORD, I have loved the habitation of thy house, and the
        place where thine honour dwelleth." (KJV) — David's love
        for God's house — the place of worship, sacrifice, and
        encounter. Not obligation but affection. He doesn't attend
        — he LOVES.

v.12 — "My foot standeth in an even place: in the congregations
        will I bless the LORD." (KJV) — "Even place" (mishor) =
        level ground, straight path. After all the examination and
        protestation, David stands on stable, honest ground and
        worships publicly.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.1   — Psalm 7:8 — "Judge me, O LORD, according to my
         righteousness." Same appeal for vindication.
v.2   — Psalm 139:23-24 — "Search me, O God, and know my heart."
         Same invitation for divine examination.
v.4   — Psalm 1:1 — "Blessed is the man that walketh not in the
         counsel of the ungodly." Same separation principle.
v.6   — Psalm 73:13 — "I have cleansed my heart in vain, and
         washed my hands in innocency." Asaph echoes David's words
         in a crisis of faith.
v.8   — Psalm 27:4 — "One thing have I desired of the LORD...
         to behold the beauty of the LORD." Same temple-love.
v.8   — Psalm 84:1-2 — "How amiable are thy tabernacles!" Same
         longing for God's house.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1 — KJV: "Judge me" / ESV: "Vindicate me" — Hebrew "shafat" =
      judge. ESV clarifies the intent: David is not asking for
      punishment but for favorable judgment — vindication. He wants
      God's verdict to clear his name.

v.4 — KJV: "vain persons... dissemblers" / ESV: "men of
      falsehood... hypocrites" — KJV "dissemblers" = those who
      conceal their true nature. ESV "hypocrites" is the modern
      equivalent. David avoids the company of pretenders.

v.6 — KJV: "I will wash mine hands in innocency" / ESV: "I wash
      my hands in innocence" — Allusion to Deut 21:6-7 (ritual
      handwashing declaring innocence of bloodshed) and priestly
      washing before altar service (Exod 30:19-21).

v.9 — KJV: "Gather not my soul with sinners" / ESV: "Do not sweep
      my soul away with sinners" — David asks not to share the fate
      of the wicked. His association with integrity should separate
      his destiny from theirs.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.1 — KJV: "I shall not slide" / ASV/WEB: "without wavering" / NET: "without wavering" — KJV's "slide" (Hebrew "ma'ad" = slip, totter) is passive — I won't be moved. ASV/WEB/NET use "without wavering," emphasizing active, sustained trust rather than passive stability.

- v.2 — KJV: "try my reins and my heart" / ASV: "Try my heart and my mind" / NET: "Evaluate my inner thoughts and motives" — KJV's "reins" (kidneys) was the Hebrew seat of deep emotions. ASV updates to "mind"; NET fully interprets as "inner thoughts and motives," making explicit what the Hebrew imagery implies.

- v.3 — KJV/ASV: "thy lovingkindness" / WEB: "your loving kindness" / NET: "your faithfulness... your loyalty" — Hebrew "chesed" rendered as lovingkindness (KJV/ASV), loving kindness (WEB), but NET splits it into two concepts: "faithfulness" and "loyalty." NET also adds "continually motivates me" where Hebrew simply says "I have walked in thy truth."

- v.6 — NET: "I maintain a pure lifestyle, so I can appear before your altar" — NET interprets the ritual handwashing imagery as "maintaining a pure lifestyle," losing the specific liturgical allusion to priestly washing. ASV/WEB retain "wash my hands in innocency/innocence," preserving the temple ritual connection.

- v.8 — KJV: "the place where thine honour dwelleth" / ASV/WEB: "the place where thy/your glory dwelleth/dwells" / NET: "the place where your splendor is revealed" — Hebrew "kavod" = glory, weight, honor. NET's "splendor is revealed" adds a dynamic element not in the Hebrew, which simply states glory "dwells" (shakan — tabernacles) there.

- v.9 — NET: "Do not sweep me away with sinners, or execute me along with violent people" — NET's "execute me" is far more dramatic than KJV's "Gather not my soul." Hebrew "asaph" = gather, collect. NET interprets the gathering of souls as execution — possible but significantly more interpretive than the original.

- v.12 — KJV: "in the congregations will I bless the LORD" / ASV: "In the congregations will I bless Jehovah" / WEB: "In the congregations I will bless Yahweh" / NET: "among the worshipers I will praise the Lord" — NET's "worshipers" interprets "congregations" (maqhelim = assemblies). ASV/WEB use the divine name, reminding readers that corporate worship addresses YHWH by name.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Invite God's examination (v.2). If you are innocent, welcome
   scrutiny. If you cannot invite God to test your heart, that
   itself is diagnostic. Confidence before God is not arrogance —
   it is the fruit of integrity.

2. Who you sit with matters (vv.4-5). David is defined partly by
   who he refuses to associate with. Not isolation but
   discernment. You become like your company. Choose your
   companions with the same care you choose your commitments.

3. Love God's house (v.8). Not dutiful attendance but genuine
   affection. When you truly love the place where God is
   worshipped, attendance is not obligation but delight. Examine
   whether your worship attendance flows from love or duty.

4. Worship requires preparation (v.6). "Washing hands" before
   approaching the altar — worship is not casual. There is
   something to be PUT RIGHT before we come to God. Not
   perfection, but intentional preparation.

5. Stand on level ground (v.12). After examination, after
   separation from evil, after worship — David stands on stable
   ground. Integrity produces stability. When your life is honest,
   your footing is sure.
