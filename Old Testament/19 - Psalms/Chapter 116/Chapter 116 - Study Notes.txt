Psalms - Chapter 116
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A Hallel thanksgiving psalm — 'I love the LORD, because he hath heard my voice'; the psalmist was near death but God delivered him; 'What shall I render unto the LORD? I will take the cup of salvation and call upon his name.'

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Individual Thanksgiving / Hallel Psalm
Attributed Author: Anonymous (part of the Egyptian Hallel)
Key Themes: Love for God because He heard prayer, deliverance
from death, rest for the soul, paying vows publicly, the
preciousness of saints' deaths to God, gratitude in worship

Historical Context: Psalm 116 is part of the Egyptian Hallel
(113-118) sung at Passover. It is deeply personal — the psalmist
nearly died and was rescued by God. The psalm's language of
deliverance from death, taking the "cup of salvation" (v.13), and
paying vows in the presence of God's people makes it especially
meaningful in the context of Jesus' last Passover. When Jesus took
the cup at the Last Supper, this psalm would have been fresh on
His lips — yet for Him, the "sorrows of death" would not be
averted but embraced. Paul quotes v.10 in 2 Corinthians 4:13:
"I believed, therefore have I spoken."

Structure:
- Love for God Who Heard (vv.1-2)
- Deliverance from Death (vv.3-4)
- God's Character (vv.5-6)
- Return to Rest (vv.7-9)
- Faith Expressed (vv.10-11)
- Response of Gratitude (vv.12-19)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.12 — "What shall I render unto the LORD for all his benefits
        toward me?" (KJV) — The central question of gratitude.
        What can you GIVE BACK to God for what He's given you?
        The answer follows: take the cup, call on His name, pay
        your vows, offer thanksgiving. You can't repay — but you
        can respond.

v.13 — "I will take the cup of salvation, and call upon the name
        of the LORD." (KJV) — The cup of salvation is received,
        not earned. You don't brew it — you TAKE it. And the
        response: call on His name. Eucharistic overtones are
        unmistakable — the Lord's Supper is this verse enacted.

v.15 — "Precious in the sight of the LORD is the death of his
        saints." (KJV) — Often misunderstood. "Precious" (Hebrew
        "yaqar") = costly, weighty, significant. God does not
        treat the death of His faithful ones lightly. Every
        believer's death matters immensely to God.

v.7  — "Return unto thy rest, O my soul; for the LORD hath dealt
        bountifully with thee." (KJV) — The soul is commanded to
        rest. After crisis and deliverance, the soul needs to
        settle back into trust. "The LORD has dealt bountifully" —
        past tense, proven, done.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.11 — KJV: "I said in my haste, All men are liars" / ESV: "I
       said in my alarm, 'All mankind are liars'" — Hebrew
       "chaphaz" = alarm, haste, panic. In his panic, the psalmist
       concluded all men are unreliable. Not cool theological
       reflection but a crisis statement — yet theologically true
       (Rom 3:4).

v.15 — "Precious" (yaqar) = costly, heavy, rare. Not "beautiful"
       but "weighty." God considers the death of His saints a
       matter of great consequence — He does not allow it
       carelessly.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.3 — KJV: "the pains of hell gat hold upon me" / ASV/WEB: "the pains of Sheol" / NET: "the snares of Sheol" — KJV translates "Sheol" as "hell," obscuring the Hebrew concept of the underworld/grave. ASV and WEB preserve "Sheol" as a proper noun. NET adds interpretive "snares" for Hebrew "metsarim" (straits/distress).
- v.3 — KJV: "The sorrows of death" / ASV/WEB: "The cords of death" / NET: "The ropes of death" — Hebrew "chebel" means cord/rope, not sorrow. KJV follows a textual variant; ASV/WEB/NET follow the more likely reading of death's binding cords.
- v.6 — NET: "The Lord protects the untrained" — Interprets KJV/ASV/WEB's "the simple" (Hebrew "pethi" = naive, inexperienced) as "untrained," adding a pedagogical nuance absent from other translations.
- v.12 — NET: "How can I repay the Lord for all his acts of kindness to me?" — Paraphrases KJV's "What shall I render unto the LORD for all his benefits toward me?" NET turns "benefits" into "acts of kindness," making it more relational.
- v.13 — NET: "I will celebrate my deliverance" — Significantly departs from KJV/ASV/WEB's "I will take the cup of salvation." NET removes the concrete cup imagery and renders the meaning abstractly.
- v.15 — NET: "The Lord values the lives of his faithful followers" — Radically different from KJV's "Precious in the sight of the LORD is the death of his saints." NET interprets the verse as God valuing their LIVES (not wanting their death), while KJV preserves the paradox of death being "precious."
- v.16 — WEB: "You have freed me from my chains" — Vivid rendering of KJV's "thou hast loosed my bonds." Both convey liberation, but WEB's "chains" is more concrete for modern readers.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "What shall I render?" (v.12). Ask this question regularly.
   In light of all God has done, what is your response? Not guilt
   but gratitude. Not obligation but overflow.

2. Take the cup (v.13). Salvation is received, not achieved. Your
   part is to take what God offers and call on His name. The cup
   is ready — extend your hand.

3. God values your life and death (v.15). You are not disposable.
   Your death is not trivial to God. Every one of His saints is
   precious — their departure is costly in His sight.

4. Return to rest (v.7). After every crisis, command your soul
   back to rest. The crisis is over; God has proven faithful.
   Don't live in perpetual anxiety when deliverance has already
   come.

5. Pay your vows publicly (vv.14, 18). Gratitude is not private
   only. What you promised God in the dark, fulfill in the light.
   Public testimony of God's faithfulness is a vow paid.
