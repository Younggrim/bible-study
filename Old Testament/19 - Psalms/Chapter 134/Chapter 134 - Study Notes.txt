Psalms - Chapter 134
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A Song of Ascents — the final Song of Ascents: a call to the night-watch servants in the temple to bless the LORD, and a benediction: 'The LORD that made heaven and earth bless thee out of Zion.'

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Song of Ascents / Hymn of Praise
Attributed Author: Anonymous (superscription: "A Song of degrees")
Key Themes: Night worship, blessing the LORD, lifting hands in
the sanctuary, priestly blessing, the Creator who blesses

Historical Context: Psalm 134 is the final Song of Ascents (15th
of 15) — a brief evening benediction of 3 verses. The collection
that began in distress and exile (Psalm 120) concludes at the
temple at night, with worshipers calling priests/Levites to bless
the LORD during the night watch, and receiving blessing in return.
The pattern: worshipers bless God (vv.1-2), then God blesses
worshipers (v.3). It is a fitting conclusion to the pilgrim
journey — arrival at the temple, nighttime worship, mutual
blessing between God and His people. The journey is complete.

Structure:
- Call to the Night-Servants to Bless God (vv.1-2)
- God's Blessing in Return (v.3)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "Behold, bless ye the LORD, all ye servants of the LORD,
        which by night stand in the house of the LORD." (KJV) —
        Even at NIGHT, worship continues. The servants who keep
        vigil in the temple are called to bless the LORD. There
        is no hour when worship is inappropriate.

v.3  — "The LORD that made heaven and earth bless thee out of
        Zion." (KJV) — The Creator of the universe blesses YOU
        from Zion. The Maker of everything directs personal
        blessing toward individual worshipers. Cosmic power,
        personal care.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------

v.2   — 1 Timothy 2:8 — "Lifting up holy hands."
v.3   — Numbers 6:24-26 — The Aaronic blessing.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.1 — KJV: "Behold, bless ye the LORD" / WEB: "Look! Praise Yahweh" / NET: "Attention! Praise the Lord" — NET's "Attention!" is a striking rendering of Hebrew "hinneh" (behold/look). It commands alertness rather than inviting observation.
- v.1 — NET: "who serve in the Lord's temple during the night" — Expands KJV's "which by night stand in the house of the LORD." NET adds "serve" and changes "house" to "temple," making the priestly function explicit.
- v.2 — KJV: "Lift up your hands in the sanctuary" / NET: "Lift your hands toward the sanctuary" — Small but notable: KJV has hands lifted IN the sanctuary (already inside); NET has them lifted TOWARD it (possibly from outside), changing the spatial orientation.
- v.3 — NET: "May the Lord, the Creator of heaven and earth, bless you from Zion" — Restructures KJV's "The LORD that made heaven and earth bless thee out of Zion." NET makes "Creator" a title rather than a relative clause.
- v.1 — ASV: "Jehovah" (×3) / WEB: "Yahweh" (×3) / NET: "the Lord" (×3) — The divine name appears three times in three verses, making translation choice highly visible in this brief psalm.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Worship in the night (v.1). Night seasons — both literal and
   spiritual — are times for worship, not just waiting. Bless
   God in the darkness.

2. Bless God; God blesses you (vv.1-3). The exchange of blessing:
   you bless Him with praise, He blesses you from Zion. Worship
   is not one-directional.

3. The pilgrim journey is complete. From the distress of Psalm
   120 to the night-rest of Psalm 134, the worshiper has arrived.
   The Songs of Ascents end in the temple, in worship, in
   blessing. Journey over. Home.
