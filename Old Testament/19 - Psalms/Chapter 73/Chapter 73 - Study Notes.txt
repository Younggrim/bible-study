Psalms - Chapter 73
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A wisdom psalm of Asaph — opening Book III; the psalmist nearly stumbled envying the wicked's prosperity, until he entered God's sanctuary and understood their end; 'whom have I in heaven but thee?'

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Wisdom Psalm
Attributed Author: Asaph
Key Themes: The prosperity of the wicked, the suffering of the
righteous, envy and doubt, the sanctuary as turning point, God as
ultimate portion, nearness to God as supreme good

Historical Context: Psalm 73 opens Book III of the Psalter (Psalms
73-89). Asaph was David's chief musician and a Levite worship
leader. This psalm is often called the "crisis of faith" psalm —
Asaph nearly loses his faith by observing the prosperity of the
wicked and the suffering of the righteous. The turning point comes
in v.17: "Until I went into the sanctuary of God." Worship
reoriented his perspective. The psalm moves from near-apostasy to
one of the most beautiful confessions of faith in Scripture
(vv.23-28). It addresses the same problem as Job and Ecclesiastes:
why do the wicked prosper? The answer is eschatological — their
end reveals everything.

Structure:
- Thesis: God Is Good (v.1)
- The Crisis: Envy of the Wicked (vv.2-14)
- The Silence: Not Wanting to Lead Others Astray (vv.15-16)
- The Turning Point: Entering the Sanctuary (vv.17-20)
- The Confession: Foolishness Acknowledged (vv.21-22)
- The Resolution: God as Portion Forever (vv.23-28)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2-3 — "But as for me, my feet were almost gone; my steps had
        well nigh slipped. For I was envious at the foolish, when
        I saw the prosperity of the wicked." (KJV) — Brutal
        honesty. A worship leader nearly lost his faith because
        the wicked were doing better than the righteous. This is
        the universal struggle of faith.

v.17 — "Until I went into the sanctuary of God; then understood I
        their end." (KJV) — THE turning point. Everything changed
        in worship. The problem looked unsolvable UNTIL Asaph
        entered God's presence. Then he saw the END — their
        slippery destination, their sudden destruction. Perspective
        is everything.

v.25 — "Whom have I in heaven but thee? and there is none upon
        earth that I desire beside thee." (KJV) — The greatest
        declaration of devotion in the OT. Having moved through
        envy to understanding, Asaph arrives at pure desire: God
        alone is enough. Nothing in heaven or earth compares.

v.26 — "My flesh and my heart faileth: but God is the strength of
        my heart, and my portion for ever." (KJV) — Everything
        else will fail — body, emotions, circumstances. But God
        remains. "Portion" (cheleq) = inheritance, lot, share.
        God Himself is what Asaph inherits. Not God's gifts, but
        God Himself.

v.28 — "But it is good for me to draw near to God." (KJV) — The
        psalm's final verdict. After wrestling with envy, doubt,
        and near-apostasy, the conclusion is simple: nearness to
        God is the supreme good. Not prosperity, not answers — but
        proximity.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.4 — KJV: "there are no bands in their death" / ESV: "they have
      no pangs until death" — Hebrew is debated. KJV suggests the
      wicked die without pain or struggle; ESV similarly. The point:
      even their DEATHS are easy, adding to the righteous person's
      frustration.

v.22 — KJV: "I was as a beast before thee" / ESV: "I was like a
       beast toward you" — When Asaph envied the wicked, he was
       functioning at the level of an animal — reactive, instinctual,
       lacking eternal perspective. Only in the sanctuary did he
       recover his humanity.

v.24 — KJV: "afterward receive me to glory" / ESV: "afterward you
       will receive me to glory" — This may be the clearest OT
       expression of hope in an afterlife with God. "Glory" here
       points beyond death to eternal reception into God's presence.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.1 — NET: "Certainly God is good to Israel, and to those whose motives are pure" vs. KJV: "Truly God is good to Israel, even to such as are of a clean heart" — NET changes "clean heart" to "pure motives," shifting from moral purity to intentionality. ASV/WEB: "pure in heart" — aligning with Jesus' beatitude (Matthew 5:8).

- v.22 — ASV: "So brutish was I, and ignorant; I was as a beast before thee" / WEB: "I was so senseless and ignorant. I was a brute beast before you" vs. KJV: "So foolish was I, and ignorant: I was as a beast before thee" — ASV's "brutish" and WEB's "brute beast" are stronger than KJV's "beast," emphasizing the raw animality of envy-driven thinking.

- v.24 — NET: "You guide me by your wise advice, and then you will lead me to a position of honor" vs. KJV: "Thou shalt guide me with thy counsel, and afterward receive me to glory" — NET drastically reduces the eschatological hope. "Position of honor" is this-worldly; KJV's "receive me to glory" points to eternal life. This is theologically significant.

- v.26 — NET: "God always protects my heart and gives me stability" vs. KJV: "God is the strength of my heart, and my portion for ever" — NET loses "my portion for ever" — one of the psalm's most powerful declarations (God as eternal inheritance). WEB/ASV preserve: "God is the strength of my heart and my portion forever."

- v.27 — ASV: "Thou hast destroyed all them that play the harlot, departing from thee" / NET: "you destroy everyone who is unfaithful to you" vs. KJV: "thou hast destroyed all them that go a whoring from thee" — KJV's graphic "whoring" captures the Hebrew "zanah" (spiritual adultery) most forcefully. ASV softens to "play the harlot"; NET/WEB to "unfaithful."

- v.28 — ASV: "I have made the Lord Jehovah my refuge" / WEB: "I have made the Lord Yahweh my refuge" vs. KJV: "I have put my trust in the Lord GOD" — The double divine name (Adonai Yahweh) is explicit in ASV/WEB. NET: "I have made the Sovereign Lord my shelter."

- v.25 — NET: "On earth there is no one I desire but you" vs. KJV: "there is none upon earth that I desire beside thee" — Same meaning, but NET's "no one" makes it personal (no person replaces God), while KJV's "none" could include things and persons. WEB: "There is no one on earth whom I desire besides you."

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. It's normal to struggle with the prosperity of the wicked
   (vv.2-3). If even Asaph — the worship leader — nearly stumbled,
   you are not uniquely faithless for asking why the wicked thrive.
   The question is honest; just don't stop there.

2. The sanctuary changes everything (v.17). What you cannot
   understand in isolation, you understand in worship. Get into
   God's presence. Perspective comes not from more thinking but
   from more worship. The answer to "why?" is found in "Who."

3. God Himself is your portion (v.26). Not His blessings, His
   gifts, His answers — HIM. If everything else failed but you
   had God, you would have more than if everything succeeded
   without Him.

4. Nearness to God is the definition of "good" (v.28). After
   wrestling with what is "good" (the wicked seem to have all the
   "good things"), Asaph redefines good: it is simply being near
   God. That is the supreme value. Everything else is negotiable.

5. Think eschatologically (v.17). "Their END." The present chapter
   is not the whole story. The wicked prosper NOW; they perish
   THEN. Don't judge God's justice by chapter 3 when the book has
   100 chapters.
