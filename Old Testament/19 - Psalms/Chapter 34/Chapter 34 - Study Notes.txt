Psalms - Chapter 34
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
An acrostic thanksgiving psalm — David blesses the LORD after deliverance (when he feigned madness before Abimelech); 'taste and see that the LORD is good'; the LORD is near the brokenhearted.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Thanksgiving Psalm / Acrostic (Alphabetic) Psalm
Attributed Author: David ("A Psalm of David, when he changed his
behaviour before Abimelech; who drove him away, and he departed")
Key Themes: Tasting God's goodness, the angel of the LORD
encamping around the faithful, the many afflictions of the
righteous, Messianic prophecy (no broken bones), the fear of
the LORD as wisdom

Historical Context: The superscription refers to the event
recorded in 1 Samuel 21:10-15, when David fled from Saul to Gath
and feigned madness before the Philistine king. Notably, 1 Samuel
calls the king "Achish," while the psalm superscription says
"Abimelech." This is likely because "Abimelech" ("my father is
king") was a dynastic title for Philistine rulers (similar to
"Pharaoh" for Egyptian kings), as seen in Genesis 20 and 26.

This is an acrostic psalm — each verse begins with successive
letters of the Hebrew alphabet (22 letters), though the letter
"Vav" (the 6th letter) is missing and an extra "Pe" line is added
at the end. The acrostic structure served as a memory aid and
symbolized completeness — praising God from A to Z. Despite the
formal structure, the psalm overflows with warmth and personal
testimony.

Psalm 34 holds special Messianic significance. Verse 20 ("He
keepeth all his bones: not one of them is broken") is cited in
John 19:36 as fulfilled when the soldiers did not break Jesus'
legs on the cross. The psalm also connects to the Passover lamb
whose bones were not to be broken (Exodus 12:46, Numbers 9:12),
pointing to Christ as the ultimate Passover Lamb.

Structure:
- Personal Praise and Testimony (vv. 1-7)
- Invitation to Experience God's Goodness (vv. 8-10)
- Wisdom Instruction: The Fear of the LORD (vv. 11-14)
- God's Relationship to Righteous and Wicked (vv. 15-18)
- The Afflictions and Deliverance of the Righteous (vv. 19-22)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
Verse 8 — "O taste and see that the LORD is good: blessed is the
man that trusteth in him." (KJV)
One of the most beloved invitations in Scripture. "Taste" implies
personal, experiential knowledge — not merely intellectual assent
but direct encounter. You cannot know the sweetness of honey by
reading about it; you must taste it. God invites us to experience
His goodness firsthand.

Verse 7 — "The angel of the LORD encampeth round about them that
fear him, and delivereth them." (KJV)
"The angel of the LORD" (malak YHWH) is often understood as a
pre-incarnate appearance of Christ (Christophany) in the Old
Testament. This angel doesn't merely visit but "encamps" —
establishing a military encampment as a permanent guard around
God's people.

Verse 18 — "The LORD is nigh unto them that are of a broken
heart; and saveth such as be of a contrite spirit." (KJV)
God's proximity is not to the proud, the self-sufficient, or the
spiritually impressive — but to the broken. "Contrite" (dakka)
means crushed, pulverized. God draws near to those who have been
crushed by life, sin, or suffering.

Verse 19-20 — "Many are the afflictions of the righteous: but the
LORD delivereth him out of them all. He keepeth all his bones: not
one of them is broken." (KJV)
Verse 19 honestly acknowledges that righteousness does not prevent
suffering — it invites it ("many"). But God's faithfulness is
demonstrated not in preventing trials but in delivering through
them. Verse 20 is cited in John 19:36 regarding Christ's
crucifixion.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.7 — Genesis 32:1-2; 2 Kings 6:17: Angels encamping for
protection — Jacob saw the camp of God, Elisha's servant saw
chariots of fire surrounding them.

v.8 — 1 Peter 2:3: Peter directly references this verse: "if
indeed you have tasted that the Lord is good," connecting the
Old Testament invitation to New Testament experience of Christ.

v.12-16 — 1 Peter 3:10-12: Peter quotes these verses extensively
in his instruction about Christian conduct and suffering, showing
their enduring applicability.

v.14 — Romans 12:18; Hebrews 12:14: "Seek peace, and pursue it"
— echoed in New Testament ethics of peacemaking and pursuing
holiness.

v.18 — Isaiah 57:15; Isaiah 66:2: God dwells with the contrite
and lowly — His transcendent majesty paired with intimate nearness
to the humble.

v.19 — John 16:33; Acts 14:22: Jesus promised tribulation; Paul
taught that we enter the kingdom through many tribulations. The
promise is deliverance through, not exemption from, suffering.

v.20 — John 19:36; Exodus 12:46; Numbers 9:12: "Not a bone shall
be broken" — fulfilled in Christ, connecting to the Passover lamb
regulations.

v.22 — Romans 8:1: "None of them that trust in him shall be
desolate" — no condemnation for those in Christ Jesus.

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.5 — "They looked unto him, and were lightened" (KJV) / "Those
who look to him are radiant" (ESV): Hebrew nahar means to beam,
shine, be radiant. KJV's "lightened" suggests illumination or
having burdens lifted. ESV's "radiant" captures the Hebrew image
of faces glowing with reflected divine glory — like Moses after
meeting God (Exodus 34:29).

v.8 — "trusteth in him" (KJV) / "takes refuge in him" (ESV):
Hebrew chasah means to take refuge, to seek shelter — more
active than mere mental trust. It implies running to God as one
runs to a fortress or shelter in a storm.

v.18 — "of a broken heart...of a contrite spirit" (KJV) /
"brokenhearted...the crushed in spirit" (ESV): Hebrew nishberey-
lev (broken of heart) and dakke-ruach (crushed of spirit). ESV's
"crushed" for dakke is more visceral — it's the word used for
grinding grain or pulverizing rock. This is not mild sadness but
devastating brokenness.

v.20 — "He keepeth all his bones" (KJV) / "He keeps all his
bones" (ESV): The shift from plural "righteous" in v.19 to
singular "his" in v.20 is significant. While v.19 speaks
generally of the righteous, v.20's singular focus points toward
a specific Righteous One — the Messiah whose bones were
preserved unbroken at the cross.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "I will bless the LORD at all times" (v.1) — David wrote this
after a humiliating episode of feigning insanity. He chose to
praise God not when circumstances were dignified but precisely
when they were shameful. Can you bless God in your most
embarrassing, lowest moments?

2. "O taste and see that the LORD is good" (v.8) is an invitation
to move from secondhand religion to firsthand experience. Are you
living on borrowed faith — the testimonies of others, the theology
of books — or have you personally tasted God's goodness? What
would it mean to "taste" today?

3. "Many are the afflictions of the righteous" (v.19) honestly
contradicts prosperity theology. Godliness attracts suffering in a
fallen world. But the promise is not immunity — it's deliverance.
God does not promise to prevent the furnace but to walk through it
with you (Daniel 3:25). How does this reframe your expectations?

4. The angel of the LORD "encamps" (v.7) — not visits briefly, not
occasionally checks in, but establishes permanent military
presence. You are more guarded than you know. How would you live
differently if you truly believed in this unseen protection?

5. Verses 11-14 offer practical wisdom: guard your tongue, depart
from evil, pursue peace. Experiential knowledge of God's goodness
(v.8) naturally leads to ethical living. True spirituality is never
divorced from moral conduct. Which of these practical commands
needs the most attention in your life right now?
