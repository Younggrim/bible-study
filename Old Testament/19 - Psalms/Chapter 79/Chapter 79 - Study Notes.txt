Psalms - Chapter 79
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A communal lament — the nations have invaded, defiled the temple, and poured out blood like water around Jerusalem; the psalmist pleads for God's compassion, vengeance on the nations, and restoration for His people.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Community Lament Psalm
Attributed Author: Asaph (or Asaphite descendant)
Key Themes: Temple desecration, Jerusalem's destruction, unburied
dead, national shame, God's reputation among the nations, plea for
mercy and vengeance, generational praise

Historical Context: Like Psalm 74, this psalm describes the
destruction of Jerusalem and the temple — almost certainly the
Babylonian conquest of 586 BC. The details are graphic: bodies left
unburied (v.2-3), blood poured like water (v.3), and Jerusalem
"laid on heaps" (v.1). The psalmist is deeply concerned with God's
reputation among the nations: "Why should the nations say, 'Where
is their God?'" (v.10). The prayer moves between acknowledgment
that Israel's sin caused this (v.8-9) and outrage that pagan
nations have desecrated God's holy place. It concludes with a
covenant promise: "We thy people and sheep of thy pasture will give
thee thanks for ever" (v.13).

Structure:
- Description of the Devastation (vv.1-4)
- How Long? (v.5)
- Prayer Against the Nations (vv.6-7)
- Prayer for Mercy Despite Sin (vv.8-9)
- Plea for God's Name and Vindication (vv.10-12)
- Vow of Perpetual Praise (v.13)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.5  — "How long, LORD? wilt thou be angry for ever? shall thy
        jealousy burn like fire?" (KJV) — The perennial cry of the
        suffering: "How long?" Not denying God's justice but asking
        for its limit. Even justified anger must end somewhere.

v.9  — "Help us, O God of our salvation, for the glory of thy
        name: and deliver us, and purge away our sins, for thy
        name's sake." (KJV) — The appeal is to God's NAME — His
        reputation. "Do it for YOUR sake, not ours." When we have
        no merit to claim, we can still claim God's glory as
        motivation for His intervention.

v.10 — "Wherefore should the heathen say, Where is their God?"
        (KJV) — The reputational argument. If God's people are
        destroyed and He does nothing, the nations conclude He is
        impotent or nonexistent. The psalmist appeals to God's
        concern for His own honor.

v.13 — "So we thy people and sheep of thy pasture will give thee
        thanks for ever: we will shew forth thy praise to all
        generations." (KJV) — The vow: if God delivers, His people
        will praise Him forever and declare His works to every
        generation. Rescue produces perpetual gratitude.

         Where is their God?"

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.8 — KJV: "let thy tender mercies speedily prevent us" / ESV:
      "let your compassion come speedily to meet us" — "Prevent"
      (KJV) = go before, anticipate (archaic English). ESV's "come
      speedily to meet" captures the Hebrew better: God's mercy
      racing ahead to intercept them before destruction is
      complete.

v.11 — KJV: "those that are appointed to die" / ESV: "those
       doomed to die" — Hebrew "beney temutah" = literally "sons of
       death." Those already sentenced and awaiting execution. The
       prayer: rescue even those on death row.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.2 — NET: "the flesh of your loyal followers" / KJV: "the flesh of thy saints" / ASV: "the flesh of thy saints" — NET renders חֲסִידֶיךָ (chasideka) as "loyal followers" rather than "saints." The Hebrew implies those who are faithful/devoted to God — not sainthood in the later Christian sense.

- v.5 — ASV: "How long, O Jehovah?" / WEB: "How long, Yahweh?" / KJV: "How long, LORD?" — The divine name YHWH in a direct cry of anguish. Each translation's rendering carries different emotional weight in a prayer of this intensity.

- v.8 — KJV: "let thy tender mercies speedily prevent us" / NET: "Quickly send your compassion our way" / WEB: "Let your tender mercies speedily meet us" / ASV: "Let thy tender mercies speedily meet us" — KJV's archaic "prevent" (= go before) is corrected by all others to "meet." NET paraphrases most freely, losing "tender mercies" (רַחֲמֶיךָ, rachameka = womb-compassion).

- v.9 — NET: "For the sake of your glorious reputation, rescue us. Forgive our sins for the sake of your reputation" — NET renders "for thy name's sake" as "for the sake of your reputation" twice, making explicit that God's "name" means His reputation/honor among the nations.

- v.10 — KJV: "the heathen" / ASV: "the nations" / NET: "the nations" / WEB: "the nations" — KJV's "heathen" carries pejorative connotations in modern English that the neutral Hebrew גּוֹיִם (goyim = nations) does not. All modern translations use "nations."

- v.11 — NET: "Use your great strength to set free those condemned to die" / KJV: "preserve thou those that are appointed to die" — NET interprets "preserve" as "set free," which is stronger than the Hebrew. KJV/ASV/WEB all use "preserve" — keeping alive, not necessarily liberating.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. God's reputation is a valid prayer appeal (vv.9-10). When you
   have no personal merit, appeal to God's name. "For YOUR glory,
   intervene." God cares about how He is perceived among the
   nations — use that as leverage in prayer.

2. Acknowledging sin and asking for mercy are not contradictory
   (vv.8-9). The psalmist says both: "We sinned" AND "Help us."
   Confession and petition go together. You don't have to earn
   deliverance before asking for it.

3. "How long?" is the prayer of endurance, not abandonment (v.5).
   Asking "how long" proves you're still holding on. You haven't
   given up — you're asking for a timeline. That is faith under
   pressure.

4. Deliverance produces generational praise (v.13). When God
   rescues, the response is not relief that fades but praise that
   echoes through generations. Every deliverance becomes a story
   to tell.
