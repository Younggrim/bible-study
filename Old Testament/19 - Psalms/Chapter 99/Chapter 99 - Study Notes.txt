Psalms - Chapter 99
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
An enthronement psalm — 'The LORD reigneth; let the people tremble'; He is great in Zion, a lover of justice; Moses, Aaron, and Samuel called upon Him and He answered; He is holy (repeated three times).

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Enthronement / Holiness Psalm
Attributed Author: Anonymous
Key Themes: The LORD reigns, God's holiness (triple refrain),
trembling before God, justice and equity, God answering His
servants, forgiveness AND consequences

Historical Context: The last of the enthronement psalms (93-99).
Its distinguishing feature is the triple refrain "He is holy"
(vv.3,5,9) — echoing the Seraphim's "Holy, holy, holy" in Isaiah
6:3. The psalm moves from God's cosmic reign (vv.1-3) to His just
rule over Jacob (vv.4-5) to His historical relationship with Moses,
Aaron, and Samuel (vv.6-9). These three intercessors exemplify the
psalm's dual theme: God forgives (answered their prayers) AND
avenges (took vengeance on their inventions/wrongdoings). Grace
and accountability coexist in God's holiness. He pardons sin but
does not ignore consequences.

Structure:
- The LORD Reigns: Tremble! (vv.1-3)
- The King Loves Justice (vv.4-5)
- God Answered Moses, Aaron, Samuel (vv.6-7)
- Forgiveness AND Consequences (vv.8-9)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "The LORD reigneth; let the people tremble: he sitteth
        between the cherubims; let the earth be moved." (KJV) —
        The response to God's reign is TREMBLING, not mere
        celebration. He is enthroned on the cherubim (Ark of the
        Covenant imagery) — both accessible (temple) and
        terrifying (holiness).

v.5  — "Exalt ye the LORD our God, and worship at his footstool;
        for he is holy." (KJV) — His footstool is the Ark of the
        Covenant — the place where heaven and earth meet. Worship
        there. And the reason: HE IS HOLY. This refrain defines
        the psalm.

v.8  — "Thou answeredst them, O LORD our God: thou wast a God that
        forgavest them, though thou tookest vengeance of their
        inventions." (KJV) — The profound theological balance: God
        FORGAVE (personally pardoned) AND took vengeance on their
        wrongdoings (enforced consequences). Forgiveness does not
        mean absence of all consequences. God is both gracious and
        just.

v.9  — "Exalt the LORD our God, and worship at his holy hill; for
        the LORD our God is holy." (KJV) — The final, fullest
        refrain. Not just "he is holy" but "the LORD our God IS
        holy." His holiness is not a quality He possesses — it is
        who He IS.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.1   — Psalm 80:1 — "Thou that dwellest between the cherubims,
         shine forth." Same enthroned-on-cherubim imagery.
v.1   — Isaiah 6:1-3 — "Holy, holy, holy, is the LORD of hosts."
         The triple holiness declaration.
v.6   — 1 Samuel 12:18 — Samuel called on the LORD and the LORD
         answered with thunder. God responding to intercessors.
v.6   — Exodus 32:11-14 — Moses interceding and God answering.
v.8   — Numbers 20:12 — Moses forgiven but denied entry to Canaan.
         Grace with consequences.
v.8   — Numbers 12:1-15 — Miriam forgiven but leprous seven days.
         Same principle.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.3 — KJV: "thy great and terrible name; for it is holy" / ESV:
      "your great and awesome name! Holy is he!" — ESV restructures
      to make "Holy is he" an exclamation/refrain. This helps the
      reader see the triple "holy" structure (vv.3,5,9).

v.8 — KJV: "thou tookest vengeance of their inventions" / ESV:
      "an avenger of their wrongdoings" — Hebrew "alilotheihem" =
      their deeds/practices/inventions. God forgave Moses and Aaron
      personally but still enforced consequences for their specific
      actions (Moses couldn't enter Canaan; Aaron's sons died).

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.1 — ASV: "Jehovah reigneth; let the peoples tremble" / WEB: "Yahweh reigns! Let the peoples tremble" / NET: "The Lord reigns! The nations tremble" — The divine name is rendered three ways: ASV "Jehovah," WEB "Yahweh," NET "the Lord." NET also changes "peoples" to "nations" (Hebrew "ammim" = peoples/nations) and shifts from jussive command ("let them tremble") to indicative statement ("the nations tremble").

- v.1 — KJV: "he sitteth between the cherubims" / ASV: "He sitteth above the cherubim" / WEB: "He sits enthroned among the cherubim" / NET: "He sits enthroned above the cherubim" — KJV's "between" suggests spatial positioning on the Ark; ASV/NET's "above" emphasizes sovereignty; WEB's "among" suggests dwelling presence. Hebrew "yoshev" = sits/is enthroned.

- v.3 — KJV: "thy great and terrible name" / ASV: "thy great and terrible name" / WEB: "your great and awesome name" / NET: "your great and awesome name" — KJV/ASV retain "terrible" (meaning awe-inspiring, not frightening in the modern sense). WEB/NET use "awesome" — the modern English equivalent of the Hebrew "nora" (inspiring reverential fear).

- v.4 — KJV: "The king's strength also loveth judgment" / NET: "The king is strong; he loves justice" / ASV: "The king's strength also loveth justice" — NET restructures entirely, breaking one clause into two sentences and interpreting "judgment" (mishpat) as "justice." NET adds: "You ensure that legal decisions will be made fairly" — a significant interpretive expansion of the Hebrew "equity" (mesharim).

- v.5 — ASV: "Exalt ye Jehovah our God" / WEB: "Exalt Yahweh our God" / NET: "Praise the Lord our God" — NET renders "exalt" as "praise" — a subtly different action. To exalt is to lift up/magnify; to praise is to express admiration. The Hebrew "romemu" specifically means to lift high/exalt.

- v.8 — KJV: "thou tookest vengeance of their inventions" / ASV: "thou tookest vengeance of their doings" / WEB: "you took vengeance for their doings" / NET: "one who punished their sinful deeds" — "Inventions" (KJV) is archaic for deeds/practices (Hebrew "alilotheihem"). NET adds "sinful" as an interpretive qualifier not in the Hebrew. NET also restructures: "They found you to be a forgiving God" — much freer than the literal "Thou wast a God that forgavest them."

- v.7 — KJV: "He spake unto them in the cloudy pillar" / ASV: "He spake unto them in the pillar of cloud" / NET: "He spoke to them from a pillar of cloud" / WEB: "He spoke to them in the pillar of cloud" — Minor but notable: NET's "from" (separation) vs. KJV/ASV/WEB's "in" (locative). Also KJV's "testimonies, and the ordinance" / NET: "regulations and the ordinance" — NET uses "regulations" for "testimonies" (Hebrew "edoth"), an interpretive shift from witness-language to legal-language.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Holiness should produce trembling (v.1). Not casual familiarity
   but holy awe. God is accessible through grace — but He is still
   HOLY. Approach with reverence, not flippancy.

2. God forgives AND allows consequences (v.8). These are not
   contradictions. A parent can forgive a child's misdeed while
   still enforcing natural consequences. God pardons sin while
   allowing its earthly effects to play out. Grace does not erase
   all consequences.

3. God answers those who call on Him (v.6). Moses called — God
   answered. Aaron called — God answered. Samuel called — God
   answered. The pattern: those who call receive answers. Do you
   call? Do you expect answers?

4. "He is holy" is the foundation of everything (vv.3,5,9). Not
   just one attribute among many — holiness IS God's essential
   character. Everything else flows from this. Worship, justice,
   forgiveness, consequences — all are expressions of His holiness.
