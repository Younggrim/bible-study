Psalms - Chapter 71
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
An elderly man's lament and trust — the psalmist has trusted God from youth and the womb; now old and gray, he begs God not to forsake him but to continue his strength so he can declare God's power to the next generation.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Individual Lament / Trust Psalm
Attributed Author: Anonymous (likely David in old age)
Key Themes: Trust from youth to old age, fear of being forsaken in
weakness, God's faithfulness across a lifetime, declaring God's
works to the next generation, continued praise despite aging

Historical Context: This psalm has no superscription — unusual for
this section of the Psalter — but is clearly the prayer of an
elderly person (vv.9,18 — "old age," "gray hairs"). The opening
verses closely parallel Psalm 31:1-3, suggesting either shared
authorship or deliberate quotation. Many scholars attribute it to
David in his latter years, possibly during Absalom's rebellion when
enemies said "God hath forsaken him" (v.11). The psalm is a
lifetime of faith distilled: from the womb (v.6) through youth
(v.17) to old age (v.18). It is the prayer of someone who has
walked with God for decades and still needs Him desperately.

Structure:
- Cry for Refuge and Deliverance (vv.1-4)
- Lifelong Trust from the Womb (vv.5-8)
- Prayer Against Abandonment in Old Age (vv.9-13)
- Commitment to Continual Praise (vv.14-16)
- Declaration to the Next Generation (vv.17-21)
- Musical Praise and Joy (vv.22-24)


CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.1-3 — Psalm 31:1-3 — Nearly identical opening. Shared language suggesting same author or tradition.
v.6 — Psalm 22:9-10 — Same womb-to-present trust.

REFLECTION
------------------------------------
1. A lifetime of faith does not eliminate the need for prayer
   (vv.1-4). Even after decades with God, David still cries out.
   Spiritual maturity doesn't mean self-sufficiency — it means
   deeper dependence.

2. Fear of being discarded in old age is human and valid (v.9).
   If you feel this fear — that your best years of usefulness are
   behind you — bring it to God honestly. He does not discard His
   servants.

3. Your purpose in old age: testify to the next generation (v.18).
   The elderly have something irreplaceable — lived experience of
   God's faithfulness. Your job is not to retire from God's
   service but to pass on what you've witnessed.

4. Past deliverances prove future faithfulness (v.20). Count them.
   Remember them. Every time God brought you through is evidence
   that He will do it again. Your history with God is your
   strongest argument for hope.
