Psalms - Chapter 71
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
An elderly man's lament and trust — the psalmist has trusted God from youth and the womb; now old and gray, he begs God not to forsake him but to continue his strength so he can declare God's power to the next generation.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Individual Lament / Trust Psalm
Attributed Author: Anonymous (likely David in old age)
Key Themes: Trust from youth to old age, fear of being forsaken in
weakness, God's faithfulness across a lifetime, declaring God's
works to the next generation, continued praise despite aging

Historical Context: This psalm has no superscription — unusual for
this section of the Psalter — but is clearly the prayer of an
elderly person (vv.9,18 — "old age," "gray hairs"). The opening
verses closely parallel Psalm 31:1-3, suggesting either shared
authorship or deliberate quotation. Many scholars attribute it to
David in his latter years, possibly during Absalom's rebellion when
enemies said "God hath forsaken him" (v.11). The psalm is a
lifetime of faith distilled: from the womb (v.6) through youth
(v.17) to old age (v.18). It is the prayer of someone who has
walked with God for decades and still needs Him desperately.

Structure:
- Cry for Refuge and Deliverance (vv.1-4)
- Lifelong Trust from the Womb (vv.5-8)
- Prayer Against Abandonment in Old Age (vv.9-13)
- Commitment to Continual Praise (vv.14-16)
- Declaration to the Next Generation (vv.17-21)
- Musical Praise and Joy (vv.22-24)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.5  — "For thou art my hope, O Lord GOD: thou art my trust from
        my youth." (KJV) — A lifetime of trust. David's
        relationship with God is not new or untested — it spans
        from youth to old age. Long faithfulness gives deep
        confidence.

v.9  — "Cast me not off in the time of old age; forsake me not
        when my strength faileth." (KJV) — The vulnerable prayer
        of the aging. Physical decline is real, and with it comes
        the fear that usefulness to God may have ended. David asks
        God not to discard him in his weakness.

v.17-18 — "O God, thou hast taught me from my youth... Now also
        when I am old and greyheaded, O God, forsake me not; UNTIL
        I have shewed thy strength unto this generation." (KJV) —
        The driving purpose: to declare God's power to the next
        generation. David doesn't want to live longer for
        comfort — he wants time to TESTIFY.

v.20 — "Thou, which hast shewed me great and sore troubles, shalt
        quicken me again, and shalt bring me up again from the
        depths of the earth." (KJV) — Faith based on pattern. God
        has brought David through MANY troubles ("great and sore").
        He will do it again. Past deliverances are the evidence
        for future hope.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.1-3 — Psalm 31:1-3 — Nearly identical opening. Shared language
         suggesting same author or tradition.
v.6   — Psalm 22:9-10 — "Thou didst make me hope when I was upon
         my mother's breasts." Same womb-to-present trust.
v.9   — 2 Timothy 4:6-8 — Paul's equivalent: aging servant still
         needing God, still declaring His faithfulness.
v.18  — Psalm 78:4-6 — "We will not hide them from their children,
         showing to the generation to come the praises of the
         LORD." Same intergenerational mission.
v.19  — Exodus 15:11 — "Who is like unto thee, O LORD?" Same
         incomparability declaration.
v.20  — 2 Corinthians 1:9-10 — "God which raiseth the dead: who
         delivered us... and doth deliver: in whom we trust that
         he will yet deliver us."

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.3 — KJV: "Be thou my strong habitation" / ESV: "Be to me a rock
      of refuge" — The Hebrew manuscripts vary here. Some read
      "rock of dwelling" (ma'on tsur), others "rock of refuge"
      (tsur ma'oz). Both express the same truth: God as permanent,
      fortified dwelling place.

v.7 — KJV: "I am as a wonder unto many" / ESV: "I have been as a
      portent to many" — Hebrew "mopheth" = sign, portent, wonder.
      David's life — its trials and deliverances — is a visible
      demonstration to observers of God's faithfulness.

v.15 — KJV: "for I know not the numbers thereof" / ESV: "for
       their number is past my knowledge" — God's righteous acts
       are literally uncountable. David has run out of capacity to
       enumerate God's goodness.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.1 — ASV: "In thee, O Jehovah, do I take refuge" / WEB: "In you, Yahweh, I take refuge" vs. KJV: "In thee, O LORD, do I put my trust" — The divine name is explicit. Also, ASV/WEB use "take refuge" (a physical hiding metaphor) rather than KJV's "trust" (an internal disposition).

- v.5 — ASV: "O Lord Jehovah" / WEB: "Lord Yahweh" / NET: "O Sovereign Lord" vs. KJV: "O Lord GOD" — The double divine title "Adonai Yahweh" rendered four different ways. NET's "Sovereign Lord" interprets; ASV/WEB transliterate.

- v.16 — ASV: "I will come with the mighty acts of the Lord Jehovah" / WEB: "the mighty acts of the Lord Yahweh" / NET: "the mighty acts of the Sovereign Lord" vs. KJV: "I will go in the strength of the Lord GOD" — KJV reads "come/go" as approaching God; NET/ASV/WEB read it as declaring God's mighty deeds.

- v.20 — NET: "Though you have allowed me to experience much trouble and distress, revive me once again" vs. KJV: "Thou, which hast shewed me great and sore troubles, shalt quicken me again" — NET softens from God causing troubles ("shewed me") to God allowing them ("allowed me to experience"). This is theologically significant.

- v.22 — NET: "praising your faithfulness, O my God" vs. KJV: "even thy truth, O my God" — NET renders "emet" (truth) as "faithfulness," emphasizing the relational/covenantal dimension. ASV/WEB keep "truth." "Faithfulness" and "truth" are both valid readings of this Hebrew term.

- v.18 — WEB: "God, don't forsake me, until I have declared your strength to the next generation, your might to everyone who is to come" — WEB matches KJV's urgent prayer for continued life in order to transmit faith generationally. NET is similar but adds "about" — "until I tell the next generation about your strength."

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. A lifetime of faith does not eliminate the need for prayer
   (vv.1-4). Even after decades with God, David still cries out.
   Spiritual maturity doesn't mean self-sufficiency — it means
   deeper dependence.

2. Fear of being discarded in old age is human and valid (v.9).
   If you feel this fear — that your best years of usefulness are
   behind you — bring it to God honestly. He does not discard His
   servants.

3. Your purpose in old age: testify to the next generation (v.18).
   The elderly have something irreplaceable — lived experience of
   God's faithfulness. Your job is not to retire from God's
   service but to pass on what you've witnessed.

4. Past deliverances prove future faithfulness (v.20). Count them.
   Remember them. Every time God brought you through is evidence
   that He will do it again. Your history with God is your
   strongest argument for hope.
