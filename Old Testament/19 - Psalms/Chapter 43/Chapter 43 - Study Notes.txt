Psalms - Chapter 43
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A lament continuation of Psalm 42 — the psalmist pleads for God to send His light and truth to lead him back to the holy hill and the altar of God, repeating the refrain: 'Hope in God, for I shall yet praise him.'

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Individual Lament
Attributed Author: Anonymous (no superscription — likely a
continuation of Psalm 42, attributed to the Sons of Korah)
Key Themes: Prayer for vindication, light and truth as guides,
longing for worship, hope in God

Historical Context: Psalm 43 is one of the few psalms in Book II
without a superscription, strongly suggesting it was originally the
third stanza of Psalm 42. Both psalms share identical vocabulary,
theme, tone, and the same refrain ("Why art thou cast down, O my
soul?"). Ancient manuscripts including some Hebrew texts and the
Septuagint treat them as a single composition. The refrain appears
three times across the combined work (42:5, 42:11, 43:5), creating
a perfect tripartite structure.

While Psalm 42 expressed the despair of exile and spiritual
thirst, Psalm 43 moves toward resolution. The psalmist shifts from
lament to active petition — requesting that God send His light and
truth to guide him back to the holy hill and the altar. This is
the turning point of the combined psalm: from looking backward in
grief to looking forward in faith.

The prayer for vindication against an "ungodly nation" and
"deceitful and unjust man" suggests a specific adversary, possibly
connected to David's exile during Absalom's rebellion or to the
Levitical singers' displacement from their temple ministry. The
psalm demonstrates that honest complaint before God naturally leads
to renewed petition and restored hope.

Structure:
- Prayer for Vindication and Deliverance (vv. 1-2)
- Request for Light and Truth as Guides (v. 3)
- Anticipation of Restored Worship (v. 4)
- Third Refrain: Self-Counsel to Hope (v. 5)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
Verse 1 — "Judge me, O God, and plead my cause against an ungodly
nation: O deliver me from the deceitful and unjust man."
The psalmist appeals to God as judge and advocate — asking not for
mercy (since he claims innocence in this matter) but for justice.
"Judge me" is a bold request, implying confidence that God's
verdict will be favorable. The "ungodly nation" and "unjust man"
represent both corporate and individual opposition.

Verse 3 — "O send out thy light and thy truth: let them lead me;
let them bring me unto thy holy hill, and to thy tabernacles."
The theological climax of Psalms 42-43. Light and truth are
personified as divine guides leading the exile home. God's truth
illuminates the path; His light dispels the darkness of despair.
The destination is not merely geographic (Jerusalem) but spiritual
(God's presence at the altar). This anticipates Christ as "the
light of the world" and "the truth" (John 8:12; 14:6).

Verse 4 — "Then will I go unto the altar of God, unto God my
exceeding joy: yea, upon the harp will I praise thee, O God my
God."
The resolution of the psalmist's longing. "God my exceeding joy"
(Hebrew: "el simchat gili" — God, the gladness of my rejoicing)
expresses that God Himself — not merely the temple experience — is
the source of joy. The altar represents restored access, sacrifice,
and communion with God.

Verse 5 — "Why art thou cast down, O my soul? and why art thou
disquieted within me? hope in God: for I shall yet praise him, who
is the health of my countenance, and my God."
The final occurrence of the refrain. After active petition (vv.
1-4), the self-counsel to hope carries greater conviction. The
journey from Psalm 42:5 (raw despair) through 42:11 (continued
struggle) reaches its resolution here with forward-looking
confidence: "I shall yet praise him."

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v. 1 — Psalm 26:1 — "Judge me, O LORD; for I have walked in mine
integrity." The same bold appeal to God's righteous judgment.

v. 1 — Psalm 35:1 — "Plead my cause, O LORD, with them that strive
with me." God as advocate pleading the believer's legal case.

v. 2 — Psalm 42:9 — "I will say unto God my rock, Why hast thou
forgotten me?" The recurring question of perceived divine rejection
bridges both psalms.

v. 3 — John 8:12 — "I am the light of the world: he that followeth
me shall not walk in darkness." Christ as the ultimate fulfillment
of divine light leading God's people.

v. 3 — John 14:6 — "I am the way, the truth, and the life." Jesus
embodies the truth that guides believers to the Father's presence.

v. 3 — Psalm 27:4 — "One thing have I desired of the LORD, that
will I seek after; that I may dwell in the house of the LORD all
the days of my life." The singular desire to be in God's presence.

v. 4 — Psalm 84:1-2 — "How amiable are thy tabernacles, O LORD of
hosts! My soul longeth, yea, even fainteth for the courts of the
LORD." Intense longing for temple worship.

v. 5 — Lamentations 3:24 — "The LORD is my portion, saith my soul;
therefore will I hope in him." Self-counsel to hope based on God
as one's inheritance.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v. 1 — KJV "Judge me" vs. ESV "Vindicate me" — The Hebrew
"shaphat" (שָׁפַט) means to judge, govern, or render a verdict.
KJV preserves the literal meaning; ESV interprets the expected
outcome (vindication). The ESV choice clarifies that the psalmist
is not asking for condemnation but for God to rule in his favor.

v. 1 — KJV "ungodly nation" vs. ESV "ungodly people" — The Hebrew
"goy lo chasid" literally means "a nation not faithful/loyal." KJV
uses "nation" (closer to the Hebrew "goy"); ESV uses "people." The
term may refer to a foreign nation or to faithless people within
Israel.

v. 2 — KJV "the God of my strength" vs. ESV "the God in whom I
take refuge" — The Hebrew "elohey mauzzi" means "God of my
fortress/stronghold." KJV emphasizes the strength aspect; ESV
emphasizes the refuge/shelter aspect. Both are valid renderings of
the fortress metaphor.

v. 4 — KJV "God my exceeding joy" vs. ESV "God my exceeding joy"
— Both translations agree. The Hebrew "el simchat gili" is an
intensive construction: literally "God, the gladness of my
exultation/rejoicing." God is not just a source of joy but joy
at its most intense and overflowing.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. When lost in darkness, pray for light and truth. The psalmist
   does not try to find his own way back — he asks God to send
   guides. In spiritual confusion, the answer is not self-help but
   divine revelation. God's Word (light) and God's character
   (truth) lead us home.

2. Honest prayer includes bold requests. "Judge me" is not timid.
   The psalmist asks God to take up his cause, to act as his
   advocate. We can bring our grievances to God with confidence
   when our cause is just, trusting His righteous judgment.

3. The goal of deliverance is worship. The psalmist doesn't merely
   want to escape his enemies — he wants to reach the altar (v. 4).
   True spiritual health is measured not by the absence of trouble
   but by the presence of worship. Our petitions should aim toward
   restored communion with God.

4. Depression has a trajectory toward hope. The three-fold refrain
   across Psalms 42-43 shows movement. The psalmist does not
   immediately feel better, but he persistently speaks truth to
   his soul. Healing from spiritual depression is often gradual,
   not instantaneous — and persistence in hope is itself an act
   of faith.

5. "My God" — the possessive pronoun matters. The psalm ends with
   the deeply personal claim: "my God" (v. 5). In the midst of
   feeling abandoned, the psalmist still claims God as his own.
   Maintaining personal trust even when God seems distant is the
   essence of persevering faith.
