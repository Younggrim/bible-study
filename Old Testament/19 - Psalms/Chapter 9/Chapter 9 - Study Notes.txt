Psalms - Chapter 9
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A thanksgiving psalm (acrostic with Psalm 10) — David praises God for executing judgment against the nations, declares the LORD a stronghold for the oppressed, and affirms that the wicked will not prevail forever.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Multiple authors — primarily David (73 psalms attributed),
plus Asaph (12), Sons of Korah (11), Solomon (2), Moses (1),
Heman (1), Ethan (1), and anonymous. The Psalter was compiled over
approximately 1000 years and served as Israel's hymnal and prayer
book. The book is divided into five 'books' (1-41, 42-72, 73-89,
90-106, 107-150), paralleling the five books of Moses. Key themes:
worship, lament, praise, trust, kingship, creation, wisdom,
Messianic prophecy, and the full range of human emotion brought
before God.

Classification: Thanksgiving / Hymn of Praise with Lament Elements
Attributed Author: David — "To the chief Musician upon
Muth-labben, A Psalm of David"
Key Themes: Wholehearted praise, God as righteous judge, refuge
for the oppressed, nations judged, the wicked trapped by their
own schemes, hope for the poor

Historical Context: Psalms 9 and 10 form an acrostic pair (each
section begins with successive Hebrew letters, though the pattern
is partially broken). In the LXX they are combined as one psalm.
"Muth-labben" is obscure — possibly "death of the son" (a tune
name) or "death of the champion" (referencing Goliath?). The psalm
celebrates God's righteous judgments over nations while also
containing plea elements (v.13). It moves between past deliverance
(vv.1-6), present confidence (vv.7-12), petition (vv.13-14), and
prophetic certainty about the future (vv.15-20). The theme of
God as champion of the oppressed dominates.

Structure:
- Wholehearted Praise for Deliverance (vv.1-6)
- God Enthroned as Righteous Judge (vv.7-10)
- Call to Praise and Petition (vv.11-14)
- Self-Destruction of the Wicked (vv.15-16)
- The Fate of the Wicked and Hope of the Poor (vv.17-20)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.9  — "The LORD also will be a refuge for the oppressed, a refuge
        in times of trouble." (KJV) — God's special orientation
        toward the vulnerable. He is not merely a general
        protector but specifically a stronghold for those who are
        crushed and have nowhere else to turn.

v.10 — "And they that know thy name will put their trust in thee:
        for thou, LORD, hast not forsaken them that seek thee."
        (KJV) — Knowledge of God's character (name) produces trust.
        The basis of faith is not ignorance but knowledge — knowing
        WHO God is leads to trusting WHAT He does. And the track
        record: He has NOT forsaken seekers.

v.16 — "The LORD is known by the judgment which he executeth: the
        wicked is snared in the work of his own hands." (KJV) —
        God reveals His character through His justice. And His
        justice often operates through poetic irony: the wicked's
        own schemes become their punishment.

v.18 — "For the needy shall not alway be forgotten, and the hope
        of the poor shall not perish for ever." (KJV) — A promise
        for the suffering: your current condition is not permanent.
        God's apparent delay is not abandonment. The poor have
        hope, and that hope will not be disappointed.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.1   — Psalm 111:1 — "I will praise the LORD with my whole
         heart." Wholehearted worship as standard.
v.7   — Psalm 93:2 — "Thy throne is established of old." God's
         eternal sovereignty.
v.9   — Psalm 46:1 — "God is our refuge and strength, a very
         present help in trouble." The same refuge theology.
v.10  — Psalm 91:14 — "Because he hath known my name... I will
         deliver him." Knowledge of God produces protection.
v.15  — Psalm 7:15 — "He is fallen into the ditch which he made."
         Same self-destruction principle.
v.17  — Psalm 50:22 — "Now consider this, ye that forget God."
         Forgetting God leads to judgment.
v.18  — Luke 6:20 — "Blessed be ye poor: for yours is the kingdom
         of God." NT fulfillment of hope for the poor.
v.20  — Isaiah 31:3 — "The Egyptians are men, and not God." Same
         reminder that nations are merely human.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.9 — KJV: "a refuge" / ESV: "a stronghold" — Hebrew "misgav" =
      high place, stronghold, fortress. ESV's "stronghold" conveys
      the military defense imagery better than KJV's generic
      "refuge."

v.12 — KJV: "When he maketh inquisition for blood" / ESV: "For he
       who avenges blood is mindful of them" — Hebrew "doresh
       damim" = the one who investigates/avenges bloodshed. The
       idea is God as the divine investigator of murder/violence.
       He does not overlook shed blood.

v.16 — "Higgaion" — A musical/liturgical term meaning "meditation"
       or "resounding music." Combined with Selah, it indicates a
       significant pause for deep reflection on what was just
       declared: God is known by His judgments.

v.17 — KJV: "turned into hell" / ESV: "return to Sheol" — Hebrew
       "Sheol" = the realm of the dead, not the NT concept of
       eternal punishment. ESV preserves the Hebrew term; KJV's
       "hell" imports later theological concepts.

v.20 — KJV: "that the nations may know themselves to be but men" /
       ESV: "Let the nations know that they are but men!" — The
       final word is "enosh" (frail mortals). The prayer: let
       nations who think themselves divine learn they are merely
       human — finite, mortal, accountable.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.1 — ASV/WEB: "I will give thanks unto Jehovah/Yahweh" vs. KJV: "I will praise thee, O LORD" — ASV uses "Jehovah" and WEB uses "Yahweh" for the divine name (Hebrew YHWH), while KJV/ESV render it "LORD" (small caps). The ASV and WEB preserve the personal covenant name of God rather than substituting a title.

- v.5 — NET: "You terrified the nations with your battle cry. You destroyed the wicked; you permanently wiped out all memory of them" — NET paraphrases expansively here. KJV/ASV simply read "Thou hast rebuked the nations" (Hebrew "ga'ar" = rebuke/roar at). NET's "battle cry" is interpretive but captures the warrior-God imagery.

- v.9 — ASV: "Jehovah also will be a high tower for the oppressed" / WEB: "Yahweh will also be a high tower for the oppressed" / NET: "the Lord provides safety for the oppressed" — KJV uses "refuge" and NET uses the generic "safety," but ASV/WEB preserve the Hebrew "misgav" (high tower/stronghold) which carries specific military fortress imagery.

- v.12 — NET: "For the one who takes revenge against murderers took notice of the oppressed" vs. KJV: "When he maketh inquisition for blood, he remembereth them" — NET dramatically interprets "doresh damim" (the one who investigates bloodshed) with an active, justice-oriented paraphrase. ASV retains the literal "maketh inquisition for blood."

- v.16 — WEB: "Meditation. Selah." vs. KJV/ASV: "Higgaion. Selah" — WEB translates the Hebrew musical term "Higgaion" as "Meditation," while other versions transliterate it. NET places it in parentheses: "(Higgaion. Selah)."

- v.17 — ASV/WEB: "turned back unto Sheol" vs. KJV: "turned into hell" — ASV and WEB preserve the Hebrew "Sheol" (realm of the dead), avoiding KJV's anachronistic "hell" which imports NT eternal-punishment concepts into an OT passage about death and the grave.

- v.18 — NET: "the hopes of the oppressed are not forever dashed" vs. KJV: "the expectation of the poor shall not perish for ever" — NET uses dynamic language ("dashed") and substitutes "oppressed" for "poor" (Hebrew "aniyim"). ASV uses "expectation of the poor" closely following the Hebrew.

- v.20 — NET: "Terrify them, Lord. Let the nations know they are mere mortals" vs. ASV: "Put them in fear, O Jehovah: Let the nations know themselves to be but men" — Both capture "enosh" (frail mortals), but NET's "mere mortals" is more forceful in modern English. The divine name difference (Jehovah/Yahweh/LORD) remains consistent throughout.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Praise with your WHOLE heart (v.1). Not partial, not distracted,
   not reserved. Wholehearted praise is both the goal and the
   evidence of genuine faith. If you cannot praise wholeheartedly,
   ask why — and bring that honestly to God.

2. Knowing God's name produces trust (v.10). Trust is not blind
   faith — it is informed confidence based on known character.
   Study WHO God is, and trust will follow naturally. Distrust
   often stems from ignorance of His character.

3. God has special concern for the oppressed (vv.9, 12, 18). This
   is not peripheral theology — it is central. The God of the
   Bible is consistently oriented toward the vulnerable, the
   crushed, the forgotten. If you are oppressed, He is YOUR
   stronghold specifically. If you are not, align yourself with
   His concerns.

4. "They are but men" (v.20). The great equalizer. Nations,
   rulers, oppressors — all merely mortal. The most powerful human
   is still "enosh" — frail, finite, dust-bound. This truth
   removes both the fear of men and the worship of men.

5. Evil is self-defeating (vv.15-16). The pit made for others
   catches the digger. The net hidden for others traps the one
   who set it. You do not always need to fight evil directly —
   often you need only wait for it to consume itself.
