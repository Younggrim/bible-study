Genesis - Chapter 16
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Sarai gives her servant Hagar to Abram; Hagar conceives Ishmael, but conflict erupts and God meets Hagar in the wilderness.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 16 records Abram and Sarai's attempt to fulfill
God's promise through human effort — taking Hagar (Sarai's Egyptian maid) as
a surrogate. This was culturally acceptable in the ancient Near East but
represented a failure of faith. The result: Ishmael — not the promised son
but a son of the flesh. The chapter also contains the first appearance of
"the angel of the LORD" (v.7) — likely a pre-incarnate appearance of Christ
(theophany) — who meets Hagar in her distress.

Sarai's Plan (vv.1-3): Sarai is barren (v.1). After 10 years in Canaan with
no child (v.3), she proposes Hagar as a surrogate: "go in unto my maid; it
may be that I may obtain children by her" (v.2). Abram agrees (v.2). This was
legal in ancient culture (cf. Code of Hammurabi) but not God's plan.

The Consequences (vv.4-6): Hagar conceives and despises Sarai (v.4). Sarai
blames Abram (v.5). Abram abdicates responsibility: "thy maid is in thy hand"
(v.6). Sarai mistreats Hagar; Hagar flees (v.6). A mess of human making.

The Angel of the LORD and Hagar (vv.7-14): The angel finds Hagar in the
wilderness (v.7). He asks: "whence camest thou? and whither wilt thou go?"
(v.8). Commands: return and submit (v.9). Promises: innumerable descendants
(v.10). Names the child: Ishmael — "God hears" (v.11). Describes Ishmael:
"a wild man; his hand will be against every man" (v.12). Hagar names God:
"Thou God seest me" (v.13) — El Roi.

Ishmael's Birth (vv.15-16): Hagar returns. Ishmael born. Abram is 86 (v.16).

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
• Beer-lahai-roi (v.14) — meaning "the well of the Living One who sees me."
  Located between Kadesh and Bered in the Negev desert, along the road to
  Shur (toward Egypt).
• The wilderness of Shur (v.7) — the desert region between Canaan and Egypt,
  along the trade route. Hagar flees toward her homeland (Egypt).

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "Go in to my servant; it may be that I shall obtain children by her."
        (ESV) — Human effort to fulfill God's promise.

v.7  — "The angel of the LORD found her by a spring of water in the
        wilderness." (ESV) — God seeks the outcast.

v.11 — "You shall call his name Ishmael, because the LORD has listened to
        your affliction." (ESV) — Ishmael = "God hears."

v.12 — "He shall be a wild donkey of a man, his hand against everyone."
        (ESV) — Ishmael's prophetic character.

v.13 — "You are a God of seeing." (ESV) — El Roi: the God who sees.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.2  — KJV: "obtain children by her" / ESV: "obtain children by her" —
       Both identical. Hebrew "ibbaneh" — be built up through her. Surrogate
       motherhood was legal in ancient Mesopotamia. The child would legally
       be Sarai's. Culturally acceptable but not God's plan.

v.7  — KJV: "the angel of the LORD" / ESV: "The angel of the LORD" — Both
       identical. Hebrew "malak Yahweh." First appearance of this figure. In
       many OT passages, the angel of the LORD speaks as God, is identified
       as God, yet is distinct from God — suggesting a pre-incarnate
       appearance of Christ.

v.11 — KJV: "Ishmael" / ESV: "Ishmael" — Both identical. Hebrew "Yishma'el"
       — "God hears." KJV: "the LORD hath heard thy affliction" / ESV: "the
       LORD has listened to your affliction" — Both equivalent; ESV uses
       "listened" for Hebrew "shama."

v.12 — KJV: "a wild man" / ESV: "a wild donkey of a man" — Hebrew "pere
       adam" — a wild donkey of a man. ESV translates more literally,
       including "donkey." The wild donkey was untameable, free-roaming,
       independent. Ishmael's descendants (Arab peoples) would be fierce,
       independent, and in conflict.

v.13 — KJV: "Thou God seest me" / ESV: "You are a God of seeing" — Hebrew
       "El Roi" — the God of seeing, the God who sees me. Both capture the
       meaning; ESV emphasizes God's attribute of seeing, KJV emphasizes the
       personal experience of being seen. Hagar gives God a name based on her
       experience of being seen by Him.

v.14 — KJV: "Beer-lahai-roi" / ESV: "Beer-lahai-roi" — Both identical.
       "The well of the Living One who sees me." A memorial to God's watchful
       care over the outcast.

TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.2 — NET: "please sleep with my servant" — renders Hebrew "bo el" (go in
  to) with modern directness where KJV/ASV use euphemistic "go in unto."
  NET also: "Perhaps I can have a family by her" — interprets "be built up"
  (Hebrew "ibaneh") as "have a family," preserving the wordplay on "banah"
  (build) and "ben" (son).
- v.7 — WEB: "Yahweh's angel" — preserves the construct form of the Hebrew.
  NET: "The angel of the Lord found Hagar" — emphasizes God actively seeking
  the outcast.
- v.11 — NET: "the Lord has heard your painful groans" — interprets
  "affliction" (KJV/ESV) as "painful groans," making Hagar's suffering
  more vivid and connecting it to the name Ishmael ("God hears").
- v.12 — NET: "He will be a wild donkey of a man" — vividly renders the
  Hebrew "pere adam" as an untameable figure of independence and conflict.
- v.13 — WEB: "You are a God who sees" — renders "El Roi" in accessible
  English. NET: "You are the God who sees me" — adds "me" for personal
  emphasis.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Hagar          — Sarai's Egyptian maid, given to Abram as a surrogate.
               Mother of Ishmael. An outcast whom God sees and cares for.

Ishmael        — "God hears." Abram's son through Hagar. Not the promised
               son but blessed by God. Ancestor of Arab peoples.

Angel of the LORD — The first appearance of this figure. Likely a pre-
               incarnate Christ (theophany). Speaks as God, acts as God.

El Roi         — "The God who sees me." Hagar's name for God. He sees the
               invisible, the outcast, the forgotten.

Wild man       — Ishmael's prophetic character: fierce, independent,
               untameable, in conflict. Fulfilled in his descendants.

Human effort to fulfill God's promise — Taking matters into your own hands
               rather than waiting for God's timing. Always produces
               complications.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Sarai's plan (v.2) was culturally acceptable but not God's plan. Where are
   you trying to fulfill God's promises through human effort rather than
   waiting for His timing? "Helping God" often creates more problems.

2. "The angel of the LORD found her" (v.7). God seeks the outcast, the
   runaway, the desperate. Have you ever felt invisible? God sees you. He
   finds you in the wilderness.

3. "Thou God seest me" (v.13). El Roi — the God who sees. He sees your pain,
   your situation, your need. You are not invisible to Him. Does this comfort
   you?

4. Ishmael was born from impatience — Abram was 86 and had waited 10 years
   (v.3). Isaac wouldn't come for 14 more years. God's timing often requires
   longer waiting than we expect. Can you wait?

5. The consequences of Sarai's plan continue to this day (Arab-Israeli
   conflict). Our impatient decisions can have generational consequences.
   What decisions are you making that might affect generations?
