Genesis - Chapter 20
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Abraham deceives Abimelech by calling Sarah his sister; God protects Sarah and rebukes the king in a dream.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 20 records Abraham's second "she is my sister"
deception — this time with Abimelech king of Gerar (the first was with
Pharaoh in ch.12). Remarkably, this happens AFTER God's promise of Isaac
through Sarah (chs.17-18). Abraham's recurring sin of fear-driven deception
threatens the very promise God made. Yet God protects Sarah (and the promise)
despite Abraham's failure. The chapter reveals both Abraham's persistent
weakness and God's persistent faithfulness.

Abraham's Deception (vv.1-2): Abraham moves to Gerar and again says Sarah is
his sister (v.2). Abimelech takes Sarah (v.2). This is the same sin as
chapter 12 — Abraham hasn't fully conquered this fear.

God Protects (vv.3-7): God warns Abimelech in a dream: "thou art but a dead
man" (v.3). Abimelech protests his innocence (vv.4-5). God acknowledges:
"I know... I also withheld thee from sinning" (v.6). God protected Sarah
even from Abraham's failure. Command: restore her; Abraham will pray for you
(v.7).

The Confrontation (vv.8-13): Abimelech rebukes Abraham (vv.9-10). Abraham's
excuse: "I thought, Surely the fear of God is not in this place" (v.11). He
reveals it's a half-truth: Sarah IS his half-sister (v.12). He admits this
was a pre-arranged deception from the beginning (v.13).

Resolution (vv.14-18): Abimelech gives gifts and land (vv.14-15). Abraham
prays; God heals Abimelech's household (vv.17-18).

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
• Gerar (v.1) — a Philistine city in the southern coastal plain, between
  Kadesh and Shur. Abraham moves southwest from Hebron into this region.
• This is Philistine territory — the coastal lowlands of southern Canaan
  (distinct from the hill country where Abraham usually dwells).

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.6  — "I also kept you from sinning against me. Therefore I did not let you
        touch her." (ESV) — God's sovereign protection of His plan.

v.7  — "He is a prophet, and he will pray for you." (ESV) — Abraham's
        identity despite his failure.

v.11 — "I did it because I thought, 'There is no fear of God at all in this
        place.'" (ESV) — Fear-based assumptions leading to sin.

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.2  — Hebrew "achoti hi" — she is my sister.
       KJV: "She is my sister"
       ESV: "She is my sister"
       ASV: "She is my sister"
       NET: "She is my sister"
       WEB: "She is my sister"
       Analysis: All five translations are identical. The same deception as 12:13.
       A half-truth (v.12) used to deceive. Repeated sin — Abraham's persistent
       weakness that threatened the very promise of Isaac.

v.3  — Hebrew "hinneka meth" — behold you are dead / you are a dead man.
       KJV: "thou art but a dead man"
       ESV: "you are a dead man"
       ASV: "thou art but a dead man"
       NET: "You are as good as dead because of the woman you have taken"
       WEB: "you are a dead man"
       Analysis: KJV/ASV/ESV/WEB give the direct Hebrew statement. NET uses the
       strikingly colloquial "as good as dead," a modern idiom that captures the
       urgency of the divine warning. Hebrew "Elohim" (not Yahweh) — God
       communicates with pagans too, to protect His purposes. ESV adds "by night"
       for contextual clarity about the dream.

v.4  — Hebrew "hagoy gam-tsaddiq taharog" — will you also slay a righteous nation?
       KJV: "Lord, wilt thou slay also a righteous nation?"
       ESV: "Lord, will you kill an innocent people?"
       ASV: "Lord, wilt thou slay also a righteous nation?"
       NET: "Lord, would you really slaughter an innocent nation?"
       WEB: "Lord, will you kill even a righteous nation?"
       Analysis: NET uses "slaughter" — stronger than "slay" or "kill" — and frames
       it as a rhetorical question about God's justice. Abimelech's protest
       parallels Abraham's intercession in 18:23-25 regarding divine justice.

v.7  — Hebrew "nabi" — prophet (first use of this term in Scripture).
       KJV: "he is a prophet"
       ESV: "he is a prophet"
       ASV: "he is a prophet"
       NET: "he is a prophet"
       WEB: "he is a prophet"
       Analysis: All identical. First use of "prophet" in Scripture. Despite his
       failure, Abraham retains his prophetic identity and intercessory role. God
       defines identity by calling, not by performance.

v.11 — Hebrew "en yir'ath Elohim" — there is no fear of God.
       KJV: "the fear of God is not in this place"
       ESV: "There is no fear of God at all in this place"
       ASV: "Surely the fear of God is not in this place"
       NET: "Surely no one fears God in this place"
       WEB: "Surely the fear of God is not in this place"
       Analysis: ESV adds "at all" for emphasis. NET personalizes it — "no one
       fears God" — making it about people rather than an abstract quality of the
       place. Ironically, Abimelech showed more integrity than Abraham in this
       episode. Abraham's fear-based assumptions were wrong.

v.13 — Hebrew "hith'u" — caused to wander.
       KJV: "God caused me to wander from my father's house"
       ESV: "God caused me to wander from my father's house"
       ASV: "God caused me to wander from my father's house"
       NET: "When God made me wander from my father's house"
       WEB: "when God caused me to wander from my father's house"
       Analysis: All use "wander" for Hebrew "hith'u." Abraham reveals that he sees
       his entire life as divinely-directed wandering — and that this deception was
       a pre-arranged strategy from the very beginning of his journey.

v.16 — Hebrew "kesuth 'enayim" — a covering of the eyes (legal vindication).
       KJV: "he is to thee a covering of the eyes"
       ESV: "it is a sign of your innocence"
       ASV: "it is for thee a covering of the eyes"
       NET: "This is compensation for you so that you will stand vindicated"
       WEB: "it is for you a covering of the eyes"
       Analysis: The Hebrew idiom is difficult. KJV/ASV/WEB retain the literal
       "covering of the eyes." ESV interprets as "sign of innocence." NET makes the
       cultural/legal significance fully explicit — the silver payment serves as
       legal vindication for Sarah's honor before all.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Repeated sin   — Abraham's recurring deception about Sarah. Persistent
               weakness that God's grace covers but doesn't excuse.

God withheld   — Sovereign protection. God prevented Abimelech from sinning
               — protecting Sarah and the promise despite Abraham's failure.

Prophet        — First use of this term in Scripture. Applied to Abraham —
               one who speaks for God and intercedes before God.

Half-truth     — Sarah was Abraham's half-sister (v.12). Technically true
               but intentionally deceptive. Half-truths are whole lies.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Abraham repeated the same sin from chapter 12. Do you have recurring sins
   — patterns you keep falling into? What would it take to break the cycle?

2. God protected His plan despite Abraham's failure (v.6). Your failures
   cannot derail God's purposes. Does this comfort you? It should — but it
   shouldn't make you careless.

3. Abraham assumed "the fear of God is not in this place" (v.11). He was
   wrong — Abimelech had more integrity than Abraham. Are you making
   assumptions about people that lead you to compromise?

4. Despite his failure, Abraham was still called "a prophet" (v.7) and still
   interceded effectively (v.17). God uses imperfect people. Your failures
   don't disqualify you from service — but they do need to be addressed.
