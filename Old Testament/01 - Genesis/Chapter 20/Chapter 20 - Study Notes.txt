Genesis - Chapter 20
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Abraham deceives Abimelech by calling Sarah his sister; God protects Sarah and rebukes the king in a dream.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 20 records Abraham's second "she is my sister"
deception — this time with Abimelech king of Gerar (the first was with
Pharaoh in ch.12). Remarkably, this happens AFTER God's promise of Isaac
through Sarah (chs.17-18). Abraham's recurring sin of fear-driven deception
threatens the very promise God made. Yet God protects Sarah (and the promise)
despite Abraham's failure. The chapter reveals both Abraham's persistent
weakness and God's persistent faithfulness.

Abraham's Deception (vv.1-2): Abraham moves to Gerar and again says Sarah is
his sister (v.2). Abimelech takes Sarah (v.2). This is the same sin as
chapter 12 — Abraham hasn't fully conquered this fear.

God Protects (vv.3-7): God warns Abimelech in a dream: "thou art but a dead
man" (v.3). Abimelech protests his innocence (vv.4-5). God acknowledges:
"I know... I also withheld thee from sinning" (v.6). God protected Sarah
even from Abraham's failure. Command: restore her; Abraham will pray for you
(v.7).

The Confrontation (vv.8-13): Abimelech rebukes Abraham (vv.9-10). Abraham's
excuse: "I thought, Surely the fear of God is not in this place" (v.11). He
reveals it's a half-truth: Sarah IS his half-sister (v.12). He admits this
was a pre-arranged deception from the beginning (v.13).

Resolution (vv.14-18): Abimelech gives gifts and land (vv.14-15). Abraham
prays; God heals Abimelech's household (vv.17-18).

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
• Gerar (v.1) — a Philistine city in the southern coastal plain, between
  Kadesh and Shur. Abraham moves southwest from Hebron into this region.
• This is Philistine territory — the coastal lowlands of southern Canaan
  (distinct from the hill country where Abraham usually dwells).

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.6  — "I also kept you from sinning against me. Therefore I did not let you
        touch her." (ESV) — God's sovereign protection of His plan.

v.7  — "He is a prophet, and he will pray for you." (ESV) — Abraham's
        identity despite his failure.

v.11 — "I did it because I thought, 'There is no fear of God at all in this
        place.'" (ESV) — Fear-based assumptions leading to sin.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.2  — KJV: "She is my sister" / ESV: "She is my sister" — Both identical.
       The same deception as 12:13. A half-truth (v.12) used to deceive.
       Repeated sin — Abraham's persistent weakness.

v.3  — KJV: "God came to Abimelech in a dream" / ESV: "God came to
       Abimelech in a dream by night" — Both equivalent; ESV adds "by night"
       for clarity. Hebrew "Elohim" (not Yahweh). God communicates with
       pagans too — to protect His purposes. KJV: "thou art but a dead man"
       / ESV: "you are a dead man" — Both equivalent.

v.7  — KJV: "he is a prophet" / ESV: "he is a prophet" — Both identical.
       Hebrew "nabi" — first use of "prophet" in Scripture. Despite his
       failure, Abraham retains his prophetic identity and intercessory role.

v.11 — KJV: "the fear of God is not in this place" / ESV: "There is no fear
       of God at all in this place" — Both equivalent; ESV adds "at all" for
       emphasis. Abraham assumed the worst about pagans. Ironically,
       Abimelech showed more integrity than Abraham in this episode.

TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.3 — NET: "You are as good as dead because of the woman you have taken" —
  strikingly colloquial rendering of Hebrew "hinneka meth" (KJV: "thou art
  but a dead man"). NET's modern idiom captures the urgency of the warning.
- v.4 — NET: "Lord, would you really slaughter an innocent nation?" — uses
  "slaughter" (stronger than "slay/destroy") and frames it as a rhetorical
  question about God's justice.
- v.13 — NET: "When God made me wander from my father's house" — uses "wander"
  (Hebrew "hith'u"), revealing that Abraham sees his life as divinely-directed
  wandering; KJV/ESV use "caused me to wander."
- v.16 — NET: "This is compensation for you so that you will stand vindicated"
  — interprets the silver payment as legal vindication for Sarah, making the
  cultural/legal significance explicit.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Repeated sin   — Abraham's recurring deception about Sarah. Persistent
               weakness that God's grace covers but doesn't excuse.

God withheld   — Sovereign protection. God prevented Abimelech from sinning
               — protecting Sarah and the promise despite Abraham's failure.

Prophet        — First use of this term in Scripture. Applied to Abraham —
               one who speaks for God and intercedes before God.

Half-truth     — Sarah was Abraham's half-sister (v.12). Technically true
               but intentionally deceptive. Half-truths are whole lies.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Abraham repeated the same sin from chapter 12. Do you have recurring sins
   — patterns you keep falling into? What would it take to break the cycle?

2. God protected His plan despite Abraham's failure (v.6). Your failures
   cannot derail God's purposes. Does this comfort you? It should — but it
   shouldn't make you careless.

3. Abraham assumed "the fear of God is not in this place" (v.11). He was
   wrong — Abimelech had more integrity than Abraham. Are you making
   assumptions about people that lead you to compromise?

4. Despite his failure, Abraham was still called "a prophet" (v.7) and still
   interceded effectively (v.17). God uses imperfect people. Your failures
   don't disqualify you from service — but they do need to be addressed.
