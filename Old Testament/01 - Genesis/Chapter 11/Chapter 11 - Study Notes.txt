Genesis - Chapter 11
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Humanity rebels at Babel, building a tower to make a name for themselves; God confuses their language and scatters them, then traces the line from Shem to Abram.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 11 has two parts: the Tower of Babel (vv.1-9) and
the genealogy from Shem to Abram (vv.10-32). Babel represents humanity's
united rebellion against God — building a tower to "make us a name" rather
than filling the earth as commanded. God responds by confusing languages and
scattering the people. The genealogy then narrows from all nations to one
family — Terah's — setting the stage for Abram's call in chapter 12.

The Tower of Babel (vv.1-9): One language, one people (v.1). They settle in
Shinar (Babylon) and decide to build a city and tower "whose top may reach
unto heaven" (v.4). Their motive: "let us make us a name, lest we be
scattered" (v.4) — directly opposing God's command to fill the earth (9:1).
God "came down" to see their work (v.5 — ironic: their tower reaching heaven
is so small God must come down). God confuses their language (v.7) and
scatters them (v.8). The city is named "Babel" — confusion (v.9).

The Genealogy of Shem (vv.10-26): Ten generations from Shem to Abram.
Lifespans decrease dramatically (Shem 600 → Terah 205). The line narrows
toward the one man through whom God will bless all nations.

Terah's Family (vv.27-32): Terah has three sons: Abram, Nahor, Haran. Haran
dies young in Ur (v.28). Abram marries Sarai — "she was barren" (v.30). Terah
takes the family from Ur toward Canaan but stops at Haran (v.31). Terah dies
in Haran (v.32). The stage is set for God's call.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
• Shinar (v.2) — the plain of Babylonia (modern southern Iraq). The people
  migrate eastward (away from God's presence, the recurring Genesis pattern).
• Babel (v.9) — Babylon, one of the most significant cities in ancient
  history. Its name means "gate of god" but is linked to "confusion" (balal).
• Ur of the Chaldees (v.28) — Abram's origin city in southern Mesopotamia
  (modern Tell el-Muqayyar, Iraq). A major Sumerian urban center.
• Haran (v.31) — a city in northwest Mesopotamia (modern Harran, Turkey), a
  caravan crossroads on the route between Ur and Canaan.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.4  — "Come, let us build ourselves a city and a tower with its top in the
        heavens, and let us make a name for ourselves, lest we be dispersed."
        (ESV) — Human rebellion: self-glory and self-determination.

v.5  — "And the LORD came down to see the city and the tower." (ESV) —
        Ironic: their great tower is so small God must descend to see it.

v.7  — "Come, let us go down and there confuse their language." (ESV) —
        God's judgment on united rebellion.

v.9  — "Therefore its name was called Babel, because there the LORD confused
        the language of all the earth." (ESV) — Babel = confusion.

v.30 — "Now Sarai was barren; she had no child." (ESV) — The obstacle that
        sets up God's miraculous provision.

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1  — Hebrew "saphah echath udbarim achadim" — one lip and one set of words.
       KJV: "the whole earth was of one language, and of one speech"
       ESV: "the whole earth had one language and the same words"
       ASV: "the whole earth was of one language and of one speech"
       NET: "the whole earth had a common language and a common vocabulary"
       WEB: "The whole earth was of one language and of one speech"
       Analysis: NET uniquely distinguishes between "language" (grammar/
       system) and "vocabulary" (shared words) — rendering the Hebrew double
       expression as two distinct concepts. KJV/ASV/WEB combine them as
       "one language and one speech." ESV's "same words" is closest to the
       literal Hebrew "dbarim achadim."

v.3  — Hebrew "chemar lachem lachomer" — bitumen/tar for mortar.
       KJV: "slime had they for mortar"
       ESV: "bitumen for mortar"
       ASV: "slime had they for mortar"
       NET: "tar for mortar"
       WEB: "they used tar for mortar"
       Analysis: KJV/ASV's "slime" is archaic English for bitumen/tar. ESV
       uses the technical "bitumen." NET/WEB use "tar" — most accessible for
       modern readers. All describe the same waterproof asphalt substance
       abundant in Mesopotamia.

v.4  — Hebrew "umigdal verosho bashamayim vena'aseh-lanu shem"
       KJV: "a tower, whose top may reach unto heaven... let us make us a name"
       ESV: "a tower with its top in the heavens... let us make a name for ourselves"
       ASV: "a tower, whose top may reach unto heaven... let us make us a name"
       NET: "a tower whose top reaches to the heavens... Let us make a name for ourselves"
       WEB: "a tower whose top reaches to the sky... let's make a name for ourselves"
       Analysis: WEB uses "sky" instead of "heaven(s)" — distinguishing
       physical height from spiritual aspiration. A ziggurat — a stepped
       temple-tower. Self-glorification rather than glorifying God. Contrast
       with 12:2 where GOD makes Abram's name great.

v.6  — Hebrew "lo-yibbazer mehem kol asher yazmu la'asoth"
       KJV: "nothing will be restrained from them, which they have imagined to do"
       ESV: "nothing that they propose to do will now be impossible for them"
       ASV: "nothing will be withholden from them, which they purpose to do"
       NET: "nothing they plan to do will be beyond them"
       WEB: "nothing will be withheld from them, which they intend to do"
       Analysis: NET's "beyond them" is most dynamic. ESV's "impossible" is
       strong. KJV/ASV/WEB retain the restraint language. God acknowledges
       human potential when unified — and acts to limit it because their
       unified purpose is rebellion.

v.7  — Hebrew "havah nerdah venablah sham sephatam"
       KJV: "let us go down, and there confound their language"
       ESV: "let us go down and there confuse their language"
       ASV: "let us go down, and there confound their language"
       NET: "Come, let's go down and confuse their language"
       WEB: "Come, let's go down, and there confuse their language"
       Analysis: The divine plural again (cf. 1:26, 3:22) — the Trinity in
       action. ESV/NET/WEB modernize "confound" to "confuse" — same Hebrew
       "balal." NET/WEB use informal "let's" instead of formal "let us."

v.9  — Hebrew "Bavel... balal" — Babel... confused (wordplay).
       KJV: "Therefore is the name of it called Babel; because the LORD did there confound the language"
       ESV: "Therefore its name was called Babel, because there the LORD confused the language"
       ASV: "Therefore was the name of it called Babel; because Jehovah did there confound the language"
       NET: "That is why its name was called Babel—because there the LORD confused the language"
       WEB: "Therefore its name was called Babel, because there Yahweh confused the language"
       Analysis: All preserve the Hebrew wordplay: "Babel" from "balal"
       (confuse/mix). The Babylonians claimed "Babel" meant "gate of God"
       (Bab-ili). God says it means "confusion." ASV uses "Jehovah"; WEB
       uses "Yahweh."

v.30 — Hebrew "aqarah" — barren, unable to conceive.
       KJV: "Sarai was barren; she had no child"
       ESV: "Sarai was barren; she had no child"
       ASV: "Sarai was barren; she had no child"
       NET: "Sarai was barren; she had no child"
       WEB: "Sarai was barren. She had no child"
       Analysis: All translations identical. A critical detail: God's promise
       of a great nation (12:2) will require a miracle. The impossible
       situation sets up divine intervention.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Tower of Babel — Humanity's united attempt to reach heaven and make a name
               apart from God. Represents organized rebellion.

Babel/Confusion — The result of God's judgment on human pride. Languages
               confused, people scattered.

"Let us make us a name" — Self-glorification. The opposite of living for
               God's glory. The root sin of Babel.

God came down  — Ironic divine descent. Humanity's greatest achievement is
               so small that God must stoop to see it.

Sarai was barren — The humanly impossible situation that sets up God's
               miraculous intervention. God works through impossibility.

Ur of the Chaldees — Abram's hometown in southern Mesopotamia (modern Iraq).
               A sophisticated pagan city. God calls Abram OUT of paganism.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Let us make us a name" (v.4). Where are you building for YOUR name rather
   than God's? Self-glory is the essence of Babel. Are you building a tower
   of reputation, achievement, or control?

2. "The LORD came down to see" (v.5). Your greatest achievements are tiny
   from God's perspective. Does this humble you? Does it free you from the
   pressure to impress?

3. God scattered what humanity united in rebellion (v.8). But at Pentecost
   (Acts 2), God united what was scattered — through the gospel. The cure for
   Babel is not human unity but Spirit-created unity in Christ.

4. "Sarai was barren" (v.30). God's greatest works often begin with
   impossibility. Where in your life does the situation seem impossible? That
   may be exactly where God plans to work.

5. Terah started toward Canaan but stopped at Haran (v.31). He got halfway
   and settled. Are you settling for halfway obedience? God's call requires
   going all the way.
