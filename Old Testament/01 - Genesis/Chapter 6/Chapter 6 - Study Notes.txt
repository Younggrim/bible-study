Genesis - Chapter 6
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Humanity's wickedness grieves God; He determines to send a flood but saves Noah by grace.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 6 describes the world's moral collapse before the
flood and God's decision to judge humanity while preserving Noah. The chapter
raises difficult interpretive questions (the "sons of God," the Nephilim) but
its main point is clear: sin had so corrupted humanity that God grieved having
made them. Yet grace prevails — "Noah found grace in the eyes of the LORD"
(v.8). God provides a way of salvation even as He announces judgment.

The Corruption of Humanity (vv.1-7): "Sons of God" took "daughters of men"
(v.2) — debated: (1) fallen angels cohabiting with humans (cf. Jude 6-7,
2 Pet 2:4), (2) the godly line of Seth intermarrying with Cain's ungodly
line, (3) powerful rulers/tyrants taking women by force. The result: Nephilim
("giants/fallen ones," v.4) and total moral corruption. "Every imagination of
the thoughts of his heart was only evil continually" (v.5). God was "grieved
at his heart" (v.6) and determined to destroy (v.7).

Noah Finds Grace (vv.8-10): "But Noah found grace" (v.8) — the first mention
of grace in Scripture. Noah was "just" (righteous), "perfect in his
generations" (blameless among his contemporaries), and "walked with God" (v.9).

The Ark Commanded (vv.11-22): The earth was "corrupt" and "filled with
violence" (vv.11-12). God tells Noah His plan and commands him to build an ark
(vv.13-16): 300 cubits long (~450 feet), 50 wide (~75 feet), 30 high (~45
feet). God establishes His covenant with Noah (v.18). Two of every kind to be
preserved (vv.19-20). "Thus did Noah; according to all that God commanded him,
so did he" (v.22).

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
• No specific geography in this chapter — the focus is on universal human
  wickedness and God's command to build the ark.
• The flood narrative is global in scope; no particular region is named.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.5  — "The LORD saw that the wickedness of man was great in the earth, and
        that every intention of the thoughts of his heart was only evil
        continually." (ESV) — Total depravity before the flood.

v.6  — "And the LORD regretted that he had made man on the earth, and it
        grieved him to his heart." (ESV) — God's grief over sin.

v.8  — "But Noah found favor in the eyes of the LORD." (ESV) — Grace: the
        first occurrence in Scripture.

v.9  — "Noah was a righteous man, blameless in his generation. Noah walked
        with God." (ESV) — Noah's character.

v.22 — "Noah did this; he did all that God commanded him." (ESV) — Complete
        obedience.

Charles Spurgeon:
  Sermon: "Noah Found Grace" on v.8 (MTP Vol. 22, No. 1310)
  "BUT Noah — the most important 'but' in the chapter. The whole world is
  corrupt, violent, evil continually — BUT Noah. In a sea of wickedness, one
  man stands. And how? He FOUND grace — he didn't earn it, didn't deserve it,
  didn't manufacture it. He found it. Grace found him. In the darkest hour of
  human history, grace was still available. It still is."
  Sermon: "Complete Obedience" on v.22 (MTP Vol. 14, No. 827)
  "According to ALL that God commanded him, so did he. Not some — all. Not
  mostly — completely. Not approximately — exactly. Noah didn't edit God's
  instructions. He didn't improve on the design. He didn't skip the hard
  parts. All that God commanded — so did he. This is the obedience that saves:
  complete, unquestioning, faithful."

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.2  — Hebrew "bene ha-Elohim... toboth" — sons of God... good/beautiful.
       KJV: "the sons of God saw the daughters of men that they were fair"
       ESV: "the sons of God saw that the daughters of man were attractive"
       ASV: "the sons of God saw the daughters of men that they were fair"
       NET: "the sons of God saw that the daughters of humankind were beautiful"
       WEB: "God's sons saw that men's daughters were beautiful"
       Analysis: In Job 1:6, 2:1, 38:7 "bene ha-Elohim" refers to angels.
       The debate: fallen angels, godly Sethites, or rulers? WEB uniquely
       renders "God's sons" (possessive) and "men's daughters." NET uses
       "humankind." KJV/ASV's "fair" is archaic for "beautiful."

v.3  — Hebrew "lo-yadon ruchi va'adam... me'ah ve'esrim shanah"
       KJV: "My spirit shall not always strive with man... an hundred and twenty years"
       ESV: "My Spirit shall not abide in man forever... 120 years"
       ASV: "My Spirit shall not strive with man for ever... a hundred and twenty years"
       NET: "My Spirit will not remain in humankind indefinitely, since they are mortal. They will remain for 120 more years"
       WEB: "My Spirit will not strive with man forever... one hundred twenty years"
       Analysis: Hebrew "yadon" is debated — strive, contend, abide, remain.
       KJV/ASV/WEB read it as God's patience contending with sin. ESV/NET
       read it as God's Spirit not remaining in/with man. NET adds "since
       they are mortal" as explanatory gloss and makes the countdown explicit.

v.4  — Hebrew "Nephilim" — from "naphal" (to fall).
       KJV: "There were giants in the earth"
       ESV: "The Nephilim were on the earth"
       ASV: "The Nephilim were in the earth"
       NET: "The Nephilim were on the earth"
       WEB: "The Nephilim were in the earth"
       Analysis: KJV alone translates as "giants" (following LXX "gigantes").
       All modern translations retain the Hebrew untranslated, preserving
       the ambiguity. The Nephilim may have been large, but "fallen ones" is
       the root meaning. NET also renders the sexual union bluntly: "the sons
       of God would sleep with the daughters of humankind" where others use
       "came in to."

v.6  — Hebrew "nacham... itstsabon" — grieved/sorry... pained.
       KJV: "it repented the LORD that he had made man... it grieved him at his heart"
       ESV: "the LORD regretted that he had made man... it grieved him to his heart"
       ASV: "it repented Jehovah that he had made man... it grieved him at his heart"
       NET: "the LORD was sorry that he had made humankind... he was highly offended"
       WEB: "Yahweh was sorry that he had made man... it grieved him in his heart"
       Analysis: KJV/ASV's "repented" can confuse readers — God doesn't
       repent of sin. ESV's "regretted" and NET's "was sorry" are clearer.
       NET's "highly offended" for the second clause is strikingly different
       — shifting from grief/sorrow to offense/anger. ASV uses "Jehovah";
       WEB uses "Yahweh."

v.8  — Hebrew "chen" — favor, grace, kindness.
       KJV: "Noah found grace in the eyes of the LORD"
       ESV: "Noah found favor in the eyes of the LORD"
       ASV: "Noah found favor in the eyes of Jehovah"
       NET: "Noah found favor in the sight of the LORD"
       WEB: "Noah found favor in Yahweh's eyes"
       Analysis: KJV's "grace" carries stronger theological weight; ESV/ASV/
       NET/WEB all use "favor" — more literal for "chen." First occurrence
       of "chen" in Scripture. Grace precedes righteousness — Noah didn't
       earn it, he found it.

v.9  — Hebrew "tsaddiq tamim" — righteous, blameless/complete.
       KJV: "a just man and perfect in his generations"
       ESV: "a righteous man, blameless in his generation"
       ASV: "a righteous man, and perfect in his generations"
       NET: "a godly man; he was blameless among his contemporaries"
       WEB: "a righteous man, blameless among the people of his time"
       Analysis: NET uses "godly" for "tsaddiq" (usually righteous/just) and
       "contemporaries" for "generations." KJV/ASV's "perfect" can mislead —
       Hebrew "tamim" means whole, having integrity, not sinless perfection.
       WEB's "people of his time" is the most interpretive for "generations."

v.14 — Hebrew "atsey-gopher... kopher" — gopher wood... pitch/covering.
       KJV: "Make thee an ark of gopher wood... pitch it within and without with pitch"
       ESV: "Make yourself an ark of gopher wood... cover it inside and out with pitch"
       ASV: "Make thee an ark of gopher wood... shalt pitch it within and without with pitch"
       NET: "Make for yourself an ark of cypress wood... coat it with pitch inside and out"
       WEB: "Make a ship of gopher wood... coat it inside and outside with pitch"
       Analysis: NET uniquely identifies "gopher" as "cypress" — a scholarly
       guess. WEB renders "tevah" (ark) as "ship," emphasizing function.
       Hebrew "kopher" (pitch) shares the same root as "atonement" (kaphar)
       — the ark was literally "atoned"/covered/sealed.

v.15 — Dimensions: 300 × 50 × 30 cubits (ratio 6:1:0.6 — remarkably stable).
       KJV: "three hundred cubits... fifty cubits... thirty cubits"
       ESV: "300 cubits... 50 cubits... 30 cubits"
       ASV: "three hundred cubits... fifty cubits... thirty cubits"
       NET: "450 feet long, 75 feet wide, and 45 feet high"
       WEB: "three hundred cubits... fifty cubits... thirty cubits"
       Analysis: NET converts to modern measurements (feet), making
       dimensions immediately accessible. All others retain ancient cubit
       measurements.

v.22 — Hebrew "vayyaas Noach kekhol asher tsivvah oto Elohim ken asah"
       KJV: "Thus did Noah; according to all that God commanded him, so did he"
       ESV: "Noah did this; he did all that God commanded him"
       ASV: "Thus did Noah; according to all that God commanded him, so did he"
       NET: "And Noah did all that God commanded him—he did indeed"
       WEB: "Thus Noah did. He did all that God commanded him"
       Analysis: All convey complete, exact obedience. NET adds "he did
       indeed" — emphasizing thoroughness. The repetition ("did... did")
       underscores Noah's faithful, total compliance.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Sons of God    — Debated figures who intermarried with "daughters of men."
               Options: fallen angels, godly Sethites, or powerful rulers.

Nephilim       — "Giants" or "fallen ones." Mighty beings of the pre-flood
               world. Their exact nature remains debated.

Every imagination only evil continually — Total depravity. The complete
               corruption of human thought and desire before the flood.

It repented the LORD — God's genuine grief over sin. Not a change of mind
               but deep emotional response to humanity's corruption.

Noah found grace — The first mention of grace in Scripture. Unmerited favor
               given to one man in a world of wickedness.

The ark        — God's provision of salvation through judgment. A vessel of
               preservation — one door, one way of escape.

According to all that God commanded — Complete, exact obedience. Noah did
               everything God said, exactly as God said it.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Every imagination of the thoughts of his heart was only evil continually"
   (v.5). This describes humanity without God's restraint. What does your
   thought life look like? Are you guarding your mind or letting it run
   unchecked?

2. "Noah found grace" (v.8). In the worst generation in history, grace was
   available. No matter how dark your world, grace is findable. Have you
   found it?

3. "Noah walked with God" (v.9). Like Enoch before him (5:24), Noah walked
   with God in a corrupt generation. You don't need a godly culture to walk
   with God — you can walk with Him anywhere. Are you?

4. "According to all that God commanded him, so did he" (v.22). Noah's
   obedience was complete — not partial, not selective. Is your obedience
   complete? Or do you obey the comfortable commands and ignore the hard ones?

5. The ark had one door (v.16). There was one way of salvation. Jesus said:
   "I am the door" (John 10:9). Have you entered through the one door?
