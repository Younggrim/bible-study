Genesis - Chapter 6
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Humanity's wickedness grieves God; He determines to send a flood but saves Noah by grace.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 6 describes the world's moral collapse before the
flood and God's decision to judge humanity while preserving Noah. The chapter
raises difficult interpretive questions (the "sons of God," the Nephilim) but
its main point is clear: sin had so corrupted humanity that God grieved having
made them. Yet grace prevails — "Noah found grace in the eyes of the LORD"
(v.8). God provides a way of salvation even as He announces judgment.

The Corruption of Humanity (vv.1-7): "Sons of God" took "daughters of men"
(v.2) — debated: (1) fallen angels cohabiting with humans (cf. Jude 6-7,
2 Pet 2:4), (2) the godly line of Seth intermarrying with Cain's ungodly
line, (3) powerful rulers/tyrants taking women by force. The result: Nephilim
("giants/fallen ones," v.4) and total moral corruption. "Every imagination of
the thoughts of his heart was only evil continually" (v.5). God was "grieved
at his heart" (v.6) and determined to destroy (v.7).

Noah Finds Grace (vv.8-10): "But Noah found grace" (v.8) — the first mention
of grace in Scripture. Noah was "just" (righteous), "perfect in his
generations" (blameless among his contemporaries), and "walked with God" (v.9).

The Ark Commanded (vv.11-22): The earth was "corrupt" and "filled with
violence" (vv.11-12). God tells Noah His plan and commands him to build an ark
(vv.13-16): 300 cubits long (~450 feet), 50 wide (~75 feet), 30 high (~45
feet). God establishes His covenant with Noah (v.18). Two of every kind to be
preserved (vv.19-20). "Thus did Noah; according to all that God commanded him,
so did he" (v.22).

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
• No specific geography in this chapter — the focus is on universal human
  wickedness and God's command to build the ark.
• The flood narrative is global in scope; no particular region is named.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.5  — "The LORD saw that the wickedness of man was great in the earth, and
        that every intention of the thoughts of his heart was only evil
        continually." (ESV) — Total depravity before the flood.

v.6  — "And the LORD regretted that he had made man on the earth, and it
        grieved him to his heart." (ESV) — God's grief over sin.

v.8  — "But Noah found favor in the eyes of the LORD." (ESV) — Grace: the
        first occurrence in Scripture.

v.9  — "Noah was a righteous man, blameless in his generation. Noah walked
        with God." (ESV) — Noah's character.

v.22 — "Noah did this; he did all that God commanded him." (ESV) — Complete
        obedience.

  "BUT Noah — the most important 'but' in the chapter. The whole world is
  corrupt, violent, evil continually — BUT Noah. In a sea of wickedness, one
  man stands. And how? He FOUND grace — he didn't earn it, didn't deserve it,
  didn't manufacture it. He found it. Grace found him. In the darkest hour of
  human history, grace was still available. It still is."
  Sermon: "Complete Obedience" on v.22 (MTP Vol. 14, No. 827)

  mostly — completely. Not approximately — exactly. Noah didn't edit God's
  instructions. He didn't improve on the design. He didn't skip the hard
  parts. All that God commanded — so did he. This is the obedience that saves:
  complete, unquestioning, faithful."

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.2  — KJV: "sons of God saw the daughters of men that they were fair" /
       ESV: "the sons of God saw that the daughters of man were attractive"
       — Both equivalent. Hebrew "bene ha-Elohim." In Job 1:6, 2:1, 38:7
       this phrase refers to angels. The debate: are these fallen angels,
       godly Sethites, or human rulers? Each view has strengths and problems.
       KJV's "fair" and ESV's "attractive" both render Hebrew "toboth"
       (good/beautiful).

v.3  — KJV: "My spirit shall not always strive with man" / ESV: "My Spirit
       shall not abide in man forever" — Hebrew "yadon" — debated: strive,
       contend, abide, remain. KJV reads it as God's patience contending
       with sinful man; ESV reads it as God's Spirit not remaining in/with
       man indefinitely. Both are legitimate readings of a difficult Hebrew
       word. "An hundred and twenty years" — either the remaining time before
       the flood or a new limit on human lifespan.

v.4  — KJV: "giants" / ESV: "Nephilim" — Hebrew "Nephilim" — from "naphal"
       (to fall). ESV retains the Hebrew word untranslated; KJV translates
       it as "giants" (following the LXX "gigantes"). ESV's approach
       preserves the ambiguity of the original; the Nephilim may have been
       large but "fallen ones" is the root meaning.

v.5  — KJV: "every imagination of the thoughts of his heart was only evil
       continually" / ESV: "every intention of the thoughts of his heart was
       only evil continually" — Hebrew "yetser" — imagination, inclination,
       intention, formation. ESV's "intention" and KJV's "imagination" both
       capture aspects of this word. Five intensifiers: every, intention/
       imagination, thoughts, only, continually. Total, comprehensive,
       unrelenting evil.

v.6  — KJV: "it repented the LORD" / ESV: "the LORD regretted" — Hebrew
       "nacham" — to be grieved, to be sorry, to relent. ESV's "regretted"
       is more natural modern English; KJV's "repented" can confuse readers
       (God doesn't repent of sin). Not that God changed His mind (Num 23:19)
       but that He experienced genuine grief. Anthropopathic language —
       describing God's emotions in human terms.

v.7  — KJV: "I will destroy man" / ESV: "I will blot out man" — Hebrew
       "emcheh" — to wipe away, blot out, erase. ESV's "blot out" is more
       literal to the Hebrew; KJV's "destroy" captures the effect.

v.8  — KJV: "grace" / ESV: "favor" — Hebrew "chen" — favor, grace,
       kindness. Both are valid translations. KJV's "grace" carries stronger
       theological weight in English; ESV's "favor" is more literal. First
       occurrence of "chen" in Scripture. Noah didn't earn favor — he found
       it. Grace precedes righteousness.

v.9  — KJV: "a just man and perfect in his generations" / ESV: "a righteous
       man, blameless in his generation" — Hebrew "tsaddiq" (righteous/just)
       and "tamim" (blameless/complete/without blemish). ESV's "blameless" is
       clearer than KJV's "perfect" (which modern readers may take as
       sinless). "Tamim" means whole, complete, having integrity — not
       sinless perfection.

v.14 — KJV: "gopher wood" / ESV: "gopher wood" — Both identical. Unknown
       wood type. Possibly cypress. "Pitch" — Hebrew "kopher" — same root as
       "atonement" (kaphar). The ark was "atoned" — covered/sealed.

v.15 — The ark's dimensions (300 x 50 x 30 cubits) give a ratio of 6:1:0.6
       — remarkably stable for a vessel. Both translations render the
       measurements identically.

v.16 — KJV: "A window shalt thou make" / ESV: "Make a roof for the ark" —
       Hebrew "tsohar" — debated: window, roof opening, or light source. KJV
       reads it as "window"; ESV reads it as "roof." The Hebrew is uncertain.

v.22 — KJV: "Thus did Noah; according to all that God commanded him, so did
       he" / ESV: "Noah did this; he did all that God commanded him" — Both
       equivalent. Complete, exact obedience.

TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.3 — NET: "My Spirit will not remain in humankind indefinitely, since they
  are mortal" — interprets difficult Hebrew "yadon" as "remain" and adds
  "since they are mortal" as an explanatory gloss. Also: "They will remain
  for 120 more years" — makes the countdown to judgment explicit.
- v.4 — ASV retains "Nephilim" untranslated (like ESV), while NET renders the
  sexual union bluntly: "the sons of God would sleep with the daughters of
  humankind" — making the transgression graphic where KJV/ESV use "came in to."
- v.6 — NET: "he was highly offended" — a strikingly different rendering of
  Hebrew "itstsabon" (KJV: "it grieved him at his heart" / ESV: "it grieved
  him to his heart"). NET shifts from grief/sorrow to offense/anger.
- v.14 — NET: "cypress wood" — translates "gopher wood" as "cypress" where
  all others leave it untranslated, reflecting a scholarly identification.
  WEB: "Make a ship" — renders "tevah" (ark) as "ship," emphasizing its
  function as a seagoing vessel.
- v.15 — NET converts cubits to modern measurements (450 feet, 75 feet, 45
  feet), making dimensions immediately accessible. Other translations retain
  the ancient cubit measurements.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Sons of God    — Debated figures who intermarried with "daughters of men."
               Options: fallen angels, godly Sethites, or powerful rulers.

Nephilim       — "Giants" or "fallen ones." Mighty beings of the pre-flood
               world. Their exact nature remains debated.

Every imagination only evil continually — Total depravity. The complete
               corruption of human thought and desire before the flood.

It repented the LORD — God's genuine grief over sin. Not a change of mind
               but deep emotional response to humanity's corruption.

Noah found grace — The first mention of grace in Scripture. Unmerited favor
               given to one man in a world of wickedness.

The ark        — God's provision of salvation through judgment. A vessel of
               preservation — one door, one way of escape.

According to all that God commanded — Complete, exact obedience. Noah did
               everything God said, exactly as God said it.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Every imagination of the thoughts of his heart was only evil continually"
   (v.5). This describes humanity without God's restraint. What does your
   thought life look like? Are you guarding your mind or letting it run
   unchecked?

2. "Noah found grace" (v.8). In the worst generation in history, grace was
   available. No matter how dark your world, grace is findable. Have you
   found it?

3. "Noah walked with God" (v.9). Like Enoch before him (5:24), Noah walked
   with God in a corrupt generation. You don't need a godly culture to walk
   with God — you can walk with Him anywhere. Are you?

4. "According to all that God commanded him, so did he" (v.22). Noah's
   obedience was complete — not partial, not selective. Is your obedience
   complete? Or do you obey the comfortable commands and ignore the hard ones?

5. The ark had one door (v.16). There was one way of salvation. Jesus said:
   "I am the door" (John 10:9). Have you entered through the one door?
