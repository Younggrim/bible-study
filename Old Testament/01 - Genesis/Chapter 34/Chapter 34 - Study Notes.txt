Genesis - Chapter 34
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Shechem defiles Dinah; her brothers Simeon and Levi deceive the city and slaughter its men in revenge, bringing trouble on Jacob.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 34 records one of the darkest episodes in Genesis
— the rape of Dinah and the violent revenge of Simeon and Levi. Jacob has
settled near Shechem (33:18-19) rather than returning to Bethel as God
commanded. His daughter Dinah is violated by Shechem (the prince), and her
brothers respond with treacherous, disproportionate violence — massacring an
entire city. The chapter shows the consequences of settling in the wrong place
and the danger of unchecked anger. Jacob's passivity and his sons' brutality
are both condemned.

The Violation of Dinah (vv.1-4): Dinah "went out to see the daughters of the
land" (v.1). Shechem "took her, and lay with her, and defiled her" (v.2).
Then he "loved" her and wanted to marry her (vv.3-4). The violation is real
regardless of his subsequent affection.

The Negotiation (vv.5-19): Jacob hears but "held his peace" — passive (v.5).
His sons are "grieved" and "very wroth" (v.7). Hamor proposes intermarriage
and land-sharing (vv.8-10). Shechem offers any bride-price (vv.11-12). The
sons "answered deceitfully" (v.13): agree to the marriage IF all males are
circumcised (vv.14-17). Hamor and Shechem agree (vv.18-19).

The Massacre (vv.20-29): The city's men are circumcised (v.24). On the third
day (when they're most sore), Simeon and Levi attack — killing every male
(v.25). They rescue Dinah (v.26). The other sons plunder the city (vv.27-29).

Jacob's Response (vv.30-31): Jacob rebukes Simeon and Levi — but for
pragmatic reasons (danger to himself), not moral ones (v.30). Their response:
"Should he deal with our sister as with an harlot?" (v.31). The chapter ends
without resolution — both the violation and the revenge are wrong.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
• Shechem (v.2) — a major city in the central hill country, located in the
  pass between Mount Ebal and Mount Gerizim. A crossroads city with mixed
  Canaanite (Hivite) population.
• The city's strategic location made it important for trade and governance —
  it later becomes Israel's first capital under Jeroboam (1 Kings 12:25).

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "He seized her and lay with her and humiliated her." (ESV) — Sexual
        violence.

v.7  — "He had done an outrageous thing in Israel... for such a thing must
        not be done." (ESV) — The brothers' righteous anger.

v.13 — "The sons of Jacob answered Shechem and his father Hamor deceitfully."
        (ESV) — Deception used for revenge.

v.25 — "Simeon and Levi... came against the city while it felt secure and
        killed all the males." (ESV) — Disproportionate, treacherous
        violence.

v.30 — "You have brought trouble on me by making me stink to the inhabitants
        of the land." (ESV) — Jacob's pragmatic (not moral) concern.

v.31 — "Should he treat our sister like a prostitute?" (ESV) — The
        unanswered question.

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1  — Hebrew "yatsa lirot bibnot ha'arets" — went out to see the daughters of the land.
       KJV: "Dinah the daughter of Leah...went out to see the daughters of the land"
       ESV: "Dinah the daughter of Leah...went out to see the women of the land"
       ASV: "Dinah the daughter of Leah...went out to see the daughters of the land"
       NET: "Dinah...went to meet the young women of the land"
       WEB: "Dinah, the daughter of Leah...went out to see the daughters of the land"
       Analysis: Hebrew "lir'ot bibnot ha'arets" — ESV uses "women" instead
       of "daughters." NET renders as "meet" rather than "see" (KJV/ASV/WEB),
       suggesting purposeful social engagement rather than passive observation.
       Not necessarily sinful but unwise — a young woman alone among pagans.

v.2  — Hebrew "vayyiqach...vayyishkav...wayeannehah" — took...lay with...humbled her.
       KJV: "took her, and lay with her, and defiled her"
       ESV: "he seized her and lay with her and humiliated her"
       ASV: "took her, and lay with her, and humbled her"
       NET: "he grabbed her, forced himself on her, and sexually assaulted her"
       WEB: "took her, lay with her, and humbled her"
       Analysis: Hebrew "wayeannehah" — he humbled/afflicted her. The same
       word used for rape in Deuteronomy 22:29 and 2 Samuel 13:14. NET uses
       three explicit terms, removing all ambiguity about the violent nature
       of the act. ESV captures the root meaning with "humiliated." KJV's
       "defiled" is more euphemistic. NET is notably the most direct.

v.7  — Hebrew "nebalah beYisrael" — an outrageous/disgraceful thing in Israel.
       KJV: "he had wrought folly in Israel...which thing ought not to be done"
       ESV: "he had done an outrageous thing in Israel...for such a thing must not be done"
       ASV: "he had wrought folly in Israel...which thing ought not to be done"
       NET: "by sexually assaulting Jacob's daughter—a crime that should not be committed"
       WEB: "he had done folly in Israel...which thing ought not to be done"
       Analysis: Hebrew "nebalah" is a technical term for serious sexual sin
       (cf. Judg 20:6, 2 Sam 13:12). KJV/ASV/WEB use "folly" (understating
       the severity); ESV renders as "outrageous thing." NET makes the moral
       judgment explicit: "a crime that should not be committed." This is not
       mere "foolishness" but a grave violation of community standards.

v.13 — Hebrew "bemirmah" — with deceit/treachery.
       KJV: "the sons of Jacob answered Shechem and his father Hamor deceitfully"
       ESV: "the sons of Jacob answered Shechem and his father Hamor deceitfully"
       ASV: "the sons of Jacob answered Shechem and his father Hamor with guile"
       NET: "Jacob's sons answered Shechem and his father Hamor deceitfully"
       WEB: "the sons of Jacob answered Shechem and Hamor his father deceitfully"
       Analysis: The same family trait: Abraham deceived, Isaac deceived,
       Jacob deceived, now his sons deceive. ASV uses "guile" while others
       use "deceitfully." All preserve the damning assessment — the brothers'
       response was calculated treachery, not righteous justice.

v.25 — Hebrew "Shim'on veLevi achey Dinah" — Simeon and Levi, Dinah's brothers.
       KJV: "Simeon and Levi, Dinah's brethren, took each man his sword"
       ESV: "Simeon and Levi, Dinah's brothers, took their swords and came against the city"
       ASV: "Simeon and Levi, Dinah's brethren, took each man his sword"
       NET: "Simeon and Levi, Dinah's brothers, each took his sword and went to the unsuspecting city"
       WEB: "Simeon and Levi, Dinah's brothers, each took his sword, came upon the city"
       Analysis: Full brothers (all Leah's children) — they took personal
       responsibility for avenging their sister. NET adds "unsuspecting"
       (rendering Hebrew "betach" — feeling secure/confident), emphasizing
       the treacherous nature of the attack on men recovering from
       circumcision. ESV's "came against" conveys hostile intent.

v.30 — Hebrew "achartem oti lehav'isheni" — you have troubled me, making me odious.
       KJV: "Ye have troubled me to make me to stink among the inhabitants of the land"
       ESV: "You have brought trouble on me by making me stink to the inhabitants of the land"
       ASV: "Ye have troubled me, to make me odious to the inhabitants of the land"
       NET: "You have brought ruin on me by making me a stench to the inhabitants of the land"
       WEB: "You have troubled me, to make me odious to the inhabitants of the land"
       Analysis: Jacob's concern is self-preservation ("I being few in
       number"), not justice or morality. His passivity throughout the chapter
       is notable — he neither protects Dinah nor restrains his sons. NET's
       "brought ruin" and "a stench" are stronger than the other renderings,
       emphasizing the severity of the consequences. ASV/WEB use "odious"
       for "stink."

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
"Went out to see" — Dinah's curiosity about Canaanite culture. Not sinful
               in itself but unwise given the context.

"Wrought folly in Israel" — A technical term for serious sexual sin. An act
               that violates the community's standards.

Answered deceitfully — Using circumcision (a sacred covenant sign) as a
               weapon of treachery. Profaning the holy for revenge.

Disproportionate revenge — Killing an entire city for one man's crime.
               Righteous anger twisted into unjust violence.

Jacob's passivity — His failure to protect Dinah or restrain his sons. A
               father's abdication with devastating consequences.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Jacob settled near Shechem instead of returning to Bethel (33:18). Wrong
   location led to tragedy. Are you where God wants you? Or have you settled
   somewhere convenient but dangerous?

2. Jacob "held his peace" (v.5). His passivity — failing to act as a father
   and leader — contributed to the disaster. Where might your passivity be
   allowing harm?

3. The brothers' anger was righteous (v.7) but their response was sinful
   (v.25). Righteous anger doesn't justify unrighteous action. How do you
   handle anger — with justice or with vengeance?

4. They used circumcision (a sacred sign) as a weapon (vv.14-15). Using
   spiritual things for selfish purposes profanes them. Are you ever
   "spiritual" in your manipulation?

5. This chapter has no hero — everyone fails. Shechem violates, Jacob is
   passive, the brothers are treacherous. Yet God's purposes continue. He
   works through deeply flawed people. Does this give you hope?
