Genesis - Chapter 47
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Jacob's family settles in Goshen; Joseph administers Egypt through the famine, and Jacob blesses Pharaoh.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 47 covers three major sections: (1) Jacob's
family settles in Goshen with Pharaoh's blessing; (2) Joseph's administration
of Egypt during the famine — buying up all money, cattle, land, and people
for Pharaoh; (3) Jacob's final years and his request to be buried in Canaan.
Jacob blesses Pharaoh (the lesser blesses the greater — Hebrews 7:7), lives
17 years in Egypt (the same number of years he had Joseph before losing him),
and makes Joseph swear to bury him in the Promised Land — an act of faith
that Canaan, not Egypt, is home.

Settlement in Goshen (vv.1-12): Joseph presents five brothers to Pharaoh
(v.2). They identify as shepherds and request Goshen (vv.3-4). Pharaoh
grants it and offers positions over his cattle (v.6). Jacob is brought before
Pharaoh and blesses him (v.7). Pharaoh asks his age; Jacob replies: "The
days of the years of my pilgrimage are an hundred and thirty years: few and
evil have the days of the years of my life been" (v.9). Jacob blesses Pharaoh
again (v.10). Joseph settles them in "the land of Rameses" (v.11) and
nourishes the whole family (v.12).

Joseph's Administration (vv.13-26): The famine intensifies. Joseph collects
all money for grain (v.14). When money runs out, he takes cattle (vv.15-17).
When cattle are gone, the people offer their land and themselves (vv.18-19).
Joseph buys all the land for Pharaoh (v.20), relocates the people to cities
(v.21), exempts the priests (v.22), and establishes a 20% tax — the people
keep 80% (vv.23-26). The people respond: "Thou hast saved our lives" (v.25).

Jacob's Final Request (vv.27-31): Israel dwells in Goshen 17 years; they
"grew, and multiplied exceedingly" (v.27). Jacob lives to 147 (v.28). As
death approaches, he makes Joseph swear — hand under the thigh — to bury him
in Canaan, not Egypt (vv.29-30). "Israel bowed himself upon the bed's head"
(v.31) — worship in his final days.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
• Goshen (v.6) — confirmed as the family's settlement. It is the "best of
  the land" (v.11) — fertile grazing territory in the eastern Nile Delta,
  separate from Egypt's major cities and temples.
• Jacob's burial request — "bury me with my fathers" in Canaan (vv.29-30) —
  shows that even in Egypt, the Promised Land remains home. The family's
  identity is tied to the land of Canaan, not Egypt.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.7  — "And Jacob blessed Pharaoh." (ESV) — The lesser does not bless the
        greater; the patriarch blesses the king. Jacob's authority is
        spiritual.

v.9  — "The days of the years of my sojourning are 130 years. Few and evil
        have been the days of the years of my life." (ESV) — Jacob's honest
        assessment of a hard life.

v.25 — "You have saved our lives." (ESV) — The Egyptians' gratitude to
        Joseph.

v.27 — "Thus Israel settled in the land of Egypt, in the land of Goshen.
        And they gained possessions in it, and were fruitful and multiplied
        greatly." (ESV) — God's promise being fulfilled.

v.29 — "Do not bury me in Egypt." (ESV) — Faith that Canaan is the Promised
        Land — even in death.

v.31 — "Then Israel bowed himself upon the head of his bed." (ESV) — Worship
        at the end of life.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES

Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/genesis-47/
  Guzik discusses Jacob's self-assessment ("few and evil"), Joseph's
  administration, and Jacob's faith-filled request for burial in Canaan.

Charles Spurgeon:
  Sermon: "The Pilgrim's Confession" on v.9 (MTP Vol. 25, No. 1487)
  "Few and evil — this is Jacob's summary of 130 years. Few? Compared to
  Abraham's 175 and Isaac's 180, yes. Evil? Full of trouble — Esau's hatred,
  Laban's deception, Dinah's violation, Joseph's loss, famine, grief. Yet
  this same Jacob BLESSES Pharaoh. A man whose life has been 'few and evil'
  still has authority to bless the most powerful man on earth. Why? Because
  his authority comes not from his experience but from his God. Christian,
  your life may feel 'few and evil' — but you carry the blessing of the
  Almighty. You are a pilgrim, yes. But you are a pilgrim with a destination."

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.9  — Hebrew "megurim" — sojournings, temporary dwellings.
       KJV: "pilgrimage"
       ESV: "sojourning"
       ASV: "pilgrimage"
       NET: "travels"
       WEB: "pilgrimage"
       Analysis: The ESV's "sojourning" is more literal; the KJV's
       "pilgrimage" adds theological weight. NET's "travels" is the most
       neutral. Jacob sees his entire life as temporary — the pilgrim
       theology of Hebrews 11.

v.9  — Hebrew "me'at vera'im" — few and bad/difficult.
       KJV: "few and evil"
       ESV: "few and evil"
       ASV: "few and evil"
       NET: "few and painful"
       WEB: "few and evil"
       Analysis: Most translations agree on "few and evil." NET's "painful"
       is blunter, interpreting "ra'im" as hardship rather than retaining
       the broader "evil." Not moral evil but the experience of suffering
       and difficulty.

v.21 — Hebrew "he'evir" — he transferred them.
       KJV: "he removed them to cities"
       ESV: "he moved the people into the cities"
       ASV: "he removed the people to the cities"
       NET: "Joseph acquired all the land of Egypt for Pharaoh"
       WEB: "he moved the people to the cities"
       Analysis: Both KJV and ESV are equivalent. Some manuscripts read "he
       made them slaves" (he'evid). The relocation was administrative. All
       translations show Joseph centralizing power under Pharaoh.

v.29 — Hebrew "sim yadekha tachat yerekhi" — put your hand under my thigh.
       KJV: "put thy hand under my thigh"
       ESV: "put your hand under my thigh"
       ASV: "put thy hand under my thigh"
       NET: "put your hand under my thigh"
       WEB: "please put your hand under my thigh"
       Analysis: All translations identical. An ancient oath ritual involving
       the seat of procreation — swearing by one's descendants. Deeply
       solemn.

v.31 — Hebrew "rosh hamittah" — the head of the bed.
       KJV: "bowed himself upon the bed's head"
       ESV: "bowed himself on the head of his bed"
       ASV: "bowed himself upon the bed's head"
       NET: "bowed down at the head of his bed"
       WEB: "bowed himself on the bed's head"
       Analysis: All translations equivalent. The LXX reads "the top of his
       staff" (matteh vs. mittah) — quoted in Hebrews 11:21. The Hebrew
       "mittah" (bed) vs. LXX "matteh" (staff) presents a textual variant:
       the difference is only in vowel pointing. Either way: Jacob worships.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Pilgrimage     — Jacob's word for his life: a temporary journey, not a
               permanent home. The theology of the stranger and sojourner
               (Hebrews 11:13; 1 Peter 2:11).

"Few and evil" — Jacob's honest assessment: his 130 years have been marked
               by hardship. Honesty before God about suffering is not
               faithlessness — it is realism.

Land of Rameses — Another name for Goshen or a district within it. Later
               associated with the city built by Israelite slaves
               (Exodus 1:11).

Hand under the thigh — An ancient oath ritual of extreme solemnity. Swearing
               by one's descendants — the most binding oath available.

"Israel bowed himself" — Worship at the end of life. Despite "few and evil"
               days, Jacob ends in worship — faith triumphs over suffering.

The fifth part — Joseph's 20% tax on Egyptian produce. The people keep 80%.
               By ancient standards, this was moderate taxation.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Few and evil have the days of the years of my life been" (v.9). Jacob
   is honest about his suffering. Are you honest with God about yours? You
   don't have to pretend life has been easy. God can handle your honesty.

2. Jacob blessed Pharaoh (v.7). Despite his hard life, Jacob carries
   spiritual authority. Your suffering doesn't disqualify you from blessing
   others — it may be what qualifies you.

3. "Bury me not in Egypt" (v.29). Jacob's faith looks beyond death to the
   Promised Land. Where is your hope anchored? In present comfort or in
   God's promises?

4. "Israel bowed himself upon the bed's head" (v.31). Jacob's last recorded
   act in this chapter is worship. How do you want to end your life? Start
   practicing now what you want to be doing then.

5. "Thou hast saved our lives" (v.25). The Egyptians recognized Joseph as
   their savior. Do people around you recognize God's provision through your
   life and work?
