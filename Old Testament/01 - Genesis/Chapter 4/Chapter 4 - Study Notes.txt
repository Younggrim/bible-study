Genesis - Chapter 4
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Cain murders Abel out of jealousy over God's acceptance of Abel's sacrifice; sin escalates rapidly in the first family.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 4 shows the immediate consequences of the fall —
sin spreading from the first couple to the first family. Cain murders Abel
in the first act of human violence, demonstrating that sin escalates rapidly.
The chapter traces two lines: Cain's line (vv.17-24) — characterized by
civilization without God, ending in Lamech's boastful violence — and Seth's
line (vv.25-26) — the line of faith through which the Redeemer will come.

Cain and Abel's Offerings (vv.1-7): Both bring offerings to God. Abel brings
"the firstlings of his flock and of the fat thereof" — the best, with blood
(v.4). Cain brings "of the fruit of the ground" — no indication of firstfruits
or best (v.3). God accepts Abel's; rejects Cain's (vv.4-5). The difference:
not the type of offering but the heart behind it (cf. Hebrews 11:4). God
warns Cain: "sin lieth at the door" (v.7).

The First Murder (vv.8-16): Cain kills Abel in the field (v.8). God asks:
"Where is Abel thy brother?" (v.9). Cain's defiant answer: "Am I my brother's
keeper?" (v.9). Abel's blood "crieth unto me from the ground" (v.10). Cain is
cursed — a fugitive and vagabond (vv.11-12). God mercifully marks Cain for
protection (v.15). Cain goes "out from the presence of the LORD" (v.16).

Cain's Line (vv.17-24): Civilization develops: cities (v.17), polygamy (v.19),
nomadic herding (v.20), music (v.21), metalwork (v.22). But also escalating
violence: Lamech boasts of killing and claims seventy-sevenfold vengeance
(vv.23-24). Culture without God produces both achievement and brutality.

Seth's Line (vv.25-26): Seth born — "another seed instead of Abel" (v.25).
His son Enos born. "Then began men to call upon the name of the LORD" (v.26)
— the beginning of public worship.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
• The "land of Nod" (v.16) — meaning "wandering" — is located east of Eden.
  The eastward movement continues the pattern of departure from God's presence.
• Cain builds a city (v.17), the first mention of urbanization in Scripture.
  The location is unspecified, but civilization develops away from Eden.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.4-5 — "And the LORD had regard for Abel and his offering, but for Cain
        and his offering he had no regard." (ESV) — God evaluates the heart,
        not just the act.

v.7  — "If you do well, will you not be accepted? And if you do not do well,
        sin is crouching at the door." (ESV) — Sin crouching, ready to
        pounce.

v.9  — "Am I my brother's keeper?" (ESV) — Cain's defiant evasion of
        responsibility.

v.10 — "The voice of your brother's blood is crying to me from the ground."
        (ESV) — Innocent blood has a voice before God.

v.26 — "At that time people began to call upon the name of the LORD." (ESV)
        — The beginning of worship.

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1  — Hebrew "qaniti ish eth Yahweh" — I have gotten/acquired a man with/from the LORD.
       KJV: "I have gotten a man from the LORD"
       ESV: "I have gotten a man with the help of the LORD"
       ASV: "I have gotten a man with the help of Jehovah"
       NET: "I have created a man just as the Lord did!"
       WEB: "I have gotten a man with Yahweh's help"
       Analysis: NET is strikingly different — reads Eve as claiming creative
       power parallel to God's, a theologically provocative rendering. The
       Hebrew "eth" is ambiguous (can mean "with" or be a direct object
       marker). Eve may have thought Cain was the promised seed (3:15).
       ASV uses "Jehovah"; WEB uses "Yahweh."

v.7  — Hebrew "chattath robets" — sin is crouching.
       KJV: "sin lieth at the door... unto thee shall be his desire, and thou shalt rule over him"
       ESV: "sin is crouching at the door. Its desire is contrary to you, but you must rule over it"
       ASV: "sin coucheth at the door... its desire is unto thee, but do thou rule over it"
       NET: "sin is crouching at the door. It desires to dominate you, but you must subdue it"
       WEB: "sin crouches at the door. Its desire is for you, but you are to rule over it"
       Analysis: ESV's "crouching" and NET's "crouches" vividly capture the
       predatory "robets" (used of animals lying in wait). NET makes the
       power-struggle explicit with "dominate" and "subdue." KJV uses
       masculine pronouns ("his"/"him") for sin; all modern translations
       use neuter. The Hebrew participle "robets" is masculine — suggesting
       a personified predator.

v.8  — Hebrew "wayyo'mer Qayin el-Hevel..." — And Cain said to Abel...
       KJV: "And Cain talked with Abel his brother... and slew him"
       ESV: "Cain spoke to Abel his brother... and killed him"
       ASV: "And Cain told Abel his brother... and slew him"
       NET: "Cain said to his brother Abel, 'Let's go out to the field'... and killed him"
       WEB: "Cain said to Abel, his brother, 'Let's go into the field'... and killed him"
       Analysis: NET/WEB uniquely supply Cain's words ("Let's go out to the
       field") — following the Samaritan Pentateuch, LXX, and Syriac, filling
       a gap where the Hebrew Masoretic text has "Cain said to Abel" with no
       recorded speech. This is a significant textual-critical decision.

v.12 — Hebrew "na wanad" — wandering and fleeing.
       KJV: "a fugitive and a vagabond shalt thou be"
       ESV: "a fugitive and a wanderer shall you be"
       ASV: "a fugitive and a wanderer shalt thou be"
       NET: "a homeless wanderer on the earth"
       WEB: "you will be a fugitive and a wanderer in the earth"
       Analysis: NET compresses to "homeless wanderer" — interpretive but
       vivid. KJV's "vagabond" carries negative connotations in modern
       English. ESV/ASV/WEB preserve both terms from the Hebrew.

v.15 — Hebrew "vayyasem Yahweh leQayin oth" — the LORD set/appointed a sign for Cain.
       KJV: "the LORD set a mark upon Cain"
       ESV: "the LORD put a mark on Cain"
       ASV: "Jehovah appointed a sign for Cain"
       NET: "the LORD put a special mark on Cain"
       WEB: "Yahweh appointed a sign for Cain"
       Analysis: ASV/WEB use "appointed a sign for" — closer to Hebrew "sim
       oth" (set/appoint a sign), suggesting a divinely ordained protective
       signal rather than a physical marking. NET adds "special." Not a curse
       mark but a protection mark — God's mercy even in judgment.

v.22 — Hebrew "lotesh kol-choresh nechosheth uvarzel"
       KJV: "an instructer of every artificer in brass and iron"
       ESV: "he was the forger of all instruments of bronze and iron"
       ASV: "the forger of every cutting instrument of brass and iron"
       NET: "he was the forger of all kinds of bronze and iron tools"
       WEB: "the forger of every cutting instrument of bronze and iron"
       Analysis: KJV's "brass" is incorrect — Hebrew "nechosheth" means
       bronze or copper, not brass (an alloy unknown in the ancient world).
       All modern translations correctly use "bronze." ESV/NET use general
       "instruments/tools"; ASV/WEB specify "cutting instrument."

v.26 — Hebrew "az huchal liqro beshem Yahweh" — then began to call upon the name of the LORD.
       KJV: "then began men to call upon the name of the LORD"
       ESV: "At that time people began to call upon the name of the LORD"
       ASV: "Then began men to call upon the name of Jehovah"
       NET: "At that time people began to worship the LORD"
       WEB: "Then people began to call on Yahweh's name"
       Analysis: NET interprets "call upon the name" as "worship" — making
       the cultic meaning explicit. All others retain the more literal "call
       upon the name." Public, corporate worship begins. ASV uses "Jehovah";
       WEB uses "Yahweh."

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Cain and Abel  — The first brothers. Abel = "breath/vapor" (fragile life).
               Cain = "acquired/gotten." Their offerings reveal their hearts.

Sin at the door — Sin personified as a crouching predator. It desires to
               master you but can be ruled through obedience.

Am I my brother's keeper? — Cain's evasion of responsibility. The implied
               answer: yes, you are responsible for others.

Blood cries from the ground — Innocent blood has a voice before God. He
               hears and responds to injustice.

Cain's line    — Civilization without God. Cultural achievement (cities,
               arts, technology) alongside moral decay (violence, polygamy).

Seth's line    — The line of faith. "Another seed instead of Abel." The line
               through which the Redeemer will come.

Call upon the name of the LORD — Public worship of Yahweh. The beginning of
               corporate faith expression.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "The LORD had respect unto Abel and to his offering" (v.4). God looks at
   the heart behind the offering, not just the offering itself. What is the
   condition of your heart when you worship, give, or serve?

2. "Sin lieth at the door" (v.7). Sin is crouching at YOUR door right now —
   waiting for an opportunity. Are you vigilant? Are you ruling over it, or
   is it ruling over you?

3. "Am I my brother's keeper?" (v.9). Yes — you are. You are responsible for
   others. Who is God calling you to care for, protect, or look after?

4. "Cain went out from the presence of the LORD" (v.16). The worst
   consequence of sin: separation from God's presence. Are you moving toward
   God or away from Him?

5. Two lines emerge: Cain's (culture without God) and Seth's (worship of
   God). Which line are you building? Achievement without God, or life
   centered on calling upon His name?
