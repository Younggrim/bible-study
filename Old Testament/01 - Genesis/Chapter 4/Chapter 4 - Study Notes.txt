Genesis - Chapter 4
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Cain murders Abel out of jealousy over God's acceptance of Abel's sacrifice; sin escalates rapidly in the first family.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 4 shows the immediate consequences of the fall —
sin spreading from the first couple to the first family. Cain murders Abel
in the first act of human violence, demonstrating that sin escalates rapidly.
The chapter traces two lines: Cain's line (vv.17-24) — characterized by
civilization without God, ending in Lamech's boastful violence — and Seth's
line (vv.25-26) — the line of faith through which the Redeemer will come.

Cain and Abel's Offerings (vv.1-7): Both bring offerings to God. Abel brings
"the firstlings of his flock and of the fat thereof" — the best, with blood
(v.4). Cain brings "of the fruit of the ground" — no indication of firstfruits
or best (v.3). God accepts Abel's; rejects Cain's (vv.4-5). The difference:
not the type of offering but the heart behind it (cf. Hebrews 11:4). God
warns Cain: "sin lieth at the door" (v.7).

The First Murder (vv.8-16): Cain kills Abel in the field (v.8). God asks:
"Where is Abel thy brother?" (v.9). Cain's defiant answer: "Am I my brother's
keeper?" (v.9). Abel's blood "crieth unto me from the ground" (v.10). Cain is
cursed — a fugitive and vagabond (vv.11-12). God mercifully marks Cain for
protection (v.15). Cain goes "out from the presence of the LORD" (v.16).

Cain's Line (vv.17-24): Civilization develops: cities (v.17), polygamy (v.19),
nomadic herding (v.20), music (v.21), metalwork (v.22). But also escalating
violence: Lamech boasts of killing and claims seventy-sevenfold vengeance
(vv.23-24). Culture without God produces both achievement and brutality.

Seth's Line (vv.25-26): Seth born — "another seed instead of Abel" (v.25).
His son Enos born. "Then began men to call upon the name of the LORD" (v.26)
— the beginning of public worship.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
• The "land of Nod" (v.16) — meaning "wandering" — is located east of Eden.
  The eastward movement continues the pattern of departure from God's presence.
• Cain builds a city (v.17), the first mention of urbanization in Scripture.
  The location is unspecified, but civilization develops away from Eden.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.4-5 — "And the LORD had regard for Abel and his offering, but for Cain
        and his offering he had no regard." (ESV) — God evaluates the heart,
        not just the act.

v.7  — "If you do well, will you not be accepted? And if you do not do well,
        sin is crouching at the door." (ESV) — Sin crouching, ready to
        pounce.

v.9  — "Am I my brother's keeper?" (ESV) — Cain's defiant evasion of
        responsibility.

v.10 — "The voice of your brother's blood is crying to me from the ground."
        (ESV) — Innocent blood has a voice before God.

v.26 — "At that time people began to call upon the name of the LORD." (ESV)
        — The beginning of worship.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1  — KJV: "I have gotten a man from the LORD" / ESV: "I have gotten a man
       with the help of the LORD" — Hebrew "qaniti ish eth Yahweh." The
       Hebrew "eth" is ambiguous — it can mean "with" or be a direct object
       marker. ESV adds "with the help of" for clarity. Eve may have thought
       Cain was the promised seed (3:15). She was wrong — tragically.

v.2  — KJV: "a tiller of the ground" / ESV: "a worker of the ground" —
       Hebrew "obed adamah." Both equivalent; ESV uses modern "worker" vs.
       KJV's "tiller."

v.3  — KJV: "in process of time" / ESV: "In the course of time" — Both
       equivalent. Hebrew "miqets yamim" — at the end of days. A set time
       for worship/offering — possibly the first hint of regular worship.

v.4  — KJV: "firstlings of his flock and of the fat thereof" / ESV:
       "firstborn of his flock and of their fat portions" — Both equivalent.
       Abel brought the best (firstborn) and the richest part (fat). His
       offering cost him something and involved blood.

v.5  — KJV: "Cain was very wroth, and his countenance fell" / ESV: "Cain
       was very angry, and his face fell" — Hebrew "charah" (burned with
       anger) and "naphal panim" (face fell). ESV uses modern "angry" and
       "face" vs. KJV's archaic "wroth" and "countenance." Same meaning.

v.7  — KJV: "sin lieth at the door" / ESV: "sin is crouching at the door"
       — Hebrew "chattath robets" — sin is crouching. ESV's "crouching"
       vividly captures the predatory image of "robets" (used of animals
       lying in wait). KJV's "lieth" is less vivid.
       KJV: "unto thee shall be his desire, and thou shalt rule over him" /
       ESV: "Its desire is for you, and you must rule over it" — KJV uses
       masculine pronouns ("his"/"him") for sin; ESV uses neuter ("its"/
       "it"). Hebrew "chattath" (sin) is feminine, but the participle
       "robets" is masculine — suggesting a personified predator.

v.8  — KJV: "slew him" / ESV: "killed him" — Both equivalent. Hebrew
       "wayahargehu" — and he killed him. ESV uses common "killed"; KJV uses
       older "slew."

v.9  — KJV: "Am I my brother's keeper?" / ESV: "Am I my brother's keeper?"
       — Both identical. Hebrew "hashomer achi anoki?" — The answer God
       implies: YES. You ARE responsible for your brother.

v.12 — KJV: "a fugitive and a vagabond" / ESV: "a fugitive and a wanderer"
       — Hebrew "na wanad" — wandering and fleeing. ESV's "wanderer" is
       more neutral than KJV's "vagabond" (which carries negative
       connotations in modern English).

v.15 — KJV: "set a mark upon Cain" / ESV: "put a mark on Cain" — Both
       equivalent. Hebrew "oth" — a sign. Not a curse mark but a protection
       mark. God's mercy even in judgment.

v.16 — KJV: "the land of Nod" / ESV: "the land of Nod" — Both identical.
       Hebrew "nod" = wandering. Cain went to the land of wandering —
       fulfilling his sentence as a fugitive.

v.21 — KJV: "the harp and organ" / ESV: "the lyre and pipe" — Hebrew
       "kinnor" (lyre/harp) and "ugab" (pipe/flute). ESV's "lyre and pipe"
       are more precise; KJV's "harp and organ" reflect 1611 English
       instrument names.

v.22 — KJV: "an instructer of every artificer in brass and iron" / ESV: "he
       was the forger of all instruments of bronze and iron" — Hebrew
       "lotesh" — one who sharpens/forges. ESV's "forger" is more direct.
       Also, "brass" (KJV) vs. "bronze" (ESV) — Hebrew "nechosheth" means
       bronze or copper, not brass (an alloy unknown in the ancient world).

v.25 — KJV: "another seed instead of Abel" / ESV: "another offspring
       instead of Abel" — Hebrew "zera" — seed, offspring, descendant. ESV's
       "offspring" is clearer for modern readers; KJV's "seed" is more
       literal.

v.26 — KJV: "then began men to call upon the name of the LORD" / ESV: "At
       that time people began to call upon the name of the LORD" — Both
       equivalent. Hebrew "qara beshem Yahweh" — to proclaim, invoke,
       worship the name of Yahweh. Public, corporate worship begins.

TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.1 — NET: "I have created a man just as the Lord did!" — a strikingly
  different interpretation of Hebrew "qaniti ish eth Yahweh." Where KJV/ESV
  read "gotten a man with/from the LORD," NET reads Eve as claiming creative
  power parallel to God's — a theologically provocative rendering.
- v.7 — NET: "It desires to dominate you, but you must subdue it" — makes
  the power-struggle explicit. ASV: "sin coucheth at the door" — the archaic
  "coucheth" vividly renders the animal-crouching image of Hebrew "robets."
- v.8 — NET: "Let's go out to the field" — uniquely supplies Cain's words to
  Abel (following the Samaritan Pentateuch, LXX, and Syriac), filling a gap
  where the Hebrew Masoretic text has "Cain said to Abel" with no speech.
- v.15 — ASV: "appointed a sign for Cain" — "appointed" (rather than
  "set/put a mark") suggests a divinely ordained protective signal, keeping
  closer to the Hebrew "sim" (set, appoint).
- v.26 — NET: "people began to worship the Lord" — interprets "call upon the
  name" as "worship," making the cultic meaning explicit. KJV/ESV/ASV retain
  the more literal "call upon the name of the LORD/Jehovah."

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Cain and Abel  — The first brothers. Abel = "breath/vapor" (fragile life).
               Cain = "acquired/gotten." Their offerings reveal their hearts.

Sin at the door — Sin personified as a crouching predator. It desires to
               master you but can be ruled through obedience.

Am I my brother's keeper? — Cain's evasion of responsibility. The implied
               answer: yes, you are responsible for others.

Blood cries from the ground — Innocent blood has a voice before God. He
               hears and responds to injustice.

Cain's line    — Civilization without God. Cultural achievement (cities,
               arts, technology) alongside moral decay (violence, polygamy).

Seth's line    — The line of faith. "Another seed instead of Abel." The line
               through which the Redeemer will come.

Call upon the name of the LORD — Public worship of Yahweh. The beginning of
               corporate faith expression.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "The LORD had respect unto Abel and to his offering" (v.4). God looks at
   the heart behind the offering, not just the offering itself. What is the
   condition of your heart when you worship, give, or serve?

2. "Sin lieth at the door" (v.7). Sin is crouching at YOUR door right now —
   waiting for an opportunity. Are you vigilant? Are you ruling over it, or
   is it ruling over you?

3. "Am I my brother's keeper?" (v.9). Yes — you are. You are responsible for
   others. Who is God calling you to care for, protect, or look after?

4. "Cain went out from the presence of the LORD" (v.16). The worst
   consequence of sin: separation from God's presence. Are you moving toward
   God or away from Him?

5. Two lines emerge: Cain's (culture without God) and Seth's (worship of
   God). Which line are you building? Achievement without God, or life
   centered on calling upon His name?
