Genesis - Chapter 27
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Jacob deceives his blind father Isaac and steals Esau's blessing, forcing him to flee for his life.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 27 records one of the most dramatic and troubling
family episodes in Genesis — Jacob's deception of Isaac to steal Esau's
blessing. Every family member sins: Isaac tries to bless Esau against God's
revealed will (25:23), Rebekah schemes and manipulates, Jacob lies repeatedly,
and Esau plans murder. Yet God's sovereign purpose prevails — the blessing
goes to Jacob as God intended. The chapter shows that God's purposes are
accomplished despite (not because of) human sin.

Isaac's Plan (vv.1-4): Isaac is old and blind (v.1). He plans to bless Esau
before dying (vv.2-4) — ignoring God's oracle that "the elder shall serve the
younger" (25:23). His motive: "that my soul may bless thee" — personal
preference over divine purpose.

Rebekah's Scheme (vv.5-17): Rebekah overhears and devises a plan (vv.5-10).
Jacob objects — not morally but practically: "I shall seem to him as a
deceiver" (v.12). Rebekah: "Upon me be thy curse" (v.13). She disguises Jacob
with goatskins and Esau's clothes (vv.15-16).

Jacob's Deception (vv.18-29): Jacob lies to Isaac repeatedly: "I am Esau"
(v.19), "The LORD thy God brought it to me" (v.20 — using God's name in a
lie). Isaac is suspicious but deceived by touch and smell (vv.21-27). He
blesses Jacob with prosperity, dominion, and the Abrahamic blessing (vv.28-29).

Esau's Discovery (vv.30-40): Esau arrives too late (vv.30-31). Isaac "trembled
very exceedingly" — recognizing God's hand (v.33). "Yea, and he shall be
blessed" — the blessing is irrevocable (v.33). Esau's "great and exceeding
bitter cry" (v.34). He receives a lesser blessing: living by the sword,
serving his brother, eventually breaking free (vv.39-40).

The Aftermath (vv.41-46): Esau plans to kill Jacob (v.41). Rebekah sends
Jacob to Laban — "a few days" that becomes 20 years (vv.43-44). She never
sees Jacob again.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
• Beer-sheba — the family home where Isaac dwells, Jacob deceives, and Esau
  hunts in the surrounding wilderness (the Negev).
• Jacob will flee from here northward to Haran (ch.28) — a journey of
  approximately 500 miles. The deception forces a geographic separation.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.20 — "Because the LORD your God brought it to me." (ESV) — Jacob using
        God's name to support a lie.

v.22 — "The voice is Jacob's voice, but the hands are the hands of Esau."
        (ESV) — Deception almost detected.

v.33 — "Yes, and he shall be blessed." (ESV) — The blessing is irrevocable
        despite the deception.

v.34 — "He cried out with an exceedingly great and bitter cry... 'Bless me,
        even me also, O my father!'" (ESV) — Esau's anguish.

v.36 — "Is he not rightly named Jacob? For he has cheated me these two
        times." (ESV) — Jacob living up to his name.

v.41 — "Esau said to himself... 'I will kill my brother Jacob.'" (ESV) —
        Hatred born of stolen blessing.

Charles Spurgeon:
  Sermon: "The Stolen Blessing" on v.33 (MTP Vol. 14, No. 839)
  "Yea, and he SHALL be blessed — Isaac recognizes that despite the deception,
  God's purpose has been accomplished. The blessing cannot be recalled. This
  is both terrifying and comforting: terrifying because human schemes can have
  permanent consequences; comforting because God's purposes cannot be thwarted.
  Jacob sinned in HOW he obtained the blessing — but the blessing itself was
  God's will. God's sovereignty works through (not because of) human sin."

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1  — KJV: "his eyes were dim" / ESV: "his eyes were dim so that he could
       not see" — ESV adds explanatory clause. Isaac was likely around 137
       (he lived to 180, 35:28). He thought death was near but lived 43 more
       years.

v.12 — KJV: "a deceiver" / ESV: "mocking him" — Hebrew "meta'tea" — a
       mocker, one who deceives. KJV focuses on the deception; ESV on the
       mockery. Jacob's concern was getting caught, not the morality of lying.

v.33 — KJV: "trembled very exceedingly" / ESV: "trembled very violently" —
       Hebrew "charad charadah gedolah ad meod" — trembled with a great
       trembling exceedingly. Both capture the intensity. Isaac realized he
       had been fighting God's will — and God won.

v.36 — KJV: "supplanted me" / ESV: "cheated me" — Hebrew "Ya'aqob" —
       supplanter. KJV preserves the wordplay with Jacob's name; ESV uses
       the more accessible "cheated." Esau makes the connection: his name
       means what he does.

v.39 — KJV: "thy dwelling shall be the fatness of the earth" / ESV: "away
       from the fatness of the earth shall your dwelling be" — Hebrew "min"
       can mean "from" or "of." ESV reads "away from" (privative); KJV reads
       "of" (partitive). Edom's territory was largely arid, supporting ESV's
       reading.

TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.12 — NET: "My father may touch me! Then he'll think I'm mocking him" —
  renders Hebrew "meta'tea" as "mocking" where KJV has "a deceiver" and ESV
  "mocking." NET captures Jacob's fear of being caught and shamed.
- v.20 — NET: "How in the world did you find it so quickly, my son?" — adds
  "How in the world" to convey Isaac's surprise/suspicion, not literally in
  the Hebrew but implied by the context.
- v.36 — NET typically renders Esau's bitter lament: "He has tripped me up
  two times!" — "tripped me up" vividly renders the wordplay on Jacob's name
  (ya'aqob/heel-grabber → supplanter → tripper).
- v.27 — NET: "the smell of a field that the Lord has blessed" — same as
  KJV/ESV. WEB: "the smell of a field which Yahweh has blessed" — using the
  divine name in the patriarchal blessing.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
The stolen blessing — Jacob obtaining Isaac's blessing through deception.
               God's purpose accomplished through sinful means.

Irrevocable blessing — Once spoken, the patriarchal blessing could not be
               recalled. Words had binding power.

"He shall be blessed" — Isaac's recognition that God's will prevailed
               despite human manipulation.

Supplanter     — Jacob's character: grasping, scheming, taking what belongs
               to others. A name he must grow beyond.

Every family member sinned — Isaac (ignoring God's will), Rebekah (scheming),
               Jacob (lying), Esau (murderous intent). A dysfunctional family
               through which God still works.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Every person in this chapter sinned — yet God's purpose was accomplished.
   This doesn't excuse sin but demonstrates sovereignty. God works through
   broken people. Does this encourage you despite your failures?

2. Isaac tried to bless Esau against God's revealed will (25:23). Where might
   you be resisting what God has clearly said — because of personal
   preference?

3. Jacob lied and used God's name to do it (v.20). Using God's name to
   support deception is serious. Are you ever "spiritual" in your dishonesty?

4. Esau's "bitter cry" (v.34) came too late. Hebrews 12:17 warns: some
   consequences are irreversible. Are you making choices now that you'll
   regret with tears later?

5. Rebekah's "few days" (v.44) became 20 years — she never saw Jacob again.
   Sin's consequences often exceed what we anticipate. What might your
   current compromises cost you long-term?
