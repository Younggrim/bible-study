Genesis - Chapter 24
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Abraham sends his servant to find a wife for Isaac; God providentially leads him to Rebekah, who willingly returns to marry Isaac.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 24 is the longest chapter in Genesis (67 verses)
and one of the most beautiful narratives in Scripture — the story of finding
a bride for Isaac. Abraham sends his servant (likely Eliezer, cf. 15:2) to
his homeland to find a wife from his own family. The servant prays, God
answers specifically, and Rebekah is brought to Isaac. The chapter is rich
with typology: Abraham (the Father) sends his servant (the Spirit) to find
a bride (the Church) for his son (Christ).

Abraham's Commission (vv.1-9): Abraham is old; God has blessed him (v.1). He
makes his servant swear: don't take a Canaanite wife for Isaac (v.3). Go to
my kindred (v.4). Don't take Isaac back there (v.6). God will send His angel
before you (v.7).

The Servant's Prayer and Answer (vv.10-27): At the well, the servant prays
for a specific sign: the woman who offers water to him AND his camels (vv.12-
14). "Before he had done speaking" — Rebekah appears (v.15). She does exactly
what he prayed (vv.18-20). The servant worships: "the LORD led me" (v.27).

The Negotiation (vv.28-60): The servant tells the whole story to Rebekah's
family (vv.34-48). Laban and Bethuel agree: "The thing proceedeth from the
LORD" (v.50). Rebekah is asked: "Wilt thou go?" She answers: "I will go"
(v.58).

The Meeting (vv.61-67): Isaac meditates in the field at evening (v.63).
Rebekah sees Isaac and dismounts (v.64). Isaac brings her into Sarah's tent;
she becomes his wife; he loves her; he is comforted (v.67).

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
• Mesopotamia / "the city of Nahor" (v.10) — Abraham's servant travels to
  northwest Mesopotamia (Haran region, modern southeastern Turkey/Syria
  border) to find a wife for Isaac.
• The journey back to Canaan covers approximately 500 miles along ancient
  caravan routes through the Fertile Crescent.
• Beer-lahai-roi (v.62) — where Isaac dwells in the Negev, the same well
  where God appeared to Hagar (16:14).

  "I being in the way, the LORD led me — the servant was IN THE WAY (obedient,
  moving, available) and the LORD LED (directed, guided, orchestrated). Both
  are necessary: you must be moving for God to steer. A parked car cannot be
  directed. Get in the way — obey what you know — and the LORD will lead you
  to the right place at the right time."

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.2  — Hebrew "sim yad'ka tachat yerekhi" — put your hand under my thigh.
       An ancient oath ceremony associating the vow with one's descendants.
       KJV: "Put, I pray thee, thy hand under my thigh"
       ESV: "Put your hand under my thigh"
       ASV: "Put, I pray thee, thy hand under my thigh"
       NET: "Put your hand under my thigh"
       WEB: "Please put your hand under my thigh"
       Analysis: All translations agree on this rendering. NET adds context
       in its notes explaining this as an ancient oath-taking gesture
       involving the source of descendants. Swearing on the thigh meant
       pledging on one's posterity.

v.12 — Hebrew "haqreh" and "chesed" — cause to happen / covenant faithfulness.
       KJV: "send me good speed this day, and shew kindness unto my master"
       ESV: "please grant me success today and show steadfast love to my master"
       ASV: "send me good speed this day, and show kindness unto my master"
       NET: "guide me today. Be faithful to my master Abraham"
       WEB: "please send me good speed today, and show kindness to my master"
       Analysis: NET renders the prayer as "guide me" and "be faithful,"
       making the relational covenant language explicit. ESV's "steadfast
       love" captures "chesed" most precisely. The servant's prayer reveals
       dependence on God's active guidance.

v.14 — Hebrew "had" — pitcher/jar. The servant's sign tested character.
       KJV: "Let down thy pitcher, I pray thee, that I may drink"
       ESV: "Please let down your jar that I may drink"
       ASV: "Let down thy pitcher, I pray thee, that I may drink"
       NET: "May the one you have chosen for your servant Isaac reply"
       WEB: "Please let down your pitcher, that I may drink"
       Analysis: KJV/ASV/WEB use "pitcher"; ESV uses "jar." NET adds "you
       have chosen" (interpretive), making God's sovereign selection explicit.
       The sign tested generosity, initiative, and hard work — watering 10
       camels meant drawing hundreds of gallons.

v.16 — Hebrew "lo yeda'ah ish" — no man had known her.
       KJV: "neither had any man known her"
       ESV: "no man had known her"
       ASV: "neither had any man known her"
       NET: "no man had ever been physically intimate with her"
       WEB: "no man had known her"
       Analysis: NET expands the euphemistic "no man had known her" into plain
       modern English, making the meaning explicit for contemporary readers.
       KJV/ESV/ASV/WEB preserve the Hebrew idiom.

v.21 — Hebrew "mishtaeh lah macharish" — gazing at her in silence.
       KJV: "the man wondering at her held his peace"
       ESV: "the man gazed at her in silence"
       ASV: "the man looked steadfastly on her, holding his peace"
       NET: "Silently the man watched her with interest to determine if the Lord had made his journey successful"
       WEB: "the man watched her closely, holding his peace"
       Analysis: NET adds "silently" and "with interest" (not literally in
       the Hebrew) to make the scene more vivid. The servant watches in
       reverent anticipation as God's answer unfolds before his eyes.

v.27 — Hebrew "ani baderek" — I being on the road/path.
       KJV: "I being in the way, the LORD led me"
       ESV: "As for me, the LORD has led me in the way"
       ASV: "I being in the way, Jehovah led me"
       NET: "the Lord has led me to the house of my master's relatives"
       WEB: "as for me, Yahweh has led me on the way"
       Analysis: KJV/ASV preserve the Hebrew construction emphasizing the
       servant's active movement; ESV/NET restructure to emphasize God's
       leading. Both truths are present: he was actively moving in obedience,
       and God directed his steps. A parked car cannot be steered.

v.58 — Hebrew "elek" — I will go.
       KJV: "I will go"
       ESV: "I will go"
       ASV: "I will go"
       NET: "I'll go"
       WEB: "I will go"
       Analysis: All translations agree. Rebekah's willing, immediate
       response — leaving everything for an unseen bridegroom. A picture of
       faith: choosing the unknown based on testimony about the groom.

v.63 — Hebrew "lasuach" — to muse, ponder, pray.
       KJV: "to meditate"
       ESV: "to meditate"
       ASV: "to meditate"
       NET: "to meditate"
       WEB: "to meditate"
       Analysis: All translations agree. Isaac was a contemplative man —
       found in the field at evening, reflecting. The word appears only here
       in the OT in this form. Isaac's quiet character emerges.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
The servant's mission — A type of the Holy Spirit's work: sent by the Father
               to find a bride for the Son among the nations.

"I being in the way, the LORD led me" — The principle of guided obedience:
               be moving in faithfulness and God directs your path.

"Wilt thou go?" — The bride's willing choice. God doesn't force; He invites.
               Rebekah chose to go to an unseen bridegroom.

Rebekah's character — Generous (offered water), hardworking (watered camels),
               hospitable (offered lodging), decisive ("I will go").

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "I being in the way, the LORD led me" (v.27). Are you "in the way" —
   actively obeying, moving forward in faith? God guides those who are
   moving, not those who are sitting still.

2. The servant prayed specifically and God answered specifically (vv.12-15).
   Do you pray with specificity? God is not bothered by detailed requests.

3. Rebekah's character was revealed by her actions: generosity, hard work,
   hospitality (vv.18-25). What do your actions reveal about your character?

4. "Wilt thou go with this man? I will go" (v.58). Rebekah left everything
   for an unseen bridegroom. Faith requires leaving the familiar for the
   promised. What is God asking you to leave?

5. "The thing proceedeth from the LORD" (v.50). When God orchestrates events,
   even unbelievers can recognize it. Is God's hand visible in your life?
