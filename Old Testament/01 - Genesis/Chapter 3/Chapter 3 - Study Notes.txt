Genesis - Chapter 3
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
The serpent tempts Eve, Adam and Eve eat the forbidden fruit, sin and death enter creation, but God promises a Redeemer.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 3 is the most consequential chapter in the Bible
after Genesis 1. It records the fall of humanity — the entrance of sin, death,
and curse into God's good creation. Everything that follows in Scripture (and
in human history) is the result of what happens here. But even in judgment,
God provides hope: the first gospel promise (v.15) and the first sacrifice
(v.21). The chapter answers the question: What went wrong?

The Temptation (vv.1-6): The serpent (Satan, cf. Rev 12:9) questions God's
word: "Yea, hath God said?" (v.1). He denies God's warning: "Ye shall not
surely die" (v.4). He impugns God's motive: "God doth know..." (v.5). Eve
sees three appeals: good for food (flesh), pleasant to eyes (eyes), desired
for wisdom (pride) — cf. 1 John 2:16. She eats and gives to Adam (v.6).

The Consequences (vv.7-13): Eyes opened — shame, nakedness, fig leaves (v.7).
They hide from God (v.8). God seeks them: "Where art thou?" (v.9). Adam
blames Eve; Eve blames the serpent (vv.12-13).

The Judgments (vv.14-19): Serpent cursed — on its belly, enmity with woman's
seed (vv.14-15). The protoevangelium (v.15): her seed will crush the
serpent's head. Woman: pain in childbirth, relational conflict (v.16). Man:
cursed ground, toil, sweat, death — "dust thou art, and unto dust shalt thou
return" (vv.17-19).

Grace in Judgment (vv.20-24): Adam names Eve "mother of all living" — faith
in God's promise of life (v.20). God makes coats of skins — the first death,
the first blood sacrifice, the first covering (v.21). Expelled from Eden —
but mercifully, to prevent eternal life in a fallen state (vv.22-24).

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
• Eden — humanity is expelled eastward (v.24). In Genesis, moving "east"
  consistently symbolizes movement away from God's presence (cf. 4:16, 11:2).
• Cherubim and a flaming sword guard the eastern entrance to Eden (v.24),
  indicating Eden as sacred space with controlled access — a proto-temple.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "Did God actually say, 'You shall not eat of any tree in the
        garden'?" (ESV) — Satan's strategy: question God's word.

v.4  — "You will not surely die." (ESV) — Satan's lie: deny God's
        consequences.

v.6  — "She took of its fruit and ate, and she also gave some to her husband
        who was with her, and he ate." (ESV) — The fall.

v.9  — "Where are you?" (ESV) — God seeking fallen humanity.

v.15 — "I will put enmity between you and the woman, and between your
        offspring and her offspring; he shall bruise your head, and you shall
        bruise his heel." (ESV) — The protoevangelium: the first gospel
        promise.

v.19 — "For you are dust, and to dust you shall return." (ESV) — The
        sentence of death.

v.21 — "And the LORD God made for Adam and for his wife garments of skins
        and clothed them." (ESV) — God's provision through sacrifice.

  Sermon: "The Protoevangelium" on v.15 (MTP Vol. 14, No. 829)
  "It shall bruise thy head — a fatal wound. Thou shalt bruise his heel — a
  painful but not fatal wound. Here is the gospel in seed form: the woman's
  seed (Christ, born of a virgin) will crush the serpent's head (destroy
  Satan's power) at the cost of His own suffering (the cross — a bruised
  heel). The first promise of redemption — spoken not to Adam but to the
  serpent, in Adam's hearing. Grace announced in the very moment of judgment."
  Sermon: "Where Art Thou?" on v.9 (MTP Vol. 5, No. 264)
  "Where art thou? — not because God didn't know but because Adam needed to
  face his condition. God seeks the sinner. The sinner hides; God pursues.
  Adam ran; God called. This is the pattern of all redemption: God seeking
  those who are hiding from Him. He is the seeking God — and He has never
  stopped calling."

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1  — KJV: "subtil" / ESV: "crafty" — Hebrew "arum" — crafty, shrewd,
       cunning. ESV's "crafty" is more immediately understood by modern
       readers than KJV's archaic "subtil." The same root as "naked" (arom)
       in 2:25 — a wordplay connecting the chapters.

v.1  — KJV: "Yea, hath God said, Ye shall not eat of every tree?" / ESV:
       "Did God actually say, 'You shall not eat of any tree in the
       garden'?" — Both convey the same distortion. ESV's "actually" captures
       the incredulous tone of the Hebrew. Satan's first recorded words: a
       question that casts doubt on God's word.

v.5  — KJV: "ye shall be as gods" / ESV: "you will be like God" — Hebrew
       "kelohim" — like God/gods. The Hebrew "Elohim" can be translated
       either way. ESV takes it as referring to God Himself; KJV's "gods"
       (lowercase) suggests becoming divine beings. Both are legitimate
       readings; ESV's is more common among modern scholars.

v.6  — KJV: "pleasant to the eyes" / ESV: "a delight to the eyes" — Both
       equivalent. Hebrew "ta'avah" — desirable, delightful. Three categories
       matching 1 John 2:16: lust of the flesh, lust of the eyes, pride of
       life.

v.7  — KJV: "made themselves aprons" / ESV: "made themselves loincloths" —
       Hebrew "chagoroth" — coverings for the waist/loins. ESV's "loincloths"
       is more precise; KJV's "aprons" reflects 1611 English usage for a
       waist-covering garment.

v.8  — KJV: "the voice of the LORD God walking" / ESV: "the sound of the
       LORD God walking" — Hebrew "qol" — voice, sound, noise. ESV's "sound"
       is more natural here (one hears the sound of someone walking); KJV's
       "voice" is a more literal default for "qol."

v.15 — KJV: "it shall bruise thy head, and thou shalt bruise his heel" /
       ESV: "he shall bruise your head, and you shall bruise his heel" —
       Hebrew "hu yeshupeka rosh" — he (masculine pronoun) shall crush your
       head. ESV correctly renders the masculine pronoun "he" (pointing to a
       specific male descendant — Christ). KJV's "it" obscures the personal
       reference. "Bruise" — Hebrew "shuph" — crush, strike. Head-crushing =
       fatal; heel-bruising = painful but survivable.

v.16 — KJV: "I will greatly multiply thy sorrow and thy conception" / ESV:
       "I will surely multiply your pain in childbearing" — Hebrew "itstsabon"
       — pain, toil, labor. ESV's "pain in childbearing" is clearer than
       KJV's "sorrow and thy conception" which can confuse modern readers.

v.17 — KJV: "cursed is the ground for thy sake" / ESV: "cursed is the
       ground because of you" — Hebrew "ba'abureka" — on your account,
       because of you. ESV is more transparent; KJV's "for thy sake" could
       be misread as "for your benefit."

v.19 — KJV: "In the sweat of thy face" / ESV: "By the sweat of your face"
       — Both equivalent. Minor preposition difference only.

v.21 — KJV: "coats of skins" / ESV: "garments of skins" — Hebrew "kotnoth
       or" — tunics/garments of skin. Both equivalent. An animal died to
       provide covering. The first death in Scripture. The first blood shed.
       The first substitution. God Himself provides the covering — humanity's
       fig leaves are inadequate.

v.24 — KJV: "Cherubims, and a flaming sword which turned every way, to keep
       the way" / ESV: "the cherubim and a flaming sword that turned every
       way to guard the way" — Hebrew "lishmor" — to guard, keep, watch
       over. ESV's "guard" is more specific than KJV's "keep." Also, KJV
       adds English plural "-s" to "Cherubim" (already plural in Hebrew);
       ESV correctly uses "cherubim" without the extra "s."

TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.1 — NET: "shrewder than any of the wild animals" — uses "shrewder"
  (morally neutral term for intelligence) rather than "subtle/crafty"
  (KJV/ESV), and "wild animals" instead of "beasts of the field."
- v.8 — NET: "the breezy time of the day" — a distinctive rendering of
  Hebrew "ruach hayom" (KJV/ESV: "cool of the day"), emphasizing the wind/
  breeze element of "ruach" rather than temperature.
- v.15 — WEB: "hostility between you and the woman...He will bruise your
  head" — uses "hostility" (also NET) rather than "enmity" (KJV/ESV),
  more accessible to modern readers. Correctly renders masculine "He."
- v.16 — NET: "You will want to control your husband, but he will dominate
  you" — a strikingly interpretive rendering. KJV/ESV: "thy desire shall be
  to thy husband." NET makes explicit the conflict interpretation of Hebrew
  "teshuqah" (desire/longing as desire to control, cf. Gen 4:7).
- v.24 — NET: "angelic sentries who used the flame of a whirling sword" —
  expands "cherubim" to "angelic sentries" for clarity and renders the Hebrew
  construction as an instrumental use ("used the flame"), making the image
  more vivid than KJV/ESV's more literal rendering.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
The serpent    — Satan using a serpent as his instrument. Crafty, deceptive,
               questioning God's word and character.

"Hath God said?" — Satan's primary strategy: casting doubt on God's word.
               If he can make you question Scripture, he can make you fall.

The fall       — Humanity's rebellion against God through disobedience.
               The entrance of sin, death, and curse into creation.

Protoevangelium — "First gospel." Genesis 3:15 — the first promise of a
               Redeemer who will crush Satan at the cost of His own suffering.

Seed of the woman — Christ. Born of a woman (virgin birth implied — seed of
               woman, not man). The one who crushes the serpent's head.

Coats of skins — God's provision of covering through animal sacrifice. The
               first picture of substitutionary atonement.

Curse          — The consequences of sin on the serpent, the woman, the man,
               and the ground. Reversed ultimately in Christ (Rev 22:3).

"Where art thou?" — God seeking fallen humanity. Not ignorance but
               invitation. God pursues those who hide from Him.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Yea, hath God said?" (v.1). Where is Satan causing you to doubt God's
   word? His strategy hasn't changed: question what God said, deny the
   consequences, impugn God's motives. How do you respond?

2. "She took... and did eat, and gave also unto her husband" (v.6). Sin
   rarely stays private — it spreads. Where might your sin be affecting
   others?

3. "Where art thou?" (v.9). God seeks you even when you're hiding. Are you
   hiding from God right now — in shame, guilt, or fear? He's calling. Will
   you answer?

4. "It shall bruise thy head" (v.15). Even in the darkest moment of human
   history, God promises redemption. The Redeemer is coming. Whatever
   darkness you face, God has already planned the victory.

5. "The LORD God make coats of skins, and clothed them" (v.21). Your fig
   leaves (self-effort, self-righteousness) are inadequate. Only God can
   provide proper covering — and it costs a life. Have you received His
   covering?
