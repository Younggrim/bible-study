Genesis - Chapter 5
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
The genealogy from Adam to Noah traces ten generations, showing both long life and the relentless refrain 'and he died' — death reigns after the Fall.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 5 is the genealogy from Adam to Noah — ten
generations spanning approximately 1,656 years (using the ages given). This
is the "book of the generations of Adam" (v.1) — the second of eleven
"toledoth" (generations) sections that structure Genesis. The chapter traces
Seth's godly line (in contrast to Cain's line in ch.4) and demonstrates both
the reality of death ("and he died" — repeated 8 times) and the hope of life
(Enoch, who didn't die).

The Refrain of Death (vv.1-31): Each entry follows a pattern: lived X years,
begat a son, lived Y more years, begat sons and daughters, "and he died."
Eight times the phrase "and he died" tolls like a funeral bell — the
consequence of Genesis 3:19 playing out generation after generation.

The Exception: Enoch (vv.21-24): "Enoch walked with God: and he was not; for
God took him" (v.24). In a chapter dominated by death, one man doesn't die.
He "walked with God" — intimate, sustained fellowship — and God took him
directly to Himself. A preview of resurrection hope.

Noah's Birth (vv.28-32): Lamech names his son Noah ("rest/comfort"), saying:
"This same shall comfort us concerning our work and toil of our hands, because
of the ground which the LORD hath cursed" (v.29). A prophetic hope that the
curse might be addressed.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
• No specific geography in this chapter — it is a genealogy from Adam to Noah.
• The long lifespans and genealogical structure suggest humanity spreading
  across the earth, though no locations are named.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "When God created man, he made him in the likeness of God." (ESV) —
        Reaffirming the image of God despite the fall.

v.3  — "When Adam had lived 130 years, he fathered a son in his own likeness,
        after his image." (ESV) — Now Adam's likeness (fallen), not just
        God's.

v.5  — "Thus all the days that Adam lived were 930 years, and he died."
        (ESV) — Death's reality.

v.24 — "Enoch walked with God, and he was not, for God took him." (ESV) —
        Life beyond death through walking with God.

v.29 — "He called his name Noah, saying, 'Out of the ground that the LORD
        has cursed, this one shall bring us relief.'" (ESV) — Hope for
        relief from the curse.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1  — KJV: "This is the book of the generations of Adam. In the day that
       God created man, in the likeness of God made he him" / ESV: "This is
       the book of the generations of Adam. When God created man, he made him
       in the likeness of God" — Both equivalent. Hebrew "sepher toledoth" —
       the written record of the descendants. The second "toledoth" section
       in Genesis (the first is 2:4). ESV restructures for modern English
       word order.

v.2  — KJV: "called their name Adam" / ESV: "named them Man" — Hebrew
       "Adam" means "man/mankind." ESV translates the name to show its
       meaning; KJV retains the proper name. Both are valid — "Adam" is both
       a name and the Hebrew word for "man."

v.3  — KJV: "begat a son in his own likeness, after his image" / ESV:
       "fathered a son in his own likeness, after his image" — Both
       equivalent. KJV's "begat" vs. ESV's "fathered" — same Hebrew "yalad"
       (to beget/father). Adam's son bears Adam's (fallen) image, not just
       God's original image. The image of God continues but is now
       transmitted through a fallen nature.

v.22 — KJV: "walked with God" / ESV: "walked with God" — Both identical.
       Hebrew "hithhallek eth ha-Elohim" — walked habitually with God. The
       hithpael (reflexive) form suggests ongoing, deliberate, intimate
       companionship.

v.24 — KJV: "he was not; for God took him" / ESV: "he was not, for God took
       him" — Both identical. Hebrew "einennu ki laqach oto Elohim" — he was
       no more because God took/received him. Enoch was translated directly
       to God's presence without dying.

v.27 — Methuselah's name may mean "when he dies, it shall come" — possibly
       prophetic of the flood. He died the year the flood came. Both KJV and
       ESV render the name identically.

v.29 — KJV: "This same shall comfort us concerning our work and toil of our
       hands, because of the ground which the LORD hath cursed" / ESV: "Out
       of the ground that the LORD has cursed, this one shall bring us relief
       from our work and from the painful toil of our hands" — Hebrew
       "yenachamenu" — shall comfort/bring relief. ESV restructures for
       clarity and uses "relief" and "painful toil" to make the connection
       to the curse of 3:17-19 more explicit. "Noah" — Hebrew "Noach" —
       rest, comfort. Related to "nacham" (comfort).

TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.1 — NET: "the record of the family line of Adam" — renders Hebrew
  "sepher toledoth" as "record of the family line" rather than "book of the
  generations" (KJV/ESV/ASV), modernizing the genealogical formula.
- v.2 — NET: "named them 'humankind'" — translates Hebrew "Adam" as the
  common noun "humankind" rather than the proper name, emphasizing the
  collective sense (male and female together named "Adam/humanity").
- v.24 — NET: "he disappeared because God took him away" — uniquely renders
  "he was not" as "he disappeared," making the supernatural event more vivid
  and explicit for modern readers.
  WEB: "he was not found, for God took him" — adds "found" (not in the
  Hebrew), clarifying that people looked for Enoch but could not locate him.
- v.29 — ASV brackets "[which cometh]" to show this is supplied for clarity
  and not in the Hebrew, demonstrating ASV's practice of marking translator
  additions. This transparency about the Hebrew text is unique to ASV.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Toledoth       — "Generations/account." A structural marker in Genesis
               introducing a new section of family history.

"And he died"  — The repeated refrain demonstrating death's universal reign
               since the fall. The wages of sin paid generation after
               generation.

Walked with God — Intimate, sustained, habitual fellowship with God. A life
               lived in conscious companionship with the Creator.

Translated/Taken — Enoch's removal from earth without dying. God took him
               directly to Himself — a preview of resurrection hope.

In his own likeness — Adam's fallen image passed to his descendants. The
               image of God remains but is now transmitted through a sinful
               nature.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "And he died" (repeated 8 times). Death is the universal human experience
   since the fall. Does the reality of death create urgency in your life?
   Are you living with eternity in view?

2. "Enoch walked with God" (v.24). One man in a chapter of death didn't die
   — because he walked with God. What does "walking with God" look like for
   you daily? Is it habitual or occasional?

3. "In his own likeness, after his image" (v.3). We inherit a fallen nature.
   But through Christ, we are being renewed in God's image (Col 3:10). Are
   you being transformed, or just passing on the old nature?

4. "This same shall comfort us" (v.29). Even in a world under curse, there
   is hope for comfort and rest. Where do you find rest from the curse's
   effects? Ultimately, only in Christ.

5. The genealogy connects Adam to Noah — showing God's faithfulness across
   generations. God keeps His promises over centuries. Can you trust Him with
   your lifetime?
