Genesis - Chapter 46
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Jacob's entire family — seventy souls — moves to Egypt; God assures Jacob in a vision that He will make him a great nation there.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 46 records the migration of Jacob's entire family
to Egypt — the beginning of Israel's 430-year sojourn (Exodus 12:40). Jacob
stops at Beer-sheba (where both Abraham and Isaac worshipped) and God confirms
the move with a night vision: "Fear not to go down into Egypt; for I will
there make of thee a great nation" (v.3). The genealogy (vv.8-27) records 70
souls — the seed of a nation. The chapter ends with the emotional reunion of
Jacob and Joseph after 22 years apart.

God's Confirmation at Beer-sheba (vv.1-7): Jacob stops at Beer-sheba — the
southernmost point of Canaan, the last stop before Egypt (v.1). He offers
sacrifices "unto the God of his father Isaac" (v.1). God speaks: "Jacob,
Jacob" — the doubled name indicates urgency and intimacy (v.2). God's
promises: (1) "I will there make of thee a great nation" (v.3); (2) "I will
go down with thee" (v.4); (3) "I will also surely bring thee up again" (v.4);
(4) "Joseph shall put his hand upon thine eyes" — Jacob will die peacefully
with Joseph present (v.4).

The Genealogy (vv.8-27): The sons of Leah: 33 souls (vv.8-15). The sons of
Zilpah: 16 souls (vv.16-18). The sons of Rachel: 14 souls (vv.19-22). The
sons of Bilhah: 7 souls (vv.23-25). Total: 66 from Jacob's body (v.26),
plus Jacob himself, Joseph, and Joseph's two sons = 70 (v.27). This number
is significant — 70 nations in Genesis 10, 70 elders later appointed
(Exodus 24:1), 70 sent by Jesus (Luke 10:1).

The Reunion (vv.28-34): Judah is sent ahead to Goshen (v.28) — Judah the
leader, not Reuben the firstborn. Joseph rides his chariot to meet Jacob:
"fell on his neck, and wept on his neck a good while" (v.29). Jacob: "Now
let me die, since I have seen thy face" (v.30) — echoing Simeon's words
centuries later (Luke 2:29-30). Joseph coaches them to identify as shepherds
so they'll be settled in Goshen — separate from Egyptian society (vv.31-34).

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
• Beer-sheba (v.1) — Jacob's last stop in Canaan before entering Egypt. He
  offers sacrifices here, the traditional southern boundary of the Promised
  Land. God appears to confirm the journey.
• Goshen (v.28) — the eastern Nile Delta, where Jacob reunites with Joseph.
  This fertile region becomes Israel's home for the next 400+ years.
• The journey from Beer-sheba to Goshen crosses the Sinai Peninsula —
  approximately 200 miles through desert terrain.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "Jacob, Jacob.' And he said, 'Here I am.'" (ESV) — God calls; Jacob
        responds. The doubled name: intimacy and urgency.

v.3  — "Do not be afraid to go down to Egypt, for there I will make you into
        a great nation." (ESV) — God's promise: Egypt is not a detour but a
        destination in God's plan.

v.4  — "I myself will go down with you to Egypt, and I will also bring you
        up again." (ESV) — God's presence and God's promise of return.

v.27 — "All the persons of the house of Jacob who came into Egypt were
        seventy." (ESV) — 70 souls become a nation of millions.

v.29 — "He fell on his neck and wept on his neck a good while." (ESV) —
        22 years of separation end in tears of joy.

v.30 — "Now let me die, since I have seen your face and know that you are
        still alive." (ESV) — Jacob's deepest desire fulfilled.

Charles Spurgeon:
  Sermon: "Fear Not" on v.3 (MTP Vol. 12, No. 680)
  "Fear not to go down into Egypt — God does not say 'Egypt will be pleasant'
  or 'Egypt will be easy.' He says 'I will go down WITH THEE.' That is
  enough. The presence of God transforms any Egypt into a Goshen. Jacob
  feared Egypt — and rightly so, for his grandfather Abraham sinned there,
  and Egypt would later enslave his descendants. But God says: fear not. Not
  because Egypt is safe, but because I am with you. Christian, your Egypt —
  whatever trial you face — is not safe. But God is with you in it. And He
  will surely bring you up again."

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1  — Hebrew "zabach zebachim" — slaughtered sacrifices.
       KJV: "offered sacrifices"
       ESV: "offered sacrifices"
       ASV: "offered sacrifices"
       NET: "offered sacrifices"
       WEB: "offered sacrifices"
       Analysis: All identical. Plural — multiple offerings. Jacob worships
       before the journey at Beer-sheba, where both Abraham and Isaac
       worshipped.

v.2  — Hebrew "mar'ot halaylah" — night visions.
       KJV: "visions of the night"
       ESV: "visions of the night"
       ASV: "visions of the night"
       NET: "visions during the night"
       WEB: "visions of the night"
       Analysis: All identical in meaning. Not a dream but a vision — God
       speaks directly. This is the last recorded theophany to a patriarch
       in Genesis.

v.3  — Hebrew "al tira" — do not be afraid.
       KJV: "fear not to go down into Egypt"
       ESV: "Do not be afraid to go down to Egypt"
       ASV: "fear not to go down into Egypt"
       NET: "Do not be afraid to go down to Egypt, for I will make you into a great nation there"
       WEB: "Don't be afraid to go down into Egypt"
       Analysis: ESV/NET/WEB are more natural in modern English; KJV is more
       concise. God's reassurance to Jacob uses the patriarchal promise
       language — the same command given to Abraham (15:1), Isaac (26:24),
       and now Jacob.

v.4  — Hebrew "Yosef yashith yado al einekha" — Joseph shall put his hand
       upon your eyes.
       KJV: "Joseph shall put his hand upon thine eyes"
       ESV: "Joseph's hand shall close your eyes"
       ASV: "Joseph shall put his hand upon thine eyes"
       NET: "I will go down with you to Egypt and I myself will certainly bring you back from there. Joseph will close your eyes"
       WEB: "Joseph will close your eyes"
       Analysis: ESV/WEB/NET are more explicit about the action (closing the
       eyes of the dead). KJV/ASV preserve the literal Hebrew. "Joseph will
       close your eyes" is a tender promise of dying with his son present —
       rendered consistently across all translations.

v.26 — Hebrew "shishim vashesh" — sixty-six.
       KJV: "threescore and six"
       ESV: "sixty-six"
       ASV: "threescore and six"
       NET: "sixty-six persons"
       WEB: "sixty-six"
       Analysis: KJV/ASV use archaic "threescore" (3 x 20); ESV/NET/WEB use
       the numeral. Adding Jacob, Joseph, and Joseph's two sons = 70. Stephen
       says 75 (Acts 7:14), following the LXX which includes additional
       descendants.

v.27 — Hebrew "shiv'im nefesh" — seventy souls.
       KJV: "all the souls of the house of Jacob...were threescore and ten"
       ESV: "All the persons of the house of Jacob who came into Egypt were seventy"
       ASV: "all the souls of the house of Jacob, that came into Egypt, were threescore and ten"
       NET: "All those who went to Egypt with Jacob—his direct descendants, not counting the wives of Jacob's sons—numbered sixty-six persons"
       WEB: "All the souls of the house of Jacob, who came into Egypt, were seventy"
       Analysis: NET adds a clarifying parenthetical about which persons are
       counted, distinguishing the 66 direct descendants from the total of
       70. The number 70 is significant — 70 nations in Genesis 10, 70
       elders later appointed (Exodus 24:1).

v.34 — Hebrew "to'avat Mitsrayim" — an abomination to the Egyptians.
       KJV: "every shepherd is an abomination unto the Egyptians"
       ESV: "every shepherd is an abomination to the Egyptians"
       ASV: "every shepherd is an abomination unto the Egyptians"
       NET: "all shepherds are disgusting to the Egyptians"
       WEB: "every shepherd is an abomination to the Egyptians"
       Analysis: NET's "disgusting" is blunter than KJV/ESV/ASV/WEB's
       "abomination." The Egyptians despised shepherds. This cultural
       prejudice providentially keeps Israel separate in Goshen — preserving
       their distinct identity and faith.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Beer-sheba     — "Well of the oath" — the southernmost city of Canaan.
               Sacred to Abraham and Isaac. Jacob's last stop before Egypt.

"Jacob, Jacob" — The doubled name indicates divine intimacy and urgency.
               God uses this pattern with Abraham, Moses, Samuel, and
               others at critical moments.

Seventy souls  — The total number entering Egypt. A small family that will
               become a great nation — God's faithfulness to His covenant
               promise.

Goshen         — A fertile region in the eastern Nile Delta, ideal for
               shepherds. Separate from mainstream Egyptian society —
               preserving Israel's identity and faith.

"Joseph shall put his hand upon thine eyes" — The custom of closing a
               deceased person's eyes. God promises Jacob a peaceful death
               with Joseph present — comfort after 22 years of grief.

Theophany      — A visible or audible manifestation of God. Genesis 46:2-4
               is the last theophany to a patriarch in Genesis.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Fear not to go down into Egypt; for I will there make of thee a great
   nation" (v.3). God doesn't promise the journey will be easy — He promises
   His presence and purpose. What "Egypt" are you facing? God goes with you.

2. "I will go down with thee... and I will also surely bring thee up again"
   (v.4). God promises both presence AND return. Whatever valley you're in,
   it's not permanent. God will bring you through.

3. Jacob stopped at Beer-sheba to worship before the journey (v.1). Do you
   seek God's confirmation before major life decisions? Or do you rush ahead?

4. 70 souls became a nation of millions. Never despise small beginnings
   (Zechariah 4:10). What has God entrusted to you that seems small? Be
   faithful — He multiplies.

5. "Now let me die, since I have seen thy face" (v.30). Jacob's deepest
   longing fulfilled. What is your deepest longing? Trust God's timing —
   even if it takes 22 years.
