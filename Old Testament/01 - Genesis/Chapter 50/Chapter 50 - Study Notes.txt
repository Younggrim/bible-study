Genesis - Chapter 50
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Jacob is buried in Canaan; Joseph reassures his fearful brothers that God meant their evil for good, and dies in Egypt at 110.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 50 closes the book of Genesis — the book of
beginnings ends with a coffin in Egypt (v.26). Yet it ends in faith, not
despair. Jacob is buried in Canaan as promised. Joseph forgives his brothers
with the greatest theological statement in Genesis: "Ye thought evil against
me; but God meant it unto good" (v.20). Joseph dies at 110, making his
brothers swear to carry his bones to Canaan when God "visits" them — an act
of faith fulfilled 400 years later (Exodus 13:19; Joshua 24:32). Genesis
begins with God creating life; it ends with a coffin — but between them
stands the promise of redemption.

Jacob's Burial (vv.1-14): Joseph weeps over his father and kisses him (v.1).
Jacob is embalmed — 40 days for embalming, 70 days of Egyptian mourning
(vv.2-3). Joseph requests permission to fulfill his oath (vv.4-6). A massive
funeral procession — "a very great company" of Egyptian officials, chariots,
and horsemen accompanies the family (vv.7-9). Seven days of mourning at the
threshing floor of Atad (v.10). The Canaanites name it "Abel-mizraim" —
"mourning of Egypt" (v.11). Jacob is buried in Machpelah with Abraham,
Sarah, Isaac, Rebekah, and Leah (v.13).

The Brothers' Fear (vv.15-21): With Jacob dead, the brothers fear Joseph
will take revenge (v.15). They send a message claiming Jacob commanded
forgiveness (v.16-17) — possibly fabricated. Joseph weeps (v.17). They fall
before him: "we be thy servants" (v.18) — dreams fulfilled one final time.
Joseph's response: "Fear not: for am I in the place of God?" (v.19). Then
the summit of Genesis theology: "Ye thought evil against me; but God meant
it unto good, to bring to pass, as it is this day, to save much people
alive" (v.20). He comforts them and promises to nourish them (v.21).

Joseph's Death (vv.22-26): Joseph lives to 110 — considered the ideal
lifespan in Egyptian culture (v.22). He sees great-grandchildren (v.23).
His dying words: "God will surely visit you, and bring you out of this land
unto the land which he sware to Abraham, to Isaac, and to Jacob" (v.24).
He makes them swear to carry his bones out (v.25). Joseph dies, is embalmed,
and placed in a coffin in Egypt (v.26) — awaiting the exodus.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
• Machpelah/Hebron (v.13) — Jacob is buried in the cave Abraham purchased
  (ch.23), alongside Abraham, Sarah, Isaac, Rebekah, and Leah. The
  patriarchal family tomb in the Promised Land.
• The threshing floor of Atad (v.10) — beyond the Jordan, where the funeral
  procession mourns. The Canaanites name it Abel-mizraim ("mourning of
  Egypt") because of the great Egyptian entourage.
• Joseph's bones remain in Egypt "in a coffin" (v.26), awaiting the Exodus —
  a statement of faith that God will bring Israel back to Canaan (v.24-25).

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.17 — "Please forgive the transgression of your brothers and their sin."
        (ESV) — The plea for forgiveness.

v.19 — "Do not fear, for am I in the place of God?" (ESV) — Joseph refuses
        to play God. Vengeance belongs to the Lord.

v.20 — "As for you, you meant evil against me, but God meant it for good, to
        bring it about that many people should be kept alive, as they are
        today." (ESV) — THE theological statement of Genesis. Human evil,
        divine sovereignty, redemptive purpose.

v.24 — "God will surely visit you and bring you up out of this land to the
        land that he swore to Abraham, to Isaac, and to Jacob." (ESV) —
        Faith looking forward to the exodus.

v.25 — "You shall carry up my bones from here." (ESV) — Joseph's faith:
        Canaan is home, even in death.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/genesis-50/
  Guzik emphasizes v.20 as the theological climax of Genesis — God's
  sovereignty over human evil for redemptive purposes. Also covers Joseph's
  faith in the future exodus.

Charles Spurgeon:
  Sermon: "God Meant It Unto Good" on v.20 (MTP Vol. 35, No. 2084)
  "YE thought evil — BUT GOD meant it unto good. Here are two wills at work:
  the will of man, which is evil; and the will of God, which is good. And
  God's will overrules man's will without destroying it. The brothers MEANT
  evil — they are responsible. God MEANT good — He is sovereign. Both are
  true simultaneously. This is not fatalism — fatalism says 'it doesn't
  matter what you do.' This is providence — 'what you did was evil, AND God
  used it for good.' The cross is the ultimate proof: wicked hands crucified
  Christ — AND God's determinate counsel ordained it for the salvation of
  the world. Joseph's story is the gospel in miniature: rejected by his
  brothers, exalted to save them, forgiving them freely."

  Sermon: "Joseph's Bones" on v.25 (MTP Vol. 22, No. 1300)
  "Carry up my bones — Joseph's faith looked beyond death to the promise.
  He did not say 'bury me in a pyramid.' He said 'carry me HOME.' Four
  hundred years his bones waited in that coffin — but God kept His word.
  Christian, your faith may outlast your lifetime. The promises you trust
  may be fulfilled after you're gone. But they WILL be fulfilled."

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1  — Hebrew "yippol al penei" — fell upon his face.
       KJV: "fell upon his father's face"
       ESV: "fell on his father's face"
       ASV: "fell upon his father's face"
       NET: "hugged his father's face"
       WEB: "fell on his face"
       Analysis: NET interprets the gesture as an embrace ("hugged") rather
       than prostration. KJV, ESV, and ASV render literally. The Hebrew
       allows both readings — an intimate gesture of grief over the dead.

v.2  — Hebrew "chanat" — to spice, to embalm.
       KJV: "embalm"
       ESV: "embalm"
       ASV: "embalm"
       NET: "embalm"
       WEB: "embalm"
       Analysis: All translations identical. An Egyptian practice, not
       typically Israelite. Jacob receives Egyptian honors because of
       Joseph's position.

v.10 — Hebrew "abel" — means both "mourning" and "meadow."
       KJV: "Abel-mizraim"
       ESV: "Abel-mizraim"
       ASV: "Abel-mizraim"
       NET: "Abel Mizraim"
       WEB: "Abel Mizraim"
       Analysis: All transliterate and preserve the wordplay. The meadow of
       mourning / the mourning of Egypt. NET explains the etymology directly
       in the text.

v.15 — Hebrew "hashev yashiv" — will surely repay.
       KJV: "will certainly requite us"
       ESV: "will pay us back"
       ASV: "will certainly requite us"
       NET: "will now repay us"
       WEB: "will certainly repay us"
       Analysis: The ESV is more colloquial; the KJV and ASV more formal.
       NET adds "now" — implying the brothers feared immediate retribution
       once Jacob's restraining presence was gone.

v.19 — Hebrew "hatachat Elohim ani" — am I in God's place?
       KJV: "am I in the place of God?"
       ESV: "Am I in the place of God?"
       ASV: "am I in the place of God?"
       NET: "Am I in the place of God?"
       WEB: "Am I in the place of God?"
       Analysis: All translations identical. Joseph refuses to usurp God's
       role as judge. This theological statement reappears from 30:2
       (Jacob's earlier words) but here carries the full weight of
       forgiveness.

v.20 — Hebrew "chashav" — to plan, to devise (used for both agents).
       KJV: "ye thought evil against me; but God meant it unto good"
       ESV: "you meant evil against me, but God meant it for good"
       ASV: "ye meant evil against me; but God meant it for good"
       NET: "you meant to harm me, but God intended it for a good purpose"
       WEB: "you meant evil against me, but God meant it for good"
       Analysis: The same Hebrew verb "chashav" is used for both: you
       PLANNED evil; God PLANNED it for good. Same verb, different agents,
       opposite intentions. NET's "intended it for a good purpose" is more
       expansive. All capture the theological summit perfectly — human
       responsibility and divine sovereignty affirmed simultaneously.

v.25 — Hebrew "paqod yifqod" — He will certainly attend to you, intervene.
       KJV: "God will surely visit you"
       ESV: "God will surely visit you"
       ASV: "God will surely visit you"
       NET: "God will surely come to you"
       WEB: "God will surely visit you"
       Analysis: NET uses "come to you" rather than "visit." The Hebrew
       "paqad" means a purposeful divine intervention. Joseph prophesies
       the exodus — fulfilled when God appeared to Moses at the burning bush.

v.26 — Hebrew "aron" — a chest, a box.
       KJV: "a coffin in Egypt"
       ESV: "a coffin in Egypt"
       ASV: "a coffin in Egypt"
       NET: "a coffin in Egypt"
       WEB: "a coffin in Egypt"
       Analysis: All translations identical. The same word "aron" is used
       for the Ark of the Covenant. Genesis ends with a coffin; the exodus
       begins with that coffin being carried out. A powerful final image.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
"God meant it unto good" — The theological summit of Genesis. God's
               sovereign purpose overrules human evil without excusing it.
               Both human responsibility and divine sovereignty are affirmed.

Embalming      — Egyptian preservation of the dead. A 40-day process. Jacob
               and Joseph are both embalmed — unusual for Hebrews but
               appropriate given their Egyptian context.

Abel-mizraim   — "Mourning of Egypt" — the place where the funeral
               procession mourned for Jacob. Even pagans recognized the
               significance of Jacob's death.

"Am I in the place of God?" — Joseph's refusal to take vengeance. He
               recognizes that judgment belongs to God alone — and that God
               has already turned the evil to good.

"God will surely visit you" — Joseph's prophecy of the exodus. "Visit" =
               divine intervention. Fulfilled when God appeared to Moses
               at the burning bush.

"A coffin in Egypt" — Genesis's final image. A book that begins with
               creation ends with death — but death awaiting resurrection.
               Joseph's bones wait for deliverance, just as believers await
               the resurrection.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Ye thought evil against me; but God meant it unto good" (v.20). This is
   the lens through which to view all suffering. Can you trust that God is
   working even through the evil done to you? This doesn't excuse the evil
   — it magnifies God's sovereignty over it.

2. "Am I in the place of God?" (v.19). Joseph refuses to take revenge. Are
   you holding onto the right to punish someone? Release it. You are not in
   the place of God.

3. "God will surely visit you" (v.24). Joseph dies in faith — trusting a
   promise he won't see fulfilled in his lifetime. What promises are you
   trusting that may outlast your life? Faith doesn't require seeing the
   fulfillment.

4. "Carry up my bones" (v.25). Joseph's identity is not Egyptian — it's
   Israelite. Despite 93 years in Egypt, his heart belongs to the Promised
   Land. Where is YOUR true home? Don't let comfort in "Egypt" make you
   forget where you belong.

5. Genesis ends with a coffin — but not without hope. The coffin awaits the
   exodus. Death awaits resurrection. Whatever "coffin" you're staring at
   today — a dead dream, a lost relationship, a failed plan — God will
   surely visit. He keeps His promises.
