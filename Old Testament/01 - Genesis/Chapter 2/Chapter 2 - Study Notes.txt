Genesis - Chapter 2
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
God forms man from dust, breathes life into him, plants Eden, and creates woman from man's side as his perfect complement.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 2 is not a second creation account but a detailed
expansion of Day 6 — focusing on the creation of man and woman and the
establishment of Eden. Chapter 1 gives the cosmic overview; chapter 2 zooms
in on humanity. The name "LORD God" (Yahweh Elohim) appears for the first
time — combining God's covenant name (personal, relational) with His power
name (majestic, sovereign).

God's Rest (vv.1-3): God finished His work and rested on the seventh day
(v.2). He blessed and sanctified it (v.3). Not rest from exhaustion but rest
of completion — the work is done. This establishes the Sabbath pattern.

Man's Creation (vv.4-7): God "formed man of the dust of the ground" (v.7) —
hands-on, intimate creation. Then "breathed into his nostrils the breath of
life" (v.7). Man is both earthly (dust) and divine (God's breath). A "living
soul" — body + breath = life.

The Garden of Eden (vv.8-17): God planted a garden — paradise (v.8). Two
special trees: the tree of life and the tree of knowledge of good and evil
(v.9). One command: eat freely of everything EXCEPT the tree of knowledge —
"in the day that thou eatest thereof thou shalt surely die" (vv.16-17).
Freedom with one boundary.

The Creation of Woman (vv.18-25): "It is not good that the man should be
alone" (v.18) — the first "not good" in creation. No animal was a suitable
partner (vv.19-20). God made woman from man's rib — "bone of my bones, and
flesh of my flesh" (v.23). Marriage established: leave, cleave, one flesh
(v.24). Naked without shame (v.25).

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
• Eden is described with four rivers: Pishon, Gihon, Tigris (Hiddekel), and
  Euphrates (vv.10-14), placing it in the Mesopotamian region (modern Iraq).
• The lands of Havilah (possibly Arabia) and Cush (possibly Ethiopia or
  Mesopotamian Kush) are mentioned in connection with the rivers.
• The garden functions as sacred space — a temple-like place where God dwells
  with humanity. Geography here is theological as much as physical.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "And on the seventh day God finished his work that he had done, and
        he rested." (ESV) — God's rest of completion.

v.7  — "Then the LORD God formed the man of dust from the ground and breathed
        into his nostrils the breath of life, and the man became a living
        creature." (ESV) — Humanity: dust + divine breath.

v.17 — "But of the tree of the knowledge of good and evil you shall not eat,
        for in the day that you eat of it you shall surely die." (ESV) —
        The one prohibition.

v.18 — "Then the LORD God said, 'It is not good that the man should be
        alone; I will make him a helper fit for him.'" (ESV) — The need for
        companionship.

v.24 — "Therefore a man shall leave his father and his mother and hold fast
        to his wife, and they shall become one flesh." (ESV) — The
        institution of marriage.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.2  — KJV: "ended his work" / ESV: "finished his work" — Both equivalent.
       Hebrew "wayekal" — completed, finished. KJV's "ended" and ESV's
       "finished" convey the same meaning.
       KJV: "rested" / ESV: "rested" — Both identical. Hebrew "shabath" —
       ceased, stopped, desisted. Root of "Sabbath." Not exhaustion but
       completion. God stopped because the work was finished.

v.3  — KJV: "sanctified it" / ESV: "made it holy" — Hebrew "wayeqaddesh" —
       set apart, made holy. ESV renders the Hebrew verb more transparently
       for modern readers; KJV uses the Latinate "sanctified." Same meaning.

v.4  — KJV: "LORD God" / ESV: "LORD God" — Both identical. Hebrew "Yahweh
       Elohim." First use of God's covenant name (Yahweh/LORD) combined with
       His power name (Elohim/God). Personal + powerful. Relational +
       sovereign.

v.5  — KJV: "every plant of the field before it was in the earth, and every
       herb of the field before it grew" / ESV: "When no bush of the field
       was yet in the land and no small plant of the field had yet sprung
       up" — Hebrew "siach" (bush/shrub) and "eseb" (plant/herb). ESV
       restructures the sentence for clarity and uses "bush" and "small
       plant" for more precise rendering.

v.6  — KJV: "a mist" / ESV: "a mist" — Both identical. Hebrew "ed" — mist,
       vapor, or spring. The exact meaning is debated; both translations use
       "mist."

v.7  — KJV: "formed man of the dust of the ground" / ESV: "formed the man
       of dust from the ground" — Both equivalent. Hebrew "yatsar" — to
       shape, mold, fashion (like a potter with clay). Intimate, hands-on
       creation.
       KJV: "a living soul" / ESV: "a living creature" — Hebrew "nephesh
       chayyah" — a living being, a breathing creature. ESV's "creature"
       avoids the theological baggage modern readers attach to "soul" (the
       same Hebrew phrase is used of animals in 1:24). KJV's "soul" reflects
       older English usage meaning "living being."

v.13 — KJV: "Ethiopia" / ESV: "Cush" — Hebrew "Kush." ESV retains the
       Hebrew name; KJV uses the Greek/English equivalent. Cush refers to the
       region south of Egypt (modern Sudan/Ethiopia area).

v.14 — KJV: "Hiddekel" / ESV: "Tigris" — Hebrew "Chiddeqel." ESV uses the
       modern name (Tigris) for clarity; KJV transliterates the Hebrew.

v.15 — KJV: "to dress it and to keep it" / ESV: "to work it and keep it" —
       Hebrew "le'abdah uleshomrah" — to serve/work it and to guard/keep it.
       ESV's "work" is more natural modern English than KJV's "dress"
       (which meant "tend" in 1611).

v.17 — KJV: "thou shalt surely die" / ESV: "you shall surely die" — Both
       equivalent (difference is only thou/you). Hebrew "moth tamuth" —
       dying you shall die. An emphatic construction: death is certain.
       Spiritual death immediately; physical death eventually.

v.18 — KJV: "an help meet for him" / ESV: "a helper fit for him" — Hebrew
       "ezer kenegdo" — a helper corresponding to him, a counterpart
       suitable for him. KJV's "help meet" is two words (help + meet/
       suitable); ESV's "helper fit" captures the same idea more clearly.
       "Ezer" is used of God Himself (Psalm 33:20, 70:5) — not a
       subordinate term but a strong one.

v.19 — KJV: "every fowl of the air" / ESV: "every bird of the heavens" —
       Hebrew "oph hashamayim." ESV uses "bird" (modern) vs. KJV's "fowl"
       (archaic), and "heavens" (literal Hebrew) vs. "air."

v.21 — KJV: "rib" / ESV: "rib" — Both identical. Hebrew "tsela" — side,
       rib, or side-chamber. Woman taken from man's side — not his head (to
       rule over him) or his feet (to be trampled) but his side (to be
       equal, near his heart).

v.23 — KJV: "This is now bone of my bones" / ESV: "This at last is bone of
       my bones" — Hebrew "zoth happa'am" — this time, now, at last. ESV's
       "at last" captures the sense of joyful culmination after naming all
       the animals and finding no match.

v.24 — KJV: "cleave unto his wife" / ESV: "hold fast to his wife" — Hebrew
       "dabaq" — to cling, stick to, be joined permanently. The strongest
       word for attachment. ESV's "hold fast" is more accessible to modern
       readers than KJV's "cleave" (which now commonly means "to split").

TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- WEB uses "Yahweh" throughout for the divine name (KJV/ESV: "the LORD").
  This reflects the actual pronunciation of the Hebrew Tetragrammaton YHWH.
  ASV uses "Jehovah" — an older English hybrid form of the same name.
- v.6 — NET: "Springs would well up from the earth" — renders Hebrew "ed" as
  "springs" rather than "mist" (KJV/ESV/ASV/WEB), reflecting a scholarly view
  that the term refers to subterranean water sources, not atmospheric vapor.
- v.8 — NET: "planted an orchard" — uniquely renders Hebrew "gan" as "orchard"
  rather than "garden" (KJV/ESV/ASV/WEB), emphasizing the food-producing trees.
- v.18 — NET: "a companion for him who corresponds to him" — interprets "ezer
  kenegdo" with relational language ("companion") vs. functional ("helper").
  WEB: "a helper comparable to him" — emphasizes equality.
- v.21 — NET: "took part of the man's side" — avoids committing to "rib,"
  reflecting the broader meaning of Hebrew "tsela" (side/side-chamber).
- v.24 — NET: "they become one family" — a distinctive rendering where all
  others have "one flesh," interpreting the unity as household/kinship.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Sabbath/Rest   — God's cessation from creative work on the seventh day. Not
               exhaustion but completion. The pattern for human rest.

Formed from dust — Humanity's humble material origin. We are earthly,
               physical, mortal — apart from God's breath.

Breath of life — God's direct, personal gift of life to humanity. What
               distinguishes humans from mere matter.

Tree of life   — Access to eternal life in God's presence. Lost at the fall;
               restored in Revelation 22.

Tree of knowledge — The one prohibition. Not evil in itself but the test of
               obedience. The boundary of human freedom.

Help meet/Ezer — A helper corresponding to the man. A strong term (used of
               God) meaning a counterpart, a complement, an equal partner.

One flesh      — The profound union of marriage — physical, emotional,
               spiritual. Two becoming one.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "God rested on the seventh day" (v.2). God modeled rest. Do you rest? Or
   do you work endlessly, as if everything depends on you? Rest is not
   laziness — it's trust.

2. "Formed man of the dust... breathed into his nostrils the breath of life"
   (v.7). You are both humble (dust) and dignified (God's breath). Do you
   live in both realities — humble before God, yet confident in your worth?

3. "Of every tree... thou mayest freely eat: but..." (vv.16-17). God gave
   enormous freedom with one boundary. How do you respond to God's boundaries
   — with gratitude for the freedom or resentment at the restriction?

4. "It is not good that the man should be alone" (v.18). God designed us for
   relationship. Are you investing in deep, meaningful relationships? Or
   trying to live in isolation?

5. "Leave... cleave... one flesh" (v.24). If you're married, are you fully
   "leaving" (old attachments) and "cleaving" (new commitment)? Is your
   marriage reflecting God's design?
