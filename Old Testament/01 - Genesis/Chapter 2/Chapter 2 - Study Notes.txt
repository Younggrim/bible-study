Genesis - Chapter 2
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
God forms man from dust, breathes life into him, plants Eden, and creates woman from man's side as his perfect complement.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 2 is not a second creation account but a detailed
expansion of Day 6 — focusing on the creation of man and woman and the
establishment of Eden. Chapter 1 gives the cosmic overview; chapter 2 zooms
in on humanity. The name "LORD God" (Yahweh Elohim) appears for the first
time — combining God's covenant name (personal, relational) with His power
name (majestic, sovereign).

God's Rest (vv.1-3): God finished His work and rested on the seventh day
(v.2). He blessed and sanctified it (v.3). Not rest from exhaustion but rest
of completion — the work is done. This establishes the Sabbath pattern.

Man's Creation (vv.4-7): God "formed man of the dust of the ground" (v.7) —
hands-on, intimate creation. Then "breathed into his nostrils the breath of
life" (v.7). Man is both earthly (dust) and divine (God's breath). A "living
soul" — body + breath = life.

The Garden of Eden (vv.8-17): God planted a garden — paradise (v.8). Two
special trees: the tree of life and the tree of knowledge of good and evil
(v.9). One command: eat freely of everything EXCEPT the tree of knowledge —
"in the day that thou eatest thereof thou shalt surely die" (vv.16-17).
Freedom with one boundary.

The Creation of Woman (vv.18-25): "It is not good that the man should be
alone" (v.18) — the first "not good" in creation. No animal was a suitable
partner (vv.19-20). God made woman from man's rib — "bone of my bones, and
flesh of my flesh" (v.23). Marriage established: leave, cleave, one flesh
(v.24). Naked without shame (v.25).

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
• Eden is described with four rivers: Pishon, Gihon, Tigris (Hiddekel), and
  Euphrates (vv.10-14), placing it in the Mesopotamian region (modern Iraq).
• The lands of Havilah (possibly Arabia) and Cush (possibly Ethiopia or
  Mesopotamian Kush) are mentioned in connection with the rivers.
• The garden functions as sacred space — a temple-like place where God dwells
  with humanity. Geography here is theological as much as physical.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "And on the seventh day God finished his work that he had done, and
        he rested." (ESV) — God's rest of completion.

v.7  — "Then the LORD God formed the man of dust from the ground and breathed
        into his nostrils the breath of life, and the man became a living
        creature." (ESV) — Humanity: dust + divine breath.

v.17 — "But of the tree of the knowledge of good and evil you shall not eat,
        for in the day that you eat of it you shall surely die." (ESV) —
        The one prohibition.

v.18 — "Then the LORD God said, 'It is not good that the man should be
        alone; I will make him a helper fit for him.'" (ESV) — The need for
        companionship.

v.24 — "Therefore a man shall leave his father and his mother and hold fast
        to his wife, and they shall become one flesh." (ESV) — The
        institution of marriage.

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.2  — Hebrew "wayekal... shabath" — completed/finished... ceased/stopped.
       KJV: "ended his work... rested"
       ESV: "finished his work... rested"
       ASV: "finished his work... rested"
       NET: "completed the work... ceased on the seventh day"
       WEB: "finished his work... rested"
       Analysis: All essentially identical. NET uses "ceased" instead of
       "rested" — closer to the Hebrew "shabath" (stopped/desisted). Root
       of "Sabbath." Not exhaustion but completion.

v.3  — Hebrew "wayeqaddesh" — set apart, made holy.
       KJV: "sanctified it"
       ESV: "made it holy"
       ASV: "hallowed it"
       NET: "set it apart as holy"
       WEB: "made it holy"
       Analysis: Four different English renderings of the same concept. KJV
       uses Latinate "sanctified"; ASV uses "hallowed" (Anglo-Saxon);
       ESV/WEB use plain "made it holy"; NET makes the action explicit:
       "set it apart as holy."

v.4  — Hebrew "Yahweh Elohim" — the LORD God.
       KJV: "the LORD God"
       ESV: "the LORD God"
       ASV: "Jehovah God"
       NET: "the LORD God"
       WEB: "Yahweh God"
       Analysis: First use of God's covenant name combined with His power
       name. ASV uses "Jehovah" — an older English hybrid. WEB uses
       "Yahweh" — the likely original pronunciation. KJV/ESV/NET use the
       traditional substitute "the LORD" (all caps).

v.6  — Hebrew "ed" — mist, vapor, or spring.
       KJV: "a mist"
       ESV: "a mist"
       ASV: "a mist"
       NET: "springs would well up from the earth"
       WEB: "a mist"
       Analysis: NET is unique — renders "ed" as "springs" rather than
       "mist," reflecting a scholarly view that the term refers to
       subterranean water sources, not atmospheric vapor. All others retain
       the traditional "mist."

v.7  — Hebrew "yatsar" (formed) + "nephesh chayyah" (living being).
       KJV: "formed man of the dust... became a living soul"
       ESV: "formed the man of dust... became a living creature"
       ASV: "formed man of the dust... became a living soul"
       NET: "formed the man from the soil... became a living being"
       WEB: "formed man from the dust... became a living soul"
       Analysis: "Yatsar" = to shape like a potter with clay — intimate,
       hands-on creation. ESV's "creature" and NET's "being" avoid the
       theological baggage modern readers attach to "soul" (the same Hebrew
       phrase "nephesh chayyah" is used of animals in 1:24). NET uses "soil"
       instead of "dust" — a farming-context rendering of "adamah."

v.8  — Hebrew "gan" — garden, enclosed area.
       KJV: "planted a garden"
       ESV: "planted a garden"
       ASV: "planted a garden"
       NET: "planted an orchard"
       WEB: "planted a garden"
       Analysis: NET uniquely renders "gan" as "orchard" — emphasizing the
       food-producing trees. All others retain "garden."

v.15 — Hebrew "le'abdah uleshomrah" — to serve/work it and to guard/keep it.
       KJV: "to dress it and to keep it"
       ESV: "to work it and keep it"
       ASV: "to dress it and to keep it"
       NET: "to care for it and to maintain it"
       WEB: "to cultivate and keep it"
       Analysis: KJV/ASV's "dress" meant "tend" in 1611. ESV's "work" is
       clear. NET's "care for... maintain" is functional. WEB's "cultivate"
       emphasizes agriculture. The Hebrew "shamar" (keep/guard) implies
       protection, not just maintenance.

v.18 — Hebrew "ezer kenegdo" — a helper corresponding to him.
       KJV: "an help meet for him"
       ESV: "a helper fit for him"
       ASV: "a help meet for him"
       NET: "a companion for him who corresponds to him"
       WEB: "a helper comparable to him"
       Analysis: NET uses relational "companion" vs. functional "helper."
       WEB emphasizes equality with "comparable." "Ezer" is used of God
       Himself (Psalm 33:20, 70:5) — not a subordinate term but a strong
       one. "Kenegdo" = opposite/corresponding to — a counterpart.

v.21 — Hebrew "tsela" — side, rib, or side-chamber.
       KJV: "took one of his ribs"
       ESV: "took one of his ribs"
       ASV: "took one of his ribs"
       NET: "took part of the man's side"
       WEB: "took one of his ribs"
       Analysis: NET avoids committing to "rib," reflecting the broader
       meaning of "tsela" (side/side-chamber). Woman taken from man's side
       — not his head (to rule) or his feet (to be trampled) but his side
       (to be equal, near his heart).

v.24 — Hebrew "dabaq" — to cling, stick to, be joined permanently.
       KJV: "shall cleave unto his wife: and they shall be one flesh"
       ESV: "shall hold fast to his wife, and they shall become one flesh"
       ASV: "shall cleave unto his wife: and they shall be one flesh"
       NET: "will be united with his wife, and they will become one family"
       WEB: "will join with his wife, and they will be one flesh"
       Analysis: NET is distinctive — "become one family" where all others
       have "one flesh," interpreting the unity as household/kinship rather
       than physical/spiritual union. ESV's "hold fast" and WEB's "join"
       are more accessible than KJV/ASV's "cleave" (which now commonly
       means "to split" in modern English).

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Sabbath/Rest   — God's cessation from creative work on the seventh day. Not
               exhaustion but completion. The pattern for human rest.

Formed from dust — Humanity's humble material origin. We are earthly,
               physical, mortal — apart from God's breath.

Breath of life — God's direct, personal gift of life to humanity. What
               distinguishes humans from mere matter.

Tree of life   — Access to eternal life in God's presence. Lost at the fall;
               restored in Revelation 22.

Tree of knowledge — The one prohibition. Not evil in itself but the test of
               obedience. The boundary of human freedom.

Help meet/Ezer — A helper corresponding to the man. A strong term (used of
               God) meaning a counterpart, a complement, an equal partner.

One flesh      — The profound union of marriage — physical, emotional,
               spiritual. Two becoming one.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "God rested on the seventh day" (v.2). God modeled rest. Do you rest? Or
   do you work endlessly, as if everything depends on you? Rest is not
   laziness — it's trust.

2. "Formed man of the dust... breathed into his nostrils the breath of life"
   (v.7). You are both humble (dust) and dignified (God's breath). Do you
   live in both realities — humble before God, yet confident in your worth?

3. "Of every tree... thou mayest freely eat: but..." (vv.16-17). God gave
   enormous freedom with one boundary. How do you respond to God's boundaries
   — with gratitude for the freedom or resentment at the restriction?

4. "It is not good that the man should be alone" (v.18). God designed us for
   relationship. Are you investing in deep, meaningful relationships? Or
   trying to live in isolation?

5. "Leave... cleave... one flesh" (v.24). If you're married, are you fully
   "leaving" (old attachments) and "cleaving" (new commitment)? Is your
   marriage reflecting God's design?
