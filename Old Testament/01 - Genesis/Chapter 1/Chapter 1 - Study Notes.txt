Genesis - Chapter 1
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
God creates the universe in six days by His word, culminating in humanity made in His image.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses. Jewish and Christian tradition unanimously attribute Genesis
to Moses (cf. Exodus 17:14, John 5:46-47, Luke 24:27, 44). Written
approximately 1445-1405 BC during the wilderness wanderings.

Title: "Genesis" = Greek for "origin/beginning." Hebrew title: "Bereshith" =
"In the beginning." The book of beginnings — the origin of the universe,
life, humanity, sin, death, nations, languages, and redemption.

Purpose: To reveal God as Creator, to establish humanity's identity and
purpose, and to begin the story of redemption that culminates in Christ.
Genesis answers the fundamental questions: Where did we come from? Why are we
here? What went wrong? What is God doing about it?

Chapter 1 Overview: The creation account — six days of God creating the
universe by His word, culminating in humanity made in His image, followed by
the seventh day of rest (2:1-3). The chapter establishes God's sovereignty,
power, wisdom, and goodness. The repeated refrain: "And God said... and it
was so... and God saw that it was good."

Structure of the Six Days:
- Days 1-3: Forming (giving shape to the formless)
  Day 1: Light/darkness. Day 2: Sky/waters. Day 3: Land/seas/vegetation.
- Days 4-6: Filling (populating the void)
  Day 4: Sun/moon/stars. Day 5: Sea creatures/birds. Day 6: Animals/humanity.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
• Eden's exact location is debated, but the Tigris and Euphrates rivers
  (modern Iraq) are named in 2:10-14, placing the setting in ancient
  Mesopotamia.
• The creation account presents the entire earth as God's domain — no single
  location is emphasized because the scope is cosmic and universal.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "In the beginning, God created the heavens and the earth." (ESV) —
        The foundational statement of all Scripture. God exists. God creates.

v.2  — "And the Spirit of God was hovering over the face of the waters."
        (ESV) — The Spirit active in creation.

v.3  — "And God said, 'Let there be light,' and there was light." (ESV) —
        Creation by divine speech. God's word has creative power.

v.26 — "Then God said, 'Let us make man in our image, after our likeness.'"
        (ESV) — The plural "us" — hints of the Trinity. Humanity as God's
        image-bearers.

v.27 — "So God created man in his own image, in the image of God he created
        him; male and female he created them." (ESV) — The dignity of
        humanity.

v.28 — "Be fruitful and multiply and fill the earth and subdue it, and have
        dominion." (ESV) — The cultural mandate.

v.31 — "And God saw everything that he had made, and behold, it was very
        good." (ESV) — Creation's original goodness.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1  — KJV: "the heaven" / ESV: "the heavens" — Hebrew "hashamayim" — the
       Hebrew is plural ("shamayim"), so ESV's "heavens" is more literal.
       KJV uses singular "heaven" as a collective. Both convey the same
       meaning: the entire expanse above.

v.1  — KJV: "In the beginning" / ESV: "In the beginning" — Both identical.
       Hebrew "bereshith" — in the beginning of, at the start of. The
       absolute beginning of created time and space. "God" — Hebrew "Elohim"
       — plural form (more than two) with singular verb ("created" = "bara"
       singular). Plurality in unity — early hint of the Trinity.

v.1  — KJV: "created" / ESV: "created" — Both identical. Hebrew "bara" — to
       create from nothing (ex nihilo). Used only of God in the OT.
       Distinguished from "asah" (to make/form from existing material) and
       "yatsar" (to shape/fashion).

v.2  — KJV: "without form, and void" / ESV: "without form and void" — Both
       equivalent. Hebrew "tohu wabohu" — formless and empty. Not chaotic
       evil but unformed and unfilled — ready to be shaped and populated in
       the six days.

v.2  — KJV: "moved upon the face of the waters" / ESV: "was hovering over
       the face of the waters" — Hebrew "merachephet" — hovered, brooded
       (like a bird over its nest). ESV's "hovering" captures the Hebrew
       participle more precisely than KJV's "moved." The Spirit actively
       present, nurturing creation.

v.5  — KJV: "the evening and the morning were the first day" / ESV: "there
       was evening and there was morning, the first day" — Hebrew days begin
       at sunset. ESV renders the Hebrew construction more literally ("there
       was evening and there was morning"). Both convey the same meaning:
       each day = evening (darkness) then morning (light) = one complete day.

v.6  — KJV: "firmament" / ESV: "expanse" — Hebrew "raqia" — something
       spread out or stretched. KJV's "firmament" (from Latin Vulgate
       "firmamentum") can imply a solid dome; ESV's "expanse" better conveys
       the Hebrew sense of an open, stretched-out space.

v.11 — KJV: "bring forth grass, the herb yielding seed" / ESV: "sprout
       vegetation, plants yielding seed" — Hebrew "deshe" (vegetation/grass)
       and "eseb" (herb/plant). ESV uses "sprout" for the verb (more vivid)
       and "vegetation" / "plants" for modern clarity. KJV's "grass" and
       "herb" are older English equivalents.

v.20 — KJV: "bring forth abundantly the moving creature that hath life, and
       fowl" / ESV: "swarm with swarms of living creatures, and let birds
       fly" — Hebrew "yishretsu sherets" (swarm with swarms) and "oph"
       (birds/fowl). ESV captures the Hebrew wordplay of "swarm with swarms"
       and uses "birds" instead of archaic "fowl."

v.21 — KJV: "great whales" / ESV: "great sea creatures" — Hebrew "tanninim
       gedolim" — large sea creatures. ESV's "sea creatures" is broader and
       more accurate; "tanninim" refers to large marine animals generally,
       not specifically whales.

v.26 — KJV: "Let us make man in our image, after our likeness" / ESV: "Let
       us make man in our image, after our likeness" — Both identical.
       Hebrew "na'aseh" — first person plural. God speaks within Himself —
       the Trinity in consultation. "Image" (tselem) = representation,
       likeness. "Likeness" (demuth) = resemblance, similarity.

v.27 — KJV: "male and female created he them" / ESV: "male and female he
       created them" — Both equivalent. Hebrew "zakar unqebah" — both
       genders equally bear God's image. Neither is more "image" than the
       other. Minor word order difference only.

v.28 — KJV: "replenish the earth" / ESV: "fill the earth" — Hebrew "mil'u"
       — to fill. ESV's "fill" is more accurate to the Hebrew. KJV's
       "replenish" in 1611 English meant "fill" (not "refill"), but modern
       readers may misunderstand it as implying a previous filling.

v.29 — KJV: "every herb bearing seed... to you it shall be for meat" / ESV:
       "every plant yielding seed... You shall have them for food" — Hebrew
       "oklah" = food. KJV's "meat" in 1611 English meant "food" generally,
       not specifically animal flesh. ESV's "food" avoids confusion.

v.31 — KJV: "very good" / ESV: "very good" — Both identical. Hebrew "tob
       meod" — exceedingly good. The only day rated "very good" — because
       humanity completes creation.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.2 — ASV: "waste and void" — more stark than KJV/ESV's "without form and
  void." The word "waste" emphasizes desolation, capturing the Hebrew "tohu"
  as barren wasteland rather than mere shapelessness.
- v.2 — NET: "without shape and empty...the watery deep" — NET uniquely
  renders "tehom" as "the watery deep" (rather than simply "the deep"),
  emphasizing the aquatic nature of the primordial chaos.
- v.5 — NET: "marking the first day" — adds interpretive clarity that evening
  and morning together "mark" a complete day, making the day-structure
  explicit where KJV/ESV leave it implicit.
- v.21 — ASV: "great sea-monsters" — more vivid than KJV's "great whales" or
  ESV's "great sea creatures." Translates Hebrew "tanninim" with a term that
  captures the mythic resonance of the original.
- v.26 — NET: "Let us make humankind" — uses gender-inclusive "humankind"
  rather than "man" (KJV/ESV/ASV), reflecting the collective meaning of
  Hebrew "adam" in this context (male and female together, v.27).

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Elohim         — The Hebrew name for God used throughout Genesis 1. Plural
               in form, singular in meaning. Emphasizes God's power and
               majesty. Hints at plurality within the Godhead.

Bara           — To create from nothing. Used exclusively of God. Distinct
               from making or forming from existing material.

Tohu wabohu    — "Without form and void." The initial state of creation
               before God shaped and filled it over six days.

Image of God (Imago Dei) — Humanity's unique status as God's representatives.
               Includes rationality, morality, creativity, relationality,
               and dominion.

Dominion       — Humanity's God-given authority to rule over creation as
               God's stewards. Not exploitation but responsible care.

Cultural mandate — God's command to be fruitful, multiply, fill the earth,
               and subdue it. The call to develop creation's potential.

"And God said" — Creation by divine speech. God's word has creative power.
               He speaks and reality responds.

"It was good"  — God's evaluation of His creation. Everything He made was
               good — functioning as intended, reflecting His character.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "In the beginning God" (v.1). Everything starts with God. Is He the
   starting point of your thinking, your decisions, your worldview? Or do
   you start with yourself and fit God in later?

2. "God said... and it was so" (vv.3, 6, 9, etc.). God's word has creative
   power. When He speaks, reality responds. Do you treat His word (Scripture)
   with that kind of authority in your life?

3. "Let us make man in our image" (v.26). You are made in God's image —
   designed to reflect Him. How does this shape your sense of identity,
   purpose, and worth? How does it shape how you treat others?

4. "Be fruitful, and multiply... and have dominion" (v.28). God gave
   humanity a mission: fill the earth and steward it well. How are you
   fulfilling this mandate — in your family, your work, your community?

5. "God saw every thing... and, behold, it was very good" (v.31). Creation
   was originally good — not evil, not meaningless. Sin corrupted it, but
   goodness is the original design. How does this shape your view of the
   physical world, your body, and material creation?

6. The chapter shows God as powerful (He creates by speaking), wise (creation
   is ordered and purposeful), and good (everything is "good"). Which
   attribute of God do you most need to trust today?
