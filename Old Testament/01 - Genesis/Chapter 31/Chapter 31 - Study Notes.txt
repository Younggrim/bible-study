Genesis - Chapter 31
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Jacob secretly flees Laban with his family and flocks; Laban pursues, but God warns him in a dream and they make a covenant of peace.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 31 records Jacob's departure from Laban after 20
years of service. God commands Jacob to return home (v.3), fulfilling the
Bethel promise (28:15). The chapter reveals Laban's exploitation (changing
wages ten times), God's protection of Jacob, Rachel's theft of Laban's
household gods, and the covenant at Mizpah. Jacob's 20-year exile is ending —
he's going home to face Esau and fulfill his destiny.

God's Command to Return (vv.1-3): Laban's sons resent Jacob's prosperity
(v.1). Laban's attitude changes (v.2). God speaks: "Return unto the land of
thy fathers... I will be with thee" (v.3).

Jacob's Case to His Wives (vv.4-16): Jacob recounts Laban's exploitation:
20 years of faithful service, wages changed ten times (vv.6-7, 41). But "God
suffered him not to hurt me" (v.7). God revealed in a dream that HE (not
Jacob's rods) caused the flocks to multiply (vv.10-12). "I am the God of
Bethel" — return home (v.13). Rachel and Leah agree: "our father... hath sold
us" (vv.14-16). "Whatsoever God hath said unto thee, do" (v.16).

The Flight (vv.17-21): Jacob flees secretly (v.20). Rachel steals Laban's
household gods (teraphim) (v.19) — possibly for inheritance rights or
lingering idolatry.

Laban's Pursuit and God's Protection (vv.22-42): Laban pursues for seven days
(v.23). God warns Laban in a dream: "Take heed that thou speak not to Jacob
either good or bad" (v.24). Laban confronts Jacob (vv.26-30). The search for
the gods — Rachel hides them (vv.33-35). Jacob's passionate defense: 20 years
of faithful, sacrificial service (vv.36-42). "Except the God of my father...
had been with me, surely thou hadst sent me away now empty" (v.42).

The Covenant at Mizpah (vv.43-55): They make a covenant of non-aggression
(vv.44-53). "The LORD watch between me and thee, when we are absent one from
another" (v.49). They part in peace (v.55).

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
• Paddan-aram/Haran (v.3) — Jacob leaves Laban's territory in northwest
  Mesopotamia, heading south toward Canaan.
• Mount Gilead (v.21) — the hilly region east of the Jordan River (modern
  Jordan). Laban overtakes Jacob here after a seven-day pursuit.
• Mizpah / Galeed (vv.47-49) — the heap of stones marking the boundary
  between Jacob and Laban. Located in the Gilead region, serving as a
  non-aggression boundary between Aramean and Israelite territories.

------------------------------------

        be with you." (ESV) — God's command and promise.

        exploitation.

        — God remembers His Bethel promise.

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.3  — Hebrew "shuv" — return, go back.
       KJV: "Return unto the land of thy fathers"
       ESV: "Return to the land of your fathers and to your kindred, and I will be with you"
       ASV: "Return unto the land of thy fathers, and to thy kindred"
       NET: "Return to the land of your fathers and to your relatives"
       WEB: "Return to the land of your fathers, and to your relatives"
       Analysis: God fulfilling His Bethel promise (28:15): "I will bring thee
       again into this land." All translations use "return" for Hebrew "shuv."
       ESV explicitly includes "I will be with you" — God's command paired
       with His promise of presence.

v.7  — Hebrew "hechelip...eser monim" — changed...ten times.
       KJV: "your father hath deceived me, and changed my wages ten times; but God suffered him not to hurt me"
       ESV: "your father has cheated me and changed my wages ten times. But God did not permit him to harm me"
       ASV: "your father hath deceived me, and changed my wages ten times"
       NET: "your father has humiliated me and changed my wages ten times"
       WEB: "Your father has deceived me, and changed my wages ten times"
       Analysis: NET's "humiliated" is stronger than "deceived" (KJV/ASV/WEB)
       or "cheated" (ESV), adding personal insult to financial harm. "Ten"
       may be literal or mean "many times." KJV's "suffered" means "allowed"
       in archaic English; ESV modernizes to "did not permit."

v.9  — Hebrew "vayyatsel...vayyitten-li" — snatched away...gave to me.
       KJV: "God hath taken away the cattle of your father, and given them to me"
       ESV: "God has taken away the livestock of your father and given them to me"
       ASV: "God hath taken away the cattle of your father, and given them to me"
       NET: "God has snatched away your father's livestock and given them to me"
       WEB: "God has taken away the livestock of your father, and given them to me"
       Analysis: NET's "snatched away" is more dramatic than "taken away"
       (KJV/ESV/ASV/WEB), emphasizing God's active, forceful redistribution
       of wealth from exploiter to faithful servant.

v.13 — Hebrew "Anokhi ha'El Beit-El" — I am the God of Bethel.
       KJV: "I am the God of Bethel, where thou anointedst the pillar"
       ESV: "I am the God of Bethel, where you anointed a pillar"
       ASV: "I am the God of Bethel, where thou anointedst a pillar"
       NET: "I am the God of Bethel, where you anointed the sacred stone"
       WEB: "I am the God of Bethel, where you anointed a pillar"
       Analysis: God identifies Himself by the place of encounter. NET uses
       "sacred stone" (referencing 28:18) where KJV/ESV/ASV/WEB have
       "pillar," highlighting the religious function of the memorial. He
       remembers the vow Jacob made — and now calls him home.

v.15 — Hebrew "nokriyyoth" — foreigners/strangers.
       KJV: "Are we not counted of him strangers?"
       ESV: "Are we not regarded by him as foreigners?"
       ASV: "Are we not accounted by him as foreigners?"
       NET: "Hasn't he treated us like foreigners? He not only sold us, but completely wasted the money"
       WEB: "Aren't we accounted by him as strangers?"
       Analysis: Rachel and Leah's complaint about their father. NET renders
       their bitterness colloquially and adds interpretive force ("completely
       wasted the money"), making their grievance accessible and vivid.

v.19 — Hebrew "teraphim" — household gods/idols.
       KJV: "Rachel had stolen the images that were her father's"
       ESV: "Rachel stole her father's household gods"
       ASV: "Rachel stole the teraphim that were her father's"
       NET: "Rachel stole the household idols that belonged to her father"
       WEB: "Rachel stole the teraphim that were her father's"
       Analysis: KJV's generic "images" obscures the religious nature of these
       objects. ESV/NET clarify as "household gods/idols." ASV/WEB retain the
       transliteration "teraphim." Small figurines used for divination and
       possibly connected to inheritance rights. Rachel's theft shows
       lingering paganism.

v.42 — Hebrew "pachad Yitschaq" — the Fear of Isaac.
       KJV: "except the God of my father, the God of Abraham, and the fear of Isaac, had been with me"
       ESV: "If the God of my father, the God of Abraham and the Fear of Isaac, had not been on my side"
       ASV: "except the God of my father, the God of Abraham, and the Fear of Isaac, had been with me"
       NET: "If the God of my father—the God of Abraham, the one whom Isaac fears—had not been with me"
       WEB: "Unless the God of my father, the God of Abraham, and the fear of Isaac, had been with me"
       Analysis: "Pachad Yitschaq" is a unique divine title. ESV capitalizes
       "Fear" as a proper divine name. NET interprets it as "the one whom
       Isaac fears" — explaining the title rather than transliterating it.
       God known by His relationship to the patriarchs.

v.47 — Hebrew/Aramaic "Yegar Sahadutha" (Aramaic) / "Gal'ed" (Hebrew) — heap of witness.
       KJV: "Jegar-sahadutha... Galeed"
       ESV: "Jegar-sahadutha... Galeed"
       ASV: "Jegar-sahadutha... Galeed"
       NET: "Jegar Sahadutha... Galeed"
       WEB: "Jegar Sahadutha... Galeed"
       Analysis: Both are identical in meaning — "heap of witness" — but in
       different languages. Laban speaks Aramaic; Jacob speaks Hebrew. Two
       languages, one meaning. The linguistic difference highlights their
       cultural separation after 20 years.

v.49 — Hebrew "Mitspah" — watchtower.
       KJV: "Mizpah; for he said, The LORD watch between me and thee"
       ESV: "Mizpah, for he said, 'The LORD watch between you and me'"
       ASV: "Mizpah, for he said, Jehovah watch between me and thee"
       NET: "Mizpah, for he said, 'May the LORD watch between us'"
       WEB: "Mizpah, for he said, 'May Yahweh watch between me and you'"
       Analysis: Often used sentimentally, but in context it's a warning: God
       is watching to ensure neither party harms the other. It's a covenant of
       mutual non-aggression, not a blessing of friendship. Note divine name
       variations: LORD (KJV/ESV/NET), Jehovah (ASV), Yahweh (WEB).

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Twenty years   — Jacob's total time with Laban: 14 years for wives + 6 years
               for flocks. A long season of exploitation and growth.

"God suffered him not to hurt me" — Divine protection from human exploitation.
               God limits what the wicked can do to His people.

"I am the God of Bethel" — God identifying Himself by the place of promise.
               He remembers His commitments and calls Jacob home.

Teraphim       — Household gods/idols. Rachel's theft shows incomplete
               separation from paganism.

The fear of Isaac — A unique divine title. God known through His relationship
               with the patriarchs.

Mizpah         — "Watchtower." God as witness between parties. He sees and
               holds accountable.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "God suffered him not to hurt me" (v.7). Even when people exploit you, God
   limits what they can do. Have you experienced God's protection in unjust
   situations?

2. "I am the God of Bethel" (v.13). God remembers His promises to you — even
   ones made years ago. What has God promised that you're still waiting for?
   He hasn't forgotten.

3. Jacob served 20 years faithfully despite exploitation (vv.38-40). Are you
   faithful in difficult circumstances — even when you're not treated fairly?
   God sees your labor.

4. "God hath seen mine affliction" (v.42). God sees. He's not distant or
   indifferent. He sees your struggle, your faithfulness, your mistreatment
   — and He will act.

5. Rachel stole the household gods (v.19). Even after years with Jacob, she
   hadn't fully left paganism behind. What "idols" are you still carrying
   from your old life?
