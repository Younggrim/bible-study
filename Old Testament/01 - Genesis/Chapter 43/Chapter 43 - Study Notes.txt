Genesis - Chapter 43
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
The brothers return to Egypt with Benjamin; Joseph hosts them at a feast but continues to conceal his identity.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 43 records the brothers' second trip to Egypt —
this time with Benjamin. The famine forces Jacob's hand: he must send Benjamin
or the family starves. Judah steps forward as surety — a dramatic change from
the man who sold Joseph (37:26-27). The chapter builds tension: Will Joseph
reveal himself? Will the brothers protect Benjamin? Joseph hosts them at a
feast, weeps privately at seeing Benjamin, and tests them further.

Judah's Surety (vv.1-14): The grain runs out (v.1). Jacob: "Go again, buy us
a little food" (v.2). Judah: we cannot go without Benjamin (vv.3-5). Jacob
blames them for mentioning Benjamin (v.6). Judah offers himself as surety:
"I will be surety for him; of my hand shalt thou require him" (v.9). Jacob
relents: "If I be bereaved of my children, I am bereaved" (v.14) — resignation
that is also faith.

The Journey (vv.15-25): They take double money, gifts, and Benjamin (v.15).
Joseph sees Benjamin and orders a feast (v.16). The brothers are afraid —
think it's about the money (vv.18). They explain to the steward about the
returned money (vv.20-22). The steward: "Your God, and the God of your father,
hath given you treasure" (v.23). Simeon released (v.23).

The Feast (vv.26-34): The brothers bow before Joseph — again (v.26). Joseph
asks about their father (v.27). He sees Benjamin: "God be gracious unto thee,
my son" (v.29). Joseph weeps privately — "his bowels did yearn upon his
brother" (v.30). They feast together. Joseph seats them in birth order — they
marvel (v.33). Benjamin receives five times more food (v.34). "They drank,
and were merry with him" (v.34).

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
• The same Canaan ↔ Egypt trade route — the brothers return to Egypt a second
  time, now with Benjamin. The journey takes several days each way.
• Joseph's house in Egypt — the brothers dine there. Joseph is now a high
  Egyptian official with a residence befitting his station.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.9  — "I will be a pledge of his safety. From my hand you shall require
        him. If I do not bring him back to you and set him before you, then
        let me bear the blame forever." (ESV) — Judah's transformation:
        from seller to surety.

v.14 — "If I am bereaved of my children, I am bereaved." (ESV) — Jacob's
        surrender to God's will.

v.23 — "Your God and the God of your father has put treasure in your sacks."
        (ESV) — God's provision acknowledged by a pagan steward.

v.29 — "May God be gracious to you, my son." (ESV) — Joseph's blessing on
        Benjamin.

v.30 — "His compassion grew warm for his brother, and he sought a place to
        weep." (ESV) — Joseph's deep emotion.

Charles Spurgeon:
  Sermon: "Judah's Surety" on v.9 (MTP Vol. 20, No. 1216)
  "I will be SURETY for him — Judah, who once sold his brother for profit,
  now offers himself as guarantee for his brother's safety. This is
  transformation. This is repentance made visible. The man who said 'What
  profit is it if we slay our brother?' (37:26) now says 'Let me bear the
  blame for ever.' From seller to surety — from exploiter to protector. This
  is what grace does. And Judah foreshadows Christ: the one from Judah's
  tribe who became surety for us all."

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.9  — Hebrew "arab" — to pledge, to guarantee.
       KJV: "I will be surety for him"
       ESV: "I will be a pledge of his safety"
       ASV: "I will be surety for him"
       NET: "I myself pledge security for him; you may hold me liable"
       WEB: "I'll be collateral for him"
       Analysis: KJV "surety" vs. ESV "pledge of his safety." NET renders
       Judah's surety pledge in legal language, making the personal risk
       explicit. Judah puts himself on the line for Benjamin. The same
       concept applied to Christ in Hebrews 7:22.

v.11 — Hebrew "zimrath" — song/choice produce/best products.
       KJV: "the best fruits in the land"
       ESV: "some of the choice fruits of the land"
       ASV: "of the choice fruits of the land"
       NET: "Take some of the best products of the land in your bags"
       WEB: "some of the best fruits of the land"
       Analysis: All similar. "Best products/fruits" renders Hebrew "zimrath"
       (song/choice produce). Jacob sends the finest Canaan has to offer as
       a gift.

v.14 — Hebrew "El Shaddai" — God Almighty / the Sovereign God.
       KJV: "God Almighty give you mercy before the man"
       ESV: "May God Almighty grant you mercy before the man"
       ASV: "God Almighty give you mercy before the man"
       NET: "May the Sovereign God grant you mercy before the man"
       WEB: "May God Almighty give you mercy before the man"
       Analysis: NET uses "Sovereign God" for "El Shaddai" (consistent with
       NET Genesis). All others use "God Almighty." Jacob's Hebrew reads
       "If I be bereaved of my children, I am bereaved" — surrender to God's
       sovereignty. Not fatalism but faith: "God's will be done, whatever it
       costs me."

v.23 — Hebrew "Eloheikhem ve'Elohei avikhem natan lakhem matmon" — your God
       and the God of your father has given you treasure.
       KJV: "Your God, and the God of your father, hath given you treasure in your sacks"
       ESV: "Your God and the God of your father has put treasure in your sacks for you"
       ASV: "Your God, and the God of your father, hath given you treasure in your sacks"
       NET: "Your God and the God of your father has given you treasure in your sacks"
       WEB: "Your God, and the God of your father, has given you treasure in your sacks"
       Analysis: All equivalent. Even Joseph's Egyptian steward acknowledges
       God's provision. God's work is visible even to outsiders.

v.30 — Hebrew "rachamav" — his compassions were stirred.
       KJV: "his bowels did yearn upon his brother"
       ESV: "his compassion grew warm for his brother"
       ASV: "his heart yearned over his brother"
       NET: "his deepest emotions were stirred"
       WEB: "his heart yearned over his brother"
       Analysis: ESV modernizes the KJV idiom. NET renders with "deepest
       emotions were stirred" — modern emotional language. Hebrew "rachamav"
       indicates deep, visceral emotion. Joseph loves his brothers intensely.

v.33 — Hebrew "habbekhor kibekhorato vehatsa'ir kitsi'rato" — the firstborn
       according to his birthright and the youngest according to his youth.
       KJV: "the firstborn according to his birthright, and the youngest according to his youth"
       ESV: "the firstborn according to his birthright and the youngest according to his youth"
       ASV: "the firstborn according to his birthright, and the youngest according to his youth"
       NET: "the firstborn according to his right of primogeniture and the youngest according to his age"
       WEB: "the firstborn according to his birthright, and the youngest according to his youth"
       Analysis: All identical in meaning. Joseph seats them in exact birth
       order. The brothers are astonished — how could this Egyptian know?

v.34 — Hebrew "mas'ath" — portion, serving.
       KJV: "Benjamin's mess was five times so much as any of theirs"
       ESV: "Benjamin's portion was five times as much as any of theirs"
       ASV: "Benjamin's mess was five times so much as any of theirs"
       NET: "he gave Benjamin five times the amount of the others"
       WEB: "Benjamin's portion was five times as much as any of theirs"
       Analysis: KJV "mess" (archaic for "portion") vs. ESV/WEB "portion."
       NET renders freely. Joseph favors Benjamin to test the brothers: will
       they be jealous (as they were of Joseph's coat) or accepting?

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Surety         — A guarantor who pledges himself for another's safety. Judah
               becomes surety for Benjamin — foreshadowing Christ as our
               surety.

"If I be bereaved, I am bereaved" — Jacob's surrender to God's will.
               Releasing control and trusting God with the outcome.

Judah's transformation — From the man who sold Joseph (37:26) to the man
               who pledges his life for Benjamin (43:9). Genuine repentance
               produces changed behavior.

Joseph's tears — His deep love for his brothers expressed privately. He
               tests them not from hatred but from love.

Five times more — Joseph's favor toward Benjamin. A test: will the brothers
               resent favoritism (as they did with Joseph) or accept it?

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "I will be surety for him" (v.9). Judah has changed — from seller to
   surety. Has YOUR character changed over the years? Can people see genuine
   transformation in you?

2. "If I be bereaved of my children, I am bereaved" (v.14). Jacob surrenders
   to God's will — whatever the cost. Can you say this? Can you release your
   grip on what you love most and trust God?

3. "Your God... hath given you treasure" (v.23). Even outsiders can see God's
   provision in your life. Is God's blessing visible to those around you?

4. Joseph wept at seeing Benjamin (v.30). Love is powerful and emotional.
   Are you moved by love for your family — even those who've hurt you?

5. Benjamin received five times more (v.34). Joseph tested whether the
   brothers would be jealous. They passed — "they drank, and were merry."
   How do you respond when others receive more than you? With jealousy or
   with joy?
