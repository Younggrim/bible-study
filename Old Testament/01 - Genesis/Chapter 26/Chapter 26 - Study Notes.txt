Genesis - Chapter 26
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Isaac settles in Gerar during famine, repeats his father's deception about his wife, and God blesses him abundantly despite Philistine opposition.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 26 is the only chapter in Genesis devoted
exclusively to Isaac (apart from the binding in ch.22 and the blessing in
ch.27). Isaac is the "quiet patriarch" — less dramatic than Abraham or Jacob.
This chapter shows him repeating both his father's failures (the "sister" lie)
and his father's blessings (God's covenant promises, prosperity, well-digging).
Isaac re-digs Abraham's wells — literally and spiritually reclaiming his
father's legacy.

God's Promise to Isaac (vv.1-5): Famine drives Isaac toward Egypt (v.1). God
appears: "Go not down into Egypt" (v.2). The Abrahamic promises transferred
to Isaac: land, descendants, blessing to all nations (vv.3-4). Reason:
"Abraham obeyed my voice" (v.5).

Isaac's Failure (vv.6-11): Isaac repeats Abraham's "sister" lie about Rebekah
(v.7). Abimelech discovers the truth and rebukes Isaac (vv.8-10). Protects
them with a decree (v.11).

Isaac's Prosperity (vv.12-22): God blesses Isaac abundantly — hundredfold
harvest (v.12). He becomes "very great" (v.13). Philistines envy him and stop
Abraham's wells (vv.14-15). Isaac re-digs them, naming them as Abraham did
(v.18). Conflict over wells: Esek ("contention"), Sitnah ("opposition"),
then Rehoboth ("room") — "the LORD hath made room for us" (vv.20-22).

God's Appearance at Beer-sheba (vv.23-25): God appears: "I am the God of
Abraham... fear not, for I am with thee" (v.24). Isaac builds an altar, calls
on the LORD, pitches his tent, digs a well (v.25) — the pattern of faith.

Covenant with Abimelech (vv.26-33): Abimelech seeks peace: "We saw certainly
that the LORD was with thee" (v.28). They make a covenant (v.31). A well
found that day — Beer-sheba ("well of the oath") (vv.32-33).

Esau's Wives (vv.34-35): Esau marries Hittite women — "a grief of mind unto
Isaac and to Rebekah" (v.35).

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
• Gerar (v.1) — Isaac settles in Philistine territory (southern coastal
  plain) during a famine, just as Abraham did (ch.20).
• Beer-sheba (v.23) — Isaac moves back to this familiar patriarchal site
  in the Negev. God appears to him here and renews the covenant.
• The wells Isaac digs — Esek ("contention"), Sitnah ("enmity"), and
  Rehoboth ("room") — trace his path through the Negev region as he seeks
  peace with the Philistines (vv.20-22).

Charles Spurgeon:
  Sermon: "Re-digging the Wells" on v.18 (MTP Vol. 22, No. 1322)
  "Isaac digged again the wells of his father Abraham — he reclaimed what
  the enemy had stopped up. Every generation must re-dig the wells of faith.
  The truths your fathers discovered, the enemy will try to fill with earth.
  You must dig them again — not new wells but the old ones, the proven ones,
  the ones that produced living water before. Re-dig the wells of prayer, of
  Scripture, of worship, of holiness."

REFLECTION
------------------------------------
1. Isaac re-dug his father's wells (v.18). What spiritual "wells" from
   previous generations need to be re-dug in your life? Prayer? Scripture?
   Worship? Don't let the enemy fill them with earth.

2. Esek (contention) → Sitnah (opposition) → Rehoboth (room). Sometimes you
   must persevere through conflict before God makes room. Are you in an Esek
   or Sitnah season? Rehoboth is coming.

3. "The LORD hath made room for us" (v.22). God makes room. You don't have
   to fight for space — He provides it. Can you trust Him to open the way?

4. "We saw certainly that the LORD was with thee" (v.28). Even enemies
   recognized God's presence with Isaac. Is God's presence with you visible
   to others — even those who oppose you?

5. Isaac repeated his father's sin (v.7). Generational patterns — both good
   and bad — tend to repeat. What patterns are you repeating? Which ones need
   to be broken?
