Genesis - Chapter 8
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
The floodwaters recede, Noah sends out birds to test the land, and finally exits the ark to offer sacrifice to God.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 8 records the flood's recession and Noah's exit
from the ark. After the de-creation of chapter 7, chapter 8 is a re-creation
— God restoring the earth through wind (Spirit), separating waters from land,
and commanding life to fill the earth again. The chapter ends with Noah's
sacrifice and God's promise never to flood the earth again. The flood lasted
approximately one year and ten days total.

God Remembers Noah (vv.1-5): "God remembered Noah" (v.1) — not that He forgot
but that He now acts on Noah's behalf. God sends a wind (Hebrew "ruach" —
same word as "Spirit" in 1:2) over the earth (v.1). Waters recede. The ark
rests on Ararat on the 7th month, 17th day (v.4). Mountains visible by the
10th month (v.5).

The Birds (vv.6-12): After 40 more days, Noah sends a raven — it flies back
and forth (v.7). He sends a dove — it returns (no dry land, v.9). Seven days
later, the dove returns with an olive leaf — waters receding (v.11). Seven
more days — the dove doesn't return — dry land (v.12).

Exiting the Ark (vv.13-19): On the 1st month, 1st day of Noah's 601st year,
the ground is dry (v.13). God commands: "Go forth" (v.16). All creatures exit
to "be fruitful, and multiply" (v.17) — echoing 1:22, 28.

Noah's Sacrifice and God's Promise (vv.20-22): Noah builds an altar — the
first altar in Scripture (v.20). He offers clean animals as burnt offerings
(v.20). God smells the "sweet savour" and promises: never again curse the
ground or destroy all living things (v.21). Seasons will continue as long as
the earth remains (v.22).

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
• The ark rests on the "mountains of Ararat" (v.4) — in the region of modern
  eastern Turkey/Armenia. Ararat is a region, not necessarily the specific
  peak known today as Mount Ararat.
• The waters recede to reveal dry land — a new beginning geographically
  paralleling the original creation emerging from the waters (cf. 1:9).

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "But God remembered Noah." (ESV) — God acts on behalf of His own.

v.11 — "In her mouth was a freshly plucked olive leaf. So Noah knew that the
        waters had subsided from the earth." (ESV) — The sign of hope and
        new life.

v.20 — "Then Noah built an altar to the LORD and... offered burnt offerings."
        (ESV) — Worship as the first response to deliverance.

v.21 — "I will never again curse the ground because of man." (ESV) — God's
        gracious promise despite human sinfulness.

v.22 — "While the earth remains, seedtime and harvest, cold and heat, summer
        and winter, day and night, shall not cease." (ESV) — The stability
        of creation guaranteed.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1  — KJV: "God remembered Noah" / ESV: "God remembered Noah" — Both
       identical. Hebrew "zakar" — to remember, to act on behalf of. Not
       recollection (God doesn't forget) but action. When God "remembers,"
       He intervenes.

v.1  — KJV: "God made a wind to pass over the earth, and the waters
       asswaged" / ESV: "God made a wind blow over the earth, and the waters
       subsided" — Hebrew "ruach" — wind/spirit/breath. Same word as Genesis
       1:2 ("Spirit of God"). The re-creation parallel is intentional. KJV's
       archaic "asswaged" = ESV's "subsided" — both mean the waters receded.

v.4  — KJV: "the ark rested... upon the mountains of Ararat" / ESV: "the
       ark came to rest on the mountains of Ararat" — Both equivalent.
       Hebrew "nuach" — same root as "Noah." Noah rests on the mountain.
       Ararat is a mountain range in modern eastern Turkey (not a single
       peak).

v.9  — KJV: "the dove found no rest for the sole of her foot" / ESV: "the
       dove found no place to set her foot" — Hebrew "manoach" — resting
       place. KJV is more literal ("rest for the sole of her foot"); ESV
       paraphrases for clarity ("no place to set her foot"). Same meaning.

v.11 — KJV: "an olive leaf pluckt off" / ESV: "a freshly plucked olive
       leaf" — Both equivalent. ESV adds "freshly" to clarify the leaf was
       newly plucked (indicating active growth). The olive tree can survive
       flooding and sprout quickly.

v.17 — KJV: "that they may breed abundantly in the earth, and be fruitful,
       and multiply" / ESV: "that they may swarm on the earth, and be
       fruitful and multiply" — Hebrew "sharats" — to swarm, teem. ESV's
       "swarm" is more literal; KJV's "breed abundantly" interprets the
       result.

v.20 — KJV: "burnt offerings" / ESV: "burnt offerings" — Both identical.
       Hebrew "oloth" — whole burnt offerings. Completely consumed by fire —
       total dedication to God. The first altar and first formal sacrifice
       recorded.

v.21 — KJV: "a sweet savour" / ESV: "the pleasing aroma" — Hebrew "reach
       nichoach" — a soothing/restful aroma. ESV's "pleasing aroma" is more
       natural modern English; KJV's "sweet savour" is archaic but evocative.
       God is "satisfied" by the sacrifice — a picture of Christ's sacrifice
       satisfying God (Eph 5:2).

v.21 — KJV: "I will not again curse the ground any more for man's sake" /
       ESV: "I will never again curse the ground because of man" — Both
       equivalent. KJV's "for man's sake" could be misread as "for man's
       benefit"; ESV's "because of man" is clearer.

v.21 — KJV: "the imagination of man's heart is evil from his youth" / ESV:
       "the intention of man's heart is evil from his youth" — Hebrew
       "yetser" — imagination, inclination, intention. Same word as 6:5.
       The same diagnosis as before, but now the basis for mercy rather than
       judgment. God knows our nature and chooses grace.

v.22 — KJV: "While the earth remaineth, seedtime and harvest, and cold and
       heat, and summer and winter, and day and night shall not cease" / ESV:
       "While the earth remains, seedtime and harvest, cold and heat, summer
       and winter, day and night, shall not cease" — Both essentially
       identical. God's guarantee of creation's stability.

TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.4 — NET: "the ark came to rest on one of the mountains of Ararat" —
  adds "one of" to clarify the Hebrew plural "mountains of Ararat" refers to
  a range, not a single peak. Other translations leave this ambiguous.
- v.21 — NET: "the inclination of their minds is evil from childhood on" —
  renders Hebrew "yetser" as "inclination of their minds" (vs. "imagination
  of man's heart" KJV) and "from his youth" as "from childhood on," making
  the doctrine of innate depravity more psychologically explicit.
- v.21 — NET: "said to himself" — renders Hebrew "said in his heart" as
  "said to himself," a more natural English idiom for internal resolve.
  ASV/WEB retain the more literal "said in his heart."
- v.22 — NET: "While the earth continues to exist" — a more dynamic rendering
  than "While the earth remaineth" (KJV/ASV), subtly acknowledging the earth
  has a future end point.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
God remembered — Divine action on behalf of His people. Not recollection
               but intervention. When God remembers, He moves.

Wind/Ruach     — The same word as "Spirit." God uses wind to recede the
               waters — paralleling the Spirit hovering over waters in 1:2.

Ararat         — The mountain range where the ark rested. In modern Turkey.
               "Rested" (nuach) connects to Noah's name.

Olive leaf     — The sign of new life and receding waters. Later associated
               with peace and hope.

Burnt offering — A sacrifice completely consumed by fire. Total dedication
               to God. Noah's first act after deliverance: worship.

Sweet savour   — God's satisfaction with the sacrifice. A picture of how
               Christ's sacrifice satisfies God's justice.

Seedtime and harvest — God's guarantee of creation's stability. The seasons
               will continue until the end.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "God remembered Noah" (v.1). In your waiting — when it feels like God has
   forgotten — He hasn't. He remembers. And when He remembers, He acts. Can
   you trust His timing?

2. Noah's first act after deliverance was worship (v.20). What is YOUR first
   response to God's deliverance — gratitude and worship, or moving on to the
   next thing?

3. "The imagination of man's heart is evil from his youth" (v.21). God knows
   our sinful nature — and STILL chooses grace. His mercy is not based on our
   goodness but on His character. Does this humble you?

4. "While the earth remaineth, seedtime and harvest... shall not cease" (v.22).
   God guarantees creation's stability. Every sunrise, every season, every
   harvest is God keeping His promise. Do you notice His faithfulness in the
   ordinary rhythms of life?

5. The flood is both judgment and salvation — destruction of the wicked and
   preservation of the righteous. God's actions always have both dimensions.
   Are you on the salvation side?
