Genesis - Chapter 21
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Isaac is born to Abraham and Sarah in their old age, fulfilling God's promise; Hagar and Ishmael are sent away but God provides for them.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 21 records the long-awaited birth of Isaac — the
fulfillment of God's 25-year-old promise. Abraham was 75 when called (12:4);
he's now 100 (v.5). The chapter also records the expulsion of Hagar and
Ishmael (painful but necessary for the covenant line) and Abraham's covenant
with Abimelech at Beer-sheba. The birth of Isaac demonstrates God's
faithfulness to His word — He does what He promises, in His timing.

Isaac's Birth (vv.1-7): "The LORD visited Sarah as he had said" (v.1). Sarah
conceives and bears a son "at the set time" (v.2). Named Isaac ("laughter")
(v.3). Circumcised on the eighth day (v.4). Abraham is 100 (v.5). Sarah's
joy: "God hath made me to laugh" (v.6). The laughter of disbelief (17:17,
18:12) becomes the laughter of joy.

Ishmael and Hagar Sent Away (vv.8-21): At Isaac's weaning feast, Ishmael
mocks (v.9). Sarah demands: "Cast out this bondwoman and her son" (v.10).
Abraham is grieved (v.11). God confirms: "In Isaac shall thy seed be called"
(v.12). Ishmael will also become a nation (v.13). Abraham sends Hagar and
Ishmael away (v.14). In the wilderness, water runs out (v.15). Hagar weeps
(v.16). God hears the boy, opens Hagar's eyes to a well (vv.17-19). God is
with Ishmael; he grows as an archer in Paran (vv.20-21).

The Covenant at Beer-sheba (vv.22-34): Abimelech recognizes "God is with thee"
(v.22). They make a covenant (v.27). Abraham names the place Beer-sheba
("well of the oath") (v.31). Abraham plants a tamarisk tree and worships "the
everlasting God" (El Olam) (v.33).

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
• Beer-sheba (v.14) — meaning "well of the oath" (or "well of seven"),
  located in the southern Negev. This becomes a key site for the patriarchs
  marking the southern boundary of the Promised Land.
• The wilderness of Beer-sheba (v.14) — where Hagar wanders after expulsion.
  Arid desert terrain in the northern Negev.
• Abraham plants a tamarisk tree at Beer-sheba (v.33) — establishing a
  permanent settlement and worship site in the south.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "The LORD visited Sarah as he had said, and the LORD did to Sarah as
        he had promised." (ESV) — God keeps His word.

v.2  — "At the time of which God had spoken to him." (ESV) — God's perfect
        timing.

v.6  — "God has made laughter for me; everyone who hears will laugh over me."
        (ESV) — Joy replacing doubt.

v.12 — "Through Isaac shall your offspring be named." (ESV) — The covenant
        line specified.

v.17 — "God heard the voice of the boy." (ESV) — God hears the outcast.

v.33 — "Abraham... called there on the name of the LORD, the Everlasting
        God." (ESV) — El Olam: the eternal God.

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1  — Hebrew "paqad" — to visit, to attend to, to act on behalf of.
       KJV: "the LORD visited Sarah as he had said"
       ESV: "The LORD visited Sarah as he had said"
       ASV: "Jehovah visited Sarah as he had said"
       NET: "The Lord visited Sarah just as he had said he would"
       WEB: "Yahweh visited Sarah as he had said"
       Analysis: All translations preserve the significant verb "visited" (paqad) —
       when God "visits," He intervenes for blessing or judgment. NET adds "just as"
       and "he would" for emphatic clarity about the promise being fulfilled exactly.
       Note the divine name variations: KJV/ESV use "LORD," ASV uses "Jehovah,"
       WEB uses "Yahweh."

v.2  — Hebrew "lamoed" — at the appointed time.
       KJV: "at the set time of which God had spoken to him"
       ESV: "at the time of which God had spoken to him"
       ASV: "at the set time of which God had spoken to him"
       NET: "at the appointed time that God had told him"
       WEB: "at the set time of which God had spoken to him"
       Analysis: KJV/ASV/WEB retain "set time"; ESV simplifies to "the time"; NET
       uses "appointed time." All convey that God's promises have schedules — He is
       never early, never late. The promise given at age 75 was fulfilled at 100.

v.6  — Hebrew "tsechoq" — laughter (same root as "Isaac" / Yitschaq).
       KJV: "God hath made me to laugh"
       ESV: "God has made laughter for me"
       ASV: "God hath made me to laugh"
       NET: "God has made me laugh"
       WEB: "God has made me laugh"
       Analysis: KJV/ASV/NET/WEB focus on the personal action ("made me laugh");
       ESV uniquely renders it as God creating laughter as a gift ("made laughter
       for me"). The same root as Isaac's name — what was once the laughter of
       doubt (17:17, 18:12) is now the laughter of fulfilled promise.

v.9  — Hebrew "metsacheq" — laughing, mocking, playing (same root as Isaac's name).
       KJV: "mocking"
       ESV: "laughing"
       ASV: "mocking"
       NET: "mocking"
       WEB: "mocking"
       Analysis: ESV alone stays closer to the neutral Hebrew root ("laughing"),
       while KJV/ASV/NET/WEB all interpret the tone as hostile "mocking." Ishmael
       was "Isaac-ing" — mocking the true heir. Paul interprets this as persecution
       (Gal 4:29). The context supports hostile intent.

v.10 — Hebrew "garesh" — to drive out, cast out, banish.
       KJV: "Cast out this bondwoman and her son"
       ESV: "Cast out this slave woman with her son"
       ASV: "Cast out this handmaid and her son"
       NET: "Banish that slave woman and her son"
       WEB: "Cast out this servant and her son"
       Analysis: NET's "banish" is the strongest rendering, emphasizing the
       permanent expulsion Sarah demanded. KJV/ESV use "cast out"; ASV uses
       "handmaid"; WEB uses "servant." All convey the harshness of the demand.

v.12 — Hebrew "ki beYitschaq yiqqare leka zara" — in Isaac shall seed be called to you.
       KJV: "in Isaac shall thy seed be called"
       ESV: "through Isaac shall your offspring be named"
       ASV: "in Isaac shall thy seed be called"
       NET: "through Isaac your descendants will be counted"
       WEB: "in Isaac will your offspring be called"
       Analysis: ESV uses "offspring" and "named"; NET uses "descendants" and
       "counted." KJV/ASV/WEB retain "seed" and "called." The covenant line runs
       through Isaac, not Ishmael. God's choice, not human arrangement.

v.14 — Hebrew "wattelekh watteta" — she went and wandered.
       KJV: "and wandered in the wilderness"
       ESV: "and wandered in the wilderness"
       ASV: "and wandered in the wilderness"
       NET: "So she went wandering aimlessly through the wilderness"
       WEB: "and wandered in the wilderness"
       Analysis: NET adds "aimlessly" to intensify Hagar's desperation — not present
       in the Hebrew text but implied by the narrative context. The addition captures
       the emotional devastation of expulsion into the desert with a child.

v.17 — Hebrew "wayyishma Elohim eth-qol hanna'ar" — God heard the voice of the boy.
       KJV: "God heard the voice of the lad"
       ESV: "God heard the voice of the boy"
       ASV: "God heard the voice of the lad"
       NET: "God heard the boy's voice right where he is crying"
       WEB: "God heard the voice of the boy"
       Analysis: NET adds "right where he is crying" to emphasize God's precise,
       located attention to suffering — not in the Hebrew but drawn from the
       theological significance. The name "Ishmael" means "God hears" (16:11),
       and here God lives up to that name.

v.33 — Hebrew "El Olam" — the Everlasting God / God of eternity.
       KJV: "the everlasting God"
       ESV: "the Everlasting God"
       ASV: "the Everlasting God"
       NET: "the Eternal God"
       WEB: "the Everlasting God"
       Analysis: NET uses "Eternal" while all others use "Everlasting" — both convey
       the Hebrew El Olam. A new divine name revealing God who transcends time.
       Abraham plants a tamarisk tree (a long-lived tree) and worships the God who
       outlasts all creation.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
"As He had spoken" — God's faithfulness to His exact word. What He promises,
               He performs — precisely, on schedule.

"At the set time" — God's appointed timing. Promises have schedules that
               cannot be rushed or delayed.

Isaac = Laughter — The child of promise named for the laughter of both
               parents. Doubt transformed into joy.

"In Isaac shall thy seed be called" — The covenant line specified. Not
               Ishmael (human effort) but Isaac (divine promise).

El Olam        — "The Everlasting God." God who transcends time — eternal,
               unchanging, faithful across all generations.

Beer-sheba     — "Well of the oath." A place of covenant, provision, and
               worship. Abraham's base for many years.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "The LORD did unto Sarah as he had spoken" (v.1). God keeps His promises
   — exactly, on time. What promise are you waiting for? He hasn't forgotten.
   The "set time" is coming.

2. "God hath made me to laugh" (v.6). Sarah's doubt became joy. What doubts
   do you have that God might turn into laughter? Trust Him with your
   impossibilities.

3. The expulsion of Ishmael (vv.10-14) was painful but necessary. Sometimes
   God requires us to release what we produced through human effort so His
   promise can flourish. What "Ishmael" do you need to release?

4. "God heard the voice of the lad" (v.17). Even the outcast, even the
   product of human failure — God hears and provides. No one is beyond His
   care.

5. "The everlasting God" (v.33). El Olam — the God who outlasts everything.
   Whatever you're facing is temporary; He is eternal. Can you rest in His
   everlasting nature?
