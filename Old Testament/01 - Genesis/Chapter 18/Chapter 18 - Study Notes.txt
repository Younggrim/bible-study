Genesis - Chapter 18
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Three visitors announce Isaac's birth to Abraham and Sarah; Abraham intercedes for Sodom, bargaining with God for the righteous.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 18 contains two remarkable scenes: the LORD's
visit to Abraham at Mamre (vv.1-15) and Abraham's intercession for Sodom
(vv.16-33). Three visitors appear — one is the LORD Himself (a theophany),
and two are angels (who go on to Sodom in ch.19). The chapter reveals God's
intimate friendship with Abraham, confirms Isaac's birth within a year, and
shows Abraham as an intercessor — boldly yet humbly pleading for the
righteous in Sodom.

The Three Visitors (vv.1-8): The LORD appears at Mamre (v.1). Abraham sees
three men and runs to serve them (vv.2-3). Lavish hospitality: water, rest,
bread, butter, milk, a calf (vv.4-8). Abraham stands by while they eat (v.8).
This is the passage referenced in Hebrews 13:2 — "entertained angels unawares."

The Promise of Isaac (vv.9-15): "Where is Sarah?" (v.9). "I will certainly
return... and Sarah shall have a son" (v.10). Sarah laughs within herself
(v.12). The LORD's response: "Is any thing too hard for the LORD?" (v.14).
Sarah denies laughing; the LORD corrects her (v.15).

Abraham's Intercession (vv.16-33): God reveals His plan to judge Sodom (vv.17-
21). "Shall I hide from Abraham?" (v.17) — God treats Abraham as a friend.
Abraham intercedes: "Wilt thou also destroy the righteous with the wicked?"
(v.23). He negotiates from 50 down to 10 righteous (vv.24-32). "Shall not
the Judge of all the earth do right?" (v.25). God agrees to spare the city
for 10 righteous — but there aren't even 10.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
• Mamre/Hebron (v.1) — Abraham's home base in the southern hill country.
  The oaks (or terebinths) of Mamre were a well-known landmark.
• Sodom (v.16) — visible from the heights near Hebron, looking east toward
  the Dead Sea plain. Abraham intercedes for the city from this vantage point.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.14 — "Is anything too hard for the LORD?" (ESV) — The rhetorical question
        that answers all doubt.

v.17 — "Shall I hide from Abraham what I am about to do?" (ESV) — God's
        friendship and transparency with Abraham.

v.19 — "For I have chosen him, that he may command his children and his
        household after him." (ESV) — God's confidence in Abraham's
        faithfulness.

v.23 — "Will you indeed sweep away the righteous with the wicked?" (ESV) —
        Abraham's bold intercession.

v.25 — "Shall not the Judge of all the earth do what is just?" (ESV) — The
        foundation of trust: God is just.

  "Is ANYTHING too hard? Not some things — anything. Too hard for the LORD?
  Not for man, not for nature, not for circumstances — for the LORD. The God
  who made the universe from nothing, who raises the dead, who calls things
  that are not as though they were — is ANYTHING too hard for Him? Sarah
  laughed at the impossibility. God laughed at her laughter. And Isaac
  ('laughter') was born. Your impossibility is God's opportunity."
  Sermon: "Abraham's Intercession" on vv.23-32 (MTP Vol. 11, No. 640)
  "Abraham drew near — boldly, yet humbly. 'I am but dust and ashes' (v.27)

  model of intercession: bold access, humble spirit, persistent pleading,
  and confidence in God's justice. 'Shall not the Judge of all the earth do
  right?' — this is the foundation: God IS just. We can trust His decisions."

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1  — Hebrew "vayyera elav Yahweh... kechom hayyom"
       KJV: "the LORD appeared unto him... in the heat of the day"
       ESV: "the LORD appeared to him... in the heat of the day"
       ASV: "Jehovah appeared unto him... in the heat of the day"
       NET: "the LORD appeared to Abraham... during the hottest time of the day"
       WEB: "Yahweh appeared to him... in the heat of the day"
       Analysis: One of the three visitors IS Yahweh (a theophany — pre-
       incarnate Christ). The other two are angels (19:1). NET adds temporal
       emphasis ("hottest time"). ASV uses "Jehovah"; WEB uses "Yahweh."

v.11 — Hebrew "chadal lihyoth leSarah orach kannashim"
       KJV: "it ceased to be with Sarah after the manner of women"
       ESV: "the way of women had ceased to be with Sarah"
       ASV: "it had ceased to be with Sarah after the manner of women"
       NET: "Sarah had long since passed menopause"
       WEB: "it had ceased to be with Sarah after the manner of women"
       Analysis: NET uses strikingly clinical modern language — "passed
       menopause" — where all others use the euphemistic Hebrew idiom.
       The impossibility of the promise is emphasized: humanly, conception
       is impossible.

v.12 — Hebrew "vaттitschaq Sarah beqirbah" — Sarah laughed within herself.
       KJV: "Therefore Sarah laughed within herself"
       ESV: "So Sarah laughed to herself"
       ASV: "And Sarah laughed within herself"
       NET: "So Sarah laughed to herself"
       WEB: "Sarah laughed within herself"
       Analysis: All essentially identical. Hebrew "titsachaq" — same root
       as "Isaac" (yitschaq). Both parents laugh — and the child is named
       for it. NET renders the follow-up: "After I am worn out will I have
       pleasure" — more natural than KJV's archaic phrasing.

v.14 — Hebrew "hayippale meYahweh davar" — Is anything too wonderful/hard for the LORD?
       KJV: "Is any thing too hard for the LORD?"
       ESV: "Is anything too hard for the LORD?"
       ASV: "Is anything too hard for Jehovah?"
       NET: "Is anything impossible for the LORD?"
       WEB: "Is anything too hard for Yahweh?"
       Analysis: Hebrew "pala" = to be marvelous, extraordinary, beyond
       human ability. NET replaces "too hard" with "impossible" — a
       stronger absolute. WEB uses "Yahweh" — making the rhetorical question
       more intimate and personal. ASV uses "Jehovah."

v.19 — Hebrew "ki yeda'tiv" — for I have known him / chosen him.
       KJV: "For I know him, that he will command his children"
       ESV: "For I have chosen him, that he may command his children"
       ASV: "For I have known him, to the end that he may command his children"
       NET: "I have chosen him so that he may command his children"
       WEB: "For I have known him, to the end that he may command his children"
       Analysis: ESV/NET render "yeda'tiv" as "chosen" — making the election
       aspect of Hebrew "know" explicit. KJV/ASV/WEB retain "known" — the
       relational/intimate sense. In Hebrew, "to know" (yada) in a
       covenantal context means to choose and enter relationship with.

v.25 — Hebrew "hashophet kol-ha'arets lo ya'aseh mishpat"
       KJV: "Shall not the Judge of all the earth do right?"
       ESV: "Shall not the Judge of all the earth do what is just?"
       ASV: "Shall not the Judge of all the earth do right?"
       NET: "Won't the judge of the whole earth do what is right?"
       WEB: "Won't the Judge of all the earth do right?"
       Analysis: All essentially identical. NET/WEB use "Won't" — more
       conversational than "Shall not." Abraham appeals to God's character
       as universal Judge. The foundation of trust: God IS just.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Theophany      — A visible appearance of God. Here, the LORD appears as one
               of three men visiting Abraham. A pre-incarnate appearance of
               Christ.

"Is anything too hard for the LORD?" — The rhetorical question that
               demolishes all doubt. Nothing is beyond God's ability.

God's friendship with Abraham — God reveals His plans, shares His purposes,
               and invites Abraham's participation. Intimacy, not distance.

Intercession   — Standing before God on behalf of others. Abraham's model:
               bold access, humble spirit, persistent pleading.

"Shall not the Judge do right?" — The foundation of trust in God's justice.
               Whatever God decides is right — because He IS right.

Dust and ashes — Abraham's humble self-assessment while interceding. Bold
               access combined with genuine humility.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Is any thing too hard for the LORD?" (v.14). What "impossible" situation
   are you facing? Is it too hard for God? Sarah laughed at impossibility —
   and God delivered. What are you laughing at that God intends to do?

2. "Shall I hide from Abraham?" (v.17). God shares His plans with His friends.
   Are you close enough to God to hear His heart? Do you seek His purposes,
   not just His blessings?

3. "Shall not the Judge of all the earth do right?" (v.25). When you don't
   understand God's actions, can you trust His character? He is just. He does
   right. Always.

4. Abraham interceded for Sodom — a wicked city. Are you interceding for your
   city, your nation, the lost? Who needs your prayers today?

5. Abraham's hospitality (vv.1-8) led to divine encounter. Are you generous
   with your home, your time, your resources? You never know who you might be
   hosting.
