Genesis - Chapter 33
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Jacob and Esau reconcile with an emotional reunion; Jacob settles in Canaan near Shechem.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 33 records the long-feared reunion of Jacob and
Esau — and it goes far better than expected. After 20 years of separation,
Esau runs to embrace his brother. The reconciliation is genuine and gracious.
Jacob, now Israel (transformed by the Peniel encounter), approaches with
humility. The chapter ends with Jacob settling near Shechem and building an
altar — "El-elohe-Israel" (God, the God of Israel).

The Reunion (vv.1-4): Jacob sees Esau with 400 men (v.1). He arranges his
family — handmaids first, Leah next, Rachel and Joseph last (vv.1-2). Jacob
goes ahead, bowing seven times (v.3). "Esau ran to meet him, and embraced
him, and fell on his neck, and kissed him: and they wept" (v.4). Grace where
judgment was expected.

The Introduction (vv.5-7): Esau meets Jacob's family. Jacob: "The children
which God hath graciously given thy servant" (v.5). Each group bows before
Esau.

The Gift Accepted (vv.8-11): Esau: "I have enough" (v.9). Jacob insists:
"I have seen thy face, as though I had seen the face of God" (v.10). "God
hath dealt graciously with me, and... I have enough" (v.11). Both brothers
say "I have enough" — contentment on both sides.

The Parting (vv.12-17): Esau offers to travel together (v.12). Jacob declines
— needs to go slowly for children and flocks (vv.13-14). They part peacefully.
Jacob goes to Succoth, then Shechem (vv.17-18).

Jacob at Shechem (vv.18-20): Jacob buys land near Shechem (v.19). Builds an
altar: "El-elohe-Israel" — "God, the God of Israel" (v.20). For the first
time, Jacob claims God as HIS God — not just the God of Abraham and Isaac.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
• Succoth (v.17) — meaning "booths," east of the Jordan. Jacob builds a
  house and shelters for livestock here — his first stop in the land.
• Shechem (v.18) — in the central hill country of Canaan, between Mount Ebal
  and Mount Gerizim. Jacob buys a parcel of land and builds an altar called
  "El-elohe-Israel" (God, the God of Israel).
• Jacob's journey: Peniel → Succoth → Shechem traces a path from the
  Transjordan across the Jordan and into the heart of Canaan.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.4  — "But Esau ran to meet him and embraced him and fell on his neck and
        kissed him, and they wept." (ESV) — Reconciliation beyond
        expectation.

v.5  — "The children whom God has graciously given your servant." (ESV) —
        Jacob attributes everything to God's grace.

v.10 — "For I have seen your face, which is like seeing the face of God, and
        you have accepted me." (ESV) — Esau's grace reflecting God's grace.

v.11 — "God has dealt graciously with me, and... I have enough." (ESV) —
        Jacob's contentment rooted in God's grace.

v.20 — "He erected there an altar and called it El-Elohe-Israel." (ESV) —
        "God, the God of Israel" — personal ownership of faith.

Charles Spurgeon:
  Sermon: "Esau's Embrace" on v.4 (MTP Vol. 14, No. 841)
  "Esau RAN — not walked, not approached cautiously — ran. And embraced him
  — not struck him, not accused him — embraced. And kissed him — not cursed
  him. And they wept — together. This is grace beyond expectation. Jacob
  expected judgment and received mercy. How often God's grace exceeds our
  fears! We expect wrath and receive embrace. We expect punishment and
  receive kiss. This is the gospel: where we deserve judgment, we receive
  love."

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.3  — Hebrew "vayyishtachu artsah sheva pe'amim" — bowed to the ground seven times.
       KJV: "bowed himself to the ground seven times, until he came near to his brother"
       ESV: "bowing himself to the ground seven times, until he came near to his brother"
       ASV: "bowed himself to the ground seven times, until he came near to his brother"
       NET: "bowed toward the ground seven times as he approached his brother"
       WEB: "bowed himself to the ground seven times, until he came near to his brother"
       Analysis: Complete submission. Seven = completeness. Jacob approaches
       as a servant, not a rival. All translations are equivalent; NET
       modernizes with "approached" while others retain the processional
       "until he came near."

v.4  — Hebrew "vayyarots...vayyechabbeqehu...vayyishaqehu" — ran...embraced...kissed.
       KJV: "Esau ran to meet him, and embraced him, and fell on his neck, and kissed him: and they wept"
       ESV: "Esau ran to meet him and embraced him and fell on his neck and kissed him, and they wept"
       ASV: "Esau ran to meet him, and embraced him, and fell on his neck, and kissed him: and they wept"
       NET: "Esau ran to meet him, embraced him, hugged his neck, and kissed him, and they wept"
       WEB: "Esau ran to meet him, embraced him, fell on his neck, kissed him, and they wept"
       Analysis: The same verb sequence used of the father in Luke 15:20.
       Jesus may have had this scene in mind when telling the prodigal son
       parable. NET expands "fell on his neck" to "hugged his neck" for
       modern clarity. Grace where judgment was expected.

v.10 — Hebrew "ki al-ken ra'ithi paneycha kir'oth peney Elohim" — for seeing your face is like seeing God's face.
       KJV: "for therefore I have seen thy face, as though I had seen the face of God"
       ESV: "for I have seen your face, which is like seeing the face of God"
       ASV: "forasmuch as I have seen thy face, as one seeth the face of God"
       NET: "for to see your face is like seeing the face of God"
       WEB: "for I have seen your face, like one sees the face of God"
       Analysis: Jacob just saw God's face at Peniel (32:30). Now he sees
       God's grace reflected in Esau's face. Reconciliation with man mirrors
       reconciliation with God. NET's "it is as if" makes the comparison
       accessible. All translations preserve this profound theological
       connection.

v.11 — Hebrew "yesh li kol" — I have everything / I have enough.
       KJV: "God hath dealt graciously with me, and because I have enough"
       ESV: "God has dealt graciously with me, and because I have enough"
       ASV: "God hath dealt graciously with me, and because I have enough"
       NET: "God has been generous to me and I have all I need"
       WEB: "God has dealt graciously with me, and because I have enough"
       Analysis: Hebrew "yesh li kol" = "I have everything." Jacob the
       grasper now says "I have enough." Transformation. NET renders
       "channani" as "been generous" and "yesh li kol" as "I have all I
       need" — more idiomatic than "dealt graciously." Both brothers say "I
       have enough" (v.9, v.11) — contentment on both sides.

v.15 — Hebrew "emtsa-chen be'einey adoni" — let me find grace in the eyes of my lord.
       KJV: "Let me find grace in the sight of my lord"
       ESV: "Let me find favor in the sight of my lord"
       ASV: "Let me find favor in the sight of my lord"
       NET: "My lord has already been kind enough to me"
       WEB: "Let me find favor in the sight of my lord"
       Analysis: NET renders Jacob's formal deference in a culturally
       sensitive, modern way — "kind enough to me" — while KJV/ESV/ASV/WEB
       retain the literal "find favor/grace in the sight of." NET interprets
       the social dynamics rather than translating word-for-word.

v.17 — Hebrew "Sukkoth" — booths/shelters.
       KJV: "Jacob journeyed to Succoth, and built him an house, and made booths for his cattle"
       ESV: "Jacob journeyed to Succoth, and built himself a house and made booths for his livestock"
       ASV: "Jacob journeyed to Succoth, and built him a house, and made booths for his cattle"
       NET: "Jacob traveled to Succoth where he built himself a house and made shelters for his livestock"
       WEB: "Jacob traveled to Succoth, built himself a house, and made shelters for his livestock"
       Analysis: Hebrew "Sukkoth" — booths/tabernacles. Jacob builds
       temporary structures — still a sojourner. NET/WEB use "shelters" for
       the livestock structures; KJV/ESV/ASV retain "booths."

v.20 — Hebrew "El-elohe-Yisra'el" — God, the God of Israel.
       KJV: "and he erected there an altar, and called it El-elohe-Israel"
       ESV: "he erected there an altar and called it El-Elohe-Israel"
       ASV: "and he erected there an altar, and called it El-elohe-Israel"
       NET: "There he set up an altar and called it 'El-Elohe-Israel'"
       WEB: "He erected an altar there, and called it El Elohe Israel"
       Analysis: "God, the God of Israel." Jacob uses his NEW name (Israel)
       and claims God as HIS God. The Bethel vow (28:21) is fulfilled: "the
       LORD shall be my God." For the first time, Jacob's faith is personal
       and owned — not inherited. Minor formatting differences (hyphenation)
       across translations.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Esau ran and embraced — Grace beyond expectation. Where judgment was feared,
               mercy was given. A picture of God's grace to sinners.

"I have enough" — Jacob's transformation from grasper to content man. The
               schemer who always wanted more now says "I have all I need."

El-elohe-Israel — "God, the God of Israel." Jacob's personal claim on God.
               No longer just his fathers' God but HIS God.

Reconciliation — The restoration of a broken relationship. Made possible by
               humility (Jacob) and grace (Esau).

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Esau ran to meet him, and embraced him" (v.4). Jacob expected the worst
   and received the best. Where are you expecting judgment that God might
   surprise you with grace?

2. "God hath dealt graciously with me, and I have enough" (v.11). Can you say
   "I have enough"? Or are you still grasping for more? Contentment comes
   from recognizing God's grace.

3. "El-elohe-Israel" (v.20). Jacob finally claims God as HIS God — not just
   his grandfather's or father's. Is your faith personal and owned, or
   inherited and secondhand?

4. The reconciliation required both humility (Jacob bowing) and grace (Esau
   running). Is there a relationship in your life that needs both? Are you
   willing to humble yourself? Are you willing to extend grace?

5. Jacob saw God's face in Esau's grace (v.10). Sometimes God's grace comes
   through unexpected people. Where might God be showing you grace through
   someone you didn't expect?
