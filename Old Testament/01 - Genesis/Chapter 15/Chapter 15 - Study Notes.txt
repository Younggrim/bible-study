Genesis - Chapter 15
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
God cuts a covenant with Abram, promising innumerable descendants and the land, and Abram believes — counted to him as righteousness.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses (see Chapter 1 notes for full authorship details).

Historical Context: Chapter 15 is one of the most theologically significant
chapters in the OT. It contains: (1) God's self-revelation as Abram's shield
and reward (v.1), (2) the promise of a biological heir (v.4), (3) Abram's
faith counted as righteousness (v.6) — the foundational text for justification
by faith, and (4) the covenant ceremony where God alone passes between the
pieces (vv.9-21) — making the covenant unconditional. This chapter is quoted
extensively by Paul in Romans 4 and Galatians 3.

God's Promise and Abram's Faith (vv.1-6): "Fear not, Abram: I am thy shield,
and thy exceeding great reward" (v.1). Abram's concern: he's still childless
(vv.2-3). God promises a biological heir (v.4). "Look now toward heaven, and
tell the stars... so shall thy seed be" (v.5). "And he believed in the LORD;
and he counted it to him for righteousness" (v.6) — THE verse on justification
by faith.

The Covenant Ceremony (vv.7-21): Abram asks: "Whereby shall I know?" (v.8).
God commands a sacrifice: animals cut in half, pieces arranged (vv.9-10). A
deep sleep and "horror of great darkness" falls on Abram (v.12). God reveals
the future: 400 years of slavery, then deliverance (vv.13-14). A smoking
furnace and burning lamp pass between the pieces (v.17) — GOD ALONE walks
through, making this a unilateral, unconditional covenant. The land promise
defined: from the river of Egypt to the Euphrates (v.18).

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
• "From the river of Egypt to the great river, the river Euphrates" (v.18) —
  the boundaries of the Promised Land defined. The "river of Egypt" is likely
  the Wadi el-Arish (not the Nile), marking the southwestern border.
• The Euphrates marks the northeastern boundary — an enormous territory that
  Israel only briefly controlled under Solomon (1 Kings 4:21).
• Ten nations listed (vv.19-21): Kenites, Kenizzites, Kadmonites, Hittites,
  Perizzites, Rephaims, Amorites, Canaanites, Girgashites, Jebusites — all
  occupying the land between these boundaries.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "Fear not, Abram, I am your shield; your reward shall be very great."
        (ESV) — God Himself as protection and reward.

v.5  — "Look toward heaven, and number the stars... So shall your offspring
        be." (ESV) — The star promise.

v.6  — "And he believed the LORD, and he counted it to him as righteousness."
        (ESV) — Justification by faith. The most important verse in Genesis.

v.13 — "Know for certain that your offspring will be sojourners in a land
        that is not theirs... for four hundred years." (ESV) — Prophecy of
        Egyptian slavery.

v.17 — "A smoking fire pot and a flaming torch passed between these pieces."
        (ESV) — God alone ratifies the covenant.

v.18 — "To your offspring I give this land, from the river of Egypt to the
        great river, the river Euphrates." (ESV) — The land boundaries
        defined.

Charles Spurgeon:
  Sermon: "Abram's Faith Counted for Righteousness" on v.6 (MTP Vol. 14)
  "He BELIEVED — not worked, not earned, not achieved — believed. In the LORD
  — not in himself, not in circumstances, not in probability — in the LORD.
  And HE (God) counted IT (faith) to HIM (Abram) for RIGHTEOUSNESS. This is
  the gospel before the gospel: righteousness credited to the account of one
  who simply believes God's promise. Not righteousness earned but
  righteousness received. Not works rewarded but faith credited. This is how
  every sinner is justified — by believing God."
  Sermon: "The Smoking Furnace and Burning Lamp" on v.17 (MTP Vol. 9, No. 528)
  "God ALONE passed between the pieces. In ancient covenants, both parties
  walked through — pledging 'may I be cut in pieces if I break this covenant.'
  But here, Abram sleeps. Only God walks through. This covenant depends on
  God alone. If it fails, GOD is cut in pieces — not Abram. And at the cross,
  God WAS 'cut' — Christ's body broken to keep the covenant Abram could never
  keep. Unconditional. Unbreakable. God's alone to fulfill."

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1  — KJV: "the word of the LORD came unto Abram in a vision" / ESV: "the
       word of the LORD came to Abram in a vision" — Both equivalent. First
       use of "the word of the LORD came" — a prophetic formula used hundreds
       of times in the OT.

v.1  — KJV: "thy shield, and thy exceeding great reward" / ESV: "your
       shield; your reward shall be very great" — Hebrew "magen" — a
       defensive shield. "Exceeding great reward" — Hebrew "sekarekha harbeh
       meod" — your reward is very great. KJV reads God Himself as the
       reward; ESV's phrasing could suggest the reward is separate. Hebrew
       supports both readings.

v.6  — KJV: "believed" / ESV: "believed" — Both identical. Hebrew "he'emin"
       — trusted, relied upon, had faith in. From the root "aman" (firm,
       sure — root of "Amen"). KJV: "counted it to him for righteousness" /
       ESV: "counted it to him as righteousness" — Both equivalent. Hebrew
       "chashab" — reckoned, credited, imputed. An accounting term: faith
       deposited to Abram's account as righteousness.

v.9  — KJV: "Take me an heifer of three years old" / ESV: "Bring me a
       heifer three years old" — Both equivalent. The covenant ceremony:
       animals cut in half, arranged in two rows. Both parties would walk
       between them, saying: "May I become like these animals if I break this
       covenant." A self-maledictory oath.

v.12 — KJV: "horror of great darkness" / ESV: "dreadful and great darkness"
       — Both equivalent. Hebrew "emah chashekhah gedolah" — a dreadful,
       great darkness. The weight of what God is about to reveal and commit
       to.

v.17 — KJV: "smoking furnace, and a burning lamp" / ESV: "a smoking fire
       pot and a flaming torch" — Hebrew "tannur ashan" (smoking oven/
       furnace) and "lappid esh" (torch of fire). ESV's "fire pot" and
       "flaming torch" are more precise modern renderings. Theophany: God's
       presence as fire and smoke (cf. Exodus 13:21, 19:18). GOD ALONE passes
       through — making this a unilateral covenant.

v.18 — KJV: "from the river of Egypt unto the great river" / ESV: "from the
       river of Egypt to the great river, the river Euphrates" — Both
       equivalent; ESV explicitly names the Euphrates. The full extent of the
       Promised Land. Never fully possessed except briefly under Solomon
       (1 Kings 4:21).

TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.1 — NET: "the Lord's message came to Abram in a vision" — renders Hebrew
  "debar Yahweh" as "the Lord's message" rather than "the word of the LORD"
  (KJV/ESV), emphasizing communication content over divine title.
- v.2 — WEB: "Lord Yahweh" — preserves the Hebrew "Adonai Yahweh" with both
  titles intact, where KJV/ESV use "Lord GOD" (two English words for two
  different Hebrew words). NET: "O Sovereign Lord" — makes the authority
  aspect of "Adonai" explicit.
- v.6 — NET: "the Lord credited it as righteousness to him" — renders the
  foundational justification verse with "credited" (accounting language),
  emphasizing imputation. ASV: "reckoned it to him" — similarly formal.
- v.12 — NET: "great terror overwhelmed him" — more intense than "a horror
  of great darkness fell upon him" (KJV/ESV), emphasizing the dread.
- v.16 — NET: "the sin of the Amorites has not yet reached its limit" —
  interprets "iniquity...not yet full" with "reached its limit," making the
  divine patience/threshold concept more accessible.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
"I am thy shield and reward" — God as both protector (shield) and prize
               (reward). He is not just the giver but the gift itself.

Believed and counted for righteousness — Justification by faith. Abram
               trusted God's promise and God credited righteousness to his
               account. The foundation of salvation by grace through faith.

The covenant ceremony — Animals cut in half; parties walk between them. A
               self-maledictory oath: "May I be cut apart if I break this."

God alone passed through — The covenant is unconditional. God takes full
               responsibility. If it fails, God bears the consequences.

Smoking furnace and burning lamp — God's presence in the covenant ceremony.
               Fire and smoke — theophany.

Four hundred years — The predicted duration of Israel's slavery in Egypt.
               Fulfilled in Exodus.

From the river of Egypt to the Euphrates — The full boundaries of the
               Promised Land. God's gift to Abram's descendants.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "I am thy shield, and thy exceeding great reward" (v.1). Is God Himself
   enough for you? Not just His gifts but HIM? He is both your protection
   and your prize.

2. "He believed in the LORD; and he counted it to him for righteousness"
   (v.6). This is how you are saved — by believing God's promise. Not by
   works but by faith. Have you believed? Is His righteousness credited to
   your account?

3. God alone passed between the pieces (v.17). Your salvation depends on
   God's faithfulness, not yours. The covenant is unconditional — God took
   full responsibility. Does this give you security?

4. "Thy seed shall be a stranger... four hundred years" (v.13). God told
   Abram the future would include suffering BEFORE fulfillment. Are you in a
   "four hundred years" season — waiting, suffering, before the promise is
   fulfilled? God sees the whole timeline.

5. "The iniquity of the Amorites is not yet full" (v.16). God is patient
   even with the wicked — giving time for repentance. But there is a limit.
   How does God's patience with evil encourage and challenge you?
