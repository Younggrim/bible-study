Esther - Chapter 5
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Esther risks her life by approaching the king unsummoned and is received with favor; she invites the king and Haman to a banquet, then a second. Haman leaves elated but furious at Mordecai, and his wife suggests building a gallows to hang him.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Unknown (possibly Mordecai). Written approximately 470-460
BC. Set during the reign of Ahasuerus/Xerxes I of Persia (486-465
BC). Unique: God's name is never mentioned, yet His providence
permeates every event. Key themes: God's hidden providence, the
preservation of the Jewish people, the reversal of evil plans,
courage in crisis ("for such a time as this"), and the origin of
the Feast of Purim.

Historical Context: Chapter 5 begins the dramatic reversal that
unfolds over the next three chapters. After three days of fasting,
Esther dons her royal robes and approaches the king uninvited —
the action she declared in 4:16. The king extends his golden scepter
(v.2), granting her life and audience. Rather than immediately
revealing her request, Esther employs patient wisdom: she invites
the king and Haman to a private banquet.

At the banquet, when the king asks her petition, Esther delays
again — inviting them to a second banquet the following day (vv.7-8).
This delay seems puzzling but proves providential: it is the night
between the two banquets when the king cannot sleep and Mordecai's
unrewarded loyalty is discovered (chapter 6). Had Esther spoken at
the first banquet, the reversal of chapter 6 would not have occurred.
Her patience — whether instinctive wisdom or divine guidance —
created space for God to work behind the scenes.

The chapter's second half (vv.9-14) contrasts Haman's emotional
volatility. He leaves the first banquet "joyful and with a glad
heart" — uniquely honored with a private royal invitation. But one
glimpse of Mordecai still refusing to bow destroys his happiness
entirely: "all this availeth me nothing, so long as I see Mordecai
the Jew sitting at the king's gate" (v.13). His wife Zeresh and
friends counsel him to build a gallows fifty cubits high (approximately
75 feet) and hang Mordecai before the second banquet. Haman agrees
enthusiastically, not knowing that he is building his own scaffold.
The chapter masterfully builds tension while demonstrating that
pride's happiness is always fragile — one unresolved grievance
poisons all abundance.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Susa (Shushan) — the Persian royal capital and winter residence, located in modern southwestern Iran.
- The story takes place entirely within the Persian palace/citadel complex — throne room, inner court, royal garden, and the king's gate.
- The Persian Empire stretched from India to Ethiopia (Cush) — 127 provinces (1:1), the largest empire the world had yet seen.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "And when the king saw Queen Esther standing in the court,
        she won favor in his sight, and he held out to Esther the
        golden scepter that was in his hand." (ESV) — The golden
        scepter extended means life granted. Esther's "if I
        perish" became "I have found favor." The moment of greatest
        risk became the moment of divine provision — God turned the
        king's heart toward His servant at the critical moment.

v.3  — "And the king said to her, 'What is it, Queen Esther? What
        is your request? It shall be given you, even to the half
        of my kingdom.'" (ESV) — The king's extravagant offer
        ("half of the kingdom") is formulaic royal generosity —
        expressing willingness to grant any reasonable request. Yet
        the irony is profound: what Esther will actually request is
        her own life and her people's survival — far less than half
        a kingdom, yet infinitely more valuable.

v.9  — "And Haman went out that day joyful and glad of heart. But
        when Haman saw Mordecai in the king's gate, that he
        neither rose nor trembled before him, he was filled with
        wrath against Mordecai." (ESV) — The chapter's
        psychological pivot. Haman's joy instantly collapses at the
        sight of one man's defiance. Pride cannot coexist with any
        opposition — it demands absolute acknowledgment.

v.13 — "Yet all this is worth nothing to me, so long as I see
        Mordecai the Jew sitting at the king's gate." (ESV) — The
        definitive statement of pride's misery. Haman has wealth,
        children, royal favor, and exclusive invitation to the
        queen's banquet — yet one man's refusal to bow nullifies
        it all. This is the bitter fruit of pride: it can never
        have enough because it demands everything.

v.14 — "Let a gallows fifty cubits high be made, and in the
        morning tell the king to have Mordecai hanged upon it.
        Then go joyfully with the king to the feast." (ESV) — The
        gallows that will become Haman's own execution device is
        constructed on his family's counsel. "Fifty cubits"
        (approximately 75 feet) was grotesquely excessive —
        designed for public spectacle, visible throughout the city.
        Pride desires not just victory but humiliation of the
        enemy.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.2  — Proverbs 21:1 — "The king's heart is a stream of water in
        the hand of the LORD; he turns it wherever he will." (ESV)
        The king's favorable reception of Esther was not chance but
        divine influence. The same God who moves kings' hearts
        turned Ahasuerus toward grace at the precise moment Esther
        risked death.

v.3  — 1 Kings 3:5 — "Ask what I shall give you." (ESV) Both
        Solomon and Esther received blank-check offers from
        authority figures. Solomon asked for wisdom; Esther would
        ask for her people's lives. The test of character lies in
        what you request when given unlimited options.

v.9  — Proverbs 27:4 — "Wrath is cruel, anger is overwhelming,
        but who can stand before jealousy?" (ESV) Haman's rage at
        Mordecai is rooted in wounded pride and envy — he cannot
        bear that one person refuses him the deference he craves.

v.13 — Ecclesiastes 5:10 — "He who loves money will not be
        satisfied with money." (ESV) Haman's insatiable
        dissatisfaction despite enormous privilege mirrors the
        Preacher's wisdom: the prideful heart can never have enough
        because it always fixates on what it lacks.

v.14 — Proverbs 26:27 — "Whoever digs a pit will fall into it."
        (ESV) The gallows Haman built for Mordecai became his own
        execution device (7:10). Scripture consistently teaches
        that traps set for the righteous ensnare the wicked
        themselves.

v.14 — Psalm 7:15 — "He makes a pit, digging it out, and falls
        into the hole that he has made." (ESV) The principle of
        reversal — evil's instruments turned against the evildoer
        — is a cornerstone of biblical justice and the dominant
        theme of Esther.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/esther-5/
  Guzik notes Esther's remarkable strategic patience — she did not
  blurt her request immediately but created conditions for maximum
  impact. Her delay also unwittingly created the space for God's
  providential intervention in chapter 6. He emphasizes that Haman's
  misery (v.13) despite extraordinary privilege reveals the self-
  destructive nature of pride: it converts blessing into torment by
  fixating on the one thing it cannot control.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "'Yet all this availeth me nothing.' Here sits a man with wealth
  beyond counting, favor above all peers, children in abundance,
  and the queen's exclusive invitation — yet one Jew's refusal to
  bow empties it all of satisfaction. Such is pride's bitter harvest.
  The proud man's cup of joy has a hole in its bottom — it can never
  be filled because it always leaks. One slight, one refusal, one
  competitor not conquered, and all his millions become worthless.
  But the humble man, who seeks God's approval alone, is satisfied
  with the least crumb from the heavenly table. Learn, O soul: the
  secret of contentment is not more honor from men but less
  dependence on it."

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1  — KJV: "Esther put on her royal apparel" / ESV: "Esther put
       on her royal robes" — Hebrew "vattilbash Esther malkhut"
       (Esther clothed herself [with] royalty). The Hebrew is
       striking: she literally "put on royalty" — not just clothes
       but authority. She entered the king's presence as queen, not
       as supplicant. Both translations capture this, with ESV's
       "robes" and KJV's "apparel" being equivalent.

v.6  — KJV: "at the banquet of wine" / ESV: "as they were drinking
       wine after the feast" — Hebrew "b'mishteh ha-yayin" (at the
       feast/drinking of wine). Persian banquets involved separate
       courses of food and wine. The "banquet of wine" was the
       drinking portion following the meal — a relaxed social
       setting where the king might be most receptive to requests.

v.9  — KJV: "he stood not up, nor moved for him" / ESV: "he
       neither rose nor trembled before him" — Hebrew "lo-qam v'lo
       za" (not rose and not trembled/moved). ESV's "trembled"
       captures the Hebrew "za" more precisely — Mordecai showed
       no physical reaction at all, neither standing nor even
       flinching. His composure was as defiant as active refusal.

v.14 — KJV: "a gallows be made of fifty cubits high" / ESV: "a
       gallows fifty cubits high be made" — Hebrew "etz gavoha
       chamishim ammah" (a tree/pole high fifty cubits). "Etz"
       means tree or wooden structure. The execution method was
       likely impalement on a tall stake (Persian practice) rather
       than hanging by rope. Fifty cubits (75 feet) was designed
       for maximum public visibility.

v.14 — KJV: "go thou in merrily with the king" / ESV: "go joyfully
       with the king" — Hebrew "vo sameyach im ha-melekh" (enter
       joyful with the king). Both translations convey Zeresh's
       counsel: dispatch Mordecai in the morning, then enjoy the
       queen's banquet with a light heart. The word "sameyach"
       (joyful) forms an ironic contrast with the "joyful" (v.9)
       that was instantly destroyed by seeing Mordecai.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Golden       — The royal scepter (Hebrew "sharvit") extended as a
Sceptre        sign of welcome and permission to live. Persian court
              protocol required that anyone approaching the king
              uninvited be killed unless the king extended his
              scepter. This made the monarch absolutely
              unapproachable — and Esther's approach an act of
              extraordinary courage. The scepter's extension
              meant: "You may live; you may speak."

Banquet      — Hebrew "mishteh" (from "shatah," to drink). Persian
              banquets were elaborate affairs combining food, wine,
              entertainment, and political discussion. Esther
              prepared two private banquets — intimate settings with
              only the king and Haman present, creating the ideal
              conditions for her devastating revelation (chapter 7).

Fifty        — Approximately 75 feet (23 meters). A cubit was
Cubits         roughly 18 inches. This extraordinary height for an
              execution device was not practical necessity but
              prideful spectacle — Haman wanted Mordecai's death
              visible to the entire city. The excessive height
              reflects the excessiveness of Haman's hatred and
              becomes the measure of his own humiliation.

Gallows      — Hebrew "etz" (tree, wood, wooden structure). Not a
              gallows in the Western sense (a frame for hanging by
              rope) but likely an impalement stake (the Persian
              method of execution). The condemned was impaled on a
              sharpened pole and displayed publicly. The height
              ensured maximum visibility and shame.

Zeresh       — Haman's wife, who counsels him to build the gallows
              (v.14) and later prophesies his doom (6:13). Her
              advice in chapter 5 seems supportive but leads Haman
              to his own destruction. In chapter 6, she reverses
              her counsel entirely: "thou shalt surely fall before
              him." She represents worldly wisdom — confident in
              power's success until the tide turns.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Patient wisdom may accomplish more than immediate action (vv.4,
   7-8). Esther delayed her request through two banquets — and
   that delay created space for God to orchestrate the providential
   events of chapter 6. Not every crisis requires instant action.
   Sometimes the wisest response is strategic patience — creating
   conditions for God to work before you move.

2. The extended scepter represents grace (v.2). Esther deserved
   death by law but received favor by grace. This is the gospel
   pattern: we approach a holy King uninvited, deserving judgment,
   and He extends His scepter of grace. Come boldly to God's
   throne — not because you deserve welcome but because He chooses
   to extend it.

3. Pride poisons abundance (v.13). Haman had everything — yet "all
   this availeth me nothing." A heart consumed with ego cannot
   enjoy genuine blessings because it obsesses over the one thing
   it cannot control. If you find yourself unable to enjoy real
   blessings because of one unresolved grievance or one person's
   disapproval, check your heart for pride's poison.

4. Those who build gallows for others often hang on them themselves
   (v.14). The principle of reaping what you sow is active in this
   narrative. The trap you set for others tends to catch you. The
   weapon you forge against the innocent tends to wound its maker.
   Instead of plotting harm, leave justice to God.

5. Courage and wisdom work together (vv.1-8). Esther was
   courageous (approaching uninvited) and wise (timing her
   revelation strategically). Courage without wisdom can be
   reckless; wisdom without courage can be cowardly. The most
   effective servants of God combine both — acting boldly but
   thoughtfully, with Spirit-directed timing.
