1 Chronicles - Chapter 29
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
David leads the assembly in generous giving for the Temple construction, offering his personal wealth and inspiring the leaders to give willingly. He closes with a magnificent prayer of praise, Solomon is anointed king a second time, and David dies after a forty-year reign.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Traditionally attributed to Ezra the scribe. Originally one
book with 2 Chronicles. Written post-exile (approximately 450-400 BC)
for the returned remnant. The Chronicler retells Israel's history from
a priestly/worship perspective, emphasizing the Davidic covenant,
Temple worship, and God's faithfulness. Key themes: genealogical
continuity (God preserved His people), proper worship (the Temple and
its services), the Davidic line (pointing to Messiah), and hope for
restoration.

Historical Context: Chapter 29 is the magnificent conclusion to
1 Chronicles and to David's life. It records three interconnected
events: David's personal offering and challenge for freewill gifts
toward the Temple (vv.1-9), David's great prayer of praise —
arguably the finest doxology in the Old Testament (vv.10-20), and
the coronation of Solomon with national celebration (vv.21-25),
followed by a summary of David's reign and death (vv.26-30). The
chapter moves from generosity to worship to succession — the three
marks of a life well-finished. David gives, David praises, David
passes the torch. He exits as he lived: with passionate devotion
to God and radical generosity toward God's house.

For the post-exilic community, this chapter was both inspiring and
convicting. They too were called to give generously for a Temple
(Ezra 1:4-6; Haggai 1:2-8), and David's example — giving from
his own "proper good" (personal treasure) beyond what was required
— set the standard. David's prayer in vv.10-19 became liturgically
significant in Jewish worship, and its theology of stewardship —
"all things come of thee, and of thine own have we given thee"
(v.14) — remains the definitive biblical statement on giving. We
do not give God what is ours; we return to God what was always His.
The people "offered willingly" with "perfect heart" and "great joy"
(v.9) — three marks of acceptable giving that challenged the
returned remnant (and challenge us) to examine the spirit behind
their generosity.

David's prayer (vv.10-19) is the theological climax of the entire
book of 1 Chronicles. It synthesizes the Chronicler's core themes:
God's sovereignty over all things, God's ownership of all
resources, human transience ("our days on the earth are as a
shadow"), the priority of heart-integrity before God, and the
need for divine grace to maintain devotion ("keep this for ever
in the imagination of the thoughts of the heart of thy people,
and prepare their heart unto thee," v.18). David acknowledges
that even the ability to give generously is itself God's gift —
radical stewardship theology that eliminates all pride in giving.
Solomon's coronation (the "second time," v.22 — the first was the
emergency anointing during Adonijah's rebellion, 1 Kings 1) brings
the narrative full circle: the kingdom that began in genealogy
(chapter 1) ends in doxology (chapter 29). David dies "full of
days, riches, and honour" (v.28) — a life completed, not cut short.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Largely parallels 1-2 Samuel geographically. Jerusalem (Zion/City of David) is the theological and political center.
- Key sites: Hebron (David's first capital, 7 years), the threshing floor of Ornan/Araunah (future Temple site, ch.21).
- Chapters 1-9 (genealogies) contain tribal allotments but minimal narrative geography.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.5  — "And who then is willing to consecrate his service this day
        unto the LORD?" (KJV) — David's challenge. The Hebrew for
        "consecrate his service" is literally "fill his hand" — a
        phrase used for priestly ordination (Exodus 28:41). David
        invites the people to fill their hands with offerings as
        priests fill their hands at consecration. Giving becomes
        a priestly act — worship through sacrifice. The question
        is not "who can afford it?" but "who is willing?"

v.11 — "Thine, O LORD, is the greatness, and the power, and the
        glory, and the victory, and the majesty: for all that is
        in the heaven and in the earth is thine; thine is the
        kingdom, O LORD, and thou art exalted as head above all."
        (KJV) — The supreme doxology of 1 Chronicles. Five
        attributes ascribed to God: greatness, power, glory,
        victory, majesty. "All that is in the heaven and in the
        earth is thine" — absolute divine ownership eliminates
        human possessiveness. "Thine is the kingdom" — echoed in
        the Lord's Prayer (Matthew 6:13). David ends where every
        believer should end: with everything belonging to God.

v.14 — "But who am I, and what is my people, that we should be
        able to offer so willingly after this sort? for all things
        come of thee, and of thine own have we given thee." (KJV)
        — The definitive theology of stewardship. David recognizes
        that even the ability to give is God's gift. "Of thine
        own have we given thee" — we never give God anything that
        did not first come from Him. This eliminates pride in
        generosity and transforms giving from human achievement
        to grateful return.

v.15 — "For we are strangers before thee, and sojourners, as were
        all our fathers: our days on the earth are as a shadow,
        and there is none abiding." (KJV) — David, king over all
        Israel for forty years, calls himself a "stranger and
        sojourner." The man with the greatest earthly position
        acknowledges the brevity and transience of human life.
        "None abiding" — nothing in this life is permanent. This
        honest eschatology empowered radical generosity: those
        who know they are passing through hold loosely what they
        cannot keep.

v.17 — "I know also, my God, that thou triest the heart, and hast
        pleasure in uprightness. As for me, in the uprightness of
        mine heart I have willingly offered all these things: and
        now have I seen with joy thy people, which are present
        here, to offer willingly unto thee." (KJV) — God tests
        hearts and delights in integrity. David's joy is doubled:
        his own willing offering and the sight of his people
        offering willingly. A leader's greatest joy is seeing
        those he leads respond to God with the same wholehearted
        devotion he models.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.5  — Exodus 35:5, 21-29 — Moses' call for tabernacle offerings:
        "Whosoever is of a willing heart, let him bring it." The
        pattern is consistent — God's house is built by willing
        hearts, never coerced giving. David's appeal echoes Moses'
        appeal, and both produced overwhelming, joyful generosity.

v.11 — Matthew 6:13 — "For thine is the kingdom, and the power,
        and the glory, for ever." The doxology of the Lord's Prayer
        (in its liturgical form) echoes David's prayer directly.
        What David declared at the end of his life, Jesus taught
        His disciples to pray daily. The kingdom always belongs to
        God — we merely serve in it.

v.14 — James 1:17 — "Every good gift and every perfect gift is from
        above, and cometh down from the Father of lights." David's
        principle restated in the New Testament: everything we have
        — including the ability and desire to give — originates
        with God. Generosity is returning a fraction of what we
        have received.

v.15 — Hebrews 11:13 — "These all died in faith... confessing that
        they were strangers and pilgrims on the earth." David's
        self-description as "stranger and sojourner" places him in
        the great company of faith — those who lived for an eternal
        city while passing through a temporal world.

v.15 — Psalm 39:12 — "I am a stranger with thee, and a sojourner,
        as all my fathers were." David's own psalm expresses the
        same theology — human life is brief and borrowed. This
        conviction was not morbid despair but liberating reality
        that freed him to give generously.

v.18 — Philippians 2:13 — "For it is God which worketh in you both
        to will and to do of his good pleasure." David's prayer
        that God would "prepare their heart" unto Himself
        anticipates Paul's teaching that even the desire to obey
        God is God's work within us. Sanctification is divine
        initiative met by human cooperation.

v.22 — 1 Kings 1:38-40 — The first anointing of Solomon during
        Adonijah's attempted coup. Chapter 29's "second time"
        anointing was the public, national coronation after the
        emergency was past — the formal transfer of power with
        all Israel assembled and consenting.

v.28 — Genesis 25:8 — Abraham "died in a good old age, an old
        man, and full of years." David's death description echoes
        Abraham's — both patriarchs died "full" (satisfied, with
        nothing lacking). To die "full of days, riches, and
        honour" is the covenant blessing of a completed life.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/1-chronicles-29/
  Guzik highlights that David's prayer in vv.10-19 is one of the
  greatest prayers in Scripture, combining praise, theological
  depth, humility, and intercession in a single address. He
  emphasizes that "of thine own have we given thee" (v.14) is the
  foundational principle of all Christian stewardship — we are
  never donors, only stewards returning what was entrusted. Guzik
  also notes the beauty of David's death: a man who finished well
  after a life of both great triumph and great failure.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "'Thine, O LORD, is the greatness, and the power, and the glory.'
  Here is a man who possessed all three — greatness as Israel's
  champion, power as her king, glory as her conqueror — and he lays
  every crown at Jehovah's feet. David knew what many wealthy givers
  forget: we do not bestow upon God from our abundance; we merely
  unclasp our fingers from what was never ours. 'Of thine own have
  we given thee.' Every gold talent came from God's earth, refined
  by God-given skill, accumulated under God-given prosperity. The
  giver has nothing that the Giver did not first give. And mark
  this: David gave 'in the uprightness of his heart' and saw his
  people give 'willingly' — and his joy was complete. The leader
  who gives first and most opens a river of generosity in those
  who follow."

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1  — KJV: "young and tender" / ESV: "young and inexperienced" —
       Hebrew "na'ar va-rakh" (young and tender/soft). KJV's
       "tender" preserves the Hebrew metaphor of softness (unformed,
       not yet hardened by experience); ESV's "inexperienced"
       interprets the metaphor for modern readers. Both convey that
       Solomon is not yet proven — the work requires maturity he
       has not yet developed.

v.2  — KJV: "glistering stones, and of divers colours" / ESV:
       "antimony, colored stones" — Hebrew "avney-puk" and "avney
       riqmah." "Puk" refers to antimony (a metallic element used
       for cosmetic eye-paint and decorative inlay); KJV's
       "glistering stones" is an interpretive guess. "Riqmah"
       means variegated/multicolored. ESV's "antimony" is
       technically more accurate but less evocative.

v.5  — KJV: "consecrate his service" / ESV: "consecrating himself"
       — Hebrew "le-malle yado" literally "to fill his hand."
       This idiom was used specifically for priestly ordination
       (Exodus 28:41; Leviticus 8:33). KJV's "consecrate his
       service" and ESV's "consecrating himself" both capture the
       priestly overtone — giving is presented as a form of
       self-offering to God's service.

v.7  — KJV: "ten thousand drams" / ESV: "10,000 darics" — Hebrew
       "adarkonim." These are Persian gold coins (darics), which
       is an acknowledged anachronism — darics did not exist in
       David's time. The Chronicler converts the value into
       currency his post-exilic readers would understand. KJV's
       "drams" (drachmas) is less precise; ESV's "darics" is
       historically specific to the Persian period.

v.18 — KJV: "keep this for ever in the imagination of the thoughts
       of the heart of thy people, and prepare their heart unto
       thee" / ESV: "keep forever such purposes and thoughts in the
       hearts of your people, and direct their hearts toward you"
       — Hebrew "yetser machshevoth lev." KJV's "imagination of
       the thoughts" preserves the Hebrew layering (yetser =
       inclination/formation; machshevoth = thoughts/plans). ESV
       simplifies to "purposes and thoughts." David prays that God
       would preserve the generous inclination permanently in the
       people's hearts — acknowledging that human devotion fades
       without divine preservation.

v.22 — KJV: "anointed him unto the LORD to be the chief governor" /
       ESV: "they anointed him as prince for the LORD" — Hebrew
       "nagid la-YHWH" (prince/ruler for the LORD). KJV's "chief
       governor" is an unusual rendering; ESV's "prince for the
       LORD" is more standard. The key phrase is "for the LORD" —
       Solomon's kingship belongs to God; he rules as God's
       representative, not as autonomous sovereign.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Talents     — Hebrew "kikkar" — the largest unit of weight in the
             Hebrew system, approximately 75 pounds (34 kg). The
             gifts totaled staggering amounts: 5,000 talents of
             gold (approximately 188 tons), 10,000 talents of
             silver (approximately 375 tons), 18,000 talents of
             bronze, and 100,000 talents of iron. Whether these
             are literal or representative of enormous wealth, they
             demonstrate extraordinary national generosity for
             God's house.

Darics/     — Hebrew "adarkonim" — Persian gold coins weighing
Drams         approximately 8.4 grams each. Named after Darius I
             of Persia, they are an anachronistic reference — the
             Chronicler converts ancient values into contemporary
             currency for his post-exilic audience. 10,000 darics
             represented substantial personal wealth dedicated to
             the Temple project.

Ophir       — A region famous for its gold, likely located in
             southern Arabia, East Africa (Somalia/Ethiopia), or
             western India. "Gold of Ophir" was the highest quality
             gold available in the ancient world (cf. Job 22:24;
             Psalm 45:9; Isaiah 13:12). David's 3,000 talents of
             Ophir gold represented the finest material available
             for God's house — nothing inferior was acceptable.

Consecrate  — Hebrew "malle yad" (fill the hand) — originally a
(his service) priestly ordination term describing the filling of a
             priest's hands with the sacrificial portions at his
             installation (Exodus 29:9; Leviticus 8:33). David
             appropriates this priestly language for giving,
             implying that generous offering is itself an act of
             priestly service — the giver fills his hands with
             gifts as the priest fills his hands with sacrifice.

Sojourner   — Hebrew "ger" (resident alien) and "toshav" (temporary
             settler). Terms for someone living in a land they do
             not own — having no permanent claim or possession.
             David applies this to all of Israel's relationship to
             the earth: even the king with forty years of reign
             is merely passing through. This theology of
             transience is fundamental to biblical generosity —
             those who know they are temporary hold loosely what
             they cannot keep.

Seer        — Hebrew "chozeh" (one who sees) and "ro'eh" (seer).
             Three prophetic sources are named for David's
             history: Samuel the "ro'eh," Nathan the "navi"
             (prophet), and Gad the "chozeh" (v.29). These
             represent different prophetic functions: Samuel was
             the priestly seer, Nathan the court prophet, and Gad
             the visionary advisor. Together they recorded David's
             complete history — indicating multiple authoritative
             sources behind the biblical record.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Everything we have comes from God and belongs to God (v.14). "Of
   thine own have we given thee" — this single statement transforms
   the entire theology of giving. We are not generous benefactors
   bestowing our possessions on a needy God. We are stewards
   returning a portion of what was entrusted. Every dollar, every
   talent, every hour was God's before it was ours. True generosity
   begins with recognizing that ownership was always an illusion.
   Give freely, because you are merely releasing what never belonged
   to you.

2. We are strangers and sojourners — hold this world loosely (v.15).
   David, at the pinnacle of earthly success (forty-year reign,
   enormous wealth, national adoration), declares himself a
   temporary resident. If the greatest king of Israel recognized
   his transience, how much more should we? The brevity of life is
   not depressing but liberating — it frees us from accumulating
   what we cannot keep and redirects our investment toward what is
   eternal. What are you clinging to that you cannot take with you?

3. Willing, joyful giving marks a healthy heart (v.9). "The people
   rejoiced, for that they offered willingly, because with perfect
   heart they offered willingly to the LORD." Joy accompanied their
   giving — it was not reluctant obligation but delighted worship.
   If giving feels like loss rather than worship, examine your
   heart. Generous giving from a willing spirit produces deep joy;
   reluctant giving from a grudging heart produces only resentment.
   Ask God to transform your heart until generosity becomes your
   greatest pleasure.

4. Leaders who give first inspire generosity in others (vv.1-9).
   David gave from his personal treasury before challenging the
   people. His example opened the floodgates — the leaders gave,
   then the people gave, and all rejoiced. Leadership in generosity
   is the most powerful form of leadership. Those who call others
   to give while withholding themselves are hypocrites; those who
   give first and most create a culture of generosity. Lead with
   your own offering before asking for anyone else's.

5. Pray that God would preserve the desire to serve Him (v.18).
   David's prayer reveals a profound awareness: human devotion is
   fragile. He asks God to "keep this for ever" in the people's
   hearts — to preserve the generous, willing spirit they currently
   feel. David knows that today's passion can become tomorrow's
   indifference without divine preservation. Pray this prayer for
   yourself and your community: "Lord, keep this devotion alive in
   our hearts. We cannot sustain our own zeal — preserve what You
   have stirred."
