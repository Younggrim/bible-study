1 Chronicles - Chapter 28
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
David publicly charges Solomon before all Israel to build the Temple, giving him the divinely inspired plans for every detail of its construction. He emphasizes that Solomon must know God personally and serve Him wholeheartedly to see the work completed.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Traditionally attributed to Ezra the scribe. Originally one
book with 2 Chronicles. Written post-exile (approximately 450-400 BC)
for the returned remnant. The Chronicler retells Israel's history from
a priestly/worship perspective, emphasizing the Davidic covenant,
Temple worship, and God's faithfulness. Key themes: genealogical
continuity (God preserved His people), proper worship (the Temple and
its services), the Davidic line (pointing to Messiah), and hope for
restoration.

Historical Context: Chapter 28 records one of the most significant
moments in Israel's history: David's public charge to Solomon and
all Israel concerning the building of the Temple. David assembles
the entire national leadership — tribal princes, military commanders,
stewards, and mighty men — and delivers what amounts to his final
public address as king. He recounts God's choice of him, God's
choice of Solomon, and God's prohibition against David building the
Temple because he was "a man of war" who had "shed blood" (v.3).
Then, most remarkably, David delivers to Solomon the architectural
plans for the Temple — plans he received "by the spirit" (v.12 KJV)
or "had in mind" (v.12 ESV) — and specifies precise weights of gold
and silver for every vessel, lampstand, table, and the mercy seat
itself. The chapter culminates in David's charge: "Be strong and of
good courage, and do it" (v.20).

For the post-exilic community, this chapter was electrifying. They
were rebuilding a Temple — and here was the divine authorization for
the original. The plans came not from David's imagination but from
God's hand (v.19): "All this... the LORD made me understand in
writing by his hand upon me, even all the works of this pattern."
This parallels Moses receiving the tabernacle pattern on Sinai
(Exodus 25:9, 40). The Temple, like the tabernacle, was divinely
designed — not a human invention but a heavenly blueprint realized
in earthly materials. For those rebuilding after exile, this meant
their Temple was not arbitrary but connected to a divine pattern
that transcended the destruction of 586 BC. The charge to Solomon
— "know the God of thy father" (v.9) — became the charge to every
generation rebuilding worship after devastation.

The theological weight of verse 9 cannot be overstated: "The LORD
searcheth all hearts, and understandeth all the imaginations of the
thoughts." David instructs Solomon that external obedience alone is
insufficient — God examines the heart and discerns every intention.
This is worship theology at its deepest: the Temple is for the God
who cannot be contained in buildings but who chooses to dwell among
a people whose hearts are wholly His. The chapter balances divine
sovereignty (God chose David, chose Solomon, designed the Temple)
with human responsibility ("if thou seek him... if thou forsake
him"). Both realities coexist without tension in David's theology.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Largely parallels 1-2 Samuel geographically. Jerusalem (Zion/City of David) is the theological and political center.
- Key sites: Hebron (David's first capital, 7 years), the threshing floor of Ornan/Araunah (future Temple site, ch.21).
- Chapters 1-9 (genealogies) contain tribal allotments but minimal narrative geography.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.3  — "But God said unto me, Thou shalt not build an house for my
        name, because thou hast been a man of war, and hast shed
        blood." (KJV) — David publicly acknowledges the limitation
        God placed on him. A lesser man might have resented this
        prohibition or hidden it. David embraces it transparently,
        teaching Israel that submission to God's "no" is as
        important as pursuing God's "yes." The Temple of peace
        required a man of peace to build it — a typological
        pointer to Christ, the Prince of Peace, who builds the
        true Temple (His Church).

v.9  — "And thou, Solomon my son, know thou the God of thy father,
        and serve him with a perfect heart and with a willing mind:
        for the LORD searcheth all hearts, and understandeth all
        the imaginations of the thoughts: if thou seek him, he
        will be found of thee; but if thou forsake him, he will
        cast thee off for ever." (KJV) — The greatest verse in
        this chapter. "Know" (yada) means intimate, experiential
        knowledge — not mere intellectual acknowledgment. "Perfect
        heart" (lev shalem) means a whole, undivided heart. "The
        LORD searcheth all hearts" — God's omniscience makes
        hypocrisy impossible. The conditional promise and warning
        are stark: seek and find, or forsake and be cast off.

v.12 — "And the pattern of all that he had by the spirit, of the
        courts of the house of the LORD." (KJV) — The Temple plans
        came "by the spirit" — whether this means the Holy Spirit
        revealed them or that David received them in his own spirit
        through divine inspiration, the result is the same: the
        Temple design was God's work, not man's. This parallels
        Exodus 25:9 where Moses received the tabernacle pattern on
        Sinai. Both worship structures originated in heaven.

v.19 — "All this, said David, the LORD made me understand in
        writing by his hand upon me, even all the works of this
        pattern." (KJV) — David claims divine inspiration for the
        architectural plans — "the LORD made me understand in
        writing by his hand upon me." This is a claim of
        inscripturation — God communicated the pattern in written
        form, just as He wrote the commandments on stone tablets.
        The Temple was as much a product of divine revelation as
        the Torah itself.

v.20 — "And David said to Solomon his son, Be strong and of good
        courage, and do it: fear not, nor be dismayed: for the
        LORD God, even my God, will be with thee; he will not fail
        thee, nor forsake thee, until thou hast finished all the
        work for the service of the house of the LORD." (KJV) —
        David echoes God's charge to Joshua (Joshua 1:5-9). The
        language is nearly identical — "be strong," "fear not,"
        "I will not fail thee nor forsake thee." Solomon's
        commission to build the Temple parallels Joshua's
        commission to take the land. Both require courage rooted
        in divine presence, not human adequacy.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.3  — 2 Samuel 7:5-13 — Nathan's prophecy: God told David "Thou
        shalt not build me an house" but promised that his son
        would build it and God would establish his throne forever.
        Chapter 28 is the public fulfillment and transmission of
        this private revelation.

v.9  — Jeremiah 29:13 — "And ye shall seek me, and find me, when
        ye shall search for me with all your heart." The
        conditional promise David gives Solomon echoes through
        the prophets — God is findable, but only by those who
        seek with undivided hearts.

v.12 — Exodus 25:9 — "According to all that I shew thee, after the
        pattern of the tabernacle, and the pattern of all the
        instruments thereof, even so shall ye make it." Moses
        received the tabernacle pattern on Sinai; David received
        the Temple pattern by the Spirit. Both worship structures
        were divinely designed, not humanly conceived.

v.18 — Exodus 25:18-22 — The original instructions for the
        cherubim over the mercy seat. David's "pattern of the
        chariot of the cherubims" (v.18) extends the original
        design — the Temple's cherubim are larger and more
        elaborate, but the concept comes from the same divine
        source.

v.19 — Hebrews 8:5 — "Who serve unto the example and shadow of
        heavenly things, as Moses was admonished of God when he was
        about to make the tabernacle... Make all things according
        to the pattern shewed to thee in the mount." The New
        Testament confirms that earthly worship structures are
        copies of heavenly realities — what David received "by
        the spirit" was a glimpse of that heavenly pattern.

v.20 — Joshua 1:5-9 — "Be strong and of a good courage... I will
        not fail thee, nor forsake thee." David's charge to Solomon
        deliberately echoes God's charge to Joshua. The mission is
        different (building vs. conquering), but the source of
        courage is identical: God's unfailing presence.

v.20 — Hebrews 13:5 — "I will never leave thee, nor forsake thee."
        The promise given to Joshua, repeated to Solomon, is
        extended to all believers in Christ. God's commitment to
        His servants endures across all dispensations.

v.9  — Psalm 139:1-4 — "O LORD, thou hast searched me, and known
        me... thou understandest my thought afar off." David's
        statement that "the LORD searcheth all hearts" reflects his
        own experience expressed in Psalm 139. He teaches Solomon
        what he himself has lived.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/1-chronicles-28/
  Guzik emphasizes the remarkable parallel between David receiving
  the Temple plans "by the spirit" and Moses receiving the
  tabernacle pattern on Sinai — both worship structures were
  divinely designed rather than humanly imagined. He highlights
  verse 9 as one of the most important single verses in Chronicles,
  combining the themes of heart-knowledge of God, willing service,
  divine omniscience, and the conditional nature of sustained
  blessing.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "David could not build the Temple, but he could prepare everything
  for the man who would. What a lesson for those whose hands are
  tied by providence! You cannot do the great work yourself? Then
  do all you can to equip the one who will. David gave Solomon the
  pattern, the materials, the personnel, and the encouragement. He
  could not lay the first stone, but he laid everything else in
  readiness. Be not idle because your specific dream is denied you.
  God has a Solomon coming after you, and your preparation is his
  provision. And mark those words: 'The LORD made me understand in
  writing by his hand upon me.' The Temple came from heaven before
  it rose from earth. Every true work of God begins in divine
  revelation, not human imagination."

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.2  — KJV: "an house of rest for the ark of the covenant of the
       LORD, and for the footstool of our God" / ESV: "a house of
       rest for the ark of the covenant of the LORD and for the
       footstool of our God" — Both render identically. The Hebrew
       "beth-menuchah" (house of rest) is theologically rich — the
       ark had been mobile since Sinai; the Temple would give it
       permanent rest. "Footstool" (hadom) presents the Temple as
       God's throne room — heaven is His seat, the ark is where
       His feet rest on earth (cf. Isaiah 66:1).

v.9  — KJV: "a perfect heart and with a willing mind" / ESV: "a
       whole heart and with a willing mind" — Hebrew "lev shalem
       ve-nephesh chaphetsah." KJV's "perfect" translates "shalem"
       (complete, whole, undivided); ESV's "whole" is more precise
       since "shalem" does not imply sinless perfection but
       undivided devotion. "Willing mind" — Hebrew "nephesh
       chaphetsah" (a soul that delights/desires) — service must
       flow from desire, not mere duty.

v.11 — KJV: "the pattern of the porch" / ESV: "the plan of the
       vestibule" — Hebrew "tavnith ha-ulam." "Tavnith" means
       pattern, model, or architectural plan. KJV's "pattern" and
       ESV's "plan" are essentially synonymous. "Ulam" refers to
       the entrance hall or vestibule of the Temple — KJV's
       "porch" reflects older English architectural vocabulary.

v.12 — KJV: "the pattern of all that he had by the spirit" / ESV:
       "the plan of all that he had in mind" — This is the most
       theologically significant translation difference in the
       chapter. Hebrew "tavnith kol asher hayah ba-ruach immo."
       KJV takes "ba-ruach" as "by the Spirit" (divine
       inspiration); ESV takes it as "in [his] mind/spirit"
       (David's own mental conception). The Hebrew "ruach" can mean
       Spirit (of God), spirit (of man), or mind. Context and
       verse 19 ("the LORD made me understand") support the KJV's
       reading — divine revelation rather than mere human planning.

v.18 — KJV: "the pattern of the chariot of the cherubims" / ESV:
       "his plan for the golden chariot of the cherubim" — Hebrew
       "tavnith ha-merkavah ha-keruvim." The cherubim over the ark
       are called a "chariot" (merkavah) — connecting to Ezekiel's
       vision of God's throne-chariot and suggesting the ark was
       understood as God's vehicle/throne. This rich imagery
       presents the mercy seat as a divine conveyance, not merely
       furniture.

v.19 — KJV: "the LORD made me understand in writing by his hand
       upon me" / ESV: "All this he made clear to me in writing
       from the hand of the LORD" — Hebrew "haskil ba-ketav
       mi-yad YHWH alay." The phrase "from the hand of the LORD
       upon me" echoes prophetic language (Ezekiel 1:3; 3:22).
       Both translations convey divine communication through
       writing, but KJV's word order emphasizes God's hand
       resting on David as the medium of revelation.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Pattern/    — Hebrew "tavnith" — a model, plan, or architectural
Plan          blueprint. Used of both the tabernacle (Exodus 25:9)
             and Temple designs. The word implies a heavenly
             prototype reproduced in earthly materials — the
             earthly structure copies a divine original. This
             concept is confirmed in Hebrews 8:5 where earthly
             worship serves as "copies and shadows of heavenly
             things."

Mercy Seat  — Hebrew "kapporeth" (from "kaphar," to cover/atone).
             The golden lid of the ark of the covenant where the
             blood of atonement was sprinkled on the Day of
             Atonement (Leviticus 16:14-15). The cherubim spread
             their wings over it, forming God's throne. It
             represents the place where divine justice and divine
             mercy meet — where sin is covered and God's presence
             dwells with His people.

Chariot of  — Hebrew "merkavah ha-keruvim" — the cherubim over the
the           ark conceived as a divine chariot or throne-vehicle.
Cherubim      This language connects to Psalm 18:10 ("He rode upon
             a cherub and did fly") and Ezekiel's vision of God's
             throne-chariot (Ezekiel 1, 10). The mercy seat was
             not static furniture but the mobile throne of the
             God who rides the heavens.

Footstool   — Hebrew "hadom raglav" (stool of his feet). The ark/
             Temple conceived as the footstool of God's cosmic
             throne. Heaven is His seat (Isaiah 66:1); the earth/
             Temple is where His feet rest. This imagery maintains
             God's transcendence (He is infinitely above) while
             affirming His immanence (He touches earth at the
             Temple/ark). It prevents the error of confining God
             to a building while affirming His real presence there.

Shewbread   — Hebrew "lechem ha-panim" (bread of the face/presence).
             Twelve loaves placed on a golden table in the Holy
             Place, representing the twelve tribes perpetually in
             God's presence. Replaced weekly on the Sabbath (the
             priests ate the old bread). David specifies gold
             tables for this bread — multiple tables in the Temple
             vs. one in the tabernacle.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. God's "no" to you may be His "yes" to the next generation (v.3).
   David desired to build the Temple but was told "thou shalt not."
   Rather than sulking or forcing his way, David poured everything
   into preparing what another would build. When God closes a door
   in your life, consider that He may be positioning you to prepare
   the way for someone else's calling. Not every vision you receive
   is yours to execute — some are yours to prepare and pass on.

2. Know God, not just know about Him (v.9). David's charge is
   "know the God of thy father" — personal, experiential, intimate
   knowledge. Head knowledge of doctrine, theology, and Bible facts
   is insufficient. God searches hearts, not merely orthodoxy. Do
   you know God Himself — His character, His voice, His presence —
   or merely truths about Him? Religion stays at information;
   relationship pursues the Person behind the information.

3. God searches the heart behind the action (v.9). "The LORD
   searcheth all hearts, and understandeth all the imaginations of
   the thoughts." External obedience with internal reluctance does
   not satisfy God. He examines motivation, not merely behavior.
   This should produce both humility (we cannot deceive God) and
   freedom (we need not perform for Him — He already knows us
   completely). Serve with a willing mind because the unwilling
   servant's work is known to be hollow.

4. Divine blueprints exist for God's work (vv.12, 19). The Temple
   was not David's design but God's revelation. This principle
   applies to every genuine work of God — there is a divine
   pattern, and our task is to receive it, not invent it. In your
   ministry, family, or calling, seek God's design rather than
   imposing your own. Ask: "Lord, what is Your pattern for this?"
   rather than assuming your ideas are automatically His.

5. Courage for great tasks comes from God's presence, not personal
   ability (v.20). "Be strong and of good courage, and do it: fear
   not, nor be dismayed." The grounds for courage are not Solomon's
   wisdom or resources but "the LORD God, even my God, will be with
   thee." When God assigns a task, He does not leave you to
   accomplish it alone. His presence is both the command and the
   resource. Whatever God has called you to — be strong and do it.
   He who called you is faithful, and He will not fail you.
