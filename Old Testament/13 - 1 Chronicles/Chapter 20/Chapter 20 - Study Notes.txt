1 Chronicles - Chapter 20
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Joab besieges and captures Rabbah, the Ammonite capital, and David takes the king's crown. Additional Philistine wars are recorded, including the slaying of giants by David's warriors, echoing David's own victory over Goliath.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Traditionally attributed to Ezra the scribe. Originally one
book with 2 Chronicles. Written post-exile (approximately 450-400 BC)
for the returned remnant. The Chronicler retells Israel's history from
a priestly/worship perspective, emphasizing the Davidic covenant,
Temple worship, and God's faithfulness. Key themes: genealogical
continuity (God preserved His people), proper worship (the Temple and
its services), the Davidic line (pointing to Messiah), and hope for
restoration.

Historical Context: Chapter 20 is one of the most theologically
significant chapters in Chronicles for what it omits rather than what
it includes. This brief chapter of only eight verses covers the
capture of Rabbah (the Ammonite capital) and three encounters with
Philistine giants. It parallels 2 Samuel 11:1, 12:26-31, and 21:18-
22 — but the Chronicler deliberately removes the entire Bathsheba-
Uriah narrative that fills 2 Samuel 11-12. The chapter moves directly
from "David tarried at Jerusalem" (the very words that in Samuel
introduce the adultery) to the capture of Rabbah, skipping over two
full chapters of sin, murder, prophetic confrontation, and
repentance. This is not ignorance or carelessness — the Chronicler
clearly knew the full story — but a deliberate editorial choice.

The Chronicler's purpose is not biography but theology. His audience
is the post-exilic community that needs to understand David as the
covenant king through whom God's promises flow. The sin with Bathsheba
was dealt with in Samuel; the Chronicler's focus is the Davidic
covenant, the Temple, and worship. He presents David's reign through
the lens of its significance for the restoration community: David's
military victories secured the kingdom, his wealth funded the Temple,
and his dynasty carries God's eternal promises. This selective
presentation does not whitewash David — the original readers knew
Samuel well — but refocuses the narrative on God's faithfulness to
His covenant despite David's failures.

The giant-killing narratives (vv.4-8) serve a specific purpose: they
demonstrate that the threat of the Philistine giants — which
terrorized Israel since Goliath — was systematically eliminated under
David's reign. Three descendants of the giants (Rephaim) fall to
David's warriors: Sippai to Sibbechai, Lahmi (Goliath's brother) to
Elhanan, and an unnamed giant with six fingers on each hand and six
toes on each foot to Jonathan son of Shimea. The phrase "these were
born unto the giant in Gath; and they fell by the hand of David, and
by the hand of his servants" (v.8) summarizes the theme: David's
kingdom overcomes every oversized enemy. For the vulnerable post-
exilic community surrounded by hostile neighbors, this was a message
of hope — God's anointed king and his servants can overcome any
giant.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Largely parallels 1-2 Samuel geographically. Jerusalem (Zion/City of David) is the theological and political center.
- Key sites: Hebron (David's first capital, 7 years), the threshing floor of Ornan/Araunah (future Temple site, ch.21).
- Chapters 1-9 (genealogies) contain tribal allotments but minimal narrative geography.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "And it came to pass, that after the year was expired, at the
        time that kings go out to battle, Joab led forth the power
        of the army, and wasted the country of the children of
        Ammon, and came and besieged Rabbah. But David tarried at
        Jerusalem." (KJV) — For readers of Samuel, "David tarried
        at Jerusalem" is loaded with tragic irony — this is when
        the Bathsheba incident occurred. The Chronicler includes
        the phrase but moves immediately past it. Theologically,
        the verse shows that Joab faithfully executed David's
        military campaigns while David remained in the capital. The
        work of the kingdom continued even when the king was not
        personally present.

v.2  — "And David took the crown of their king from off his head,
        and found it to weigh a talent of gold, and there were
        precious stones in it; and it was set upon David's head:
        and he brought also exceeding much spoil out of the city."
        (KJV) — The crown weighing a talent of gold (approximately
        75 pounds) was likely a ceremonial piece suspended above the
        throne rather than worn constantly. David takes the enemy's
        crown — a symbol of transferred sovereignty. The "exceeding
        much spoil" would later be designated for Temple
        construction (cf. 1 Chronicles 22:14), connecting military
        victory to worship preparation.

v.5  — "And there was war again with the Philistines; and Elhanan
        the son of Jair slew Lahmi the brother of Goliath the
        Gittite, whose spear staff was like a weaver's beam." (KJV)
        — This verse resolves a famous textual difficulty in
        2 Samuel 21:19, which in some manuscripts appears to say
        Elhanan killed Goliath himself. Chronicles clarifies: he
        killed Lahmi, the brother of Goliath. The "weaver's beam"
        comparison for the spear shaft indicates enormous size —
        the same description given of Goliath's spear in 1 Samuel
        17:7. The giant tradition continued in Goliath's family.

v.8  — "These were born unto the giant in Gath; and they fell by
        the hand of David, and by the hand of his servants." (KJV)
        — The summary statement attributes all giant-killing to both
        David and his servants. Though David did not personally slay
        these three, his leadership enabled his men's victories.
        This principle applies to spiritual leadership: a faithful
        leader raises up warriors who can defeat giants the leader
        himself does not personally face. David's legacy includes
        the victories of those he trained.

v.3  — "And he brought out the people that were in it, and cut them
        with saws, and with harrows of iron, and with axes. Even so
        dealt David with all the cities of the children of Ammon."
        (KJV) — This difficult verse describes the subjugation of
        the Ammonite population. Some scholars read this as forced
        labor (the ESV renders "set them to labor with saws") rather
        than execution. The Hebrew admits both readings. In the
        ancient Near East, conquered peoples were commonly pressed
        into labor gangs for building projects — consistent with
        David's massive material preparations for the Temple.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.1  — 2 Samuel 11:1 — "At the time when kings go out to battle...
        David tarried still at Jerusalem." Samuel uses this identical
        phrase to introduce the Bathsheba narrative. The Chronicler
        uses it and moves on — the most significant editorial silence
        in the Old Testament.

v.1  — 2 Samuel 12:26-31 — The parallel account of Rabbah's capture,
        where Joab calls David to come take the city personally so
        that the victory would be credited to the king. Chronicles
        compresses this into a single verse.

v.2  — 1 Chronicles 22:14 — David's preparation of "an hundred
        thousand talents of gold" for the Temple. The "exceeding
        much spoil" from Rabbah and other conquests became the
        material foundation for Temple construction.

v.4  — 2 Samuel 21:18-22 — The parallel account of the Philistine
        giant encounters. Samuel records four incidents; Chronicles
        records three (omitting the one involving David personally
        being rescued by Abishai, since it showed David's weakness).

v.5  — 1 Samuel 17:7 — "The staff of his [Goliath's] spear was like
        a weaver's beam." The same description applied to Goliath's
        brother Lahmi demonstrates that enormous physical power ran
        in this family. What Goliath was, his brother was also.

v.6  — Genesis 6:4 — "There were giants (Nephilim) in the earth in
        those days." The Rephaim/giants of Gath represent the
        continuation of the oversized warrior tradition. David's
        kingdom systematically eliminates this ancient threat.

v.8  — Numbers 13:33 — "We saw the giants, the sons of Anak... and
        we were in our own sight as grasshoppers." Israel's original
        failure at Kadesh-Barnea was caused by fear of giants. Under
        David, giants are routinely defeated — faith has replaced
        fear.

v.8  — Romans 8:37 — "In all these things we are more than
        conquerors through him that loved us." David's servants
        conquered giants not by their own superior size but through
        the anointed king's authority. Believers conquer through
        Christ's authority likewise.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/1-chronicles-20/
  Guzik highlights the Chronicler's deliberate omission of the
  Bathsheba-Uriah narrative as a key interpretive feature. He notes
  that this is not dishonesty but a different purpose: Chronicles
  presents David as the covenant king whose reign establishes the
  pattern for Temple worship. Guzik also observes that the giant-
  killing passages show David raising up a generation of warriors
  who could do what only he had done before.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "David's mighty men slew giants because David had shown them how.
  When the shepherd boy walked into the Valley of Elah with a sling
  and five smooth stones, he was not only winning a battle — he was
  training a generation. Sibbechai, Elhanan, Jonathan — these men
  learned giant-killing from their king. So it is with Christ and
  His church: our Lord has conquered every principality and power,
  and now His servants go forth in His name to slay the giants that
  remain. You face Goliaths of fear, of temptation, of despair?
  Remember that your King has already shown the way. Take up His
  weapons — faith, prayer, the Word — and strike. The giant will
  fall, for he falls 'by the hand of David, and by the hand of
  his servants.'"

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1  — KJV: "after the year was expired" / ESV: "In the spring of
       the year" — Hebrew "litshuvat hashanah" literally means "at
       the return/turning of the year." KJV interprets this as a
       completed cycle; ESV follows the understanding that this
       refers to spring — when rains ceased, roads dried, and
       military campaigns became feasible. The ESV rendering is
       widely accepted as the intended sense: ancient Near Eastern
       armies campaigned in spring and summer.

v.1  — KJV: "wasted the country" / ESV: "ravaged the country" —
       Hebrew "yashcheth" means to destroy, ruin, lay waste. Both
       translations convey systematic devastation of Ammonite
       territory. ESV's "ravaged" is slightly more vivid for
       modern readers; KJV's "wasted" carries the same force in
       its 17th-century usage.

v.3  — KJV: "cut them with saws, and with harrows of iron, and
       with axes" / ESV: "set them to labor with saws and iron
       picks and axes" — This is a significant translation
       difference. Hebrew "vayyasar bamgérah" can mean either
       "he cut with the saw" or "he set [them to work] with the
       saw." KJV reads execution/torture; ESV reads forced labor.
       The ESV interpretation is supported by the parallel in
       2 Samuel 12:31 and the context of David's massive building
       preparations. The Hebrew consonants allow both readings.

v.4  — KJV: "the children of the giant" / ESV: "the descendants of
       the giants" — Hebrew "ylidé harapha." "Rapha" (or Rephaim)
       refers to an ancient race of giant warriors. KJV's singular
       "the giant" could suggest one specific ancestor; ESV's
       plural "the giants" treats this as a clan designation. The
       Hebrew is ambiguous — "harapha" with the article could be
       either a personal name or a group identifier.

v.5  — KJV: "Elhanan the son of Jair slew Lahmi the brother of
       Goliath" / ESV: "Elhanan the son of Jair struck down Lahmi
       the brother of Goliath" — Both translations include "Lahmi
       the brother of" — clarifying the textual confusion in
       2 Samuel 21:19. The Hebrew here explicitly names the victim
       as "Lahmi" (lachmi) and identifies him as Goliath's brother,
       resolving the apparent contradiction with David killing
       Goliath in 1 Samuel 17.

v.6  — KJV: "whose fingers and toes were four and twenty, six on
       each hand, and six on each foot" / ESV: "who had six
       fingers on each hand and six toes on each foot, twenty-
       four in number" — Both translations convey the same
       information with different word order. The Hebrew describes
       polydactyly — a genetic condition associated with the
       Rephaim/giant lineage. The detail authenticates the account
       with specificity that a fabricator would not include.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Rabbah      — Capital city of the Ammonites, modern-day Amman,
             Jordan. Its full name was "Rabbath-Ammon" (great city
             of Ammon). Located at the headwaters of the Jabbok
             River, it controlled major trade routes. Its capture
             by Joab completed the Ammonite campaign begun in
             chapter 19. The city continued as a significant
             settlement through Hellenistic times (renamed
             Philadelphia under the Ptolemies).

Rephaim/    — An ancient race of exceptionally large warriors
Giants        associated with pre-Israelite Canaan. Related terms
             include Anakim, Emim, and Zamzummim (Deut. 2:10-11,
             20). Gath was a known center for this lineage — both
             Goliath and his relatives came from there. The
             systematic elimination of the Rephaim under David
             fulfilled what Joshua had begun (Joshua 11:21-22 notes
             giants survived only in Gaza, Gath, and Ashdod).

Talent      — A unit of weight in the ancient Near East, equivalent
             to approximately 75 pounds (34 kg). A talent of gold
             would be extraordinarily valuable — modern estimates
             suggest millions of dollars. The crown weighing a
             talent was likely a ceremonial object suspended above
             the throne rather than worn on the head, though some
             suggest it was placed temporarily on David's head for
             a coronation ceremony.

Gath        — One of the five major Philistine cities, home to
             Goliath and his relatives. Located in the Shephelah
             (lowlands) between the coastal plain and the Judean
             hills. The city's association with giants (Rephaim)
             suggests a warrior-aristocracy of unusual physical
             stature. David himself had once sought refuge there
             under King Achish (1 Samuel 27).

Weaver's    — A large wooden beam used in textile production. The
Beam          comparison describes a spear shaft of extraordinary
             thickness — far beyond normal military weaponry. Normal
             spears had shafts about 1 inch in diameter; a weaver's
             beam would be 2-3 inches thick. This indicates both
             the giant's enormous strength (to wield such a weapon)
             and the terror it inspired.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. God's editorial silence teaches as powerfully as His speech
   (v.1). The Chronicler's deliberate omission of Bathsheba-Uriah
   reminds us that God is not obligated to rehearse our forgiven
   sins perpetually. When God forgives, He removes transgressions
   "as far as the east is from the west" (Psalm 103:12). While
   Samuel records the sin for instructive purposes, Chronicles
   shows that God's purposes for David continued unbroken despite
   the failure. Your worst chapter does not define your entire
   story in God's economy. Grace covers, restores, and moves
   forward.

2. Leaders multiply their victories through those they train (v.8).
   "They fell by the hand of David, and by the hand of his
   servants." David's greatest legacy was not the giants he killed
   personally but the giant-killers he raised up. True leadership
   is not measured by personal accomplishments but by the
   accomplishments of those you equip. Are you raising up others
   who can fight battles you will not always be present to fight?
   Invest in people, train them in faith, show them how to face
   giants — and your influence will outlast your own strength.

3. Giants come in families (vv.4-7). Three separate giants from
   the same lineage appear — defeating one did not eliminate the
   others. Temptation works similarly: related sins cluster
   together. Defeat one form of pride, and another manifestation
   appears. Overcome one expression of lust, and a cousin temptation
   emerges. Do not be discouraged when new giants appear after old
   victories. Stay vigilant, stay armed, and recognize that the
   same faith that conquered the first will conquer the others.

4. Every giant has a weakness (vv.4-7). Despite their terrifying
   size, every giant fell to a normal-sized warrior empowered by
   God. No enemy is invincible. The six-fingered giant, Goliath's
   brother, Sippai — each was formidable, each was mortal. Whatever
   oversized problem towers over your life, it has a point of
   vulnerability. Faith does not deny the giant's size; faith
   affirms God's greater power. "Greater is he that is in you,
   than he that is in the world" (1 John 4:4).

5. Faithful service continues even when leadership fails (v.1).
   Joab led the army, besieged Rabbah, and completed the campaign
   while David remained in Jerusalem during his greatest moral
   failure. The work of God does not stop when leaders stumble.
   If you are serving under imperfect leadership (and all earthly
   leadership is imperfect), your faithfulness is to God's mission,
   not to human perfection. Continue your work, do your duty,
   serve the kingdom — God's purposes advance through faithful
   servants regardless of what the leader is doing privately.
