2 Samuel - Chapter 4
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Two captains assassinate Ish-bosheth in his bed and bring his head to David, expecting reward. David executes them for murdering an innocent man, again showing he will not profit from the blood of Saul's house.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Unknown, traditionally attributed to Nathan and Gad
(1 Chronicles 29:29). Originally one book with 1 Samuel in the Hebrew
Bible. The book covers David's entire reign — from his lament over
Saul's death to the purchase of the threshing floor for the future
Temple (approximately 1010-970 BC). Key themes: the Davidic covenant
(chapter 7), the consequences of sin even for the forgiven (chapters
11-20), God's faithfulness despite human failure, and the establishment
of the eternal throne fulfilled in Christ.

Historical Context: Chapter 4 records the final collapse of Saul's
house. With Abner dead (ch.3), Ish-bosheth has lost his only source
of strength. Two of his own captains — Rechab and Baanah, sons of
Rimmon the Beerothite — murder him in his bed during his noonday rest
and bring his head to David at Hebron, expecting reward. They invoke
God's name: "The LORD hath avenged my lord the king this day of Saul,
and of his seed" (v.8). But David, consistent with his treatment of
the Amalekite in chapter 1, refuses to reward those who murder for
political advantage. He executes them, mutilates their bodies as a
public warning, and gives Ish-bosheth's head an honorable burial in
Abner's tomb. The chapter also introduces Mephibosheth (v.4) —
Jonathan's crippled son — who will become important in chapter 9.
David's consistent principle is clear: he will not gain the throne
through the murder of his rivals. God will give him the kingdom in
God's way and God's time. Those who try to "help" God by eliminating
David's enemies find that David considers them criminals, not allies.

Ish-bosheth's Collapse (vv.1-4): When Ish-bosheth hears of Abner's
death, "his hands were feeble" (KJV) / "his courage failed" (ESV) —
Hebrew literally "his hands dropped." Without Abner, Ish-bosheth has
no military power, no political support, and no personal strength. All
Israel is "troubled" / "dismayed." The narrator then introduces two
captains of raiding bands — Rechab and Baanah — Benjamites from
Beeroth. A parenthetical note explains that the Beerothites fled to
Gittaim (their current status as sojourners). Verse 4 introduces
Mephibosheth — Jonathan's son, crippled at age five when his nurse
dropped him while fleeing after the news of Gilboa. This detail seems
parenthetical but is strategically placed: with Ish-bosheth about to
die, Mephibosheth is the last surviving heir of Saul's house — and he
is helpless. The house of Saul has no one left who can challenge David.

The Murder (vv.5-8): Rechab and Baanah come to Ish-bosheth's house
during the heat of the day — siesta time. They enter "as though they
would have fetched wheat" (KJV) — pretending to be on routine
business. They find Ish-bosheth sleeping on his bed and stab him in
the abdomen, behead him, and flee through the Arabah (Jordan Valley)
all night to reach David at Hebron. Their speech to David is
calculated: they call Saul David's "enemy, which sought thy life" and
claim "the LORD hath avenged my lord the king." They frame murder as
divine justice and expect David to see it the same way. They have
completely misread David's character.

David's Justice (vv.9-12): David's response echoes chapter 1. He
swears by "the LORD... who hath redeemed my soul out of all adversity"
— God is his deliverer, not human assassins. He cites the precedent
of the Amalekite: that man thought he brought good news of Saul's
death and was executed for it. "How much more," David argues, "when
wicked men have slain a righteous person in his own house upon his
bed?" David calls Ish-bosheth "righteous" — not in an absolute moral
sense, but in the sense that he was innocent of any crime deserving
death. He was murdered in his sleep by his own men. David commands
their execution, and their hands and feet are cut off (the instruments
of their crime — hands that killed, feet that carried them to the
deed) and displayed publicly at the pool of Hebron. Ish-bosheth's
head is buried honorably in Abner's tomb — David treats even his dead
rival with dignity.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Mahanaim to Hebron: The murderers of Ish-bosheth travel from Transjordan to David's capital — a journey of about 50 miles
- Beeroth: Hometown of the assassins Rechab and Baanah — a Gibeonite city in Benjamin's territory
- The pool at Hebron: Where David hangs the murderers' hands and feet as a public display of justice

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.9  — "As the LORD lives, who has redeemed my soul out of all
        adversity" — David's oath formula reveals his theology. God
        has delivered him from every danger — from Saul's spear, from
        Philistine territory, from the wilderness, from Ziklag's
        destruction. He does not need human assassins to accomplish
        what God has promised. His confidence is in God's redemption,
        not human scheming.

v.11 — "How much more, when wicked men have slain a righteous person
        in his own house upon his bed? shall I not therefore now
        require his blood of your hand?" — David's logic is
        devastating. If he executed the Amalekite who merely claimed
        to kill Saul (a warrior on a battlefield), how much more must
        he punish men who actually murdered a sleeping, defenseless
        man in his own home? The escalation of wickedness demands
        escalation of justice.

         blood be shed" — the foundational principle of capital justice
         that David applies.

         a warning to others. The mutilation of hands and feet
         symbolizes the removal of the instruments of crime.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/2-samuel-4/
  Guzik emphasizes that Rechab and Baanah completely misread David's
  character. They assumed David would reward them for eliminating his
  rival, but David consistently refused to benefit from the murder of
  God's anointed or their descendants. Guzik also notes the
  introduction of Mephibosheth as a narrative setup for chapter 9's
  beautiful display of covenant loyalty.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "These murderers thought to please David, but they knew him not.
  He who would not stretch forth his hand against Saul when he had
  him in his power would never reward those who slew Saul's son in
  his bed. Let all men learn that God needs no help from wickedness
  to accomplish His purposes. Those who do evil that good may come
  shall find that their evil returns upon their own heads."
TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1  — KJV: "his hands were feeble" / ESV: "his courage failed" —
       Hebrew "raphah yadav" = literally "his hands dropped/sank."
       This is an idiom for losing courage or strength. KJV is more
       literal (feeble hands); ESV interprets the idiom (failed
       courage). Both are correct — the physical image conveys the
       emotional reality.

v.4  — KJV: "lame of his feet" / ESV: "crippled in his feet" —
       Hebrew "nekeh raglayim" = struck/smitten in the feet. KJV
       "lame" and ESV "crippled" both convey permanent disability.
       The injury occurred when his nurse dropped him while fleeing —
       likely broken bones that healed improperly.

v.6  — KJV: "as though they would have fetched wheat" / ESV: "as if
       to get wheat" — Both translations agree. The murderers used a
       routine errand as their cover story to enter the house without
       suspicion. Some Hebrew manuscripts and the Septuagint read
       differently here, but the sense is the same: they entered
       under false pretenses.

v.7  — KJV: "gat them away through the plain all night" / ESV: "went
       by the way of the Arabah all night" — KJV "plain" is generic;
       ESV "Arabah" preserves the geographic term for the Jordan Rift
       Valley. They traveled the low route along the Jordan to reach
       Hebron — a long night's journey to present their "trophy."

v.11 — KJV: "a righteous person" / ESV: "a righteous man" — Hebrew
       "ish tsaddiq." David calls Ish-bosheth "righteous" — meaning
       innocent of any capital crime, not morally perfect. He was
       murdered without cause, in his own home, while sleeping. The
       term emphasizes the injustice of the act.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Beerothite     — A person from Beeroth, one of the four Gibeonite
                 cities that made a treaty with Joshua (Joshua 9:17).
                 Though originally Hivite/Gibeonite, Beeroth was
                 absorbed into Benjamin's territory. Rechab and Baanah
                 were Benjamites — from Saul's own tribe — making
                 their betrayal of Ish-bosheth especially treacherous.

Mephibosheth   — "From the mouth of shame" — originally Merib-baal
                 ("contender with Baal" — 1 Chronicles 8:34). Like
                 Ish-bosheth, his name was altered by scribes. He was
                 Jonathan's son, crippled at age five, and the last
                 significant heir of Saul's house. His introduction
                 here prepares for chapter 9.

The Arabah     — The Jordan Rift Valley — the deep geological trench
                 running from the Sea of Galilee south through the
                 Dead Sea and beyond. A natural travel corridor used
                 by the murderers for their nighttime escape route
                 from Mahanaim to Hebron.

Pool of Hebron — A public gathering place and water source in Hebron
                 where the mutilated bodies of Rechab and Baanah were
                 displayed. Public execution sites served as warnings
                 — visible to all who came for water.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. God does not need our sin to accomplish His purposes (vv.8-11).
   Rechab and Baanah thought they were doing God's work — "The LORD
   hath avenged my lord the king." But God never commissions murder,
   treachery, or wickedness to fulfill His promises. When we are
   tempted to use sinful means to achieve "good" ends, we have
   misunderstood both God's character and His sovereignty.

2. Consistency of character builds trust (vv.9-11). David's response
   to Rechab and Baanah is identical in principle to his response to
   the Amalekite in chapter 1. He is the same man in every situation
   — he will not profit from the murder of his rivals. This
   consistency is what eventually wins all Israel's trust. People
   follow leaders whose character is predictable and principled.

3. The helpless are not forgotten (v.4). Mephibosheth is introduced
   as crippled, orphaned, and politically irrelevant — the last
   remnant of a fallen house. Yet God has not forgotten him. Chapter 9
   will reveal David's extraordinary kindness to this helpless man
   "for Jonathan's sake." God sees those the world overlooks.

4. Justice must be proportional and principled (v.12). David executes
   the murderers but buries Ish-bosheth's head with honor. He
   distinguishes between the guilty (who deserve punishment) and the
   victim (who deserves dignity). Biblical justice is never mere
   vengeance — it is measured, principled, and honors the image of
   God in every person.

5. Those who misuse God's name for evil face judgment (v.8). Rechab
   and Baanah invoked the LORD's name to justify murder: "The LORD
   hath avenged..." Using God's name to sanctify wickedness is a
   violation of the third commandment — taking God's name in vain.
   God will not be made a partner in human evil.
