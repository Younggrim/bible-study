2 Samuel - Chapter 24
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
David sinfully numbers the people, and God sends a plague that kills 70,000. David buys the threshing floor of Araunah to build an altar, and the plague stops—this site will become the Temple mount.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Unknown, traditionally attributed to Nathan and Gad
(1 Chronicles 29:29). Originally one book with 1 Samuel in the
Hebrew Bible. The book covers David's entire reign — from his
lament over Saul's death to the purchase of the threshing floor
for the future Temple (approximately 1010-970 BC). Key themes:
the Davidic covenant (chapter 7), the consequences of sin even
for the forgiven (chapters 11-20), God's faithfulness despite
human failure, and the establishment of the eternal throne
fulfilled in Christ.

Historical Context: Chapter 24 is the final chapter of 2 Samuel
and one of the most theologically complex passages in the Old
Testament. It records David's sin of numbering the people, the
resulting plague that kills 70,000, and David's purchase of
Araunah's threshing floor — the site where Solomon would later
build the Temple (2 Chronicles 3:1). The chapter raises profound
questions about divine sovereignty and human responsibility: the
text says God "moved David" (KJV) or "incited David" (ESV) to
take the census, yet David is held guilty for it. The parallel
account in 1 Chronicles 21:1 says "Satan stood up against Israel,
and provoked David to number Israel." Both are true: God
permitted Satan to tempt David, and David freely chose to yield.
The sin was not in counting people per se (Moses conducted
censuses by God's command in Numbers 1 and 26) but in David's
motive — pride, self-reliance, and trust in military strength
rather than in God. Even Joab recognized the sin and objected.
The chapter ends with mercy: God stops the plague at the
threshing floor, David purchases the site, offers sacrifice, and
the plague ceases. This location — Mount Moriah — is where
Abraham offered Isaac (Genesis 22:2) and where the Temple would
stand. The book of 2 Samuel thus ends where the Temple begins:
at the intersection of judgment, sacrifice, and mercy.

David's Census — The Sin of Pride (vv.1-9): God's anger burns
against Israel (the reason is not stated — perhaps accumulated
national sin), and He permits David to be tempted to number the
people. David commands Joab to conduct a military census "from
Dan even to Beer-sheba." Joab objects — recognizing this as
sinful pride — but David overrules him. The census takes nine
months and twenty days, covering the entire land. The result:
800,000 fighting men in Israel and 500,000 in Judah (1.3 million
total). The numbers themselves reveal the sin: David wanted to
know his military strength, trusting in numbers rather than God.

David's Conviction and the Three Choices (vv.10-14): David's
heart "smote him" — his conscience convicted him immediately
after the census was complete. He confesses: "I have sinned
greatly... I have done very foolishly" (v.10). God sends the
prophet Gad with three options for judgment: seven years of
famine (KJV; three years in ESV/Chronicles), three months
fleeing from enemies, or three days of pestilence. David's
response reveals profound theology: "Let us fall now into the
hand of the LORD; for his mercies are great: and let me not fall
into the hand of man" (v.14). David chooses God's direct
judgment over human cruelty — trusting that even God's wrath is
tempered by mercy.

The Plague and God's Mercy (vv.15-17): The LORD sends pestilence,
and 70,000 die from Dan to Beersheba. When the destroying angel
reaches Jerusalem and stretches out his hand to destroy it, "the
LORD repented him of the evil" (KJV) / "the LORD relented from
the calamity" (ESV) and commands: "It is enough: stay now thine
hand." The angel stops at Araunah's threshing floor. David sees
the angel and intercedes: "These sheep, what have they done? Let
thine hand be against me" (v.17) — a shepherd's prayer,
offering himself for his flock.

The Altar and the Sacrifice (vv.18-25): Gad instructs David to
build an altar on Araunah's threshing floor. Araunah offers to
give David the site, the oxen, and the wood for free. David
refuses with the famous declaration: "Neither will I offer burnt
offerings unto the LORD my God of that which doth cost me
nothing" (v.24). He pays fifty shekels of silver, builds the
altar, offers burnt offerings and peace offerings, and "the LORD
was intreated for the land, and the plague was stayed" (v.25).

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- The census covers all Israel: "from Dan to Beer-sheba" — the full north-south extent of the kingdom
- The route: East across Jordan to Aroer (Gad), north to Dan, west to Sidon, south to Beer-sheba — a circuit of the entire land
- The threshing floor of Araunah the Jebusite: On Mount Moriah in Jerusalem — the future site of Solomon's Temple

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.1  — 1 Chronicles 21:1 — "Satan stood up against Israel, and
       provoked David to number Israel" — the parallel account
       attributing the incitement to Satan.
v.1  — Job 1:12 — God permitting Satan to act within limits — the
       same theological framework of divine sovereignty and
       satanic agency.
v.14 — Lamentations 3:32-33 — "Though he cause grief, yet will he
       have compassion according to the multitude of his mercies.
       For he does not afflict willingly."
v.16 — Genesis 22:2 — Mount Moriah — where Abraham offered Isaac,
       the same location as Araunah's threshing floor and the
       future Temple.
v.16 — 2 Chronicles 3:1 — "Solomon began to build the house of
       the LORD at Jerusalem in mount Moriah... in the place that
       David had prepared in the threshingfloor of Ornan the
       Jebusite."
v.17 — John 10:11 — "The good shepherd gives his life for the
       sheep" — David's intercession foreshadows Christ's
       substitutionary offering.
v.24 — Romans 12:1 — "Present your bodies a living sacrifice" —
       the New Testament call to costly worship.
v.25 — Hebrews 9:22 — "Without shedding of blood is no
       remission" — the principle fulfilled at this threshing
       floor and ultimately at Calvary.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/2-samuel-24/
  Guzik emphasizes the theological tension of verse 1 — God
  inciting David yet holding him responsible. He explains this
  through the lens of divine permission: God allowed Satan to
  tempt David as judgment on Israel's accumulated sin. Guzik also
  highlights the significance of the threshing floor location:
  where judgment stopped, the Temple would rise — making the
  place of near-destruction the place of perpetual mercy.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "'I will not offer burnt offerings to the LORD my God of that
  which does cost me nothing.' Here is a royal word, worthy of
  the man after God's own heart. Cheap religion is worth exactly
  what it costs — nothing. That which costs us no sacrifice, no
  self-denial, no effort, no tears — what is it worth to God? He
  gave His only Son; shall we give Him our leftovers? David would
  not insult God with a costless offering. Neither should we. Let
  our worship, our service, our giving, our obedience cost us
  something — for only then does it truly honor the God who gave
  everything."
REFLECTION
------------------------------------
1. Pride is subtle and deadly (vv.1-4). David's sin was not
   obvious immorality but quiet pride — wanting to count his
   military strength, trusting in numbers rather than God. Even
   Joab saw the danger. The most dangerous sins are often the
   most respectable: self-reliance, desire for control, trust in
   resources rather than in God. Examine your heart for the pride
   that masquerades as prudence.

2. Quick repentance is the mark of a godly heart (v.10). David's
   heart "smote him" immediately. He did not wait for a prophet
   to confront him (as with Bathsheba) — his own conscience
   convicted him. Spiritual maturity is measured not by the
   absence of sin but by the speed of repentance. When the Holy
   Spirit convicts, respond immediately.

3. God's mercy is greater than His judgment (v.14). David chose
   to fall into God's hands rather than man's — because he knew
   God's character. Even in wrath, God remembers mercy (Habakkuk
   3:2). This is not presumption but faith: trusting that the
   God who judges is also the God who saves. His mercy is not
   weakness; it is His glory.

4. True worship costs something (v.24). David refused a free
   offering. Worship that costs nothing honors nothing. This
   challenges our comfortable Christianity: Do we give God our
   best or our leftovers? Do we sacrifice time, comfort, money,
   and reputation for Him — or only what is convenient? The cross
   cost God everything; our response should reflect that.

5. Where judgment stops, mercy builds (vv.16, 25). The plague
   stopped at Araunah's threshing floor. On that exact spot,
   Solomon built the Temple — the place where God's mercy would
   be dispensed for generations through sacrifice. God transforms
   places of judgment into places of grace. The cross is the
   ultimate example: the place of God's wrath became the place of
   the world's salvation.
