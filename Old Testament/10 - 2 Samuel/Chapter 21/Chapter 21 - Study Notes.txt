2 Samuel - Chapter 21
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A three-year famine reveals Saul's broken covenant with the Gibeonites; David allows seven of Saul's descendants to be executed in atonement. David's warriors also fight Philistine giants in various battles.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Unknown, traditionally attributed to Nathan and Gad
(1 Chronicles 29:29). Originally one book with 1 Samuel in the
Hebrew Bible. The book covers David's entire reign — from his
lament over Saul's death to the purchase of the threshing floor
for the future Temple (approximately 1010-970 BC). Key themes:
the Davidic covenant (chapter 7), the consequences of sin even
for the forgiven (chapters 11-20), God's faithfulness despite
human failure, and the establishment of the eternal throne
fulfilled in Christ.

Historical Context: Chapters 21-24 form an appendix to 2 Samuel,
arranged thematically rather than chronologically. These chapters
look back over David's reign and gather material that illustrates
key theological themes: the consequences of covenant-breaking,
God's deliverance, the valor of David's warriors, and the
sovereignty of God over judgment and mercy. Chapter 21 addresses
two distinct subjects: the famine caused by Saul's violation of
the Gibeonite covenant, and the exploits of David's warriors
against Philistine giants. The Gibeonite episode likely occurred
early in David's reign, while the giant-slaying accounts span
various periods. The theological connection between the two
halves is the theme of bloodguilt and divine justice — God holds
nations accountable for broken covenants, and He raises up
warriors to defeat His enemies.

The Famine and the Gibeonites (vv.1-14): A three-year famine
strikes Israel, and David seeks the LORD's face. God reveals the
cause: Saul's violation of the ancient covenant with the
Gibeonites (Joshua 9:3-27). Though the Gibeonites had deceived
Joshua, Israel had sworn an oath before the LORD to spare them.
Saul, in misguided "zeal" for Israel, had attempted to
exterminate them — a sin God had not forgotten. The Gibeonites
request seven of Saul's descendants, and David delivers them to
be executed "before the LORD" at Gibeah. David spares
Mephibosheth (Jonathan's son) because of his oath to Jonathan.
Rizpah's vigil over the bodies (v.10) — protecting them from
birds and beasts from barley harvest until the rains came — is
one of the most poignant scenes in Scripture. Her devotion moves
David to gather the bones of Saul and Jonathan from Jabesh-gilead
and give them proper burial in the family tomb. After this, "God
was intreated for the land" (v.14 KJV).

Wars Against the Philistine Giants (vv.15-22): Four battles
against descendants of the giants in Gath are recorded. David
himself grows weary in battle and nearly falls to Ishbi-benob,
but Abishai rescues him. David's men then forbid him from going
to battle again, calling him "the light of Israel" (v.17 KJV) or
"the lamp of Israel" (v.17 ESV). Three other giants are slain:
Saph by Sibbechai, the brother of Goliath by Elhanan, and a
six-fingered giant by Jonathan (David's nephew). The summary
states: "These four were born to the giant in Gath, and fell by
the hand of David, and by the hand of his servants" (v.22).

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Gibeon: Where David hands over seven of Saul's descendants to the Gibeonites for execution — the central highlands
- Battles with the Philistines: Gob (likely Gezer area) and Gath — in the western lowlands and coastal plain
- David retrieves Saul and Jonathan's bones from Jabesh-gilead and buries them in Benjamin (Zela) — closing the Saul narrative

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "And David inquired of the LORD. And the LORD answered, It
        is for Saul, and for his bloody house, because he slew the
        Gibeonites." — God holds nations accountable for broken
        covenants, even generations later. The sin was Saul's, but
        the consequences fell on the nation. This reveals a
        principle of corporate responsibility: a leader's sin
        brings judgment on the people he leads. David's response
        is exemplary — he seeks God's face rather than assuming a
        natural explanation for the famine.

v.6  — "Let seven men of his sons be delivered to us, and we
        will hang them up to the LORD in Gibeah of Saul, whom
        the LORD did choose." — The number seven signifies
        completeness of justice. The execution takes place at
        Gibeah — Saul's own city — and "before the LORD,"
        indicating this is not mere vengeance but a judicial act
        of atonement for covenant violation.

v.14 — "And after that God was intreated for the land." — The
        resolution comes only after justice is satisfied and
        proper burial is given. God responds to the plea for the
        land when the covenant violation has been addressed. This
        foreshadows the greater atonement: God's wrath is
        satisfied only through the shedding of blood (Hebrews
        9:22).

v.17 — "You shall go no more out with us to battle, that you
        quench not the light of Israel." — David's men recognize
        that the king's life is more valuable than one warrior's
        contribution. David is "the lamp of Israel" — the one
        through whom God's covenant promises flow. His death would
        extinguish the nation's hope. This points to Christ, the
        true Light of the world, whose life is infinitely
        precious.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/2-samuel-21/
  Guzik notes that the famine was God's way of bringing an
  unresolved sin to light. Saul's attack on the Gibeonites was
  never recorded elsewhere in Scripture — it was a hidden sin that
  God nonetheless remembered. Guzik emphasizes that Rizpah's
  vigil is a picture of faithful, persistent love that moves even
  a king to action.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "God's justice sleeps not, though it may seem to slumber. The
  sin of Saul was committed years before, yet the famine came in
  David's day. Let no man think that because judgment is not
  executed speedily, therefore God has forgotten. He remembers
  every broken covenant, every violated oath, every drop of
  innocent blood — and in His own time, He will require it. But
  blessed be His name, there is an atonement that satisfies
  divine justice forever: the blood of Christ, which speaks
  better things than the blood of Abel."
TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1  — KJV: "David enquired of the LORD" / ESV: "David sought
       the face of the LORD" — Hebrew "baqash eth-penei Yahweh"
       = literally "sought the face of the LORD." The ESV is more
       literal here. The KJV simplifies to "enquired," which
       captures the idea of seeking an answer but loses the
       intimate imagery of seeking God's face — His presence and
       favor.

v.1  — KJV: "for his bloody house" / ESV: "There is bloodguilt
       on Saul and on his house" — Hebrew "el-Shaul v'el-beit
       ha-damim" = literally "concerning Saul and the house of
       blood." ESV "bloodguilt" is a more precise legal term; KJV
       "bloody house" is more vivid but less technically precise.

v.8  — KJV: "Michal the daughter of Saul" / ESV: "Merab the
       daughter of Saul" — This is a significant textual variant.
       The Hebrew Masoretic text reads "Michal," but most scholars
       believe this is a scribal error for "Merab," since Michal
       had no children (2 Samuel 6:23) and Merab was married to
       Adriel (1 Samuel 18:19). The ESV follows the correction;
       the KJV follows the Masoretic text but adds "brought up
       for" rather than "bore to" to harmonize.

v.9  — KJV: "hanged them in the hill" / ESV: "hanged them on the
       mountain" — Hebrew "ba-har" = on the mountain/hill. The
       difference is minor — KJV uses "hill" while ESV uses
       "mountain." The Hebrew word can mean either, depending on
       context.

v.16 — KJV: "the sons of the giant" / ESV: "the descendants of
       the giants" — Hebrew "yalid ha-raphah" = "born of the
       Raphah" (singular). "Raphah" may be a proper name (an
       ancestor) or a designation meaning "the giant." ESV
       pluralizes to "giants" for clarity; KJV keeps the singular
       "the giant," which may preserve the sense of a specific
       ancestral line.

v.19 — KJV: "slew the brother of Goliath the Gittite" / ESV:
       "struck down Goliath the Gittite" — This is a major
       textual difference. The KJV adds "the brother of" (in
       italics, indicating it is not in the Hebrew) to harmonize
       with 1 Chronicles 20:5, which says Elhanan slew "Lahmi the
       brother of Goliath." The ESV follows the Hebrew text as
       written. The Chronicles passage likely preserves the
       original reading.

v.1 — ASV: uses "Jehovah" for KJV's "LORD" (Hebrew YHWH).
       NET: "for three consecutive years. So" for KJV's "in the days of"
       WEB: uses "Yahweh" for KJV's "LORD" (Hebrew YHWH).
v.8 — ASV: "bare to" for KJV's "brought up for"
       NET: adds "Armoni and Mephibosheth,"
       WEB: "bore to" for KJV's "bare unto"
v.9 — ASV: uses "Jehovah" for KJV's "LORD" (Hebrew YHWH).
       NET: "Lord. The seven of them died together;" for KJV's "LORD: and"
       WEB: uses "Yahweh" for KJV's "LORD" (Hebrew YHWH).
v.16 — ASV: "and Ishbibenob, who" for KJV's "And Ishbi-benob, which"
       NET: "weapon. He had said that he would kill" for KJV's "sword, thought to have slain"
       WEB: "and Ishbibenob, who" for KJV's "And Ishbi-benob, which"
v.19 — ASV: "Jaareoregim the Beth-lehemite" for KJV's "Jaare-oregim, a Bethlehemite,"
       NET: "Philistines in Gob. On that occasion" for KJV's "Philistines, where"
       WEB: "Philistines at Gob; and" for KJV's "Philistines, where"

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Gibeonites    — Descendants of the Hivite inhabitants of Gibeon
              who deceived Joshua into making a peace covenant
              (Joshua 9). Though the covenant was obtained by
              deception, it was sworn before the LORD and remained
              binding. They were assigned as woodcutters and water
              carriers for the tabernacle. Saul's attempt to
              destroy them violated a 400-year-old oath.

Bloodguilt    — Hebrew "damim" (bloods, plural). A legal and
              theological concept indicating guilt incurred by
              shedding innocent blood. Bloodguilt pollutes the
              land and can only be atoned for by the blood of the
              guilty party (Numbers 35:33) or by divine provision
              of a substitute.

Rizpah        — Saul's concubine, mother of Armoni and
              Mephibosheth (not Jonathan's son). Her vigil over
              the bodies of her executed sons — protecting them
              from scavengers for months — is an extraordinary
              display of maternal devotion. Her faithfulness
              shamed David into giving proper burial to Saul's
              entire family.

Raphah/Giants — Hebrew "ha-raphah" — possibly a proper name for
              an ancestor of the Philistine giants of Gath, or a
              designation meaning "the giant." Related to the
              Rephaim, an ancient race of large people mentioned
              throughout the Old Testament (Genesis 14:5,
              Deuteronomy 2:11). Four descendants are killed in
              this chapter.

Light/Lamp    — Hebrew "ner" = lamp. David is called "the lamp of
of Israel       Israel" — the one whose life sustains the nation's
              hope and covenant future. If David dies, the
              Davidic covenant promises are endangered. This
              imagery recurs in 1 Kings 11:36 and 15:4, where God
              preserves a "lamp" for David in Jerusalem.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Hidden sins have consequences (v.1). Saul's attack on the
   Gibeonites is never recorded elsewhere — it was a secret sin.
   But God knew, and years later the land suffered for it. We
   cannot hide sin from God. What is concealed will eventually be
   revealed, and unconfessed sin brings consequences not only on
   ourselves but on those connected to us.

2. Covenants matter to God (vv.1-2). The oath to the Gibeonites
   was made 400 years earlier by Joshua — yet God still held
   Israel accountable for its violation. God takes vows, oaths,
   and covenants with absolute seriousness. Our word matters. Our
   commitments matter. God remembers what we promise, even when
   we forget.

3. Faithful love in the face of injustice (v.10). Rizpah could
   not prevent the execution of her sons, but she could honor
   them in death. Her months-long vigil — exposed to sun, rain,
   and wild animals — is a picture of love that will not let go.
   Sometimes faithfulness means simply being present in suffering,
   even when we cannot change the outcome.

4. Aging does not disqualify, but wisdom knows its limits
   (vv.15-17). David grew weary in battle — the great warrior was
   aging. His men wisely told him to stay back. There is no shame
   in recognizing our limitations. Wisdom knows when to step back
   and let others carry the fight, while still supporting them
   with prayer, counsel, and presence.

5. God raises up warriors for every generation (vv.18-22). David
   could no longer fight the giants himself, but God had prepared
   others — Abishai, Sibbechai, Elhanan, Jonathan. The work of
   God does not depend on one person. He always has the next
   generation ready. Our job is to train them, release them, and
   trust God with the outcome.
