2 Samuel - Chapter 3
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Abner defects to David but is murdered by Joab in revenge for his brother Asahel. David publicly mourns Abner and distances himself from the killing, winning the people's trust with his integrity.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Unknown, traditionally attributed to Nathan and Gad
(1 Chronicles 29:29). Originally one book with 1 Samuel in the Hebrew
Bible. The book covers David's entire reign — from his lament over
Saul's death to the purchase of the threshing floor for the future
Temple (approximately 1010-970 BC). Key themes: the Davidic covenant
(chapter 7), the consequences of sin even for the forgiven (chapters
11-20), God's faithfulness despite human failure, and the establishment
of the eternal throne fulfilled in Christ.

Historical Context: Chapter 3 narrates the collapse of Saul's house
and the treacherous murder of Abner. The civil war between David and
Ish-bosheth continues, but the momentum is entirely with David — he
grows "stronger and stronger" while Saul's house grows "weaker and
weaker" (v.1). The chapter opens with David's sons born at Hebron —
a list that introduces Amnon and Absalom, whose future sins will
devastate David's household. The central drama is Abner's defection:
after a quarrel with Ish-bosheth over Saul's concubine Rizpah, Abner
offers to bring all Israel to David. David agrees but demands the
return of Michal (his first wife, taken from him by Saul). Abner
negotiates with the elders of Israel and Benjamin, then comes to
Hebron to seal the covenant. David sends him away in peace. But Joab,
returning from a raid, is furious — both because Abner killed his
brother Asahel (2:23) and because Abner's alliance threatens Joab's
position as David's chief commander. Joab lures Abner back and murders
him "under the fifth rib" in the gate of Hebron. David's response is
immediate and public: he curses Joab's house, commands mourning,
follows the bier, weeps at the grave, composes a lament, and fasts
until sundown. All Israel recognizes that David had no part in Abner's
death. Yet David confesses his weakness: "these men the sons of
Zeruiah be too hard for me" (v.39) — a tension that will persist
throughout his reign.

David's Sons at Hebron (vv.1-5): The narrator lists six sons born to
David during his seven-and-a-half-year reign at Hebron, each by a
different wife. This signals David's growing household and political
alliances (Maacah was daughter of a Geshurite king). But the list also
foreshadows tragedy: Amnon (firstborn) will rape his half-sister Tamar
(ch.13); Absalom (third) will murder Amnon and rebel against David
(chs.13-18); Adonijah (fourth) will attempt to seize the throne from
Solomon (1 Kings 1). David's multiple marriages, while culturally
acceptable, plant seeds of rivalry and dysfunction.

Abner's Defection (vv.6-21): Abner has been "making himself strong"
in Saul's house — consolidating his own power. When Ish-bosheth
accuses him of taking Saul's concubine Rizpah (a claim to royal
prerogative — see 16:21-22), Abner explodes with rage. His response
reveals that he has always known God's will: "as the LORD hath sworn
to David... to set up the throne of David over Israel and over Judah,
from Dan even to Beer-sheba" (vv.9-10). Abner has been fighting
against God's revealed purpose — and now, offended by Ish-bosheth, he
switches sides out of anger rather than conviction. David accepts the
alliance but demands Michal's return — both a personal right (he paid
the bride-price) and a political statement (connecting himself to
Saul's house). The poignant detail of Paltiel weeping as Michal is
taken from him (v.16) reminds us that political maneuvering always
has human costs.

Joab Murders Abner (vv.22-30): Joab returns to find that David has
made peace with Abner — the man who killed his brother Asahel. Joab
confronts David, accusing Abner of espionage (v.25). Then, without
David's knowledge, Joab sends messengers to bring Abner back from the
well of Sirah. He takes Abner aside "in the gate" — a public place
where Abner would feel safe — and stabs him in the abdomen. The
narrator gives two motives: blood vengeance for Asahel (v.27) and
(implicitly) Joab's fear of losing his position to Abner. This murder
in a city of refuge (Hebron was a Levitical city of refuge — Joshua
21:13) makes it especially heinous.

David's Public Mourning (vv.31-39): David's response is emphatic and
multi-layered: he declares his innocence before God (v.28), curses
Joab's house with disease, poverty, and violent death (v.29), commands
public mourning (v.31), personally follows the bier (v.31), weeps at
the grave (v.32), composes a lament (vv.33-34), and fasts until
sundown (v.35). His lament asks: "Died Abner as a fool dieth?" — No!
Abner was not defeated in battle; he was murdered by treachery. His
hands were not bound, his feet not fettered — he was killed by
deception, not by superior force. David's final words reveal his
frustration: "I am this day weak, though anointed king; and these men
the sons of Zeruiah be too hard for me" (v.39). David cannot punish
Joab without losing his military commander and alienating Joab's
supporters. He commits the matter to God: "the LORD shall reward the
doer of evil according to his wickedness."

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Hebron: Where Abner negotiates defection to David — David's capital and the political center of Judah
- Mahanaim to Hebron: Abner travels west across the Jordan and south to negotiate with David
- The well of Sirah: Where Joab's messengers catch Abner and bring him back — just north of Hebron

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "David waxed stronger and stronger, and the house of Saul
        waxed weaker and weaker." — The summary statement of the civil
        war period. God's purpose cannot be stopped — David's rise is
        inevitable because God has ordained it. Those who oppose God's
        will always grow weaker, even if the process takes time.

v.9-10 — "As the LORD has sworn to David, even so I do to him; to
          translate the kingdom from the house of Saul, and to set up
          the throne of David over Israel and over Judah, from Dan
          even to Beer-sheba." — Abner's confession reveals that he
          has always known God's will. He fought against it for
          personal advantage and now switches sides for personal
          offense. His knowledge condemns him — he opposed what he
          knew to be God's purpose.

v.33-34 — "Died Abner as a fool dieth? Your hands were not bound, nor
           your feet put into fetters: as a man falls before wicked
           men, so fellest you." — David's lament for Abner. A "fool"
           in Hebrew (nabal) is one who brings destruction on himself
           through moral failure. David says Abner did NOT die as a
           fool — he was not defeated by his own folly but murdered by
           treachery. He fell "before wicked men" — Joab and Abishai.

v.39 — "I am this day weak, though anointed king; and these men the
        sons of Zeruiah be too hard for me." — David's honest
        confession of political weakness. Being God's anointed does
        not mean having unlimited power. David faces the tension every
        leader knows: the people you need are sometimes the people who
        cause you the most grief. He entrusts justice to God.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/2-samuel-3/
  Guzik notes that Abner's defection was motivated by personal offense,
  not genuine repentance or conviction about God's will. He had known
  all along that God had sworn the kingdom to David (vv.9-10) but
  fought against it until it became personally inconvenient. Guzik
  also emphasizes David's genuine grief for Abner and his inability
  to punish Joab — a weakness that would have long-term consequences.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "David said, 'These men the sons of Zeruiah be too hard for me.'
  Even the king after God's own heart found himself unable to execute
  justice against powerful men in his own camp. This is the burden of
  leadership — sometimes the instruments God uses are themselves
  deeply flawed, and the leader must wait upon God for the resolution
  he cannot achieve by his own hand. David committed the matter to
  God, and God did not forget."
TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.6  — KJV: "Abner made himself strong for the house of Saul" / ESV:
       "Abner was making himself strong in the house of Saul" —
       Hebrew "mitchazzeq" = strengthening himself. Both translations
       capture the idea, but the preposition differs: KJV "for" (on
       behalf of) vs. ESV "in" (within, gaining power within). ESV
       better captures the self-serving nature — Abner was building
       his own power base using Saul's house as his vehicle.

v.8  — KJV: "Am I a dog's head, which against Judah do shew kindness
       this day unto the house of Saul" / ESV: "Am I a dog's head of
       Judah?" — The Hebrew is difficult and translations differ
       significantly. KJV reads it as: "Am I a dog's head [worthless
       thing] that, while opposing Judah, I show loyalty to Saul's
       house?" ESV reads: "Am I a dog's head belonging to Judah?" —
       i.e., am I a worthless Judahite traitor? The KJV reading makes
       better contextual sense.

v.29 — KJV: "that leaneth on a staff" / ESV: "who holds a spindle" —
       Hebrew "machaziq b'pelek." KJV "staff" suggests a cripple who
       needs support; ESV "spindle" suggests effeminacy (doing women's
       work). Most modern scholars favor "spindle" — David curses
       Joab's house with disease, disability, effeminacy, violent
       death, and poverty. ESV is likely more accurate here.

v.33 — KJV: "Died Abner as a fool dieth?" / ESV: "Should Abner die
       as a fool dies?" — Both are valid renderings of the Hebrew.
       KJV presents it as a statement of outrage; ESV as a rhetorical
       question. The meaning is the same: Abner's death was unworthy
       of him — he was murdered, not defeated.

v.39 — KJV: "I am this day weak, though anointed king" / ESV: "And I
       was gentle today, though anointed king" — Hebrew "rakh" = soft,
       tender, weak, gentle. KJV "weak" emphasizes David's political
       inability to act; ESV "gentle" emphasizes David's character
       choice. Both meanings are present — David is both unable and
       unwilling to match Joab's violence.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Concubine      — A secondary wife with legal status but lower rank
                 than a primary wife. A king's concubines were part of
                 his royal household; taking them was a claim to his
                 throne (see 16:21-22, 1 Kings 2:22). Ish-bosheth's
                 accusation implies Abner is making a power grab.

Sons of        — Joab, Abishai, and Asahel — David's nephews through
Zeruiah          his sister Zeruiah. They were fierce warriors but
                 often acted with excessive violence that complicated
                 David's reign. David's repeated complaint that they
                 were "too hard" for him reveals the tension between
                 needing their military skill and restraining their
                 brutality.

City of        — Hebron was designated a city of refuge (Joshua 21:13)
Refuge           where those who killed accidentally could find
                 protection from blood avengers. Joab's deliberate
                 murder of Abner in Hebron was a double violation —
                 murder and desecration of a sacred city's purpose.

From Dan to    — The standard expression for all of Israel's territory
Beer-sheba       — from the northernmost city (Dan) to the
                 southernmost (Beer-sheba). Abner acknowledges that
                 God's promise to David encompasses the entire nation.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Knowing God's will is not the same as doing it (vv.9-10). Abner
   knew all along that God had sworn the kingdom to David — yet he
   fought against it for years. Only when personally offended did he
   switch sides. Knowledge without obedience is rebellion. How often
   do we know what God wants and delay until circumstances force our
   hand?

2. Personal offense is a poor motive for doing right (vv.7-12). Abner
   finally does the right thing (supporting David) for the wrong
   reason (anger at Ish-bosheth). God can use impure motives to
   accomplish His purposes, but the person acting from wrong motives
   receives no credit for righteousness. Do the right thing for the
   right reason.

3. Violence begets violence (vv.27, 30). Abner killed Asahel in
   battle (reluctantly). Joab murdered Abner in "peace." The cycle of
   vengeance escalates — each act of violence produces a worse
   response. Only God can break the cycle, which is why David commits
   justice to Him (v.39) rather than retaliating against Joab.

4. Leaders sometimes cannot fix everything immediately (v.39). David
   was anointed king but could not punish his own general. Leadership
   does not mean omnipotence. Sometimes the godly response is to
   acknowledge weakness, commit the matter to God, and wait for His
   timing. David's patience here is not cowardice — it is faith.

5. Public integrity matters (vv.36-37). David's genuine grief
   convinced all Israel that he had no part in Abner's murder. His
   character was so consistent that the people trusted him even in a
   situation that could have looked suspicious. A lifetime of
   integrity builds a reputation that survives even the worst
   circumstances.
