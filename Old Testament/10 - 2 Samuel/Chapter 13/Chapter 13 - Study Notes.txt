2 Samuel - Chapter 13
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Amnon rapes his half-sister Tamar and then despises her. Absalom nurses hatred for two years, then murders Amnon at a sheep-shearing feast and flees to Geshur—the sword has come to David's house.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Unknown, traditionally attributed to Nathan and Gad
(1 Chronicles 29:29). Originally one book with 1 Samuel in the Hebrew
Bible. The book covers David's entire reign — from his lament over
Saul's death to the purchase of the threshing floor for the future
Temple (approximately 1010-970 BC). Key themes: the Davidic covenant
(chapter 7), the consequences of sin even for the forgiven (chapters
11-20), God's faithfulness despite human failure, and the establishment
of the eternal throne fulfilled in Christ.

Historical Context: Chapter 13 begins the fulfillment of Nathan's
prophecy: "I will raise up evil against thee out of thine own house"
(12:11). The consequences of David's sin with Bathsheba now erupt
within his own family. Amnon, David's firstborn, rapes his half-sister
Tamar — Absalom's full sister. The parallels to David's sin are
deliberate: Amnon sees, desires, takes, and violates — the same
pattern as David with Bathsheba. But where David at least married
Bathsheba, Amnon discards Tamar with hatred worse than his lust.
David is "very wroth" but does nothing — perhaps because his own
guilt over Bathsheba paralyzes his moral authority. Absalom waits two
full years, then murders Amnon at a sheep-shearing feast and flees to
his maternal grandfather in Geshur. The chapter is devastating in its
portrayal of a family destroyed by sin's consequences: Tamar is
"desolate," Amnon is dead, Absalom is exiled, and David mourns
helplessly. The sword has entered David's house and will not depart.

Amnon's Lust and Jonadab's Scheme (vv.1-7): Amnon, David's firstborn
by Ahinoam, becomes obsessed with Tamar, his half-sister (Absalom's
full sister by Maacah). His "love" is actually lust — he is
"tormented" because she is a virgin and inaccessible. Jonadab, David's
nephew, is described as "very subtil" (KJV) / "very crafty" (ESV) —
he devises the scheme: Amnon should pretend illness and ask David to
send Tamar to prepare food for him. David unwittingly sends his
daughter into a trap.

The Rape of Tamar (vv.8-19): Tamar comes innocently to care for her
"sick" brother. Amnon clears the room, then seizes her. Tamar's
response is remarkable — she argues on multiple levels: (1) moral:
"no such thing ought to be done in Israel"; (2) personal: "whither
shall I cause my shame to go?"; (3) practical: "speak unto the king;
for he will not withhold me from thee." She offers every possible
alternative. But Amnon "would not hearken" — "being stronger than
she, forced her, and lay with her." Immediately after, his "love"
turns to hatred "greater than the love wherewith he had loved her."
This reveals the nature of lust: it is not love at all — it is
self-gratification that despises its object once satisfied. Amnon
throws Tamar out and bolts the door. Tamar tears her virgin's robe,
puts ashes on her head, and goes away crying — the visible signs of
her destroyed life.

David's Inaction and Absalom's Hatred (vv.20-22): Absalom takes Tamar
into his house — she will live there "desolate" for the rest of her
life. David hears and is "very wroth" — but does nothing. The
Septuagint adds: "but he would not punish Amnon his son, because he
loved him, for he was his firstborn." David's failure to act as both
father and king is catastrophic. His own guilt over Bathsheba likely
paralyzes him — how can he punish sexual sin when he committed it
himself? But his inaction leaves Tamar without justice and Absalom
without recourse — driving Absalom toward vigilante vengeance.
Absalom speaks "neither good nor bad" to Amnon — ominous silence
that conceals murderous hatred.

Absalom's Revenge (vv.23-39): Two years pass — Absalom's hatred
calcifies into a plan. He invites all the king's sons to a sheep-
shearing feast at Baal-hazor. He specifically requests Amnon's
presence — David is suspicious ("Why should he go with thee?") but
relents under pressure. Absalom commands his servants: "when Amnon's
heart is merry with wine... kill him." The murder is premeditated,
cold-blooded, and executed at a feast — echoing David's murder of
Uriah during a time of celebration. The other princes flee in panic.
A false report reaches David that all his sons are dead — he tears
his garments and lies on the ground. Jonadab (who helped cause the
original crisis) now "helpfully" clarifies: only Amnon is dead.
Absalom flees to Geshur (his mother Maacah's homeland) and remains
three years. David mourns daily — for Amnon who is dead, and for
Absalom who is gone.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Jerusalem (David's palace): Where the rape of Tamar occurs — Amnon's house is within the royal compound
- Baal-hazor: Near Ephraim, about 15 miles north of Jerusalem — where Absalom holds the sheep-shearing feast and murders Amnon
- Geshur: An Aramean kingdom northeast of the Sea of Galilee — where Absalom flees to his maternal grandfather, King Talmai

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.12 — "Nay, my brother, do not force me; for no such thing ought to
        be done in Israel: do not you this folly." — Tamar's plea
        appeals to Israel's moral identity. "Such a thing is not done
        in Israel" — this is beneath who we are as God's people. She
        calls it "folly" (nebalah) — the same word used for the worst
        moral outrages in Israel (Judges 19:23, 20:6). Her wisdom and
        courage in this moment are remarkable.

v.15 — "Then Amnon hated her exceedingly; so that the hatred
        wherewith he hated her was greater than the love wherewith he
        had loved her." — The nature of lust exposed. What Amnon
        called "love" was actually self-centered desire that, once
        gratified, turned to contempt. True love cherishes and
        protects; lust uses and discards. The reversal from "love" to
        hatred reveals that there was never love at all.

v.21 — "But when king David heard of all these things, he was very
        wroth." — David's anger without action. He is furious but
        does nothing — no punishment for Amnon, no justice for Tamar.
        This failure of leadership creates the vacuum that Absalom
        fills with murder. A father who will not discipline creates
        children who take justice into their own hands.

v.22 — "Absalom spake to his brother Amnon neither good nor bad:
        for Absalom hated Amnon." — Silence more dangerous than anger.
        Absalom's refusal to speak reveals not forgiveness but
        calculation. He is waiting — planning — for two years. Cold
        hatred is more dangerous than hot anger.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES

  healing but hardening — cold premeditation replacing hot anger.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "The sword entered David's house and would not depart. His own sin
  with Bathsheba was now reproduced in his son — but without even the
  pretense of love. Amnon's lust, David's inaction, Absalom's murder
  — all flow from that night on the rooftop. Let every man who toys
  with sin consider: you are not sinning for yourself alone. Your sin
  will bear fruit in your children and your children's children."
TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.3  — KJV: "a very subtil man" / ESV: "a very crafty man" — Hebrew
       "chakam" = wise, shrewd, crafty. The word is neutral — it can
       mean genuinely wise or cunningly shrewd. Context determines:
       Jonadab uses his intelligence for evil. KJV "subtil" (subtle)
       and ESV "crafty" both capture the negative application.

v.13 — KJV: "one of the fools in Israel" / ESV: "one of the
       outrageous fools in Israel" — Hebrew "nebalim" = fools in the
       moral sense (not intellectual). A "nabal" is one who acts with
       moral outrage against God and community (cf. Nabal in 1 Samuel
       25). ESV adds "outrageous" to clarify this is not mere
       stupidity but moral depravity.

v.18 — KJV: "a garment of divers colours" / ESV: "a long robe with
       sleeves" — Hebrew "ketonet passim" — the same phrase used for
       Joseph's coat (Genesis 37:3). Its exact meaning is debated:
       "many colors," "long sleeves," or "reaching to the extremities."
       It marked royal virgin daughters — its tearing symbolized
       Tamar's destroyed status.

v.20 — KJV: "regard not this thing" / ESV: "do not take this to
       heart" — Absalom's words to Tamar seem dismissive but may be
       an attempt to calm her while he plans his own response. His
       silence toward Amnon (v.22) shows he is not dismissing the
       crime but internalizing it for future action.

v.39 — KJV: "the soul of king David longed to go forth unto Absalom"
       / ESV: "the spirit of the king longed to go out to Absalom" —
       Hebrew "vatekhal" = to be consumed with longing, to pine. Both
       translations capture David's aching desire to see his exiled
       son. David is torn between justice (Absalom murdered Amnon) and
       love (Absalom is still his son).

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Tamar          — David's daughter by Maacah, full sister of Absalom.
                 Her name means "palm tree" — a symbol of beauty and
                 grace. She is one of Scripture's most tragic figures:
                 innocent, wise, courageous in her resistance, yet
                 destroyed by her brother's lust and her father's
                 inaction. She lived "desolate" — unmarried and
                 dishonored — for the rest of her life.

Sheep-shearing — A festive occasion in ancient Israel, comparable to
                 harvest celebrations. It involved feasting, drinking,
                 and community gathering. Absalom chose this setting
                 for Amnon's murder because wine would lower Amnon's
                 guard and the festive atmosphere would mask the plot.

Geshur         — A small Aramean kingdom northeast of the Sea of
                 Galilee, ruled by Talmai — Absalom's maternal
                 grandfather (through Maacah, 3:3). Absalom fled there
                 because it was outside David's jurisdiction and he had
                 family protection. He remained three years.

Nebalah        — Hebrew "folly" or "outrage" — but not mere foolishness.
                 It refers to acts of moral depravity that violate
                 Israel's covenant identity. Used for rape (here and
                 Genesis 34:7), sexual perversion (Judges 19:23), and
                 sacrilege. It is the strongest term for moral outrage
                 in Hebrew.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Sin reproduces itself in the next generation (vv.1-14). David's
   adultery is echoed in Amnon's rape — the son mirrors the father's
   pattern of seeing, desiring, and taking. Our sins do not stay
   contained — they teach our children patterns of behavior. The most
   powerful sermon we preach is the life we live before those who
   watch us.

2. Lust is not love (v.15). Amnon's "love" turned instantly to hatred
   once gratified. True love seeks the other's good; lust seeks
   self-gratification at the other's expense. The test is simple: does
   this desire lead me to protect and honor the other person, or to
   use them? If it uses, it is lust — regardless of what we call it.

3. Failure to act as a leader creates worse problems (v.21). David's
   anger without action left Tamar without justice and Absalom without
   recourse. When those in authority refuse to address wrong, they do
   not prevent conflict — they guarantee it will escalate. Absalom's
   murder was the direct result of David's inaction.

4. Silence can be more dangerous than anger (v.22). Absalom's two
   years of silence were not healing — they were hardening. Unresolved
   anger that goes underground does not disappear; it calcifies into
   something far more dangerous. Address conflict openly rather than
   letting it fester in silence.

5. The consequences of sin extend far beyond the sinner (vv.19-20).
   Tamar — completely innocent — lived "desolate" for the rest of her
   life. David's sin with Bathsheba set in motion a chain of events
   that destroyed his daughter's life. Sin always has collateral
   damage. The innocent suffer alongside the guilty.
