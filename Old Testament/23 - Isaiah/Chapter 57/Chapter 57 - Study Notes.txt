Isaiah - Chapter 57
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
The righteous die unnoticed while the wicked carry on. God exposes Israel's spiritual adultery — their idols in every high place and under every green tree. Yet the chapter ends with extraordinary tenderness: God dwells with the contrite, revives the humble spirit, heals backsliders, and creates the "fruit of the lips: Peace, peace to him that is far off and near." But no peace for the wicked.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Isaiah son of Amoz (see Chapter 1 notes for full details).

Classification: Prophetic Oracle — Woe and Comfort
Key Themes: The righteous dying unnoticed, spiritual adultery,
God as the lofty One who dwells with the lowly, revival for
the contrite, peace for far and near, no peace for the wicked

Historical Context: This chapter continues the critique of
unfaithful Israel begun in 56:9-12. The language of spiritual
adultery (vv.3-10) uses shocking sexual imagery — Israel as an
adulterous wife who "made thy bed" wide for idolatrous lovers.
Yet the chapter's ending (vv.15-21) contains some of Isaiah's
most tender theology: the HIGH and LOFTY One who inhabits
eternity ALSO dwells with the crushed and humble. He heals, leads,
restores comfort, and creates peace. The section closes with the
repeated refrain: "There is no peace, saith my God, to the wicked"
(v.21; cf. 48:22).

Structure:
- The Righteous Die Unnoticed (vv.1-2)
- Spiritual Adultery Exposed (vv.3-10)
- Who Have You Feared More Than God? (vv.11-13)
- The High and Lofty One Dwells with the Lowly (vv.14-16)
- Healing for the Backslider (vv.17-19)
- Peace for Near and Far (v.19)
- No Peace for the Wicked (vv.20-21)

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "The righteous perisheth, and no man layeth it to heart"
        (KJV) — The righteous die and nobody cares. But v.1b
        reveals: God removes them FROM the coming evil. Their
        death is mercy — sparing them from what's ahead.

v.15 — "For thus saith the high and lofty One that inhabiteth
        eternity, whose name is Holy; I dwell in the high and
        holy place, with him also that is of a contrite and humble
        spirit, to revive the spirit of the humble, and to revive
        the heart of the contrite ones" (KJV) — The chapter's
        theological summit. The HIGHEST God dwells with the
        LOWEST people. Eternity meets humility. Loftiness meets
        contrition.

v.18 — "I have seen his ways, and will heal him: I will lead him
        also, and restore comforts unto him" (KJV) — Despite
        seeing Israel's sinful ways, God HEALS rather than
        destroys. Grace upon grace.

v.19 — "I create the fruit of the lips; Peace, peace to him that
        is far off, and to him that is near, saith the LORD; and
        I will heal him" (KJV) — Peace for BOTH: far (Gentiles)
        and near (Jews). Paul quotes this in Ephesians 2:17.

v.21 — "There is no peace, saith my God, unto the wicked" (KJV)
        — The section's closing refrain (cf. 48:22). Peace offered
        freely — but the wicked who refuse are self-excluded.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.15 — "contrite" (KJV/ESV) — Hebrew "dakka" = crushed, ground
      to powder. Not merely sad but BROKEN — one whose self-
      sufficiency has been pulverized.

v.15 — "inhabiteth eternity" (KJV) / "inhabits eternity" (ESV)
      — Hebrew "shoken ad" = dwelling perpetually/forever. God's
      address is ETERNITY ITSELF. He lives outside time.

v.19 — "fruit of the lips" (KJV/ESV) — Hebrew "niv sefatayim" =
      the produce/utterance of lips. God CREATES the praise that
      lips offer. He both gives peace AND creates the ability to
      praise for it (cf. Hebrews 13:15).

v.20 — "troubled sea" (KJV) / "tossing sea" (ESV) — Hebrew
      "yam nigrash" = a storm-driven sea that cannot rest. The
      wicked are like water that can never be still — always
      churning, throwing up filth.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
High and Lofty One — God's transcendence described: above all,
              eternal, holy. YET this transcendent God DWELLS with
              the crushed. The greatest theological tension:
              infinite holiness choosing to inhabit human humility.

Contrite     — (Hebrew: dakka) Crushed, ground down, shattered.
              Not mere sadness but total brokenness before God.
              The ONLY "qualification" for God's indwelling
              presence.

Peace, Peace — Doubled for emphasis: complete, total shalom. Given
              to "far" (Gentiles) and "near" (Jews) — universal
              in scope.

No Peace for the Wicked — The refrain (48:22; 57:21): God offers
              peace to all, but the wicked by definition REFUSE it.
              Their inner state is the tossing sea — never at rest.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.15 — NET: "the one who rules forever" / KJV: "that inhabiteth eternity" / ASV/WEB: "that inhabiteth eternity" — NET interprets "shokhen ad" (dwelling eternally/inhabiting forever) as "rules forever," shifting from God's eternal existence to His eternal sovereignty. KJV/ASV/WEB preserve the mysterious concept of God DWELLING in eternity itself.

- v.15 — NET: "the discouraged and humiliated" / KJV: "a contrite and humble spirit" / ASV/WEB: "a contrite and humble spirit" — NET renders "dakka" (contrite/crushed) as "discouraged" and "shefal" (lowly/humble) as "humiliated." "Contrite" (literally crushed) is stronger than "discouraged" — it implies brokenness before God, not mere sadness.

- v.19 — NET: "I am the one who gives them reason to celebrate. Complete prosperity is available" / KJV: "I create the fruit of the lips: Peace, peace, to him that is far off and to him that is near" — NET paraphrases extensively, losing the Hebrew poetry. "Fruit of the lips" = praise/speech; God creates praise where there was mourning. "Shalom, shalom" (doubled for emphasis) becomes "complete prosperity."

- v.21 — NET: "There will be no prosperity... for the wicked" / KJV: "There is no peace... to the wicked" / WEB/ASV: "There is no peace... for the wicked" — NET again renders "shalom" as "prosperity" (see also 48:22). Hebrew "shalom" encompasses peace, wholeness, well-being, health, and prosperity — "peace" is the better English summary.

- v.1 — NET: "The godly perish" / KJV: "The righteous perisheth" / ASV/WEB: "The righteous perisheth/perish" — NET uses "godly" for "tsaddiq" (righteous one). "Righteous" is more precise — denoting one in right-standing with God, not general piety.

- v.13 — NET: "will have access to my holy mountain" / KJV: "shall inherit my holy mountain" / ASV/WEB: "shall inherit my holy mountain" — NET weakens "nachal" (inherit/possess as inheritance) to "have access to." Inheritance implies ownership and permanence; access implies permission that can be revoked.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. God dwells with the contrite (v.15). Not the successful, not
   the impressive, not the confident — the CRUSHED. If you feel
   broken today, congratulations: you're God's preferred address.
   He moves INTO brokenness, not away from it.

2. "I have seen his ways, and will HEAL him" (v.18). God sees
   your worst — and His response is healing, not rejection. He
   doesn't pretend your sin doesn't exist; He sees it clearly
   AND heals anyway.

3. Peace for far AND near (v.19). Wherever you are spiritually
   — whether you feel close to God or impossibly distant — peace
   is offered to YOU. "Far off" doesn't disqualify you; it
   qualifies you.

4. No peace for the wicked (v.21). This isn't cruelty; it's
   description. The wicked are like a stormy sea — UNABLE to be
   at peace because they refuse the source of peace. Peace isn't
   withheld arbitrarily; it's rejected by those who won't receive
   God's terms.

5. The righteous die unnoticed but are SPARED (v.1). If godly
   people in your life have died "before their time," consider:
   God may be removing them from coming evil. Their death was
   mercy, not abandonment.
