Isaiah - Chapter 22
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
The "Valley of Vision" oracle — God rebukes Jerusalem for celebrating prematurely instead of repenting, pronounces judgment on Shebna (a corrupt steward), and elevates Eliakim as a foreshadowing of Christ who holds the key of David.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Isaiah son of Amoz (see Chapter 1 notes for full details).

Classification: Prophetic Oracle — Burden of the Valley of Vision
Key Themes: Jerusalem's inappropriate revelry in the face of
judgment, the failure of human military preparations, the sin of
"eat and drink for tomorrow we die," the removal of a corrupt
official, Eliakim as a type of Christ, the key of David

Historical Context: The "Valley of Vision" is Jerusalem itself
(surrounded by valleys — Kidron, Hinnom, Tyropoeon — yet the
place where God REVEALS His vision). The occasion is likely
Sennacherib's withdrawal in 701 BC. Instead of repenting and
thanking God, Jerusalem threw a party — feasting and drinking
as if THEY had won. God's response: "this iniquity shall not be
purged from you till ye die" (v.14). The second half addresses
Shebna, a high-ranking steward (possibly a foreigner who gained
power), who is replaced by Eliakim son of Hilkiah.

Structure:
- Jerusalem's Inappropriate Celebration (vv.1-4)
- The Day of Destruction from the LORD (vv.5-8a)
- Trust in Military Preparations Instead of God (vv.8b-11)
- "Let Us Eat and Drink, for Tomorrow We Die" (vv.12-14)
- The Demotion of Shebna (vv.15-19)
- The Elevation of Eliakim (vv.20-25)

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.12 — "And in that day did the Lord GOD of hosts call to
        weeping, and to mourning... and behold joy and gladness,
        slaying oxen, and killing sheep, eating flesh, and
        drinking wine: let us eat and drink; for to morrow we
        shall die" (KJV) — God called for repentance; they threw
        a party. The sin of hedonistic fatalism — quoted by Paul
        in 1 Corinthians 15:32.

v.13 — "let us eat and drink; for to morrow we shall die" (KJV)
        — Fatalism masquerading as celebration. If there's no
        future accountability, pleasure is all that remains.

v.14 — "Surely this iniquity shall not be purged from you till
        ye die" (KJV) — One of Scripture's most severe sentences.
        The generation's sin is unforgivable because they
        responded to God's deliverance with self-indulgence rather
        than worship.

v.22 — "And the key of the house of David will I lay upon his
        shoulder; so he shall open, and none shall shut; and he
        shall shut, and none shall open" (KJV) — Eliakim receives
        the key of David. Jesus applies this to Himself in
        Revelation 3:7: "he that hath the key of David."

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1  — "Valley of Vision" (KJV/ESV) — Paradoxical: a valley
      (low place) of vision (revelation). Jerusalem sits in a
      valley relative to surrounding hills, yet is the place where
      God reveals Himself to prophets.

v.15 — "Shebna, which is over the house" (KJV) — Hebrew "al
      ha-bayit" = steward, chief of the royal household. The
      highest administrative official after the king. Shebna was
      likely not of Davidic lineage (no patronymic given — "son
      of ___" is missing, suggesting foreign origin).

v.22 — "key" (KJV/ESV) — Hebrew "maphteach" = key, the symbol
      of authority over access. Placed "upon his shoulder" =
      large ceremonial keys were carried on the shoulder,
      displaying the official's authority publicly.

v.23 — "peg in a sure place" (ESV) / "nail in a sure place"
      (KJV) — Hebrew "yated" = tent peg or wall peg. A secure
      mounting point from which everything hangs. Eliakim (and
      ultimately Christ) is the peg that holds everything
      together.

v.1 — NET: "This is an oracle about the Valley" for KJV's "The burden"
       WEB: "you have all" for KJV's "thou art wholly"
v.15 — ASV: uses "Jehovah" for KJV's "LORD" (Hebrew YHWH).
       NET: "Heaven’s Armies says: “Go visit" for KJV's "hosts, Go, get thee unto"
       WEB: uses "Yahweh" for KJV's "LORD" (Hebrew YHWH).
v.22 — NET: "opens the door, no one can close it; when" for KJV's "shall open, and none shall shut; and"
       WEB: "no one will shut. He will" for KJV's "none shall shut; and he shall"
v.23 — ASV: adds "of glory"
       NET: adds "he will bring honor"
       WEB: "place. He will" for KJV's "place; and he shall"

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. The wrong response to deliverance (vv.12-13). God saved
   Jerusalem — and they threw a self-indulgent party instead of
   falling on their knees. When God rescues you, does your
   response point to HIM or to your own pleasure? Celebration
   isn't wrong — celebrating without acknowledging God IS.

2. "Let us eat and drink, for tomorrow we die" (v.13). This is
   the philosophy of a culture without resurrection hope. If
   there's no future accountability, hedonism is logical. But
   there IS a resurrection — which makes how you live TODAY
   matter eternally.

3. Shebna used his position for self-glory (v.16). He carved an
   elaborate tomb — a monument to himself. Leadership is
   stewardship, not self-promotion. How are you using the
   authority God has given you — for His house or your monument?

4. Christ holds the key (v.22). Eliakim was a shadow; Jesus is
   the reality. He opens doors no one can shut and shuts doors
   no one can open. Stop trying to force open doors He's closed,
   and stop hesitating before doors He's opened.
