Isaiah - Chapter 27
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
The climax of the Isaiah Apocalypse — God slays Leviathan (the serpent/dragon), tends Israel as His vineyard, disciplines His people in measured judgment, and gathers them home with a great trumpet. Israel's enemies are crushed but Israel is preserved.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Isaiah son of Amoz (see Chapter 1 notes for full details).

Classification: Apocalyptic Prophecy — The Isaiah Apocalypse
Conclusion (chs. 24-27)
Key Themes: The slaying of Leviathan, God's vineyard protected
and fruitful, measured discipline vs. total destruction, the
gathering of the scattered, the great trumpet

Historical Context: This chapter concludes the Isaiah Apocalypse
(chs. 24-27). It begins with God slaying Leviathan — the
mythological sea-dragon representing cosmic chaos and evil (used
throughout the ancient Near East as a symbol of primordial
opposition to God). The vineyard song (vv.2-6) deliberately
contrasts with chapter 5's vineyard (which God abandoned). Now
God GUARDS His vineyard, waters it constantly, and is no longer
angry. The chapter ends with the great trumpet gathering
Israel's scattered exiles from Assyria and Egypt back to
Jerusalem — a regathering that transcends any single historical
return.

Structure:
- God Slays Leviathan (v.1)
- God's Vineyard: Guarded and Fruitful (vv.2-6)
- Measured Discipline vs. Total Destruction (vv.7-11)
- The Great Trumpet: Exiles Gathered Home (vv.12-13)

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "In that day the LORD with his sore and great and strong
        sword shall punish leviathan the piercing serpent, even
        leviathan that crooked serpent; and he shall slay the
        dragon that is in the sea" (KJV) — God defeats the cosmic
        enemy. The serpent of Genesis 3, the dragon of Revelation
        12 — the same enemy, finally destroyed.

v.3  — "I the LORD do keep it; I will water it every moment:
        lest any hurt it, I will keep it night and day" (KJV) —
        God as the tireless gardener of His people. Constant
        watering, constant watching, night and day protection.

v.4  — "Fury is not in me" (KJV) — After all the judgment
        oracles, God declares: My anger is DONE. Toward His
        vineyard, there is no more wrath — only nurture.

v.5  — "Or let him take hold of my strength, that he may make
        peace with me; and he shall make peace with me" (KJV) —
        The invitation stands: anyone can grasp God's strength
        and make peace. Peace repeated twice for emphasis.

v.13 — "And it shall come to pass in that day, that the great
        trumpet shall be blown, and they shall come which were
        ready to perish in the land of Assyria, and the outcasts
        in the land of Egypt, and shall worship the LORD in the
        holy mount at Jerusalem" (KJV) — The great ingathering.
        Trumpet blast, exiles return, worship restored.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "leviathan" (KJV/ESV) — Hebrew "livyatan" = twisted/
      coiled one. A mythological sea monster representing chaos,
      evil, and opposition to God. Not a literal animal but a
      cosmic symbol. Appeared in Canaanite mythology as Lotan —
      Isaiah claims YAHWEH defeats it, not Baal.

v.1  — "piercing serpent... crooked serpent" (KJV) / "fleeing
      serpent... twisting serpent" (ESV) — Two descriptions: one
      that flees (fast, elusive) and one that twists (deceptive,
      writhing). Both aspects of evil conquered.

v.8  — "in measure" (KJV) / "measure by measure" (ESV) — Hebrew
      "be-sa'se'ah" = measured, proportioned. God's discipline of
      His people is CALIBRATED — not excessive, not uncontrolled.

v.9  — "chalkstones" (KJV) / "chalk" (ESV) — The altar stones
      ground to chalk powder = complete destruction of idolatrous
      worship sites. Forgiveness requires removal of the
      idolatrous system.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Leviathan    — Cosmic sea-dragon/serpent representing chaos and
              evil. Used across ancient Near Eastern mythology.
              In Isaiah, it represents all supernatural opposition
              to God — defeated definitively "in that day."

Vineyard     — Recurring metaphor for Israel/God's people.
              Chapter 5: the vineyard abandoned. Chapter 27: the
              vineyard restored and guarded. The progression tells
              the story of judgment and restoration.

Great Trumpet — (v.13) Hebrew "shofar gadol" — the eschatological
              trumpet that signals the gathering of the scattered.
              Connected to the trumpet of jubilee (freedom),
              Sinai (God's presence), and the last trump (1 Cor 15:52).

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.1 — ASV: "Jehovah with his hard and great and strong sword will punish leviathan the swift serpent" / WEB: "Yahweh with his hard and great and strong sword will punish leviathan, the fleeing serpent" / NET: "the Lord will punish with his destructive, great, and powerful sword Leviathan the fast-moving serpent" — The Hebrew "bariach" (fleeing/bar-like) is variously rendered: KJV "piercing," ASV "swift," WEB "fleeing," NET "fast-moving." NET also changes "slay the dragon" to "kill the sea monster," removing the mythological resonance.

- v.3 — ASV: "I Jehovah am its keeper" / WEB: "I, Yahweh, am its keeper" / NET: "I, the Lord, protect it" — God names Himself as vineyard-keeper. ASV/WEB use the divine name directly in God's self-declaration; NET substitutes "the Lord." The personal name adds weight to the covenant promise.

- v.8 — NET: "When you summon her for divorce, you prosecute her" — A radically different reading from KJV's "in measure, when it shooteth forth, thou wilt debate with it." NET interprets the difficult Hebrew as a divorce metaphor (God sending Israel away), while KJV/ASV/WEB see measured discipline. This is one of the most debated verses in Isaiah.

- v.9 — ASV: "the Asherim and the sun-images shall rise no more" / WEB: "the Asherah poles and the incense altars shall rise no more" / KJV: "the groves and images shall not stand up" — KJV's "groves" obscures that these are Asherah poles (Canaanite goddess worship). ASV/WEB/NET name "Asherah" directly, clarifying the idolatrous nature of what must be destroyed.

- v.12 — WEB: "Yahweh will thresh from the flowing stream of the Euphrates" / NET: "the Lord will shake the tree, from the Euphrates River" / KJV: "the LORD shall beat off from the channel of the river" — The Hebrew "yachbot" (to beat/thresh) pictures God harvesting His people like grain. NET's "shake the tree" changes the agricultural metaphor from threshing to tree-shaking.

- v.13 — ASV: "they shall worship Jehovah in the holy mountain at Jerusalem" / WEB: "they will worship Yahweh in the holy mountain at Jerusalem" / NET: "They will worship the Lord on the holy mountain in Jerusalem" — The culminating vision uses the divine name (ASV/WEB) at the climax of the ingathering — the scattered exiles worship YHWH by name.

- v.4 — NET: "I am not angry. I wish I could confront some thorns and briers!" — KJV's "Fury is not in me: who would set the briers and thorns against me in battle?" is a rhetorical challenge. NET turns it into a wish ("I wish I could"), weakening the warrior imagery of God longing to fight FOR His vineyard against enemies.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. God slays the serpent (v.1). The enemy is not eternal — he has
   an expiration date. Whatever evil oppresses you, it is living
   on borrowed time. God's sword is already drawn.

2. "Fury is not in me" (v.4). Toward His redeemed people, God's
   stance is nurture, not anger. If you're in Christ, stop
   relating to God as if He's furious with you. His wrath was
   spent at the cross.

3. "I will water it every moment" (v.3). God's care for His
   people is constant — not occasional, not seasonal, but EVERY
   MOMENT. You are not forgotten between prayers. He waters you
   while you sleep.

4. Measured discipline (v.8). When God does correct His people,
   it's proportioned — "in measure." He is not an abusive parent
   who loses control. His discipline is precise, purposeful, and
   ALWAYS for restoration.

5. The trumpet gathers the lost (v.13). No one is too far gone
   — not those "ready to perish" in Assyria nor the "outcasts"
   in Egypt. God's trumpet reaches the most distant exile.
