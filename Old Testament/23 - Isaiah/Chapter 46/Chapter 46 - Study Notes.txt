Isaiah - Chapter 46
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Babylon's gods (Bel and Nebo) are loaded onto beasts of burden and carried into captivity — they cannot save themselves, let alone their worshippers. In contrast, the LORD has carried Israel from the womb to old age. He declares the end from the beginning and calls Cyrus ("a ravenous bird from the east") to accomplish His purpose.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Isaiah son of Amoz (see Chapter 1 notes for full details).

Classification: Prophetic Oracle — Idol Polemic / Sovereignty
Declaration
Key Themes: Fallen gods vs. the carrying God, idols carried vs.
God who carries, declaring the end from the beginning, God's
purpose stands, the ravenous bird from the east (Cyrus)

Historical Context: When Babylon falls to Persia (539 BC), its
idol statues will be loaded onto animals and carted away as plunder
(v.1-2). The irony: the "gods" who were supposed to protect
Babylon have to be CARRIED — they can't even walk. Meanwhile, the
true God says "I have carried you since birth and will carry you
to old age" (vv.3-4). The contrast is devastating: false gods are
a BURDEN to their worshippers; the true God CARRIES His worshippers.

Structure:
- Babylon's Gods: Carried into Captivity (vv.1-2)
- The LORD: Carrying Israel from Birth to Old Age (vv.3-4)
- No Comparison: Idols vs. the Living God (vv.5-7)
- Remember This: I Am God, None Like Me (vv.8-11)
- Near Salvation for the Stubborn (vv.12-13)

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "Bel boweth down, Nebo stoopeth" (KJV) — Babylon's chief
        gods (Marduk/Bel and Nabu/Nebo) collapse. They can't even
        stand upright. The gods of the world's greatest empire are
        luggage.

v.4  — "And even to your old age I am he; and even to hoar hairs
        will I carry you: I have made, and I will bear; even I
        will carry, and will deliver you" (KJV) — God's commitment
        from birth to death. He made you, He bears you, He carries
        you, He delivers you. FOUR verbs of faithful care.

v.9  — "I am God, and there is none like me" (KJV) — Absolute
        uniqueness. No comparison possible. God is in a category
        of one.

v.10 — "Declaring the end from the beginning, and from ancient
        times the things that are not yet done, saying, My counsel
        shall stand, and I will do all my pleasure" (KJV) — God's
        unique ability: foretelling the future. And His foretelling
        is not speculation — it's DECLARATION. His counsel STANDS.
        His pleasure is DONE.

v.11 — "Calling a ravenous bird from the east, the man that
        executeth my counsel from a far country" (KJV) — Cyrus
        (eagle/falcon imagery) — swift, predatory, coming from the
        east (Persia) to execute God's plan against Babylon.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "Bel" (KJV/ESV) — Babylonian title meaning "lord" —
      refers to Marduk, Babylon's chief deity. "Nebo" (Nabu) was
      the god of writing and wisdom, Marduk's son.

v.1  — "stoopeth" (KJV) / "stoops" (ESV) — Hebrew "qaras" =
      bends, crouches. The idol tips forward as the cart lurches.
      A "god" that can't maintain its own balance.

v.4  — "hoar hairs" (KJV) / "gray hairs" (ESV) — Hebrew "sevah"
      = gray/white hair of old age. God's care has no retirement
      age — He carries you until the end.

v.11 — "ravenous bird" (KJV) / "bird of prey" (ESV) — Hebrew
      "ayit" = a bird of prey, eagle or falcon. Speed, power,
      predatory precision — Cyrus's military campaigns described
      in a single image.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Bel          — "Lord" — title of Marduk, Babylon's patron deity.
              The Babylonian creation epic (Enuma Elish) celebrated
              Marduk as king of the gods. Isaiah says he's luggage.

Nebo/Nabu    — Babylonian god of writing, wisdom, and scribal arts.
              Son of Marduk. Nebuchadnezzar's name contains "Nabu"
              (Nabu-kudurri-usur = "Nabu, protect my boundary").

Carrying     — The central contrast: idols are CARRIED (dead
              weight); God CARRIES (living strength). The direction
              of burden reveals who is truly God.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.3 — NET: "O family of Jacob" / KJV/ASV: "O house of Jacob" — NET uses "family" for Hebrew "beth" (house), making the kinship relational rather than institutional. The covenant bond is personal.

- v.4 — NET: "Even when you are old, I will take care of you" / KJV: "even to your old age I am he" — NET paraphrases the cryptic Hebrew "ani hu" (I am He) into functional language ("I will take care of you"), losing the divine self-identification formula that echoes "I AM."

- v.8 — NET: "Remember this, so you can be brave" / KJV: "Remember this, and shew yourselves men" / ASV: "show yourselves men" — Hebrew "hit'osheshu" is difficult; NET interprets as courage, while KJV/ASV render more literally. WEB follows KJV: "show yourselves men."

- v.10 — NET: "who announces the end from the beginning and reveals beforehand what has not yet occurred" / KJV: "Declaring the end from the beginning" — NET expands for clarity, making explicit what is implicit in the Hebrew about predictive prophecy.

- v.11 — NET: "who summons an eagle from the east" / KJV: "Calling a ravenous bird from the east" / WEB: "a ravenous bird" — NET identifies the bird specifically as an eagle, while Hebrew "ayit" is generic bird of prey. All translations apply this to Cyrus.

- v.13 — NET: "I am bringing my deliverance near" / ASV: "I bring near my righteousness" / WEB: "I bring my righteousness near" — NET translates Hebrew "tsedaqah" as "deliverance" rather than "righteousness," reflecting the salvation-context meaning of this word in Isaiah 40-66 where righteousness and deliverance merge.

- v.13 — NET: "I will adorn Israel with my splendor" / KJV: "I will place salvation in Zion for Israel my glory" — NET radically reinterprets — making Israel adorned WITH God's splendor, while KJV/ASV/WEB read Israel as God's glory (or glory given to Israel in Zion).

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. False gods are a BURDEN; the true God CARRIES (vv.1-4). What
   are you carrying that was supposed to carry you? Career, money,
   status, relationships — if they're exhausting you instead of
   sustaining you, they might be idols.

2. "Even to your old age I am he" (v.4). God doesn't abandon
   you when you're no longer productive, attractive, or useful
   by the world's standards. His commitment has no expiration
   date.

3. "My counsel shall stand" (v.10). God's plan WILL happen.
   Not might, not hopefully — WILL. Your anxiety about whether
   God's purposes will be accomplished is unnecessary. They will.

4. "I will do ALL my pleasure" (v.10). God is not partially
   sovereign. He does ALL His pleasure — not some of it, not
   most of it. Rest in complete sovereignty.
