Isaiah - Chapter 5
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Isaiah sings the parable of God's vineyard that produced only wild grapes despite perfect care — then pronounces six woes against the sins of Judah: greed, drunkenness, mocking God, moral confusion, conceit, and corrupt justice.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Isaiah son of Amoz (see Chapter 1 notes for full details).

Classification: Prophetic Parable and Woe Oracle
Key Themes: God's investment in Israel unrewarded, the Song of
the Vineyard, six woes against specific sins, God whistling for
distant nations as instruments of judgment

Historical Context: Chapter 5 is one of Isaiah's most powerful
literary compositions. It begins with "the Song of the Vineyard"
(vv.1-7) — a love song that turns into a courtroom indictment.
God did EVERYTHING right: chose good ground, cleared stones, planted
choice vines, built a watchtower, dug a winepress. Yet the vineyard
produced only wild/stinking grapes. The "vineyard" is Israel; the
"wild grapes" are injustice and oppression (v.7 — a devastating
wordplay in Hebrew). The six woes (vv.8-23) then specify what the
"wild grapes" look like: land-grabbing, partying, God-mocking,
moral relativism, self-wisdom, and bribed justice. The chapter
closes with God whistling for a far-away nation (Assyria) to
execute judgment (vv.26-30).

Structure:
- The Song of the Vineyard (vv.1-7)
- Woe #1: Greed/Land-Grabbing (vv.8-10)
- Woe #2: Drunkenness and Pleasure-Seeking (vv.11-17)
- Woe #3: Mocking God's Judgment (vv.18-19)
- Woe #4: Moral Confusion — Evil Called Good (v.20)
- Woe #5: Self-Wisdom/Conceit (v.21)
- Woe #6: Corrupt Justice (vv.22-23)
- God Whistles for the Nations (vv.24-30)

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.4  — "What could have been done more to my vineyard, that I
        have not done in it? wherefore, when I looked that it
        should bring forth grapes, brought it forth wild grapes?"
        (KJV) — God's bewildered question. He invested everything
        — what MORE could He have done? The problem is not God's
        care but Israel's response.

v.7  — "he looked for judgment, but behold oppression; for
        righteousness, but behold a cry" (KJV) — Hebrew wordplay:
        He looked for mishpat (justice) but found mispach
        (bloodshed); for tsedaqah (righteousness) but found
        tse'aqah (a cry of distress). The similar sounds make the
        contrast devastating.

v.20 — "Woe unto them that call evil good, and good evil; that
        put darkness for light, and light for darkness; that put
        bitter for sweet, and sweet for bitter!" (KJV) — The
        fourth woe: moral inversion. When a society redefines evil
        as good, it has reached the most dangerous stage of
        corruption — calling darkness "enlightenment."

v.21 — "Woe unto them that are wise in their own eyes, and
        prudent in their own sight!" (KJV) — The fifth woe:
        self-referential wisdom. When you become your own standard
        of truth, you have made yourself God — the original sin
        (Genesis 3:5).

v.26 — "he will lift up an ensign to the nations from far, and
        will hiss unto them from the end of the earth" (KJV) —
        God WHISTLES for armies the way a beekeeper whistles for
        bees. He summons Assyria (unnamed but implied) as His
        instrument — pagan armies at God's command.

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1  — "wellbeloved" (KJV) / "beloved" (ESV) — Hebrew "yedid"
      = a dear friend/loved one. Isaiah sings on God's behalf —
      the song begins as a love ballad before becoming an
      indictment.

v.2  — "wild grapes" (KJV) / "wild grapes" (ESV) — Hebrew
      "be'ushim" = stinking/rotten berries. Not merely low-quality
      fruit but actively repulsive — putrid, offensive to the
      senses.

v.7  — The Hebrew wordplay is untranslatable: mishpat/mispach
      (justice/bloodshed) and tsedaqah/tse'aqah (righteousness/
      outcry). English "He looked for justice but found injustice"
      loses the devastating sonic similarity.

v.14 — "hell" (KJV) / "Sheol" (ESV) — Hebrew "she'ol" = the
      grave/realm of the dead. Personified here as a creature
      with an open mouth and insatiable appetite, swallowing
      Jerusalem's people.

v.26 — "hiss" (KJV) / "whistle" (ESV) — Hebrew "sharaq" = to
      whistle/hiss as a signal. Used for calling bees (7:18) or
      signaling dogs/servants. God summons armies casually, the
      way a man whistles for his dog.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "What more could have been done?" (v.4). God has given you
   everything needed for spiritual fruitfulness: His word, His
   Spirit, His church, His grace. If you're producing "wild
   grapes," the problem isn't insufficient resources — it's your
   response to what you've been given.

2. The six woes target sins of PROSPEROUS societies: land-grabbing
   (v.8), partying (v.11), mocking God's timing (v.19), moral
   confusion (v.20), intellectual pride (v.21), corrupt justice
   (v.23). These aren't poverty sins — they're WEALTH sins. Which
   ones characterize your culture? Which ones characterize you?

3. "Woe unto them that call evil good" (v.20). Moral relativism
   isn't new — Isaiah confronted it 2,700 years ago. When you
   redefine sin as "freedom" or "progress," you're not innovating
   — you're repeating ancient rebellion with a modern vocabulary.

4. God uses pagan nations as His instruments (vv.26-30). Assyria
   doesn't know it's serving God — but God whistles and they come.
   He is sovereign over ALL nations, not just His covenant people.
   Even your enemies are under His command.

5. The vineyard song begins as a love song (v.1) and ends as a
   judgment oracle (v.5-6). God's love and God's judgment aren't
   contradictions — the judgment flows FROM the love. He invested
   so much because He loved so deeply. Spurned love becomes the
   most painful judgment.
