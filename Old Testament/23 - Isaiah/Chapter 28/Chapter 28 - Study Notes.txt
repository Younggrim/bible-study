Isaiah - Chapter 28
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Woe to the drunkards of Ephraim — the fading crown of Samaria will be trampled. Then Isaiah turns to Judah's leaders: mocking priests and prophets who reject God's word, trusting in a "covenant with death." God responds by laying in Zion a precious cornerstone, and promises to sweep away their refuge of lies.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Isaiah son of Amoz (see Chapter 1 notes for full details).

Classification: Prophetic Woe Oracle — First of Six Woes
(chs. 28-33)
Key Themes: Drunkenness (literal and spiritual), the rejected
word of God, the "covenant with death," the precious cornerstone
in Zion, God's strange work, the farmer parable

Historical Context: This begins a new section (chs. 28-33) — six
woe oracles directed primarily at Judah's leaders during the
period when they were seeking Egyptian alliance against Assyria
(likely 705-701 BC, during Hezekiah's reign). The chapter opens
with Ephraim/Samaria (whose fall in 722 BC serves as a WARNING to
Judah) then pivots to Jerusalem. The "covenant with death" (v.15)
refers to Judah's alliance with Egypt — they thought it guaranteed
safety, but Isaiah calls it a pact with death itself. The
cornerstone (v.16) is one of Scripture's great messianic texts.

Structure:
- Woe to the Fading Crown of Ephraim (vv.1-6)
- Judah's Drunken Leaders Reject God's Word (vv.7-13)
- The Covenant with Death (vv.14-15)
- The Precious Cornerstone in Zion (v.16)
- God Sweeps Away the Refuge of Lies (vv.17-22)
- The Farmer Parable: God's Varied Methods (vv.23-29)

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.9  — "Whom shall he teach knowledge? and whom shall he make to
        understand doctrine? them that are weaned from the milk,
        and drawn from the breasts" (KJV) — The leaders mock
        Isaiah: "Who does he think he's teaching — babies?" They
        reject his message as too simple, too repetitive.

v.10 — "For precept must be upon precept, precept upon precept;
        line upon line, line upon line; here a little, and there
        a little" (KJV) — Either Isaiah's method (patient,
        incremental teaching) or the leaders' mocking imitation
        of his repetitive style. Hebrew "tsav la-tsav" may
        imitate a child's babbling.

v.15 — "We have made a covenant with death, and with hell are we
        at agreement" (KJV) — Judah's leaders boast that their
        alliance (with Egypt) protects them from Assyrian
        destruction. Isaiah renames their treaty: it's a pact
        with death itself.

v.16 — "Therefore thus saith the Lord GOD, Behold, I lay in Zion
        for a foundation a stone, a tried stone, a precious corner
        stone, a sure foundation: he that believeth shall not make
        haste" (KJV) — The messianic cornerstone. God's alternative
        to human alliances: a tested, precious foundation. Quoted
        of Christ in Romans 9:33, 1 Peter 2:6.

v.21 — "For the LORD shall rise up... that he may do his work,
        his strange work; and bring to pass his act, his strange
        act" (KJV) — Judgment is God's "strange work" — it's not
        His natural activity. He'd rather bless. But rebellion
        forces His hand.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "the fading flower of his glorious beauty" (ESV) / "whose
      glorious beauty is a fading flower" (KJV) — Samaria sat on
      a hill like a crown/wreath above the fertile valley. That
      "crown" is fading — like flowers wilting on a drunk's head.

v.10 — "precept upon precept" (KJV) / "Do this, do that" (NIV)
      — Hebrew "tsav la-tsav, qav la-qav" is onomatopoeic —
      possibly imitating a drunkard's slurred speech or a child's
      babbling. Scholars debate whether it's Isaiah's teaching
      method or the mockers' parody of it.

v.16 — "he that believeth shall not make haste" (KJV) / "whoever
      believes will not be in haste" (ESV) — Hebrew "lo yachish"
      = will not hurry/panic. Faith produces calm confidence, not
      panicked rushing from one alliance to another.

v.25 — "fitches" (KJV) / "dill" (ESV) — Hebrew "qetsach" =
      black cumin (Nigella sativa), a spice seed that requires
      gentle threshing, not crushing. The farmer parable: God
      applies different methods to different crops.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Cornerstone  — The first stone laid in construction, determining
              the alignment of the entire building. Christ as
              cornerstone: everything in God's kingdom is aligned
              to and built upon Him.

Covenant with Death — Judah's alliance with Egypt, sarcastically
              renamed. They thought it guaranteed life; Isaiah says
              it guarantees death. Any "security" built apart from
              God is ultimately a pact with destruction.

Strange Work — God's judgment described as unusual, unnatural
              for Him. Blessing is God's delight; judgment is His
              reluctant necessity when all other options are
              refused.

Precept upon Precept — Either: (1) God's patient, incremental
              method of teaching, or (2) the leaders' mocking
              imitation of Isaiah's "repetitive" preaching.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.5 — ASV: "Jehovah of hosts" / WEB: "Yahweh of Armies" / NET: "the Lord of Heaven's Armies" — The contrast: Ephraim's fading crown vs. YHWH becoming the true crown of glory for His remnant. The divine name in ASV/WEB makes this personal — God Himself becomes their adornment.

- v.10 — NET: "Indeed, they will hear meaningless gibberish, senseless babbling, a syllable here, a syllable there" — KJV/ASV/WEB retain the Hebrew formula "precept upon precept, line upon line" (tsav la-tsav, qav la-qav). NET interprets this as mocking gibberish, losing the ambiguity of whether it's God's patient teaching method or the mockers' parody.

- v.15 — KJV: "with hell are we at agreement" / ASV/WEB: "with Sheol are we at agreement" / NET: "with Sheol we have made an agreement" — KJV translates "Sheol" as "hell," which imports later Christian theology. ASV/WEB/NET retain "Sheol" (the grave/underworld), which is more accurate to the Hebrew concept of the realm of the dead.

- v.16 — ASV: "the Lord Jehovah" / WEB: "the Lord Yahweh" / NET: "the Sovereign Lord" — At the cornerstone prophecy, God speaks with full authority. NET renders "Adonai YHWH" as "Sovereign Lord" — adding interpretive weight but losing the revealed name that grounds this messianic promise in covenant.

- v.16 — NET: "The one who maintains his faith will not panic" — KJV: "he that believeth shall not make haste." ASV: "he that believeth shall not be in haste." WEB: "He who believes shall not act hastily." NET's "maintains his faith" and "panic" are more interpretive than "believeth/not make haste," but effectively capture the Hebrew sense of steady trust vs. frantic alliance-seeking.

- v.21 — WEB: "his unusual work... his extraordinary act" / KJV/ASV: "his strange work... his strange act" / NET: "his peculiar work... his strange task" — The Hebrew "zar" and "nokriyah" both mean foreign/alien. WEB's "unusual/extraordinary" softens the alienness. The point is that judgment feels FOREIGN to God's nature — it's not His native work.

- v.29 — ASV: "This also cometh forth from Jehovah of hosts, who is wonderful in counsel" / WEB: "from Yahweh of Armies, who is wonderful in counsel" / NET: "from the Lord of Heaven's Armies, who gives supernatural guidance" — NET's "supernatural guidance" interprets where KJV/ASV/WEB say "wonderful in counsel." The farmer's wisdom comes from God — NET makes the divine origin explicit but loses the poetic subtlety.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. What "covenants with death" are you trusting? (v.15). Anything
   you rely on for security INSTEAD of God is, ultimately, a pact
   with death. Career, savings, relationships, political power —
   all good things, but lethal substitutes for God as foundation.

2. The cornerstone (v.16). There is ONE sure foundation. Have you
   built your life on it — or are you still constructing on the
   refuge of lies? Christ is tested, proven, precious. He doesn't
   crack under pressure.

3. "He that believeth shall not make haste" (v.16). Faith
   produces CALM. Panic is a sign of misplaced trust. When you're
   rushing frantically from one solution to another, ask: have I
   forgotten the cornerstone?

4. God's "strange work" (v.21). Judgment grieves God. It's not
   His preferred activity. If you're experiencing discipline,
   know that it's costing God something too — but your rebellion
   left Him no gentler option.

5. The farmer parable (vv.23-29). God uses different methods for
   different situations — dill isn't threshed like wheat. Stop
   comparing your situation to someone else's. God knows exactly
   what method YOUR growth requires.
