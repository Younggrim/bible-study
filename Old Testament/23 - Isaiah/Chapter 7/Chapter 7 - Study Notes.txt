Isaiah - Chapter 7
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
During the Syro-Ephraimite crisis, God tells King Ahaz not to fear and offers him any sign — Ahaz refuses, and Isaiah gives the sign anyway: "A virgin shall conceive and bear a son, and shall call his name Immanuel."

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Isaiah son of Amoz (see Chapter 1 notes for full details).

Classification: Prophetic Narrative — The Immanuel Sign
Key Themes: Faith tested by political crisis, the offer of a
sign refused, the virgin birth prophecy, Immanuel ("God with us"),
Assyria as God's razor shaving Judah bare

Historical Context: Approximately 735 BC. Syria (under Rezin) and
the northern kingdom Israel (under Pekah) form an alliance to
resist Assyria. They want Judah to join; when Ahaz refuses, they
invade to replace him with a puppet king ("the son of Tabeal,"
v.6). Ahaz panics. God sends Isaiah with his son Shear-jashub
("a remnant shall return") to meet Ahaz at the aqueduct — telling
him: DON'T fear these two "smoking stubs of firebrands." God
offers any sign Ahaz wants (v.11). Ahaz refuses (pious excuse:
"I won't test God") — actually because he's already decided to
appeal to Assyria for help (2 Kings 16:7). Isaiah gives the sign
anyway: the virgin/young woman will bear Immanuel. Before the
child knows right from wrong, BOTH threatening nations will be
destroyed. But because Ahaz chose Assyria over God, Assyria will
also devastate Judah (vv.17-25).

Structure:
- The Syro-Ephraimite Threat (vv.1-2)
- Isaiah Meets Ahaz: "Fear Not" (vv.3-9)
- The Offer of a Sign (vv.10-11)
- Ahaz Refuses (v.12)
- The Immanuel Sign Given (vv.13-16)
- Assyria as God's Razor (vv.17-25)

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.4  — "Take heed, and be quiet; fear not, neither be
        fainthearted for the two tails of these smoking firebrands"
        (KJV) — Syria and Israel are not raging fires but SMOKING
        STUBS — almost burned out. Their threat is spent. Don't
        panic over dying embers.

v.9  — "If ye will not believe, surely ye shall not be
        established" (KJV) — A wordplay in Hebrew: "im lo ta'aminu
        ki lo te'amenu" — if you will not be FIRM (in faith), you
        will not be CONFIRMED (in security). Faith is the foundation
        of stability.

v.14 — "Therefore the Lord himself shall give you a sign; Behold,
        a virgin shall conceive, and bear a son, and shall call his
        name Immanuel" (KJV) — THE messianic prophecy. "Virgin"
        (almah = young woman of marriageable age; LXX translates
        parthenos = virgin). Matthew 1:23 confirms the virgin birth
        fulfillment in Mary and Jesus.

v.14 — "Immanuel" — Hebrew "God with us." The name IS the
        theology: God doesn't just SEND help — He COMES. The
        Incarnation in a single word.

v.15 — "Butter and honey shall he eat, that he may know to refuse
        the evil, and choose the good" (KJV) — Before this child
        reaches the age of moral discernment, the threatening kings
        will be gone. Near fulfillment: within a few years. Ultimate
        fulfillment: Christ.

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.3  — "Shearjashub" (KJV) / "Shear-jashub" (ESV) — Hebrew
      "she'ar yashub" = a remnant shall return. Isaiah's son
      is a living sign: judgment is coming, but not total — a
      remnant survives.

v.14 — "virgin" (KJV) / "virgin" (ESV) — Hebrew "almah" = a
      young woman of marriageable age. The word itself doesn't
      EXCLUSIVELY mean "virgin" (that would be "betulah"), but in
      context, the sign requires something extraordinary. The LXX
      translators (200 BC) chose "parthenos" (virgin) — and
      Matthew follows the LXX. The supernatural element IS the sign.

v.14 — "Immanuel" (KJV) / "Immanuel" (ESV) — Hebrew "immanu-El"
      = with-us-God. A compound name: "immanu" (with us) + "El"
      (God). Not "God is with us" (a statement) but "God-with-us"
      (a name/identity).

v.15 — "butter" (KJV) / "curds" (ESV) — Hebrew "chem'ah" =
      thickened milk/curds. A food of pastoral simplicity — the
      land reduced from agricultural abundance to pastoral survival.

v.20 — "razor" (KJV/ESV) — Hebrew "ta'ar" = a barber's razor.
      God will "shave" Judah with Assyria — stripping everything
      (head hair = glory, beard = dignity, body hair = even
      intimate exposure). Total humiliation.

v.3 — ASV: uses "Jehovah" for KJV's "LORD" (Hebrew YHWH).
       NET: "“Go out with your son Shear Jashub and" for KJV's "Go forth now to"
       WEB: uses "Yahweh" for KJV's "LORD" (Hebrew YHWH).
v.14 — ASV: "sign: behold," for KJV's "sign; Behold,"
       NET: "confirming sign. Look, this young woman is about to conce..." for KJV's "sign; Behold,"
v.15 — ASV: "knoweth" for KJV's "may know"
       NET: "honey, which will help him" for KJV's "honey shall he eat, that he may"
       WEB: "knows" for KJV's "eat, that he may know"
v.20 — ASV: "hired in the parts" for KJV's "hired, namely, by them"
       NET: "banks of the Euphrates River," for KJV's "river, by"
       WEB: "hired in the parts" for KJV's "hired, namely, by them"

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "If ye will not believe, ye shall not be established" (v.9).
   Faith and stability are connected. When you refuse to trust
   God in a crisis, you guarantee instability. What crisis are
   you facing where you're choosing human solutions (like Ahaz
   choosing Assyria) over God's promise?

2. Ahaz wrapped his faithlessness in piety: "I will not test the
   LORD" (v.12). He sounded spiritual while actually disobeying.
   Where do you use religious language to disguise disobedience?
   "I'm just trusting God" can mean "I'm too afraid to act."
   "I won't tempt God" can mean "I've already made my own plan."

3. God gave the sign ANYWAY (v.14). Ahaz's refusal didn't stop
   God's plan. Human disobedience doesn't derail divine purpose.
   Your faithlessness limits YOUR experience of God's blessing
   but doesn't limit GOD.

4. "Immanuel" — God WITH us. The ultimate answer to every fear.
   Syria threatening? God with us. Assyria coming? God with us.
   The solution isn't removal of the threat but presence of God
   IN the threat. Is God's presence enough for you even if
   circumstances don't change?

5. Ahaz's "solution" (Assyria) became his greatest problem
   (vv.17-25). The thing you run to instead of God often becomes
   a bigger threat than the original crisis. What worldly
   "rescue" are you pursuing that might end up consuming you?
