Isaiah - Chapter 50
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
The third Servant Song — the Servant has "the tongue of the learned" to sustain the weary, offers His back to smiters and His cheeks to beard-pluckers, sets His face like flint toward Jerusalem, and trusts God to vindicate Him. Those who walk in darkness should trust; those who kindle their own fire will lie down in sorrow.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Isaiah son of Amoz (see Chapter 1 notes for full details).

Classification: Prophetic Oracle — Third Servant Song (vv.4-9)
Key Themes: God didn't divorce Israel, the Servant's obedient
ear, suffering willingly endured, face set like flint, trust in
God amid opposition, the danger of self-made light

Historical Context: The third Servant Song (50:4-9) reveals the
Servant's OBEDIENCE amid suffering. Unlike the first song
(commission) and second (discouragement overcome), this song
shows the Servant voluntarily submitting to physical abuse. He
offers His back, His cheeks, His face — actively presenting
Himself to suffering. "I gave my back to the smiters" — not "they
took it" but "I GAVE it." Luke 9:51: "He steadfastly set his
face to go to Jerusalem" directly echoes v.7. The chapter frames
the song with God's defense of Israel (vv.1-3) and a choice for
the listener: trust God in darkness (v.10) or make your own light
and suffer (v.11).

Structure:
- God Didn't Divorce Israel (vv.1-3)
- Third Servant Song: Obedient Suffering (vv.4-9)
- Trust in Darkness vs. Self-Made Fire (vv.10-11)

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.4  — "The Lord GOD hath given me the tongue of the learned,
        that I should know how to speak a word in season to him
        that is weary" (KJV) — The Servant's gift: knowing WHAT
        to say and WHEN to say it. His words sustain the exhausted.
        Not academic learning but divine wisdom for weary people.

v.5  — "The Lord GOD hath opened mine ear, and I was not
        rebellious, neither turned away back" (KJV) — The
        Servant's obedience begins with HEARING. His ear is opened
        (like a servant having his ear pierced, Exodus 21:6) and
        He does NOT rebel or retreat.

v.6  — "I gave my back to the smiters, and my cheeks to them
        that plucked off the hair: I hid not my face from shame
        and spitting" (KJV) — Physical abuse: flogging, beard-
        ripping, spitting. All fulfilled in Christ's passion
        (Matt 26:67; 27:26,30). Note: "I GAVE" — voluntary.

v.7  — "For the Lord GOD will help me; therefore shall I not be
        confounded: therefore have I set my face like a flint"
        (KJV) — Flint = the hardest common stone. The Servant's
        resolution is rock-hard: He will NOT turn back because
        God will vindicate Him. Luke 9:51: "He steadfastly set
        his face to go to Jerusalem."

v.10 — "Who is among you that feareth the LORD... that walketh in
        darkness, and hath no light? let him trust in the name of
        the LORD, and stay upon his God" (KJV) — The prescription
        for dark seasons: TRUST. Don't demand light; trust the God
        you knew in the light even when you can't see Him.

v.11 — "Behold, all ye that kindle a fire, that compass yourselves
        about with sparks: walk in the light of your fire...
        this shall ye have of mine hand; ye shall lie down in
        sorrow" (KJV) — WARNING: making your own light (self-
        reliance, manufactured solutions) leads to lying down in
        sorrow. Don't kindle your own fire; wait for God's dawn.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.4  — "tongue of the learned" (KJV) / "tongue of those who are
      taught" (ESV) — Hebrew "leshon limudim" = tongue of
      disciples/pupils. The Servant speaks as one who has first
      LISTENED and LEARNED from God.

v.6  — "plucked off the hair" (KJV) / "pulled out the beard"
      (ESV) — Hebrew "meretim" = those who pull/pluck. Beard-
      pulling was extreme humiliation in the ancient Near East —
      worse than hitting.

v.7  — "flint" (KJV/ESV) — Hebrew "challamish" = flint stone,
      extremely hard. Flint was used for cutting tools in ancient
      times. The Servant's resolve is as hard as the hardest stone.

v.11 — "sparks" (KJV) / "torches" (ESV) — Hebrew "ziqot" =
      sparks, fiery darts, or firebrands. Self-kindled light:
      artificial, temporary, inadequate. Contrasted with God's
      true light.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Third Servant Song — Isaiah 50:4-9. The Servant reveals His
              obedient ear, voluntary suffering, and flint-like
              resolve. The most "human" portrait: we see His inner
              determination and trust.

Face Like Flint — Absolute, unshakeable determination. The
              Servant will not be turned from His mission by shame,
              pain, or opposition. Applied to Christ's journey to
              the cross.

Self-Kindled Fire — (v.11) Human attempts to create your own
              light/guidance apart from God. It produces temporary
              warmth but ends in sorrow. The alternative: wait in
              darkness trusting God's timing.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "I gave my back" (v.6). Jesus GAVE. Every blow was voluntary.
   He could have called 10,000 angels (Matt 26:53). He chose to
   stay. His suffering was not victimhood but LOVE choosing pain
   for your freedom.

2. "Set my face like a flint" (v.7). Following God requires
   RESOLVE — flint-hard determination that doesn't flinch at
   pain or turn aside at opposition. What is God calling you
   toward that requires this kind of flint-face?

3. Walk in darkness and TRUST (v.10). Sometimes God-fearing
   people are in darkness — not because of sin but because of
   season. The instruction: TRUST. Don't demand explanations;
   trust the God you knew in the light.

4. Don't kindle your own fire (v.11). When God seems silent and
   the darkness presses in, the temptation is to manufacture
   your own solutions, guidance, and light. But self-made light
   leads to sorrow. WAIT for God's dawn rather than lighting
   your own candle.

5. "A word in season to him that is weary" (v.4). The Servant's
   gift: knowing what weary people need to hear. Ask God for this
   gift — the ability to speak the right word at the right time
   to exhausted people.
