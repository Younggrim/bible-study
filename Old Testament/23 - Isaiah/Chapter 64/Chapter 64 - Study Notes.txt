Isaiah - Chapter 64
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
The prayer of 63:15 continues with passionate intensity: "Oh that thou wouldest rend the heavens and come down!" We are all unclean, our righteousnesses are filthy rags, we fade like leaves — yet "thou art our Father; we are the clay, and thou our potter." Don't be angry forever.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Isaiah son of Amoz (see Chapter 1 notes for full details).

Classification: Communal Lament and Confession
Key Themes: Prayer for God to rend the heavens, the incomparable
God who acts for those who wait, universal uncleanness, filthy
rags righteousness, fading like autumn leaves, God as Father and
Potter, appeal for mercy

Historical Context: This chapter continues the prayer begun in
63:15. It combines desperate longing for God's intervention with
honest confession of Israel's unworthiness. The prayer moves from
bold request ("rend the heavens!") to humble confession ("we are
all as an unclean thing") to trusting appeal ("thou art our
Father"). The language of v.6 ("filthy rags") became one of
Protestantism's key texts on human inability to achieve self-
righteousness. The chapter ends with a question hanging in the
air: "Wilt thou refrain thyself for these things?" (v.12) — God's
answer comes in chapters 65-66.

Structure:
- Rend the Heavens and Come Down! (vv.1-3)
- No Eye Has Seen What God Prepares (v.4)
- We Are All Unclean: Filthy Rags (vv.5-7)
- Yet Thou Art Our Father, Our Potter (v.8)
- Remember: We Are Your People (vv.9-12)

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "Oh that thou wouldest rend the heavens, that thou wouldest
        come down, that the mountains might flow down at thy
        presence" (KJV) — The most desperate prayer in Isaiah:
        TEAR OPEN the sky and COME. Mountains melting = theophany
        language. Nothing less than God's visible arrival will do.

v.4  — "For since the beginning of the world men have not heard,
        nor perceived by the ear, neither hath the eye seen, O God,
        beside thee, what he hath prepared for him that waiteth for
        him" (KJV) — Paul quotes this in 1 Corinthians 2:9. No
        human sense can perceive what God has prepared for those
        who love Him.

v.6  — "But we are all as an unclean thing, and all our
        righteousnesses are as filthy rags; and we all do fade as
        a leaf; and our iniquities, like the wind, have taken us
        away" (KJV) — Universal confession. Not just our SINS are
        unclean — our RIGHTEOUSNESSES (best efforts) are filthy
        rags. If even our good deeds are polluted, we have no
        currency before God.

v.8  — "But now, O LORD, thou art our father; we are the clay,
        and thou our potter; and we all are the work of thy hand"
        (KJV) — After confession of total unworthiness, the appeal
        shifts to RELATIONSHIP (Father) and SOVEREIGNTY (Potter).
        Not "we deserve mercy" but "You are our Father and Maker."

v.12 — "Wilt thou refrain thyself for these things, O LORD? wilt
        thou hold thy peace, and afflict us very sore?" (KJV) —
        The prayer ends with a hanging question: will You remain
        silent? Will You withhold Yourself? The answer: chapters
        65-66.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "rend" (KJV) / "tear open" (ESV) — Hebrew "qara" = to
      rip, tear apart. Violent imagery — not a gentle opening but
      a ripping through. Mark 1:10 uses the same concept (Greek
      "schizo") at Jesus' baptism.

v.6  — "filthy rags" (KJV) / "polluted garment" (ESV) — Hebrew
      "beged iddim" = a garment of menstruation/flux. Ritually
      unclean in the most visceral way (Leviticus 15:19-24).
      Our best efforts are ritually polluted.

v.6  — "fade as a leaf" (KJV/ESV) — Hebrew "nabbol ke-aleh" =
      wither like foliage. Autumn imagery: once green and alive,
      now brown, curling, falling, blown away by wind. Transience.

v.8  — "potter" (KJV/ESV) — Hebrew "yotser" = one who forms/
      shapes. Used for God forming Adam from clay (Genesis 2:7).
      The same hands that formed you can RE-form you.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Rend the Heavens — The desperate prayer for theophany: God
              tearing through the barrier between heaven and earth
              to arrive personally. Answered at the incarnation
              and baptism of Christ.

Filthy Rags  — (Hebrew: beged iddim) Our best human righteousness
              compared to ritually unclean garments. Not hyperbole
              but honest assessment: even our "good" is infected
              by sin.

Father/Potter — The double appeal (v.8): God as FATHER (relational
              warmth, obligation to children) and POTTER (creative
              sovereignty, right to reshape). Both invoke His
              compassion.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.4 — NET: "no eye has seen any God besides you, who intervenes for those who wait for him" / KJV: "neither hath the eye seen, O God, beside thee, what he hath prepared for him that waiteth for him" — The KJV reading (quoted by Paul in 1 Corinthians 2:9) emphasizes what God HAS PREPARED; NET emphasizes who God IS. The Hebrew is genuinely difficult but the "prepared" reading has stronger NT support.

- v.5 — NET: "How then can we be saved?" / KJV: "in those is continuance, and we shall be saved" / WEB: "Shall we be saved?" — The Hebrew here is extremely difficult. KJV reads hopefully (we SHALL be saved); NET/WEB read it as a question of doubt. ASV: "shall we be saved?" The tone matters: is this despair or hope?

- v.6 — NET: "all our so-called righteous acts are like a menstrual rag in your sight" / KJV: "all our righteousnesses are as filthy rags" / ASV: "all our righteousnesses are as a polluted garment" — NET is explicit about what Hebrew "beged iddim" means (menstrual cloth), making the shocking nature of the image clear. KJV's "filthy rags" is somewhat euphemistic. Even our BEST deeds are ceremonially unclean before a holy God.

- v.8 — ASV: "But now, O Jehovah, thou art our Father" / WEB: "But now, Yahweh, you are our Father" / NET: "Yet, Lord, you are our father" — The divine name with "Father" — the second time in this prayer (see 63:16). ASV/WEB preserve the personal covenant name in this intimate address.

- v.9 — ASV: "Be not wroth very sore, O Jehovah" / WEB: "Don't be furious, Yahweh" / NET: "Lord, do not be too angry!" — The direct address uses the divine name in ASV/WEB. The plea "don't remember iniquity FOREVER" implies acknowledgment that remembering it temporarily was just.

- v.12 — NET: "In light of all this, how can you still hold back, Lord? How can you be silent and continue to humiliate us?" / KJV: "Wilt thou refrain thyself for these things, O LORD? wilt thou hold thy peace, and afflict us very sore?" — NET paraphrases into modern speech. The Hebrew asks: will you RESTRAIN yourself when we need action? The prayer ends without an answer — chapter 65 provides God's response.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Rend the heavens!" (v.1). Have you ever prayed with this kind
   of desperation? Sometimes polite prayer isn't enough. There are
   seasons that demand: "God, TEAR OPEN the sky and COME." Bold
   prayer honors God's power.

2. "All our righteousnesses are filthy rags" (v.6). Your BEST
   efforts — your most sincere worship, your most sacrificial
   giving, your most disciplined obedience — are still stained.
   Not because effort is useless, but because you need a
   righteousness not your own. Christ provides it.

3. "We are the clay, thou our potter" (v.8). When you've
   confessed total bankruptcy, your only appeal is RELATIONSHIP
   and SOVEREIGNTY. "You made me — will You unmake me? You are
   my Father — will You abandon Your child?" The answer: never.

4. "What he hath prepared for him that waiteth" (v.4). The
   unimaginable awaits. Whatever you're suffering now, whatever
   you're denied now — God has PREPARED something your eyes
   haven't seen and your ears haven't heard. Keep waiting.

5. The prayer ends unanswered (v.12) — but God DOES answer
   (chs.65-66). Your prayers that seem to hang in the air
   WILL be answered. Sometimes the answer comes in the next
   chapter of your life.
