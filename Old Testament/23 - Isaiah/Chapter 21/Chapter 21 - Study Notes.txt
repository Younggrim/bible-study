Isaiah - Chapter 21
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Three oracles compressed into one chapter: the fall of Babylon ("Desert of the Sea"), the burden on Dumah (Edom), and the burden on Arabia. Isaiah sees a terrifying vision of Babylon's fall, a watchman's report, and pronounces swift judgment on Edom and the Arabian tribes.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Isaiah son of Amoz (see Chapter 1 notes for full details).

Classification: Prophetic Oracle — Burden of the Desert of the
Sea / Burden of Dumah / Burden upon Arabia
Key Themes: The fall of Babylon to Medo-Persia, the watchman
motif, the emotional toll on the prophet, Edom's uncertain future,
Arabia's fleeting glory

Historical Context: This chapter contains one of Isaiah's most
dramatic visions. The "desert of the sea" (v.1) refers to Babylon
(the marshlands of southern Mesopotamia). Isaiah sees Babylon's
fall — "Babylon is fallen, is fallen" (v.9) — a prophecy
fulfilled in 539 BC when Cyrus the Persian conquered Babylon.
The Dumah oracle (vv.11-12) addresses Edom with an enigmatic
watchman exchange. The Arabia oracle (vv.13-17) predicts the
decline of the Dedanite and Kedarite Arab tribes within one year.
These oracles likely date to different periods of Isaiah's
ministry but are grouped thematically as desert-region burdens.

Structure:
- The Desert of the Sea: Babylon's Fall (vv.1-10)
- The Burden of Dumah/Edom (vv.11-12)
- The Burden upon Arabia (vv.13-17)

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "A grievous vision is declared unto me; the treacherous
        dealer dealeth treacherously, and the spoiler spoileth"
        (KJV) — The vision is painful to receive. Isaiah doesn't
        enjoy pronouncing doom — the weight of prophetic sight
        is heavy.

v.6  — "Go, set a watchman, let him declare what he seeth" (KJV)
        — The watchman motif: someone stationed to observe and
        report. The prophet as God's watchman, alerting the people
        to what's coming.

v.9  — "Babylon is fallen, is fallen; and all the graven images
        of her gods he hath broken unto the ground" (KJV) —
        Doubled declaration of certainty. Echoed in Revelation
        18:2: "Babylon the great is fallen, is fallen." The
        idols of the world's greatest empire shattered.

v.11-12 — "Watchman, what of the night? Watchman, what of the
        night? The watchman said, The morning cometh, and also
        the night" (KJV) — One of Scripture's most cryptic
        exchanges. Morning comes (hope) but also night (continued
        darkness). For Edom, the answer is ambiguous — it depends
        on whether they seek God.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "desert of the sea" (KJV/ESV) — Hebrew "midbar-yam."
      Babylon was located in marshlands between the Tigris and
      Euphrates. "Desert of the sea" = the arid region that
      becomes like a sea (flooded marshland).

v.2  — "Elam... Media" (KJV/ESV) — The nations God sends to
      besiege Babylon. Elam (southwestern Iran) and Media
      (northwestern Iran) = the Medo-Persian empire that
      conquered Babylon under Cyrus in 539 BC.

v.11 — "Dumah" (KJV/ESV) — Wordplay: "Dumah" means "silence"
      in Hebrew. It's also a region of Edom (cf. Genesis 25:14).
      The oracle about Edom is the "silence" oracle — appropriate
      for its cryptic, unanswered quality.

v.13 — "Dedanites" (ESV) / "Dedanim" (KJV) — Arabian merchant
      tribe descended from Abraham through Keturah (Genesis 25:3).
      Major traders in the Arabian peninsula.

v.16 — "as the years of an hireling" (KJV) — Precisely counted
      time (a hired worker counts each day exactly). Within
      exactly one year — no ambiguity.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Desert of the Sea — Poetic name for Babylon's region: the
              marshlands between the rivers that appeared as a
              "sea" in the flat desert landscape.

Watchman     — A sentinel posted on city walls or towers to
              observe approaching danger and report. The prophet
              functions as God's spiritual watchman for His people.

Dumah        — "Silence" — a region associated with Edom
              (Idumea). The name becomes symbolic: God's response
              to Edom is hauntingly brief and ambiguous.

Kedar        — Arabian tribe descended from Ishmael (Genesis
              25:13). Nomadic herders known for black goat-hair
              tents and skilled archers.

Dedanites    — Arabian merchant tribe operating trade routes
              through the desert. Forced to hide in the thickets
              (v.13) when their trade routes become war zones.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.9 — ASV: "Fallen, fallen is Babylon; and all the graven images of her gods are broken unto the ground" / WEB: "Fallen, fallen is Babylon; and all the engraved images of her gods are broken to the ground" / NET: "Babylon has fallen, fallen! All the idols of her gods lie shattered on the ground!" — All three preserve the dramatic doubled "fallen, fallen" — the exact phrase echoed in Revelation 14:8 and 18:2. NET changes "graven images" to "idols" — a generic term replacing the specific Hebrew "pesilei" (carved images).

- v.10 — ASV: "that which I have heard from Jehovah of hosts, the God of Israel, have I declared unto you" / WEB: "That which I have heard from Yahweh of Armies, the God of Israel, I have declared to you" / NET: "what I have heard from the Lord of Heaven's Armies, the God of Israel, I have reported to you" — The prophet authenticates his message with the full divine title. ASV/WEB preserve the covenant name; NET uses expanded title. "Declared" (ASV/WEB) vs. "reported" (NET) — "declared" has prophetic authority; "reported" sounds journalistic.

- v.1 — ASV: "The burden of the wilderness of the sea" / WEB: same / NET: "This is an oracle about the wilderness by the Sea" — The enigmatic title "midbar-yam" (wilderness of the sea). NET adds "by the Sea" as if it's a geographic label; KJV/ASV/WEB preserve the paradoxical combination of "wilderness" and "sea" that marks Babylon's strange landscape.

- v.11 — NET: "Someone calls to me from Seir, 'Watchman, what is left of the night?'" vs. ASV/WEB: "One calleth unto me out of Seir, Watchman, what of the night?" — NET adds "is left of" (interpretive). The Hebrew is starker: "What of the night?" — a more existential question about the nature of the darkness, not just its remaining duration.

- v.16 — ASV: "Within a year, according to the years of a hireling" / WEB: "Within a year, as a worker bound by contract would count it" / NET: "Within exactly one year" — WEB helpfully explains the "hireling" idiom ("as a worker bound by contract would count it"). NET simply uses "exactly" — conveying the precision but losing the agricultural/labor imagery of counted time.

- v.17 — ASV: "for Jehovah, the God of Israel, hath spoken it" / WEB: "for Yahweh, the God of Israel, has spoken it" / NET: "the Lord God of Israel has spoken" — The closing authentication formula. When "Yahweh, the God of Israel has spoken it," the prophecy is guaranteed. ASV/WEB preserve the personal name + covenant title; NET uses the generic "Lord God."

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Babylon ALWAYS falls (v.9). Every human system that exalts
   itself against God — political, economic, cultural — eventually
   collapses. "Babylon is fallen" is not just ancient history;
   it's the pattern of all godless power. What "Babylons" in your
   world seem permanent but aren't?

2. Prophetic ministry is emotionally costly (vv.3-4). Isaiah's
   "loins filled with pain," "pangs" like childbirth, "bowed
   down," "dismayed." Seeing what God sees is not comfortable.
   Spiritual insight comes with emotional weight.

3. "Watchman, what of the night?" (v.11). The most human question
   in Isaiah. "How much longer?" "Is dawn coming?" The answer:
   morning IS coming — but so is more night. Hope is real, but
   so is continued struggle. Both are true.

4. The countdown is precise (v.16). "Within a year" — God's
   judgments have timetables. He is not vague or random. If He
   has spoken, He will perform — and on schedule.
