Isaiah - Chapter 20
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
God commands Isaiah to walk naked and barefoot for three years as a living sign that Assyria will strip Egypt and Cush bare — exposing the foolishness of Judah's trust in those nations for protection against Assyria.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Isaiah son of Amoz (see Chapter 1 notes for full details).

Classification: Prophetic Sign-Act — The Naked Prophet
Key Themes: Embodied prophecy, the humiliation of Egypt and Cush,
the folly of trusting in human alliances, Assyria as God's
instrument, a dated prophecy (year of Assyrian conquest of Ashdod)

Historical Context: The chapter is precisely dated: "the year
that Tartan came unto Ashdod, when Sargon the king of Assyria
sent him" (v.1) — 711 BC. Sargon II sent his commander (Tartan)
to besiege Ashdod, a Philistine city that had rebelled with
Egyptian backing. This event proved that Egypt's promises of
military support were empty — Egypt couldn't even protect its
own allies. God told Isaiah to walk stripped (likely to a loin
cloth, the garb of a war captive/slave) for THREE YEARS as a
visual object lesson: this is what Assyria will do to Egypt and
Cush. The audience is Judah — stop trusting Egypt!

Structure:
- The Historical Setting: Ashdod Falls (v.1)
- God's Command: Walk Naked and Barefoot (v.2)
- The Interpretation: Egypt and Cush Exposed (vv.3-4)
- Judah's Dismay: Their Hope is Shamed (vv.5-6)

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "Go and loose the sackcloth from off thy loins, and put
        off thy shoe from thy foot. And he did so, walking naked
        and barefoot" (KJV) — God asks Isaiah to sacrifice his
        dignity for three years to deliver a prophetic message.
        The cost of prophetic ministry: public humiliation for
        the sake of truth.

v.3  — "Like as my servant Isaiah hath walked naked and barefoot
        three years for a sign and wonder upon Egypt and upon
        Ethiopia" (KJV) — Three years of embodied prophecy. Every
        day for three years, Isaiah's appearance preached a sermon
        that words alone could not communicate.

v.5  — "And they shall be afraid and ashamed of Ethiopia their
        expectation, and of Egypt their glory" (KJV) — Judah's
        pro-Egypt party will be humiliated. Their "expectation"
        (Cush) and their "glory" (Egypt) will fail them. Those who
        trust in human power instead of God will be put to shame.

v.6  — "And the inhabitant of this isle shall say in that day,
        Behold, such is our expectation, whither we flee for help
        to be delivered from the king of Assyria: and how shall we
        escape?" (KJV) — The final rhetorical question: if Egypt
        can't save itself, how will it save YOU? The bankruptcy of
        human-centered hope exposed.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "Tartan" (KJV/ESV) — Not a personal name but an Assyrian
      military title (turtanu) = commander-in-chief, second in
      rank to the king.

v.2  — "naked" (KJV/ESV) — Hebrew "arom" = stripped. Does not
      necessarily mean completely unclothed. Likely stripped to a
      loin cloth — the appearance of a prisoner of war. Still
      profoundly shameful in Israelite culture.

v.3  — "sign and wonder" (KJV/ESV) — Hebrew "oth u-mofet" =
      prophetic sign and portent. The same phrase used for Moses'
      miracles (Exodus 7:3). Isaiah's body became the miracle.

v.6  — "isle" (KJV) / "coastland" (ESV) — Hebrew "i" = coastland,
      island, maritime region. Refers to the inhabitants of the
      Palestinian coast (including Judah) who looked to Egypt.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Tartan       — Assyrian military title (turtanu): the supreme
              field commander, second only to the king. Not a
              personal name.

Sargon II    — King of Assyria 722-705 BC. Completed the conquest
              of Samaria and campaigned against Ashdod (711 BC).
              Father of Sennacherib.

Ashdod       — One of the five Philistine cities, located on the
              Mediterranean coast. Rebelled against Assyria with
              Egyptian encouragement — and was crushed, proving
              Egypt's unreliability.

Sign-Act     — A prophetic action performed as a living parable.
              The prophet's body becomes the message. Other
              examples: Ezekiel's siege model (Ezek 4), Jeremiah's
              yoke (Jer 27), Hosea's marriage (Hos 1).

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. God's servants sometimes pay extreme personal costs to deliver
   His message (v.2). Isaiah walked in public humiliation for
   three years. Ministry is not always dignified. Are you willing
   to be uncomfortable, misunderstood, or embarrassed if that's
   what faithfulness requires?

2. The things we trust instead of God will eventually be exposed
   (vv.5-6). Judah trusted Egypt; Egypt fell. Whatever you're
   leaning on that isn't God — career, wealth, political power,
   relationships — can you imagine how you'd feel if it were
   "stripped bare" tomorrow?

3. A three-year sermon (v.3). Isaiah's obedience wasn't a one-time
   act but a sustained, daily choice. Faithfulness is usually not
   dramatic — it's showing up stripped and barefoot again today,
   and tomorrow, and the day after.

4. "How shall we escape?" (v.6). The question hangs unanswered.
   If your human hope fails, where will you turn? The chapter
   implies the answer: back to God, who is the only reliable
   refuge.
