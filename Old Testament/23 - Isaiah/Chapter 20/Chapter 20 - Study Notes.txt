Isaiah - Chapter 20
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
God commands Isaiah to walk naked and barefoot for three years as a living sign that Assyria will strip Egypt and Cush bare — exposing the foolishness of Judah's trust in those nations for protection against Assyria.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Isaiah son of Amoz (see Chapter 1 notes for full details).

Classification: Prophetic Sign-Act — The Naked Prophet
Key Themes: Embodied prophecy, the humiliation of Egypt and Cush,
the folly of trusting in human alliances, Assyria as God's
instrument, a dated prophecy (year of Assyrian conquest of Ashdod)

Historical Context: The chapter is precisely dated: "the year
that Tartan came unto Ashdod, when Sargon the king of Assyria
sent him" (v.1) — 711 BC. Sargon II sent his commander (Tartan)
to besiege Ashdod, a Philistine city that had rebelled with
Egyptian backing. This event proved that Egypt's promises of
military support were empty — Egypt couldn't even protect its
own allies. God told Isaiah to walk stripped (likely to a loin
cloth, the garb of a war captive/slave) for THREE YEARS as a
visual object lesson: this is what Assyria will do to Egypt and
Cush. The audience is Judah — stop trusting Egypt!

Structure:
- The Historical Setting: Ashdod Falls (v.1)
- God's Command: Walk Naked and Barefoot (v.2)
- The Interpretation: Egypt and Cush Exposed (vv.3-4)
- Judah's Dismay: Their Hope is Shamed (vv.5-6)

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.1   — 2 Kings 18:17 — Tartan (Assyrian military title) later
         sent against Jerusalem itself.
v.2   — Ezekiel 4:1-8 — Ezekiel also performed sign-acts (lying
         on his side 390 days). Embodied prophecy was costly.
v.2   — Micah 1:8 — Micah also walked stripped and barefoot in
         prophetic lament.
v.3   — Isaiah 8:18 — "I and the children... are for signs and
         wonders in Israel."
v.5   — Isaiah 30:1-5 — "Woe to them that go down to Egypt for
         help" — the same warning repeated.
v.5   — Isaiah 31:1-3 — "Woe to them that... look not unto the
         Holy One of Israel."

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. God's servants sometimes pay extreme personal costs to deliver
   His message (v.2). Isaiah walked in public humiliation for
   three years. Ministry is not always dignified. Are you willing
   to be uncomfortable, misunderstood, or embarrassed if that's
   what faithfulness requires?

2. The things we trust instead of God will eventually be exposed
   (vv.5-6). Judah trusted Egypt; Egypt fell. Whatever you're
   leaning on that isn't God — career, wealth, political power,
   relationships — can you imagine how you'd feel if it were
   "stripped bare" tomorrow?

3. A three-year sermon (v.3). Isaiah's obedience wasn't a one-time
   act but a sustained, daily choice. Faithfulness is usually not
   dramatic — it's showing up stripped and barefoot again today,
   and tomorrow, and the day after.

4. "How shall we escape?" (v.6). The question hangs unanswered.
   If your human hope fails, where will you turn? The chapter
   implies the answer: back to God, who is the only reliable
   refuge.
