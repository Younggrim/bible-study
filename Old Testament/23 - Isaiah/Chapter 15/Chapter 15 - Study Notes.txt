Isaiah - Chapter 15
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
The oracle against Moab — in a single night Moab's cities are destroyed, the entire nation weeps and wails, refugees flee southward, and even Isaiah's own heart cries out in compassion for Moab's devastation.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Isaiah son of Amoz (see Chapter 1 notes for full details).

Classification: Prophetic Oracle — Burden Against Moab
Key Themes: Sudden overnight destruction, national lamentation,
refugees fleeing, the prophet's compassion for the judged nation,
water and vegetation dried up

Historical Context: Moab lay east of the Dead Sea (modern Jordan).
Though related to Israel through Lot (Gen 19:37), Moab was
frequently hostile. The oracle (spanning chapters 15-16) describes
a devastating invasion — possibly by Assyria (under Tiglath-pileser
III or Sargon II) or earlier by unnamed raiders. What's remarkable
is Isaiah's COMPASSION: "my heart shall cry out for Moab" (v.5).
This isn't gloating but grief — the prophet weeps for a pagan
nation's suffering. The cities mentioned (Ar, Kir, Dibon, Medeba,
Heshbon, etc.) map the destruction geographically from north to
south — the invasion sweeps the entire country.

Structure:
- Overnight Destruction: Ar and Kir Ruined (vv.1-4)
- Isaiah's Heart Cries for Moab (v.5)
- Refugees Flee South (vv.5-7)
- Weeping Everywhere: Waters of Blood (vv.8-9)

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "In the night Ar of Moab is laid waste, and brought to
        silence; in the night Kir of Moab is laid waste" (KJV) —
        "In the night" — repeated. Destruction comes unexpectedly,
        while they sleep. Two major cities destroyed simultaneously
        before dawn.

v.5  — "My heart shall cry out for Moab" (KJV) — The prophet's
        compassion for an enemy nation. Isaiah doesn't rejoice in
        Moab's judgment — he weeps. God's messengers should deliver
        hard truths with tender hearts.

v.6  — "the waters of Nimrim shall be desolate: for the hay is
        withered away, the grass faileth, there is no green thing"
        (KJV) — Total ecological devastation: water dried up,
        vegetation dead. The land itself mourns — not just the
        people but the environment collapses.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.1   — Jeremiah 48 — An expanded oracle against Moab using much
         of the same language and city names.
v.2   — Numbers 21:30 — "Heshbon is perished" — an earlier
         prophecy of the same region's destruction.
v.5   — Jeremiah 48:36 — "my heart shall sound for Moab like
         pipes" — Jeremiah shares Isaiah's compassion.
v.7   — Isaiah 16:1-5 — Moab's refugees seek shelter in Judah.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "burden" (KJV) / "oracle" (ESV) — Hebrew "massa" = a
      weighty prophetic word (same as 13:1).

v.2  — "Dibon is gone up to the high places to weep" (KJV) —
      "high places" (bamot) = pagan worship sites. They weep at
      their temples — but their gods cannot help.

v.5  — "fugitives" (KJV) / "fugitives" (ESV) — Hebrew "bericheyha"
      = those who flee/escape. Refugees streaming south toward
      Edom, seeking asylum.

v.9  — "Dimon" (KJV/ESV) — likely a variant of "Dibon" with a
      wordplay on "dam" (blood). "The waters of Dimon shall be
      full of blood" — dam/Dimon sound-play.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Moab          — Nation descended from Lot (Abraham's nephew) through
               incest (Gen 19:37). Located east of the Dead Sea in
               modern Jordan. Frequent enemy of Israel despite
               kinship. Ruth was a Moabitess — showing God's grace
               even to enemy nations.

High places   — (Hebrew: bamot) Elevated worship sites, usually
               for pagan deities. Moab weeps at its temples — but
               Chemosh (Moab's god) is powerless to prevent judgment.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.1 — ASV/WEB: "The burden of Moab" / NET: "This is an oracle about Moab" — Again the "massa" (burden/oracle) rendering differs. ASV/WEB preserve "burden" while NET uses "oracle." The burden language fits the lament quality of this chapter — the prophecy weighs heavily even on the prophet.

- v.1 — ASV: "laid waste, and brought to nought" / WEB: "laid waste, and brought to nothing" / NET: "it is devastated... is destroyed!" — NET uses active, dramatic language ("devastated... destroyed") while ASV/WEB use the more passive "laid waste and brought to nothing." The Hebrew "nidmah" means "silenced/destroyed/cut off" — the city becomes silent overnight.

- v.5 — ASV: "My heart crieth out for Moab" / WEB: "My heart cries out for Moab!" / NET: "My heart cries out because of Moab's plight" — All three preserve the remarkable prophetic compassion. NET adds "plight" (interpretive), but the core message is identical: the prophet grieves for a pagan enemy nation.

- v.2 — NET: "Every head is shaved bare, every beard is trimmed off" vs. ASV/WEB: "on all their heads is baldness, every beard is cut off" — Shaving heads and beards were extreme mourning rituals (also forbidden to Israelites in Lev 19:27). NET's "trimmed off" weakens the intensity — "cut off" (ASV/WEB) better conveys the violent grief of complete removal.

- v.9 — ASV: "the waters of Dimon are full of blood" / WEB: same / NET: "the waters of Dimon are full of blood!" — All preserve the wordplay on "Dimon/dam" (blood). NET adds: "I will heap even more trouble on Dimon" where KJV/ASV have "I will bring yet more upon Dimon" — NET interprets "more" as "trouble" (added word), while the Hebrew leaves the nature of what's coming unnamed — more ominous in its vagueness.

- v.4 — NET: "Moab's soldiers shout in distress; their courage wavers" vs. ASV: "the armed men of Moab cry aloud; his soul trembleth within him" / WEB: "the armed men of Moab cry aloud. Their souls tremble within them" — NET's "courage wavers" is a less visceral rendering of Hebrew "naph'sho yar'ah lo" (his soul trembles within him). The literal image is of a warrior's inner self shaking.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. God judges nations that aren't part of His covenant people.
   Moab had no Torah, no temple, no covenant — yet God held them
   accountable. No one escapes divine justice by claiming ignorance
   of God's specific revelation.

2. "My heart shall cry out for Moab" (v.5). Do you weep for the
   people you know are under judgment? Or do you secretly enjoy
   watching enemies suffer? The prophetic heart delivers hard
   truths with real tears.

3. Destruction came "in the night" (v.1) — unexpectedly, while
   Moab slept. Are you spiritually awake? "The day of the Lord
   so cometh as a thief in the night" (1 Thess 5:2). Vigilance
   isn't paranoia — it's wisdom.
