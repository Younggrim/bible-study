Isaiah - Chapter 6
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
In the year King Uzziah dies, Isaiah sees the LORD high and lifted up in the temple, seraphim crying "Holy, holy, holy," is undone by his own sinfulness, cleansed by a coal from the altar, and commissioned to preach to a people who will hear but not understand.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Isaiah son of Amoz (see Chapter 1 notes for full details).

Classification: Prophetic Call Narrative — The Throne Room Vision
Key Themes: The holiness of God, human sinfulness exposed by
divine glory, cleansing through substitutionary atonement (the
coal from the altar), prophetic commissioning, judicial hardening

Historical Context: King Uzziah reigned 52 years (790-739 BC) —
a prosperous, stable era. His death marks the end of security and
the beginning of the Assyrian crisis. In this moment of national
uncertainty, Isaiah sees the TRUE King — the LORD on His throne,
unchanged by earthly political transitions. This is Isaiah's call
narrative (though placed in chapter 6 rather than chapter 1,
suggesting he prophesied before this formal commissioning).
The vision shapes everything that follows: Isaiah has SEEN the
Holy One of Israel, and that vision drives his entire ministry.

The commission itself is devastating: preach, but the people will
NOT understand. Their hearts will grow fat, ears heavy, eyes shut
— UNTIL cities are wasted and the land desolate (vv.11-12). Jesus
quotes this passage to explain why He speaks in parables (Matthew
13:14-15), and Paul uses it as his final word to unbelieving Jews
(Acts 28:26-27).

Structure:
- The Vision: The LORD on His Throne (v.1)
- The Seraphim and the Trisagion (vv.2-4)
- Isaiah's Response: "Woe Is Me!" (v.5)
- The Cleansing Coal (vv.6-7)
- The Commission: "Whom Shall I Send?" (v.8)
- The Hardening Message (vv.9-10)
- "How Long, O Lord?" (vv.11-13)

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "I saw also the Lord sitting upon a throne, high and
        lifted up, and his train filled the temple" (KJV) — Isaiah
        sees what no human should survive seeing. The LORD (Adonai)
        on His throne — His robe's hem alone fills the entire
        temple. The earthly king (Uzziah) is dead; the Eternal
        King reigns undisturbed.

v.3  — "And one cried unto another, and said, Holy, holy, holy,
        is the LORD of hosts: the whole earth is full of his glory"
        (KJV) — The trisagion (thrice-holy). The only divine
        attribute repeated three times in Scripture — holiness is
        God's fundamental character. Not "love, love, love" or
        "power, power, power" — HOLY, HOLY, HOLY.

v.5  — "Woe is me! for I am undone; because I am a man of unclean
        lips, and I dwell in the midst of a people of unclean lips:
        for mine eyes have seen the King, the LORD of hosts" (KJV)
        — The prophet who pronounced woes on others (ch.5) now
        pronounces woe on HIMSELF. Holiness doesn't make you feel
        good about yourself — it exposes you.

v.7  — "Lo, this hath touched thy lips; and thine iniquity is
        taken away, and thy sin purged" (KJV) — The coal from the
        ALTAR (where sacrifice burns) touches his lips. Cleansing
        comes through sacrifice, applied personally. Not Isaiah's
        effort but God's provision.

v.8  — "Also I heard the voice of the Lord, saying, Whom shall I
        send, and who will go for us? Then said I, Here am I; send
        me" (KJV) — The most famous volunteer response in Scripture.
        God asks; Isaiah answers. Note: cleansing (v.7) precedes
        commission (v.8) — you must be forgiven before you can be
        sent.

------------------------------------

         — the same God on the same throne.

         holy, holy" — the same unceasing worship.

         Isaiah expects to die from seeing God.
v.5   — Luke 5:8 — Peter: "Depart from me; for I am a sinful
         man, O Lord" — same reaction to holiness.
v.7   — Hebrews 9:22 — "Without shedding of blood is no
         remission" — the altar coal represents sacrifice.
v.8   — Romans 10:15 — "How shall they preach, except they be
         sent?" — the sending pattern.
v.9   — Matthew 13:14-15 — Jesus quotes vv.9-10 to explain
         parabolic teaching to a hard-hearted generation.
v.9   — Acts 28:26-27 — Paul's final word to Jewish unbelievers
         in Rome — quoting this same passage.

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1  — "the Lord" (KJV/ESV) — Hebrew "Adonai" (Lord/Master),
      not "Yahweh" (the personal name). Though the seraphim say
      "Yahweh of hosts" (v.3). Both titles together: sovereign
      authority + covenant faithfulness.

v.2  — "seraphims" (KJV) / "seraphim" (ESV) — Hebrew "seraphim"
      = burning ones (from "saraph" = to burn). Unique to this
      passage. Six-winged beings — two wings covering face (cannot
      look at God), two covering feet (reverent humility), two
      flying (service).

v.5  — "undone" (KJV) / "lost" (ESV) — Hebrew "nidmeiti" = I am
      destroyed/silenced/cut off. Stronger than "ruined" — Isaiah
      expects to be annihilated by holiness.

v.7  — "purged" (KJV) / "atoned for" (ESV) — Hebrew "kuppar" =
      covered/atoned (same root as Yom Kippur — Day of Atonement).
      The priestly word for sacrifice-based cleansing.

v.10 — "Make the heart of this people fat" (KJV) / "Make the
      heart of this people dull" (ESV) — Hebrew "hashmen" = make
      fat/insensitive. A judicial hardening: God commands Isaiah
      to preach knowing it will harden, not soften. Repeated
      rejection leads to inability to respond.

v.1 — NET: "high, elevated throne. The hem of" for KJV's "throne, high and lifted up, and"
v.2 — NET: "they used the remaining two to" for KJV's "with twain he did"
       WEB: "wings. With two" for KJV's "wings; with twain"
v.3 — ASV: uses "Jehovah" for KJV's "LORD" (Hebrew YHWH).
       NET: "entire earth!”" for KJV's "whole earth is full of his glory."
       WEB: uses "Yahweh" for KJV's "LORD" (Hebrew YHWH).
v.5 — ASV: uses "Jehovah" for KJV's "LORD" (Hebrew YHWH).
       NET: "destroyed, for my lips are contaminated by sin," for KJV's "undone; because I am a man of unclean lips,"
       WEB: uses "Yahweh" for KJV's "LORD" (Hebrew YHWH).
v.7 — ASV: "touched" for KJV's "laid it upon"
       NET: "He touched my mouth with" for KJV's "And he laid"
       WEB: "He touched" for KJV's "And he laid it upon"
v.10 — NET: adds "their hearts might understand"
       WEB: "fat. Make" for KJV's "fat, and make"

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Isaiah saw God AFTER Uzziah died (v.1). Sometimes God reveals
   Himself most clearly when earthly securities are removed. What
   "Uzziah" in your life needs to die before you can see the
   true King on His throne?

2. The seraphim cover their FACES before God (v.2). If sinless
   angels cannot look directly at God's holiness, how casually
   do you approach Him? Has familiarity bred contempt in your
   worship? When was the last time God's holiness genuinely
   terrified you?

3. Isaiah's first response to holiness was self-condemnation, not
   self-improvement (v.5). He didn't say "I'll try harder" — he
   said "I'm destroyed." The gospel begins with the end of
   self-help. Have you reached that point, or are you still
   trying to clean yourself up before approaching God?

4. The cleansing came TO Isaiah — he didn't go get it (vv.6-7).
   The seraphim BROUGHT the coal. Grace comes to the helpless,
   not to the self-sufficient. Stop trying to cleanse yourself
   and let God apply His altar-fire to your lips.

5. Commission follows cleansing (v.8). "Here am I, send me" only
   makes sense AFTER "your sin is purged." You cannot serve God
   effectively from a place of unresolved guilt. Get clean first,
   then go. What has God cleansed you from that qualifies you to
   speak to others who struggle with the same thing?
