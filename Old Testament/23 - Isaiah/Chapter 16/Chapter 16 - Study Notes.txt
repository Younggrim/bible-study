Isaiah - Chapter 16
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Moab's refugees appeal to Judah for shelter, Isaiah urges them to send tribute to Zion's ruler and promises a future throne of mercy from David's tent — but Moab's pride prevents repentance, and within three years its glory will be brought low.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Isaiah son of Amoz (see Chapter 1 notes for full details).

Classification: Prophetic Oracle — Burden Against Moab (continued)
Key Themes: Refugees seeking asylum, the messianic throne of
justice, Moab's pride as the barrier to mercy, the prophet's grief
for Moab, a three-year countdown to judgment

Historical Context: This chapter continues the Moab oracle from
chapter 15. Moab's refugees flee south to Sela (Petra in Edom)
and appeal to Judah for protection (v.1). Isaiah's advice: shelter
the refugees, for a just ruler will arise from David's tent (v.5)
— a messianic promise embedded in an oracle against a foreign
nation. But Moab's PRIDE (v.6 — "his pride... his haughtiness...
his wrath") prevents genuine submission. The chapter ends with a
specific timeframe: "within three years" Moab's remaining glory
will be reduced to a contemptible remnant (v.14).

Structure:
- Appeal to Judah: Send Tribute, Give Shelter (vv.1-5)
- Moab's Pride Prevents Mercy (vv.6-7)
- The Devastation of Moab's Vineyards (vv.8-12)
- The Three-Year Countdown (vv.13-14)

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.5  — "And in mercy shall the throne be established: and he
        shall sit upon it in truth in the tabernacle of David,
        judging, and seeking judgment, and hasting righteousness"
        (KJV) — A messianic promise: David's throne established in
        mercy and truth. Even in an oracle against Moab, Christ's
        kingdom breaks through.

v.6  — "We have heard of the pride of Moab; he is very proud"
        (KJV) — Pride named three times in one verse. Pride
        prevents Moab from genuinely humbling itself before Judah's
        God. The same sin that felled Babylon (ch.14) destroys Moab.

v.9  — "I will water thee with my tears, O Heshbon, and Elealeh"
        (KJV) — Again, the prophet weeps for the judged nation.
        Isaiah doesn't deliver oracles coldly — he mourns alongside
        those he condemns.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------

v.5   — Luke 1:32-33 — "The Lord God shall give unto him the

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "Send ye the lamb to the ruler of the land" (KJV) / "Send
      the lamb to the ruler of the land" (ESV) — Tribute payment
      to Judah's king. Mesha (king of Moab) previously paid
      100,000 lambs annually (2 Kings 3:4).

v.5  — "tabernacle of David" (KJV) / "tent of David" (ESV) —
      Hebrew "ohel David" = David's tent/dwelling. The dynasty
      reduced to a tent (not a palace) yet still producing a
      righteous king. Quoted in Acts 15:16.

v.14 — "within three years, as the years of an hireling" (KJV) —
      "years of a hireling" = precisely counted (a hired worker
      counts every day until his contract ends). Exact, not
      approximate.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Sela          — "Rock" — likely Petra in Edom, a cliff-city where
               Moabite refugees fled south for protection.

Tabernacle of David — David's dynasty described as a humble tent
               (not the glory it once had), yet from it comes the
               Messiah. The church as the rebuilt tent of David
               (Acts 15:16-17).

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.5 — ASV: "a throne shall be established in lovingkindness; and one shall sit thereon in truth, in the tent of David" / WEB: "A throne will be established in loving kindness. One will sit on it in truth, in the tent of David" / NET: "Then a trustworthy king will be established; he will rule in a reliable manner, this one from David's family" — The messianic verse. ASV/WEB preserve "lovingkindness" (Hebrew chesed = covenant love/loyal love). NET omits "chesed" entirely, replacing the theological concept with "trustworthy" and "reliable." This loses the covenant-love foundation of Messiah's rule.

- v.5 — ASV/WEB: "in the tent of David" / NET: "this one from David's family" — Hebrew "ohel David" = tent of David. ASV/WEB preserve the literal image (the dynasty reduced to a tent, yet still producing a king). NET replaces the evocative image with a flat genealogical statement ("from David's family"), losing the literary power and the Acts 15:16 connection.

- v.6 — NET: "But their boastful claims are empty" vs. ASV: "his boastings are nought" / WEB: "His boastings are nothing" — All three convey that Moab's pride is baseless. Hebrew "bad" = empty talk/boasting. NET's "boastful claims are empty" is the most explanatory.

- v.13 — ASV: "This is the word that Jehovah spake" / WEB: "This is the word that Yahweh spoke" / NET: "This is the message the Lord previously announced" — NET adds "previously" (interpretive) to clarify the time reference. The divine name in ASV/WEB marks this as covenant-God speech with authority.

- v.14 — ASV: "Jehovah hath spoken" / WEB: "Yahweh has spoken" / NET: "the Lord makes this announcement" — NET paraphrases the divine speech formula. ASV/WEB: "Within three years, as the years of a hireling" / NET: "Within exactly three years" — NET replaces the "hireling" imagery with "exactly" — losing the picture of a contract worker counting days precisely until payment is due.

- v.11 — ASV: "my heart soundeth like a harp for Moab" / WEB: "my heart sounds like a harp for Moab" / NET: "my heart constantly sighs for Moab, like the strumming of a harp" — Hebrew "me'ay" (inner parts/bowels) expresses deep visceral grief. NET adds "constantly" and restructures the metaphor. The prophet's gut resonates like a stringed instrument vibrating with Moab's pain.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Pride prevents people from receiving mercy (v.6). God's mercy
   was available to Moab — but Moab was too proud to genuinely
   humble itself. Is pride keeping YOU from receiving something
   God wants to give? Asking for help isn't weakness; refusing
   it because of pride IS.

2. Even in judgment oracles against enemies, God embeds messianic
   hope (v.5). The throne of mercy is coming — for ALL nations,
   not just Israel. No one is beyond the reach of David's
   righteous heir.

3. The prophet weeps AGAIN (v.9). Genuine prophetic ministry is
   costly — it breaks your heart. If you can condemn sin without
   compassion for sinners, something is wrong with your heart.
