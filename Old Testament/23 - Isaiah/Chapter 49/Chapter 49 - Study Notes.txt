Isaiah - Chapter 49
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
The second Servant Song — the Servant speaks in first person: called from the womb, named before birth, a polished arrow hidden in God's quiver. Though discouraged ("I have laboured in vain"), God expands his mission: not just to restore Israel but to be "a light to the Gentiles, that thou mayest be my salvation unto the end of the earth." Zion says "God has forgotten me" — God answers: "Can a woman forget her nursing child? I have graven thee upon the palms of my hands."

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Isaiah son of Amoz (see Chapter 1 notes for full details).

Classification: Prophetic Oracle — Second Servant Song (vv.1-6)
+ Zion's Restoration (vv.7-26)
Key Themes: The Servant's calling from the womb, apparent failure
redeemed by larger mission, light to the Gentiles, Zion not
forgotten, engraved on God's palms, children returning

Historical Context: The second Servant Song (49:1-6) shifts
perspective: the Servant now speaks in FIRST PERSON (contrasting
with 42:1-9 where God introduces Him). The Servant experiences
discouragement (v.4) yet God expands rather than abandons the
mission. The Servant is too great for merely restoring Israel —
He must reach the whole world (v.6). Simeon quotes v.6 when he
holds baby Jesus (Luke 2:32). The remainder (vv.7-26) assures
Zion that God has NOT forgotten her, using the most intimate
imagery: nursing mothers and tattooed palms.

Structure:
- Second Servant Song: Called from the Womb (vv.1-6)
- The Servant Despised, Then Honored by Kings (v.7)
- The Restoration: Prisoners Released, Highways Opened (vv.8-13)
- Zion: "The LORD Has Forsaken Me" (v.14)
- "Can a Woman Forget?" — Engraved on My Palms (vv.15-16)
- Zion's Children Return (vv.17-26)

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "And he hath made my mouth like a sharp sword; in the
        shadow of his hand hath he hid me, and made me a polished
        shaft; in his quiver hath he hid me" (KJV) — The Servant
        is PREPARED (sharp, polished) and HIDDEN (in God's shadow,
        in His quiver) until the appointed time. Preparation in
        hiddenness before public ministry.

v.4  — "Then I said, I have laboured in vain, I have spent my
        strength for nought, and in vain: yet surely my judgment
        is with the LORD, and my work with my God" (KJV) — The
        Servant's discouragement: "I worked for nothing." Yet faith
        persists: "my judgment is with the LORD." Even apparent
        failure is in God's hands.

v.6  — "I will also give thee for a light to the Gentiles, that
        thou mayest be my salvation unto the end of the earth"
        (KJV) — The mission EXPANDS. Restoring Israel is "too
        light a thing" (ESV). The Servant will be salvation to the
        ENDS of the earth. Quoted in Acts 13:47.

v.15 — "Can a woman forget her sucking child, that she should not
        have compassion on the son of her womb? yea, they may
        forget, yet will I not forget thee" (KJV) — The strongest
        human bond (nursing mother to infant) can fail — but God's
        bond CANNOT. If even motherly love can fail, God's love is
        STRONGER than the strongest human love.

v.16 — "Behold, I have graven thee upon the palms of my hands;
        thy walls are continually before me" (KJV) — God tattooed
        Zion onto His palms. Permanent, always visible, impossible
        to forget. Christ's nail-scarred hands fulfill this.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "polished shaft" (KJV) / "polished arrow" (ESV) — Hebrew
      "chets barud" = a shaft made smooth/shining through
      grinding and polishing. The Servant was prepared through
      hidden suffering to be God's weapon at the right moment.

v.6  — "too light a thing" (ESV) / implied in KJV — Hebrew
      "naqal" = too small, too insignificant. Restoring Israel
      is too small a job for THIS Servant. His destiny is global.

v.7  — "him whom man despiseth... a servant of rulers" (KJV) —
      The Servant goes from despised to honored. Kings will arise
      before Him. Fulfillment: Philippians 2:9-11.

v.16 — "graven" (KJV) / "engraved" (ESV) — Hebrew "chaqaq" =
      to inscribe, cut into. Permanent inscription — not ink that
      fades but marks cut into flesh/stone. Irremovable.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Servant Song — The second of four (49:1-6). The Servant speaks in
              first person, revealing inner experience: call from
              the womb, hidden preparation, apparent failure,
              expanded mission.

Light to the Gentiles — The Servant's scope: beyond Israel to
              all nations. Salvation is not ethnic but universal.
              This phrase defines the church's missionary mandate.

Engraved on Palms — God's permanent remembrance of His people.
              Christ's nail-scarred hands are the physical
              fulfillment: Zion's name etched in His flesh forever.

Polished Arrow — The Servant prepared in hiddenness (God's quiver)
              before being launched publicly. Hidden seasons are
              PREPARATION seasons — not wasted time.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.1 — ASV: "Jehovah hath called me from the womb" / WEB: "Yahweh has called me from the womb" / NET: "The Lord summoned me from birth; he commissioned me when my mother brought me into the world" — NET expands and interprets; ASV/WEB preserve the divine name. The Servant's pre-birth calling echoes Jeremiah 1:5 and points to Christ.

- v.3 — NET: "through whom I will reveal my splendor" / KJV: "in whom I will be glorified" / WEB: "in whom I will be glorified" — NET renders "etpa'ar" (be glorified/beautified) as "reveal my splendor," making God the active agent rather than being glorified passively through the Servant.

- v.6 — NET: "I will make you a light to the nations, so you can bring my deliverance to the remote regions of the earth" / KJV: "I will also give thee for a light to the Gentiles, that thou mayest be my salvation unto the end of the earth" — NET renders "yeshu'ah" (salvation) as "deliverance" and "goyim" (nations/Gentiles) simply as "nations." KJV's "Gentiles" emphasizes the Jewish/non-Jewish distinction central to the Servant's universal mission.

- v.7 — NET: "the Protector of Israel" / ASV/WEB: "the Redeemer of Israel" / KJV: "the Redeemer of Israel" — NET again replaces "go'el" (kinsman-redeemer) with "Protector." In a Servant Song passage, the redeemer concept is especially significant — the Servant IS the redemption God provides.

- v.7 — NET: "the one who is despised and rejected by nations" / KJV: "him whom man despiseth, to him whom the nation abhorreth" — NET uses plural "nations" where Hebrew has singular "goy" (nation). The Servant is despised by THE nation (Israel) — a more specific and painful rejection, anticipating Isaiah 53.

- v.8 — NET: "make you a covenant mediator for people" / KJV: "give thee for a covenant of the people" / ASV/WEB: "give thee for a covenant of the people" — NET adds "mediator" (not in Hebrew). The Servant doesn't just mediate a covenant — He IS the covenant. He embodies God's commitment to His people.

- v.26 — ASV: "I, Jehovah, am thy Saviour, and thy Redeemer, the Mighty One of Jacob" / WEB: "I, Yahweh, am your Savior, and your Redeemer, the Mighty One of Jacob" / NET: "I am the Lord, your Deliverer, your Protector, the Powerful One of Jacob" — NET renders three key Isaiah titles differently: "Saviour" → "Deliverer," "Redeemer" → "Protector," "Mighty One" → "Powerful One." Each substitution loses specificity from the Hebrew theological vocabulary.

- v.16 — WEB: "I have engraved you on the palms of my hands" / NET: "I have inscribed your name on my palms" / KJV: "I have graven thee upon the palms of my hands" — NET adds "your name" (not in Hebrew); the original says God engraved YOU (Zion herself), not just your name. The image is more intimate and, for Christians, evocative of nail-pierced hands.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "I have laboured in vain" (v.4). Even the Servant felt
   discouraged. If Jesus can feel that ministry seems fruitless,
   you're allowed to feel it too — without quitting. The next
   verse ALWAYS expands the mission.

2. Light to the Gentiles (v.6). Your calling may be bigger than
   you think. What feels like failure in one arena may be God
   redirecting you to a larger stage. "Too small a thing" —
   God has MORE for you.

3. "Can a woman forget her nursing child?" (v.15). Some women
   DO forget — abuse, abandonment, neglect are real. But even
   if the strongest human love fails you, God's love CANNOT.
   If you were abandoned by a parent, hear this: God's love
   surpasses the best human love.

4. "Graven upon the palms of my hands" (v.16). You are tattooed
   on God's hands. He cannot look at His own palms without seeing
   YOU. Stop believing you're forgotten. The nail prints prove
   otherwise.

5. Hidden in His quiver (v.2). If you're in a hidden season —
   unrecognized, unplatformed, seemingly unused — you may be in
   God's quiver being polished for deployment. Hiddenness is not
   abandonment; it's preparation.
