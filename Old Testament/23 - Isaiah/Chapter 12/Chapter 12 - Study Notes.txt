Isaiah - Chapter 12
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A short psalm of thanksgiving closes the "Book of Immanuel" (chapters 7-12) — the redeemed sing "God is my salvation, I will trust and not be afraid" and declare His deeds among the nations, for "great is the Holy One of Israel in the midst of thee."

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Isaiah son of Amoz (see Chapter 1 notes for full details).

Classification: Prophetic Hymn — Song of the Redeemed
Key Themes: Thanksgiving after deliverance, God's anger turned
away, God as salvation and strength, drawing water from wells of
salvation, declaring God's deeds among the nations

Historical Context: Chapter 12 is the shortest chapter in Isaiah
(only 6 verses) and functions as the conclusion to the "Book of
Immanuel" (chapters 7-12). Like Moses' song after the Red Sea
(Exodus 15) and the song of the redeemed in Revelation 15, this
is the people's response to salvation accomplished. "In that day"
(vv.1, 4) points to the messianic age when the promises of
chapters 9 and 11 are fulfilled. The people sing TWO songs: the
first personal (vv.1-3: "I will trust... my salvation... my
strength... my song") and the second communal (vv.4-6: "Declare
his doings among the people... cry out and shout, thou inhabitant
of Zion"). The section ends as it must end — in worship.

Structure:
- Song 1: Personal Thanksgiving (vv.1-3)
- Song 2: Corporate Praise and Declaration (vv.4-6)

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "Behold, God is my salvation; I will trust, and not be
        afraid: for the LORD JEHOVAH is my strength and my song;
        he also is become my salvation" (KJV) — Three "my" claims:
        my salvation, my strength, my song. This echoes Moses'
        song (Exodus 15:2) and becomes the personal testimony of
        every believer. "I will trust and not be afraid" — fear is
        the opposite of trust.

v.3  — "Therefore with joy shall ye draw water out of the wells
        of salvation" (KJV) — "Wells of salvation" — deep,
        inexhaustible supply. Not a puddle but a WELL — you must
        draw from it actively. Joy is the mood; salvation is the
        source. Jesus references this at the Feast of Tabernacles
        (John 7:37-38) when water-drawing was central to the
        celebration.

v.4  — "Declare his doings among the people, make mention that
        his name is exalted" (KJV) — Salvation produces mission.
        Those who have received can't stay silent — they DECLARE
        among the nations. Personal worship leads to public
        proclamation.

v.6  — "Cry out and shout, thou inhabitant of Zion: for great is
        the Holy One of Israel in the midst of thee" (KJV) — "In
        the midst of thee" — Immanuel fulfilled. God is not distant
        but PRESENT. The reason for shouting isn't the gifts but
        the GIVER — He is among us.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "the LORD JEHOVAH" (KJV) / "the LORD GOD" (ESV) — Hebrew
      "Yah Yahweh" — the shortened name (Yah) combined with the
      full name (Yahweh). Double divine name for emphasis. English
      "Hallelujah" contains "Yah."

v.3  — "wells of salvation" (KJV/ESV) — Hebrew "ma'yanei
      ha-yeshu'ah" = springs/fountains of salvation. "Yeshua"
      (salvation) is the Hebrew form of "Jesus." Drawing water
      from the wells of Jesus.

v.4  — "declare his doings among the people" (KJV) / "make known
      his deeds among the peoples" (ESV) — Hebrew "hodi'u
      va'ammim" = make known among the nations. Mission language —
      what God has done must reach all peoples.

v.5  — "excellent things" (KJV) / "glorious things" (ESV) —
      Hebrew "ge'ut" = majesty/exaltation. "This is known in all
      the earth" — universal awareness of God's greatness.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Wells of salvation — Deep, inexhaustible sources of God's
               delivering power. Not surface water (easily
               contaminated or dried up) but wells — deep, pure,
               constant. Joy is the bucket; salvation is the water.

Holy One of Israel — Isaiah's favorite title for God (used 25+
               times in Isaiah, rarely elsewhere). Combines God's
               transcendent holiness with His covenant commitment
               to Israel. Holy = separate/other; of Israel = bound
               to His people.

In the midst  — God's presence among His people — the fulfillment
               of Immanuel ("God with us"). Not merely sending help
               but personally BEING present. The highest blessing is
               not what God gives but that God is THERE.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.2 — ASV: "Jehovah, even Jehovah, is my strength and song" / WEB: "Yah, Yahweh, is my strength and song" / NET: "For the Lord gives me strength and protects me; he has become my deliverer" — WEB uniquely preserves both forms of the divine name ("Yah, Yahweh") reflecting the Hebrew text exactly. NET paraphrases the double name away entirely, adding "protects me" (not in Hebrew) and using "deliverer" instead of "salvation."

- v.2 — NET: "God is my deliverer" vs. KJV/ASV/WEB: "God is my salvation" — Hebrew "yeshu'ah" (salvation) is the root of the name "Jesus/Yeshua." NET's "deliverer" loses this etymological connection. When Jesus' name means "salvation" and this verse says "God is my yeshu'ah," the christological resonance is profound — and lost in NET.

- v.3 — NET: "Joyfully you will draw water from the springs of deliverance" vs. KJV/ASV/WEB: "with joy shall ye draw water out of the wells of salvation" — NET again uses "deliverance" instead of "salvation" (yeshu'ah) and "springs" instead of "wells" (ma'yanei). Wells are dug deep; springs surface naturally. The Hebrew specifically uses the word for deep water sources.

- v.4 — NET: "Publicize his mighty acts among the nations. Make it known that he is unique" vs. ASV: "declare his doings among the peoples, make mention that his name is exalted" / WEB: "Declare his doings among the peoples. Proclaim that his name is exalted!" — NET changes "his name is exalted" to "he is unique" — the Hebrew "nisgav shemo" clearly means "His name is lifted up/exalted," not "he is unique." NET imports a different theological category.

- v.6 — NET: "the Holy One of Israel acts mightily among you!" vs. KJV/ASV/WEB: "great is the Holy One of Israel in the midst of thee" — NET changes the focus from God's PRESENCE ("in the midst") to God's ACTION ("acts mightily"). The Hebrew "gadol beqirbek" emphasizes His greatness IN YOUR MIDST — the Immanuel theme completing the Book of Immanuel (chs. 7-12).

- v.1 — ASV: "I will give thanks unto thee, O Jehovah" / WEB: "I will give thanks to you, Yahweh" / NET: "I praise you, O Lord" — The redeemed address God by His covenant name. ASV/WEB make this personal and specific; NET uses the generic "Lord," losing the intimacy of calling God by name.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "I will trust and not be afraid" (v.2). Fear and trust cannot
   coexist — one displaces the other. Which dominates your current
   emotional state? Isaiah's solution isn't "try harder not to be
   afraid" — it's "behold, GOD is my salvation." Right vision
   produces right emotion.

2. "With JOY shall ye draw water" (v.3). Salvation isn't grim
   duty — it's joyful drinking. Are you drawing from God's wells
   with joy, or has your faith become a religious obligation? The
   water is there; the joy should be in the drawing.

3. Salvation produces declaration (v.4). If you've genuinely drawn
   from the wells, you can't stay silent. "Make mention... make
   known... declare." When was the last time you told someone
   what God has done for you?

4. "Great is the Holy One of Israel IN THE MIDST OF THEE" (v.6).
   The greatness of God is not distant — it's among you. Right
   now. In your mess, your struggle, your Tuesday afternoon. He
   is IN YOUR MIDST. Do you live like someone with God present,
   or like someone waiting for God to show up?

5. This chapter closes a major section (chs. 7-12) — and it ends
   in WORSHIP. After prophecy, after warning, after judgment,
   after promise — the proper response is praise. How often do
   your Bible study sessions end in actual worship rather than
   just information?
