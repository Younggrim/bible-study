Isaiah - Chapter 23
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Oracle against Tyre — God pronounces judgment on the great Phoenician commercial city, predicting its destruction and 70-year desolation, after which Tyre will return to trade but ultimately dedicate its wealth to the LORD.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Isaiah son of Amoz (see Chapter 1 notes for full details).

Classification: Prophetic Oracle — Burden of Tyre
Key Themes: The fall of commercial pride, wealth as a false god,
the 70-year desolation, Tyre as a "harlot" (commerce without
God), the ultimate dedication of trade profits to God's purposes

Historical Context: Tyre was the greatest commercial city of the
ancient Mediterranean — a Phoenician port city (in modern
Lebanon) whose merchant fleet dominated sea trade. Its island
fortress was considered impregnable. Tyre fell to various powers:
Nebuchadnezzar besieged it for 13 years (585-572 BC), and
Alexander the Great finally conquered the island city in 332 BC
by building a causeway. The "70 years" (v.15) may correspond to
the period of Babylonian dominance. This is the LAST of Isaiah's
oracles against the nations (chs.13-23) — ending with the
world's greatest commercial power.

Structure:
- Wail, Ships of Tarshish! Tyre is Destroyed (vv.1-5)
- Cross Over to Tarshish: The Refugees (vv.6-7)
- Who Planned This? The LORD of Hosts (vv.8-9)
- Tyre's Colonies Cannot Help (vv.10-12)
- The Chaldeans as God's Instrument (vv.13-14)
- The 70-Year Desolation and Restoration (vv.15-18)

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.8  — "Who hath taken this counsel against Tyre, the crowning
        city, whose merchants are princes, whose traffickers are
        the honourable of the earth?" (KJV) — Who would dare
        attack the world's wealthiest city? Answer (v.9): "The
        LORD of hosts hath purposed it, to stain the pride of all
        glory."

v.9  — "The LORD of hosts hath purposed it, to stain the pride
        of all glory, and to bring into contempt all the
        honourable of the earth" (KJV) — God's PURPOSE in judgment:
        to stain human pride. When the proudest, richest city
        falls, everyone takes notice.

v.17 — "she shall turn to her hire, and shall commit fornication
        with all the kingdoms of the world" (KJV) — Tyre returns
        to commerce (described as prostitution — selling herself
        to anyone for profit).

v.18 — "And her merchandise and her hire shall be holiness to the
        LORD: it shall not be treasured nor laid up; for her
        merchandise shall be for them that dwell before the LORD"
        (KJV) — Ultimately Tyre's wealth will serve God's people.
        Commerce redeemed — trade profits dedicated to the LORD.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "Tarshish" (KJV/ESV) — A distant port, likely in Spain
      (modern Tartessos). Tyre's ships sailed to Tarshish for tin
      and silver — the furthest reaches of known trade routes.

v.3  — "Sihor" (KJV) / "Shihor" (ESV) — The eastern branch of
      the Nile Delta. Tyre imported Egyptian grain — its
      commercial empire connected the world's resources.

v.10 — "daughter of Tarshish" (KJV/ESV) — Tyre's colonies and
      trading partners, now freed from Tyre's commercial dominance
      like a daughter freed from her parent.

v.15 — "days of one king" (KJV/ESV) — 70 years as the span of
      one dynasty/era. Same as Babylon's 70-year dominance in
      Jeremiah 25:11.

v.17 — "hire of a harlot" (KJV) / "wages of a prostitute" (ESV)
      — Commerce without moral compass described as prostitution.
      Tyre sells itself to anyone with money.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Tyre         — Premier Phoenician port city on the Mediterranean
              coast (modern Lebanon). Built partially on an island,
              considered impregnable. Known for purple dye (from
              murex snails), cedar trade, and shipbuilding.

Tarshish     — Distant western port (likely Spain) representing
              the farthest reach of maritime trade. "Ships of
              Tarshish" = large ocean-going merchant vessels.

Sidon        — Sister city of Tyre, also Phoenician, located
              north of Tyre. Often paired with Tyre in Scripture.

Chaldeans    — (v.13) The Babylonians (Neo-Babylonian Empire).
              God's instrument for Tyre's judgment under
              Nebuchadnezzar.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. God stains "the pride of all glory" (v.9). The purpose of
   Tyre's fall is theological: to demonstrate that NO amount of
   wealth makes anyone immune to God's sovereignty. What worldly
   success have you confused with divine favor?

2. Commerce without God is prostitution (v.17). Tyre sold itself
   to anyone for profit — no moral compass, no higher purpose.
   Business is not inherently evil, but commerce that serves ONLY
   profit is spiritual adultery.

3. Wealth CAN be redeemed (v.18). The chapter doesn't end with
   permanent destruction — it ends with Tyre's trade profits
   dedicated to God. Your career, your earnings, your commercial
   activity can serve God's purposes. The question is: who are
   you ultimately working FOR?

4. "Whose merchants are princes" (v.8). When a culture's heroes
   are its merchants (celebrities, billionaires), something is
   disordered. Who are the "princes" in YOUR value system?
