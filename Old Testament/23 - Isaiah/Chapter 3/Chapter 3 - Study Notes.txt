Isaiah - Chapter 3
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
God removes Judah's competent leadership as judgment, leaving the nation in chaos under immature rulers — and pronounces specific woe on the proud women of Zion whose luxury will be replaced with shame.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Isaiah son of Amoz (see Chapter 1 notes for full details).

Classification: Prophetic Oracle — Judgment Through Removal
Key Themes: God removes competent leaders as judgment, social
collapse when the righteous are taken away, the sin of oppressing
the poor, the pride of wealthy women exposed and judged

Historical Context: Isaiah continues describing Judah's present
corruption after the glorious future vision of chapter 2. God's
judgment takes a surprising form — not invasion but REMOVAL of
support. He removes bread, water, heroes, judges, prophets, elders,
and skilled craftsmen (vv.1-3). What remains is chaos: youths rule,
the people oppress each other, and society inverts (v.5: the child
behaves proudly against the ancient). Verses 16-26 describe the
wealthy women of Jerusalem in extraordinary detail — their fashion,
jewelry, and haughty walk. God will strip it all away, replacing
perfume with stench, beauty with shame, fine robes with sackcloth.

Structure:
- God Removes Every Support (vv.1-3)
- Chaos Under Incompetent Leadership (vv.4-7)
- Jerusalem's Open Sin (vv.8-9)
- The Righteous vs. the Wicked (vv.10-11)
- Oppressors of God's People Charged (vv.12-15)
- The Proud Women of Zion Judged (vv.16-26)

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "the Lord, the LORD of hosts, doth take away from
        Jerusalem and from Judah the stay and the staff, the whole
        stay of bread, and the whole stay of water" (KJV) — God
        removes the foundations: food, water, leadership. When God
        withdraws support, collapse follows.

v.5  — "the child shall behave himself proudly against the
        ancient, and the base against the honourable" (KJV) —
        Social inversion: youth disrespects elders, the ignoble
        despises the honorable. The breakdown of authority at every
        level — a sign of God's judicial abandonment.

v.10 — "Say ye to the righteous, that it shall be well with him:
        for they shall eat the fruit of their doings" (KJV) — Even
        in national judgment, the righteous are distinguished. God
        sees individuals within corrupt systems.

v.15 — "What mean ye that ye beat my people to pieces, and grind
        the faces of the poor?" (KJV) — God takes the oppression
        of the poor PERSONALLY. "MY people" — He identifies with
        the vulnerable and demands an account from their oppressors.

v.24 — "instead of sweet smell there shall be stink; and instead
        of a girdle a rent; and instead of well set hair baldness;
        and instead of a stomacher a girding of sackcloth; and
        burning instead of beauty" (KJV) — Five reversals: perfume
        to stench, belt to rope, styled hair to baldness, fine
        clothing to sackcloth, beauty to branding.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "the stay and the staff" (KJV) / "every support and
      supply" (ESV) — Hebrew "mash'en u'mash'enah" = masculine
      and feminine forms of "support" — every kind of support,
      both food and leadership.

v.3  — "the eloquent orator" (KJV) / "the skillful magician"
      (ESV) — Hebrew "nechon lachash" = skilled in whispering/
      incantation. The KJV and ESV interpret differently: eloquent
      speaker vs. enchanter.

v.16 — "wanton eyes" (KJV) / "seductive eyes" (ESV) — Hebrew
      "mesakkeroth" = ogling/casting glances. The women's
      flirtatious, attention-seeking gaze.

v.20 — "earrings" (KJV) / "amulets" (ESV) — Hebrew "lechashim"
      = whisper-charms, jewelry with magical inscriptions. Not
      merely decorative but superstitious/occult objects.

v.24 — "stomacher" (KJV) / "rich robe" (ESV) — Hebrew "pethigil"
      = an expensive garment. An archaic KJV word referring to a
      decorated front-piece of a dress.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Stay and Staff — Everything that props up a society: food, water,
               military leaders, judges, prophets, craftsmen. When
               God removes these "pillars," everything collapses.

Daughters of Zion — The wealthy women of Jerusalem. Their luxury
               items are listed in extraordinary detail (vv.18-23)
               — about 21 items of jewelry and fashion, the most
               detailed clothing list in the OT.

Grinding the faces — A metaphor for crushing oppression. The poor
               are treated like grain ground between millstones —
               reduced to powder by those with power.

Baldness       — Shaving the head was a sign of mourning and shame
               in the ancient Near East. For vain women who took
               pride in elaborate hairstyles, forced baldness was
               maximum humiliation.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.1 — ASV: "the Lord, Jehovah of hosts" / WEB: "the Lord, Yahweh of Armies" / NET: "the Sovereign Lord of Heaven's Armies" — Triple divine title. NET renders "Adonai Yahweh Tseba'oth" with maximum interpretive expansion ("Sovereign Lord of Heaven's Armies"), while ASV/WEB preserve the distinct names. NET also paraphrases "stay and staff" as "every source of security."

- v.3 — ASV: "the skilful enchanter" / WEB: "the clever enchanter" / NET: "those who know incantations" — Hebrew "nechon lachash" is debated. KJV renders "eloquent orator" (taking it positively), while ASV/WEB/NET all interpret it as sorcery/magic, reflecting the context of divination in v.2.

- v.12 — NET: "Oppressors treat my people cruelly; creditors rule over them" vs. KJV "children are their oppressors, and women rule over them" — NET completely reinterprets the Hebrew. KJV/ASV/WEB follow the literal text (children/women as rulers indicating social inversion), while NET avoids the gender reference entirely, substituting "creditors."

- v.15 — ASV: "saith the Lord, Jehovah of hosts" / WEB: "says the Lord, Yahweh of Armies" / NET: "The Sovereign Lord of Heaven's Armies has spoken" — God personally indicts the oppressors. The divine name emphasizes His covenant authority to judge His own people's leaders.

- v.16 — NET: "They skip along and the jewelry on their ankles jingles" vs. ASV: "walking and mincing as they go, and making a tinkling with their feet" — Hebrew "ta'aknah" (mincing/tripping steps) is rendered differently. NET's "skip along" is casual; ASV's "mincing" conveys the deliberate, attention-seeking gait better.

- v.17 — NET: "the Lord will make the front of their heads bald" vs. ASV: "Jehovah will lay bare their secret parts" / WEB: "Yahweh will make their scalps bald" — The Hebrew "pot" is debated (forehead? secret parts?). ASV follows the interpretation of shameful exposure; NET/WEB limit it to baldness of the scalp.

- v.24 — NET: "a prisoner's brand will replace beauty" vs. KJV "burning instead of beauty" — NET makes explicit that the "burning" (Hebrew "ki") refers to branding prisoners of war, adding interpretive specificity that the literal translations leave open.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. God's judgment sometimes looks like REMOVAL rather than
   destruction (vv.1-3). He simply takes away the competent
   people, the wise leaders, the skilled workers — and lets chaos
   do the rest. What blessings in your life might be God's
   sustaining gifts that you take for granted?

2. When youth despises age and the base mocks the honorable (v.5),
   society is under judgment. How do you honor the experienced,
   the elderly, the wise — even when culture tells you they're
   irrelevant?

3. "They declare their sin as Sodom, they hide it not" (v.9) —
   unashamed, public sin. When a culture loses the capacity for
   shame, it has crossed a dangerous line. What sins has your
   culture normalized that Scripture still calls sin?

4. God takes the oppression of the poor PERSONALLY (v.15). "MY
   people" — He claims them. How do you treat people who have no
   power to benefit you? The waitress, the janitor, the homeless
   person? God is watching.

5. Pride expressed through luxury will be stripped (vv.16-24). The
   things you use to project status, attract attention, or feel
   superior — God can remove them overnight. What identity would
   you have if everything external was taken away?
