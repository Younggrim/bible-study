Isaiah - Chapter 26
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A song of trust and victory — "In that day" the redeemed sing of God's strong city, perfect peace for the steadfast mind, the humbling of the lofty city, the path of the righteous, longing for God in the night, and the promise of resurrection: "Thy dead men shall live."

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Isaiah son of Amoz (see Chapter 1 notes for full details).

Classification: Apocalyptic Hymn — Song of the Redeemed (Isaiah
Apocalypse, chs. 24-27)
Key Themes: God's strong city vs. the lofty city, perfect peace
for the trusting mind, waiting on God in the night, the futility
of human effort without God, the dew of resurrection, hiding
during God's indignation

Historical Context: This chapter continues the Isaiah Apocalypse
(chs. 24-27). After judgment (ch.24), praise (ch.25), now a SONG
that the redeemed will sing "in that day" (v.1). The song
contrasts two cities: God's strong city (salvation as its walls)
vs. the lofty/fortified city of the proud that God brings to the
dust (vv.5-6). The song includes one of the OT's clearest
resurrection promises (v.19). The chapter ends with an invitation
to HIDE during God's indignation — a brief period of divine wrath
that will pass (vv.20-21).

Structure:
- God's Strong City: Salvation as Walls (vv.1-4)
- The Lofty City Brought Low (vv.5-6)
- The Path of the Righteous (vv.7-9)
- The Wicked Learn Nothing from Grace (v.10)
- LORD, You Will Ordain Peace (vv.11-15)
- We Could Not Save Ourselves (vv.16-18)
- The Resurrection Promise (v.19)
- Hide Until the Indignation Passes (vv.20-21)

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.3  — "Thou wilt keep him in perfect peace, whose mind is stayed
        on thee: because he trusteth in thee" (KJV) — One of
        Scripture's most beloved promises. "Perfect peace" = Hebrew
        "shalom shalom" (doubled for intensity). The condition:
        a mind FIXED on God, not wandering to fears.

v.4  — "Trust ye in the LORD for ever: for in the LORD JEHOVAH
        is everlasting strength" (KJV) — Hebrew "Yah Yahweh tsur
        olamim" = the LORD God is the Rock of Ages. The hymn
        "Rock of Ages" comes from this verse.

v.9  — "With my soul have I desired thee in the night; yea, with
        my spirit within me will I seek thee early" (KJV) —
        Longing for God in the darkness. The night seasons of the
        soul produce the deepest seeking.

v.10 — "Let favour be shewed to the wicked, yet will he not
        learn righteousness" (KJV) — Grace alone doesn't change
        the wicked. They experience God's goodness and still
        refuse to learn. Grace must be RECEIVED, not just observed.

v.19 — "Thy dead men shall live, together with my dead body shall
        they arise. Awake and sing, ye that dwell in dust: for thy
        dew is as the dew of herbs, and the earth shall cast out
        the dead" (KJV) — Resurrection! The dead will rise, the
        dust-dwellers will sing, the earth will give birth to its
        dead. One of the OT's most explicit resurrection texts.

v.20 — "Come, my people, enter thou into thy chambers, and shut
        thy doors about thee: hide thyself as it were for a little
        moment, until the indignation be overpast" (KJV) — God
        invites His people to shelter during judgment. Echoes
        Noah in the ark, Israel during the Passover, and the
        church hidden in Christ.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.3  — "perfect peace" (KJV/ESV) — Hebrew "shalom shalom" =
      peace peace. The doubling intensifies: complete, total,
      unshakable peace. Not absence of trouble but presence of
      God-centered stability.

v.4  — "everlasting strength" (KJV) / "an everlasting rock"
      (ESV) — Hebrew "tsur olamim" = Rock of Ages/eternities.
      Augustus Toplady's famous hymn title comes directly from
      this phrase.

v.19 — "dew of herbs" (KJV) / "dew of light" (ESV) — Hebrew
      "tal oroth" can mean "dew of lights/herbs." The imagery:
      as morning dew causes plants to revive and spring up, God's
      resurrection dew causes the dead to rise from the earth.

v.20 — "chambers" (KJV/ESV) — Hebrew "chadarim" = inner rooms,
      private chambers. The safest, most interior room of the
      house — the place of hiding during danger.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Shalom Shalom — Doubled "peace" expressing completeness and
              intensity. Not merely the absence of conflict but
              total well-being, wholeness, and harmony with God.

Rock of Ages  — (v.4) Hebrew "tsur olamim" — God as the eternal,
              unchanging, unshakable foundation. The source of
              Augustus Toplady's famous hymn (1763).

Dew of Resurrection — (v.19) God's life-giving power compared to
              morning dew that revives vegetation. The dead will
              "spring up" from the earth as plants spring up at
              dawn.

Indignation   — (v.20) Hebrew "za'am" — God's righteous anger,
              specifically His end-times wrath poured on the earth.
              Brief ("a little moment") — not eternal for His
              people.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.4 — ASV: "Trust ye in Jehovah for ever; for in Jehovah, even Jehovah, is an everlasting rock" / WEB: "Trust in Yahweh forever; for in Yah, Yahweh, is an everlasting Rock" / KJV: "in the LORD JEHOVAH is everlasting strength" — The Hebrew contains both "Yah" and "YHWH" together. WEB uniquely preserves both short and full forms. KJV renders "tsur olamim" as "everlasting strength" while ASV/WEB correctly translate as "everlasting Rock" — the basis for the hymn "Rock of Ages."

- v.3 — NET: "You keep completely safe the people who maintain their faith" — KJV's iconic "perfect peace, whose mind is stayed on thee" preserves the Hebrew "shalom shalom" (doubled peace) and the image of a mind anchored/leaned upon God. NET paraphrases away both the doubled "peace" and the "stayed mind" imagery.

- v.8 — ASV: "to thy name, even to thy memorial name, is the desire of our soul" / WEB: "Your name and your renown are the desire of our soul" / NET: "We desire your fame and reputation to grow" — ASV retains "memorial name" (Hebrew "zeker" — the name by which God is remembered across generations). NET reduces this to "fame and reputation," losing the covenantal significance of God's revealed Name.

- v.19 — NET: "Your dead will come back to life; your corpses will rise up. Wake up and shout joyfully, you who live in the ground!" — KJV's "Thy dead men shall live, together with my dead body shall they arise" includes "my dead body" (the prophet's personal resurrection hope). NET omits the personal "my dead body," weakening the individual identification with the promise.

- v.21 — ASV: "Jehovah cometh forth out of his place" / WEB: "Yahweh comes out of his place" / NET: "the Lord is coming out of the place where he lives" — NET adds "where he lives" (interpretive expansion). The Hebrew is simply "his place" (meqomo) — God's dwelling is unnamed and mysterious in the original.

- v.12 — NET: "you make us secure, for even all we have accomplished you have done for us" / KJV: "thou also hast wrought all our works in us" — A theologically significant difference. KJV/ASV emphasize God working IN us (internal transformation); NET says God has done things FOR us (external provision). The Hebrew "pa'alta lanu" supports both readings.

- v.16 — NET: "they uttered incantations because of your discipline" — KJV/ASV/WEB have "they poured out a prayer." NET's "incantations" (Hebrew "lachash" — whisper/charm) is a more literal rendering of the unusual Hebrew word, suggesting desperate, whispered prayers rather than formal petition.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Perfect peace, whose mind is STAYED on thee" (v.3). Peace is
   not the absence of problems — it's the presence of a fixed
   focus. Your mind will go where you point it. Are you STAYING
   it on God, or letting it wander to every worst-case scenario?

2. The Rock of Ages (v.4). "Trust in the LORD FOREVER" — not just
   today, not just during this crisis, but as a permanent
   lifestyle. He is the rock of ETERNITIES. Your problems are
   temporary; He is everlasting.

3. The wicked don't learn from grace (v.10). Grace is not
   irresistible to the unwilling. God's goodness is all around us
   — but some people see it and still refuse to change. Don't
   mistake God's patience for God's approval.

4. Resurrection: "Thy dead men SHALL live" (v.19). The dead are
   not gone forever. The earth will give them back. If you've
   lost someone, this verse is for you: death is not the end of
   their story.

5. "Hide thyself for a little moment" (v.20). God provides
   shelter during judgment. You are hidden in Christ (Colossians
   3:3). The wrath passes OVER you, not through you. Your job
   in the storm: get inside, shut the door, and wait.
