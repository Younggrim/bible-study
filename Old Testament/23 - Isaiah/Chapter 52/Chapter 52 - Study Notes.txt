Isaiah - Chapter 52
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
"Awake, awake, put on thy strength, O Zion!" — Jerusalem is commanded to rise from the dust, shake off her chains, and put on beautiful garments. The beautiful feet of those who bring good news. God reigns! The LORD has bared His holy arm. Then the fourth Servant Song begins: the Servant will be exalted but first will be marred beyond human recognition.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Isaiah son of Amoz (see Chapter 1 notes for full details).

Classification: Prophetic Salvation Oracle + Beginning of Fourth
Servant Song (52:13-15)
Key Themes: Zion's awakening, liberation from captivity, beautiful
feet bringing good news, God reigns, departure from Babylon, the
Servant exalted yet marred beyond recognition

Historical Context: The chapter calls Zion to WAKE UP — captivity
is over, freedom has come. Isaiah 52:1-12 celebrates liberation
(from Babylon historically, from sin ultimately). Verse 7 —
"beautiful feet" — is quoted by Paul in Romans 10:15 as the
gospel proclamation. Then 52:13-15 begins the fourth Servant Song
(continuing through 53:12). The tone shifts dramatically: from
celebration to horror. The Servant will be "exalted and extolled"
(v.13) yet His appearance is "marred more than any man" (v.14).
Kings will shut their mouths in astonishment. This is the prelude
to Isaiah 53's atonement portrait.

Structure:
- Awake, Zion! Put on Strength (vv.1-2)
- Sold for Nothing, Redeemed Without Money (vv.3-6)
- Beautiful Feet: Good News of Peace (vv.7-8)
- Break Forth Together: God Has Redeemed Jerusalem (vv.9-10)
- Depart from Babylon: Be Clean (vv.11-12)
- Fourth Servant Song Begins: Exalted Yet Marred (vv.13-15)

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "Awake, awake; put on thy strength, O Zion; put on thy
        beautiful garments, O Jerusalem" (KJV) — The wake-up call:
        exchange captivity rags for royal garments. Your identity
        has changed — dress accordingly.

v.7  — "How beautiful upon the mountains are the feet of him that
        bringeth good tidings, that publisheth peace" (KJV) — The
        most celebrated verse on evangelism. Beautiful FEET — not
        eloquent mouths. The beauty is in the GOING, the running
        to deliver news of liberation.

v.10 — "The LORD hath made bare his holy arm in the eyes of all
        the nations; and all the ends of the earth shall see the
        salvation of our God" (KJV) — God rolls up His sleeves.
        His salvation is PUBLIC — all nations witness it.

v.13 — "Behold, my servant shall deal prudently, he shall be
        exalted and extolled, and be very high" (KJV) — The
        Servant's destiny: HIGHEST exaltation. Philippians 2:9 —
        "God highly exalted him."

v.14 — "his visage was so marred more than any man, and his form
        more than the sons of men" (KJV) — The cost of exaltation:
        disfigurement so severe He no longer looked human. The
        crucifixion deformed Christ beyond recognition.

v.15 — "So shall he sprinkle many nations; the kings shall shut
        their mouths at him" (KJV) — "Sprinkle" = priestly
        purification language (Leviticus 14:7). The Servant's
        suffering achieves cleansing for MANY nations. Kings are
        speechless — stunned into silence.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.7  — "beautiful" (KJV/ESV) — Hebrew "na'vu" = lovely,
      comely, fitting. The feet themselves become beautiful
      BECAUSE of the message they carry. Their beauty is derived
      from their mission.

v.13 — "deal prudently" (KJV) / "act wisely" (ESV) — Hebrew
      "yaskil" = prosper, have insight, act with prudence. The
      Servant's approach is not reckless but strategically wise.

v.14 — "marred" (KJV/ESV) — Hebrew "mishchat" = disfigured,
      ruined beyond recognition. The same root as "corruption/
      destruction." His appearance was DESTROYED.

v.15 — "sprinkle" (KJV/ESV) — Hebrew "yazzeh" = to sprinkle
      (ritual purification — as a priest sprinkles blood or water).
      Some translate "startle" (the nations will be startled).
      Either way: the Servant's suffering produces a reaction
      in many nations.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Beautiful Feet — The feet of gospel messengers. Beauty defined
              not by appearance but by PURPOSE — feet are beautiful
              when they carry good news to those who need it.

Bare His Arm — God "rolling up His sleeve" — His saving power
              visibly demonstrated. No longer hidden but
              PUBLIC, forceful, unmistakable.

Marred Beyond Recognition — The Servant's disfigurement from
              suffering so severe He no longer appears human.
              Fulfilled in the crucifixion: scourging, crown of
              thorns, nails, spear.

Sprinkle Many Nations — Priestly purification extending globally.
              One sacrifice cleanses not just Israel but MANY
              nations. The universal scope of atonement.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.3 — ASV: "thus saith Jehovah" / WEB: "Yahweh says" / NET: "this is what the Lord says" — The divine name rendered three ways. In a verse about redemption "without money," the personal covenant name emphasizes that God Himself is the ransom price.

- v.7 — NET: "How delightful it is to see approaching over the mountains the feet of a messenger who announces peace" / KJV: "How beautiful upon the mountains are the feet of him that bringeth good tidings, that publisheth peace" — NET adds "delightful" and "approaching" for readability. Hebrew "na'avu" = beautiful/comely. Paul quotes this in Romans 10:15 of gospel preachers.

- v.10 — NET: "The Lord reveals his royal power in the sight of all the nations; the entire earth sees our God deliver" / KJV: "The LORD hath made bare his holy arm in the eyes of all the nations" / ASV: "Jehovah hath made bare his holy arm" — NET replaces the concrete image of God baring His arm (rolling up His sleeve for battle) with the abstract "reveals his royal power." Also renders "yeshu'ah" (salvation) as "deliver" (verb).

- v.13 — NET: "Look, my servant will succeed!" / KJV: "my servant shall deal prudently" / ASV: "my servant shall deal wisely" / WEB: "my servant will deal wisely" — NET renders Hebrew "yaskil" as "succeed" (result) rather than "deal wisely/prudently" (method). The beginning of the Fourth Servant Song — theologically critical. The Servant's wisdom leads to exaltation.

- v.14 — NET: "he was so disfigured he no longer looked like a man; his form was so marred he no longer looked human" / KJV: "his visage was so marred more than any man, and his form more than the sons of men" — NET makes explicit what is implied: the Servant's disfigurement makes Him unrecognizable as human. This anticipates the crucifixion descriptions.

- v.15 — NET: "so now he will startle many nations" / KJV: "So shall he sprinkle many nations" / WEB: "so he will cleanse many nations" / ASV: "so shall he sprinkle many nations" — This is one of the most debated verses in Isaiah. Hebrew "yazzeh" can mean "sprinkle" (priestly purification, Leviticus 4:6) or possibly "startle/cause to leap." NET follows the "startle" interpretation; KJV/ASV preserve the priestly "sprinkle" reading; WEB interprets as "cleanse." The Christological implication of "sprinkle" is enormous — the Servant as priest purifying nations through His sacrifice.

- v.9 — NET: "the Lord consoles his people; he protects Jerusalem" / KJV: "the LORD hath comforted his people, he hath redeemed Jerusalem" / ASV: "Jehovah hath comforted his people, he hath redeemed Jerusalem" — NET replaces "redeemed" (Hebrew "ga'al") with "protects," again softening the redemption/ransom theology. In context (v.3 "redeemed without money"), the redemption theme is central.

- v.12 — NET: "the God of Israel is your rear guard" / KJV: "the God of Israel will be your rereward" / WEB: "the God of Israel will be your rear guard" — All modern translations clarify KJV's archaic "rereward." Hebrew "me'asseph" = one who gathers/rear guard. God goes before AND behind — total protection.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Awake, awake!" (v.1). If you're living as a captive when
   God has already set you free, WAKE UP. Put on your new
   identity. Stop wearing bondage clothes when freedom garments
   are available.

2. Beautiful feet (v.7). The world needs people who GO — who RUN
   with good news. Your feet become beautiful when they carry
   the gospel. Who needs you to show up with good news today?

3. "Marred more than any man" (v.14). The cost of your salvation
   disfigured the Son of God beyond recognition. Don't take the
   gospel lightly — it cost EVERYTHING. His beauty was destroyed
   so yours could be restored.

4. "Kings shall shut their mouths" (v.15). Even the most powerful
   people will be speechless before the Servant's sacrifice.
   The cross silences all human boasting. No one has anything
   to add.

5. "Depart ye, depart ye" (v.11). When God opens the door from
   captivity — LEAVE. Don't linger in Babylon. Don't take
   Babylonian souvenirs. Go clean and go completely.
