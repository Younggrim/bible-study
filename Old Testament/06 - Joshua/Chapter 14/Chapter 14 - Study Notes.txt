Joshua - Chapter 14
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Caleb, at eighty-five years old, claims Hebron as his inheritance, declaring he is still strong for war. Judah receives its territorial allotment, and Caleb drives out the Anakim giants from his portion.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Hebron (Kiriath-arba): In the southern hill country, about 19 miles south of Jerusalem — given to Caleb as his inheritance
- Hebron was formerly inhabited by the Anakim (giants); Caleb drives them out at age 85
- Gilgal: Where the allotment takes place; still serving as Israel's administrative base

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.8  — "Nevertheless my brethren that went up with me made the heart of
        the people melt: but I wholly followed the LORD my God." (ESV) — The
        phrase "wholly followed" (Heb. "male' acharei") means to fill up
        after, to follow completely. Caleb's obedience was total, not
        partial. He did not waver when the majority doubted.

v.9  — "Surely the land on which your feet have trodden shall be your
        inheritance, and your children's for ever, because you hast
        wholly followed the LORD my God." (ESV) — Moses' oath, now 45 years
        old, is still valid. God's promises do not expire. What He swore
        at Kadesh-barnea He fulfills at Gilgal.

v.10 — "And now, behold, the LORD has kept me alive, as he said, these
        forty and five years." (ESV) — Caleb attributes his longevity to God's
        faithfulness, not his own strength. God preserved him through 38
        years of wilderness wandering and 7 years of conquest — all to
        fulfill a promise.

v.11 — "As yet I am as strong this day as I was in the day that Moses
        sent me: as my strength was then, even so is my strength now,
        for war, both to go out, and to come in." (ESV) — At 85, Caleb's
        physical vigor matches his spiritual vigor. God sustained both.
        He is ready for battle, not retirement.

v.12 — "Now therefore give me this mountain, whereof the LORD spoke in
        that day; for you heardest in that day how the Anakims were
        there, and that the cities were great and fenced: if so be the
        LORD will be with me, then I shall be able to drive them out, as
        the LORD said." (ESV) — Caleb does not ask for easy territory. He wants
        the mountain where the giants live. His confidence is not in
        himself but in God: "if so be the LORD will be with me." Faith
        does not avoid difficulty — it embraces it with God.

v.14 — "Hebron therefore became the inheritance of Caleb the son of
        Jephunneh the Kenezite to this day, because that he wholly
        followed the LORD God of Israel." (ESV) — Three times in this chapter
        Caleb is described as one who "wholly followed the LORD" (vv.8,
        9,14). This is his defining characteristic. Wholehearted
        devotion is the mark of those who receive God's promises.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/joshua-14/
  Guzik highlights Caleb's extraordinary faith: at 85, he asks for the
  hardest assignment. He doesn't want a comfortable retirement — he wants
  the mountain with the giants. Guzik notes that Caleb's strength was
  preserved by God specifically for this purpose. His faith at 85 matches
  his faith at 40 because it was always rooted in God, not in himself.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "Give me this mountain! What a prayer for an old man! Most men of
  eighty-five ask for a quiet corner and a soft pillow. Caleb asks for
  a mountain full of giants. This is the spirit of faith — it does not
  grow old, it does not retire, it does not seek ease. It says, 'Where
  is the hardest fight? Give it to me. Where is the steepest hill? I
  will climb it. For the LORD is with me, and that is enough.'"
TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1  — KJV: "the countries which the children of Israel inherited" /
       ESV: "the inheritances that the people of Israel received" —
       Hebrew "nachalah" = inheritance, hereditary possession. ESV's
       "received" captures the passive reception from God; KJV's
       "inherited" emphasizes the permanent, hereditary nature.

v.4  — KJV: "suburbs for their cattle" / ESV: "pasturelands for their
       livestock" — Hebrew "migrash" = open land around a city, pasture
       ground. KJV's "suburbs" is misleading to modern readers (it
       suggests residential areas). ESV's "pasturelands" correctly
       conveys open grazing land surrounding Levitical cities.

v.6  — KJV: "Caleb the son of Jephunneh the Kenezite" / ESV: "Caleb
       the son of Jephunneh the Kenizzite" — "Kenizzite/Kenezite"
       indicates Caleb's clan within Judah, descended from Kenaz
       (cf. Genesis 36:11,42). Some scholars note this may indicate
       non-Israelite ancestry absorbed into Judah — making Caleb's
       faith all the more remarkable.

v.7  — KJV: "to espy out the land" / ESV: "to spy out the land" —
       Hebrew "l'ragel" = to spy, reconnoiter. KJV's "espy" is archaic
       English for "spy" or "observe secretly."

v.8  — KJV: "I wholly followed the LORD my God" / ESV: "I wholly
       followed the LORD my God" — Hebrew "mile'ti acharei YHWH" (lit.
       "I filled after the LORD"). The idiom means to follow fully,
       completely, without reservation. Both translations render it
       identically here.

v.12 — KJV: "give me this mountain" / ESV: "give me this hill country"
       — Hebrew "ha-har ha-zeh" = "this mountain" or "this hill
       country." "Har" can mean a single mountain or a mountainous
       region. KJV's "mountain" is more dramatic and memorable; ESV's
       "hill country" is geographically more precise (Hebron sits in
       the Judean hill country, not on a single peak).

v.12 — KJV: "the Anakims" / ESV: "the Anakim" — Hebrew "Anaqim"
       (plural). The Anakim were descendants of Anak, a race of
       unusually tall people. KJV adds the English plural "-s" to the
       already-plural Hebrew form. ESV preserves the Hebrew plural.

v.15 — KJV: "Kirjath-arba; which Arba was a great man among the
       Anakims" / ESV: "Kiriath-arba. (Arba was the greatest man among
       the Anakim.)" — Hebrew "ha-adam ha-gadol" = "the great man" or
       "the greatest man." ESV's "greatest" (superlative) may better
       capture the sense — Arba was the preeminent figure among the
       Anakim, possibly their ancestor or chief warrior.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Caleb          — "Dog" (Hebrew). Son of Jephunneh the Kenizzite, of the
               tribe of Judah. One of the twelve spies sent from
               Kadesh-barnea (Numbers 13:6). One of only two adults from
               the Exodus generation to enter the Promised Land (with
               Joshua). His defining trait: he "wholly followed the LORD."

Kenizzite      — A clan designation. Kenaz was an Edomite clan name
               (Genesis 36:11,42), suggesting Caleb's family may have
               been absorbed into Judah. This makes his wholehearted
               devotion to Israel's God especially notable.

Kadesh-barnea  — The location in the wilderness of Paran/Zin where Israel
               camped and sent the twelve spies into Canaan (Numbers 13).
               Israel's refusal to enter from Kadesh-barnea resulted in
               38 additional years of wandering.

Anakim         — Descendants of Anak, a race of unusually tall people
               dwelling in the hill country of Judah, especially around
               Hebron. The ten faithless spies feared them (Numbers
               13:33). Caleb, by faith, conquered them.

Hebron         — One of the oldest cities in Canaan (Numbers 13:22 says
               it was built seven years before Zoan in Egypt). Located
               in the Judean hill country, about 19 miles south of
               Jerusalem. Associated with Abraham, Sarah, Isaac, Jacob.
               Later David's first capital. Elevation: ~3,000 feet.

Wholly Followed — Hebrew "male' acharei YHWH" — to fill up after the
               LORD, to follow completely without holding back. Used of
               Caleb repeatedly (Numbers 14:24; 32:12; Deuteronomy 1:36;
               Joshua 14:8,9,14). The opposite of the half-hearted
               obedience that characterized most of Israel.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.1 — ASV: "the inheritances which the children of Israel took" / NET: "a record of the territory assigned to the Israelites" — NET uses "territory assigned" where ASV/KJV use "inheritances" (nachalot). The inheritance concept (nachalah) is the dominant theme of chapters 14-21; NET's administrative language obscures this theological framework.
- v.8 — ASV: "I wholly followed Jehovah my God" / WEB: "I remained completely faithful to Yahweh my God" / NET: "I remained loyal to the Lord my God" / KJV: "I wholly followed the LORD my God" — Hebrew "male acharey YHWH" = "fully followed after YHWH." KJV/ASV's "wholly followed" is most literal; NET/WEB interpret as loyalty/faithfulness. The Hebrew image is of a person walking completely in God's footsteps without deviation.
- v.9 — NET: "Surely the land on which you walked will belong to you and your descendants permanently" / KJV: "Surely the land whereon thy feet have trodden shall be thine inheritance" / ASV: "the land whereon thy foot hath trodden shall be an inheritance to thee" — NET replaces "inheritance" with "belong to you permanently." "Inheritance" (nachalah) carries multigenerational covenant weight beyond mere ownership.
- v.12 — ASV: "give me this hill-country, whereof Jehovah spake in that day" / WEB: "give me this hill country, of which Yahweh spoke" / NET: "assign me this hill country that the Lord promised me" — NET uses "assign" (bureaucratic) where ASV/WEB/KJV use "give" (Hebrew "tenah" — give me!). Caleb's request is bold and personal: "Give me!" — not a polite administrative request.
- v.13 — NET: "Joshua asked God to empower Caleb" / KJV: "And Joshua blessed him" / ASV: "And Joshua blessed him" / WEB: "Joshua blessed him" — NET dramatically reinterprets "blessed" (barakh — to bless/kneel) as "asked God to empower." KJV/ASV/WEB preserve the simple and beautiful "Joshua blessed him" — a patriarchal act of conferring divine favor.
- v.14 — ASV: "he wholly followed Jehovah, the God of Israel" / WEB: "he completely followed Yahweh, the God of Israel" / NET: "he remained loyal to the Lord God of Israel" — The phrase repeats from v.8. Caleb's defining characteristic in Scripture is total devotion to YHWH, rendered consistently as "wholly followed" in KJV/ASV.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Give me this mountain" (v.12). Caleb at 85 does not ask for ease —
   he asks for the hardest assignment. Faith does not grow timid with age.
   Are we asking God for mountains, or are we settling for comfortable
   valleys? The giants on the mountain do not intimidate faith that rests
   in God's promises.

2. "I wholly followed the LORD" (vv.8,9,14). This phrase appears three
   times — it is Caleb's epitaph. Partial obedience characterized the
   ten spies and most of Israel. Caleb stood apart because his devotion
   was complete. What would our epitaph be? Do we follow wholly or
   halfway?

3. God's promises do not expire (vv.9-10). Forty-five years passed
   between the promise and its fulfillment. God kept Caleb alive
   specifically to fulfill His word. If God has promised something, time
   does not cancel it. Wait on the Lord — His timing is perfect.

4. Strength preserved for purpose (v.11). Caleb's vigor at 85 was not
   accidental — God sustained him for the task ahead. When God calls us
   to something, He provides the strength to accomplish it. His
   assignments come with His enablement.

5. Faith sees opportunity where others see obstacles (v.12). The Anakim
   and fortified cities were the same obstacles that terrified the ten
   spies 45 years earlier. Caleb saw the same giants — but he also saw
   God. The difference between faith and fear is not what we see but
   WHO we see standing behind it.

6. "The land had rest from war" (v.15). After Caleb takes his mountain,
   peace comes. When God's people act in faith and obedience, rest
   follows. The battles we avoid in fear only prolong our unrest.
