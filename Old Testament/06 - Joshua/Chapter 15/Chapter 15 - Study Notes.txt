Joshua - Chapter 15
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
The detailed boundaries of the tribe of Judah's inheritance are described, stretching from the Dead Sea to the Mediterranean. Caleb's daughter Achsah asks for springs of water, but the Jebusites in Jerusalem remain unconquered.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Judah's territory: The largest allotment — from the Dead Sea east to the Mediterranean west, Kadesh-barnea south to Beth-shemesh north
- Key cities in Judah: Hebron, Debir, Beer-sheba (Negev), En-gedi (Dead Sea coast), Lachish (Shephelah/lowlands)
- Jerusalem (Jebus) is on Judah's northern border — listed as unconquered (v.63); Jebusites remain

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.13 — "And to Caleb the son of Jephunneh he gave a part among the
        children of Judah, according to the commandment of the LORD to
        Joshua, even the city of Arba the father of Anak, which city is
        Hebron." (ESV) — Caleb receives what was promised. God's word through
        Moses is fulfilled through Joshua. The city of the giants becomes
        the inheritance of the man of faith.

v.14 — "And Caleb drove there the three sons of Anak, Sheshai, and
        Ahiman, and Talmai, the children of Anak." (ESV) — The giants that
        terrified ten spies 45 years earlier are driven out by one
        85-year-old man of faith. What fear could not face, faith
        conquered.

v.16 — "And Caleb said, He that smiteth Kirjath-sepher, and takes it,
        to him will I give Achsah my daughter to wife." (ESV) — Caleb
        incentivizes courage. He raises up the next generation of
        warriors by rewarding boldness. Othniel's response shows he
        inherited Caleb's spirit of faith.

v.19 — "Give me a blessing; for you hast given me a south land; give
        me also springs of water. And he gave her the upper springs, and
        the nether springs." (ESV) — Achsah's wisdom: she asks for what will
        make her inheritance fruitful. Land without water is barren.
        She asks boldly and receives generously — both upper and lower
        springs.

v.63 — "As for the Jebusites the inhabitants of Jerusalem, the children
        of Judah could not drive them out: but the Jebusites dwell with
        the children of Judah at Jerusalem to this day." (ESV) — Another
        failure of incomplete conquest. Jerusalem would not fall until
        David's time (2 Samuel 5:6-7). "Could not" likely means "did
        not" — lack of faith, not lack of power.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/joshua-15/
  Guzik notes that Achsah's request for springs of water is a picture of
  asking God for spiritual refreshment to accompany our inheritance. Land
  without water is useless; blessings without the Spirit's refreshing are
  barren. He also highlights that Judah's failure with the Jebusites
  shows that even the strongest tribe had areas of incomplete obedience.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "Achsah said, 'Give me springs of water.' She had the land — but land
  without water is a desert. So the believer may have doctrine without
  devotion, position without power, inheritance without refreshment. Ask
  for the springs! Ask for the upper springs of heavenly communion and
  the nether springs of earthly provision. God gives both to those who
  ask boldly."
TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1  — KJV: "the lot of the tribe of the children of Judah" / ESV: "The
       allotment for the tribe of the people of Judah" — Hebrew "goral"
       = lot, portion determined by lot. ESV's "allotment" emphasizes
       the result; KJV's "lot" emphasizes the method of determination.

v.2  — KJV: "the salt sea" / ESV: "the Salt Sea" — The Dead Sea. Called
       "salt sea" (yam ha-melach) because of its extreme salinity. Also
       called the Sea of the Arabah (Deuteronomy 3:17).

v.3  — KJV: "Maaleh-acrabbim" / ESV: "the ascent of Akrabbim" — Hebrew
       "ma'aleh aqrabbim" = "ascent of scorpions." KJV transliterates;
       ESV translates the first word and transliterates the second. A
       steep pass in the southern Negev, likely infested with scorpions.

v.4  — KJV: "the river of Egypt" / ESV: "the Brook of Egypt" — Hebrew
       "nachal Mitsrayim." This is Wadi el-Arish, not the Nile. "Nachal"
       = a seasonal stream/wadi, not a major river ("nahar"). ESV's
       "Brook" is more accurate than KJV's "river."

v.8  — KJV: "the valley of the giants" / ESV: "the Valley of Rephaim"
       — Hebrew "emeq Repha'im." As in 13:12, KJV translates "Rephaim"
       as "giants"; ESV preserves the proper name. This valley southwest
       of Jerusalem was later a Philistine staging ground (2 Samuel 5:18).

v.15 — KJV: "Kirjath-sepher" / ESV: "Kiriath-sepher" — Hebrew "Qiryat
       Sepher" = "City of the Book" or "City of Writing." This suggests
       it may have been a scribal center or archive city. Later called
       Debir.

v.18 — KJV: "she lighted off her ass" / ESV: "she dismounted from her
       donkey" — Hebrew "titsna me'al ha-chamor." "Tsanach" = to
       descend, dismount. Dismounting was a sign of respect and
       earnestness when making a request. ESV modernizes "ass" to
       "donkey."

v.19 — KJV: "the upper springs, and the nether springs" / ESV: "the
       upper springs and the lower springs" — Hebrew "gullot 'illiyot"
       and "gullot tachtiyot." "Gullah" = a spring, basin, bowl.
       KJV's "nether" is archaic for "lower." Caleb gives generously —
       both sets of springs, not just one.

v.63 — KJV: "could not drive them out" / ESV: "could not drive out" —
       Hebrew "lo yakh'lu l'horisham." "Yakol" = to be able, to prevail.
       The question is whether this indicates inability or unwillingness.
       Given God's promise to drive out all inhabitants (Exodus 23:30),
       the failure was likely one of faith, not power.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Judah          — Fourth son of Jacob and Leah. The tribe of Judah received
               the largest allotment and held preeminence among the tribes.
               The Davidic dynasty and ultimately the Messiah came from
               Judah (Genesis 49:10).

Negev          — Hebrew "negev" = south, dry land. The arid southern
               region of Judah's territory. Achsah's "south land" was
               in this dry region, hence her need for springs.

Shephelah      — The "lowland" — the foothills between the coastal plain
               and the Judean hill country. A strategically important
               buffer zone between Israel and the Philistines.

Valley of Hinnom — Hebrew "Ge-Hinnom" (later "Gehenna"). A valley on
               Jerusalem's south side. Later became a site of child
               sacrifice (2 Kings 23:10) and a garbage dump. Jesus used
               "Gehenna" as a term for hell (Mark 9:43-48).

Othniel        — Son of Kenaz, Caleb's younger brother (or nephew).
               Captured Debir and married Achsah. Later became Israel's
               first judge, delivering them from Cushan-rishathaim
               (Judges 3:9-11). A model of Spirit-empowered leadership.

Achsah         — Caleb's daughter, given in marriage to Othniel. Her
               bold request for springs of water shows wisdom and
               initiative. She understood that inheritance without
               resources is incomplete.

Jebusites      — The Canaanite inhabitants of Jerusalem (called "Jebus").
               They remained unconquered until David's time (~1000 BC),
               nearly 400 years after Joshua.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.13 — ASV: "according to the commandment of Jehovah to Joshua" / NET: "according to the Lord's instructions to Joshua" / KJV: "according to the commandment of the LORD to Joshua" — NET softens "commandment" (Hebrew "peh YHWH" — mouth/command of YHWH) to "instructions." The Hebrew conveys direct divine speech — a command, not a suggestion.
- v.16 — ASV: "He that smiteth Kiriath-sepher, and taketh it" / NET: "To the man who attacks and captures Kiriath Sepher" — Both are adequate; NET modernizes the challenge. The cultural context of a warrior earning a bride through military valor is preserved in both.
- v.18 — NET: "One time Achsah came and charmed her father so that she could ask him for some land" / KJV: "she moved him to ask of her father a field" / ASV: "she moved him to ask of her father a field" — NET adds "charmed" (not in Hebrew). The Hebrew "sut" means to incite or urge — Achsah persuaded (either her husband or herself was moved to ask). NET's "charmed" introduces a nuance absent from the text.
- v.19 — ASV: "Give me a blessing; for that thou hast set me in the land of the South, give me also springs of water" / NET: "Please give me a special present. Since you have given me land in the Negev, now give me springs of water" — Hebrew "b'rakhah" (blessing) is rendered "special present" by NET, losing the theological dimension. Achsah asks for a "blessing" — she understands inheritance as divine favor, not just real estate.
- v.20 — ASV: "This is the inheritance of the tribe of the children of Judah" / NET: "This is the land assigned to the tribe of Judah by its clans" — Again, NET replaces "inheritance" (nachalah) with "land assigned" — an administrative rather than covenantal concept.
- v.63 — ASV: "the children of Judah could not drive them out" / NET: "The men of Judah were unable to conquer the Jebusites living in Jerusalem" — NET's "conquer" vs. ASV/KJV's "drive out" (Hebrew "horish" — to dispossess). The Hebrew emphasizes removal/dispossession rather than mere military conquest.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Caleb drives out the giants (v.14). What terrified a generation, one
   man of faith conquers. The "impossible" obstacles in our lives are not
   impossible to God. Caleb at 85 does what Israel at full strength
   feared to attempt. Age and difficulty are no barriers to faith.

2. Raising the next generation (vv.16-17). Caleb challenges the young
   men: "Who will take this city?" He incentivizes courage and raises up
   Othniel — who becomes Israel's first judge. Godly leaders reproduce
   themselves by challenging others to step up.

3. "Give me springs of water" (v.19). Achsah's request is a model for
   prayer. She had land but needed water to make it fruitful. We may have
   position, knowledge, or opportunity — but without the refreshing of
   God's Spirit, it remains barren. Ask boldly for spiritual springs.

4. The failure at Jerusalem (v.63). Even Judah — the strongest tribe —
   had areas of defeat. No believer is immune to compromise. The enemies
   we fail to conquer become permanent residents. What "Jebusites" have
   we allowed to remain in our lives?

5. God's detailed care (vv.1-12). The precise boundary descriptions show
   that God cares about specifics. He is not vague about His promises or
   His provisions. Every border, every city, every spring is known to Him
   and allocated with purpose.
