Joshua - Chapter 1
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
God commissions Joshua to lead Israel into the Promised Land, commanding him three times to be strong and courageous. Joshua rallies the people, who pledge complete obedience to his leadership.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Joshua, with possible later editorial additions. Joshua was Moses'
assistant and successor, who led Israel into the Promised Land. The book
covers approximately 25 years (1406-1381 BC) — from the crossing of the
Jordan to Joshua's death. Hebrew title: 'Yehoshua' — 'YHWH saves' (the
same name as Jesus in Greek). The book demonstrates God's faithfulness to
His land promise to Abraham (Genesis 15:18-21) and shows that victory
comes through faith and obedience, not human strength.

Historical Context: Chapter 1 opens immediately after the death of Moses
(Deuteronomy 34). Israel is camped on the plains of Moab, east of the
Jordan River, opposite Jericho. Forty years of wilderness wandering are
over. The old generation has died. A new generation stands at the border
of the Promised Land — and they need a new leader. God speaks directly to
Joshua, commissioning him as Moses' successor. The chapter contains God's
threefold command to "be strong and of a good courage" (vv.6,7,9), the
command to meditate on the law day and night (v.8), and the people's
pledge of complete obedience to Joshua's leadership (vv.16-18). This is
a chapter of transition: from Moses to Joshua, from wilderness to
conquest, from waiting to action. The key phrase is "arise, go over this
Jordan" (v.2) — the time for preparation is over; the time for possession
has come.

God's Commission to Joshua (vv.1-9): "Moses my servant is dead; now
therefore arise, go over this Jordan" (v.2). God does not dwell on the
past — Moses is dead, now move forward. The promise: "Every place that
the sole of your foot shall tread upon, that have I given unto you" (v.3).
The territory defined: from the wilderness to Lebanon, to the Euphrates,
to the Great Sea (v.4). The assurance: "as I was with Moses, so I will be
with thee: I will not fail thee, nor forsake thee" (v.5). Three commands
to be strong and courageous (vv.6,7,9) — each with a different emphasis:
v.6 — courage for the task (dividing the inheritance); v.7 — courage for
obedience (keeping the law); v.9 — courage against fear (God is with you).
The condition for success: meditate on the law day and night, observe to
do all that is written (v.8).

Joshua Commands the People (vv.10-15): Joshua immediately acts on God's
commission. He commands the officers: "Prepare you victuals; for within
three days ye shall pass over this Jordan" (v.11). He reminds the
Transjordan tribes (Reuben, Gad, half-Manasseh) of their obligation to
fight alongside their brothers before returning to their inheritance east
of the Jordan (vv.12-15). This fulfills the agreement made with Moses
(Numbers 32).

The People's Response (vv.16-18): The people pledge total obedience: "All
that thou commandest us we will do, and whithersoever thou sendest us, we
will go" (v.16). They affirm Joshua's authority as equal to Moses': "as
we hearkened unto Moses in all things, so will we hearken unto thee" (v.17).
They even pronounce the death penalty on any who rebel (v.18). Their final
word to Joshua echoes God's own command: "only be strong and of a good
courage" (v.18).

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Plains of Moab: Israel's camp east of the Jordan River, opposite Jericho — the staging ground for the conquest
- Jordan River: The main boundary between wilderness wandering and the Promised Land; at flood stage during spring crossing
- Territory promised (v.4): From the wilderness (Negev) to Lebanon, from the Euphrates to the Great Sea (Mediterranean)
- Gilgal: Will become Israel's first base camp west of the Jordan, near Jericho (established in ch.4-5)

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "Moses my servant is dead. Now therefore arise, go over this
        Jordan, you and all this people, into the land that I am giving
        to them, to the people of Israel." (ESV) — God does not linger
        on loss. Moses is dead — now arise. The work of God does not stop
        with the death of any servant. New leaders must step forward.

v.3  — "Every place that the sole of your foot will tread upon I have
        given to you, just as I promised to Moses." (ESV) — The land is
        already given (past tense) but must be possessed by faith. God's
        promises require human action to appropriate them.

v.5  — "No man shall be able to stand before you all the days of your
        life. Just as I was with Moses, so I will be with you. I will not
        leave you or forsake you." (ESV) — The guarantee of God's
        presence. Quoted in Hebrews 13:5. The same God who parted the Red
        Sea will be with Joshua.

v.7  — "Only be strong and very courageous, being careful to do according
        to all the law that Moses my servant commanded you. Do not turn
        from it to the right hand or to the left, that you may have good
        success wherever you go." (ESV) — True courage is obedience.
        Strength is needed not just for battle but for faithfulness to
        God's Word.

v.8  — "This Book of the Law shall not depart from your mouth, but you
        shall meditate on it day and night, so that you may be careful to
        do according to all that is written in it. For then you will make
        your way prosperous, and then you will have good success." (ESV)
        — The secret of success: constant meditation on and obedience to
        Scripture. Cf. Psalm 1:1-3. Prosperity comes from the Word, not
        from military strategy.

v.9  — "Have I not commanded you? Be strong and courageous. Do not be
        frightened, and do not be dismayed, for the LORD your God is with
        you wherever you go." (ESV) — The third and final command to
        courage. The basis: God's presence. Fear is incompatible with
        faith in God's promise.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/joshua-1/
  Guzik notes that God's threefold command to be strong and courageous
  indicates Joshua needed encouragement — he was stepping into enormous
  shoes. The emphasis on the law (v.8) shows that Israel's conquest would
  succeed not by military genius but by spiritual obedience. Guzik also
  highlights that the land was "given" (past tense) but still had to be
  "possessed" — a picture of how believers must actively appropriate what
  God has already provided in Christ.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "God buries His workmen but carries on His work. Moses is dead — but
  Jehovah lives. The servant falls — but the Master stands. Joshua must
  now arise and go forward. And what is his equipment? Not military
  training, not political cunning, but the Book of the Law in his mouth
  and the presence of God at his side. That is enough for any man."
TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1  — KJV: "Moses' minister" / ESV: "Moses' assistant" — Hebrew
       "mesharet Mosheh." "Mesharet" = one who serves, ministers, attends.
       It denotes a personal attendant of high rank — not a menial servant
       (which would be "eved") but a trusted aide. Joshua served Moses as
       a disciple serves a master.

v.2  — KJV: "which I do give to them" / ESV: "that I am giving to them"
       — Hebrew "asher ani noten lahem." The participle "noten" (giving)
       indicates ongoing, present action — God is actively giving the land
       NOW. It is both a completed act (v.3 "I have given") and a present
       reality being unfolded.

v.4  — KJV: "shall be your coast" / ESV: "shall be your territory" —
       Hebrew "g'vulkhem" = your border/territory. KJV's "coast" is
       archaic English for "border" or "boundary," not a seashore. ESV's
       "territory" is clearer for modern readers.

v.5  — KJV: "I will not fail thee, nor forsake thee" / ESV: "I will not
       leave you or forsake you" — Hebrew "lo arpe'ka v'lo e'ezavka."
       "Raphah" = to let go, relax the grip, abandon. "Azav" = to leave,
       forsake. Two different words for abandonment — God will neither
       loosen His hold nor walk away. Hebrews 13:5 quotes this with five
       negatives in Greek for emphasis.

v.7  — KJV: "that thou mayest prosper" / ESV: "that you may have good
       success" — Hebrew "l'ma'an taskil." "Sakal" = to be prudent, act
       wisely, prosper, have insight. It implies both wisdom in action and
       the success that follows from wise action. True prosperity is the
       fruit of obedience.

v.8  — KJV: "thou shalt have good success" / ESV: "you will have good
       success" — Hebrew "tatsliach." From "tsalach" = to rush, advance,
       prosper, succeed. This is a different word from "sakal" in v.7.
       Together they paint a full picture: wisdom (sakal) and advancement
       (tsalach) — both come from meditating on God's Word.

v.11 — KJV: "Prepare you victuals" / ESV: "Prepare your provisions" —
       Hebrew "hakinu lakhem tsedah." "Tsedah" = provisions, food for a
       journey. The manna would soon cease (Joshua 5:12); they must now
       prepare natural food. The transition from miraculous provision to
       normal means.

v.14 — KJV: "all the mighty men of valour" / ESV: "all the men of valor"
       — Hebrew "kol gibborey hachayil." "Gibbor" = mighty man, warrior.
       "Chayil" = strength, valor, army. These are the fighting men — the
       Transjordan tribes must send their warriors, not just token forces.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Joshua (Yehoshua) — "YHWH saves" or "YHWH is salvation." The same name
               as "Jesus" (Greek: Iesous). Moses' assistant from youth
               (Exodus 33:11), one of the two faithful spies (Numbers
               14:6-9), commissioned as Moses' successor (Numbers 27:18-23).
               From the tribe of Ephraim.

Minister/Assistant — Hebrew "mesharet." A personal attendant of high
               rank. Joshua served Moses in this capacity from the time
               of the Exodus (Exodus 24:13). The term implies close
               proximity, trusted service, and preparation for leadership.

The Jordan     — The main river of Israel, flowing from the Sea of Galilee
               south to the Dead Sea. At the time of crossing (spring
               harvest), it was at flood stage (Joshua 3:15). Crossing it
               required divine intervention.

Transjordan Tribes — Reuben, Gad, and the half-tribe of Manasseh. They
               received their inheritance east of the Jordan (Numbers 32)
               but were obligated to help conquer the western land before
               settling permanently.

Book of the Law — Hebrew "sepher ha-torah." The written law of Moses —
               likely the Pentateuch (or at minimum Deuteronomy). Joshua
               was to have it constantly in his mouth (speaking it),
               mind (meditating on it), and life (obeying it).

Meditate       — Hebrew "hagah." To murmur, mutter, speak quietly to
               oneself. Hebrew meditation is not silent emptying of the
               mind but active, verbal repetition and reflection on
               God's Word. Cf. Psalm 1:2.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.1 — ASV/WEB: "Jehovah"/"Yahweh" vs. KJV/ESV: "the LORD" — ASV uses "Jehovah" and WEB uses "Yahweh" to render the divine name (YHWH) directly, while KJV/ESV follow the convention of substituting "the LORD" (small caps). This distinction appears throughout Joshua.
- v.1 — NET: "the Lord's servant" / ASV: "the servant of Jehovah" / KJV: "the servant of the LORD" — NET drops the small-caps convention entirely, using "Lord" in standard case, making the divine name indistinguishable from the title "Adonai."
- v.2 — NET: "Get ready! Cross the Jordan River" / KJV: "arise, go over this Jordan" — NET uses dynamic equivalence, making God's command sound urgent and contemporary ("Get ready!"), while KJV/ASV/WEB retain the literal Hebrew "arise" (qum), preserving the imagery of standing up to take action.
- v.6 — NET: "You must lead these people in the conquest of this land" / KJV: "unto this people shalt thou divide for an inheritance the land" / ASV: "thou shalt cause this people to inherit the land" — NET interprets "inherit" as "conquest," adding military connotation. KJV/ASV/WEB preserve the inheritance (nachalah) language, which connects to God's covenant promise to the fathers.
- v.8 — NET: "This law scroll must not leave your lips. You must memorize it day and night" / KJV: "thou shalt meditate therein day and night" / WEB: "you shall meditate on it day and night" — NET renders "hagah" (meditate/murmur) as "memorize," losing the Hebrew concept of vocal, repetitive meditation. ASV/WEB/KJV preserve "meditate," which implies speaking and pondering, not mere mental recall.
- v.9 — NET: "Don't be afraid and don't panic" / KJV: "be not afraid, neither be thou dismayed" / ASV: "be not affrighted, neither be thou dismayed" — NET uses colloquial "don't panic" for Hebrew "al techat" (dismayed/shattered). ASV's archaic "affrighted" and KJV's "afraid" are more literal but less accessible.
- v.11 — NET: "begin the conquest of the land" / WEB: "to go in to possess the land" / KJV: "to go in to possess the land" — NET again inserts "conquest" language where the Hebrew uses "yarash" (to possess/inherit). WEB and KJV preserve the theological concept that the land is an inheritance to be possessed, not merely a military objective.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. God buries His workers but continues His work (vv.1-2). Moses is dead,
   but God's purposes march on. No human leader is indispensable. When one
   servant falls, God raises another. The work is God's, not ours.

2. The threefold call to courage (vv.6,7,9). Joshua needed to hear this
   three times — and so do we. Courage for the task (v.6), courage for
   obedience (v.7), courage against fear (v.9). True courage is not the
   absence of fear but faith in God's presence despite fear.

3. The secret of success is the Word (v.8). Not strategy, not strength,
   not numbers — but meditation on Scripture day and night. The leader's
   first priority is not planning but saturating himself in God's Word.
   Success flows from obedience, and obedience flows from meditation.

4. Promises must be possessed (v.3). God says "I have given" (past tense)
   but Joshua must still "tread" on it. Divine sovereignty and human
   responsibility work together. God gives the land; we must walk in faith
   to take it. Spiritual blessings in Christ are ours — but we must
   appropriate them by faith.

5. "I will not fail thee, nor forsake thee" (v.5). The same promise given
   to Joshua is given to every believer (Hebrews 13:5). God's presence is
   the answer to every fear. If God is with us, it does not matter who is
   against us (Romans 8:31).

6. Unity in the body (vv.12-15). The Transjordan tribes had their
   inheritance but were still obligated to fight for their brothers. Having
   received our own blessing does not exempt us from helping others obtain
   theirs. We are one body; one member's battle is every member's battle.
