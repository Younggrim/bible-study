Joshua - Chapter 9
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
The Gibeonites deceive Israel with moldy bread and worn-out clothes, pretending to be from a far country. Israel makes a covenant with them without consulting the LORD, and must honor it despite the deception.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Joshua, with possible later editorial additions. Joshua was Moses'
assistant and successor, who led Israel into the Promised Land. The book
covers approximately 25 years (1406-1381 BC) — from the crossing of the
Jordan to Joshua's death. Hebrew title: 'Yehoshua' — 'YHWH saves' (the
same name as Jesus in Greek). The book demonstrates God's faithfulness to
His land promise to Abraham (Genesis 15:18-21) and shows that victory
comes through faith and obedience, not human strength.

Historical Context: Chapter 9 records the Gibeonite deception — a
cautionary tale about the danger of acting without consulting God. While
most Canaanite kings unite to fight Israel (vv.1-2), the Gibeonites
choose a different strategy: deception. They disguise themselves as
travelers from a distant land and trick Israel into making a peace treaty.
The critical failure: "the men took of their victuals, and asked not
counsel at the mouth of the LORD" (v.14). Israel relied on their own
judgment — examining the evidence (old bread, worn sandals, torn
wineskins) — rather than seeking God's guidance. The result: a binding
oath that cannot be broken, even though it was obtained by fraud. The
Gibeonites are spared but reduced to permanent servitude as "hewers of
wood and drawers of water" for the tabernacle. This chapter teaches that
even after great victories (Jericho, Ai), God's people remain vulnerable
to deception when they fail to seek divine counsel. The enemy's most
dangerous weapon is not military force but cunning — and the only defense
is dependence on God.

The Canaanite Coalition (vv.1-2): All the kings west of the Jordan —
Hittites, Amorites, Canaanites, Perizzites, Hivites, and Jebusites —
hear of Israel's victories and unite "with one accord" to fight (vv.1-2).
This is the expected response: military opposition.

The Gibeonite Deception (vv.3-13): The Gibeonites choose cunning over
combat (v.3). They "did work wilily" (v.4) — disguising themselves with
old sacks, torn wineskins, worn shoes, old garments, and dry, moldy bread
(vv.4-5). They come to Gilgal claiming to be from "a far country" (v.6)
and ask for a treaty. Israel is suspicious: "Peradventure ye dwell among
us" (v.7). The Gibeonites claim they came "because of the name of the
LORD thy God" — citing the fame of what God did in Egypt and to Sihon and
Og (vv.9-10). They carefully avoid mentioning Jericho and Ai (recent
events that would prove they are nearby). They present their "evidence":
bread that was hot when they left, now dry; new wineskins, now torn;
fresh clothes, now worn (vv.12-13).

Israel's Failure (vv.14-15): The fatal verse: "And the men took of their
victuals, and asked not counsel at the mouth of the LORD" (v.14). They
examined the physical evidence but did not pray. They trusted their senses
instead of seeking God. Joshua makes peace, makes a covenant to let them
live, and the princes swear an oath (v.15).

The Deception Discovered (vv.16-21): Three days later, Israel discovers
the Gibeonites are neighbors — their cities (Gibeon, Chephirah, Beeroth,
Kirjath-jearim) are nearby (vv.16-17). Israel cannot attack them because
of the oath sworn "by the LORD God of Israel" (v.18). The congregation
murmurs against the leaders (v.18). The princes insist: the oath must be
honored, "lest wrath be upon us" (vv.19-20). Solution: the Gibeonites
will live but serve as "hewers of wood and drawers of water" (v.21).

Joshua's Judgment (vv.22-27): Joshua confronts the Gibeonites: "Wherefore
have ye beguiled us?" (v.22). He pronounces their sentence: "ye are
cursed, and there shall none of you be freed from being bondmen, and
hewers of wood and drawers of water for the house of my God" (v.23). The
Gibeonites explain their motive: they feared for their lives because God
commanded Moses to destroy all inhabitants (v.24). They submit: "as it
seemeth good and right unto thee to do unto us, do" (v.25). Joshua spares
them and assigns them to permanent tabernacle service (v.27).

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Gibeon: A major city in the central highlands, about 6 miles northwest of Jerusalem
- The Gibeonites controlled four cities: Gibeon, Chephirah, Beeroth, and Kiriath-jearim — all in Benjamin's territory
- Gibeon's strategic location made it a key ally (or threat) for controlling the central hill country

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.4  — "They did work wilily, and went and made as if they had been
        ambassadors." (ESV) — The enemy's most dangerous weapon is not force
        but deception. When frontal assault fails, Satan uses cunning
        (2 Corinthians 11:14).

v.14 — "And the men took of their provisions, and asked not counsel at the
        mouth of the LORD." (ESV) — The chapter's key verse. Israel's failure
        was not military but spiritual — they did not pray. Self-reliance
        in discernment led to deception. Always seek God's counsel before
        making binding decisions.

v.15 — "And Joshua made peace with them, and made a league with them, to
        let them live: and the princes of the congregation swore to
        them." (ESV) — A binding oath made in haste, without divine guidance.
        Once sworn in God's name, it cannot be broken — even though
        obtained by fraud.

v.19 — "We have sworn to them by the LORD God of Israel: now therefore
        we may not touch them." (ESV) — The sanctity of an oath. Even a
        foolish oath sworn in God's name must be honored. God takes vows
        seriously (cf. Ecclesiastes 5:4-5). Saul later violated this oath
        and brought judgment on Israel (2 Samuel 21:1-2).

v.23 — "Now therefore you are cursed, and there shall none of you be freed
        from being bondmen, and hewers of wood and drawers of water for
        the house of my God." (ESV) — The Gibeonites are spared but reduced to
        servitude. Deception saves their lives but costs their freedom.
        Yet their service is for God's house — even judgment has grace.

v.27 — "Joshua made them that day hewers of wood and drawers of water for
        the congregation, and for the altar of the LORD." (ESV) — The
        Gibeonites serve the tabernacle. What began as deception ends in
        proximity to God's presence. Later, the Nethinim (temple servants)
        may have descended from them.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/joshua-9/
  Guzik emphasizes that the Gibeonites' deception succeeded because Israel
  trusted their own senses rather than seeking God. The moldy bread and
  worn sandals were convincing — but physical evidence can be fabricated.
  Only God's counsel is infallible. He also notes that the Gibeonites'
  faith (like Rahab's) was real — they believed God would destroy them
  and acted to survive. Their method was wrong, but their assessment of
  God's power was correct.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "'Asked not counsel at the mouth of the LORD' — there is the whole
  disaster in one sentence. They looked at the bread and believed their
  eyes. They felt the shoes and trusted their fingers. But they never
  bent the knee and asked the Almighty. How many treaties have we made
  with the world because we examined the evidence but forgot to pray?
  The eye deceives; the ear misleads; but God never lies."
TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.4  — Hebrew "gam hemmah asu b'ormah" — "ormah" = craftiness, cunning, shrewdness; Hebrew "va-yitstayyaru" — debated: from "tsir" (ambassador) or "tsedah" (provisions).
       KJV: "They did work wilily... made as if they had been ambassadors"
       ESV: "they on their part acted with cunning... made ready provisions"
       ASV: "they also did work wilily"
       NET: "they did something clever"
       WEB: "they also resorted to a ruse"
       Analysis: "Ormah" is used positively of wisdom in Proverbs (1:4; 8:5) but here negatively — deceptive cunning. The Gibeonites were shrewd, not wise. The debated word "va-yitstayyaru" yields different meanings: KJV derives from "tsir" (ambassador/envoy); ESV from "tsedah" (provisions). NET/WEB avoid the ambiguity with general language. The Hebrew context supports either reading.

v.8  — Hebrew "avadekha" — your servants; a diplomatic formula expressing submission.
       KJV: "We are thy servants"
       ESV: "We are your servants"
       ASV: "We are thy servants"
       NET: "We are willing to be your subjects"
       WEB: "We are your servants"
       Analysis: NET interprets "avadekha" (your servants) as "willing to be your subjects," adding political nuance not in the Hebrew. KJV/ASV/ESV/WEB preserve the simple declaration of servitude, which is both a diplomatic formula and a genuine expression of submission.

v.9  — Hebrew "shem" — name; representing God's character, authority, and presence.
       KJV: "because of the name of the LORD thy God"
       ESV: "because of the name of the LORD your God"
       ASV: "because of the name of Jehovah thy God"
       NET: "because of the reputation of the Lord your God"
       WEB: "because of the name of Yahweh your God"
       Analysis: NET renders "shem" (name) as "reputation," which captures one aspect but loses the Hebrew theology of "the Name" (ha-Shem) as representing God's character, authority, and presence — not merely reputation but identity.

v.14 — Hebrew "v'et pi Adonai lo sha'alu" — literally "the mouth of the LORD they did not ask."
       KJV: "asked not counsel at the mouth of the LORD"
       ESV: "did not ask counsel from the LORD"
       ASV: "asked not counsel at the mouth of Jehovah"
       NET: "they failed to ask the Lord's advice"
       WEB: "didn't ask counsel from Yahweh's mouth"
       Analysis: NET's "failed to ask the Lord's advice" is mild. KJV/ASV/WEB's "at the mouth of" preserves the Hebrew "peh" (mouth), implying direct, verbal, revelatory communication — not just general "advice" but prophetic inquiry through the Urim and Thummim (Numbers 27:21) or direct prayer. This was Israel's critical failure.

v.15 — Hebrew "va-yikhrot lahem b'rit" — "karat b'rit" = to cut a covenant. Formal covenant-making language.
       KJV: "made peace with them, and made a league with them"
       ESV: "made peace with them and made a covenant with them"
       ASV: "made peace with them, and made a covenant with them"
       NET: "made a peace treaty with them and agreed to let them live"
       WEB: "made peace with them, and made a covenant with them"
       Analysis: NET's "peace treaty" and KJV's "league" both render Hebrew "b'rit" (covenant), but ESV/ASV/WEB's "covenant" better captures the binding, sacred nature of the agreement. Once cut, it could not be undone — breaking it brought divine wrath (cf. 2 Samuel 21:1-2).

v.21 — Hebrew "chot'vey etsim v'sho'avey mayim" — the lowest forms of manual labor.
       KJV: "hewers of wood and drawers of water"
       ESV: "cutters of wood and drawers of water"
       ASV: "hewers of wood and drawers of water"
       NET: "woodcutters and water carriers"
       WEB: "wood cutters and drawers of water"
       Analysis: All translations convey the essential meaning. These were tasks typically assigned to the lowest social class. KJV/ASV "hewers" is archaic but dignified. NET "woodcutters and water carriers" is plainest. Yet their service was "for the altar of the LORD" (v.27) — menial work in sacred service.

v.23 — Hebrew "arar" — to curse; "v'lo yikkaret mikkem eved" — a servant shall not be cut off from you.
       KJV: "ye are cursed, and there shall none of you be freed from being bondmen"
       ESV: "some of you shall never be anything but servants"
       ASV: "Now therefore ye are cursed"
       NET: "Now you are condemned to perpetual servitude"
       WEB: "Now therefore you are cursed"
       Analysis: NET softens "cursed" (Hebrew "arar") to "condemned to servitude." KJV/ASV/WEB preserve the stronger "cursed," connecting to the covenant curses of Deuteronomy. ESV interprets without using "cursed." The curse is perpetual servitude — there will always be servants among them. Yet this "curse" placed them in proximity to God's altar, a paradoxical blessing.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Gibeon         — A major Hivite city about 6 miles northwest of Jerusalem.
               One of the largest cities in the region — "a great city,
               as one of the royal cities" (Joshua 10:2). Its inhabitants
               chose deception over war to survive. Later became a
               significant Israelite city (the tabernacle was there in
               Solomon's time — 1 Kings 3:4-5).

Hivites        — One of the seven Canaanite nations. The Gibeonites were
               Hivites (v.7). They were among the peoples God commanded
               Israel to destroy (Deuteronomy 7:1).

League/Covenant — Hebrew "b'rit." A solemn, binding agreement between
               parties, often sealed with an oath in God's name. Once
               made, it was considered inviolable — breaking it brought
               divine wrath (cf. 2 Samuel 21:1-2).

Hewers of Wood and Drawers of Water — The lowest service roles in
               Israelite society. Assigned to the Gibeonites as their
               permanent status. Yet their service was specifically for
               the tabernacle/altar — placing them in proximity to God's
               presence despite their low status.

Wilily/Cunning — Hebrew "ormah." Craftiness, shrewdness. Can be positive
               (prudence) or negative (deception) depending on context.
               Here it describes the Gibeonites' elaborate ruse.

Mouth of the LORD — Hebrew "pi Adonai." God's spoken guidance, accessed
               through prayer, the Urim and Thummim, or prophetic word.
               Israel's failure was not consulting this source before
               making their decision.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Asked not counsel at the mouth of the LORD" (v.14). The most
   dangerous decisions are those made without prayer. Israel examined the
   evidence carefully — but evidence can be fabricated. Only God's counsel
   is infallible. Before every major decision: pray first, examine second.

2. The enemy uses deception when force fails (vv.3-5). When the
   Canaanites cannot defeat Israel militarily, they try cunning. Satan
   operates the same way: "transformed into an angel of light"
   (2 Corinthians 11:14). Be more vigilant against deception than against
   open attack.

3. Haste in covenant-making (v.15). Joshua made a binding oath in three
   days — without waiting, without praying, without investigating. Major
   commitments deserve time, prayer, and counsel. Do not let urgency
   (real or manufactured) pressure you into premature decisions.

4. The sanctity of oaths (vv.18-20). Even a foolish oath sworn in God's
   name must be honored. The princes refuse to break their word despite
   the deception. God takes vows seriously. Be slow to swear and faithful
   to fulfill. Saul later broke this oath and brought famine on Israel
   (2 Samuel 21:1-2).

5. God redeems even our mistakes (v.27). The Gibeonites — obtained by
   deception — end up serving God's altar. What began as Israel's failure
   becomes ongoing service to God's house. God weaves even our errors
   into His purposes. This does not excuse the error, but it demonstrates
   His sovereignty over our failures.

6. Fear can produce faith — even imperfect faith (v.24). The Gibeonites
   feared God's power and acted to survive. Their method was deceptive,
   but their theology was correct: God had commanded the destruction of
   all Canaanites. Like Rahab, they responded to what they heard about
   God — imperfectly, but genuinely.
