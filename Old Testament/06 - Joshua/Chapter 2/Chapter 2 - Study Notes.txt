Joshua - Chapter 2
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Joshua sends two spies to Jericho; Rahab the prostitute hides them and confesses faith in Israel's God. She secures a promise of protection for her family when Israel attacks.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Joshua, with possible later editorial additions. Joshua was Moses'
assistant and successor, who led Israel into the Promised Land. The book
covers approximately 25 years (1406-1381 BC) — from the crossing of the
Jordan to Joshua's death. Hebrew title: 'Yehoshua' — 'YHWH saves' (the
same name as Jesus in Greek). The book demonstrates God's faithfulness to
His land promise to Abraham (Genesis 15:18-21) and shows that victory
comes through faith and obedience, not human strength.

Historical Context: Chapter 2 records the mission of two spies sent
secretly from Shittim to reconnoiter Jericho — the first major obstacle
to Israel's conquest. Unlike the disastrous spy mission of Numbers 13-14
(twelve spies, forty days, a faithless report), this mission is small,
focused, and successful. The central figure is Rahab — a Canaanite
prostitute who hides the spies, confesses faith in Israel's God, and
secures salvation for her entire family through the scarlet cord. Rahab's
confession (v.11) is one of the most remarkable statements of faith in
the Old Testament, coming from a pagan woman in a doomed city. She is
later honored in the genealogy of Christ (Matthew 1:5), in the hall of
faith (Hebrews 11:31), and as an example of faith producing works (James
2:25). The chapter demonstrates that God's grace extends beyond ethnic
Israel to anyone who believes — a Gentile prostitute becomes an ancestor
of the Messiah.

The Spies Sent to Jericho (vv.1-7): Joshua sends two spies secretly from
Shittim (Israel's camp east of the Jordan) to "view the land, even
Jericho" (v.1). They enter Rahab's house — likely an inn on the city wall,
a logical place for strangers to lodge without suspicion. The king of
Jericho learns of their presence and demands Rahab surrender them (vv.2-3).
Rahab hides the spies under stalks of flax on her roof and misdirects the
pursuers toward the Jordan fords (vv.4-7).

Rahab's Confession of Faith (vv.8-11): Before the spies sleep, Rahab
makes her extraordinary declaration: "I know that the LORD hath given you
the land" (v.9). She recounts what Jericho has heard — the Red Sea
crossing and the defeat of Sihon and Og (v.10). The result: "our hearts
did melt, neither did there remain any more courage in any man" (v.11).
Her climactic confession: "the LORD your God, he is God in heaven above,
and in earth beneath" (v.11). This is monotheistic faith from a pagan
woman — she acknowledges YHWH as the one true God of all creation.

The Covenant and the Scarlet Cord (vv.12-21): Rahab asks the spies to
swear by the LORD to spare her family (vv.12-13). They agree: "Our life
for yours" (v.14). She lets them down by a cord through her window in the
city wall (v.15). The conditions: she must tie "this line of scarlet
thread in the window" (v.18), gather her family into her house (v.18),
and keep silent about their mission (v.20). Anyone who leaves the house
forfeits protection (v.19). Rahab immediately binds the scarlet cord in
her window (v.21) — an act of faith before the promise is fulfilled.

The Spies' Report (vv.22-24): After hiding three days in the hills, the
spies return to Joshua with a confident report: "Truly the LORD hath
delivered into our hands all the land; for even all the inhabitants of
the country do faint because of us" (v.24). Unlike the ten faithless
spies of Numbers 13, these two bring a report of faith.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Shittim: Israel's camp in the plains of Moab, east of the Jordan — the spies' departure point
- Jericho: First major Canaanite city west of the Jordan, located in the Jordan Valley near the Dead Sea
- Rahab's house was built into the city wall itself, overlooking the surrounding plain

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "And Joshua the son of Nun sent out of Shittim two men to spy
        secretly, saying, Go view the land, even Jericho." (ESV) — A focused,
        faith-filled reconnaissance. Only two spies (not twelve), sent
        secretly (not publicly). Joshua learned from the failure of
        Numbers 13.

v.9  — "I know that the LORD has given you the land, and that your
        terror is fallen upon us, and that all the inhabitants of the
        land faint because of you." (ESV) — Rahab's faith. She knows what
        Israel's God has done and draws the right conclusion. Faith comes
        by hearing (Romans 10:17).

v.11 — "For the LORD your God, he is God in heaven above, and in earth
        beneath." (ESV) — Rahab's confession — monotheistic faith from a pagan
        prostitute. She acknowledges YHWH as the universal God. This is
        saving faith (Hebrews 11:31).

v.14 — "Our life for yours, if you utter not this our business." (ESV) — The
        spies pledge their lives as surety for Rahab's family. A covenant
        of mutual faithfulness.

v.18 — "You shall bind this line of scarlet thread in the window which
        you didst let us down by." (ESV) — The scarlet cord — a sign of
        salvation. As the Passover blood on the doorposts saved Israel in
        Egypt, the scarlet cord in the window saves Rahab in Jericho.
        Both point to the blood of Christ.

v.21 — "And she bound the scarlet line in the window." (ESV) — Immediate
        obedience. Rahab does not wait until the army arrives — she acts
        in faith now. True faith acts on the promise before seeing the
        fulfillment.

v.24 — "Truly the LORD has delivered into our hands all the land." (ESV) —         A report of faith, not fear. These spies see what the ten spies
        of Numbers 13 could not: God has already given the victory.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/joshua-2/
  Guzik emphasizes that Rahab's faith was genuine despite her imperfect
  understanding and sinful background. God does not require perfect
  theology or perfect morality before He saves — He requires faith. The
  scarlet cord is a beautiful picture of salvation by the blood: those
  inside the house (under the cord) are saved; those outside perish.
  Rahab's inclusion in Christ's genealogy shows that no one is beyond
  God's grace.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "Rahab's faith was not perfect — she lied to protect the spies. But
  her faith was real. She believed God when her own people did not. She
  risked everything on the God of Israel. And that imperfect faith saved
  her — not because it was strong, but because it was placed in a strong
  God. The scarlet thread in the window — what a picture of the blood of
  Christ! One cord, one window, one house of safety in a city of doom."
TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1  — Hebrew "zonah" — unambiguously means "prostitute" (not merely "innkeeper" as some have suggested).
       KJV: "an harlot's house, named Rahab"
       ESV: "the house of a prostitute whose name was Rahab"
       ASV: "a harlot whose name was Rahab"
       NET: "a prostitute named Rahab"
       WEB: "a prostitute whose name was Rahab"
       Analysis: The New Testament confirms this meaning with Greek "porne" (Hebrews 11:31, James 2:25). KJV/ASV retain the older English "harlot" while ESV/NET/WEB use the modern "prostitute." NET adds interpretive detail elsewhere, calling the men "clients of mine" in v.4, suggesting Rahab used her profession as cover. God's grace reaches the lowest.

v.4  — Hebrew "yadah" — to know, be aware of.
       KJV: "I wist not whence they were"
       ESV: "I did not know where they were from"
       ASV: "I knew not whence they were"
       NET: "I have no idea where they were from"
       WEB: "I didn't know where they came from"
       Analysis: KJV's "wist" is archaic for "knew." NET's "I have no idea" is colloquial and emphatic. Rahab's deception raises ethical questions — she lied to protect the spies. Scripture records her lie without explicitly approving it; what is commended is her faith (Hebrews 11:31), not her method.

v.9  — Hebrew "namogu" — "mug" = to melt, dissolve, faint. The image is of courage dissolving like wax.
       KJV: "all the inhabitants of the land faint because of you"
       ESV: "all the inhabitants of the land melt away before you"
       ASV: "all the inhabitants of the land melt away before you"
       NET: "the Lord is handing this land over to you"
       WEB: "all the inhabitants of the land melt away before you"
       Analysis: KJV "faint" captures the effect; ESV/ASV/WEB "melt away" is more literal to the Hebrew "mug." NET uses present tense "is handing over" (interpretive) where Hebrew uses past tense "natan" (has given), weakening the sense of completed divine decree. ASV uses "Jehovah" and WEB "Yahweh" for the divine name. The terror of God has gone before Israel.

v.10 — Hebrew "cherem" — devoted to destruction; total destruction as an act of divine judgment.
       KJV: "what ye did unto the two kings of the Amorites"
       ESV: "what you did to the two kings of the Amorites"
       ASV: "whom ye utterly destroyed"
       NET: "how you annihilated the two Amorite kings"
       WEB: "what you did to the two kings of the Amorites"
       Analysis: NET's "annihilated" interprets the Hebrew "cherem" (devoted to destruction), which ASV renders as "utterly destroyed." The cherem concept is more than military annihilation — it is sacred devotion to God's judgment. KJV/ESV/WEB use the general "what you did," allowing the reader to fill in the meaning from context.

v.11 — Hebrew "v'lo qamah od ruach b'ish" — literally "no spirit rose again in any man."
       KJV: "neither did there remain any more courage in any man"
       ESV: "there was no spirit left in any man"
       ASV: "neither did there remain any more spirit in any man"
       NET: "no one has the courage to fight you"
       WEB: "neither did there remain any more spirit in any man"
       Analysis: "Ruach" here = spirit, courage, will to resist. KJV interprets ruach as "courage" — accurate but narrowing. ESV/ASV/WEB preserve "spirit," capturing the broader collapse of will. NET paraphrases with "courage to fight," adding military language not in the Hebrew. The inhabitants are spiritually defeated before Israel arrives.

v.12 — Hebrew "ot emet" — "ot" = sign, token, pledge; "emet" = truth, faithfulness, reliability. Hebrew "chesed" = loyal love/kindness.
       KJV: "give me a true token... since I have shewed you kindness"
       ESV: "give me a sure sign... since I have dealt kindly with you"
       ASV: "give me a true token... since I have dealt kindly with you"
       NET: "Because I have shown allegiance to you, show allegiance to my family"
       WEB: "give me a true token... since I have dealt kindly with you"
       Analysis: Rahab wants a trustworthy guarantee — something she can depend on absolutely. NET renders Hebrew "chesed" (loyal love/kindness) as "allegiance," which captures the covenant dimension but loses the warmth of "kindness." KJV/ASV/WEB "true token" preserves both Hebrew words (ot + emet). ESV "sure sign" is equivalent.

v.14 — Hebrew oath formula — "Our life for yours" (naphshenu tachteykhem).
       KJV: "Our life for yours, if you utter not this our business"
       ESV: "Our life for yours even to death"
       ASV: "Our life for yours, if ye utter not this our business"
       NET: "If you die, may we die too!"
       WEB: "Our life for yours, if you don't talk about our business"
       Analysis: NET dramatically reinterprets the Hebrew oath formula as a conditional curse. KJV/ASV/WEB preserve the literal pledge — a straightforward exchange of loyalty. ESV adds "even to death" for emphasis. The spies pledge their lives as surety for Rahab's family — a covenant of mutual faithfulness.

v.15 — Hebrew description of Rahab's house location in the wall.
       KJV: "her house was upon the town wall, and she dwelt upon the wall"
       ESV: "her house was built into the city wall, so that she lived in the wall"
       ASV: "her house was upon the side of the wall, and she dwelt upon the wall"
       NET: "her house was built into the city wall"
       WEB: "her house was on the side of the wall, and she lived on the wall"
       Analysis: Archaeological evidence from Jericho confirms houses built into the casemate (double) walls. ESV/NET clarify the meaning for modern readers ("built into"). KJV/ASV/WEB preserve the Hebrew preposition more literally. Rahab's location on the wall explains how she could let the spies down by a cord to the outside.

v.18 — Hebrew "tikvat chut ha-shani" — "tikvah" = cord/line (also means "hope"); "chut" = thread, cord; "shani" = scarlet, crimson.
       KJV: "this line of scarlet thread"
       ESV: "this scarlet cord"
       ASV: "this line of scarlet thread"
       NET: "tie this red rope in the window"
       WEB: "this line of scarlet thread"
       Analysis: NET simplifies to "red rope," losing the layered Hebrew vocabulary. KJV/ASV/WEB preserve all three Hebrew words poetically. ESV condenses to "scarlet cord" — accurate but less rich. The word for "cord" (tikvah) being the same as "hope" is a beautiful wordplay: the scarlet cord IS her hope. As the Passover blood on the doorposts saved Israel in Egypt, the scarlet cord saves Rahab in Jericho.

v.24 — Hebrew perfect tense "natan" — completed action (has given/delivered).
       KJV: "Truly the LORD hath delivered into our hands all the land"
       ESV: "Truly the LORD has given all the land into our hands"
       ASV: "Truly Jehovah hath delivered into our hands all the land"
       NET: "Surely the Lord is handing over all the land to us"
       WEB: "Truly Yahweh has delivered all the land into our hands"
       Analysis: NET uses present continuous tense where Hebrew uses the perfect (completed action). The faith of the spies rests on what God has already done, not what He is in process of doing. KJV/ASV/ESV/WEB preserve the past tense — a report of faith, not fear. These spies see what the ten spies of Numbers 13 could not: God has already given the victory.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Rahab          — A Canaanite prostitute in Jericho who hid the Israelite
               spies and was saved when the city fell. She married Salmon
               (an Israelite) and became the mother of Boaz, great-
               grandmother of David, and ancestor of Christ (Matthew 1:5).
               Hebrew "Rachav" = wide, broad.

Shittim        — Israel's final camp east of the Jordan, in the plains of
               Moab. Also called Abel-shittim ("meadow of acacias"). The
               site of Israel's sin with Moabite women (Numbers 25) and
               the launching point for the conquest.

Scarlet Cord   — Hebrew "tikvat chut ha-shani." The crimson rope Rahab
               tied in her window as the sign of the covenant with the
               spies. It marked her house for salvation when Jericho fell.
               Typologically parallel to the Passover blood (Exodus 12).

Devoted to Destruction — Hebrew "cherem" (v.10, referring to Sihon and
               Og). The total destruction of a city and its inhabitants
               as an act of divine judgment. Everything is given to God —
               nothing is kept as plunder.

Fords          — Shallow crossing points on the Jordan River. The pursuers
               went toward the fords assuming the spies would cross back
               to Israel's camp.

Flax           — A plant whose stalks were dried on rooftops and used to
               make linen. Rahab's flax on the roof provided convenient
               concealment for the spies and indicates she was involved
               in textile production.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. God's grace knows no boundaries (v.1). A Canaanite prostitute in a
   doomed city — yet God saves her. No one is too sinful, too far gone,
   or too "other" for God's grace. Rahab's story demolishes every excuse:
   wrong nationality, wrong profession, wrong city — but right faith.

2. Faith comes by hearing (vv.9-11). Rahab heard about the Red Sea and
   the defeat of the Amorite kings — and she believed. The same facts
   were available to all Jericho, but only Rahab responded in faith.
   Information alone does not save; response to information saves.

3. The scarlet cord and the blood of Christ (v.18). As the Passover blood
   on the doorposts saved Israel from the destroyer, the scarlet cord
   saves Rahab from Jericho's destruction. Both are pictures of Christ's
   blood: visible, applied by faith, protecting all who are "inside."
   Those who leave the house (reject the covering) perish.

4. Imperfect faith is still saving faith (vv.4-5). Rahab lied. Her
   theology was incomplete. Her past was shameful. Yet Hebrews 11:31
   commends her faith. God does not require perfect understanding or
   perfect behavior — He requires trust in Him. Come as you are; He will
   transform you.

5. Faith acts (v.21). Rahab immediately tied the scarlet cord in her
   window — before the army came, before the walls fell. She acted on the
   promise before seeing the fulfillment. "Faith is the substance of
   things hoped for, the evidence of things not seen" (Hebrews 11:1).

6. One person's faith can save a household (vv.12-13,18). Rahab's faith
   saved her father, mother, brothers, sisters, and all who belonged to
   them. Never underestimate the impact of one believer's faith on an
   entire family. Pray for your household — your faith may be their
   bridge to salvation.
