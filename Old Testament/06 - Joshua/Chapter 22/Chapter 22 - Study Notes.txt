Joshua - Chapter 22
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
The Transjordan tribes build an altar by the Jordan, alarming the other tribes who fear apostasy. The near-civil war is averted when the eastern tribes explain it is a memorial of unity, not a rival altar.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- The Transjordan tribes (Reuben, Gad, half-Manasseh) return east across the Jordan to their allotments
- The great altar: Built on the east bank of the Jordan near the crossing point — visible from the western side
- The Jordan River serves as the boundary line that nearly divides the nation; the altar is meant to unify

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "You have kept all that Moses the servant of the LORD commanded
        you, and have obeyed my voice in all that I commanded you." (ESV) —         Joshua's commendation. The eastern tribes were faithful — they
        fought for their brothers for seven years before returning home.
        They kept their word (Numbers 32:20-22).

v.5  — "But take diligent heed to do the commandment and the law...
        to love the LORD your God, and to walk in all his ways, and to
        keep his commandments, and to hold fast to him, and to serve him
        with all your heart and with all your soul." (ESV) — Joshua's parting
        charge. Five imperatives: love, walk, keep, cleave, serve. This
        is the essence of covenant faithfulness.

v.10 — "The children of Reuben and the children of Gad and the half
        tribe of Manasseh built there an altar by Jordan, a great altar
        to see to." (ESV) — The altar that sparked the crisis. "Great... to
        see to" = imposing, conspicuous. It was meant to be noticed —
        a visible memorial. But its visibility caused misunderstanding.

v.16 — "What trespass is this that you have committed against the God of
        Israel, to turn away this day from following the LORD?" (ESV) — The
        western tribes' accusation. They assumed the worst. Their zeal
        for God's honor was right; their assumption without investigation
        was premature.

v.22 — "The LORD God of gods, the LORD God of gods, he knows, and
        Israel he shall know." (ESV) — The eastern tribes' passionate defense.
        They invoke God three times (El, Elohim, YHWH) — the strongest
        possible oath. They appeal to God's omniscience: He knows their
        hearts.

v.27 — "But that it may be a witness between us, and you, and our
        generations after us, that we might do the service of the LORD
        before him." (ESV) — The altar's true purpose: not sacrifice but
        testimony. A witness to future generations that the eastern
        tribes belong to the LORD.

v.31 — "This day we perceive that the LORD is among us, because you have
        not committed this trespass against the LORD." (ESV) — Phinehas'
        relief. The LORD's presence is confirmed by the absence of sin.
        Unity is preserved. God is honored.

v.34 — "And the children of Reuben and the children of Gad called the
        altar Ed: for it shall be a witness between us that the LORD is
        God." (ESV) — "Ed" = Witness. The altar's name declares its purpose:
        not worship but witness. Not sacrifice but solidarity.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/joshua-22/
  Guzik highlights the wisdom of sending a delegation before going to
  war. The western tribes were right to be concerned about apostasy, but
  they were wise to investigate before attacking. He draws the
  application: in the church, we must confront potential sin — but we
  must also listen before condemning. Many conflicts arise from
  misunderstanding, not malice.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "How many wars — in churches, in families, in nations — could be
  prevented by a simple conversation! The western tribes assumed the
  worst. They were ready to fight. But they sent Phinehas first — and
  discovered that what looked like rebellion was actually devotion. Let
  us learn: before you draw the sword, send the messenger. Before you
  condemn, ask. Before you judge, listen. Many an 'altar of rebellion'
  is actually an altar of witness."
TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.5  — KJV: "cleave unto him" / ESV: "cling to him" — Hebrew
       "u-l'davqah bo." "Davaq" = to cling, stick to, adhere. The same
       word used in Genesis 2:24 — a man shall "cleave" to his wife.
       It implies intimate, inseparable attachment. Our relationship to
       God should be as close as marriage.

v.10 — KJV: "a great altar to see to" / ESV: "an altar of imposing
       size" — Hebrew "mizbeach gadol l'mar'eh." Literally "a great
       altar to appearance/sight." KJV's "to see to" is archaic but
       captures the visual impact. ESV's "imposing size" is clearer.
       The altar was deliberately conspicuous — meant to be seen from
       a distance.

v.16 — KJV: "What trespass is this" / ESV: "What is this breach of
       faith" — Hebrew "mah ha-ma'al ha-zeh." "Ma'al" = unfaithfulness,
       treachery, breach of trust. A strong term implying betrayal of
       covenant relationship. ESV's "breach of faith" captures the
       relational dimension better than KJV's "trespass."

v.17 — KJV: "Is the iniquity of Peor too little for us" / ESV: "Have
       we not had enough of the sin at Peor" — Hebrew "ha-m'at lanu et
       avon P'or." The delegation invokes Israel's most recent
       catastrophic sin — the idolatry and immorality at Baal-peor
       (Numbers 25) where 24,000 died. The memory is still fresh.

v.22 — KJV: "The LORD God of gods, the LORD God of gods" / ESV: "The
       Mighty One, God, the LORD! The Mighty One, God, the LORD!" —
       Hebrew "El Elohim YHWH, El Elohim YHWH." Three divine names
       repeated twice — the most solemn oath formula possible. KJV
       renders all three as aspects of "LORD God"; ESV distinguishes
       them: "Mighty One" (El), "God" (Elohim), "the LORD" (YHWH).

v.28 — KJV: "the pattern of the altar of the LORD" / ESV: "the copy of
       the altar of the LORD" — Hebrew "tavnit mizbach YHWH." "Tavnit"
       = pattern, model, copy, likeness. The altar was a replica — not
       a functioning altar but a representation pointing back to the
       true altar at the tabernacle.

v.34 — KJV: "called the altar Ed" / ESV: "called the altar Witness" —
       Hebrew "Ed" = witness, testimony. KJV transliterates the Hebrew
       name; ESV translates it. The altar's name encapsulates its
       purpose: it testifies that the eastern tribes belong to the LORD.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Phinehas       — Son of Eleazar, grandson of Aaron. Known for his zeal
               at Peor where he executed an Israelite man and Midianite
               woman engaged in idolatrous immorality, stopping the
               plague (Numbers 25:7-13). God gave him a "covenant of
               peace" and perpetual priesthood. His selection to lead
               this delegation was strategic — he was zealous for God's
               honor but also a man of discernment.

Ed (Witness)   — The name given to the altar built by the eastern tribes.
               Hebrew "ed" = witness, testimony. The altar testified to
               future generations that the Transjordan tribes were part
               of Israel and worshiped the LORD.

Peor           — Baal-peor, the Moabite deity. At Shittim, Israel was
               seduced into idolatry and sexual immorality with Moabite
               women (Numbers 25). 24,000 died in the resulting plague.
               The delegation cites this as a warning of corporate
               consequences for apostasy.

Achan          — Son of Zerah, of the tribe of Judah. He took devoted
               things from Jericho (Joshua 7), bringing defeat at Ai
               and judgment on all Israel. Cited here (v.20) as proof
               that one person's sin affects the entire community.

Cleave/Cling (davaq) — To adhere, stick to, hold fast. Used of marriage
               (Genesis 2:24) and of devotion to God (Deuteronomy 10:20;
               11:22). Implies intimate, unbreakable attachment.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.5 — ASV: "to love Jehovah your God, and to walk in all his ways, and to keep his commandments, and to cleave unto him" / NET: "Love the Lord your God, follow all his instructions, obey his commands, be loyal to him" / WEB: "to love Yahweh your God, to walk in all his ways, to keep his commandments, to hold fast to him" — NET renders "davak" (cleave/cling) as "be loyal," losing the physical imagery. KJV/ASV "cleave" and WEB "hold fast" preserve the Hebrew picture of clinging to God as in marriage (same verb as Genesis 2:24).
- v.16 — ASV: "Thus saith the whole congregation of Jehovah, What trespass is this?" / NET: "The entire community of the Lord says, 'Why have you disobeyed the God of Israel?'" — ASV/KJV's "trespass" (ma'al — unfaithfulness/breach of trust) is more precise than NET's general "disobeyed." Ma'al implies covenant violation — a betrayal of trust, not mere disobedience.
- v.20 — ASV: "Did not Achan the son of Zerah commit a trespass in the devoted thing" / NET: "When Achan son of Zerah disobeyed the command about the city's riches" — NET again replaces "devoted thing" (cherem) with "city's riches," losing the sacred-ban concept. The parallel to Achan's sin is about violating cherem, not stealing riches.
- v.22 — ASV: "The Mighty One, God, Jehovah, the Mighty One, God, Jehovah, he knoweth" / WEB: "The Mighty One, God, Yahweh, the Mighty One, God, Yahweh! He knows!" / NET: "El, God, the Lord! El, God, the Lord! He knows the truth!" — A powerful triple divine invocation: El (Mighty One), Elohim (God), YHWH (LORD). ASV/WEB preserve all three names in their full weight. NET transliterates "El" but uses "the Lord" for YHWH.
- v.27 — ASV: "a witness between us and you...that we may do the service of Jehovah" / NET: "a reminder to us and you...that we will honor the Lord" — ASV/KJV's "witness" (ed) is the altar's purpose and name. NET's "reminder" is weaker; a witness testifies before God and man. NET also replaces "service" (avodah — worship/service) with "honor."
- v.34 — ASV: "the children of Reuben and the children of Gad called the altar Ed: For, said they, it is a witness between us that Jehovah is God" / NET: "The Reubenites and Gadites named the altar, 'Surely it is a Reminder to us that the Lord is God'" — ASV preserves the altar name "Ed" (witness) with its explanation. NET substitutes "Reminder" and loses the proper name. WEB: "a witness between us that Yahweh is God."

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Communication prevents catastrophe (vv.13-14, 30-33). The western
   tribes were ready for war — but they sent a delegation first. That
   conversation saved Israel from civil war. How many conflicts in
   churches, families, and friendships could be resolved by simply
   asking before assuming? "Be swift to hear, slow to speak, slow to
   wrath" (James 1:19).

2. Zeal must be paired with wisdom (vv.12-14). The western tribes' zeal
   for God's honor was commendable — they took apostasy seriously. But
   zeal without investigation leads to injustice. Phinehas was both
   zealous AND wise — he confronted AND listened. We need both.

3. Concern for future generations (vv.24-27). The eastern tribes built
   the altar thinking about their children and grandchildren. They
   feared their descendants would be excluded from God's people. Do we
   think generationally? What are we building today that will testify
   to our children that we belong to the LORD?

4. Corporate responsibility (vv.17-20). The delegation understood that
   one group's sin affects the whole community. Peor and Achan proved
   this. We are not isolated individuals — our faithfulness or
   unfaithfulness impacts the body. Sin is never merely "personal."

5. The danger of distance (v.25). The Jordan River created a physical
   barrier that the eastern tribes feared would become a spiritual
   barrier. Physical distance from God's people can lead to spiritual
   distance from God. Stay connected to the body of believers.

6. Joshua's parting charge (v.5). Five words summarize covenant life:
   love, walk, keep, cleave, serve. These are not five separate things
   but one life of devotion expressed in five dimensions. Love is the
   root; the others are its fruit.
