Joshua - Chapter 10
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Five Amorite kings attack Gibeon; Joshua defeats them as God hurls hailstones and makes the sun stand still. Joshua conquers the southern cities of Canaan in a sweeping military campaign.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Joshua, with possible later editorial additions. Joshua was Moses'
assistant and successor, who led Israel into the Promised Land. The book
covers approximately 25 years (1406-1381 BC) — from the crossing of the
Jordan to Joshua's death. Hebrew title: 'Yehoshua' — 'YHWH saves' (the
same name as Jesus in Greek). The book demonstrates God's faithfulness to
His land promise to Abraham (Genesis 15:18-21) and shows that victory
comes through faith and obedience, not human strength.

Historical Context: Chapter 10 records the southern campaign — the most
dramatic military chapter in Joshua, featuring the day the sun stood
still. When five Amorite kings attack Gibeon (Israel's new treaty ally),
Joshua marches all night from Gilgal and launches a surprise attack. God
fights for Israel with three supernatural interventions: panic/confusion
on the enemy (v.10), giant hailstones from heaven (v.11), and the sun
standing still to extend the day of battle (vv.12-14). The sun miracle is
one of the most extraordinary events in Scripture — "there was no day like
that before it or after it, that the LORD hearkened unto the voice of a
man" (v.14). After defeating the coalition, Joshua systematically conquers
the major cities of southern Canaan: Makkedah, Libnah, Lachish, Eglon,
Hebron, and Debir. The chapter's refrain: "the LORD God of Israel fought
for Israel" (v.42). The southern campaign establishes Israelite control
over the entire southern region from Kadesh-barnea to Gaza to Gibeon.

The Five Kings Attack Gibeon (vv.1-5): Adoni-zedek king of Jerusalem
fears because Gibeon — "a great city, as one of the royal cities" (v.2)
— has made peace with Israel. He forms a coalition with four other kings:
Hebron, Jarmuth, Lachish, and Eglon (v.3). They attack Gibeon for
defecting to Israel (vv.4-5).

Joshua's Night March and God's Promise (vv.6-9): Gibeon appeals to Joshua:
"Slack not thy hand from thy servants; come up to us quickly" (v.6).
Joshua responds immediately — marching all night from Gilgal (an uphill
climb of about 20 miles and 3,300 feet elevation, v.9). God assures him:
"Fear them not: for I have delivered them into thine hand" (v.8).

The Battle and the Sun Standing Still (vv.10-14): God throws the enemy
into panic (v.10). Israel pursues them along the Beth-horon pass. God
hurls "great stones from heaven" (hailstones) — killing more than Israel's
swords (v.11). Then Joshua's extraordinary prayer: "Sun, stand thou still
upon Gibeon; and thou, Moon, in the valley of Ajalon" (v.12). "And the
sun stood still, and the moon stayed, until the people had avenged
themselves upon their enemies" (v.13). The sun "hasted not to go down
about a whole day" (v.13). The narrator's comment: "there was no day like
that before it or after it, that the LORD hearkened unto the voice of a
man: for the LORD fought for Israel" (v.14).

The Five Kings Captured (vv.16-27): The five kings hide in a cave at
Makkedah (v.16). Joshua seals the cave and continues pursuing the army
(vv.18-19). After the battle, he brings out the kings and commands his
captains: "put your feet upon the necks of these kings" (v.24). Joshua
encourages them: "Fear not, nor be dismayed, be strong and of good
courage: for thus shall the LORD do to all your enemies" (v.25). The kings
are executed and hanged on trees until evening, then buried in the cave
(vv.26-27).

The Southern Cities Conquered (vv.28-43): Joshua systematically takes
Makkedah (v.28), Libnah (vv.29-30), Lachish (vv.31-32), defeats Gezer's
king who came to help (v.33), takes Eglon (vv.34-35), Hebron (vv.36-37),
and Debir (vv.38-39). The refrain repeats: "he left none remaining."
Summary: "Joshua smote all the country of the hills, and of the south,
and of the vale, and of the springs, and all their kings... as the LORD
God of Israel commanded" (v.40). The territory: from Kadesh-barnea to
Gaza to Gibeon (v.41). "All these kings and their land did Joshua take at
one time, because the LORD God of Israel fought for Israel" (v.42).

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Southern campaign route: Gibeon → Beth-horon pass (descent to the west) → Makkedah → Libnah → Lachish → Eglon → Hebron → Debir
- Gibeon: Where the battle begins with the Amorite coalition; the sun stands still over Gibeon, moon over the Valley of Aijalon
- Beth-horon: The steep pass descending from the central highlands to the coastal plain — hailstones fall on the fleeing enemy here

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.8  — "Fear them not: for I have delivered them into your hand; there
        shall not a man of them stand before you." (ESV) — God's promise before
        the battle. Past tense again: "I have delivered." The victory is
        already determined. Joshua fights from assurance, not anxiety.

v.11 — "They were more which died with hailstones than they whom the
        children of Israel slew with the sword." (ESV) — God kills more of the
        enemy than Israel does. The battle belongs to the LORD. Human
        effort participates, but divine power dominates.

v.12 — "Sun, stand you still upon Gibeon; and you, Moon, in the valley
        of Ajalon." (ESV) — Joshua commands the celestial bodies. The audacity
        of faith — asking God to alter the cosmos to complete the victory.
        And God does it.

v.13 — "And the sun stood still, and the moon stayed, until the people
        had avenged themselves upon their enemies... So the sun stood
        still in the midst of heaven, and hasted not to go down about a
        whole day." (ESV) — The longest day in history. God suspends the normal
        order of creation to fight for His people.

v.14 — "And there was no day like that before it or after it, that the
        LORD listened to the voice of a man: for the LORD fought for
        Israel." (ESV) — Unique in all history. God responded to a man's
        command to the sun. The Creator obeyed the prayer of His creature.
        "The LORD fought for Israel" — the chapter's theme.

v.25 — "Fear not, nor be dismayed, be strong and courageous: for
        thus shall the LORD do to all your enemies against whom you fight." (ESV)
        — Joshua's encouragement to his captains, echoing God's words to
        him (1:6-9). The defeated kings are an object lesson: this is
        what God does to His enemies.

v.42 — "All these kings and their land did Joshua take at one time,
        because the LORD God of Israel fought for Israel." (ESV) — The summary
        statement. The entire southern campaign succeeded because God
        fought. Human strategy and effort were involved, but the decisive
        factor was divine intervention.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/joshua-10/
  Guzik notes that the sun miracle has been explained various ways
  (refraction, poetic language, local phenomenon), but the text presents
  it as a literal, unprecedented cosmic event. He emphasizes that Joshua's
  bold prayer came from confidence in God's promise (v.8). The feet on
  the kings' necks is a prophetic picture of Christ's ultimate victory
  over all enemies (Psalm 110:1).

Charles Spurgeon:
  "Joshua commanded the sun — and it obeyed! What manner of faith is
  this? It is the faith that takes God at His word and asks for whatever
  is needed to complete the victory God has promised. If God says 'I have
  delivered them,' then Joshua can say 'Sun, stand still' — because the
  God who promised the victory will provide whatever is needed to
  accomplish it. Ask largely! Your God controls the sun."
TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1  — Hebrew "cherem" (herem/devoted to destruction) — the sacred-judgment concept.
       KJV: "had utterly destroyed it"
       ESV: "had devoted it to destruction"
       ASV: "had utterly destroyed it"
       NET: "annihilated it"
       WEB: "had utterly destroyed it"
       Analysis: NET consistently uses "annihilated" where KJV/ASV/WEB use "utterly destroyed," which better conveys the sacred-judgment dimension of total destruction. ESV "devoted to destruction" is the most theologically transparent rendering of cherem.

v.8  — Hebrew perfect tense "natatti" — "I have delivered/given" — completed action.
       KJV: "I have delivered them into thine hands"
       ESV: "I have given them into your hands"
       ASV: "I have delivered them into thy hands"
       NET: "I am handing them over to you"
       WEB: "I have delivered them into your hands"
       Analysis: Once again, NET uses present progressive where Hebrew uses the perfect (past) tense. KJV/ASV/ESV/WEB preserve God's declaration of accomplished fact — the victory is already determined before the battle begins. This is the consistent pattern of divine promise in Joshua.

v.10 — Hebrew "va-y'hummem Adonai" — "hamam" = to confuse, throw into confusion, rout, panic.
       KJV: "the LORD discomfited them"
       ESV: "the LORD threw them into a panic"
       ASV: "Jehovah discomfited them before Israel"
       NET: "The Lord routed them before Israel"
       WEB: "Yahweh confused them before Israel"
       Analysis: This is supernatural confusion — God Himself disrupts the enemy's ability to fight coherently. ASV's archaic "discomfited" and WEB's "confused" retain the divine terror dimension. NET's "routed" implies military defeat without the supernatural panic element. ESV "threw into a panic" is vivid and accurate. The same word is used of God's action at the Red Sea (Exodus 14:24).

v.11 — Hebrew "avanim g'dolot min ha-shamayim" / "avaney ha-barad" — great stones/hailstones from heaven.
       KJV: "great stones from heaven upon them... hailstones"
       ESV: "large stones from heaven on them... hailstones"
       ASV: "great stones from heaven upon them... hailstones"
       NET: "large hailstones from the sky"
       WEB: "great stones from the sky on them... hailstones"
       Analysis: All translations agree on the substance. Supernatural hail — targeted at the enemy, not at Israel. They killed more of the enemy than Israel's swords — emphasizing that the battle belonged to God. Selective divine judgment from the sky.

v.12 — Hebrew "shemesh b'Giv'on dom" — "dom" = be silent, be still, cease; Hebrew "amar" = to say/speak.
       KJV: "Then spake Joshua to the LORD... Sun, stand thou still upon Gibeon"
       ESV: "Joshua spoke to the LORD... Sun, stand still at Gibeon"
       ASV: "Then spake Joshua to Jehovah... Sun, stand thou still upon Gibeon"
       NET: "Joshua prayed to the Lord... O sun, stand still over Gibeon"
       WEB: "Then Joshua spoke to Yahweh... Sun, stand still on Gibeon"
       Analysis: NET interprets "spoke to" as "prayed to." The Hebrew "amar" is broader — Joshua speaks a command to the sun and moon in God's presence. It is poetic, bold, prophetic speech, not merely prayer. "Dom" means "be silent" — to stop its activity. The context (needing more daylight) supports the traditional interpretation of extended daylight.

v.13 — Hebrew "sepher ha-Yashar" — the Book of Jashar/the Upright. A now-lost Hebrew poetic anthology.
       KJV: "the book of Jasher"
       ESV: "the Book of Jashar"
       ASV: "the book of Jashar"
       NET: "the Scroll of the Upright One"
       WEB: "the book of Jashar"
       Analysis: NET renders "sepher ha-yashar" as "Scroll of the Upright One," interpreting "Jashar" as a description rather than preserving the proper name. KJV/ASV/ESV/WEB retain the Hebrew name. Its citation confirms the event was recorded in multiple sources (also referenced in 2 Samuel 1:18). "Yom tamim" = a complete/whole day — the sun delayed its setting by approximately one full day.

v.24 — Ancient Near Eastern symbol of total victory — foot on the neck of defeated kings.
       KJV: "put your feet upon the necks of these kings"
       ESV: "Put your feet on the necks of these kings"
       ASV: "put your feet upon the necks of these kings"
       NET: "put your feet on the necks of these kings"
       WEB: "put your feet on the necks of these kings"
       Analysis: All translations agree. The conqueror's foot on the enemy's neck signifies complete dominion. This imagery appears in Psalm 110:1 and is fulfilled in Christ's victory over all enemies (Romans 16:20).

v.25 — Hebrew commissioning formula echoing God's words to Joshua in chapter 1.
       KJV: "Fear not, nor be dismayed; be strong and of good courage"
       ESV: "Do not fear or be dismayed; be strong and courageous"
       ASV: "Fear not, nor be dismayed; be strong and of good courage"
       NET: "Don't be afraid and don't panic! Be strong and brave"
       WEB: "Don't be afraid, nor be dismayed. Be strong and courageous"
       Analysis: Joshua echoes God's threefold command to him (1:6,7,9). NET's "don't panic" and "brave" are colloquial; KJV/ASV/ESV/WEB preserve the formal commissioning language that ties this moment back to chapter 1's divine charge.

v.40 — Hebrew "hecherim kol n'shamah" — "cherem" + "n'shamah" = devoted to destruction all that breathed.
       KJV: "utterly destroyed all that breathed"
       ESV: "devoted to destruction all that breathed"
       ASV: "utterly destroyed all that breathed, as Jehovah, the God of Israel, commanded"
       NET: "He annihilated everything that breathed, just as the Lord God of Israel had commanded"
       WEB: "utterly destroyed all that breathed, as Yahweh, the God of Israel, commanded"
       Analysis: The critical theological note: the destruction was commanded by God. This fulfills Deuteronomy 20:16. ASV/WEB's "utterly destroyed" (cherem language) and the divine name (Jehovah/Yahweh) make the theological framework most explicit. ESV "devoted to destruction" best captures the full meaning of cherem.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Adoni-zedek    — "Lord of righteousness." King of Jerusalem who formed
               the southern coalition against Gibeon. His name ironically
               echoes "Melchizedek" (king of righteousness) — Jerusalem's
               earlier righteous king (Genesis 14:18). This Adoni-zedek
               opposes God's people.

Beth-horon     — A strategic pass (upper and lower) connecting the
               coastal plain to the central highlands. The fleeing army
               was trapped in this narrow descent when God hurled
               hailstones upon them.

Book of Jashar/Jasher — A lost Hebrew poetic anthology celebrating
               Israel's heroes and victories. Referenced here and in
               2 Samuel 1:18. Its existence confirms that multiple written
               sources documented Israel's history.

Valley of Aijalon — A valley west of Gibeon where the moon was commanded
               to stand still. The sun over Gibeon (east) and moon over
               Aijalon (west) suggests early morning positioning.

Hailstones     — Supernatural stones thrown by God from heaven. They
               killed more of the enemy than Israel's swords — emphasizing
               that the battle belonged to God. Targeted judgment: they
               struck the enemy but not Israel.

Feet on Necks  — An ancient Near Eastern symbol of total conquest and
               subjugation. The victor places his foot on the defeated
               enemy's neck, signifying complete dominion. A prophetic
               picture of Christ's ultimate victory (Psalm 110:1;
               Romans 16:20).

Southern Campaign — Joshua's systematic conquest of southern Canaan after
               the defeat of the five-king coalition. Cities taken:
               Makkedah, Libnah, Lachish, Eglon, Hebron, Debir. Territory
               secured from Kadesh-barnea to Gaza to Gibeon.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. God fights for His people (vv.11,14,42). Three times the chapter
   declares that God fought for Israel. Hailstones, cosmic intervention,
   supernatural panic — God's arsenal is unlimited. When God fights for
   you, the outcome is not in doubt. "If God be for us, who can be
   against us?" (Romans 8:31).

2. The audacity of faith (v.12). Joshua commands the sun to stand still.
   This is not presumption — it flows from God's promise (v.8). When God
   has declared the victory, faith can ask for whatever is needed to
   complete it. Ask largely. Your God controls the cosmos.

3. God responds to a man's voice (v.14). "The LORD hearkened unto the
   voice of a man." The Creator of the universe responds to human prayer.
   This is staggering. Your prayers move the God who moves the sun.
   Never underestimate the power of believing prayer.

4. Honor your commitments (vv.6-7). Joshua marches all night to defend
   Gibeon — a city he was tricked into protecting. He honors the treaty
   even though it was obtained by deception. Integrity means keeping your
   word even when it costs you. And God honors those who honor their
   commitments.

5. Feet on the necks of kings (vv.24-25). Joshua makes his captains
   participate in the victory — placing their feet on the defeated kings.
   This is both encouragement and prophecy: "thus shall the LORD do to
   all your enemies." Every enemy you face will end up under your feet
   (Romans 16:20). The victory is certain.

6. Systematic follow-through (vv.28-42). After the dramatic miracle,
   Joshua does the unglamorous work of systematically conquering city
   after city. Miracles open doors; discipline walks through them.
   Spiritual breakthroughs must be followed by faithful, persistent
   effort.
