Joshua - Chapter 7
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Achan's secret sin causes Israel's defeat at Ai, and thirty-six men die. God reveals the hidden theft; Achan and his household are stoned and burned in the Valley of Achor.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Joshua, with possible later editorial additions. Joshua was Moses'
assistant and successor, who led Israel into the Promised Land. The book
covers approximately 25 years (1406-1381 BC) — from the crossing of the
Jordan to Joshua's death. Hebrew title: 'Yehoshua' — 'YHWH saves' (the
same name as Jesus in Greek). The book demonstrates God's faithfulness to
His land promise to Abraham (Genesis 15:18-21) and shows that victory
comes through faith and obedience, not human strength.

Historical Context: Chapter 7 records Israel's first defeat — and its
cause: Achan's sin. After the miraculous victory at Jericho, Israel
suffers a shocking rout at the small city of Ai. The contrast is
deliberate: chapter 6 shows what happens when Israel obeys completely;
chapter 7 shows what happens when even ONE person disobeys. Achan took
"devoted things" (cherem) from Jericho — a Babylonian garment, silver,
and gold — violating the explicit command of 6:18. His private sin brought
public defeat: 36 men died, the army fled, and Israel's hearts "melted"
(the same word used of the Canaanites in 2:11 and 5:1). The chapter
teaches the corporate nature of sin in covenant community — one man's
transgression affects the entire nation. God declares "Israel hath sinned"
(v.11) though only Achan acted. The sin is discovered through a process
of elimination by lot (tribe → clan → household → individual), and Achan
is executed with his family and possessions in the Valley of Achor
("trouble"). Only after the sin is purged does God's anger turn away.

Israel's Defeat at Ai (vv.1-5): The chapter opens with the narrator's
explanation: Achan "took of the accursed thing" and "the anger of the
LORD was kindled against the children of Israel" (v.1). Unaware of the
sin, Joshua sends spies to Ai (v.2). They report it is small — only two
or three thousand men needed (v.3). But when 3,000 attack, they are
routed: 36 killed, the rest chased to Shebarim (v.5). "The hearts of the
people melted, and became as water" (v.5) — the same terror that gripped
the Canaanites now grips Israel.

Joshua's Lament (vv.6-9): Joshua tears his clothes, falls before the ark,
and puts dust on his head — signs of extreme grief (v.6). His prayer
echoes Israel's wilderness complaints: "wherefore hast thou at all brought
this people over Jordan, to deliver us into the hand of the Amorites?"
(v.7). He fears the Canaanites will hear of the defeat and destroy Israel
(v.9). His final appeal: "what wilt thou do unto thy great name?" (v.9)
— God's reputation is at stake.

God's Response (vv.10-15): God's answer is blunt: "Get thee up; wherefore
liest thou thus upon thy face?" (v.10). This is not a time for prayer but
for action. "Israel hath sinned, and they have also transgressed my
covenant" (v.11). The charges: they have taken devoted things, stolen,
lied ("dissembled"), and hidden them (v.11). The consequence: "neither
will I be with you any more, except ye destroy the accursed from among
you" (v.12). God's presence — the guarantee of victory — is withdrawn
because of sin. The remedy: consecrate the people, identify the sinner
by lot, and execute judgment (vv.13-15).

Achan Discovered (vv.16-23): The lot narrows: tribe of Judah → clan of
Zerah → household of Zabdi → Achan son of Carmi (vv.16-18). Joshua
addresses him tenderly: "My son, give glory to the LORD God of Israel,
and make confession" (v.19). Achan confesses: "I saw... I coveted... I
took" (v.21) — the same progression as Eve in Eden (Genesis 3:6). The
items: a Babylonian garment, 200 shekels of silver, a gold bar of 50
shekels — hidden in the ground under his tent (v.21). Messengers confirm
the find (vv.22-23).

Judgment in the Valley of Achor (vv.24-26): Achan, his family, his
possessions, and the stolen goods are brought to the Valley of Achor
(v.24). Joshua pronounces: "Why hast thou troubled us? the LORD shall
trouble thee this day" (v.25) — a wordplay on "Achor" (trouble). All
Israel stones him, then burns everything (v.25). A great heap of stones
marks the spot (v.26). "So the LORD turned from the fierceness of his
anger" (v.26). Justice satisfied, God's wrath is appeased.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Ai: A city in the central hill country, near Bethel — about 15 miles west and uphill from Jericho
- The Valley of Achor: Where Achan is judged; located in the wilderness between Jericho and Ai
- The ascent from Jericho to Ai represented Israel's push from the Jordan Valley into the highlands

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "The children of Israel committed a trespass in the accursed
        thing... and the anger of the LORD was kindled against the
        children of Israel." (ESV) — One man sinned; all Israel is charged.
        In covenant community, individual sin has corporate consequences.

v.5  — "The hearts of the people melted, and became as water." (ESV) — The
        same language used of the Canaanites (2:11; 5:1) now describes
        Israel. Sin reverses the spiritual dynamic — the conquerors
        become the conquered.

v.11 — "Israel has sinned, and they have also transgressed my covenant
        which I commanded them: for they have even taken of the accursed
        thing, and have also stolen, and dissembled also, and they have
        put it even among their own stuff." (ESV) — Five charges in one verse:
        sinned, transgressed, taken, stolen, lied. One act of
        disobedience is described as multiple violations.

v.12 — "Neither will I be with you any more, except you destroy the
        accursed from among you." (ESV) — God's presence is conditional on
        holiness. Sin separates. Without God's presence, Israel cannot
        stand. The solution is not more effort but more purity.

v.13 — "There is an accursed thing in the midst of you, O Israel: you
        can not stand before your enemies, until you take away the
        accursed thing from among you." (ESV) — Hidden sin blocks God's power.
        You cannot advance while harboring what God has forbidden.

v.21 — "When I saw among the spoils a goodly Babylonish garment, and two
        hundred shekels of silver, and a wedge of gold of fifty shekels
        weight, then I coveted them, and took them." (ESV) — The anatomy of
        sin: saw → coveted → took. The same pattern as Genesis 3:6 and
        James 1:14-15. Sin begins with the eyes, moves to the heart,
        then to the hands.

v.25 — "Why hast you troubled us? the LORD shall trouble you this day." (ESV)
        — Wordplay on "Achor" (trouble). The one who troubled Israel is
        now troubled by God. Sin's consequences return upon the sinner.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/joshua-7/
  Guzik notes the overconfidence of the spies' report — "they are but
  few" — showing that Israel was already operating in presumption rather
  than dependence on God. He emphasizes that Achan's sin was not
  impulsive but deliberate: he saw, coveted, took, and carefully hid the
  items. The corporate nature of sin in covenant community is a key
  theme — one person's hidden sin can bring defeat to an entire church.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "Achan thought his sin was secret — buried under his tent where no eye
  could see. But God saw. And the whole camp suffered for what one man
  hid. There is no such thing as private sin in the body of Christ. Your
  hidden disobedience weakens the whole army. Your secret compromise
  costs others their victory. Dig it up. Confess it. Destroy it. Before
  it destroys you and those around you."
TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1  — Hebrew "ma'alu ma'al ba-cherem" — "ma'al" = to act unfaithfully, treacherously; doubling intensifies: "acted with great unfaithfulness."
       KJV: "committed a trespass in the accursed thing"
       ESV: "broke faith in regard to the devoted things"
       ASV: "committed a trespass in the devoted thing"
       NET: "disobeyed the command about the city's riches"
       WEB: "committed a trespass in the devoted things"
       Analysis: NET replaces the "cherem" (devoted thing) concept with "the city's riches," losing the theological weight entirely. The sin was not about stealing "riches" but violating what was under the ban — devoted to God's exclusive claim. ESV's "broke faith" best captures the covenant-breaking nature. ASV/WEB preserve "devoted thing." KJV's "accursed" conveys danger but not the consecration aspect. The anger of the LORD was kindled: ASV/WEB preserve the Hebrew fire metaphor (charah — to burn hot), while NET's "furious" is accurate but loses the imagery.

v.5  — Hebrew "ba-morad" — "morad" = descent, slope.
       KJV: "smote them in the going down"
       ESV: "struck them at the descent"
       ASV: "smote them at the going down"
       NET: "chased them down the slope"
       WEB: "struck them at the descent"
       Analysis: NET's "chased them down the slope" is vivid and dynamic. KJV/ASV "the going down" is literal but archaic. ESV/WEB "the descent" is concise. The men of Ai chased Israel downhill — a rout, not a retreat. The psychological impact was devastating.

v.7  — Hebrew "Adonai YHWH" — Lord + divine name; Joshua's lament.
       KJV: "Alas, O Lord GOD"
       ESV: "Alas, O Lord GOD"
       ASV: "Alas, O Lord Jehovah"
       NET: "O, Sovereign Lord!"
       WEB: "Alas, Lord Yahweh"
       Analysis: ASV/WEB preserve the Hebrew "Adonai YHWH" with both title and divine name. NET renders this as "Sovereign Lord," interpreting "Adonai" as sovereign. KJV/ESV use "Lord GOD" (caps for YHWH). Joshua's appeal is to God's reputation — if Israel is destroyed, the nations will conclude God is weak.

v.9  — Hebrew "u-mah ta'aseh l'shimkha ha-gadol?" — appeal to God's reputation and great name.
       KJV: "what wilt thou do unto thy great name?"
       ESV: "what will you do for your great name?"
       ASV: "what wilt thou do for thy great name?"
       NET: "What will you do to protect your great reputation?"
       WEB: "what will you do for your great name?"
       Analysis: NET adds "to protect," making the implicit explicit. KJV/ASV/ESV/WEB preserve the open-ended Hebrew question. Joshua appeals to God's glory — the same argument Moses used (Numbers 14:13-16; Exodus 32:12). God's reputation among the nations is at stake.

v.11 — Hebrew "v'gam kichashu" — "kachash" = to deny, deceive, lie, act deceptively.
       KJV: "dissembled also"
       ESV: "lied"
       ASV: "have also dissembled"
       NET: "have stolen and lied"
       WEB: "have also denied"
       Analysis: KJV/ASV's "dissembled" (pretended, concealed the truth) is archaic. ESV's "lied" is direct and accessible. NET adds "stolen and" for clarity. WEB's "denied" captures the Hebrew "kachash." Achan's sin included deception — hiding the truth from the community while Israel suffered defeat.

v.12 — Hebrew "cherem" — Israel itself has become subject to the ban.
       KJV: "they were accursed"
       ESV: "they have become devoted for destruction"
       ASV: "they are become accursed"
       NET: "they have become subject to annihilation"
       WEB: "they have become devoted for destruction"
       Analysis: ESV/WEB give the fullest rendering — "devoted for destruction" captures both the consecration and judgment aspects. NET's "subject to annihilation" is clinical. KJV/ASV "accursed" is traditional but ambiguous. The terrifying point: by taking what was cherem, Israel itself becomes cherem.

v.15 — Hebrew "asah n'valah b'Yisra'el" — "nevalah" = folly, disgraceful act, moral outrage.
       KJV: "he hath wrought folly in Israel"
       ESV: "he has done an outrageous thing in Israel"
       ASV: "he hath wrought folly in Israel"
       NET: "he has violated the Lord's covenant"
       WEB: "he has done a disgraceful thing in Israel"
       Analysis: "Nevalah" is a technical term for a serious covenant violation that shames the community (cf. Genesis 34:7; Judges 19:23). ESV "outrageous thing" and WEB "disgraceful thing" capture the moral weight. KJV/ASV "folly" understates it — this is not mere foolishness but moral outrage. NET interprets rather than translates.

v.21 — Hebrew "adderet Shin'ar achat tovah" — "adderet" = mantle, cloak; "Shin'ar" = Babylonia.
       KJV: "a goodly Babylonish garment"
       ESV: "a beautiful cloak from Shinar"
       ASV: "a goodly Babylonish mantle"
       NET: "a beautiful Babylonian robe"
       WEB: "a beautiful Babylonian robe"
       Analysis: All translations convey the essential meaning — an expensive imported luxury garment from Babylon. KJV's "goodly" is archaic for "beautiful." ESV preserves "Shinar" (the Hebrew name); others use "Babylonian/Babylonish." Achan coveted luxury from the very culture God was judging.

v.25 — Hebrew wordplay: "troubled" (achar) → "Achor" (valley of trouble).
       KJV: "Why hast thou troubled us? the LORD shall trouble thee this day"
       ESV: "Why did you bring trouble on us? The LORD brings trouble on you today"
       ASV: "Why hast thou troubled us? Jehovah shall trouble thee this day"
       NET: "The Lord will bring disaster on you today!"
       WEB: "Why have you troubled us? Yahweh will trouble you today"
       Analysis: The wordplay "troubled" (achar) → "Achor" (valley of trouble) is preserved in KJV/ASV/ESV/WEB but obscured in NET, which uses "disaster" rather than "trouble." The valley name (Achor) comes directly from the verb Joshua uses. The severity of the punishment reflects the severity of covenant violation.

v.26 — Hebrew "Emeq Akhor" — Valley of Trouble; Hebrew "charon af" — fierce anger.
       KJV: "The valley of Achor, unto this day. So the LORD turned from the fierceness of his anger"
       ESV: "the Valley of Achor to this day. And the LORD turned from his burning anger"
       ASV: "The valley of Achor, unto this day. So Jehovah turned from the fierceness of his anger"
       NET: "the Lord's anger subsided"
       WEB: "the Valley of Achor to this day. So Yahweh turned from the fierceness of his anger"
       Analysis: NET's "subsided" is bland where the Hebrew pictures God actively turning away from fierce wrath (charon af). KJV/ASV/ESV/WEB preserve the dramatic anthropomorphic language. Yet Hosea 2:15 promises God will make "the valley of Achor a door of hope" — God redeems even places of judgment.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Achan          — Son of Carmi, of the tribe of Judah. He took devoted
               things from Jericho (a Babylonian garment, silver, and
               gold), bringing defeat on Israel at Ai. Discovered by lot,
               he confessed and was executed with his family in the Valley
               of Achor. Called "Achar" (troubler) in 1 Chronicles 2:7.

Ai             — A small city east of Bethel, near Beth-aven. Israel's
               first defeat occurred here due to Achan's sin. Later
               conquered by ambush (chapter 8). The name "Ai" means
               "ruin" or "heap."

Cherem (Accursed/Devoted Thing) — Items placed under the ban of
               destruction at Jericho. Taking them was stealing from God
               and brought the curse of destruction upon the thief and
               his community.

Valley of Achor — Hebrew "Emeq Akhor" = Valley of Trouble. The place
               where Achan was executed. Located near Gilgal/Jericho.
               Later referenced in Hosea 2:15 as a future "door of hope"
               — God redeems even places of judgment.

Dissembled/Lied — Hebrew "kachash." To act deceptively, deny the truth.
               Achan's sin was not only theft but deception — he hid the
               stolen goods and said nothing while Israel suffered defeat.

Lot            — A method of divine determination used to identify God's
               will or discover hidden truth. The process narrowed from
               tribe to clan to household to individual, with God
               directing the outcome at each stage.

Corporate Responsibility — The biblical principle that in covenant
               community, one member's sin affects all. God charged
               "Israel" with sin though only Achan acted. The community
               shares in both blessing and judgment.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. One person's sin affects the whole community (v.1). Achan sinned alone,
   but 36 men died and all Israel was defeated. In the body of Christ,
   there is no truly "private" sin. Hidden disobedience weakens the entire
   church. Your secret compromise may be costing others their victory.

2. The anatomy of sin: saw → coveted → took (v.21). The same pattern from
   Eden to today. Sin enters through the eyes, takes root in desire, and
   produces action. Guard your eyes. Kill covetousness at the root before
   it produces the fruit of disobedience.

3. Overconfidence after victory (vv.2-3). Fresh from Jericho's miraculous
   fall, the spies say "they are but few" — no need for the whole army.
   They did not consult God. Victory breeds presumption. The moment after
   your greatest triumph is your moment of greatest vulnerability.

4. God's presence is conditional on holiness (v.12). "Neither will I be
   with you any more, except ye destroy the accursed from among you."
   God does not tolerate sin in His camp. His presence — the source of
   all victory — withdraws when we harbor what He has forbidden. Deal
   with sin or lose God's power.

5. Hidden sin will be found (vv.16-18). Achan buried the evidence under
   his tent. God found it anyway — narrowing from 2 million people to one
   man through the lot. "Be sure your sin will find you out" (Numbers
   32:23). It is better to confess voluntarily than to be exposed
   publicly.

6. The severity of God (vv.24-26). Achan and his family are stoned and
   burned. This seems harsh — but it demonstrates the seriousness of
   covenant violation. God is not casual about sin. The severity of the
   judgment matches the severity of the offense: Achan's sin cost 36
   lives and nearly cost the entire conquest.
