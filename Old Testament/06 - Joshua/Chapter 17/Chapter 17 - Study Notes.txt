Joshua - Chapter 17
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
The rest of Manasseh receives its territory, but complains their allotment is too small. Joshua challenges them to clear the forested hill country rather than shrink from the Canaanites with iron chariots.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- West Manasseh: Central highlands north of Ephraim, extending to the Jezreel Valley
- Key unconquered cities: Beth-shean, Taanach, Dor, Ibleam, Megiddo — all in the fertile Jezreel/Esdraelon plain
- Zelophehad's daughters receive land in west Manasseh — the hill country is forested and must be cleared

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.4  — "The LORD commanded Moses to give us an inheritance among our
        brethren. Therefore according to the commandment of the LORD he
        gave them an inheritance among the brethren of their father." (ESV) —         The daughters of Zelophehad claim God's promise boldly. They
        know the Word and stand on it. Their faith is rewarded.

v.12 — "Yet the children of Manasseh could not drive out the inhabitants
        of those cities; but the Canaanites would dwell in that land." (ESV)
        — Another failure of incomplete conquest. Beth-shean, Megiddo,
        Dor, Taanach — major cities that remained Canaanite. "Could not"
        again likely means "would not" — lack of faith, not lack of
        divine power.

v.13 — "Yet it came to pass, when the children of Israel were waxen
        strong, that they put the Canaanites to tribute; but did not
        utterly drive them out." (ESV) — When Israel grew strong enough to
        conquer, they chose profit over obedience. Strength without
        faithfulness leads to compromise.

v.14 — "Why hast you given me but one lot and one portion to inherit,
        seeing I am a great people, forasmuch as the LORD has blessed
        me hitherto?" (ESV) — The Joseph tribes complain. Note the
        contradiction: they claim to be "great" but are unwilling to
        do the work of greatness. They want more territory handed to
        them without effort.

v.15 — "If you be a great people, then get you up to the wood country,
        and cut down for thyself there in the land of the Perizzites and
        of the giants, if mount Ephraim be too narrow for you." (ESV) —         Joshua's brilliant response: You say you're great? Prove it.
        Clear the forest. Fight the giants. Your greatness is your
        solution — use it.

v.18 — "But the mountain shall be your; for it is a wood, and you
        shall cut it down: and the outgoings of it shall be your: for
        you shall drive out the Canaanites, though they have iron
        chariots, and though they be strong." (ESV) — Joshua's final word:
        the iron chariots are not an excuse. God's people can overcome
        any obstacle if they will act in faith. Stop fearing and start
        clearing.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/joshua-17/
  Guzik contrasts the Joseph tribes with Caleb: Caleb asked for the
  mountain with the giants; Joseph's tribes complained about the
  difficulty of their territory. Joshua's response is essentially:
  "Don't come to me with complaints — go conquer." Guzik notes that
  Joshua refused to enable their laziness. He affirmed their strength
  but directed it toward action, not entitlement.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "The children of Joseph said, 'The hill is not enough for us.' And
  Joshua said, 'Then clear the forest and take the valley.' They wanted
  more room but less work. They wanted a larger inheritance without a
  larger faith. But God does not give territory to those who will not
  fight for it. If you want more of God's blessing, you must be willing
  to do more of God's work."
TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1  — Hebrew "b'khor Yosef" — firstborn of Joseph.
       KJV: "for he was the firstborn of Joseph"
       ESV: "for he was the firstborn of Joseph"
       ASV: "for he was the firstborn of Joseph"
       NET: "because he was Joseph's firstborn"
       WEB: "for he was the firstborn of Joseph"
       Analysis: Both identical across versions. Though Manasseh was
       firstborn, Jacob gave Ephraim the greater blessing (Genesis
       48:19-20). Yet Manasseh still receives recognition as firstborn
       here — the birthright was not entirely revoked.

v.2  — Hebrew "b'nei Menasheh ha-z'kharim" — the sons/descendants of
       Manasseh, the males.
       KJV: "the male children of Manasseh"
       ESV: "the male descendants of Manasseh"
       ASV: "the rest of the children of Manasseh"
       NET: "the rest of Manasseh's descendants"
       WEB: "the rest of the children of Manasseh"
       Analysis: The specification "male" sets up the contrast with
       Zelophehad's daughters in v.3. ESV/KJV make this explicit;
       ASV/NET/WEB focus on "the rest" after Machir's line.

v.3  — Hebrew "lo hayu lo banim ki im banot" — he had no sons, only
       daughters.
       KJV: "Zelophehad... had no sons, but daughters"
       ESV: "Zelophehad... had no sons, but only daughters"
       ASV: "Zelophehad... had no sons, but daughters"
       NET: "Zelophehad... had no sons, only daughters"
       WEB: "Zelophehad... had no sons, but daughters"
       Analysis: The case of Zelophehad established that daughters could
       inherit when there were no sons — a remarkable provision in the
       ancient Near East. All versions convey this clearly.

v.4  — Hebrew "nachalah" — inheritance (covenant rights).
       KJV: "The LORD commanded Moses to give us an inheritance among our brethren"
       ESV: "The LORD commanded Moses to give us an inheritance among our brothers"
       ASV: "Jehovah commanded Moses to give us an inheritance among our brethren"
       NET: "The Lord told Moses to assign us land among our relatives"
       WEB: "Yahweh commanded Moses to give us an inheritance among our brothers"
       Analysis: Zelophehad's daughters appeal to God's command (Numbers
       27:7). NET's "assign land" weakens what ASV/KJV/WEB call
       "inheritance" (nachalah). The women claim covenant rights, not
       administrative allocation. WEB/ASV foreground the divine name.

v.11 — Hebrew "sh'loshet ha-napheth" — a difficult/obscure phrase.
       KJV: "even three countries"
       ESV: "the third is Naphath"
       ASV: "even the three heights"
       NET: "the three of Naphoth"
       WEB: "the three heights"
       Analysis: This is a difficult phrase. KJV reads it as "three
       regions/districts." ESV transliterates "Naphath" as a place name.
       ASV/WEB interpret as "heights." The Hebrew is obscure; it may refer
       to three highland districts or to the region of Naphoth-dor
       (Joshua 12:23).

v.12 — Hebrew "lo yakh'lu... l'horish" — could not dispossess/drive out.
       KJV: "Yet the children of Manasseh could not drive out the inhabitants of those cities"
       ESV: "Yet the people of Manasseh could not take possession of those cities"
       ASV: "the children of Manasseh could not drive out the inhabitants of those cities"
       NET: "the men of Manasseh were unable to conquer these cities"
       WEB: "the children of Manasseh couldn't drive out the inhabitants of those cities"
       Analysis: Again, NET uses "conquer" for Hebrew "horish"
       (dispossess/drive out). The command was not mere conquest but
       complete removal of the Canaanite population. KJV/ASV/WEB preserve
       "drive out."

v.13 — Hebrew "mas" — forced labor/corvée. Hebrew "lo horish horisho"
       — did not utterly drive out.
       KJV: "put the Canaanites to tribute; but did not utterly drive them out"
       ESV: "put the Canaanites to forced labor, but did not utterly drive them out"
       ASV: "put the Canaanites to taskwork, and did not utterly drive them out"
       NET: "they forced the Canaanites to do hard labor, but they never totally conquered them"
       WEB: "put the Canaanites to forced labor, and didn't utterly drive them out"
       Analysis: ASV/KJV/ESV/WEB's "did not utterly drive them out"
       emphasizes incomplete obedience. NET's "never totally conquered"
       frames it as military failure rather than moral compromise. When
       Israel grew strong, they chose profit over obedience.

v.14 — Hebrew "am rav" — many/numerous/great people. Hebrew "barakh"
       — blessed.
       KJV: "seeing I am a great people, forasmuch as the LORD has blessed me hitherto"
       ESV: "since I am a numerous people, since all along the LORD has blessed me"
       ASV: "forasmuch as hitherto Jehovah hath blessed me"
       NET: "After all, we have many people, for until now the Lord has enabled us to increase in number"
       WEB: "since Yahweh has blessed me so far"
       Analysis: "Rav" = many, numerous, great. ESV's "numerous" focuses
       on population size; KJV's "great" could imply both size and
       importance. ASV preserves "Jehovah hath blessed me," keeping the
       personal divine name and covenant language. NET expands
       interpretively with "enabled us to increase in number."

v.15 — Hebrew "ya'ar" — forest, woodland. Hebrew "Repha'im."
       KJV: "the wood country... the giants"
       ESV: "the forest... the Rephaim"
       ASV: "get thee up to the forest, and cut down for thyself"
       NET: "go up into the forest and clear out a place to live"
       WEB: "go up to the forest, and clear ground for yourself"
       Analysis: The central hill country was heavily forested in this
       period. Joshua tells them to clear it — create your own space
       through labor. Both ASV and NET convey Joshua's challenge. ASV
       preserves the direct, imperative tone of the Hebrew. As elsewhere,
       KJV translates "Rephaim" generically as "giants"; ESV preserves
       the proper name.

v.16 — Hebrew "rekhev barzel" — chariots of iron.
       KJV: "chariots of iron"
       ESV: "chariots of iron"
       ASV: "chariots of iron"
       NET: "chariots with iron-rimmed wheels"
       WEB: "chariots of iron"
       Analysis: Iron-fitted chariots were the advanced military technology
       of the day — the ancient equivalent of tanks. Israel had no
       chariots and no iron-working capability at this time. NET adds
       interpretive detail ("iron-rimmed wheels") not explicit in the
       Hebrew. The Canaanites had overwhelming military superiority in
       the valleys.

v.18 — Hebrew "u-verata" from "bara" — to cut down, clear (trees).
       KJV: "thou shalt cut it down... thou shalt drive out the Canaanites, though they have iron chariots, and though they be strong"
       ESV: "you shall clear it... you shall drive out the Canaanites, though they have chariots of iron, and though they are strong"
       ASV: "thou shalt drive out the Canaanites, though they have chariots of iron, and though they are strong"
       NET: "You can conquer the Canaanites, though they have chariots with iron-rimmed wheels and are strong"
       WEB: "you shall drive out the Canaanites, though they have iron chariots, and though they are strong"
       Analysis: This "bara" is different from the creation verb in
       Genesis 1:1. Joshua commands them to transform wilderness into
       habitable land through hard work. Joshua's words are a prophetic
       promise ("thou shalt drive out"), not merely permission ("you can
       conquer"). ASV/KJV/WEB better capture the assurance of faith.
       NET's "you can conquer" weakens the prophetic certainty.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Manasseh       — Firstborn son of Joseph. His tribe was split: half
               received Gilead/Bashan east of the Jordan (under Moses),
               half received territory in central Canaan west of the
               Jordan (under Joshua).

Machir         — Firstborn of Manasseh, described as "a man of war."
               His descendants received the Transjordan territory of
               Gilead and Bashan, which they had conquered.

Zelophehad's Daughters — Mahlah, Noah, Hoglah, Milcah, and Tirzah.
               Their father died without sons. They petitioned Moses
               (Numbers 27) and established the legal right of daughters
               to inherit when there are no male heirs. Here they claim
               that inheritance from Joshua.

Iron Chariots  — The Canaanites' advanced military technology. Chariots
               fitted with iron were devastating in flat terrain (valleys
               and plains). Israel, as a foot-soldier army, was at a
               severe disadvantage in open ground. Yet God had promised
               victory regardless (Deuteronomy 20:1).

Perizzites     — One of the pre-Israelite peoples of Canaan. Often
               listed among the nations to be dispossessed (Exodus 3:8).
               They inhabited the forested hill country that Joshua
               tells the Joseph tribes to clear.


PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Zelophehad's daughters: bold faith (vv.3-4). They knew God's promise
   and claimed it. They did not passively accept exclusion — they
   approached the leaders and stated their case based on God's Word.
   Do we know God's promises well enough to claim them? Do we approach
   Him boldly (Hebrews 4:16)?

2. Complaint vs. conquest (vv.14-18). The Joseph tribes wanted more
   territory but less effort. They complained to Joshua rather than
   conquering their land. How often do we complain about our
   circumstances rather than doing the work God has set before us?

3. Joshua's challenge: "If you are great, act great" (v.15). Joshua
   does not coddle them. He turns their own claim back on them. True
   greatness is demonstrated in action, not in complaint. If God has
   blessed you with strength, use it — don't demand that others make
   your life easier.

4. Iron chariots are no excuse (v.18). The Canaanites had superior
   technology. But Joshua says: "You shall drive them out, though they
   have iron chariots." God's power is not limited by human technology.
   What "iron chariots" do we use as excuses for inaction? No obstacle
   is too great when God has commanded us to advance.

5. The contrast with Caleb. Caleb: "Give me this mountain — where the
   giants are." Joseph's tribes: "The hill is not enough — and the
   valley has iron chariots." Same God, same promises, opposite
   responses. Faith embraces difficulty; unbelief shrinks from it.

6. Strength without obedience (v.13). When Israel grew strong, they
   enslaved the Canaanites rather than driving them out. Increased
   capacity did not produce increased obedience. Spiritual maturity
   should lead to greater holiness, not greater compromise.
