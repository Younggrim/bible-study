Joshua - Chapter 16
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
The tribes of Ephraim and the half-tribe of Manasseh receive their inheritance in the central hill country. They fail to drive out the Canaanites fully, settling for forced labor rather than complete obedience.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Ephraim's territory: Central hill country between Benjamin (south) and Manasseh (north)
- Key boundaries: Jordan River (east) to the Mediterranean (west); includes Bethel area and the ridge route
- Gezer (western lowlands) remains unconquered — Canaanites dwell among Ephraim as forced laborers

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "And the lot of the children of Joseph fell from Jordan by
        Jericho, to the water of Jericho on the east, to the
        wilderness that goes up from Jericho throughout mount Bethel." (ESV)
        — The Joseph tribes receive central Canaan — the heartland.
        Their territory begins at the Jordan (east) and extends into
        the hill country. This is prime land, reflecting Joseph's
        favored status.

v.4  — "So the children of Joseph, Manasseh and Ephraim, took their
        inheritance." (ESV) — Joseph's double portion fulfilled. Though Joseph
        was one son, his two sons became two tribes, giving the "house
        of Joseph" twice the territorial inheritance of any other son
        of Jacob.

v.9  — "And the separate cities for the children of Ephraim were among
        the inheritance of the children of Manasseh, all the cities with
        their villages." (ESV) — Tribal boundaries were not absolute. Ephraim
        had cities within Manasseh's territory, showing the intermingled
        nature of the two Joseph tribes.

v.10 — "And they drave not out the Canaanites that dwelt in Gezer: but
        the Canaanites dwell among the Ephraimites to this day, and
        serve under tribute." (ESV) — The key verse of the chapter. Ephraim
        chose economic benefit (forced labor/tribute) over complete
        obedience (driving them out). This compromise — keeping enemies
        as slaves rather than removing them — violated God's explicit
        command and planted seeds of future apostasy.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/joshua-16/
  Guzik notes that Ephraim's failure at Gezer illustrates a common
  spiritual pattern: we prefer to manage our sins (put them to "tribute")
  rather than eliminate them completely. Keeping Canaanites as slaves
  seemed pragmatic — free labor! — but it violated God's command and
  eventually led to spiritual contamination. The short-term gain became
  a long-term snare.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "They did not drive them out but put them to tribute. How many
  Christians do the same with their sins! They do not slay them — they
  domesticate them. They do not cast out their lusts — they put them to
  work. But a tamed lion is still a lion. A sin under tribute is still a
  sin. And one day it will rise from its servitude and devour its master."
TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1  — KJV: "the lot of the children of Joseph fell" / ESV: "The
       allotment of the people of Joseph went" — Hebrew "goral" = lot.
       KJV's "fell" reflects the physical casting of lots; ESV's "went"
       describes the resulting boundary line. Both are valid renderings
       of the Hebrew construction.

v.2  — KJV: "goeth out from Bethel to Luz" / ESV: "going from Bethel
       to Luz" — This is puzzling since Bethel and Luz are generally
       identified as the same place (Genesis 28:19). Some scholars
       suggest Luz was the original Canaanite city and Bethel the
       nearby sacred site, later merged into one name. Others see them
       as adjacent locations at this early period.

v.3  — KJV: "Beth-horon the nether" / ESV: "Lower Beth-horon" — Hebrew
       "Bet-Choron ha-tachton." "Tachton" = lower. There were two
       Beth-horons: Upper and Lower, controlling a strategic pass from
       the coastal plain to the hill country. KJV's "nether" is archaic
       for "lower."

v.8  — KJV: "the river Kanah" / ESV: "the brook Kanah" — Hebrew
       "nachal Qanah." "Nachal" = wadi, seasonal stream. ESV's "brook"
       is more accurate than KJV's "river" — this was a seasonal
       watercourse, not a permanent river.

v.10 — KJV: "they drave not out" / ESV: "they did not drive out" —
       "Drave" is the archaic past tense of "drive" in KJV English.
       Same Hebrew verb "yarash" (in hiphil: to dispossess, drive out).

v.10 — KJV: "serve under tribute" / ESV: "have been made to do forced
       labor" — Hebrew "la-mas" = forced labor, corvée. This is the
       same term used for Solomon's labor gangs (1 Kings 5:13). ESV's
       "forced labor" is more precise than KJV's "tribute" (which
       suggests monetary payment rather than labor service).

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Ephraim        — Younger son of Joseph, elevated above his older brother
               Manasseh by Jacob's crossed-hands blessing (Genesis 48:14).
               His tribe occupied the central hill country and became the
               dominant northern tribe. "Ephraim" later became a synonym
               for the entire northern kingdom (Isaiah 7:2; Hosea 5:3).

Joseph Tribes  — Ephraim and Manasseh together. Because Joseph received
               a double portion through his two sons, the "twelve tribes"
               are maintained even though Levi receives no territorial
               allotment (12 - Levi + Ephraim/Manasseh split = 12).

Gezer          — A major Canaanite city-state on the border between
               Ephraim and the coastal plain. Strategically located on
               the road from Egypt to Mesopotamia. Remained Canaanite
               until Pharaoh gave it to Solomon (1 Kings 9:16).

Beth-horon     — Twin cities (Upper and Lower) controlling the main pass
               from the coastal plain to the central hill country. Site
               of Joshua's great victory over the Amorite coalition
               (Joshua 10:10-11). Later fortified by Solomon (1 Kings
               9:17).

Forced Labor (mas) — Hebrew "mas" = corvée, compulsory labor service.
               Rather than obeying God's command to drive out the
               Canaanites, Israel enslaved them. This compromise seemed
               economically advantageous but violated the divine command
               and led to spiritual contamination.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.4 — ASV: "the children of Joseph, Manasseh and Ephraim, took their inheritance" / NET: "Joseph's descendants, Manasseh and Ephraim, were assigned their land" — NET uses passive "were assigned" where ASV/KJV use active "took their inheritance" (nachalah). The active voice preserves the faith dimension — the tribes must claim and possess what God has given.
- v.8 — ASV: "This is the inheritance of the tribe of the children of Ephraim" / NET: "This is the land assigned to the tribe of Ephraim by its clans" — The consistent NET pattern: replacing "inheritance" with "land assigned." The nachalah concept ties each tribe's land to God's covenant with Abraham.
- v.10 — ASV: "they drove not out the Canaanites that dwelt in Gezer" / WEB: "They didn't drive out the Canaanites who lived in Gezer" / NET: "The Ephraimites did not conquer the Canaanites living in Gezer" — NET uses "conquer" where Hebrew "horish" means specifically "to drive out/dispossess." The failure was not in battle but in completion — they did not expel the inhabitants as commanded.
- v.10 — ASV: "are become servants to do taskwork" / WEB: "but the Canaanites lived among the Ephraimites...and became servants doing forced labor" / NET: "do hard labor as their servants" — KJV uses "serve under tribute," ASV "servants to do taskwork" — Hebrew "mas oved" = forced labor/corvée. Israel exploited rather than expelled — disobedience disguised as pragmatism.
- v.1 — ASV: "the lot came out for the children of Joseph" / NET: "The land allotted to Joseph's descendants" — ASV/KJV preserve "the lot came out" (Hebrew "yatza ha-goral"), describing the sacred casting of lots before God. NET's passive "allotted" removes the active divine guidance implied in the lot-casting ceremony (cf. Proverbs 16:33).

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "They drave not out the Canaanites" (v.10). Ephraim's failure is a
   warning: partial obedience is not obedience. They kept the Canaanites
   for economic benefit — forced labor. We do the same when we manage
   our sins rather than mortify them. A domesticated sin is still a sin.

2. Tribute instead of expulsion (v.10). It seemed wise — why waste
   resources driving them out when you can profit from their labor? But
   God's command was clear: drive them out completely. Human wisdom that
   contradicts divine command is foolishness, no matter how pragmatic it
   appears.

3. Joseph's double portion (v.4). God's faithfulness to His promises
   spans generations. What Jacob blessed in Genesis 48, God fulfills in
   Joshua 16. The promises of God are not empty words — they are
   accomplished across centuries.

4. Intermingled boundaries (v.9). Ephraim had cities within Manasseh's
   territory. God's people are not meant to be isolated from one another.
   Tribal boundaries existed, but cooperation and shared space were part
   of God's design. The body of Christ has distinct members but shared
   life.

5. The seeds of future failure. Gezer's Canaanites "dwell among the
   Ephraimites unto this day." What we tolerate today becomes permanent
   tomorrow. The compromises of one generation become the bondage of the
   next. Deal with sin now — before it takes root.
