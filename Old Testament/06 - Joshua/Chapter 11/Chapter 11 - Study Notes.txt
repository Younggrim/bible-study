Joshua - Chapter 11
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A massive northern coalition under Jabin of Hazor gathers against Israel, but God delivers them all into Joshua's hand. Joshua conquers the entire land, fulfilling everything the LORD commanded Moses.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Joshua, with possible later editorial additions. Joshua was Moses'
assistant and successor, who led Israel into the Promised Land. The book
covers approximately 25 years (1406-1381 BC) — from the crossing of the
Jordan to Joshua's death. Hebrew title: 'Yehoshua' — 'YHWH saves' (the
same name as Jesus in Greek). The book demonstrates God's faithfulness to
His land promise to Abraham (Genesis 15:18-21) and shows that victory
comes through faith and obedience, not human strength.

Historical Context: Chapter 11 records the northern campaign — the final
major military operation of the conquest. After subduing the south
(chapter 10), Joshua now faces a massive northern coalition led by Jabin
king of Hazor. This coalition is far larger than the southern one: armies
"as the sand that is upon the sea shore in multitude, with horses and
chariots very many" (v.4). Israel has never faced chariots before — the
ancient equivalent of tanks. Yet God says: "Be not afraid... thou shalt
hough their horses, and burn their chariots with fire" (v.6). Israel is
not to keep the horses and chariots for themselves — they must trust God,
not military technology. The chapter concludes with a sweeping summary of
the entire conquest: "Joshua took the whole land" (v.23). It also records
the destruction of the Anakim — the giants who terrified the spies 40
years earlier (Numbers 13:33). The generation that feared the giants is
dead; the generation that trusts God destroys them. The chapter ends with
the beautiful statement: "And the land rested from war" (v.23).

The Northern Coalition (vv.1-5): Jabin king of Hazor — "the head of all
those kingdoms" (v.10) — hears of Joshua's victories and organizes the
largest coalition yet. He summons kings from Madon, Shimron, Achshaph,
and throughout the north (vv.1-3). Their combined army is "as the sand
that is upon the sea shore" with "horses and chariots very many" (v.4).
They gather at the waters of Merom to fight Israel (v.5).

God's Promise and Joshua's Attack (vv.6-9): God reassures Joshua: "Be not
afraid because of them: for to morrow about this time will I deliver them
up all slain before Israel" (v.6). God commands: hamstring the horses and
burn the chariots (v.6) — Israel must not rely on captured military
technology. Joshua attacks suddenly at Merom (v.7). God delivers them;
Israel chases them to Great Sidon, Misrephoth-maim, and the Valley of
Mizpeh — "until they left them none remaining" (v.8). Joshua obeys
regarding the horses and chariots (v.9).

Hazor Destroyed (vv.10-14): Joshua turns back and captures Hazor — the
coalition's capital (v.10). He burns it with fire (v.11) — the only
northern city burned (v.13). All other cities are captured but left
standing (v.13). The people are destroyed but the spoil and livestock are
kept by Israel (v.14). "Neither left they any to breathe" (v.14).

Summary of Obedience (v.15): "As the LORD commanded Moses his servant, so
did Moses command Joshua, and so did Joshua; he left nothing undone of all
that the LORD commanded Moses." A chain of obedience: God → Moses →
Joshua. Complete, total faithfulness to every command.

Summary of the Conquest (vv.16-23): Joshua takes "all that land" — hills,
south country, Goshen, valley, plain, mountains of Israel (v.16). The
territory: from Mount Halak (south, toward Edom) to Baal-gad (north,
under Mount Hermon) (v.17). The war took "a long time" (v.18). No city
made peace except Gibeon (v.19). God hardened the Canaanites' hearts so
they would fight rather than surrender — ensuring their complete
destruction as commanded (v.20). Joshua destroys the Anakim — the giants
— from the hill country (v.21). Only remnants survive in Gaza, Gath, and
Ashdod (v.22 — Goliath later comes from Gath). "So Joshua took the whole
land... and the land rested from war" (v.23).

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Hazor: The largest Canaanite city, located in upper Galilee — head of the northern coalition
- Waters of Merom: A lake/spring in upper Galilee where Joshua surprises the northern kings
- The northern campaign sweeps from Merom through upper and lower Galilee to Sidon and Misrephoth-maim on the coast

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.4  — "They went out, they and all their hosts with them, much people,
        even as the sand that is upon the sea shore in multitude, with
        horses and chariots very many." (ESV) — The largest army Israel has
        faced. Horses and chariots — terrifying military technology. Yet
        God says "Be not afraid."

v.6  — "Be not afraid because of them: for to morrow about this time
        will I deliver them up all slain before Israel: you shall hough
        their horses, and burn their chariots with fire." (ESV) — God promises
        victory AND commands the destruction of captured weapons. Israel
        must trust God, not military technology.

v.15 — "As the LORD commanded Moses his servant, so did Moses command
        Joshua, and so did Joshua; he left nothing undone of all that the
        LORD commanded Moses." (ESV) — The chain of faithful obedience. Joshua's
        epitaph: he left nothing undone. Complete, thorough, total
        obedience to every divine command.

v.20 — "For it was of the LORD to harden their hearts, that they should
        come against Israel in battle, that he might destroy them utterly." (ESV)
        — Divine hardening. God hardened the Canaanites so they would
        fight rather than flee or make peace. Their judgment was
        determined; their resistance ensured its completeness.

v.21 — "And at that time came Joshua, and cut off the Anakims from the
        mountains." (ESV) — The giants that terrified the spies 40 years ago
        (Numbers 13:33) are now destroyed. What the faithless generation
        feared, the faithful generation conquers.

v.23 — "So Joshua took the whole land, according to all that the LORD
        said to Moses; and Joshua gave it for an inheritance to Israel
        according to their divisions by their tribes. And the land rested
        from war." (ESV) — The conquest complete. Promise fulfilled. The land
        rests. God's word to Abraham (Genesis 15:18-21) is accomplished.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/joshua-11/
  Guzik emphasizes the command to hamstring horses and burn chariots —
  God wanted Israel to trust Him, not captured technology. He notes that
  v.20 (God hardening hearts) parallels Pharaoh's hardening: God confirms
  people in the direction they have already chosen. The Anakim's
  destruction is the final answer to the faithless spies of Numbers 13.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "The giants are fallen! The Anakim — those terrible sons of Anak before
  whom Israel trembled forty years ago — are cut off. What the fathers
  feared, the children conquer. Unbelief makes giants invincible; faith
  makes them fall. The same enemies that paralyzed one generation are
  destroyed by the next — the difference is not in the enemy's size but
  in the people's faith."
TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.4  — Hebrew "ka-chol asher al s'fat ha-yam la-rov" — standard hyperbolic expression for an innumerable multitude.
       KJV: "as the sand that is upon the sea shore in multitude"
       ESV: "in number like the sand that is on the seashore"
       ASV: "as the sand that is upon the seashore in multitude"
       NET: "as numerous as the sand on the seashore"
       WEB: "as the sand that is on the seashore in multitude"
       Analysis: All translations preserve this classic biblical comparison (cf. Genesis 22:17). The northern coalition was vastly larger than anything Israel had faced — yet God's assurance (v.6) removes all fear. The hyperbole emphasizes the impossibility of the odds from a human perspective.

v.6  — Hebrew "t'aqqer et suseihem" — "aqar" = to hamstring, cut the leg tendons. God commands disabling enemy horses.
       KJV: "thou shalt hough their horses"
       ESV: "You shall hamstring their horses"
       ASV: "thou shalt hock their horses"
       NET: "You must hamstring their horses"
       WEB: "You shall hamstring their horses"
       Analysis: KJV's "hough" (pronounced "hock") and ASV's "hock" are archaic. ESV/NET/WEB "hamstring" is accessible to modern readers. The purpose: Israel must not keep war horses. Their trust must remain in God, not in military technology. The divine name in this verse: ASV "Jehovah," WEB "Yahweh," NET "the Lord" — God's personal assurance before a massive coalition battle.

v.11 — Hebrew "cherem" — utterly destroying; devoted to destruction.
       KJV: "they smote all the souls... utterly destroying them"
       ESV: "striking them with the edge of the sword, devoting them to destruction"
       ASV: "utterly destroying them; there was none left that breathed"
       NET: "They annihilated everyone who lived there with the sword"
       WEB: "utterly destroying them. There was no one left who breathed"
       Analysis: The cherem language continues throughout Joshua. NET's "annihilated" vs. ASV/WEB's "utterly destroying" — the literal translations preserve the technical term for sacred devotion to destruction. ESV's "devoting them to destruction" most fully captures the theological meaning.

v.13 — Hebrew "ha-arim ha-omdot al tillam" — "tel" = an artificial mound formed by successive settlements.
       KJV: "the cities that stood still in their strength"
       ESV: "the cities that stood on mounds"
       ASV: "the cities that stood on their mounds"
       NET: "the cities built on mounds"
       WEB: "the cities that stood on their mounds"
       Analysis: KJV interprets "tillam" (their mounds/tells) as "their strength" — misleading. ESV/ASV/NET/WEB correctly identify "tel" as an archaeological mound. These fortified cities on their tells were left intact — Israel would inhabit them. Only Hazor was burned.

v.15 — Hebrew "lo hesir davar" — literally "he did not turn aside a thing." "Sur" = to turn aside, deviate.
       KJV: "he left nothing undone"
       ESV: "He left nothing undone"
       ASV: "he left nothing undone"
       NET: "He did not deviate at all from what the Lord had commanded Moses"
       WEB: "He left nothing undone"
       Analysis: NET is more interpretive but adds helpful clarity. KJV/ASV/ESV/WEB are concise and powerful. Joshua did not deviate from a single command — total, comprehensive obedience. The highest commendation a leader can receive.

v.20 — Hebrew "me'et Adonai hay'tah l'chazzeq et libbam" — "chazaq" = to strengthen, harden, make firm. Same word used of Pharaoh.
       KJV: "it was of the LORD to harden their hearts"
       ESV: "it was the LORD's doing to harden their hearts"
       ASV: "it was of Jehovah to harden their hearts"
       NET: "the Lord determined to make them obstinate"
       WEB: "it was of Yahweh to harden their hearts"
       Analysis: NET interprets "harden" as "make obstinate" and "no favor" (Hebrew "t'chinah" — no grace/supplication) as "without mercy." KJV/ASV/ESV/WEB's "harden their hearts" and "no favour" better preserve the parallel with Pharaoh and the concept that God removed any possibility of the Canaanites seeking terms of peace. God strengthened their resolve to fight — confirming them in their chosen rebellion — so that judgment would be complete.

v.21 — Hebrew "ha-Anaqim" — descendants of Anak, a race of giants. Hebrew "karath" = to cut off.
       KJV: "Joshua... cut off the Anakim"
       ESV: "Joshua... cut off the Anakim"
       ASV: "Joshua... cut off the Anakim"
       NET: "Joshua attacked and eliminated the Anakites"
       WEB: "Joshua... cut off the Anakim"
       Analysis: NET's "eliminated" is general; KJV/ASV/ESV/WEB's "cut off" (Hebrew "karath") is a covenant term meaning to sever completely, used for both covenant-making and judgment. They terrified the spies in Numbers 13:33 — their destruction by Joshua fulfills what the faithless generation refused to attempt.

v.23 — Hebrew "v'ha-aretz shaq'tah mil-chamah" — "shaqat" = to be quiet, at rest, undisturbed; Hebrew "nachalah" = inheritance.
       KJV: "the land rested from war... Joshua gave it for an inheritance"
       ESV: "the land had rest from war... Joshua gave it for an inheritance"
       ASV: "the land had rest from war... Joshua gave it for an inheritance unto Israel"
       NET: "the land was free of war... he assigned Israel their tribal portions"
       WEB: "the land had rest from war... Joshua gave it for an inheritance to Israel"
       Analysis: NET's "assigned tribal portions" is bureaucratic; KJV/ASV/ESV/WEB's "inheritance" (nachalah) preserves the theological concept that the land is a God-given inheritance, not merely an administrative allocation. The land itself rests — peace after conquest. This foreshadows the ultimate rest God provides (Hebrews 4:8-9).

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Hazor          — The largest and most powerful city in northern Canaan,
               "the head of all those kingdoms" (v.10). Located in upper
               Galilee. Archaeological excavations confirm it was a major
               city destroyed by fire in the Late Bronze Age. Its king
               Jabin organized the northern coalition.

Waters of Merom — A body of water in upper Galilee (possibly Lake Huleh
               or a nearby spring) where the northern coalition gathered.
               Joshua attacked them here suddenly, before they could
               organize their superior forces.

Hamstring      — Hebrew "aqar." To cut the tendons of a horse's hind
               legs, permanently disabling it. God commanded this to
               prevent Israel from building a chariot force and trusting
               in military technology rather than in Him.

Anakim/Anakims — A race of giants descended from Anak. They inhabited
               the hill country of Canaan and terrified the Israelite
               spies (Numbers 13:28,33). Joshua destroyed them except for
               remnants in Gaza, Gath, and Ashdod. Goliath was likely a
               descendant of this remnant (1 Samuel 17:4).

Mount Halak    — "The smooth/bald mountain." The southern boundary of
               Joshua's conquest, rising toward Seir (Edom).

Baal-gad       — The northern boundary of Joshua's conquest, in the
               Valley of Lebanon below Mount Hermon. The name means "Lord
               of fortune" — a Canaanite worship site.

Hardening of Hearts — God's judicial act of confirming people in their
               chosen rebellion. As with Pharaoh, God does not force
               people into sin but strengthens the resolve of those who
               have already chosen to resist Him, ensuring that judgment
               is complete.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Trust God, not technology (v.6). God commands Israel to destroy the
   horses and chariots — the most advanced military technology of the day.
   Israel must depend on God, not on captured weapons. What "chariots" do
   you trust instead of God? Resources, connections, strategies — all good
   things, but dangerous when they replace dependence on God.

2. The giants fall (v.21). The Anakim — the giants that paralyzed Israel
   with fear 40 years earlier — are destroyed by the faithful generation.
   The enemies that defeated your fathers do not have to defeat you. What
   the previous generation feared, you can conquer through faith.

3. "He left nothing undone" (v.15). Joshua's epitaph: complete obedience.
   Not partial, not selective, not convenient — total. He did everything
   God commanded through Moses. This is the standard: leave nothing
   undone. Finish what God has given you to do.

4. God hardens those who harden themselves (v.20). The Canaanites had 400+
   years to repent (Genesis 15:16). They chose rebellion. God confirmed
   their choice by hardening their hearts for battle. God does not force
   anyone into sin — but He will confirm the direction you persistently
   choose. Choose wisely.

5. "The land rested from war" (v.23). After long, hard fighting — rest.
   God's people are not meant to be perpetually at war. There is a rest
   that comes after faithful obedience. Hebrews 4 points to the ultimate
   rest in Christ. Fight faithfully now; rest is coming.

6. A long time (v.18). "Joshua made war a long time with all those kings."
   The conquest was not instantaneous — it took years of persistent
   effort. Do not expect overnight victory. Faithfulness over a long time
   produces complete conquest. Persevere.
