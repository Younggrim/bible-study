Joshua - Chapter 24
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Joshua gathers all Israel at Shechem, recounts God's faithfulness from Abraham to the conquest, and calls them to choose whom they will serve. The people covenant to serve the LORD, and Joshua dies at 110 years old.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Shechem: Located between Mount Ebal and Mount Gerizim in the central highlands — Israel's covenant renewal site
- "Beyond the River" (v.2): Abraham's origin in Ur/Haran, beyond the Euphrates River in Mesopotamia
- Joshua buried at Timnath-serah in the hill country of Ephraim; Joseph's bones buried at Shechem

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "Your fathers dwelt on the other side of the flood in old time,
        even Terah, the father of Abraham, and the father of Nachor: and
        they served other gods." (ESV) — The starting point: Abraham's family
        were idolaters in Mesopotamia. Israel's story begins not with
        their merit but with God's sovereign choice. Grace chose Abraham
        out of paganism.

v.12 — "I sent the hornet before you, which drave them out from before
        you... but not with your sword, nor with your bow." (ESV) — God's
        emphatic claim: the victory was MINE, not yours. Israel's
        weapons did not win the land — God's power did. This prevents
        all boasting.

v.13 — "I have given you a land for which you did not labour, and cities
        which you built not, and you dwell in them; of the vineyards and
        oliveyards which you planted not do you eat." (ESV) — Pure grace. Every
        blessing is unearned. The land, the cities, the vineyards — all
        gifts. This is the foundation for the call to serve: you owe
        everything to God.

v.14 — "Now therefore fear the LORD, and serve him in sincerity and in
        truth: and put away the gods which your fathers served on the
        other side of the flood, and in Egypt; and serve you the LORD." (ESV)
        — The demand that follows grace. Because God has done all this,
        therefore serve Him — sincerely, truthfully, exclusively. Note:
        "put away the gods" — some Israelites still had foreign gods
        among them!

v.15 — "And if it is evil in your eyes to serve the LORD, choose this day
        whom you will serve, whether the gods your fathers served in the
        region beyond the River, or the gods of the Amorites in whose land
        you dwell. But as for me and my house, we will serve the LORD."
        (ESV) — The most famous verse in Joshua. The choice is stark:
        ancestral gods, local gods, or the LORD. There is no fourth option.
        Neutrality is impossible. Joshua declares his choice first —
        leading by example.

v.19 — "You cannot serve the LORD: for he is an holy God; he is a
        jealous God; he will not forgive your transgressions nor your
        sins." (ESV) — Joshua's shocking response to their pledge. He is not
        discouraging them but testing the depth of their commitment.
        Serving God is not casual — He demands absolute loyalty. This
        is not a God who tolerates divided hearts.

v.23 — "Now therefore put away, said he, the strange gods which are
        among you, and incline your heart to the LORD God of Israel." (ESV)
        — The practical test: if you mean what you say, ACT. Put away
        the foreign gods you still possess. Commitment without action
        is empty words.

v.27 — "Behold, this stone shall be a witness to us; for it has
        heard all the words of the LORD which he spoke to us: it shall
        be therefore a witness to you, lest you deny your God." (ESV) — The
        witness stone. A permanent, silent testimony to the covenant
        made this day. If Israel breaks faith, the stone will testify
        against them.

v.29 — "Joshua the son of Nun, the servant of the LORD, died, being an
        hundred and ten years old." (ESV) — Joshua's title at death: "the
        servant of the LORD" — the same title given to Moses
        (Deuteronomy 34:5). The highest honor: faithful service to God
        until the end.

v.31 — "Israel served the LORD all the days of Joshua, and all the days
        of the elders that overlived Joshua." (ESV) — Joshua's lasting
        influence. His generation remained faithful. But the ominous
        implication: after those elders died, faithfulness ended. The
        book of Judges begins where this verse ends.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/joshua-24/
  Guzik emphasizes that Joshua's "choose you this day" is not an
  invitation to consider options but a demand for decisive commitment.
  The choice is not whether to serve — everyone serves something — but
  WHOM to serve. He also notes Joshua's surprising "Ye cannot serve the
  LORD" (v.19) as a test of sincerity: Joshua wants them to count the
  cost before pledging. Cheap commitment is worthless to a holy God.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "'Choose you this day whom ye will serve.' Not tomorrow — today. Not
  eventually — now. Joshua does not say, 'Think about it.' He says,
  'Choose.' And then he leads: 'As for me and my house, we will serve
  the LORD.' He does not wait for a committee vote. He does not take a
  poll. He declares his allegiance and invites others to follow. This is
  leadership. This is faith. This is the courage of conviction that
  changes nations."
TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.2  — KJV: "on the other side of the flood" / ESV: "beyond the
       Euphrates" — Hebrew "b'ever ha-nahar." "Nahar" = river (the
       Euphrates). KJV's "flood" is archaic English for "river" (not
       Noah's flood). ESV correctly identifies it as the Euphrates.
       Abraham's family lived in Mesopotamia (Ur, then Haran) — beyond
       the great river.

v.2  — KJV: "they served other gods" / ESV: "they served other gods" —
       A startling admission: Abraham's own family were idolaters. Jewish
       tradition (based on this verse) says Terah made and sold idols.
       God's choice of Abraham was pure grace — not a reward for prior
       faithfulness.

v.12 — KJV: "I sent the hornet before you" / ESV: "I sent the hornet
       before you" — Hebrew "ha-tsir'ah." "Tsir'ah" = hornet, wasp.
       Debated whether this is literal (actual hornets/wasps) or
       figurative (terror, panic, or Egypt — whose symbol was a bee/
       hornet). Either way, God claims the victory: "not with thy sword,
       nor with thy bow."

v.14 — KJV: "in sincerity and in truth" / ESV: "in sincerity and in
       faithfulness" — Hebrew "b'tamim u-v'emet." "Tamim" = complete,
       whole, without blemish (same word used of sacrificial animals).
       "Emet" = truth, faithfulness, reliability. KJV's "truth" and
       ESV's "faithfulness" both translate "emet" — which encompasses
       both concepts.

v.15 — KJV: "choose you this day" / ESV: "choose this day" — Hebrew
       "bacharu lakhem ha-yom." "Bachar" = to choose, select, prefer.
       The imperative is plural — addressed to all Israel. The choice
       is individual but made publicly, corporately, decisively.

v.19 — KJV: "Ye cannot serve the LORD" / ESV: "You are not able to
       serve the LORD" — Hebrew "lo tukh'lu la'avod et YHWH." "Yakol"
       = to be able. Joshua's statement is provocative — designed to
       test their resolve. He is saying: "Do you understand what you're
       committing to? This God is holy and jealous. Half-hearted service
       is worse than none."

v.26 — KJV: "under an oak" / ESV: "under the terebinth" — Hebrew
       "tachat ha-elah." "Elah" = terebinth (a large tree, not
       technically an oak). KJV's "oak" is a common but imprecise
       translation. This may be the same tree under which Jacob buried
       the foreign gods (Genesis 35:4) — a fitting location for
       covenant renewal.

v.29 — KJV: "the servant of the LORD" / ESV: "the servant of the LORD"
       — Hebrew "eved YHWH." Joshua's final title — the same given to
       Moses (Deuteronomy 34:5). In chapter 1:1, Joshua was "Moses'
       minister/assistant." Now he is "the servant of the LORD" — he
       has grown into his own direct relationship with God.

v.32 — KJV: "for an hundred pieces of silver" / ESV: "for a hundred
       pieces of money" — Hebrew "b'me'ah q'sitah." "Qesitah" is an
       ancient unit of value — its exact worth is unknown. KJV's
       "silver" is interpretive; ESV's "money" is more cautious. The
       term appears only here, Genesis 33:19, and Job 42:11.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Shechem        — A city in the central hill country between Mount Ebal
               and Mount Gerizim. One of the most historically
               significant sites in Israel: Abraham's first altar
               (Genesis 12:6-7), Jacob's well and land purchase (Genesis
               33:18-19), the covenant renewal here, and later the
               first capital of the northern kingdom (1 Kings 12:25).

Choose (bachar) — To select, prefer, decide for. In Hebrew thought,
               choosing is not merely intellectual preference but
               decisive commitment expressed in action. To "choose" God
               means to serve Him exclusively.

Sincerity (tamim) — Completeness, wholeness, integrity. The same word
               used for unblemished sacrificial animals. Service to God
               must be whole — not partial, not divided, not defective.

Jealous God (El qanna) — God's jealousy is not petty envy but the
               righteous demand of exclusive devotion from those in
               covenant with Him. As a husband rightly demands his
               wife's exclusive love, God demands His people's exclusive
               worship.

Servant of the LORD (eved YHWH) — The highest title given to God's
               chosen leaders: Moses (Deuteronomy 34:5), Joshua (24:29),
               David (Psalm 36 title), and ultimately the Messiah
               (Isaiah 42:1; 52:13). It denotes both humble submission
               and honored commission.

Joseph's Bones — Joseph made Israel swear to carry his bones back to
               Canaan (Genesis 50:25). Moses took them from Egypt
               (Exodus 13:19). Now, after the conquest, they are finally
               buried at Shechem — in the land Jacob purchased (Genesis
               33:19). A 400-year promise fulfilled.

Terebinth (elah) — A large, long-lived tree common in Canaan. Often
               associated with sacred sites. The terebinth at Shechem
               was likely ancient and well-known — a landmark for
               covenant ceremonies.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Choose you this day whom ye will serve" (v.15). The most urgent
   question any person faces. There is no neutrality — everyone serves
   something. The only question is WHAT. Joshua presents the options
   clearly: dead gods of the past, local gods of the culture, or the
   living LORD. Choose. Today. Not tomorrow.

2. "As for me and my house, we will serve the LORD" (v.15). Joshua does
   not wait for consensus. He leads by declaring his own commitment
   first. Leaders go first. Fathers go first. The household follows the
   head. What have you declared for your house?

3. Grace precedes demand (vv.2-13). Before God asks for commitment, He
   recounts His gifts. "I took... I gave... I sent... I brought... I
   delivered." The call to serve is grounded in what God has already
   done. We do not serve to earn His favor — we serve because we have
   already received it.

4. "Ye cannot serve the LORD" (v.19). Joshua's shocking statement tests
   their sincerity. Serving God is not casual. He is holy, jealous, and
   will not tolerate rivals. Count the cost before you pledge (Luke
   14:28). Better to not vow than to vow and not pay (Ecclesiastes 5:5).

5. "Put away the strange gods which are among you" (v.23). Even after
   the conquest, some Israelites still had foreign gods! Commitment to
   the LORD requires active removal of rivals. What "gods" do we still
   harbor — comfort, approval, control, pleasure? Put them away. Today.

6. Joseph's bones buried at Shechem (v.32). A 400-year-old promise
   fulfilled. Joseph believed God would bring Israel to Canaan — and
   made them swear to bring his bones. Faith looks beyond death. Faith
   makes plans for a future it will not see. Joseph's bones testify:
   God keeps His word across centuries.

7. "Israel served the LORD all the days of Joshua" (v.31). One
   generation's faithfulness. But the next generation "knew not the LORD"
   (Judges 2:10). Faithfulness is not automatically inherited. Each
   generation must choose for itself. We cannot coast on our parents'
   faith.

8. Three burials close the book (vv.29-33). Joshua, Joseph, Eleazar —
   the leader, the patriarch, the priest. An era ends. The generation
   that conquered Canaan passes away. What remains? The land, the
   covenant, the choice — and the question: will the next generation
   serve the LORD?
