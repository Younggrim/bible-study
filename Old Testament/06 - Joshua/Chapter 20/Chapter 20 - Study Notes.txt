Joshua - Chapter 20
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Six cities of refuge are designated—three on each side of the Jordan—where those who kill accidentally may flee from the avenger of blood. These cities reflect God's justice tempered with mercy.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Six cities of refuge: West — Kedesh (Naphtali/north), Shechem (Ephraim/central), Hebron (Judah/south)
- East — Bezer (Reuben/south), Ramoth-gilead (Gad/central), Golan (Manasseh/north, in Bashan)
- Strategically spaced so no one in Israel was more than a day's journey from a city of refuge

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "Appoint out for you cities of refuge, whereof I spoke to you
        by the hand of Moses." (ESV) — God fulfills His earlier command
        (Numbers 35:9-34). His instructions given in the wilderness are
        now implemented in the land. God's plans are not forgotten —
        they are fulfilled in their proper time.

v.3  — "That the slayer that killeth any person unawares and unwittingly
        may flee thither: and they shall be your refuge from the avenger
        of blood." (ESV) — The key distinction: "unawares and unwittingly" —
        without premeditation or hatred. God distinguishes between
        murder (intentional) and manslaughter (accidental). Justice
        requires this distinction.

v.5  — "They shall not deliver the slayer up into his hand; because he
        smote his neighbour unwittingly, and hated him not beforetime." (ESV)
        — The test of intent: did the killer hate the victim previously?
        Was there prior animosity? If not, the killing was accidental
        and the manslayer deserves protection, not execution.

v.6  — "Until the death of the high priest that shall be in those days:
        then shall the slayer return, and come to his own city." (ESV) — The
        high priest's death ends the manslayer's exile. This is deeply
        typological: Christ our High Priest died, and His death sets us
        free. The high priest's death atones in some sense for the blood
        that was shed, releasing the manslayer from guilt's consequences.

v.7  — "And they appointed Kedesh in Galilee in mount Naphtali, and
        Shechem in mount Ephraim, and Kirjath-arba, which is Hebron, in
        the mountain of Judah." (ESV) — Three cities west of Jordan, evenly
        distributed north to south. No Israelite was far from refuge.
        God's mercy is always accessible.

v.9  — "These were the cities appointed for all the children of Israel,
        and for the stranger that sojourneth among them." (ESV) — The cities
        were open to ALL — Israelite and foreigner alike. God's mercy
        is not limited to the covenant people. The stranger receives
        equal protection. Cf. "whosoever will" (Revelation 22:17).

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/joshua-20/
  Guzik develops the typology of Christ as our city of refuge: (1) The
  manslayer had to flee — there was urgency; (2) The city was accessible
  — no one was too far away; (3) The gates were always open; (4) The
  protection was complete — the avenger could not reach him inside;
  (5) The high priest's death brought full release. Every element points
  to Christ's saving work.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "The cities of refuge are Christ. The manslayer fled — he did not
  saunter, he ran for his life. So must the sinner flee to Christ. There
  is an avenger behind you — the justice of God, the penalty of the law.
  Run! The gates stand open. Christ receives all who come. And once
  inside, no avenger can touch you. You are safe — not because of your
  innocence, but because of your refuge. And when the High Priest dies?
  Our High Priest has died — and risen! We are free forever."
TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.2  — Hebrew "tenu lakhem" — give/designate for yourselves.
       KJV: "Appoint out for you cities of refuge"
       ESV: "Designate the cities of refuge"
       ASV: "Assign you the cities of refuge"
       NET: "Have the Israelites select the cities of refuge"
       WEB: "Assign the cities of refuge"
       Analysis: NET's "select" implies Israel chooses; KJV/ASV/WEB's "appoint/assign"
       (Hebrew "tenu" — give/designate) implies God's prior directive being carried out.
       God selected the concept; Israel designated the specific cities. The difference
       matters theologically: refuge is God's provision, not human invention.

v.3  — Hebrew "bishgagah b'vli da'at" — inadvertently, without knowledge/intent.
       KJV: "the slayer that killeth any person unawares and unwittingly"
       ESV: "the manslayer who kills any person without intent or unknowingly"
       ASV: "the manslayer that killeth any person unwittingly and unawares"
       NET: "Anyone who accidentally kills someone"
       WEB: "the manslayer who kills any person accidentally and unintentionally"
       Analysis: Two Hebrew terms emphasize the accidental nature: "bishgagah"
       (unintentionally) and "b'vli da'at" (without knowledge/premeditation). NET
       simplifies both to "accidentally," losing the dual emphasis. WEB preserves both
       distinctions ("accidentally and unintentionally"). KJV's "unawares and unwittingly"
       is somewhat redundant in modern English; ESV's "without intent or unknowingly"
       distinguishes the two concepts more clearly.

v.3  — Hebrew "go'el ha-dam" — kinsman-redeemer of blood.
       KJV: "the avenger of blood"
       ESV: "the avenger of blood"
       ASV: "the avenger of blood"
       NET: "the avenger of blood"
       WEB: "the avenger of blood"
       Analysis: All translations agree. "Go'el" = kinsman-redeemer, the nearest relative
       responsible for avenging a family member's death. The same word used for Boaz as
       Ruth's "kinsman-redeemer." The go'el had both the right and duty to pursue blood
       justice.

v.4  — Hebrew "petach sha'ar ha-ir" — entrance of the gate of the city.
       KJV: "shall stand at the entering of the gate of the city"
       ESV: "shall stand at the entrance of the gate of the city"
       ASV: "shall stand at the entrance of the gate of the city"
       NET: "shall stand at the entrance of the city gate"
       WEB: "shall stand at the entrance of the gate of the city"
       Analysis: Minor word order variation only. The city gate was the place of legal
       proceedings in ancient Israel (Ruth 4:1-2). The manslayer presents his case
       publicly to the elders who sit at the gate.

v.5  — Hebrew "lo sone' lo mit'mol shilshom" — not hating him yesterday or the day before.
       KJV: "hated him not beforetime"
       ESV: "did not hate him in the past"
       ASV: "because he smote his neighbor unawares, and hated him not beforetime"
       NET: "for he accidentally killed his fellow man without premeditation"
       WEB: "because he struck his neighbor unintentionally, and didn't hate him previously"
       Analysis: KJV/ASV's "hated him not beforetime" preserves the Hebrew criterion for
       distinguishing manslaughter from murder: absence of prior hatred. NET's "without
       premeditation" is legally accurate but loses the relational dimension — the Hebrew
       tests the heart (hatred), not just the mind (premeditation).

v.6  — Hebrew "ad mot ha-kohen ha-gadol" — until the death of the high priest.
       KJV: "until the death of the high priest that shall be in those days"
       ESV: "until the death of him who is high priest at the time"
       ASV: "until he stand before the congregation for judgment, until the death of the high priest"
       NET: "until his case is decided by the assembly, and the high priest dies"
       WEB: "until he stands before the congregation for judgment, until the death of the high priest who shall be in those days"
       Analysis: ASV/WEB preserve both requirements: judicial standing before the
       congregation AND remaining until the high priest's death. The high priest's death
       served as a kind of atonement or epoch-marker that ended the manslayer's exile.
       Theologically, this points to Christ whose death as High Priest brings complete
       release from guilt.

v.7  — Hebrew "va-yaqdiShu" — they set apart/consecrated.
       KJV: "they appointed Kedesh in Galilee"
       ESV: "they set apart Kedesh in Galilee"
       ASV: "they set apart Kedesh in Galilee"
       NET: "they selected Kedesh in Galilee"
       WEB: "they designated Kedesh in Galilee"
       Analysis: Hebrew "qadash" (to consecrate/set apart) is significant: these cities
       were sanctified for a holy purpose. ASV/ESV's "set apart" preserves the
       sanctification concept; NET's "selected" loses it entirely. The older Canaanite
       name "Kirjath-arba" appears alongside "Hebron" — also Caleb's inheritance (14:13)
       and a Levitical city (21:13), showing cities could serve multiple functions.

v.8  — Hebrew "b'midbar b'mishor" — in the wilderness on the tableland.
       KJV: "Bezer in the wilderness upon the plain"
       ESV: "Bezer in the wilderness on the tableland"
       ASV: "Bezer in the wilderness in the plain"
       NET: "Bezer in the wilderness on the plateau"
       WEB: "Bezer in the wilderness, in the plain"
       Analysis: "Mishor" = level place, plateau, tableland. This is the Transjordan
       plateau east of the Dead Sea. All translations convey elevated flat terrain, with
       slight variation: KJV/ASV/WEB use "plain," ESV "tableland," NET "plateau."

v.9  — Hebrew "ger ha-gar b'tokham" — the stranger sojourning among them.
       KJV: "for all the children of Israel, and for the stranger that sojourneth among them"
       ESV: "for anyone of the people of Israel or any stranger sojourning among them"
       ASV: "for the stranger that sojourneth among them"
       NET: "for resident foreigners living among them"
       WEB: "for the foreigner who lives among them"
       Analysis: The cities of refuge were available to non-Israelites too. ASV's "stranger
       that sojourneth" translates "ger" (sojourner/resident alien). God's justice extends
       to all — foreshadowing the gospel's inclusion of Gentiles (cf. "whosoever will,"
       Revelation 22:17).

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Cities of Refuge — Six Levitical cities designated as sanctuaries for
               those who killed accidentally. Three west of Jordan
               (Kedesh, Shechem, Hebron) and three east (Golan, Ramoth,
               Bezer). They provided protection from blood vengeance
               until a fair trial could be held.

Avenger of Blood — Hebrew "go'el ha-dam." The nearest male relative of
               a murder victim, responsible for executing justice by
               killing the murderer. The cities of refuge protected
               accidental killers from this otherwise legitimate pursuit.

Go'el          — Kinsman-redeemer. The nearest relative with
               responsibility to redeem (buy back) family property,
               marry a childless widow, or avenge a relative's death.
               The same term used of Boaz (Ruth) and of God Himself
               (Isaiah 44:6; 47:4).

Manslayer      — One who kills another person accidentally, without
               premeditation or prior hatred. Distinguished from a
               murderer, who kills intentionally. The manslayer deserved
               protection; the murderer deserved death (Numbers 35:16-21).

High Priest    — The chief priest of Israel, descended from Aaron. His
               death marked the end of the manslayer's exile. Typologically,
               Christ is our High Priest whose death brings eternal
               release (Hebrews 9:11-15).

Kedesh         — "Holy place." A city in upper Galilee (Naphtali).
               Also the home of Barak (Judges 4:6).

Shechem        — A city in the central hill country between Mount Ebal
               and Mount Gerizim. Site of Abraham's first altar in
               Canaan (Genesis 12:6), Jacob's well (John 4), and
               Joshua's covenant renewal (Joshua 24).

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Christ is our city of refuge. As the manslayer fled to the city for
   protection from the avenger, so we flee to Christ for protection from
   God's just wrath against sin (Hebrews 6:18). The gates are always
   open. The refuge is always accessible. No one who enters is turned
   away.

2. The urgency of fleeing (vv.3-4). The manslayer ran — his life depended
   on reaching the city before the avenger caught him. There is urgency
   in coming to Christ. Tomorrow is not guaranteed. The avenger of
   justice pursues. Flee now.

3. The death of the High Priest sets free (v.6). The manslayer's exile
   ended when the high priest died. Christ our High Priest has died —
   and risen. We are not merely paroled but fully free. His death is our
   release. His resurrection is our guarantee.

4. Accessible to all (v.9). The cities were for Israelite AND stranger.
   God's mercy knows no ethnic boundary. Christ receives all who come —
   Jew and Gentile, insider and outsider. "Whosoever will may come."

5. Justice tempered with mercy. God does not ignore the shedding of blood
   — even accidental death has consequences (exile until the high
   priest's death). But He distinguishes between intent and accident,
   between murder and misfortune. God's justice is precise, not
   indiscriminate.

6. Strategic placement (vv.7-8). The six cities were distributed so no
   one was far from refuge. God ensures His mercy is always within reach.
   No matter where you are — north, south, east, west — refuge is near.
   "The LORD is nigh unto all them that call upon him" (Psalm 145:18).
