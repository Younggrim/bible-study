Joshua - Chapter 18
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
The tabernacle is set up at Shiloh, and Joshua rebukes seven tribes for being slow to possess their inheritance. Surveyors are sent out, and the remaining land is divided by lot before the LORD.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Shiloh: The tabernacle is moved here from Gilgal — in the hill country of Ephraim, central to all tribes
- Seven remaining tribes receive allotments by lot at Shiloh; surveyors map the land into seven portions
- Benjamin's allotment: A narrow strip between Judah (south) and Ephraim (north), including Jerusalem and Jericho

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "And the whole congregation of the children of Israel assembled
        together at Shiloh, and set up the tabernacle of the
        congregation there. And the land was subdued before them." (ESV) —         Shiloh becomes Israel's worship center. The tabernacle's
        placement signals permanence — the wandering is over. "The land
        was subdued" — the major battles are won.

v.3  — "How long are you slack to go to possess the land, which the LORD
        God of your fathers has given you?" (ESV) — Joshua's rebuke. "Slack"
        (Hebrew "raphah") = to be lax, idle, negligent. God has given
        the land; the battles are won; yet seven tribes sit idle. The
        problem is not God's provision but Israel's passivity.

v.6  — "That I may cast lots for you here before the LORD our God." (ESV) —         The lot ensures divine direction. This is not random chance but
        God's sovereign allocation (Proverbs 16:33). By casting lots
        "before the LORD," Joshua acknowledges that God determines each
        tribe's inheritance.

v.9  — "And the men went and passed through the land, and described it
        by cities into seven parts in a book." (ESV) — A written survey. This
        is one of the earliest references to a written land document in
        Israel. The surveyors create a systematic record — orderly,
        detailed, accountable. God's work is done decently and in order.

v.10 — "And Joshua cast lots for them in Shiloh before the LORD: and
        there Joshua divided the land to the children of Israel
        according to their divisions." (ESV) — The culmination: God's
        sovereignty (the lot) meets human responsibility (the survey).
        Both divine direction and practical planning work together.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/joshua-18/
  Guzik emphasizes Joshua's rebuke in v.3 — the problem was not that God
  had failed to provide but that Israel was too passive to possess. He
  draws the parallel to Christians who have every spiritual blessing in
  Christ (Ephesians 1:3) but fail to appropriate them through faith and
  action. Guzik also notes the significance of Shiloh as the worship
  center — it would remain so for over 300 years.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "'How long are ye slack?' This is a question for every idle Christian.
  God has given you promises — why do you not claim them? God has given
  you a calling — why do you not pursue it? God has given you gifts —
  why do you not use them? The land is before you, subdued and waiting.
  Rise up and possess it! Slackness in spiritual things is a grievous
  sin when God has already done the hard work of conquest."
TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1  — Hebrew "ohel mo'ed" — tent of meeting/appointment.
       KJV: "the tabernacle of the congregation"
       ESV: "the tent of meeting"
       ASV: "the tent of meeting"
       NET: "the tent of meeting"
       WEB: "the Tent of Meeting"
       Analysis: KJV's "tabernacle of the congregation" is a traditional
       but somewhat misleading rendering. "Ohel" = tent; "mo'ed" =
       appointed time/meeting. ESV/ASV/NET/WEB's "tent of meeting" is
       more literal — it was the place where God met with His people.

v.1  — Hebrew "nikb'shah ha-aretz lifneihem" — the land was subdued
       before them. "Kavash" = to subdue, bring into subjection.
       KJV: "the land was subdued before them"
       ESV: "The land lay subdued before them"
       ASV: "the land was subdued before them"
       NET: "the land was subdued before them"
       WEB: "the land was subdued before them"
       Analysis: The land's resistance has been broken — the major
       Canaanite coalitions are defeated. Individual pockets remain, but
       the land as a whole is under Israel's control. All versions render
       this similarly; ESV adds "lay" for literary effect.

v.3  — Hebrew "ad anah attem mitrapim" — "Raphah" (hitpael) = to show
       oneself slack, be negligent. Hebrew "yarash" = to possess.
       KJV: "How long are ye slack to go to possess the land"
       ESV: "How long will you put off going in to take possession of the land"
       ASV: "How long are ye slack to go in to possess the land, which Jehovah, the God of your fathers, hath given you?"
       NET: "How long do you intend to put off occupying the land the Lord God of your ancestors has given you?"
       WEB: "How long will you neglect to go in to possess the land, which Yahweh, the God of your fathers, has given you?"
       Analysis: KJV's "slack" captures the Hebrew well. ESV's "put off"
       interprets it as procrastination. Both convey culpable delay.
       NET's "put off occupying" is close to ASV/KJV's "slack to go in
       to possess." ASV's "possess" (yarash) carries stronger inheritance
       connotations than NET's "occupying." WEB's "neglect" is also
       strong.

v.4  — Hebrew "yikht'vu otah" — they shall write/describe it.
       KJV: "describe it according to the inheritance"
       ESV: "write a description of it with a view to their inheritances"
       ASV: "describe the land into seven portions"
       NET: "survey the land and describe it"
       WEB: "describe the land into seven portions"
       Analysis: The surveyors are to create a written record of the
       land's features and divisions. This is systematic, documented
       land allocation — remarkable administrative sophistication.

v.6  — Hebrew "yarah goral" — throw/cast the lot.
       KJV: "I will cast lots for you here before the LORD our God"
       ESV: "I will cast lots for you here before the LORD our God"
       ASV: "I will cast lots for you here before Jehovah our God"
       NET: "I will draw lots for you here before the Lord our God"
       WEB: "I will cast lots for you here before Yahweh our God"
       Analysis: NET uses "draw lots" where ASV/KJV/WEB use "cast lots."
       The Hebrew "yarah goral" (throw/cast the lot) implies active,
       decisive action in God's presence. The lot was a sacred means of
       divine guidance (Proverbs 16:33).

v.7  — Hebrew "k'hunnat YHWH nachalato" — the priesthood of the LORD
       is their inheritance. Hebrew "natan" — to give.
       KJV: "the priesthood of the LORD is their inheritance"
       ESV: "the priesthood of the LORD is their heritage"
       ASV: "the priesthood of Jehovah is their inheritance"
       NET: "their inheritance is to serve the Lord"
       WEB: "the priesthood of Yahweh is their inheritance"
       Analysis: KJV and ESV differ only in "inheritance" vs. "heritage"
       — both translate "nachalah." NET interprets "the priesthood of
       YHWH" as "to serve the Lord," losing the specific identification
       of the priestly office as the Levites' inheritance. The Hebrew
       emphasizes that God's service replaces territorial land as their
       portion. For Moses giving the Transjordan: NET uses "assigned"
       for "gave" (natan) — KJV/ASV/WEB's "gave" is more personal and
       generous.

v.9  — Hebrew "b'sepher" — in a book/scroll/document.
       KJV: "described it by cities into seven parts in a book"
       ESV: "wrote in a book a description of it by towns in seven divisions"
       ASV: "described it by cities into seven portions in a book"
       NET: "mapped it and its cities out into seven regions on a scroll"
       WEB: "described it by cities into seven portions in a book"
       Analysis: This written survey document is a remarkable detail —
       showing literacy and administrative sophistication in early Israel.
       NET's "scroll" is technically more precise than ASV's "book"
       (Hebrew "sepher" can mean scroll or written document). NET's
       "mapped" captures the cartographic nature of the work.

v.10 — Hebrew "machlekot" — tribal divisions.
       KJV: "Joshua divided the land unto the children of Israel according to their divisions"
       ESV: "there Joshua divided the land to the people of Israel, according to their divisions"
       ASV: "Joshua divided the land unto the children of Israel according to their divisions"
       NET: "divided the land among the Israelites according to their allotted portions"
       WEB: "Joshua divided the land to the children of Israel according to their divisions"
       Analysis: ASV/KJV/WEB preserve "divided" with "according to their
       divisions" (machlekot — tribal divisions), while NET uses the more
       generic "allotted portions." The culmination: God's sovereignty
       (the lot) meets human responsibility (the survey).

v.28 — Hebrew "Jebus" — the Canaanite name for Jerusalem.
       KJV: "Jebusi, which is Jerusalem"
       ESV: "Jebus (that is, Jerusalem)"
       ASV: "Jebus (the same is Jerusalem)"
       NET: "Jebus (that is, Jerusalem)"
       WEB: "Jebus (the same is Jerusalem)"
       Analysis: The Canaanite name for Jerusalem was "Jebus," inhabited
       by the Jebusites. It fell within Benjamin's allotment but was not
       conquered until David's time (2 Samuel 5:6-7). All versions
       identify the equivalence.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Shiloh         — A city in the hill country of Ephraim where the
               tabernacle was set up after the conquest. It served as
               Israel's central worship site for over 300 years (from
               Joshua to Samuel). Later destroyed, probably by the
               Philistines after capturing the ark (1 Samuel 4).
               Jeremiah uses its destruction as a warning (Jeremiah 7:12).

Tabernacle/Tent of Meeting — Hebrew "ohel mo'ed." The portable sanctuary
               built at Sinai (Exodus 25-40), housing the ark of the
               covenant. God's presence dwelt there between the cherubim.
               At Shiloh it became semi-permanent.

Slack (raphah) — To be lax, idle, negligent, to let one's hands drop.
               Joshua uses this word to rebuke Israel's passivity. The
               same root appears in Nehemiah 6:9 — "Their hands shall be
               weakened from the work."

Benjamin       — Youngest son of Jacob and Rachel. His tribe received a
               small but strategic territory between Judah and Ephraim,
               including Jerusalem and Jericho. Notable Benjamites: King
               Saul, the apostle Paul (Philippians 3:5).

The Lot        — Hebrew "goral." A method of determining God's will,
               likely involving marked stones drawn from a container.
               Used throughout the OT for divine guidance (Leviticus
               16:8; 1 Samuel 14:42; Proverbs 16:33). Superseded in
               the NT by the Holy Spirit's guidance (last biblical use:
               Acts 1:26).


PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "How long are ye slack?" (v.3). Joshua's rebuke applies to every
   believer who has received God's promises but fails to act on them.
   The land was given, the battles were won — yet seven tribes sat idle.
   What promises of God are we failing to possess? What spiritual
   territory lies unclaimed because of our passivity?

2. The tabernacle at Shiloh (v.1). Worship is established at the center
   of the land. Before the remaining tribes receive their inheritance,
   God's dwelling place is fixed. Priority: God's presence first, then
   personal provision. Seek first the kingdom (Matthew 6:33).

3. Orderly process (vv.4-10). Joshua combines divine sovereignty (the
   lot) with human responsibility (the survey). God directs, but His
   people must do the practical work. Faith does not eliminate planning —
   it sanctifies it. We pray AND we prepare.

4. Written records (v.9). The surveyors wrote their findings "in a book."
   God values documentation, precision, and accountability. The Bible
   itself is a written record. Our faith is not mystical vagueness but
   grounded in specific, recorded truth.

5. Benjamin's small but strategic territory (v.11). The smallest tribe
   gets the most strategic location — between Judah and Joseph, including
   Jerusalem. God does not measure significance by size. Small and
   faithful is better than large and unfaithful. God places His people
   where He needs them, regardless of their apparent stature.
