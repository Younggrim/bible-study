Joshua - Chapter 6
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Israel marches around Jericho for seven days, and on the seventh day the walls fall flat at the sound of trumpets and the people's shout. Rahab and her family are spared; the city is devoted to destruction.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Joshua, with possible later editorial additions. Joshua was Moses'
assistant and successor, who led Israel into the Promised Land. The book
covers approximately 25 years (1406-1381 BC) — from the crossing of the
Jordan to Joshua's death. Hebrew title: 'Yehoshua' — 'YHWH saves' (the
same name as Jesus in Greek). The book demonstrates God's faithfulness to
His land promise to Abraham (Genesis 15:18-21) and shows that victory
comes through faith and obedience, not human strength.

Historical Context: Chapter 6 records the fall of Jericho — one of the
most dramatic events in Scripture. Jericho was a heavily fortified city,
the gateway to Canaan, and the first obstacle to Israel's conquest. God's
battle plan is deliberately absurd by military standards: march silently
around the city for six days, then shout on the seventh. No siege engines,
no battering rams, no military strategy — only obedience to God's bizarre
instructions. The number seven dominates: 7 days, 7 priests, 7 trumpets,
7 circuits on the 7th day. Seven is the number of completion and divine
perfection. The walls fall "flat" (literally "under itself" — collapsing
straight down) by divine power alone. The city is placed under "cherem"
(the ban/devotion to destruction) — everything is destroyed or given to
God's treasury. Nothing is kept as plunder. Rahab and her family are
saved as promised. The chapter demonstrates that Israel's first victory
comes entirely by faith and obedience, not by human strength — setting
the pattern for the entire conquest.

Jericho Sealed and God's Promise (vv.1-5): Jericho is "straitly shut up"
— completely sealed in fear of Israel (v.1). God speaks: "See, I have
given into thine hand Jericho" (v.2) — past tense. The victory is already
accomplished in God's mind; Israel must simply obey to receive it. The
instructions: march around the city once daily for six days (v.3). Seven
priests with seven ram's horn trumpets go before the ark (v.4). On the
seventh day, march seven times, then at the long trumpet blast, all the
people shout — "and the wall of the city shall fall down flat" (v.5).

The March Begins (vv.6-14): Joshua organizes the procession: armed men
first, then seven priests with trumpets, then the ark, then the rear
guard (vv.6-9). Critical command: absolute silence — "Ye shall not shout,
nor make any noise with your voice" (v.10). Only the trumpets sound. For
six days they march once around the city in silence and return to camp
(vv.11-14). Imagine Jericho watching this strange, silent procession day
after day — the psychological pressure building.

The Seventh Day (vv.15-20): On the seventh day they rise at dawn and
march seven times around the city (v.15). At the seventh circuit, Joshua
commands: "Shout; for the LORD hath given you the city" (v.16). He
declares the city "accursed" (cherem — devoted to destruction) with the
exception of Rahab (v.17). Warning: do not take any devoted thing, or you
will bring a curse on Israel's camp (v.18). Silver, gold, bronze, and
iron go to God's treasury (v.19). The people shout, the trumpets blow —
"and the wall fell down flat, so that the people went up into the city,
every man straight before him" (v.20).

The Destruction and Rahab's Salvation (vv.21-25): Everything in the city
is utterly destroyed — "man and woman, young and old, and ox, and sheep,
and ass" (v.21). Joshua sends the two spies to rescue Rahab and her
family as sworn (vv.22-23). They are placed "without the camp of Israel"
(v.23) — outside temporarily, but Rahab "dwelleth in Israel even unto
this day" (v.25). The city is burned; metals go to God's treasury (v.24).

The Curse on Jericho (vv.26-27): Joshua pronounces a curse on anyone who
rebuilds Jericho: "he shall lay the foundation thereof in his firstborn,
and in his youngest son shall he set up the gates of it" (v.26). This
curse was fulfilled in 1 Kings 16:34 when Hiel of Bethel rebuilt Jericho
and lost his sons. "So the LORD was with Joshua; and his fame was noised
throughout all the country" (v.27).

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Jericho: Located in the Jordan Valley, about 800 feet below sea level — one of the lowest cities on earth
- The city was heavily fortified with double walls; strategically controlled access from the Jordan to the central highlands
- Rahab's house in the wall (north side) — she and her family survive in the section that did not collapse

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "See, I have given into your hand Jericho, and the king of it,
        and the mighty men of valour." (ESV) — Past tense: "I have given."
        The victory is already accomplished in God's sovereign plan.
        Israel's obedience does not earn the victory — it receives it.

v.5  — "The wall of the city shall fall down flat, and the people shall
        ascend up every man straight before him." (ESV) — No breach in one
        spot — the entire wall collapses simultaneously. Every soldier
        goes straight forward from wherever he stands. Total, miraculous
        destruction.

v.16 — "Shout; for the LORD has given you the city." (ESV) — The shout of
        faith. They shout BEFORE the walls fall — declaring God's victory
        before seeing it. Faith proclaims what God has promised before
        the evidence appears.

v.17 — "The city shall be accursed, even it, and all that are in it,
        to the LORD: only Rahab the harlot shall live." (ESV) — Total
        destruction with one exception: faith. In the midst of universal
        judgment, faith finds salvation. Rahab's scarlet cord saves her
        household.

v.20 — "The wall fell down flat." (ESV) — Hebrew "vatippol ha-chomah
        tachteha" — the wall fell "under itself," collapsing straight
        down. Archaeological evidence at Jericho confirms walls that
        collapsed outward and downward, creating ramps for attackers.

v.26 — "Cursed be the man before the LORD, that rises up and buildeth
        this city Jericho." (ESV) — Jericho is to remain a permanent monument
        to God's judgment. Rebuilding it defies God's verdict. The curse
        was fulfilled centuries later (1 Kings 16:34).

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/joshua-6/
  Guzik emphasizes that God's battle plan was designed to make clear that
  the victory belonged to Him alone. No military commander would devise
  such a strategy — it is foolishness by human standards but divine wisdom
  (1 Corinthians 1:25). The silence for six days followed by the shout
  pictures patient faith followed by triumphant proclamation. He also
  addresses the ethical question of the destruction: Jericho's judgment
  was the culmination of 400+ years of divine patience (Genesis 15:16).

Charles Spurgeon:
  "March around the walls in silence — what a battle plan! No general in
  history would propose it. But God's ways are not our ways. He chose the
  foolish thing to confound the wise. And when the shout came — oh, what
  a shout! The shout of faith that declares the victory before the walls
  have moved. Shout, believer! Shout before you see the answer! For the
  LORD has given you the city."
TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1  — KJV: "straitly shut up" / ESV: "shut up inside and outside" —
       Hebrew "sogereth um'suggereth." An intensive construction using the
       same root twice (active and passive participles): "shutting and
       shut" — completely, thoroughly sealed. No one in, no one out.
       Jericho was in total lockdown.

v.2  — KJV: "I have given into thine hand" / ESV: "I have given Jericho
       into your hand" — Hebrew "natatti b'yadekha." Perfect tense: "I
       have given" — already accomplished in God's decree. The victory is
       a fait accompli; Israel's obedience simply appropriates what God
       has already determined.

v.4  — KJV: "trumpets of rams' horns" / ESV: "trumpets of rams' horns" —
       Hebrew "shofrot ha-yov'lim." "Shofar" = ram's horn trumpet.
       "Yovel" = ram/jubilee. These are not silver trumpets (chatsotsrot)
       but primitive ram's horns — instruments of worship, not war. The
       battle is liturgical, not military.

v.17 — KJV: "the city shall be accursed" / ESV: "the city... shall be
       devoted to the LORD for destruction" — Hebrew "cherem." This is
       the ban of total destruction — everything is given to God either
       by destruction (living things) or by dedication to His treasury
       (metals). Nothing is kept for personal gain. ESV's "devoted to
       destruction" captures the theological meaning better than KJV's
       "accursed."

v.20 — KJV: "the wall fell down flat" / ESV: "the wall fell down flat" —
       Hebrew "vatippol ha-chomah tachteha." Literally "the wall fell
       under itself" — it collapsed in place, falling straight down (or
       outward). Archaeological excavations at Tell es-Sultan (ancient
       Jericho) by John Garstang and Kathleen Kenyon found evidence of
       collapsed walls.

v.25 — KJV: "she dwelleth in Israel even unto this day" / ESV: "she has
       lived in Israel to this day" — This editorial note indicates
       Rahab was still alive (or her family line still identifiable) when
       this was written. She married Salmon and became an Israelite
       (Matthew 1:5).

v.26 — KJV: "he shall lay the foundation thereof in his firstborn" /
       ESV: "At the cost of his firstborn shall he lay its foundation" —
       Hebrew "biv'khoro y'yasd'nah." "B'" = in/at the cost of. The
       builder's firstborn will die when the foundation is laid, and his
       youngest when the gates are set. This is not a sacrifice but a
       divine judgment — the rebuilder will lose his sons.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Cherem (Devoted to Destruction) — Hebrew "cherem." The total dedication
               of a conquered city to God through destruction. All living
               things are killed; precious metals go to God's treasury.
               Nothing is kept as personal plunder. Violation of cherem
               brings the curse upon the violator and all Israel (as
               Achan discovered in chapter 7).

Shofar (Ram's Horn) — The trumpet made from a ram's horn, used in
               worship, warfare, and proclamation. At Jericho, seven
               shofars blown by seven priests accompanied the march.
               The shofar is associated with God's presence (Exodus 19:16)
               and divine intervention.

Rereward/Rear Guard — The troops marching behind the ark, protecting the
               rear of the procession. The order: armed men, priests with
               trumpets, ark, rear guard.

Flat (tachteha) — Hebrew "under itself." The wall collapsed in place,
               falling straight down rather than being breached at one
               point. This allowed every soldier to go "straight before
               him" — attacking from all directions simultaneously.

Jericho        — The oldest known walled city in the world, located in
               the Jordan Valley near the Dead Sea. Called "the city of
               palm trees" (Deuteronomy 34:3). Its destruction was
               Israel's first conquest in Canaan — the gateway city that
               controlled access to the central highlands.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.2 — NET: "See, I am about to defeat Jericho for you" / KJV: "See, I have given into thine hand Jericho" / ASV: "See, I have given into thy hand Jericho" / WEB: "Behold, I have given Jericho into your hand" — NET changes past tense "have given" (Hebrew "natatti" — perfect tense) to future "am about to defeat," weakening the faith dimension. God speaks of the victory as already accomplished; NET makes it merely impending.
- v.17 — KJV: "the city shall be accursed" / ASV: "the city shall be devoted...to Jehovah" / WEB: "The city shall be devoted...to Yahweh" / NET: "The city and all that is in it must be set apart for the Lord" — The Hebrew "cherem" (devoted to destruction) is rendered differently: KJV "accursed," ASV/WEB "devoted," NET "set apart." NET's "set apart" sounds positive; the reality of cherem is total destruction as divine judgment. ASV/WEB's "devoted" better captures the dual sense of consecration and destruction.
- v.18 — KJV: "keep yourselves from the accursed thing" / ASV: "keep yourselves from the devoted thing" / WEB: "keep yourselves from what is devoted to destruction" / NET: "be careful when you are setting apart the riches for God" — NET paraphrases extensively, losing the warning tone. The Hebrew warns against taking from what is "cherem" (under the ban), not about properly "setting apart riches." WEB's "devoted to destruction" is the most transparent rendering.
- v.21 — NET: "They annihilated with the sword everything that breathed in the city" / KJV: "they utterly destroyed all that was in the city" / ASV: "they utterly destroyed all that was in the city" / WEB: "They utterly destroyed all that was in the city" — NET uses "annihilated" for the Hebrew "cherem" (hiphil — devoted to destruction). KJV/ASV/WEB use "utterly destroyed," which preserves the intensifying construction of the Hebrew (hacharimu... cherem).
- v.26 — NET: "The man who attempts to rebuild this city of Jericho will stand condemned before the Lord" / KJV: "Cursed be the man before the LORD, that riseth up and buildeth this city" / ASV: "Cursed be the man before Jehovah" / WEB: "Cursed is the man before Yahweh" — NET replaces the direct curse formula ("cursed be the man") with indirect language ("will stand condemned"), softening the prophetic solemnity of Joshua's oath. Fulfilled in 1 Kings 16:34.
- v.27 — ASV: "So Jehovah was with Joshua; and his fame was in all the land" / WEB: "So Yahweh was with Joshua; and his fame was in all the land" / NET: "The Lord was with Joshua, and he became famous throughout the land" — The divine name distinction persists. NET's "became famous" is more interpretive than "his fame was in all the land" (KJV/ASV/WEB), which states the result without making Joshua the agent of his own fame.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. God's methods confound human wisdom (vv.3-5). Marching and shouting
   is not a military strategy — it is an act of worship and obedience.
   God deliberately chose a method that could not be explained by human
   skill. When God calls you to something that makes no earthly sense,
   obey anyway. His foolishness is wiser than our wisdom (1 Corinthians
   1:25).

2. Silent obedience before the shout of faith (v.10). Six days of silence
   — no complaining, no questioning, no commentary. Just obedient,
   silent marching. Sometimes God calls us to seasons of quiet obedience
   before the breakthrough comes. Be faithful in the silent days.

3. The shout comes before the fall (v.16). "Shout; for the LORD hath
   given you the city" — present tense faith. They shout while the walls
   still stand. Faith declares God's victory before the evidence appears.
   Praise is not a response to victory — it is the weapon that brings it.

4. Cherem — everything belongs to God (vv.17-19). The firstfruits of
   conquest belong entirely to God. Israel cannot profit from Jericho —
   it all goes to Him. The first belongs to God: first city, first
   fruits, first of everything. When we give God the first, He blesses
   the rest.

5. Grace in the midst of judgment (vv.22-25). In a city under total
   destruction, one household is saved — by faith. Rahab's scarlet cord
   in the window saves her family while everything else perishes. Even in
   judgment, God remembers mercy. Even in wrath, faith finds refuge.

6. Do not touch the devoted thing (v.18). Joshua's warning is clear and
   specific — yet Achan will violate it in the very next chapter. The
   things God has declared off-limits are off-limits for our protection.
   Taking what belongs to God brings a curse, not a blessing.
