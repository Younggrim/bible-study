Joshua - Chapter 12
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A catalog of thirty-one kings defeated by Israel—first under Moses east of the Jordan, then under Joshua west of the Jordan. This list serves as a record of God's faithfulness in giving the land.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Joshua, with possible later editorial additions. Joshua was Moses'
assistant and successor, who led Israel into the Promised Land. The book
covers approximately 25 years (1406-1381 BC) — from the crossing of the
Jordan to Joshua's death. Hebrew title: 'Yehoshua' — 'YHWH saves' (the
same name as Jesus in Greek). The book demonstrates God's faithfulness to
His land promise to Abraham (Genesis 15:18-21) and shows that victory
comes through faith and obedience, not human strength.

Historical Context: Chapter 12 is a summary chapter — a catalog of all
the kings defeated by Israel, both under Moses (east of the Jordan) and
under Joshua (west of the Jordan). It serves as the conclusion to the
conquest narrative (chapters 1-12) and the transition to the land
distribution narrative (chapters 13-24). The list totals 31 kings defeated
west of the Jordan plus 2 kings east of the Jordan (Sihon and Og) — 33
kings in all. This chapter functions as a victory roll call, a permanent
record of God's faithfulness in fulfilling His promise. Each "one" after
a king's name is like a check mark — conquered, done, fulfilled. The
chapter demonstrates that the land of Canaan was not a unified kingdom
but a collection of independent city-states, each with its own king. God
defeated them all — not one stood against Israel when Israel walked in
obedience. The chapter also connects Moses' conquests (east) with
Joshua's conquests (west), showing continuity of God's purpose across
both leaders.

Kings Defeated East of the Jordan — Under Moses (vv.1-6): Two kings are
listed: (1) Sihon king of the Amorites, who ruled from Heshbon over the
territory from the Arnon River to the Jabbok River — half of Gilead
(vv.2-3). (2) Og king of Bashan, "of the remnant of the giants"
(Rephaim), who ruled from Ashtaroth and Edrei over Mount Hermon, Salcah,
and all Bashan (vv.4-5). Moses defeated them and gave their land to
Reuben, Gad, and half-Manasseh (v.6). These victories are recorded in
Numbers 21:21-35 and Deuteronomy 2-3.

Kings Defeated West of the Jordan — Under Joshua (vv.7-24): The territory:
from Baal-gad (north, under Mount Hermon) to Mount Halak (south, toward
Seir/Edom) (v.7). The terrain: hill country, lowland, Arabah, slopes,
wilderness, Negeb (v.8). The peoples: Hittites, Amorites, Canaanites,
Perizzites, Hivites, Jebusites (v.8). Then the list of 31 kings, each
marked with "one" — a tally of individual victories:

The list follows a roughly geographical/chronological order:
- Jericho and Ai (v.9) — the initial conquests (chapters 6-8)
- Jerusalem, Hebron, Jarmuth, Lachish, Eglon (vv.10-12) — the southern
  coalition of chapter 10
- Gezer, Debir, Geder, Hormah, Arad, Libnah, Adullam, Makkedah (vv.12-16)
  — southern campaign cities
- Bethel, Tappuah, Hepher, Aphek, Lasharon (vv.16-18) — central region
- Madon, Hazor, Shimron-meron, Achshaph (vv.19-20) — northern coalition
  of chapter 11
- Taanach, Megiddo, Kedesh, Jokneam, Dor, Goiim, Tirzah (vv.21-24) —
  additional northern and central conquests

Total: "all the kings thirty and one" (v.24).

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Summary of conquered territory: East of Jordan (from the Arnon gorge to Mount Hermon) and west of Jordan (from Mount Halak in the south to Baal-gad in the north)
- 31 kings defeated west of the Jordan — listed south to north, showing the scope of the conquest
- Two Transjordan kings (Sihon of Heshbon and Og of Bashan) defeated under Moses

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "Now these are the kings of the land, which the children of Israel
        smote, and possessed their land." (ESV) — The opening declaration: these
        are the defeated. God's people have conquered. The promise is
        being fulfilled.

v.6  — "Them did Moses the servant of the LORD and the children of Israel
        smite: and Moses the servant of the LORD gave it for a possession
        to the Reubenites, and the Gadites, and the half tribe of
        Manasseh." (ESV) — Moses' conquests are included — the work began before
        Joshua. God's purposes span multiple leaders and generations.

v.7  — "And these are the kings of the country which Joshua and the
        children of Israel smote on this side Jordan on the west... which
        Joshua gave to the tribes of Israel for a possession according
        to their divisions." (ESV) — Joshua's conquests. The land is not just
        conquered but distributed — it becomes Israel's inheritance.

v.24 — "All the kings thirty and one." (ESV) — The final tally. Thirty-one
        kings defeated. Thirty-one kingdoms conquered. Thirty-one
        victories won. Each "one" is a testimony to God's faithfulness.
        Not one enemy stood when God fought for Israel.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/joshua-12/
  Guzik notes that this chapter is like a trophy room — each king listed
  is a monument to God's faithfulness. The repetition of "one... one...
  one" emphasizes individual victories. He also observes that some cities
  listed here (like Jerusalem, Gezer, Taanach, Megiddo) were not fully
  possessed until later — the kings were defeated but the cities not
  permanently occupied until David's time or later.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "Count your victories! Number your conquered foes! Let the roll call of
  defeated kings remind you of what God has done. Thirty-one kings — and
  not one of them could stand before Israel when Israel walked with God.
  Make your own list, believer. Write down the enemies God has conquered
  in your life. The sins overcome, the fears defeated, the obstacles
  removed. Count them — and give glory to the God who fights for you."
TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1  — KJV: "on the other side Jordan toward the rising of the sun" /
       ESV: "beyond the Jordan toward the sunrise" — Hebrew "b'ever
       ha-Yarden mizrach ha-shemesh." "Ever" = the other side, beyond.
       "Mizrach" = rising, east. "Shemesh" = sun. The poetic "toward the
       rising of the sun" = east of the Jordan.

v.4  — KJV: "of the remnant of the giants" / ESV: "one of the remnant of
       the Rephaim" — Hebrew "min yeter ha-Repha'im." "Repha'im" = an
       ancient race of giants. KJV translates the term; ESV transliterates.
       Og was one of the last survivors of this ancient giant race. His
       iron bed was 13.5 feet long (Deuteronomy 3:11).

v.7  — KJV: "on this side Jordan on the west" / ESV: "on the west side
       of the Jordan" — Hebrew "b'ever ha-Yarden yamah." "Yamah" = toward
       the sea = westward. The perspective is from east of the Jordan
       (where the narrative began).

v.8  — KJV: "in the springs" / ESV: "in the slopes" — Hebrew
       "ba-ashedot." "Ashedot" = slopes, descents (the eastern slopes
       descending toward the Jordan Valley and Dead Sea). KJV's "springs"
       is a less likely translation; ESV's "slopes" better fits the
       geographical context.

v.9-24 — KJV: "one" / ESV: "one" — Hebrew "echad" after each king. This
       counting word functions as a tally mark — "one [king]." The
       repetition creates a rhythmic, cumulative effect: king after king
       after king, all defeated. The total: thirty-one.

v.23 — KJV: "the king of the nations of Gilgal" / ESV: "the king of
       Goiim in Galilee" — Hebrew "melekh goyim l'Gilgal." "Goyim" =
       nations/peoples. This is debated: KJV reads "Gilgal" (a place);
       ESV reads "Galilee" (following some manuscripts). "Goyim" may be
       a place name or may mean "king of the nations" (a mixed-population
       area). The exact identification is uncertain.

v.24 — KJV: "all the kings thirty and one" / ESV: "in all, thirty-one
       kings" — Hebrew "kol m'lakhim sh'loshim v'echad." The grand total.
       Thirty-one city-state kings defeated by Joshua west of the Jordan.
       Each one a testimony to God's promise fulfilled.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Sihon          — King of the Amorites, ruling from Heshbon. Defeated by
               Moses east of the Jordan (Numbers 21:21-30). His territory
               was given to Reuben and Gad.

Og             — King of Bashan, "of the remnant of the giants/Rephaim."
               Defeated by Moses (Numbers 21:33-35; Deuteronomy 3:1-11).
               His iron bed was 9 cubits long (about 13.5 feet). His
               territory was given to half-Manasseh.

Rephaim        — An ancient race of giants in Canaan. Og was one of their
               last survivors. Related to the Anakim. Their name may mean
               "terrible ones" or be connected to the shades of the dead.

City-State     — The political structure of Canaan: each major city had
               its own king ruling a small surrounding territory. There
               was no unified Canaanite kingdom — only independent city-
               states, sometimes forming temporary coalitions.

Thirty-One Kings — The total number of kings defeated by Joshua west of
               the Jordan. This number represents the complete conquest
               of Canaan's political structure — every independent ruler
               was overthrown.

Arabah         — The rift valley running from the Sea of Galilee south
               through the Jordan Valley to the Dead Sea and beyond. A
               major geographical feature of the land.

Sea of Chinneroth — The Sea of Galilee (also called Gennesaret, Tiberias).
               "Chinneroth" may mean "harp-shaped" — describing the
               lake's outline.

Salt Sea       — The Dead Sea (also called the Sea of the Arabah). The
               lowest point on earth, extremely salty, at the southern
               end of the Jordan Valley.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.1 — ASV: "smote, and possessed their land beyond the Jordan toward the sunrising" / WEB: "struck, and possessed their land beyond the Jordan toward the sunrise" / NET: "defeated and drove from their land on the east side of the Jordan" — KJV/ASV/WEB's "possessed their land" preserves the inheritance language (yarash — to possess/inherit). NET's "drove from their land" focuses on military displacement rather than the theological concept of possession.
- v.1 — ASV: "the Arabah" / WEB: "the Arabah" / NET: "the eastern rift valley" — NET consistently interprets geographic names rather than preserving them. "Arabah" is the Hebrew name for the Jordan Rift; NET's "rift valley" is a geological description that loses the biblical place name.
- v.4 — ASV: "the remnant of the Rephaim" / WEB: "the remnant of the Rephaim" / NET: "one of the few remaining Rephaites" — NET rephrases "she'erit" (remnant) as "one of the few remaining," weakening the concept of a nearly-extinct race of giants. KJV/ASV/WEB preserve the dramatic "remnant" language.
- v.6 — ASV: "Moses the servant of Jehovah" / WEB: "Moses the servant of Yahweh" / NET: "Moses the Lord's servant" — The honorific title recurs throughout this chapter. The divine name usage (Jehovah/Yahweh vs. "the Lord") emphasizes that Moses served the personal, covenant God of Israel.
- v.7 — ASV: "gave it unto the tribes of Israel for a possession" / WEB: "gave it to the tribes of Israel for a possession" / NET: "assigned this territory to the Israelite tribes" — NET uses "assigned territory" (administrative language) where KJV/ASV/WEB use "possession" (inheritance language). The Hebrew "y'rushah" (possession/inheritance) carries the weight of God's covenant promise.
- v.23 — ASV: "the king of Goiim in Gilgal" / WEB: "the king of Goiim in Gilgal" / NET: "the king of Goyim—near Gilgal" — "Goiim/Goyim" means "nations" in Hebrew. ASV/WEB preserve the Hebrew word; NET adds a dash and "near" for clarity. This may refer to a coalition king of various nations rather than a specific city.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Count your victories (vv.9-24). This chapter is a victory catalog —
   a permanent record of what God has done. Do you keep a record of God's
   faithfulness? Write down the enemies He has defeated, the prayers He
   has answered, the obstacles He has removed. Remembering past victories
   builds faith for future battles.

2. Every king falls (v.24). Thirty-one kings — not one could stand. When
   God fights for His people, no enemy survives. Whatever "kings" oppose
   you — fear, sin, circumstance, opposition — they will fall. "If God be
   for us, who can be against us?" (Romans 8:31).

3. Continuity of God's purpose (vv.1-7). Moses defeated Sihon and Og;
   Joshua defeated 31 kings. Different leaders, same God, same purpose.
   God's work does not depend on one person. Leaders change; God's
   faithfulness continues across generations.

4. The land is distributed (v.7). Conquest leads to inheritance. The
   battles are not an end in themselves — they secure the inheritance for
   God's people. Our spiritual battles are not pointless suffering; they
   secure the promises God has given us.

5. Canaan was divided (vv.9-24). Thirty-one independent kings — no unity
   among them (except temporary coalitions). The enemy is divided; God's
   people are united. Division weakens; unity strengthens. "A house
   divided against itself shall not stand" (Matthew 12:25).

6. The record stands (v.24). This chapter is written for all time — a
   permanent testimony that God keeps His promises. The kings are named,
   counted, and recorded. God's victories are not forgotten. Your
   testimony of God's faithfulness matters — write it down for future
   generations.
