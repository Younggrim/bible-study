Leviticus - Chapter 20
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
God prescribes penalties for idolatry, occult practices, and sexual sins — holiness has consequences, and God separates His people from wickedness.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses. Leviticus is the third book of the Pentateuch, written during
the wilderness period (approximately 1445-1405 BC). Hebrew title: "Vayikra"
— "And He called." God speaks from the newly completed Tabernacle, giving
Israel instructions for worship, sacrifice, and holy living.

Historical Context: Chapter 20 is the companion to chapter 18. Where
chapter 18 lists the prohibited sexual acts, chapter 20 prescribes the
PENALTIES for those acts. The two chapters form a pair: prohibition (ch.18)
and punishment (ch.20), with chapter 19 (practical holiness) sandwiched
between them. The chapter also addresses penalties for Molech worship
(vv.1-5) and consulting mediums (v.6), which were mentioned in chapter 19.
The structure is deliberate: the most severe penalties (death) come first,
followed by lesser penalties ("cut off," childlessness). The chapter opens
and closes with calls to holiness (vv.7-8, 26) — the penalties are not
arbitrary cruelty but the necessary enforcement of holiness in a covenant
community. God is forming a nation that will be distinct from all others:
"I the LORD am holy, and have severed you from other people, that ye
should be mine" (v.26). The severity of the penalties reflects the
seriousness of the sins — these are not minor infractions but acts that
destroy families, defile the land, and profane God's name.

Molech Worship — Death Penalty (vv.1-5): Anyone who gives his children to
Molech "shall surely be put to death" — stoned by the people (v.2). God
Himself will "set my face against that man" and cut him off (v.3). The
reasons: it defiles God's sanctuary and profanes His holy name (v.3). If
the community ignores it ("hide their eyes"), God will act against the man,
his family, and all who follow him in this idolatry (vv.4-5). Communal
responsibility: tolerating evil makes the community complicit.

Mediums and Necromancers (v.6): Anyone who turns to mediums or spiritists
— God will "set my face against that soul" and cut him off. Seeking
guidance from the dead or from spirits is spiritual adultery ("whoring
after them").

The Call to Holiness (vv.7-8): "Sanctify yourselves therefore, and be ye
holy: for I am the LORD your God" (v.7). "Ye shall keep my statutes, and
do them: I am the LORD which sanctify you" (v.8). God both commands
holiness AND provides it — "I am the LORD which sanctify you." Holiness
is both human responsibility and divine gift.

Cursing Parents — Death (v.9): "Every one that curseth his father or his
mother shall be surely put to death" (v.9). "His blood shall be upon him"
— he bears responsibility for his own death. The family is the foundation
of society; attacking it at its root (dishonoring parents) is a capital
offense.

Adultery — Death (v.10): Both adulterer and adulteress "shall surely be
put to death." The marriage covenant is sacred; its violation is treated
with the same severity as murder.

Incest and Sexual Perversion — Death (vv.11-16): Father's wife (v.11) —
death. Daughter-in-law (v.12) — death; "they have wrought confusion."
Homosexuality (v.13) — death; "they have committed an abomination." A man
taking a woman and her mother (v.14) — burned with fire; "it is
wickedness." Bestiality — man (v.15) or woman (v.16) — death for both
person and animal.

Lesser Penalties — Cut Off / Childlessness (vv.17-21): Sister (v.17) —
"cut off in the sight of their people." During menstruation (v.18) — both
"cut off from among their people." Mother's or father's sister (v.19) —
"they shall bear their iniquity." Uncle's wife (v.20) — "they shall die
childless." Brother's wife (v.21) — "they shall be childless." The
graduated penalties show degrees of severity — but all are serious.

The Closing Exhortation (vv.22-27): "Keep all my statutes... that the
land... spue you not out" (v.22). Do not walk in the customs of the
nations being driven out — "I abhorred them" (v.23). "I have said unto
you, Ye shall inherit their land" — a land flowing with milk and honey
(v.24). "I am the LORD your God, which have separated you from other
people" (v.24). Distinguish clean from unclean (v.25). THE CLIMAX: "And
ye shall be holy unto me: for I the LORD am holy, and have severed you
from other people, that ye should be mine" (v.26). Mediums and
necromancers — stoned to death (v.27). The chapter ends where it began:
with the death penalty for spiritual rebellion.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.3  — "I will set my face against that man, and will cut him off from
        among his people; because he hath given of his seed unto Molech,
        to defile my sanctuary, and to profane my holy name." — God
        personally opposes those who sacrifice children.

v.7  — "Sanctify yourselves therefore, and be ye holy: for I am the LORD
        your God." — The call to holiness amid the penalties for sin.

v.8  — "I am the LORD which sanctify you." — God both commands holiness
        and provides it. Sanctification is His work.

v.9  — "Every one that curseth his father or his mother shall be surely
        put to death." — The family is sacred. Dishonoring parents is a
        capital offense.

v.23 — "You shall not walk in the customs of the nation that I am driving
        out before you, for they did all these things, and therefore I
        detested them." (ESV) — The Canaanites are judged for these very
        sins.

v.26 — "You shall be holy to me, for I the LORD am holy and have separated
        you from the peoples, that you should be mine." (ESV) — The
        purpose of separation: belonging to God.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/leviticus-20/
  Guzik notes the relationship between chapters 18 and 20 (prohibition
  and penalty) and emphasizes that the severity of penalties reflects God's
  view of these sins, not human cruelty. He highlights v.26 as the key:
  separation is for the purpose of BELONGING — "that ye should be mine."

Charles Spurgeon:
  Sermon: "A Peculiar People"
  "God says: I have SEVERED you from other people, that ye should be MINE.
  There is the whole of it — separation FOR possession. God does not
  separate His people merely to make them different; He separates them to
  make them HIS. You are not merely called out of the world; you are called
  INTO God's own family. The holiness He demands is not cold isolation but
  warm belonging. 'That ye should be mine' — this is the sweetest word in
  all of Leviticus. You are His peculiar treasure, His special possession,
  His beloved people. And because you are His, you must be holy — for He
  is holy. A king's child bears the king's character."

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.2  — KJV: "giveth any of his seed unto Molech" / ESV: "gives any of his
       children to Molech" — Hebrew "yitten mi-zar'o la-Molekh" — gives
       from his seed to Molech. KJV's "seed" is literal; ESV's "children"
       is interpretive but correct. "Seed" (zera) here means offspring.

v.3  — KJV: "to defile my sanctuary" / ESV: "to make my sanctuary unclean"
       — Hebrew "l'tamme et miqdashi." Both convey the same meaning. Child
       sacrifice defiles God's sanctuary — even though it occurs outside
       the sanctuary. Sin committed in the community pollutes the place
       where God dwells.

v.6  — KJV: "familiar spirits... wizards" / ESV: "mediums and
       necromancers" — Hebrew "ha-ovot v'ha-yid'onim." "Ov" = a spirit of
       the dead, or one who channels such spirits (a medium). "Yid'oni" =
       a knowing one, a spiritist (one who claims supernatural knowledge).
       KJV's "familiar spirits" refers to the spirit that is "familiar"
       (known/attached) to the medium. ESV uses modern terminology.

v.9  — KJV: "curseth his father or his mother" / ESV: "curses his father
       or his mother" — Hebrew "y'qallel et aviv v'et immo." "Qalal" = to
       make light of, to curse, to treat with contempt. The opposite of
       "kavad" (to honor/make heavy). Not merely profanity but fundamental
       disrespect — treating parents as worthless.

v.12 — KJV: "they have wrought confusion" / ESV: "they have committed
       perversion" — Hebrew "tevel asu" — they have done confusion/
       perversion. Same word as 18:23 (bestiality). Implies a violation of
       created order — mixing what God has distinguished.

v.14 — KJV: "it is wickedness" / ESV: "it is depravity" — Hebrew "zimmah
       hi" — it is a scheme/plan/heinous sin. Implies calculated,
       deliberate wickedness — not a moment of weakness but a planned
       perversion.

v.17 — KJV: "it is a wicked thing" / ESV: "it is a disgrace" — Hebrew
       "chesed hu" — here used in its negative sense: a shameful thing, a
       disgrace. (The same word "chesed" usually means lovingkindness —
       but in this rare usage it means shame/reproach. Context determines
       meaning.)

v.26 — KJV: "have severed you from other people" / ESV: "have separated
       you from the peoples" — Hebrew "va-avdil etkhem min ha-ammim" —
       and I have divided/separated you from the peoples. "Avdil" = to
       divide, separate (same root as Genesis 1 — God "divided" light from
       darkness). God's separation of Israel mirrors His creative work of
       distinction.

v.27 — KJV: "a familiar spirit, or that is a wizard" / ESV: "a medium or
       a necromancer" — Same terms as v.6. The chapter ends where it began
       — with the death penalty for occult practices. Bookend structure.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
"His blood shall be upon him" — A legal formula meaning the executed person
               bears responsibility for his own death. He brought it upon
               himself by his actions. The community is not guilty of
               murder in executing him — his blood is on his own head.

Molech worship  — Child sacrifice to the Ammonite/Canaanite deity Molech.
               Children were burned alive in the arms of a heated metal
               idol or passed through fire. The most extreme form of
               idolatry — offering what is most precious (children) to
               what is most evil (a demon). God's response is the most
               severe: death penalty + divine opposition ("I will set my
               face against").

"Set my face against" — An expression of personal divine opposition. God
               Himself becomes the enemy of the one who commits these sins.
               Not merely legal punishment but personal hostility from the
               Almighty. Used for Molech worship (v.3), consulting mediums
               (v.6), and tolerating Molech worship (v.5).

"Cut off" (Karat) — Separation from the covenant community. May involve
               death by God's hand, excommunication, or extinction of
               family line. Less severe than execution by the community
               but still devastating — loss of covenant membership and
               its blessings.

Childlessness  — The penalty for certain sexual sins (vv.20-21). In
               ancient Israel, childlessness was the extinction of one's
               name and legacy. It meant no inheritance in the Promised
               Land, no continuation of the family line, no one to carry
               on your memory. A living death.

"That ye should be mine" — The purpose of separation. God does not
               separate His people merely to make them different but to
               make them His own possession. Holiness is not cold isolation
               but intimate belonging. Separation FROM the world is
               separation TO God.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Sanctify yourselves... I am the LORD which sanctify you" (vv.7-8).
   Both are true simultaneously. YOU must sanctify yourself (human
   responsibility). GOD sanctifies you (divine enablement). Holiness is
   not passive — you must pursue it. But it is not merely human effort —
   God empowers it. Work out your salvation, for God works in you
   (Philippians 2:12-13).

2. "If the people of the land do any ways hide their eyes" (v.4). God
   holds the community responsible for tolerating evil. Looking away is
   not neutrality — it is complicity. When you see sin and say nothing,
   do nothing, you share in the guilt. Are you "hiding your eyes" from
   evil in your family, church, or community?

3. "They committed all these things, and therefore I abhorred them" (v.23).
   The Canaanites were not expelled for their ethnicity but for their
   behavior. God's judgment is moral, not racial. And the same standard
   applies to Israel — and to any nation. No people group is exempt from
   moral accountability before God.

4. "I the LORD am holy, and have severed you from other people, that ye
   should be mine" (v.26). The purpose of holiness is BELONGING. You are
   set apart not for isolation but for intimacy with God. "That ye should
   be mine" — this is the sweetest motivation for holiness. You are His
   treasured possession. Live like it.

5. "Such were some of you: but ye are washed" (1 Corinthians 6:11). The
   penalties in this chapter are severe — but the gospel offers cleansing
   even from these sins. No one is beyond redemption. The blood of Christ
   is sufficient for every sin listed here. The question is not "how bad
   is my sin?" but "how great is His grace?"
