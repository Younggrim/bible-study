Leviticus - Chapter 17
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
God commands that all sacrifices be brought to the Tabernacle and strictly forbids eating blood — 'the life of the flesh is in the blood.'

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses. Leviticus is the third book of the Pentateuch, written during
the wilderness period (approximately 1445-1405 BC). Hebrew title: "Vayikra"
— "And He called." God speaks from the newly completed Tabernacle, giving
Israel instructions for worship, sacrifice, and holy living.

Historical Context: Chapter 17 is a pivotal transition chapter. It closes
the first half of Leviticus (sacrifice and cleanness, chs.1-16) and opens
the second half (the Holiness Code, chs.17-26). Some scholars place it
with the first half (as the conclusion of sacrificial law); others with
the second half (as the foundation of holy living). In truth, it bridges
both — it establishes the theological principle that undergirds everything:
"the life of the flesh is in the blood" (v.11). This single verse is the
theological foundation of all biblical sacrifice, from Abel's lamb to
Calvary. The chapter addresses three issues: (1) all slaughter of
sacrificial animals must occur at the Tabernacle (vv.1-9); (2) the
absolute prohibition of eating blood (vv.10-14); and (3) regulations for
eating animals found dead or torn (vv.15-16). The first regulation
prevents idolatrous sacrifice "unto devils" (v.7) — Israel had been
sacrificing to goat-demons in the open field (a practice learned in Egypt).
The second regulation protects the sanctity of blood as God's appointed
means of atonement. Blood is not food — it is sacred, reserved for the
altar.

Centralized Slaughter at the Tabernacle (vv.1-9): God speaks to Moses for
Aaron, his sons, and ALL Israel (vv.1-2). Any Israelite who kills an ox,
lamb, or goat — whether in the camp or outside it — and does NOT bring it
to the Tabernacle entrance as an offering to the LORD: "blood shall be
imputed unto that man; he hath shed blood" (v.4). He is "cut off from
among his people" (v.4). The purpose: that Israel may bring their
sacrifices to the LORD rather than offering them "in the open field" (v.5).
They are to be peace offerings at the Tabernacle (v.5). The priest
sprinkles blood on the altar and burns fat (v.6). The reason revealed:
"they shall no more offer their sacrifices unto devils, after whom they
have gone a whoring" (v.7). Hebrew "se'irim" — goat-demons or satyrs.
Israel had been practicing idolatrous sacrifice in the open fields. This
law centralizes all sacrifice at God's altar, eliminating the opportunity
for syncretism. The same applies to burnt offerings (vv.8-9): anyone —
Israelite or stranger — who offers sacrifice and does not bring it to the
Tabernacle is cut off.

The Prohibition of Eating Blood (vv.10-14): "Whatsoever man... that eateth
any manner of blood; I will even set my face against that soul" (v.10).
God Himself opposes the one who eats blood. The penalty: "cut off from
among his people" (v.10). The reason — verse 11, the theological heart of
the chapter and one of the most important verses in Leviticus: "For the
life of the flesh is in the blood: and I have given it to you upon the
altar to make an atonement for your souls: for it is the blood that maketh
an atonement for the soul." Three truths: (1) Blood = life. (2) God gave
blood for the altar — it is His gift, not man's invention. (3) Blood
makes atonement — life given in place of life. Therefore no one may eat
blood (v.12) — neither Israelite nor stranger. Even hunted game: the blood
must be poured out and covered with earth (v.13). "For it is the life of
all flesh; the blood of it is for the life thereof" (v.14). Blood is
sacred because it represents life, and life belongs to God alone.

Animals Found Dead or Torn (vv.15-16): Anyone who eats an animal that died
naturally or was killed by predators must wash clothes, bathe, and be
unclean until evening (v.15). This applies to native and stranger alike.
If he does not wash, "he shall bear his iniquity" (v.16). Such animals
were not properly slaughtered — their blood was not drained — hence the
uncleanness.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.4  — "Bloodguilt shall be imputed to that man. He has shed blood, and
        that man shall be cut off from among his people." (ESV) —
        Unauthorized slaughter is treated as bloodguilt. All sacrifice
        belongs to God.

v.7  — "So they shall no more sacrifice their sacrifices to goat demons,
        after whom they whore." (ESV) — The reason for centralized
        sacrifice: to prevent idolatry.

v.11 — "For the life of the flesh is in the blood, and I have given it for
        you on the altar to make atonement for your souls, for it is the
        blood that makes atonement by the life." (ESV) — THE key verse.
        The theological foundation of all sacrifice. Life is in the blood.
        Blood atones. God gave it.

v.14 — "For the life of every creature is its blood: its blood is its
        life." (ESV) — Blood = life. Life belongs to God. Therefore blood
        is sacred and must not be consumed.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/leviticus-17/
  Guzik highlights verse 11 as one of the most important theological
  statements in the Old Testament. He notes three truths: blood carries
  life, God appointed blood for atonement (it is His idea, not man's),
  and blood makes atonement by the principle of substitutionary life.
  He connects this directly to Christ's blood in the New Testament.

Charles Spurgeon:
  Sermon: "The Blood"
  "The life of the flesh is in the blood — this is God's own declaration.
  And He has GIVEN it to you upon the altar. Mark that — GIVEN. Atonement
  is not man's invention; it is God's gift. Man did not devise the plan of
  salvation by blood; God appointed it. From the first lamb slain in Eden
  to cover Adam's nakedness, to the Lamb slain from the foundation of the
  world, it is all God's doing. And what does the blood do? It makes
  ATONEMENT for the soul. Not for the body merely, not for outward
  behavior, but for the SOUL — the deepest part of man. Nothing else can
  reach the soul. Education cannot. Morality cannot. Religion cannot. Only
  blood — only life given for life — only the death of the Innocent in
  place of the guilty. This is the scarlet thread that runs from Genesis
  to Revelation."

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.4  — KJV: "blood shall be imputed unto that man" / ESV: "bloodguilt
       shall be imputed to that man" — Hebrew "dam ye'chashev la-ish
       ha-hu" — blood shall be reckoned to that man. ESV's "bloodguilt"
       makes the legal implication explicit. The man is charged with
       murder — treating an animal's life carelessly is treated as
       shedding blood.

v.6  — KJV: "a sweet savour unto the LORD" / ESV: "a pleasing aroma to
       the LORD" — Hebrew "re'ach nichoach la-YHWH." KJV's "sweet savour"
       is traditional; ESV's "pleasing aroma" is more natural in modern
       English. The anthropomorphism: God is pleased by proper sacrifice
       as one is pleased by a good aroma.

v.7  — KJV: "unto devils" / ESV: "to goat demons" — Hebrew "la-se'irim"
       — to the hairy ones / goat-demons / satyrs. KJV generalizes to
       "devils"; ESV translates more literally. These were goat-shaped
       demonic entities worshiped in the open fields — a practice Israel
       picked up in Egypt. The word appears also in Isaiah 13:21; 34:14
       and 2 Chronicles 11:15.

v.11 — KJV: "For the life of the flesh is in the blood" / ESV: "For the
       life of the flesh is in the blood" — Hebrew "ki nefesh ha-basar
       ba-dam hi" — for the soul/life of the flesh, in the blood it [is].
       Both translations are identical here. "Nefesh" = soul, life, being.
       The life-principle resides in the blood.

v.11 — KJV: "for it is the blood that maketh an atonement for the soul" /
       ESV: "for it is the blood that makes atonement by the life" —
       Hebrew "ki ha-dam hu ba-nefesh y'khapper." A crucial difference:
       KJV reads "atonement FOR the soul" (blood atones on behalf of the
       soul); ESV reads "atonement BY the life" (blood atones by means of
       the life in it). The Hebrew allows both readings. The preposition
       "ba" can mean "for" or "by means of." Both are theologically true:
       blood atones BY the life it contains, and it atones FOR the life
       of the sinner.

v.13 — KJV: "cover it with dust" / ESV: "cover it with earth" — Hebrew
       "v'khissahu be-afar" — cover it with dust/earth. Both are accurate.
       The blood must be covered — it is sacred, not to be left exposed
       or trampled. Even in hunting (non-sacrificial killing), blood is
       treated with reverence.

v.15 — KJV: "that which died of itself, or that which was torn with
       beasts" / ESV: "what dies of itself or what is torn by beasts" —
       Hebrew "n'velah u-t'refah" — a carcass and a torn [animal]. These
       technical terms become important in later Jewish dietary law:
       "n'velah" = an animal not properly slaughtered; "t'refah" = an
       animal killed by predators. Both are unfit because the blood was
       not properly drained.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
"The life is in the blood" — The foundational principle of biblical
               sacrifice. Blood represents life itself — not merely a
               symbol but the actual vehicle of life. When blood is shed,
               life is given. Atonement requires life for life: the
               innocent dies in place of the guilty. This principle runs
               from Genesis 3 (animals slain to cover Adam and Eve) to
               Revelation 5 (the Lamb slain from the foundation of the
               world).

Se'irim (Goat-demons) — Hebrew "hairy ones." Demonic entities associated
               with goats, worshiped in open fields. Israel had adopted
               this practice in Egypt. The centralization of sacrifice at
               the Tabernacle eliminates the opportunity for this
               idolatry. Related to the "satyrs" of Isaiah 13:21; 34:14.

Atonement (Kaphar) — To cover, to make reconciliation, to ransom. Blood
               "makes atonement" — it covers sin, reconciles the sinner
               to God, and ransoms the guilty life with an innocent life.
               The word is related to "kapporet" (mercy seat) — the place
               where atonement is made.

"Cut off" (Karat) — The penalty for eating blood or unauthorized sacrifice.
               Separation from God's people — whether by divine judgment,
               excommunication, or death. The severity shows how seriously
               God takes the sanctity of blood.

Bloodguilt     — The legal charge of having shed blood unlawfully. In v.4,
               even killing an animal outside the Tabernacle is treated as
               bloodguilt — because all life belongs to God and all
               slaughter of sacrificial animals must be offered to Him.

"Given it to you upon the altar" — Atonement is God's gift, not man's
               invention. God GAVE blood as the means of atonement. The
               sacrificial system is not human religion reaching up to God
               but God reaching down to provide the way of reconciliation.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "The life of the flesh is in the blood" (v.11). This is the foundation
   of everything. Without this truth, the cross makes no sense. Christ's
   death is not merely an example or a moral lesson — it is LIFE GIVEN
   for life. His blood = His life, poured out as a substitute for yours.
   Do you understand the cross in these terms?

2. "I have GIVEN it to you upon the altar" (v.11). Atonement is God's
   idea, God's gift, God's provision. You did not invent the way of
   salvation. You did not earn it. God gave it. The entire sacrificial
   system — and its fulfillment in Christ — is grace from start to finish.

3. "They shall no more offer their sacrifices unto devils" (v.7). Israel
   was sacrificing to goat-demons in the open field — syncretism, mixing
   worship of God with worship of demons. Are there areas of your life
   where you offer your devotion, time, or energy to things that are not
   God while claiming to worship Him? Centralize your worship. Bring
   everything to the LORD's altar.

4. Blood must not be eaten (vv.10-14). Blood is sacred — it represents
   life, and life belongs to God. This teaches reverence for life itself.
   In a culture that cheapens life, the Bible insists that life is sacred,
   blood is sacred, and both belong to God alone.

5. Even hunted game — pour out the blood and cover it (v.13). Even in
   non-sacrificial contexts, blood is treated with reverence. There is no
   area of life where God's principles do not apply. Whether at the altar
   or in the field, blood is sacred. Whether in worship or in daily life,
   God's holiness governs everything.
