Leviticus - Chapter 4
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Section 1 — Introduction: Sin Through Ignorance (vv.1-2):
 God speaks to Moses: "If anyone sins unintentionally in any of the
 LORD's commandments about things not to be done, and does any one of
 them" — the sin offering is prescribed.

Section 2 — The Anointed Priest's Sin Offering (vv.3-12):
 The priest who sins brings guilt on the people. He must offer a young
 bull without blemish. He lays his hand on its head, kills it, and
 brings the blood into the tent of meeting. He sprinkles blood seven
 times before the veil, puts blood on the horns of the incense altar,
 and pours the rest at the base of the burnt offering altar. The fat,
 kidneys, and liver lobe are burned on the altar. The entire remainder
 — skin, flesh, head, legs, entrails, dung — is carried outside the
 camp to the ash heap and burned.

Section 3 — The Congregation's Sin Offering (vv.13-21):
 When the whole congregation sins unintentionally, the elders lay their
 hands on a young bull's head. The same blood ritual as the priest's
 offering is performed. The bull is burned outside the camp. "The priest
 shall make atonement for them, and they shall be forgiven."

Section 4 — The Ruler's Sin Offering (vv.22-26):
 A leader who sins unintentionally brings a male goat without blemish.
 He lays his hand on its head and kills it where the burnt offering is
 killed. The priest puts blood on the horns of the burnt offering altar
 (not the incense altar) and pours the rest at its base. The fat is
 burned. "The priest shall make atonement for him... and he shall be
 forgiven."

Section 5 — The Common Person's Sin Offering (vv.27-35):
 A common person who sins unintentionally brings a female goat or female
 lamb without blemish. Same procedure: laying on of hands, killing at
 the place of burnt offering, blood on the horns of the altar, fat
 burned. "The priest shall make atonement for him... and he shall be
 forgiven."

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses. Leviticus is the third book of the Pentateuch, written during
the wilderness period (approximately 1445-1405 BC). Hebrew title: "Vayikra"
— "And He called." God speaks from the Tabernacle, giving instructions for
worship, sacrifice, and holy living.

Historical Context: The sin offering (Hebrew: "chattat," from the root
"chata" — to miss the mark, to sin) addresses unintentional sins — sins
committed in ignorance or inadvertence. This is significant: even sins
committed without knowledge or intent require atonement. Ignorance does not
equal innocence before a holy God. The sin offering deals with the sinful
nature and its inevitable expressions, not merely deliberate rebellion.

The chapter introduces a graduated system based on the offender's status:
the higher the position, the greater the responsibility, and the costlier
the sacrifice. A priest's sin brings guilt on the entire people; a ruler's
sin affects his domain; a common person's sin affects himself. This principle
— "to whom much is given, much is required" (Luke 12:48) — runs throughout
Scripture. The sin offering points directly to Christ, who was "made sin for
us" (2 Cor 5:21) and who bore our sins "outside the camp" (Heb 13:11-12),
just as the bull was burned outside the camp.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2 — "If anyone sins unintentionally in any of the LORD's commandments
 about things not to be done, and does any one of them." — Unintentional
 sin still requires atonement. Ignorance is not innocence. Our sinful
 nature produces sin even when we are unaware.

v.3 — "If it is the anointed priest who sins, thus bringing guilt on the
 people." — Greater privilege means greater responsibility. A leader's
 sin has corporate consequences.

v.12 — "All the rest of the bull — he shall carry outside the camp to a
 clean place, to the ash heap, and shall burn it up." — Christ suffered
 "outside the gate" (Heb 13:12). He bore our sin away from God's
 presence.

v.20 — "The priest shall make atonement for them, and they shall be
 forgiven." — The refrain of the chapter: atonement leads to
 forgiveness. This is the heart of the gospel.

v.26 — "So the priest shall make atonement for him for his sin, and he
 shall be forgiven." — Forgiveness is certain when atonement is made.
 Not "might be" but "shall be."

v.35 — "And the priest shall make atonement for him for the sin which he
 has committed, and he shall be forgiven." — Repeated four times in this
 chapter: forgiveness is God's response to proper atonement.

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.2  — Hebrew "bishgagah" — from "shagag" (to go astray, to err).
       KJV: "sin through ignorance"
       ESV: "sins unintentionally"
       ASV: "sin unwittingly"
       NET: "sins by straying unintentionally"
       WEB: "sins unintentionally"
       Analysis: NET's "straying unintentionally" captures both the
       accidental nature and the wandering imagery. KJV's "ignorance" is
       slightly misleading — the sin is unintentional, not necessarily
       unknown. Not deliberate rebellion but inadvertent transgression.

v.2  — Hebrew YHWH — in context of commandments broken.
       KJV: "the LORD"
       ESV: "the LORD"
       ASV: "Jehovah hath commanded"
       NET: "the Lord's commandments"
       WEB: "Yahweh has commanded"
       Analysis: The divine name difference is theologically significant
       here: the commandments broken are personally issued by YHWH Himself,
       not abstract rules.

v.3  — Hebrew "ha-kohen ha-mashiach" — the priest, the anointed one.
       Hebrew "chattath" — sin offering.
       KJV: "the priest that is anointed... sin offering"
       ESV: "the anointed priest... sin offering"
       ASV: "the anointed priest... sin-offering"
       NET: "the high priest... sin offering"
       WEB: "the anointed priest... sin offering"
       Analysis: NET interprets "ha-kohen ha-mashiach" as "the high priest"
       for clarity. ASV/WEB/ESV translate more literally. The anointed
       priest IS the high priest, but the Hebrew emphasizes his anointing.
       The same root as "Messiah." Some scholars prefer "purification
       offering" since the offering purifies from sin's contamination.

v.6  — Hebrew "paroketh" — the veil/curtain.
       KJV: "the vail of the sanctuary"
       ESV: "the veil of the sanctuary"
       ASV: "before the veil of the sanctuary"
       NET: "toward the front of the special curtain of the sanctuary"
       WEB: "before the veil of the sanctuary"
       Analysis: NET's "special curtain" interprets the Hebrew "paroketh"
       to distinguish it from ordinary curtains. KJV/ASV/WEB all use "veil"
       — the curtain separating the Holy Place from the Most Holy.

v.7  — Hebrew "mizbach qetoreth ha-sammim" — the altar of fragrant incense.
       KJV: "altar of sweet incense"
       ESV: "altar of fragrant incense"
       ASV: "altar of sweet incense"
       NET: "altar of fragrant incense"
       WEB: "altar of sweet incense"
       Analysis: The golden altar inside the Holy Place. Blood on this altar
       signified that sin had penetrated to the place of prayer and worship.

v.12 — Hebrew "mi-chutz la-machaneh" — outside the camp. Hebrew "shephek
       ha-deshen" — the ash/fat-ash dump.
       KJV: "without the camp... where the ashes are poured out"
       ESV: "outside the camp... the ash heap"
       ASV: "without the camp... where the ashes are poured out"
       NET: "outside the camp... to the fatty-ash pile"
       WEB: "outside the camp... where the ashes are poured out"
       Analysis: NET's "fatty-ash pile" is a technical rendering of the
       designated dump site for altar ashes mixed with sacrificial fat.
       More specific than the generic "ashes" of other versions. A place of
       separation and rejection. Christ was crucified outside Jerusalem's
       walls (Heb 13:12).

v.20 — Hebrew "kipper" — to cover, to make propitiation.
       KJV: "make an atonement"
       ESV: "make atonement"
       ASV: "make atonement"
       NET: "make atonement"
       WEB: "make atonement"
       Analysis: All translations agree. The root idea is covering sin so
       that God's wrath is turned away. Christ is our "propitiation"
       (hilasmos, 1 John 2:2).

v.22 — Hebrew "nasi" — a prince, chief, or leader.
       KJV: "When a ruler hath sinned"
       ESV: "When a leader sins"
       ASV: "When a ruler sinneth"
       NET: "Whenever a leader sins"
       WEB: "When a ruler sins"
       Analysis: The ruler's offering is a male goat (more costly than the
       common person's female goat/lamb), reflecting greater accountability.

v.27 — Hebrew "am ha-aretz" — people of the land.
       KJV: "any one of the common people"
       ESV: "anyone of the common people"
       ASV: "any one of the common people"
       NET: "an ordinary individual"
       WEB: "anyone of the common people"
       Analysis: The ordinary citizen, as distinct from priest, congregation,
       or ruler. NET's "ordinary individual" modernizes the phrase.

v.35 — Hebrew "isheh YHWH" — fire offerings of the LORD.
       KJV: "offerings made by fire unto the LORD"
       ESV: "the LORD's food offerings"
       ASV: "the offerings of Jehovah made by fire"
       NET: "the gifts of the Lord"
       WEB: "the offerings of Yahweh made by fire"
       Analysis: The sin offering's fat is burned on the altar just like
       the peace offering's fat. Even in dealing with sin, God finds
       satisfaction in the atoning sacrifice.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Chattat (Sin Offering) — From "chata" (to miss the mark). A mandatory
 offering for unintentional sin. Deals with the guilt of sin and the
 need for cleansing. Different from the trespass/guilt offering which
 addresses specific violations requiring restitution.

Unintentional Sin (Bishgagah) — Sin committed in ignorance, inadvertence,
 or carelessness — not deliberate, high-handed rebellion. Still requires
 atonement because sin defiles regardless of intent.

Laying on of Hands — Identification and transfer. The sinner places his
 hand on the animal, symbolically transferring his guilt. The animal
 bears his sin and dies in his place.

Outside the Camp — The place of rejection, separation, and curse. The sin
 offering for the priest and congregation was burned here — not on the
 altar. Christ suffered "outside the gate" (Heb 13:12), bearing our
 curse away from God's holy presence.

Horns of the Altar — Projections at the four corners of the altar. Blood
 applied to the horns signified the power and authority of the atonement.
 For the priest/congregation: the incense altar horns. For the ruler/
 common person: the burnt offering altar horns.

Atonement (Kipper) — To cover, to make propitiation, to reconcile. The
 sacrifice covers sin so that God's wrath is satisfied and the sinner
 is restored to fellowship.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Unintentional sins still require atonement (v.2). How many sins do you
 commit daily without even realizing it? Sins of omission, careless words,
 selfish motives you never examine? Thank God that Christ's blood covers
 even the sins you cannot name. Pray with David: "Declare me innocent from
 hidden faults" (Ps 19:12).

2. The priest's sin brought guilt on the people (v.3). If you are in any
 position of spiritual leadership — parent, teacher, pastor, mentor — your
 sin has consequences beyond yourself. Does this reality make you more
 vigilant? More dependent on grace?

3. The bull was burned "outside the camp" (v.12). Christ suffered outside
 Jerusalem, bearing our shame and rejection. Are you willing to go to Him
 "outside the camp, bearing the reproach he endured" (Heb 13:13)? What
 does it cost you to identify with a rejected Savior?

4. "The priest shall make atonement... and he shall be forgiven" (vv.20, 26,
 31, 35). Four times the promise is repeated. God wants you to be certain:
 where there is atonement, there is forgiveness. Do you doubt your
 forgiveness? Rest in the certainty of Christ's finished work.

5. The graduated scale of sacrifice (bull, goat, lamb) shows that greater
 privilege brings greater responsibility. What has God entrusted to you?
 How does your level of accountability match your level of faithfulness?
