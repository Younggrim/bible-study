Leviticus - Chapter 24
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
God commands the continual lamp and showbread in the Tabernacle; a blasphemer is stoned — God's name must be honored.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses. Leviticus is the third book of the Pentateuch, written during
the wilderness period (approximately 1445-1405 BC). Hebrew title: "Vayikra"
— "And He called." God speaks from the newly completed Tabernacle, giving
Israel instructions for worship, sacrifice, and holy living.

Historical Context: Chapter 24 is a transitional chapter that combines
ritual instruction with narrative — an unusual structure in Leviticus. It
contains three distinct sections: (1) The perpetual lamp and showbread
(vv.1-9) — ongoing worship in the Tabernacle; (2) The blasphemer stoned
(vv.10-16, 23) — a narrative case illustrating the holiness of God's name;
and (3) The law of retaliation — lex talionis (vv.17-22) — equal justice
for all. The placement is significant: after the feasts (ch.23) which
celebrate God's redemptive plan, chapter 24 addresses the daily, ongoing
worship (lamp and bread) and the seriousness of profaning God's name. The
blasphemy incident is the only narrative in the Holiness Code (chs.17-26)
and serves as a concrete example of what it means to "profane my holy name"
(22:32). The chapter also establishes a foundational principle of biblical
justice: one law for all — native and stranger alike (v.22). The lamp that
never goes out and the bread that is always present speak of Christ: the
Light of the World and the Bread of Life.

The Perpetual Lamp (vv.1-4): God commands Israel to bring "pure oil olive
beaten for the light" (KJV) / "pure oil from beaten olives for the lamp"
(ESV) (v.2). The lamp must burn "continually" (tamid) — perpetually,
without interruption (v.2). Aaron tends it from evening to morning "before
the LORD continually" (v.3) — outside the veil, in the Holy Place. The
lamps are on "the pure candlestick" (KJV) / "the lampstand of pure gold"
(ESV) (v.4). The oil is beaten (not pressed) — the finest quality,
producing the clearest flame. Christ is the Light of the World (John 8:12);
the Holy Spirit is the oil; the Church is called to let its light shine
(Matthew 5:14-16). The light never goes out — God's presence and truth
are perpetual.

The Showbread (vv.5-9): Twelve cakes of fine flour, two-tenths of an
ephah each (v.5). Set in two rows of six on "the pure table" (KJV) /
"the table of pure gold" (ESV) (v.6). Pure frankincense placed on each
row as a memorial/food offering to the LORD (v.7). Replaced every Sabbath
— a perpetual covenant (v.8). The old bread is eaten by Aaron and his
sons in the holy place — "it is most holy" (v.9). Twelve loaves = twelve
tribes — all Israel perpetually before God. The bread of the Presence
(Hebrew: "lechem ha-panim" — bread of the face/presence) represents
God's provision and Israel's constant fellowship with Him. Christ is the
Bread of Life (John 6:35) — always present, always sustaining.

The Blasphemer (vv.10-16, 23): A man of mixed parentage — Israelite mother
(Shelomith, daughter of Dibri, of Dan) and Egyptian father (v.10) — fights
with an Israelite in the camp (v.10). He "blasphemed the name of the LORD,
and cursed" (KJV) / "blasphemed the Name, and cursed" (ESV) (v.11). He is
brought to Moses and held in custody until God reveals His will (v.12).
God's verdict: bring him outside the camp; all who heard him lay hands on
his head (transferring guilt back to him); the whole congregation stones
him (v.14). The general principle: anyone who curses God bears his sin
(v.15). Anyone who blasphemes the NAME of the LORD — death by stoning,
whether native or stranger (v.16). The sentence is carried out (v.23).
The severity reveals the weight of God's name. To blaspheme (naqav —
to pierce, bore through) the Name is to assault God's very character and
authority. The laying on of hands by witnesses shows corporate
responsibility — those who heard the blasphemy return the guilt to its
source.

The Law of Retaliation — Lex Talionis (vv.17-22): Whoever kills a man
shall be put to death (v.17). Whoever kills an animal shall make
restitution — "life for life" (v.18). If a man injures his neighbor: "as
he hath done, so shall it be done to him" (v.19). "Breach for breach, eye
for eye, tooth for tooth" (v.20). Kill an animal — restore it; kill a man
— death (v.21). ONE LAW for stranger and native alike (v.22). This is NOT
a license for personal vengeance but a principle of PROPORTIONAL JUSTICE
administered by the community. The punishment must fit the crime — no more,
no less. It limits excessive retaliation (you cannot kill a man for
knocking out your tooth) and ensures justice for the powerless (the
stranger receives the same protection as the native). Jesus addresses
this in Matthew 5:38-39, not abolishing the principle of justice but
forbidding personal vengeance — "resist not evil" applies to personal
insult, not to civil justice.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "Command the people of Israel to bring you pure oil from beaten
        olives for the lamp, that a light may be kept burning regularly."
        (ESV) — Pure oil, perpetual light. Christ the Light never goes out.

v.4  — "He shall arrange the lamps on the lampstand of pure gold before
        the LORD regularly." (ESV) — Continual maintenance of the light —
        ongoing faithfulness in worship.

v.8  — "Every Sabbath day Aaron shall arrange it before the LORD regularly;
        it is from the people of Israel as a covenant forever." (ESV)
        — The bread of the Presence: perpetual provision, perpetual
        fellowship.

v.11 — "and the Israelite woman's son blasphemed the Name, and cursed."
        (ESV) — The gravity of profaning God's name.

v.16 — "Whoever blasphemes the name of the LORD shall surely be put to
        death... the sojourner as well as the native." (ESV)
        — God's name is sacred; justice applies equally to all.

v.20 — "fracture for fracture, eye for eye, tooth for tooth." (ESV)
        — Proportional justice. The punishment fits the crime.

v.22 — "You shall have the same rule for the sojourner and for the native,
        for I am the LORD your God." (ESV) — Equal justice under one law.
        God shows no partiality.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/leviticus-24/
  Guzik notes the connection between the perpetual lamp/bread and Christ
  as Light and Bread. He emphasizes that lex talionis was a limitation on
  vengeance, not a license for it — it prevented blood feuds by requiring
  proportional justice.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "The lamp must never go out. The bread must always be there. These are
  pictures of Christ who is always the Light, always the Bread, always
  before the Father on our behalf. And when a man blasphemes that Name —
  the Name above every name — he strikes at the very foundation of all
  that is holy. God will not hold him guiltless. The severity of the
  penalty reveals the infinite worth of the Name that was profaned."

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.2  — Hebrew "shemen zayit zakh katit la-ma'or" — pure beaten olive oil for the light.
       KJV: "pure oil olive beaten for the light"
       ESV: "pure oil from beaten olives for the lamp"
       ASV: "pure olive oil beaten for the light"
       NET: "pure oil of beaten olives for the light"
       Analysis: "Katit" means beaten/crushed — the olives are pounded in a mortar (not pressed in a press), producing the purest, clearest oil with no sediment. This is the highest quality oil, reserved for the sacred lamp.

v.2  — Hebrew "tamid" — continually, perpetually, regularly.
       KJV: "to cause the lamps to burn continually"
       ESV: "that a light may be kept burning regularly"
       Analysis: KJV's "continually" and ESV's "regularly" both translate "tamid." The lamp burned from evening to morning (v.3); it was tended daily to ensure it never went out.

v.3  — Hebrew "ohel mo'ed" — tent of meeting.
       KJV: "Without the vail of the testimony, in the tabernacle of the congregation"
       ESV: "Outside the veil of the testimony, in the tent of meeting"
       ASV: "Without the veil of the testimony"
       NET: "Outside the special curtain of the congregation"
       Analysis: The lampstand stood in the Holy Place (not the Most Holy Place), outside the veil that covered the ark. NET clarifies "paroketh ha-eduth" as the special curtain near the testimony (ark).

v.4  — Hebrew "ha-menorah ha-tehorah" — the pure lampstand.
       KJV: "the pure candlestick"
       ESV: "the lampstand of pure gold"
       ASV: "the pure candlestick"
       NET: "the ceremonially pure lampstand"
       Analysis: KJV's "candlestick" is misleading to modern readers (it was an oil lamp, not a candle). ESV's "lampstand" is more accurate. NET avoids "candlestick" (anachronistic). "Pure" (tehorah) refers to the pure gold construction.

v.5  — Hebrew "challot" — round loaves/cakes.
       KJV: "twelve cakes"
       ESV: "twelve loaves"
       ASV: "fine flour"
       NET: "choice wheat flour"
       Analysis: The same word used for the bread offering. Twelve loaves representing the twelve tribes of Israel. NET specifies "choice wheat flour" (Hebrew "soleth") for the showbread.

v.7  — Hebrew "l'azkarah" — a memorial, a reminder.
       KJV: "for a memorial, even an offering made by fire"
       ESV: "as a memorial portion as a food offering"
       Analysis: The frankincense burned as the memorial portion while the bread itself was eaten by the priests.

v.8  — Hebrew "berit olam" — an everlasting/perpetual covenant.
       KJV: "by an everlasting covenant"
       ESV: "as a covenant forever"
       Analysis: The weekly renewal of the showbread is a perpetual covenant obligation — God's ongoing provision and Israel's ongoing fellowship with Him.

v.11 — Hebrew "vayyiqqov... et ha-Shem vayyeqallel" — he pierced/blasphemed the Name and cursed.
       KJV: "blasphemed the name of the LORD, and cursed"
       ESV: "blasphemed the Name, and cursed"
       Analysis: "Naqav" means to pierce, bore through, designate — here it means to curse/blaspheme by pronouncing the divine Name in a curse. "Qalal" means to make light of, curse. ESV capitalizes "the Name" (ha-Shem) — the same term Jews use today to avoid pronouncing God's name.

v.12 — Hebrew "liphrosh lahem al pi YHWH" — to make clear by the mouth of the LORD.
       KJV: "that the mind of the LORD might be shewed them"
       ESV: "till the will of the LORD should be clear to them"
       Analysis: This was a new situation requiring direct divine guidance. Moses did not presume to judge without God's explicit instruction.

v.16 — Hebrew "naqav shem YHWH" — blaspheming the name of the LORD.
       KJV: "he that blasphemeth the name of the LORD"
       ASV: "he that blasphemeth the name of Jehovah, he shall surely be put to death"
       NET: "the one who blasphemes the name of the Lord must surely be put to death"
       WEB: "he who blasphemes Yahweh's name, he shall surely be put to death"
       Analysis: Note the divine name difference: NET says "blasphemes the name of the Lord" while ASV/WEB actually use the name itself. The irony: the chapter is about misusing the Name.

v.20 — Hebrew "shever tachat shever, ayin tachat ayin, shen tachat shen" — exact equivalence.
       KJV: "Breach for breach, eye for eye, tooth for tooth"
       ESV: "fracture for fracture, eye for eye, tooth for tooth"
       ASV: "breach for breach, eye for eye, tooth for tooth"
       NET: "fracture for fracture, eye for eye, tooth for tooth"
       Analysis: "Tachat" means "in place of, instead of" — exact equivalence. KJV/ASV's "breach" and ESV/NET's "fracture" both translate "shever" (a breaking). This is the principle of proportional justice (lex talionis), not personal vengeance.

v.22 — Hebrew "mishpat echad" — one judgment/justice.
       KJV: "one manner of law"
       ESV: "the same rule"
       Analysis: The same standard applies to everyone regardless of origin. This was revolutionary in the ancient world where foreigners typically had fewer legal protections.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Showbread / Bread of the Presence — Hebrew "lechem ha-panim" — literally
               "bread of the face" or "bread of the presence." Twelve
               loaves set before God's face on the golden table in the
               Holy Place. Replaced every Sabbath; eaten by priests. It
               represents God's provision for His people and their
               perpetual fellowship before Him.

Menorah (Lampstand) — The seven-branched golden lampstand in the Holy
               Place. Fed with pure beaten olive oil. Represents God's
               light, truth, and the Holy Spirit's illumination. Christ
               is the Light; the Church reflects His light.

Blaspheme (naqav) — To pierce, bore through; to pronounce the divine Name
               in a curse or with contempt. To assault God's character
               and authority with words. The most serious speech-sin in
               the Torah, carrying the death penalty.

Lex Talionis — Latin: "law of retaliation." The principle of proportional
               justice: the punishment must correspond exactly to the
               injury. Not a license for vengeance but a LIMIT on
               punishment — ensuring justice is neither excessive nor
               insufficient.

"Outside the Camp" — The place of execution and uncleanness. The
               blasphemer is brought outside the camp to be stoned (v.14).
               This is the same location where sin offerings are burned
               (4:12; 16:27) and where Christ suffered (Hebrews 13:12).

Laying on of Hands — The witnesses lay hands on the blasphemer's head
               (v.14) — transferring the guilt/responsibility back to him.
               Similar to the scapegoat ritual (16:21) but in reverse:
               here the community returns the defilement to its source
               before executing judgment.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. The perpetual lamp (vv.2-4). The light must never go out. In the
   Church, we are called to be light-bearers (Matthew 5:14-16). The oil
   must be pure (the Holy Spirit's work), the lamp must be tended daily
   (spiritual disciplines), and the light must shine continually (faithful
   witness). Is your lamp burning? Is it tended? Or has neglect allowed
   it to flicker?

2. The showbread (vv.5-9). Twelve loaves — always before God. Every tribe
   represented, none forgotten. God sees all His people, all the time.
   You are never out of His sight or His provision. The bread is renewed
   every Sabbath — God's mercies are new every morning (Lamentations 3:23).

3. The blasphemer (vv.10-16). Words matter. God's name is not to be used
   carelessly, flippantly, or in cursing. In our culture, God's name is
   used as an expletive constantly — and believers often become desensitized.
   "Neither shall ye profane my holy name" (22:32). Do you treat God's
   name with the weight it deserves? Do you speak of Him with reverence?

4. "Eye for eye" (v.20). This is not cruelty — it is JUSTICE. It prevents
   both excessive punishment (you cannot kill for a bruise) and insufficient
   punishment (the powerful cannot escape consequences). It also means
   equal justice for all (v.22). God is impartial. His justice does not
   favor the rich or the native-born. In Christ, "there is neither Jew
   nor Greek" (Galatians 3:28).

5. One law for all (v.22). The stranger receives the same justice as the
   native. God's moral law is universal — it does not change based on
   ethnicity, status, or origin. This principle of equal justice under
   law is foundational to Western civilization and flows directly from
   this text.
