Leviticus - Chapter 22
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
God demands that offerings be without blemish and that priests be ceremonially clean — nothing defective may be brought before a holy God.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses. Leviticus is the third book of the Pentateuch, written during
the wilderness period (approximately 1445-1405 BC). Hebrew title: "Vayikra"
— "And He called." God speaks from the newly completed Tabernacle, giving
Israel instructions for worship, sacrifice, and holy living.

Historical Context: Chapter 22 continues the "Holiness Code" section of
Leviticus (chapters 17-27), specifically addressing priestly holiness in
relation to eating holy food and the quality of sacrificial animals. Chapter
21 dealt with the physical qualifications of priests; chapter 22 now deals
with the conditions under which priests may eat the holy portions and the
conditions under which animals may be offered. The chapter divides into
two major sections: (1) Who may eat holy things and when they are
disqualified (vv.1-16), and (2) What animals are acceptable for sacrifice
(vv.17-33). The underlying principle is that God's holiness demands
corresponding holiness in everything connected with His worship — both
the worshippers and the offerings must be without defilement. The chapter
closes with one of the great declarations of Leviticus: "Neither shall ye
profane my holy name; but I will be hallowed among the children of Israel"
(v.32). God's name — His reputation, character, and glory — must not be
treated as common. The repeated refrain "I am the LORD" (appearing seven
times) underscores divine authority behind every regulation.

Priestly Holiness in Eating Holy Things (vv.1-9): God commands Aaron and
his sons to "separate themselves from the holy things" (KJV) / "abstain
from the holy things" (ESV) when they are in a state of uncleanness (v.2).
The holy things are the portions of offerings that belong to the priests
(breast, shoulder, grain offerings, etc.). A priest with uncleanness who
approaches these holy things will be "cut off from my presence" (v.3).
Specific disqualifications: leprosy, bodily discharge (v.4), touching
anything unclean by the dead, emission of semen (v.4), touching swarming
creatures, or touching an unclean person (v.5). The remedy: wait until
evening, bathe in water, then he is clean and may eat (vv.6-7). He must
not eat what dies of itself or is torn by beasts (v.8). Penalty for
profaning: death (v.9). The principle: even legitimate priests with
legitimate access must be in a state of cleanness to handle holy things.
Privilege does not override purity.

Who May and May Not Eat Holy Things (vv.10-16): A "stranger" (KJV) /
"lay person" (ESV) — anyone not of the priestly family — may not eat
holy food (v.10). This includes a priest's guest or hired worker (v.10).
However, a slave purchased by the priest and anyone born in his household
MAY eat (v.11) — they are part of his household. A priest's daughter
married to a non-priest ("stranger" KJV / "layman" ESV) may not eat
(v.12). But if she is widowed or divorced with no children and returns to
her father's house, she may eat again (v.13). If someone eats holy food
unintentionally, he must add a fifth (20%) to its value and give it to
the priest (v.14). The priests must not allow the people to profane the
holy things by eating them improperly (vv.15-16).

Acceptable Sacrificial Animals (vv.17-25): This section addresses ALL
Israel and sojourners (v.18) — not just priests. For burnt offerings:
the animal must be male, without blemish — from cattle, sheep, or goats
(v.19). Anything with a blemish "shall not be acceptable for you" (v.20).
Peace offerings must be "perfect" (KJV) / "perfect" (ESV) — no blemish
(v.21). Specific disqualifications listed: blind, broken, maimed/mutilated,
having a wen/discharge, scurvy/itch, scabbed/scabs (v.22). Exception: an
animal with a limb too long or too short may be used for a freewill
offering but NOT for a vow (v.23). Animals with damaged reproductive
organs (bruised, crushed, broken, cut) are forbidden (v.24). Even animals
from foreigners with blemishes are unacceptable (v.25). The principle:
God deserves the BEST. A blemished offering insults His worth. This points
to Christ — the Lamb "without blemish and without spot" (1 Peter 1:19).

Additional Regulations (vv.26-30): A newborn animal must stay with its
mother seven days; from the eighth day onward it is acceptable for
offering (v.27). A mother and her young must not be killed on the same
day (v.28) — a provision showing God's compassion even in sacrifice. A
thanksgiving offering must be eaten the same day — none left until morning
(vv.29-30).

The Closing Declaration (vv.31-33): "Keep my commandments and do them"
(v.31). "Neither shall ye profane my holy name; but I will be hallowed
among the children of Israel" (v.32). God's identity: "I am the LORD
which hallow you, that brought you out of the land of Egypt, to be your
God" (vv.32-33). The God who sanctifies them is the God who redeemed
them. Holiness flows from redemption.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "Tell Aaron and his sons to separate themselves from the holy things
        of the people of Israel, which they dedicate to me, so that they
        do not profane my holy name." (ESV) — Priests must handle holy
        things with reverence, not presumption.

v.3  — "that person shall be cut off from my presence: I am the LORD." (ESV)
        — Uncleanness in the presence of holiness brings judgment.

v.9  — "They shall therefore keep my charge, lest they bear sin for it and
        die thereby when they profane it: I am the LORD who sanctifies
        them." (ESV) — Profaning holy things carries the penalty of death.

v.19 — "to be accepted you shall offer a male without blemish, of the
        bulls, the sheep, or the goats." (ESV) — Only the best for God.

v.21 — "it shall be perfect to be accepted; there shall be no blemish in
        it." (ESV) — Perfection required. Points to Christ's sinlessness.

v.32 — "And you shall not profane my holy name, that I may be sanctified
        among the people of Israel. I am the LORD who sanctifies you." (ESV)
        — God's name must be treated as holy. He is the one who makes them
        holy.

v.33 — "who brought you out of the land of Egypt to be your God: I am the
        LORD." (ESV) — Redemption is the basis for holiness demands.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/leviticus-22/
  Guzik emphasizes that the requirement for unblemished animals points
  directly to Christ as the perfect sacrifice. He notes that Malachi 1:8
  shows Israel eventually violated this principle by offering blind and
  lame animals — treating God's altar with contempt.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "God will not accept from you that which costs you nothing. The lame,
  the blind, the sick — these are what men offer when they do not truly
  value God. But He who gave His only Son — the spotless Lamb — demands
  that we bring our best. Not because our best earns anything, but because
  anything less reveals a heart that does not treasure Him."

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.2  — KJV: "that they separate themselves from the holy things" / ESV:
       "that they abstain from the holy things" — Hebrew "yinnazru" from
       "nazar" (to separate, dedicate, abstain). The priests must distance
       themselves from holy food when unclean. KJV's "separate" and ESV's
       "abstain" both capture the idea of deliberate withdrawal.

v.2  — KJV: "which they hallow unto me" / ESV: "which they dedicate to
       me" — Hebrew "maqdishtm" from "qadash" (to make holy, set apart).
       KJV's "hallow" is archaic English for "make holy." ESV's "dedicate"
       is more accessible but slightly less precise.

v.4  — KJV: "a running issue" / ESV: "a discharge" — Hebrew "zav" — a
       bodily discharge (cf. Leviticus 15). KJV's phrasing is archaic;
       ESV modernizes.

v.8  — KJV: "That which dieth of itself, or is torn with beasts" / ESV:
       "what dies of itself or is torn by beasts" — Hebrew "nevelah"
       (carcass/what dies naturally) and "terefah" (torn by animals). Both
       render accurately. These are the same categories from Leviticus 17:15.

v.10 — KJV: "There shall no stranger eat of the holy thing" / ESV: "A lay
       person shall not eat of a holy thing" — Hebrew "zar" — one who is
       outside, a non-priest. KJV's "stranger" can be confusing (it does
       not mean foreigner here but non-priest). ESV's "lay person" is
       clearer for modern readers.

v.11 — KJV: "if the priest buy any soul with his money" / ESV: "if a
       priest buys a slave as his property for money" — Hebrew "qinyan
       kaspo" — acquisition of his silver/money. KJV uses "soul" (nephesh)
       for the person purchased. ESV makes explicit that this refers to a
       slave. The purchased person becomes part of the household and shares
       in priestly food.

v.19 — KJV: "at your own will" / ESV: "if it is to be accepted for you"
       — Hebrew "lirtzonkhem" — for your acceptance/pleasure. KJV's "at
       your own will" means "for your acceptance" (not "whenever you feel
       like it"). ESV clarifies the meaning.

v.22 — KJV: "having a wen, or scurvy, or scabbed" / ESV: "having a
       discharge or an itch or scabs" — Hebrew "yabbelet" (wart/wen),
       "garav" (itch/scurvy), "yallepheth" (scab). KJV uses archaic
       medical terms; ESV modernizes. All refer to skin conditions that
       render an animal blemished.

v.23 — KJV: "any thing superfluous or lacking in his parts" / ESV: "a
       part too long or too short" — Hebrew "sarua" (stretched/extended)
       and "qalut" (stunted/contracted). Refers to a limb deformity —
       one part disproportionate. KJV is vague; ESV is more specific.

v.24 — KJV: "bruised, or crushed, or broken, or cut" / ESV: "its
       testicles bruised or crushed or torn or cut" — Hebrew refers
       specifically to damage to reproductive organs. ESV makes explicit
       what KJV leaves implicit. These are four types of castration —
       all forbidden for sacrifice.

v.32 — KJV: "I will be hallowed among the children of Israel" / ESV:
       "that I may be sanctified among the people of Israel" — Hebrew
       "v'niqdashti" — passive of "qadash" (to be holy). Both "hallowed"
       (KJV) and "sanctified" (ESV) translate the same Hebrew root. God
       will be treated as holy among His people.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Holy Things     — The portions of sacrifices designated for priestly
               consumption: the breast and right shoulder of peace
               offerings, portions of grain offerings, etc. These are
               "most holy" and may only be eaten by qualified priests in
               a state of cleanness.

Profane (chalal) — To treat as common what God has declared holy. To
               desecrate, defile, or pollute. The opposite of "sanctify."
               A priest profanes holy food by eating it while unclean; a
               worshipper profanes God's name by offering blemished animals.

Without Blemish (tamim) — Complete, whole, perfect, without defect. The
               standard for all sacrificial animals. Points to Christ's
               sinless perfection as the ultimate sacrifice.

Wen (yabbelet) — A wart, cyst, or growth on the skin. One of the
               blemishes that disqualifies an animal for sacrifice.

Scapegoat/Stranger (zar) — In this context, "zar" means an outsider to
               the priestly family — a layperson. Not a foreigner but
               anyone not descended from Aaron.

The Fifth Part — A 20% penalty added when holy things are consumed
               improperly (v.14). The same penalty appears in the trespass
               offering (5:16) and in redemption of tithes (27:31). It
               represents restitution beyond mere replacement.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Separate themselves from the holy things" (v.2). Even those with
   legitimate access to God's holy things must approach with reverence.
   Familiarity breeds contempt. Pastors, teachers, worship leaders — those
   who handle God's Word and lead His worship — are especially warned:
   do not let proximity to holy things make you casual about holiness.
   "To whom much is given, much is required" (Luke 12:48).

2. "Without blemish" (vv.19-21). God demands the best, not the leftovers.
   Israel would later offer blind, lame, and sick animals (Malachi 1:8)
   — giving God what cost them nothing. What do you offer God? Your best
   time, energy, and attention — or what's left over? "Shall I offer to
   the LORD that which costs me nothing?" (2 Samuel 24:24).

3. "Neither shall ye profane my holy name" (v.32). God's name — His
   reputation — is at stake in how His people live and worship. When
   believers live carelessly, they profane God's name before the world.
   When we worship half-heartedly, we declare God is not worth our best.
   His name is hallowed when His people reflect His character.

4. "I am the LORD which hallow you, that brought you out of Egypt" (vv.32-33).
   The demand for holiness is grounded in redemption. God does not say
   "Be holy and then I will save you." He says "I have saved you —
   therefore be holy." Grace precedes demand. Redemption is the foundation
   of sanctification.

5. The eighth day (v.27). A newborn animal is not acceptable until the
   eighth day. Eight is the number of new beginnings in Scripture
   (circumcision on the eighth day, resurrection on the first day of a
   new week). There is a divine timing to acceptability — God's purposes
   unfold according to His schedule, not ours.
