Leviticus - Chapter 15
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
God gives laws regarding bodily discharges that cause ceremonial uncleanness, requiring washing and offerings for restoration.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses. Leviticus is the third book of the Pentateuch, written during
the wilderness period (approximately 1445-1405 BC). Hebrew title: "Vayikra"
— "And He called." God speaks from the newly completed Tabernacle, giving
Israel instructions for worship, sacrifice, and holy living.

Historical Context: Chapter 15 concludes the section on ritual cleanness
that began in chapter 11. Chapters 11-15 form a unified block: clean and
unclean animals (ch.11), purification after childbirth (ch.12), skin
diseases (chs.13-14), and now bodily discharges (ch.15). The logic is
comprehensive — uncleanness can come from what you eat (ch.11), from
giving life (ch.12), from the skin (chs.13-14), and from the body's
internal functions (ch.15). Together these chapters teach that the human
body in its fallen state is a source of uncleanness that must be addressed
before approaching a holy God. The chapter deals with four categories:
(1) chronic male discharge (vv.2-15), (2) seminal emission (vv.16-18),
(3) menstruation (vv.19-24), and (4) chronic female bleeding (vv.25-30).
The structure is symmetrical: the two chronic conditions (male and female)
require sacrifice; the two normal conditions require only washing and
waiting until evening. The chapter ends with a solemn warning (v.31): the
purpose of these laws is to separate Israel from uncleanness "that they
die not in their uncleanness, when they defile my tabernacle."

Chronic Male Discharge (vv.1-15): The LORD speaks to both Moses and Aaron
(v.1) — Aaron is included because priests must adjudicate these matters.
A man with a "running issue" (KJV) / "discharge" (ESV) from his body is
unclean (v.2). Whether the discharge flows or is blocked, it is uncleanness
(v.3). This likely refers to a pathological genital discharge (gonorrhea or
similar). Everything he touches becomes unclean: his bed (v.4), his seat
(v.4), anyone who touches his bed (v.5), sits where he sat (v.6), touches
his body (v.7), or is spit upon by him (v.8). His saddle is unclean (v.9).
Anything under him transmits uncleanness (v.10). Anyone he touches without
having washed his hands is made unclean (v.11). Earthen vessels he touches
must be broken; wooden vessels rinsed (v.12). When cleansed, he waits seven
days, washes clothes, bathes in "running water" (KJV) / "fresh water" (ESV)
(v.13). On the eighth day — two turtledoves or pigeons: one sin offering,
one burnt offering (vv.14-15). The priest makes atonement.

Seminal Emission (vv.16-18): A normal bodily function, not pathological.
The man bathes and is unclean until evening (v.16). Any garment or skin
with semen is washed and unclean until evening (v.17). If a man lies with
a woman, both bathe and are unclean until evening (v.18). No sacrifice
required — only washing and waiting. The uncleanness is brief and minor.

Menstruation (vv.19-24): A woman in her regular period is "put apart"
(KJV) / "in her menstrual impurity" (ESV) for seven days (v.19). Anyone
who touches her is unclean until evening. Everything she lies or sits on
is unclean (v.20). Touching her bed or seat requires washing clothes,
bathing, and uncleanness until evening (vv.21-23). If a man lies with her
during this time, he becomes unclean seven days and his bed is unclean
(v.24). No sacrifice required — this is a normal bodily function.

Chronic Female Bleeding (vv.25-30): A woman with prolonged bleeding outside
her normal period — or bleeding that extends beyond it — is unclean for
the entire duration (v.25). Same rules as menstruation apply (vv.26-27).
When cleansed, she counts seven days, then is clean (v.28). On the eighth
day — two turtledoves or pigeons: one sin offering, one burnt offering
(vv.29-30). The priest makes atonement. This is the condition of the woman
in Mark 5:25-34 who had "an issue of blood twelve years" — she was
perpetually unclean, cut off from worship and community.

The Purpose Statement (vv.31-33): "Thus shall ye separate the children of
Israel from their uncleanness; that they die not in their uncleanness,
when they defile my tabernacle that is among them" (v.31). The stakes are
life and death. God dwells among them — His holiness demands separation
from all that is unclean. Verses 32-33 summarize the entire chapter.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "When any man has a discharge from his body, his discharge is
        unclean." (ESV) — The body itself can be a source of
        uncleanness. The fall affects every aspect of human existence.

v.13 — "And when the one with a discharge is cleansed of his discharge,
        then he shall count for himself seven days for his cleansing, and
        wash his clothes. And he shall bathe his body in fresh water and
        shall be clean." (ESV)
        — Cleansing requires time (seven days), washing, and living water.

v.15 — "And the priest shall make atonement for him before the LORD for
        his discharge." (ESV) — Even bodily conditions require atonement.
        Sin has affected the whole person.

v.31 — "Thus you shall keep the people of Israel separate from their
        uncleanness, lest they die in their uncleanness by defiling my
        tabernacle that is in their midst." (ESV) — The purpose of all
        cleanness laws: protecting the people from death by keeping God's
        dwelling holy.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.2   — Leviticus 22:4 — A priest with a discharge cannot eat holy things.
v.13  — Leviticus 14:8-9 — Similar cleansing procedure for the leper:
        seven days, washing, bathing.
v.14  — Leviticus 12:8 — Same offering (two turtledoves/pigeons) for
        purification after childbirth — the offering of the poor.
v.14  — Luke 2:24 — Mary and Joseph offered "a pair of turtledoves" — the
        offering prescribed here for purification.
v.18  — 1 Samuel 21:4-5 — David's men must be clean from women to eat
        holy bread.
v.19  — Leviticus 18:19 — Sexual relations during menstruation prohibited.
v.19  — Leviticus 20:18 — Penalty for violating the menstrual prohibition.
v.25  — Mark 5:25-34 — The woman with the issue of blood twelve years.
        She was perpetually unclean under this law. Jesus heals her and
        declares her "whole" — clean.
v.25  — Luke 8:43-48 — Parallel account of the woman with the issue of
        blood.
v.31  — Numbers 19:13 — "Whoever touches a dead person... and does not
        cleanse himself, defiles the tabernacle of the LORD." (ESV)
v.31  — Leviticus 20:3 — God sets His face against those who defile His
        sanctuary.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/leviticus-15/
  Guzik notes that these laws taught Israel that the body in its fallen
  state is a source of uncleanness, and that approaching God requires
  dealing with the whole person — not just moral sin but the effects of
  living in a fallen world. He connects the woman with the issue of blood
  (Mark 5) to this chapter's regulations.

Charles Spurgeon:
  Sermon: "The Healing of the Woman with the Issue of Blood"
  "For twelve years she had been unclean — cut off from the Temple, cut
  off from the congregation, cut off from normal life. Everything she
  touched was defiled. She could not worship, could not fellowship, could
  not be embraced. And then she touched HIM — and instead of her
  uncleanness defiling Him, His cleanness healed her. This is the gospel:
  Christ is not contaminated by our sin; rather, His holiness cleanses our
  defilement. One touch of faith, and twelve years of uncleanness vanish."

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.2  — Hebrew "zav mi-b'saro" — a flow/discharge from his flesh.
       KJV: "a running issue out of his flesh"
       ESV: "a discharge from his body"
       ASV: "an issue out of his flesh"
       NET: "a discharge from his body"
       WEB: "a discharge from his body"
       Analysis: KJV's "running issue" is vivid but archaic. ESV/NET/WEB use clinical "discharge." ASV's "issue" is archaic. The Hebrew "zav" becomes a technical term for this condition (a "zav" = one who has a discharge). NET's "discharge" is clinically neutral; the distinction matters — the discharge renders the person ceremonially unclean.

v.3  — Hebrew "zav" — whether flowing or retained.
       KJV: "whether his flesh run with his issue, or his flesh be stopped from his issue"
       ESV: "whether his body runs with his discharge, or his body is blocked up by his discharge"
       ASV: "because of his issue he is unclean"
       NET: "his discharge is unclean"
       WEB: "his discharge is unclean"
       Analysis: Whether flowing or retained, the condition renders unclean. The blockage may indicate swelling or infection. ASV says the person is unclean; NET/WEB say the discharge is unclean. Both are implied, but the distinction matters: the discharge renders the person ceremonially unclean.

v.13 — Hebrew "mayim chayyim" — literally "living water."
       KJV: "running water"
       ESV: "fresh water"
       ASV: "when he that hath an issue is cleansed of his issue"
       NET: "when the man with the discharge becomes clean from his discharge"
       Analysis: Water that flows (a spring or stream), not stagnant water. The same phrase Jesus uses in John 4:10 and 7:38 — "living water." Cleansing requires living water. NET's seven-day waiting period after healing mirrors quarantine principles.

v.15 — Hebrew "chattat" and "olah" — sin offering and burnt offering.
       KJV: "the one for a sin offering, and the other for a burnt offering"
       ESV: "one for a sin offering and the other for a burnt offering"
       ASV: "the one for a sin-offering, and the other for a burnt-offering"
       NET: "one for a sin offering and one for a burnt offering"
       Analysis: All translations agree. Two birds: one for purification (sin offering), one for consecration (burnt offering) — restoring the person to both cleanness and worship.

v.16 — Hebrew "shikbat zera" — a lying-down of seed.
       KJV: "seed of copulation"
       ESV: "emission of semen"
       Analysis: KJV is literal but archaic. ESV is direct and modern.

v.19 — Hebrew "niddah" — her separation/impurity.
       KJV: "she shall be put apart seven days"
       ESV: "she shall be in her menstrual impurity for seven days"
       Analysis: KJV's "put apart" captures the social separation. ESV names the condition directly.

v.24 — Hebrew "niddatah" — her impurity.
       KJV: "her flowers be upon him"
       ESV: "her menstrual impurity comes upon him"
       Analysis: KJV's "flowers" is an archaic euphemism for menstruation (from the idea of flowing). ESV is explicit.

v.29 — Hebrew "torim" — turtledoves.
       KJV: "two turtles"
       ESV: "two turtledoves"
       Analysis: KJV abbreviates; ESV gives the full word. These are the offering of the poor (Leviticus 5:7; 12:8).

v.31 — Hebrew "hizzartem" — to separate/set apart.
       KJV: "that they die not in their uncleanness, when they defile my tabernacle that is among them"
       ESV: "lest they die in their uncleanness by defiling my tabernacle that is in their midst"
       ASV: "that they die not in their uncleanness, when they defile my tabernacle"
       NET: "so that they do not die in their uncleanness by defiling my tabernacle"
       WEB: "Thus you shall separate the children of Israel from their uncleanness"
       Analysis: Both convey the same grave warning. God's presence among them is both blessing and danger. Uncleanness + God's presence = death. NET's "set apart" carries the same root as "Nazirite" — separation unto holiness. NET's "defiling my tabernacle" emphasizes God's possessive claim — it is HIS dwelling, and its holiness must be guarded.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Discharge (Zav) — A pathological bodily flow, likely referring to a
               chronic genital discharge. The person with this condition
               is called a "zav" (male) or "zavah" (female). Distinguished
               from normal bodily functions by its chronic nature and the
               requirement of sacrifice for cleansing.

Niddah         — Hebrew term for menstrual impurity or separation. From
               the root "nadad" (to flee, wander, be removed). The woman
               in niddah is separated — set apart from normal contact.
               Later rabbinic law developed extensive regulations around
               this concept (the "laws of niddah").

Living/Running Water (Mayim Chayyim) — Flowing water from a spring or
               stream, as opposed to stagnant or collected water. Required
               for cleansing from serious uncleanness (v.13; also 14:5-6).
               Symbolically points to the Holy Spirit and Christ's
               cleansing (John 4:10; 7:38; Zechariah 14:8).

Unclean until the even — The minimum period of uncleanness: from the
               moment of contact until sunset. The new day begins at
               sunset in Hebrew reckoning, so "until the even" means
               uncleanness lasts until the start of the next day.

Turtledoves/Pigeons — The offering of the poor. Those who cannot afford
               a lamb may bring two birds: one for sin offering, one for
               burnt offering. The same offering Mary brought after Jesus'
               birth (Luke 2:24), indicating the holy family's poverty.

"Cut off"      — Hebrew "karat." The penalty for defiling the Tabernacle.
               Debated: may mean death by God's hand, excommunication from
               the community, or extinction of one's family line. In any
               case, it is severe — separation from God and His people.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. The body matters to God. These laws teach that holiness is not merely
   spiritual — it encompasses the whole person, including the physical
   body. In a fallen world, even normal bodily functions remind us that
   we are not yet glorified. "We groan within ourselves, waiting for the
   adoption, the redemption of our body" (Romans 8:23).

2. Uncleanness is contagious; holiness must be guarded. One person's
   uncleanness spreads to beds, seats, vessels, and other people. Sin
   and its effects are never purely private. Your condition affects those
   around you. Are you spreading cleanness or uncleanness in your
   relationships?

3. The woman with the issue of blood (Mark 5:25-34). Twelve years under
   this chapter's regulations — perpetually unclean, unable to worship,
   unable to be touched. When she touches Jesus, His holiness is not
   contaminated by her uncleanness; rather, His power cleanses her
   completely. Christ reverses the direction of contamination. In Him,
   holiness is more contagious than sin.

4. "Living water" for cleansing (v.13). The most serious uncleanness
   requires "running water" — mayim chayyim, living water. Jesus offers
   living water (John 4:10; 7:38). The deepest defilement of the human
   condition is cleansed not by stagnant religion but by the flowing,
   living power of the Holy Spirit.

5. "That they die not" (v.31). God's presence is both the greatest
   blessing and the greatest danger. To approach Him in uncleanness is
   death. But He provides the means of cleansing — washing, waiting,
   sacrifice. In Christ, we are cleansed once for all (Hebrews 10:10)
   and may "come boldly unto the throne of grace" (Hebrews 4:16).
