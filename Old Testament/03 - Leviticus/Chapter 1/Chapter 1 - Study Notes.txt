Leviticus - Chapter 1
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
God instructs Israel on burnt offerings — a voluntary act of worship where the entire animal is consumed on the altar, symbolizing complete dedication.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses. Leviticus is the third book of the Pentateuch, written during
the wilderness period (approximately 1445-1405 BC). Hebrew title: "Vayikra"
— "And He called." God speaks from the newly completed Tabernacle, giving
Israel instructions for worship, sacrifice, and holy living. Leviticus
answers the question: How does a holy God dwell among a sinful people?

Historical Context: Chapter 1 opens with God calling to Moses from the
Tabernacle — the first words spoken from God's new dwelling place among His
people. The book begins with sacrifice because that is the foundation of
relationship with a holy God. The burnt offering (Hebrew "olah" — "that which
ascends") is the first offering described because it represents total
consecration — the entire animal is consumed on the altar. Nothing is held
back. Three economic levels are provided: cattle (wealthy), sheep/goats
(middle), birds (poor) — ensuring every Israelite can approach God regardless
of wealth. Every detail points to Christ's complete, voluntary, substitutionary
sacrifice.

The Burnt Offering from the Herd (vv.1-9): A male without blemish (v.3).
Offered voluntarily (v.3). The worshipper lays his hand on the animal's head
— identification and transfer (v.4). "It shall be accepted for him to make
atonement for him" (v.4). The worshipper kills it; priests handle the blood
(v.5). The animal is cut in pieces, washed, and entirely burned (vv.6-9).
"A sweet savour unto the LORD" (v.9).

The Burnt Offering from the Flock (vv.10-13): Same requirements: male,
without blemish (v.10). Killed on the north side of the altar (v.11). Same
procedure: blood sprinkled, pieces arranged, entirely burned (vv.11-13).

The Burnt Offering of Birds (vv.14-17): Turtledoves or young pigeons (v.14).
The provision for the poor — no one is excluded from worship by poverty. The
priest handles the entire process (vv.15-17). Same result: "a sweet savour
unto the LORD" (v.17).

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "The LORD called Moses and spoke to him from the tent of meeting" (ESV)
        — God speaks from His dwelling place.

v.3  — "a male without blemish... he shall offer it at the entrance of the
        tent of meeting, that he may be accepted before the LORD" (ESV)
        — The offering must be perfect and willing.

v.4  — "He shall lay his hand on the head of the burnt offering, and it
        shall be accepted for him to make atonement for him." (ESV)
        — Identification, substitution, atonement.

v.9  — "The priest shall burn all of it on the altar, as a burnt offering,
        a food offering with a pleasing aroma to the LORD." (ESV) — Total
        consecration; pleasing to God.

v.14 — "If his offering to the LORD is a burnt offering of birds, then he
        shall bring his offering of turtledoves or pigeons." (ESV) — Provision
        for the poor.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/leviticus-1/
  Guzik covers the burnt offering as total consecration, the laying on of
  hands as identification/substitution, and the three economic levels as
  God's accessibility to all.

Charles Spurgeon:
  Sermon: "The Burnt Offering" (MTP Vol. 12, No. 681)
  "He shall put his hand upon the HEAD of the burnt offering — here is the
  transfer. The worshipper's sin passes to the animal; the animal's
  acceptance passes to the worshipper. One hand, one head, one moment — and
  the exchange is made. This is the gospel: Christ took our sin; we receive
  His righteousness. Not by works, not by merit, but by identification. Put
  your hand on HIS head — trust Him, lean on Him, transfer your guilt to
  Him — and His acceptance becomes yours."

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1  — Hebrew "ohel mo'ed" — tent of meeting/appointment.
       KJV: "the tabernacle of the congregation"
       ESV: "the tent of meeting"
       ASV: "the tent of meeting"
       NET: "the Meeting Tent"
       WEB: "the Tent of Meeting"
       Analysis: ESV/ASV are most literal. KJV's "tabernacle of the
       congregation" conflates two terms. NET capitalizes and reverses word
       order to emphasize function. All except KJV avoid "tabernacle of the
       congregation." This is where God MEETS with His people.

v.1  — Hebrew YHWH — the divine name (tetragrammaton).
       KJV: "the LORD"
       ESV: "the LORD"
       ASV: "Jehovah"
       NET: "the Lord"
       WEB: "Yahweh"
       Analysis: ASV uses "Jehovah" and WEB uses "Yahweh" for the divine
       name, where KJV/ESV render "the LORD" in small caps. NET uses "the
       Lord." Each reflects a different tradition for handling the
       tetragrammaton.

v.2  — Hebrew "ki yaqriv" — when he brings near. "Qorban" from "qarab"
       (to draw near).
       KJV: "If any man of you bring an offering"
       ESV: "When any one of you brings an offering"
       ASV: "offereth an oblation"
       NET: "presents an offering"
       WEB: "offers an offering"
       Analysis: ESV's "when" (not "if") suggests this is expected, not
       optional. ASV uses the archaic "oblation" (Hebrew "qorban"). NET's
       "presents an offering" is the most natural modern English. An
       offering is what brings you near to God.

v.3  — Hebrew "olah" — that which ascends/goes up. Hebrew "tamim" —
       without blemish, perfect.
       KJV: "a burnt sacrifice... a male without blemish"
       ESV: "a burnt offering... a male without blemish"
       ASV: "a male without blemish"
       NET: "a flawless male"
       WEB: "a male without defect"
       Analysis: ESV's "offering" is more precise than KJV's "sacrifice"
       (though both are valid). NET's "flawless" and WEB's "without defect"
       modernize KJV/ASV's "without blemish," each capturing the idea of
       physical perfection required in sacrifice.

v.3  — Hebrew "lirtsono" — for his acceptance/pleasure.
       KJV: "of his own voluntary will"
       ESV: "that he may be accepted"
       ASV: "that he may be accepted"
       NET: "so that the Lord might accept it on his behalf"
       WEB: "for his acceptance"
       Analysis: Significant difference: KJV reads it as the worshipper's
       voluntary will; ESV/ASV/NET/WEB read it as God's acceptance of the
       worshipper. The Hebrew supports both: the offering is voluntary AND
       results in acceptance.

v.4  — Hebrew "kaphar" — to cover, to make propitiation.
       KJV: "atonement"
       ESV: "atonement"
       ASV: "atonement"
       NET: "atonement"
       WEB: "atonement"
       Analysis: All translations identical. The animal's death covers the
       worshipper's sin. Universal agreement reflects the clarity of the
       Hebrew term.

v.9  — Hebrew "re'ach nichoach" — a soothing/restful aroma. Hebrew "isheh"
       — fire offering/gift.
       KJV: "a sweet savour unto the LORD"
       ESV: "a food offering with a pleasing aroma to the LORD"
       ASV: "a sweet savor unto Jehovah"
       NET: "a gift of a soothing aroma to the Lord"
       WEB: "a pleasant aroma to Yahweh"
       Analysis: NET uniquely adds "gift" (translating Hebrew "isheh") and
       uses "soothing aroma." ESV adds "food offering." WEB modernizes to
       "pleasant." ASV retains the archaic "sweet savor." The divine name
       difference is also visible here. NET renders "hiqtir" as "offer up
       in smoke" rather than simply "burn," clarifying that the burning is
       a ritual act of sending the offering heavenward, not mere
       destruction.

v.14 — Hebrew "min ha'oph" — from the birds.
       KJV: "of fowls"
       ESV: "of birds"
       ASV: "of birds"
       NET: "of birds"
       WEB: "of birds"
       Analysis: ESV/ASV/NET/WEB use modern "birds"; KJV uses archaic
       "fowls." The provision for the poor: even those who cannot afford
       cattle or sheep can bring turtledoves or pigeons.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Burnt offering (Olah) — "That which ascends." The entire animal is consumed
               on the altar — nothing held back. Represents total
               consecration and dedication to God. Points to Christ's
               complete self-offering.

"Without blemish" (tamim) — Perfect, whole, without defect. The sacrifice
               must be the best, not the leftovers. Christ was without sin
               — the perfect sacrifice.

Laying on of hands — The worshipper places his hand on the animal's head.
               Identification: "this animal represents me." Transfer: "my
               sin passes to it." Substitution: "it dies in my place."

Atonement (kaphar) — To cover, to propitiate, to make reconciliation. The
               sacrifice covers sin and restores relationship with God.

"Sweet savour" (re'ach nichoach) — A pleasing/soothing aroma to God. Not
               the smell of burning flesh but what it represents: obedience,
               faith, and consecration. Christ's sacrifice is a "sweet
               savour" to God (Ephesians 5:2).

Voluntary      — The burnt offering is not compelled but willing. True
               worship cannot be forced. Christ offered Himself willingly
               (John 10:18).

Three economic levels — Bull (wealthy), sheep/goat (middle), bird (poor).
               God ensures no one is excluded from worship by poverty.
               Access to God is not determined by wealth.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "He shall offer it of his own voluntary will" (v.3). True worship is
   voluntary — not compelled, not reluctant. Is your devotion to God
   willing? Or are you going through motions?

2. "He shall put his hand upon the head" (v.4). Have you placed your hand
   on Christ's head — identifying with Him, transferring your sin to Him,
   receiving His acceptance? This is faith: leaning on the substitute.

3. "The priest shall burn ALL on the altar" (v.9). The burnt offering holds
   nothing back. Is your consecration total? Or are you keeping parts of
   your life off the altar?

4. Three levels of offering (bull, sheep, bird) — God meets you where you
   are economically. He doesn't demand what you don't have. But He does
   demand your BEST, whatever that is. Are you giving God your best?

5. "A sweet savour unto the LORD" (v.9). Your worship — your sacrifice of
   time, talent, treasure, self — is pleasing to God. Not because you're
   perfect but because you come through the substitute. Does knowing this
   motivate your worship?
