Leviticus - Chapter 21
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
God sets higher standards of holiness for priests — restrictions on mourning, marriage, and physical qualifications for those who serve at His altar.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses. Leviticus is the third book of the Pentateuch, written during
the wilderness period (approximately 1445-1405 BC). Hebrew title: "Vayikra"
— "And He called." God speaks from the newly completed Tabernacle, giving
Israel instructions for worship, sacrifice, and holy living.

Historical Context: Chapter 21 shifts focus from the holiness required of
ALL Israel (chapters 18-20) to the HIGHER holiness required of priests.
If all Israel must be holy, the priests must be holier still — and the
high priest holiest of all. The chapter establishes a graduated scale of
holiness: common Israelite → priest → high priest. Each level has stricter
requirements. The logic is proximity to God: the closer one serves to
God's presence, the higher the standard. Priests handle holy things, offer
God's "bread" (sacrifices), and mediate between God and the people. Their
lives must reflect the holiness of the One they serve. The chapter
addresses three areas: (1) restrictions on mourning and defilement by the
dead (vv.1-6, 10-12); (2) restrictions on marriage (vv.7-8, 13-15); and
(3) restrictions based on physical defects (vv.16-24). These regulations
are not about the personal worth of the priest but about the symbolic
integrity of the office. The priest represents God to the people — his
life and person must visibly reflect God's wholeness and holiness. This
chapter points forward to Christ, our perfect High Priest — without
blemish, without defilement, wholly consecrated to God (Hebrews 7:26).

Priests and Defilement by the Dead (vv.1-6): God speaks to Moses for "the
priests the sons of Aaron" (v.1). A priest "shall none be defiled for the
dead among his people" (v.1) — contact with a corpse makes one unclean
for seven days (Numbers 19:11). Exceptions: immediate family — mother,
father, son, daughter, brother (v.2), and a virgin sister who has no
husband (v.3). For these closest relatives, a priest may defile himself.
But he "shall not defile himself, being a chief man among his people"
(v.4) — his position limits his mourning. Priests must not make bald
patches on their heads, shave the edges of their beards, or cut their
flesh (v.5) — these were pagan mourning customs. "They shall be holy unto
their God, and not profane the name of their God" (v.6). The reason: "the
offerings of the LORD made by fire, and the bread of their God, they do
offer: therefore they shall be holy" (v.6). Their function demands their
holiness.

Priestly Marriage Restrictions (vv.7-9): A priest shall not marry a
prostitute, a defiled woman, or a divorced woman (v.7). "For he is holy
unto his God" (v.7). The congregation must "sanctify him" — treat him as
holy (v.8). "He shall be holy unto thee: for I the LORD, which sanctify
you, am holy" (v.8). A priest's daughter who becomes a prostitute
"profaneth her father" — she is burned with fire (v.9). The priest's
family reflects on his office.

The High Priest — Highest Standard (vv.10-15): "He that is the high priest
among his brethren" — the one anointed and consecrated to wear the
garments (v.10). He shall NOT uncover his head (let hair hang loose) or
tear his clothes (v.10) — the normal signs of mourning are forbidden to
him entirely. He shall not go near ANY dead body — not even his father or
mother (v.11). He shall not leave the sanctuary (v.12). "The crown of the
anointing oil of his God is upon him" (v.12) — his consecration overrides
all other obligations. Marriage: he must take a virgin (v.13). Not a widow,
divorced woman, defiled woman, or prostitute — only "a virgin of his own
people" (v.14). The purpose: "that he may not profane his seed among his
people" (v.15). The high priest's family line must be pure. He represents
the highest standard of holiness in Israel.

Physical Blemishes and Priestly Service (vv.16-24): God speaks to Moses
for Aaron (v.16). No descendant of Aaron with a physical blemish may
"approach to offer the bread of his God" (v.17). The list: blind, lame,
mutilated face, limb too long (v.18), injured foot or hand (v.19),
hunchback, dwarf, eye defect, skin disease, scabs, crushed testicles
(v.20). No blemished priest may "come nigh to offer the offerings of the
LORD made by fire" (v.21). However — he MAY eat the priestly food, "both
of the most holy, and of the holy" (v.22). He retains his priestly
identity and provision — he simply cannot serve at the altar or enter
beyond the veil (v.23). "That he profane not my sanctuaries: for I the
LORD do sanctify them" (v.23). Moses communicates this to Aaron, his sons,
and all Israel (v.24).

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.6  — "They shall be holy to their God and not profane the name of their
        God. For they offer the LORD's food offerings, the bread of their
        God; therefore they shall be holy." (ESV) — Function demands
        holiness. Those who handle holy things must be holy.

v.7  — "They shall not marry a prostitute or a woman who has been defiled,
        neither shall they marry a woman divorced from her husband, for the
        priest is holy to his God." (ESV) — The priest's marriage reflects
        his office.

v.8  — "For I, the LORD, who sanctify you, am holy." (ESV) — God's
        holiness is both the standard and the source of priestly holiness.

v.11 — "He shall not go in to any dead bodies nor make himself unclean,
        even for his father or for his mother." (ESV) — The high priest's
        consecration overrides even family obligations.

v.12 — "for the consecration of the anointing oil of his God is upon him:
        I am the LORD." (ESV) — The anointing sets the high priest apart
        absolutely.

v.22 — "He may eat the bread of his God, both of the most holy and of the
        holy things." (ESV) — A blemished priest retains his identity and
        provision even though he cannot serve at the altar.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/leviticus-21/
  Guzik emphasizes the graduated holiness: all Israel is holy, priests are
  holier, the high priest is holiest. He notes that the physical blemish
  restrictions are not about personal worth but about symbolic
  representation — the priest's body must visibly represent God's
  wholeness. He connects this to Christ as the perfect High Priest.

Charles Spurgeon:
  Sermon: "The Perfect Priest"
  "The priest must be without blemish — not because a lame man is less
  loved by God, but because the priest REPRESENTS God to the people. He is
  a living picture of the Holy One. And the picture must not be marred.
  Every physical requirement points forward to Christ — our High Priest
  who is 'holy, harmless, undefiled, separate from sinners, and made
  higher than the heavens' (Hebrews 7:26). No blemish in Him — no sin, no
  defect, no weakness, no failure. He is the perfect Priest who perfectly
  represents the perfect God. And because He is perfect, His offering is
  perfect, and His intercession is perfect, and His salvation is perfect.
  What Aaron could only picture, Christ IS."

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1  — Hebrew "lo yitamma b'ammav" — he shall not make himself unclean among his people.
       KJV: "There shall none be defiled for the dead among his people"
       ESV: "No one shall make himself unclean for the dead among his people"
       ASV: "There shall none defile himself for the dead among his people"
       NET: "For a dead person no priest is to defile himself"
       Analysis: ESV's "make himself unclean" emphasizes the voluntary nature — the priest must actively avoid defilement. NET's "dead person" (Hebrew "nephesh" — literally "soul/person") clarifies that defilement comes from contact with a corpse. The same word "nephesh" means both "living soul" and "dead body."

v.4  — Hebrew "lo yitamma ba'al b'ammav l'hechallo" — a difficult verse.
       KJV: "he shall not defile himself, being a chief man among his people, to profane himself"
       ESV: "He shall not make himself unclean as a husband among his people and so profane himself"
       Analysis: KJV reads "ba'al" as "chief/master" — his position prevents defilement. ESV reads "ba'al" as "husband" — he shall not defile himself for in-laws (by marriage). Both are possible readings of the Hebrew.

v.5  — Hebrew "lo yiqr'chu qorchah b'rosham" — shaving the head as pagan mourning.
       KJV: "They shall not make baldness upon their head"
       ESV: "They shall not make bald patches on their heads"
       ASV: "they must not cut slashes in their body"
       NET: "they must not cut slashes in their body"
       Analysis: Shaving the head was a pagan mourning custom (also practiced in Egypt). NET's "slashes" specifies the mourning practice of cutting oneself for the dead — a pagan custom forbidden for all Israel (19:28) and especially for priests.

v.6  — Hebrew "isheh YHWH" and "lechem" — the LORD's offerings and bread/food.
       KJV: "the offerings of the LORD made by fire, and the bread of their God"
       ASV: "the offerings of Jehovah made by fire, the bread of their God"
       NET: "the Lord's gifts, the food of their God"
       Analysis: NET renders "isheh YHWH" as "the Lord's gifts" and "lechem" as "food." ASV uses "bread" (more literal). The sacrifices are called God's "food" — anthropomorphic language for offerings that satisfy/please Him.

v.7  — Hebrew "zonah va-chalalah" — a prostitute and a profaned woman.
       KJV: "a wife that is a whore, or profane"
       ESV: "a prostitute or a woman who has been defiled"
       Analysis: "Zonah" = a prostitute/harlot. "Chalalah" = a profaned/defiled woman (one who has been sexually violated or who has lost her virginal status in a dishonorable way). KJV's "profane" is literal; ESV interprets as "defiled."

v.10 — Hebrew "et rosho lo yifra v'et b'gadav lo yifrom" — not loosening hair or tearing clothes.
       KJV: "shall not uncover his head, nor rend his clothes"
       ESV: "shall not let the hair of his head hang loose nor tear his clothes"
       ASV: "he that is the high priest among his brethren"
       NET: "The high priest"
       Analysis: "Yifra" = to let loose, unbind (the hair). KJV's "uncover" suggests removing a head covering; ESV's "let hang loose" refers to disheveling the hair as a sign of mourning. ESV is more accurate to the Hebrew. NET simply says "The high priest" where ASV/KJV describe "he that is the high priest among his brethren."

v.12 — Hebrew "nezer shemen ha-mishchah" — crown/consecration of anointing oil.
       KJV: "the crown of the anointing oil"
       ESV: "the consecration of the anointing oil"
       Analysis: "Nezer" = crown, consecration, separation (same root as "Nazirite"). The anointing oil is his "crown" — his mark of consecration. It sets him apart as surely as a crown sets apart a king.

v.13 — Hebrew "b'tulah" — a virgin.
       KJV: "he shall take a wife in her virginity"
       ESV: "He shall take a wife in her virginity"
       ASV: "he shall take a wife in her virginity"
       NET: "He must marry a virgin"
       WEB: "He shall take a wife in her virginity"
       Analysis: NET is direct; ASV follows the Hebrew idiom more closely. The high priest's bride must be a virgin — pointing to Christ and His pure bride, the Church.

v.17 — Hebrew "mum" — blemish/physical flaw.
       KJV: "Whosoever he be of thy seed throughout their generations that hath a blemish"
       ASV: "Whosoever he be of thy seed throughout their generations that hath a blemish"
       NET: "No man from your descendants throughout their generations who has a physical flaw is to approach to present the food of his God"
       Analysis: NET's "physical flaw" clarifies this is not moral but physical disqualification from altar service, while still permitting them to eat priestly food (v.22).

v.18 — Hebrew "iver o fisse'ach o charum o sarua" — blind, lame, mutilated, extended.
       KJV: "a blind man, or a lame, or he that hath a flat nose, or any thing superfluous"
       ESV: "a man blind or lame, or one who has a mutilated face or a limb too long"
       Analysis: "Charum" = flat-nosed/mutilated face (KJV: "flat nose"; ESV: "mutilated face"). "Sarua" = extended/stretched (KJV: "superfluous" = extra; ESV: "limb too long"). Both translations attempt to render rare Hebrew terms for physical conditions.

v.20 — Hebrew "gibben o daq" — hunchbacked or thin/small.
       KJV: "crookbackt, or a dwarf"
       ESV: "a hunchback or a dwarf"
       Analysis: "Gibben" = hunchbacked (KJV's "crookbackt" is archaic). "Daq" = thin/small (a dwarf or emaciated person). These are rare terms, and exact identification is debated.

v.20 — Hebrew "m'ro'ach ashekh" — damaged reproductive organs.
       KJV: "hath his stones broken"
       ESV: "crushed testicles"
       Analysis: KJV's "stones broken" is a euphemism; ESV is direct. A man with damaged reproductive organs cannot serve at the altar — the priest must represent wholeness and the capacity for life.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
"Bread of God" (Lechem Elohim) — A term for the sacrificial offerings,
               especially the food offerings burned on the altar. Not
               literal bread for God to eat, but the sacrifices presented
               as God's "food" — His portion. The priest who offers God's
               "bread" must be holy, just as the bread itself must be
               without defect.

Graduated Holiness — The principle that holiness increases with proximity
               to God. All Israel is holy (set apart from the nations).
               Priests are holier (set apart from Israel for service).
               The high priest is holiest (set apart even from other
               priests). The closer to God's presence, the stricter the
               requirements.

Anointing Oil (Shemen ha-Mishchah) — The sacred oil poured on the high
               priest's head at his consecration (Exodus 30:22-33). Made
               of myrrh, cinnamon, calamus, cassia, and olive oil. It
               marks him as God's chosen representative. Called his "crown"
               (nezer) — his mark of authority and consecration.

Physical Blemish (Mum) — Any physical defect that disqualifies a priest
               from altar service. Not a statement about personal worth
               but about symbolic representation. The priest's body must
               visibly represent God's wholeness and perfection. A
               blemished priest may still eat holy food (v.22) — he
               retains his priestly identity.

"Profane" (Chalal) — To make common, to defile, to treat as unholy. The
               opposite of "sanctify" (qadash). A priest profanes himself
               by violating his consecration — improper mourning, improper
               marriage, or improper service. To profane is to blur the
               line between holy and common.

Virgin (B'tulah) — The high priest must marry a virgin — one who has not
               been "profaned" by prior sexual contact. This represents
               the purity and exclusivity of the priestly line. Points to
               Christ and His bride, the Church — "a chaste virgin"
               (2 Corinthians 11:2).

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Greater privilege = greater responsibility. Priests have higher
   standards than common Israelites. The high priest has higher standards
   than regular priests. "Unto whomsoever much is given, of him shall be
   much required" (Luke 12:48). If God has given you a position of
   spiritual leadership — pastor, teacher, parent, elder — you are held
   to a higher standard. Not because you are better, but because you
   represent God to others.

2. The high priest cannot mourn even for his parents (v.11). His
   consecration overrides even the deepest human bonds. Christ said:
   "He that loveth father or mother more than me is not worthy of me"
   (Matthew 10:37). Is there any relationship or obligation you place
   above your consecration to God?

3. A blemished priest may eat but not serve (v.22). He is not rejected —
   he is still provided for, still part of the priestly family. But he
   cannot represent God at the altar. This is not cruelty but integrity
   of representation. God's representative must visibly reflect God's
   wholeness. Christ fulfills this perfectly — He is the unblemished
   Priest (Hebrews 7:26).

4. "I the LORD do sanctify them" (v.23). God sanctifies both the priests
   and the sanctuaries. Holiness is ultimately God's work, not man's
   achievement. He sets apart, He consecrates, He makes holy. Your job is
   to cooperate with His sanctifying work — not to resist it or neglect it.

5. Every requirement points to Christ. No human priest could perfectly
   fulfill these standards — they all had blemishes of character if not
   of body. But Christ is "holy, harmless, undefiled, separate from
   sinners" (Hebrews 7:26). He is the perfect Priest that every Aaronic
   priest could only foreshadow. Rest in His perfection, not your own.
