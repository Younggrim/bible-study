Leviticus - Chapter 27
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
God gives rules for redeeming vows and dedicated things — what is consecrated to the Lord must be honored.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses. Leviticus is the third book of the Pentateuch, written during
the wilderness period (approximately 1445-1405 BC). Hebrew title: "Vayikra"
— "And He called." God speaks from the newly completed Tabernacle, giving
Israel instructions for worship, sacrifice, and holy living.

Historical Context: Chapter 27 is the concluding chapter of Leviticus,
functioning as an appendix to the book. After the formal conclusion of the
Holiness Code in 26:46, this chapter addresses a practical matter: vows
and dedications to the LORD. When someone makes a voluntary vow to dedicate
a person, animal, house, or field to God, how is that vow valued and
fulfilled? The chapter provides a systematic valuation system and
establishes principles for redemption (buying back what was dedicated).
The placement is significant: after the blessings and curses of chapter 26
(what God requires), chapter 27 addresses voluntary devotion (what people
choose to give beyond what is required). It moves from obligation to
gratitude, from law to love. The chapter also addresses the tithe (vv.30-33)
— the foundational principle that a tenth of everything belongs to God.
The final verse (v.34) closes the entire book: "These are the commandments,
which the LORD commanded Moses for the children of Israel in mount Sinai."
Everything in Leviticus — from the first sacrifice to the last tithe —
comes from God, through Moses, at Sinai. The book that began with God
calling from the Tabernacle ends with the reminder that all these
instructions originated at the holy mountain.

Valuation of Persons Dedicated by Vow (vv.1-8): When someone makes a
"singular vow" (KJV) / "special vow" (ESV) — dedicating a person to the
LORD's service (v.2). Since the person cannot literally be sacrificed, a
monetary equivalent is established:
- Male 20-60 years: 50 shekels of silver (v.3)
- Female 20-60 years: 30 shekels (v.4)
- Male 5-20 years: 20 shekels (v.5)
- Female 5-20 years: 10 shekels (v.5)
- Male 1 month-5 years: 5 shekels (v.6)
- Female 1 month-5 years: 3 shekels (v.6)
- Male 60+ years: 15 shekels (v.7)
- Female 60+ years: 10 shekels (v.7)
If the person is too poor to pay the standard valuation, the priest
assesses according to ability (v.8). The valuations reflect economic
productivity in an agricultural society, not inherent human worth. God
accommodates poverty — no one is excluded from making vows by inability
to pay the full amount. Note: Jesus was valued at 30 pieces of silver
(Matthew 26:15) — the price of a female slave or a male between 5-20.
The Messiah was sold for the price of the least valuable category of
adult.

Dedication of Animals (vv.9-13): If the vowed animal is one that may be
offered to the LORD, it becomes holy and cannot be exchanged — good for
bad or bad for good (vv.9-10). If someone attempts a substitution, BOTH
animals become holy (v.10). If the animal is unclean (not suitable for
sacrifice), the priest values it (vv.11-12). If the owner wishes to
redeem it, he adds a fifth (20%) to the valuation (v.13). The principle:
what is given to God cannot be taken back casually. A vow to God is
serious — "better is it that thou shouldest not vow, than that thou
shouldest vow and not pay" (Ecclesiastes 5:5).

Dedication of Houses (vv.14-15): When a man dedicates his house to the
LORD, the priest values it (v.14). If the owner wishes to redeem it, he
adds a fifth to the price (v.15). The same principle: dedication is
serious, and redemption costs more than the original value.

Dedication of Fields (vv.16-25): If a man dedicates part of his inherited
field: the valuation is based on the seed required — a homer of barley
seed = 50 shekels (v.16). If dedicated from the Jubilee year, full
valuation stands (v.17). If after Jubilee, the priest calculates based
on remaining years until the next Jubilee (v.18). If the owner redeems:
add a fifth (v.19). If he does NOT redeem, or sells it to another, it
can never be redeemed — at Jubilee it becomes permanently holy, belonging
to the priests as a devoted field (vv.20-21). If a man dedicates a
PURCHASED field (not inherited): the priest calculates value until Jubilee;
the man pays that day; at Jubilee the field returns to the original owner
from whom it was purchased (vv.22-24). All valuations use the sanctuary
shekel: 20 gerahs per shekel (v.25).

Firstborn Animals (v.26): The firstborn already belongs to God (Exodus
13:2) — therefore no one may "dedicate" it as a vow. You cannot give God
what already belongs to Him. "Whether it be ox, or sheep: it is the
LORD's."

Unclean Firstborn and Devoted Things (vv.27-29): An unclean firstborn
animal: redeemed at valuation plus a fifth, or sold at valuation (v.27).
"Devoted things" (cherem) — anything devoted to the LORD under a ban of
total dedication — cannot be sold or redeemed. "Every devoted thing is
most holy unto the LORD" (v.28). A person under the ban of devotion
(cherem) "shall surely be put to death" (v.29). This is the most extreme
form of dedication — irrevocable, total, and in the case of persons under
divine judgment, resulting in death (cf. the ban on Jericho, Joshua 6:17;
Achan's violation, Joshua 7).

The Tithe (vv.30-33): "All the tithe of the land, whether of the seed of
the land, or of the fruit of the tree, is the LORD's: it is holy unto the
LORD" (v.30). If someone wishes to redeem part of his tithe (keep it and
pay money instead), he adds a fifth (v.31). The tithe of herds and flocks:
every tenth animal that passes under the counting rod is holy to the LORD
(v.32). The owner must not select good or bad — whatever passes tenth is
the LORD's. If he substitutes, both the original and substitute become
holy (v.33). The tithe is not a gift — it is a recognition that everything
belongs to God and He claims a tenth as His own.

Closing Statement (v.34): "These are the commandments, which the LORD
commanded Moses for the children of Israel in mount Sinai." This closes
the entire book of Leviticus. Everything — sacrifices, priesthood,
cleanness laws, holiness code, feasts, blessings, curses, vows, and
tithes — all originated from God, delivered through Moses, at Sinai.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "If anyone makes a special vow to the LORD involving the valuation
        of persons." (ESV) — Voluntary dedication to God, assessed at
        proper value.

v.8  — "But if someone is too poor to pay the valuation, then he shall be
        made to stand before the priest, and the priest shall value him;
        the priest shall value him according to what the vower can afford."
        (ESV) — God accommodates poverty. No one is excluded from devotion
        by inability.

v.10 — "He shall not exchange it or make a substitute for it, good for bad,
        or bad for good." (ESV) — What is given to God must not be taken
        back or downgraded.

v.26 — "But a firstborn of animals, which as a firstborn belongs to the
        LORD, no man may dedicate... it is the LORD's." (ESV) — You cannot
        give God what already belongs to Him.

v.28 — "every devoted thing is most holy to the LORD." (ESV) — The cherem:
        total, irrevocable dedication.

v.30 — "Every tithe of the land, whether of the seed of the land or of
        the fruit of the trees, is the LORD's; it is holy to the LORD."
        (ESV) — The tenth belongs to God. It is not optional giving but
        divine ownership.

v.32 — "every tenth animal of all that pass under the herdsman's staff
        shall be holy to the LORD." (ESV) — God chooses the tenth; man
        does not select.

v.34 — "These are the commandments that the LORD commanded Moses for the
        people of Israel on Mount Sinai." (ESV) — The closing of Leviticus:
        all from God, all at Sinai, all through Moses.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/leviticus-27/
  Guzik notes that this chapter addresses voluntary devotion beyond
  required obedience — the overflow of a grateful heart. He emphasizes
  that vows to God are serious and should not be made lightly. He connects
  the 30 pieces of silver valuation to Christ's betrayal price.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "A vow to God is a sacred thing. It is not to be made in the heat of
  emotion and forgotten in the cold of morning. When you have promised God
  something — your time, your talent, your treasure — He holds you to it.
  Not because He needs it, but because your word reveals your heart. 'It
  is a snare to the man who devoureth that which is holy, and after vows
  to make enquiry' (Proverbs 20:25). Count the cost before you vow. But
  having vowed — pay."

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.2  — Hebrew "ki yaphli neder" — when he makes extraordinary/special a vow.
       KJV: "When a man shall make a singular vow"
       ESV: "If anyone makes a special vow"
       ASV: "When a man shall accomplish a vow, the persons shall be for Jehovah by thy estimation"
       NET: "When a man makes a special votive offering based on the conversion value of a person to the Lord"
       WEB: "to Yahweh"
       Analysis: "Yaphli" is from "pala" (to be wonderful, extraordinary, difficult). This is not an ordinary vow but a special, extraordinary dedication. KJV's "singular" and ESV's "special" both capture the unusual nature. NET's "conversion value" clarifies the system: a person dedicated to God by vow can be redeemed by paying their monetary equivalent.

v.2  — Hebrew "b'erkekha nefashot la-YHWH" — by your valuation, persons to the LORD.
       KJV: "the persons shall be for the LORD by thy estimation"
       ESV: "involving the valuation of persons"
       ASV: "for Jehovah"
       NET: "to the Lord"
       WEB: "to Yahweh"
       Analysis: The vow dedicates a person's value (not the person as a sacrifice) to God's service. Vows are made to YHWH personally — not to a concept or institution but to the living God who holds you accountable.

v.3  — Hebrew "shekel ha-qodesh" — the shekel of the sanctuary.
       KJV: "fifty shekels of silver, after the shekel of the sanctuary"
       ESV: "fifty shekels of silver, according to the shekel of the sanctuary"
       Analysis: The sanctuary shekel was the standard weight (v.25: 20 gerahs = 1 shekel). This prevented fraud by using a lighter shekel. All sacred transactions use the sanctuary standard.

v.8  — Hebrew "al pi asher tassig yad ha-noder" — according to what the hand of the vower can reach.
       KJV: "according to his ability that vowed shall the priest value him"
       ESV: "the priest shall value him according to what the vower can afford"
       Analysis: Beautiful idiom: "what his hand can reach" = what he can afford. God does not demand beyond ability.

v.10 — Hebrew "lo yahliphenu v'lo yamir" — he shall not exchange it and not substitute it.
       KJV: "He shall not alter it, nor change it"
       ESV: "He shall not exchange it or make a substitute for it"
       ASV: "he shall not alter it, nor change it"
       NET: "he must not exchange it or substitute for it"
       WEB: "He shall not alter it, nor change it"
       Analysis: Two verbs emphasizing the same prohibition: once given to God, it cannot be swapped for something lesser. NET distinguishes "exchange" (swap entirely) from "substitute" (replace).

v.21 — Hebrew "k'sdeh ha-cherem" — like a field of the ban/devotion.
       KJV: "as a field devoted"
       ESV: "like a field that has been devoted"
       ASV: "the field, when it goeth out in the jubilee, shall be holy unto Jehovah, as a field devoted"
       NET: "it will be holy to the Lord like a permanently dedicated field; it will become the priest's property"
       Analysis: "Cherem" is the most extreme form of dedication — irrevocable, total consecration to God. NET clarifies "cherem" as "permanently dedicated." Unlike normal vows (which can be redeemed), "devoted things" cannot be bought back. A devoted field becomes permanent priestly property.

v.28 — Hebrew "kol cherem" — every devoted thing.
       KJV: "no devoted thing"
       ESV: "no devoted thing"
       ASV: "devoted"
       NET: "set apart to the Lord"
       Analysis: "Cherem" (ban, devotion) is distinct from ordinary dedication. It is total, irrevocable, and cannot be redeemed. NET's "set apart" is softer than the Hebrew implies — "cherem" means irrevocably given to God, often for destruction (as in the conquest of Jericho). Nothing devoted can be sold or redeemed.

v.29 — Hebrew "kol cherem asher yacharam min ha-adam lo yippadeh; mot yumat."
       KJV: "None devoted, which shall be devoted of men, shall be redeemed; but shall surely be put to death"
       ESV: "No one devoted, who is to be devoted for destruction from mankind, shall be ransomed; he shall surely be put to death"
       Analysis: ESV adds "for destruction" to clarify that this refers to persons under divine judgment (not voluntary dedication of living persons). This applies to those under God's ban of judgment — such as the inhabitants of Canaan (Deuteronomy 7:2) or Achan (Joshua 7:15).

v.30 — Hebrew "kol ma'sar ha-aretz" — every tenth of the land.
       KJV: "all the tithe of the land"
       ESV: "Every tithe of the land"
       Analysis: "Ma'sar" from "eser" (ten) — a tenth. The tithe is not a voluntary offering but a recognition of God's ownership. It is "holy to the LORD" — set apart as His property.

v.32 — Hebrew "kol asher ya'avor tachat ha-shevet" — all that passes under the rod/staff.
       KJV: "whatsoever passeth under the rod"
       ESV: "every tenth animal of all that pass under the herdsman's staff"
       Analysis: The practice: animals are driven single-file through a narrow gate; every tenth one is marked with a rod dipped in dye. The owner does not choose — God chooses by the count. KJV preserves the Hebrew image; ESV explains the practice.

v.34 — Hebrew "b'har Sinai" — on/in Mount Sinai.
       KJV: "in mount Sinai"
       ESV: "on Mount Sinai"
       ASV: "These are the commandments, which Jehovah commanded Moses for the children of Israel in mount Sinai"
       NET: "These are the commandments that the Lord commanded Moses to give to the Israelites at Mount Sinai"
       Analysis: This closing statement brackets the entire book with its opening (1:1 — God speaking from the Tabernacle, which was at Sinai). All of Leviticus is Sinai legislation — divine instruction given at the holy mountain. NET adds "to give" for natural English.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Vow (Neder)    — A voluntary promise to God, usually made in gratitude or
               in petition. Once made, it is binding (Numbers 30:2;
               Deuteronomy 23:21). Vows could dedicate persons, animals,
               property, or money to God's service.

Valuation (Erekh) — The assessed monetary value of a person or thing
               dedicated by vow. Set by God (standard rates) or by the
               priest (for the poor). The valuation allows the vow to be
               fulfilled with money rather than literal service.

Redemption (Ga'al / Padah) — Buying back what was dedicated to God. The
               redeemer pays the valuation plus a fifth (20% penalty).
               Redemption is possible for most dedications but NOT for
               devoted things (cherem) or firstborn animals.

The Fifth (Chomesh) — A 20% surcharge added when redeeming dedicated
               items (vv.13, 15, 19, 27, 31). The penalty discourages
               casual vowing and casual redemption. It costs MORE to take
               back what you gave to God.

Cherem (Devoted Thing) — The most extreme form of dedication: total,
               irrevocable, unredeemable. Applied to: (1) voluntary
               irrevocable gifts to God; (2) persons/cities under divine
               judgment (Jericho, Amalek). Once something is cherem, it
               belongs absolutely to God — no human may benefit from it.

Tithe (Ma'sar) — A tenth of all produce and livestock, belonging to God.
               Not a gift but a recognition of divine ownership. Three
               tithes in the Torah: (1) Levitical tithe (Numbers 18:21);
               (2) Festival tithe (Deuteronomy 14:22-27); (3) Poor tithe
               every third year (Deuteronomy 14:28-29).

Shekel of the Sanctuary — The standard weight for all sacred transactions:
               20 gerahs = 1 shekel (v.25). Approximately 11.4 grams of
               silver. Used to prevent fraud by ensuring a consistent,
               honest measure.

"Passeth under the rod" — The method of selecting the tithe from livestock.
               Animals pass single-file; every tenth is marked and belongs
               to God. The owner has no choice in which animals are
               selected — preventing the temptation to give God the worst.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Vows are serious (vv.2-8). In our casual culture, promises are made
   and broken easily. But a vow to God is sacred. "When thou vowest a vow
   unto God, defer not to pay it" (Ecclesiastes 5:4). Have you made
   promises to God — in prayer, in crisis, in worship — that you have not
   kept? God remembers. Better to fulfill a small vow than to make a grand
   one and forget it.

2. You cannot give God what already belongs to Him (v.26). The firstborn
   is already His — you cannot "dedicate" it as though it were yours to
   give. Similarly, the tithe is already God's (v.30). Tithing is not
   generosity — it is honesty. Generosity begins ABOVE the tithe. Are you
   giving God what is already His, or are you giving beyond what is
   required?

3. God accommodates poverty (v.8). The poor man is not excluded from
   making vows. The priest assesses according to ability. God does not
   demand what you cannot give. He asks for your heart's devotion expressed
   according to your means. "If there be first a willing mind, it is
   accepted according to that a man hath, and not according to that he
   hath not" (2 Corinthians 8:12).

4. The cost of taking back (the fifth). Every redemption costs 20% more
   than the original value. There is a cost to taking back what you gave
   to God. This should make us count the cost BEFORE we vow, and think
   twice before we reclaim what we dedicated. "No man, having put his hand
   to the plough, and looking back, is fit for the kingdom of God"
   (Luke 9:62).

5. "These are the commandments... in mount Sinai" (v.34). Leviticus ends
   where it began — with God speaking. The entire book is God's word to
   His redeemed people about how to live in His presence. From the first
   burnt offering to the last tithe, it is all about one thing: a holy
   God making a way for unholy people to dwell with Him. And it all points
   to Christ — the perfect sacrifice, the true priest, the final
   atonement, the Jubilee, the Light, the Bread, the Lamb without blemish.
