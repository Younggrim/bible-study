Leviticus - Chapter 3
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Section 1 — Peace Offering from the Herd (vv.1-5):
 Male or female, without blemish. The worshipper lays his hand on the
 animal's head and kills it at the entrance of the tent of meeting. The
 priests sprinkle the blood on the altar. The fat covering the entrails,
 the two kidneys with their fat, and the long lobe of the liver are
 burned on the altar atop the burnt offering — a food offering with a
 pleasing aroma.

Section 2 — Peace Offering from the Flock: A Lamb (vv.6-11):
 Male or female, without blemish. Same procedure: laying on of hands,
 killing, blood sprinkled. The fat, the entire fat tail (cut off close
 to the backbone), the fat on the entrails, kidneys, and liver lobe are
 burned. The priest burns it as "a food offering to the LORD."

Section 3 — Peace Offering from the Flock: A Goat (vv.12-16):
 Same procedure as above. The fat, entrails, kidneys, and liver lobe are
 burned. "All fat is the LORD's" (v.16).

Section 4 — The Perpetual Statute (v.17):
 A permanent command for all generations: "You shall eat neither fat nor
 blood." The fat belongs to God; the blood is the life and belongs to God
 alone.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses. Leviticus is the third book of the Pentateuch, written during
the wilderness period (approximately 1445-1405 BC). Hebrew title: "Vayikra"
— "And He called." God speaks from the Tabernacle, giving instructions for
worship, sacrifice, and holy living.

Historical Context: The peace offering (Hebrew: "shelamim," from "shalom" —
peace, wholeness, well-being) is the third offering described in Leviticus.
Unlike the burnt offering (entirely consumed) or the grain offering (shared
between God and priests), the peace offering was a communal meal — portions
were given to God (the fat burned on the altar), to the priest (the breast
and right thigh, detailed in Lev 7:31-34), and to the worshipper and his
family (the remaining meat). It was the only offering where the worshipper
ate a portion.

This offering was voluntary — an expression of thanksgiving, fulfillment of
a vow, or a freewill act of devotion (Lev 7:11-16). It celebrated fellowship
and communion with God. The shared meal pictured reconciliation — God and man
eating together in peace. This points forward to Christ, who is "our peace"
(Eph 2:14), who broke down the dividing wall and reconciled us to God,
making fellowship possible through His blood.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1 — "If his offering is a sacrifice of peace offering, if he offers an
 animal from the herd, male or female, he shall offer it without blemish
 before the LORD." — Peace with God requires a spotless sacrifice.
 Christ offered Himself "without blemish" (1 Pet 1:19).

v.2 — "He shall lay his hand on the head of his offering and kill it." —
 Identification: the worshipper identifies with the sacrifice. The
 animal dies in his place. We are identified with Christ in His death.

v.5 — "It is a food offering with a pleasing aroma to the LORD." — God
 finds satisfaction in the peace offering. Our fellowship with God
 through Christ is pleasing to the Father.

v.11 — "The priest shall burn it on the altar as a food offering to the
 LORD." — Called "a food offering" (ESV) — not that God needs
 food, but that He receives it with satisfaction, as one receives a meal
 prepared with love.

v.16 — "All fat is the LORD's." — The fat represents the best, the richest
 portion. God claims the best for Himself. The first and finest belongs
 to Him.

v.17 — "It shall be a statute forever throughout your generations... that
 you eat neither fat nor blood." — Fat belongs to God (the best is His);
 blood belongs to God (life is His). These are non-negotiable.

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1  — Hebrew "zevach shelamim" — sacrifice of peace offerings. "Shelamim"
       from "shalom" (peace, wholeness, completeness).
       KJV: "sacrifice of peace offering"
       ESV: "sacrifice of peace offering"
       ASV: "sacrifice of peace-offerings"
       NET: "peace-offering sacrifice"
       WEB: "sacrifice of peace offerings"
       Analysis: All retain "peace offering." NET reverses the word order
       for natural English. KJV's rendering is identical to WEB here. It
       is a sacrifice that celebrates and expresses peace/fellowship.

v.1  — Hebrew YHWH — the divine name (used 8 times in this chapter).
       KJV: "before the LORD"
       ESV: "before the LORD"
       ASV: "before Jehovah"
       NET: "before the Lord"
       WEB: "before Yahweh"
       Analysis: ASV uses "Jehovah," WEB "Yahweh," NET "the Lord." This
       chapter uses the divine name 8 times, making the pattern especially
       visible.

v.2  — Hebrew "petach ohel moed" — entrance of the tent of meeting. Hebrew
       "shachat" — to slaughter.
       KJV: "door of the tabernacle of the congregation"
       ESV: "entrance of the tent of meeting"
       ASV: "door of the tent of meeting"
       NET: "the entrance of the Meeting Tent"
       WEB: "the door of the Tent of Meeting"
       Analysis: KJV's "tabernacle of the congregation" and ESV "tent of
       meeting" translate the same Hebrew phrase. ESV is more literal. NET
       uses "slaughter" where KJV has "kill" — NET's "slaughter" (Hebrew
       "shachat") is more technically precise for sacrificial killing.

v.3  — Hebrew "isheh" — fire offering/food offering.
       KJV: "an offering made by fire unto the LORD"
       ESV: "a food offering to the LORD"
       ASV: "an offering made by fire unto Jehovah"
       NET: "a gift to the Lord"
       WEB: "an offering made by fire to Yahweh"
       Analysis: KJV/ASV/WEB emphasize the fire; ESV emphasizes it as
       food/sustenance; NET's concise "gift" captures a different nuance.
       Both fire and food aspects are present in the Hebrew.

v.4  — Hebrew "yothereth ha-kaved" — the lobe/appendage of the liver.
       KJV: "the caul above the liver"
       ESV: "the long lobe of the liver"
       ASV: "the caul upon the liver"
       NET: "the protruding lobe on the liver"
       WEB: "the cover on the liver"
       Analysis: KJV/ASV "caul" is an archaic term for a membrane or lobe.
       ESV clarifies: it refers to the caudate lobe of the liver. NET's
       "protruding lobe" is the most anatomically descriptive. Modern
       readers benefit from NET's specificity.

v.9  — Hebrew "ha-alyah" — the fat tail.
       KJV: "the whole rump"
       ESV: "the whole fat tail"
       ASV: "the fat tail entire"
       NET: "the entire fat tail"
       WEB: "the entire fat tail"
       Analysis: The fat tail of the broad-tailed sheep common in the Near
       East (weighing 10-15 pounds of pure fat). KJV's "rump" is less
       precise. ESV/ASV/NET/WEB all specify "fat tail," which was
       considered the choicest portion and belonged entirely to God.

v.11 — Hebrew "lechem isheh" — bread/food of the fire-offering.
       KJV: "it is the food of the offering made by fire"
       ESV: "a food offering to the LORD"
       ASV: "the food of the offering made by fire unto Jehovah"
       NET: "a food gift to the Lord"
       WEB: "the food of the offering made by fire to Yahweh"
       Analysis: NET's concise "food gift" captures both Hebrew terms
       efficiently. God does not eat, but the language of "food" expresses
       His satisfaction and acceptance. The concept of sacrifice as God's
       "food" is anthropomorphic language.

v.16 — Hebrew "chelev" — fat. "All the fat is the LORD's."
       KJV: "all the fat is the LORD's"
       ESV: "All fat is the LORD's"
       ASV: "all the fat is Jehovah's"
       NET: "all the fat belongs to the Lord"
       WEB: "all the fat is Yahweh's"
       Analysis: All translations preserve this absolute statement. The fat
       represents the best and richest portion — it belongs exclusively to
       God. A universal, permanent claim.

v.17 — Hebrew "chuqqath olam" — an eternal ordinance.
       KJV: "a perpetual statute for your generations"
       ESV: "a statute forever throughout your generations"
       ASV: "a perpetual statute throughout your generations"
       NET: "a perpetual statute throughout your generations"
       WEB: "a perpetual statute throughout your generations"
       Analysis: All translations agree. The prohibition of fat and blood
       was permanent and universal for Israel.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Shelamim (Peace Offering) — From "shalom" (peace, wholeness). A voluntary
 offering celebrating fellowship with God. The only offering where the
 worshipper ate a portion — a communal meal shared between God, priest,
 and worshipper.

Laying on of Hands — The worshipper placed his hand on the animal's head,
 signifying identification and transfer. The animal represents the
 offerer; its death is his death; its acceptance is his acceptance.

Fat (Chelev) — The internal fat surrounding the organs. Represents the
 best, richest, choicest portion. Always belonged to God and was burned
 on the altar. Israelites were forbidden to eat it.

Blood — The life of the creature (Lev 17:11). Sacred to God because life
 belongs to Him alone. Never to be consumed. Blood makes atonement
 because it represents life given in place of life.

Food Offering (Isheh) — An offering made by fire to the LORD. The term
 conveys both the fire and the concept of "food" presented to God —
 expressing His satisfaction and delight in the sacrifice.

Fat Tail (Alyah) — The large, fatty tail of the broad-tailed sheep common
 in the ancient Near East. Considered the choicest portion of the animal
 and given entirely to God.

Without Blemish — Free from defect, disease, or imperfection. Every
 sacrifice had to be the best of the flock — pointing to Christ, the
 Lamb "without blemish and without spot" (1 Pet 1:19).

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. The peace offering was a shared meal — God, priest, and worshipper eating
 together. Through Christ, you have been invited to God's table. Do you
 enjoy fellowship with God, or do you treat your relationship as distant
 and formal? The Lord's Supper is your peace offering meal.

2. "All fat is the LORD's" (v.16). The best belongs to God — not the
 leftovers. Do you give God the first and best of your time, energy,
 finances, and attention? Or does He receive what remains after you've
 served yourself?

3. The worshipper laid his hand on the offering's head (v.2) — identification.
 You are identified with Christ: His death is your death; His acceptance
 is your acceptance. Do you live in the confidence of that identification,
 or do you still try to earn your standing before God?

4. Peace with God (Rom 5:1) is the foundation for the peace of God (Phil
 4:7). Have you settled the question of peace WITH God through faith in
 Christ? Only then can you experience the peace OF God in daily life.

5. The peace offering was voluntary — an overflow of gratitude and joy. Is
 your worship obligatory or voluntary? Do you come to God because you
 must, or because you delight to? What would it look like to offer a
 "peace offering" of thanksgiving this week?
