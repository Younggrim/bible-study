Leviticus - Chapter 19
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
God calls Israel to holiness in everyday life — love your neighbor as yourself, care for the poor, deal honestly, and revere God in all things.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses. Leviticus is the third book of the Pentateuch, written during
the wilderness period (approximately 1445-1405 BC). Hebrew title: "Vayikra"
— "And He called." God speaks from the newly completed Tabernacle, giving
Israel instructions for worship, sacrifice, and holy living.

Historical Context: Chapter 19 is the heart of the Holiness Code and one
of the most remarkable chapters in the entire Bible. It opens with the
foundational command: "Ye shall be holy: for I the LORD your God am holy"
(v.2). What follows is a sweeping collection of laws that touch every area
of life — family, worship, economics, agriculture, justice, speech,
relationships, and love. The chapter is addressed to "all the congregation"
(v.2) — not just priests, not just leaders, but every Israelite. Holiness
is not reserved for a spiritual elite; it is the calling of the entire
community. The chapter contains the second greatest commandment: "thou
shalt love thy neighbour as thyself" (v.18) — quoted by Jesus as the
summary of the law's second table (Matthew 22:39; Mark 12:31). Many of
the Ten Commandments are echoed here (honor parents, keep Sabbath, no
idols, no stealing, no lying, no false witness), but they are expanded
and applied to practical daily life. The refrain "I am the LORD" (or "I
am the LORD your God") appears sixteen times — grounding every command in
God's identity. Holiness is not abstract — it is concrete, practical, and
all-encompassing.

The Foundation: Be Holy (vv.1-4): God speaks to Moses for "all the
congregation" (v.1-2). "Ye shall be holy: for I the LORD your God am
holy" (v.2) — the thesis statement of the entire Holiness Code. Holiness
is not optional; it is the nature of God and therefore the calling of His
people. Immediately practical: "Ye shall fear every man his mother, and
his father" (v.3) — honor parents (note: mother listed first, unusual).
"Keep my sabbaths" (v.3). "Turn ye not unto idols, nor make to yourselves
molten gods" (v.4). Family, worship, and loyalty to God — the foundations.

Sacrifice and Worship (vv.5-8): Peace offerings must be eaten within two
days; anything remaining on the third day must be burned (vv.5-6). Eating
it on the third day is "abominable" — the offering is profaned and the
person bears iniquity and is cut off (vv.7-8). Worship must follow God's
instructions precisely.

Economic Justice and Compassion (vv.9-10): Gleaning laws — do not harvest
to the very edges of your field; do not pick up what falls (v.9). Do not
strip your vineyard bare or gather fallen grapes (v.10). Leave them "for
the poor and stranger" (v.10). This is institutionalized compassion —
built into the economic system. The story of Ruth depends on this law
(Ruth 2:2-3).

The Ethical Commands (vv.11-18): A rapid-fire series of moral commands:
No stealing, no lying, no false dealing (v.11). No false swearing by God's
name (v.12). No oppression or robbery; pay wages on time (v.13). No
cursing the deaf or tripping the blind (v.14) — protecting the vulnerable
who cannot defend themselves. No injustice in court — neither favoring the
poor nor deferring to the rich (v.15). No slander; no standing against
your neighbor's life (v.16). No hatred in the heart; rebuke your neighbor
openly rather than harboring sin (v.17). No vengeance or grudge-bearing
(v.18). THE CLIMAX: "thou shalt love thy neighbour as thyself: I am the
LORD" (v.18). Love is the fulfillment of all these commands.

Laws of Separation (v.19): "Ye shall keep my statutes" — then three
prohibitions: no crossbreeding cattle, no sowing mixed seed, no garments
of mixed fabric (linen and wool). These laws teach the principle of
distinction — God separates, and His people maintain distinctions. They
are object lessons in holiness (separation/distinction).

Sexual Sin and Its Remedy (vv.20-22): A man who lies with a betrothed
slave woman — "she shall be scourged" (KJV) / "a distinction shall be
made" (ESV) (v.20). They are not put to death because she was not free
(v.20). He brings a guilt/trespass offering — a ram (v.21). The priest
makes atonement and he is forgiven (v.22). Even in cases of sexual sin,
there is a path to forgiveness through sacrifice.

Agricultural Laws (vv.23-25): Fruit trees in the land — the fruit is
"uncircumcised" (forbidden) for three years (v.23). In the fourth year,
all fruit is holy to the LORD (v.24). In the fifth year, they may eat it
(v.25). Patience, consecration, then enjoyment. First fruits belong to God.

Miscellaneous Holiness Laws (vv.26-31): No eating with blood (v.26). No
divination or fortune-telling (v.26). No rounding the corners of the head
or marring the beard (v.27) — likely prohibiting pagan mourning customs.
No cutting the body for the dead or tattoo marks (v.28). Do not prostitute
your daughter (v.29). Keep Sabbaths and reverence the sanctuary (v.30).
Do not consult mediums or necromancers (v.31).

Honor, Love, and Justice (vv.32-37): Rise before the elderly and honor
them (v.32). Do not wrong the stranger — love him as yourself, "for ye
were strangers in the land of Egypt" (vv.33-34). No dishonesty in
measurements — just balances, just weights, just measures (vv.35-36).
"I am the LORD your God, which brought you out of the land of Egypt" (v.36)
— the exodus is the motivation for justice. Final summary: "observe all my
statutes, and all my judgments, and do them: I am the LORD" (v.37).

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/leviticus-19/
  Guzik calls this chapter "a remarkable collection of laws" that shows
  holiness touching every area of life. He emphasizes that "love your
  neighbor as yourself" is not a New Testament invention but an Old
  Testament command — Jesus was quoting Moses. He notes the balance of
  justice in v.15: neither favoring the poor nor the rich.

Charles Spurgeon:
  Sermon: "Love Thy Neighbour"
  "Love thy neighbour as thyself — this is not a suggestion, it is a
  COMMAND. And it is not a new command — it is as old as Leviticus. Our
  Lord did not invent it; He quoted it. Moses wrote it by the Spirit of
  God a thousand years before Christ walked the earth. And what does it
  mean? It means: treat your neighbour's interests as you treat your own.
  His reputation — guard it as you guard yours. His property — respect it
  as you respect yours. His feelings — consider them as you consider yours.
  His needs — attend to them as you attend to yours. This is not natural
  to fallen man. It requires a new heart, a new nature, the very Spirit
  of God within. But it is the standard — and Christ both commands it and
  enables it."

REFLECTION
------------------------------------
1. "Be ye holy: for I the LORD your God am holy" (v.2). Holiness is not
   optional for believers — it is the family resemblance. God's children
   bear His character. And holiness is not abstract — this chapter shows
   it is intensely practical: how you treat your parents, your employees,
   the disabled, the poor, the foreigner, your neighbor. Where is your
   holiness merely theoretical rather than practical?

2. "Leave them for the poor and stranger" (v.10). God builds compassion
   into the economic system. Generosity is not merely voluntary charity —
   it is structural justice. Are you using all your resources for yourself,
   or leaving "edges" for those in need?

3. "Thou shalt not curse the deaf, nor put a stumblingblock before the
   blind" (v.14). Do not exploit those who cannot defend themselves or
   detect your exploitation. God sees what the deaf cannot hear and what
   the blind cannot see. "Fear thy God" — He is the witness when no one
   else is watching.

4. "Love thy neighbour as thyself" (v.18). The standard is not "love your
   neighbor as much as you can manage" but "as yourself." With the same
   urgency, the same concern, the same investment you naturally give to
   your own interests. This is impossible without the Spirit of God. But
   it is the standard — and it is the fulfillment of the entire law
   (Romans 13:9; Galatians 5:14).

5. "The stranger that dwelleth with you shall be unto you as one born
   among you" (v.34). Love is not limited to your own people, your own
   tribe, your own kind. The foreigner is to be loved "as yourself" —
   the same standard as the neighbor. "For ye were strangers in the land
   of Egypt" — remember where you came from. You were once the outsider.
