Leviticus - Chapter 19
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
God calls Israel to holiness in everyday life — love your neighbor as yourself, care for the poor, deal honestly, and revere God in all things.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses. Leviticus is the third book of the Pentateuch, written during
the wilderness period (approximately 1445-1405 BC). Hebrew title: "Vayikra"
— "And He called." God speaks from the newly completed Tabernacle, giving
Israel instructions for worship, sacrifice, and holy living.

Historical Context: Chapter 19 is the heart of the Holiness Code and one
of the most remarkable chapters in the entire Bible. It opens with the
foundational command: "Ye shall be holy: for I the LORD your God am holy"
(v.2). What follows is a sweeping collection of laws that touch every area
of life — family, worship, economics, agriculture, justice, speech,
relationships, and love. The chapter is addressed to "all the congregation"
(v.2) — not just priests, not just leaders, but every Israelite. Holiness
is not reserved for a spiritual elite; it is the calling of the entire
community. The chapter contains the second greatest commandment: "thou
shalt love thy neighbour as thyself" (v.18) — quoted by Jesus as the
summary of the law's second table (Matthew 22:39; Mark 12:31). Many of
the Ten Commandments are echoed here (honor parents, keep Sabbath, no
idols, no stealing, no lying, no false witness), but they are expanded
and applied to practical daily life. The refrain "I am the LORD" (or "I
am the LORD your God") appears sixteen times — grounding every command in
God's identity. Holiness is not abstract — it is concrete, practical, and
all-encompassing.

The Foundation: Be Holy (vv.1-4): God speaks to Moses for "all the
congregation" (v.1-2). "Ye shall be holy: for I the LORD your God am
holy" (v.2) — the thesis statement of the entire Holiness Code. Holiness
is not optional; it is the nature of God and therefore the calling of His
people. Immediately practical: "Ye shall fear every man his mother, and
his father" (v.3) — honor parents (note: mother listed first, unusual).
"Keep my sabbaths" (v.3). "Turn ye not unto idols, nor make to yourselves
molten gods" (v.4). Family, worship, and loyalty to God — the foundations.

Sacrifice and Worship (vv.5-8): Peace offerings must be eaten within two
days; anything remaining on the third day must be burned (vv.5-6). Eating
it on the third day is "abominable" — the offering is profaned and the
person bears iniquity and is cut off (vv.7-8). Worship must follow God's
instructions precisely.

Economic Justice and Compassion (vv.9-10): Gleaning laws — do not harvest
to the very edges of your field; do not pick up what falls (v.9). Do not
strip your vineyard bare or gather fallen grapes (v.10). Leave them "for
the poor and stranger" (v.10). This is institutionalized compassion —
built into the economic system. The story of Ruth depends on this law
(Ruth 2:2-3).

The Ethical Commands (vv.11-18): A rapid-fire series of moral commands:
No stealing, no lying, no false dealing (v.11). No false swearing by God's
name (v.12). No oppression or robbery; pay wages on time (v.13). No
cursing the deaf or tripping the blind (v.14) — protecting the vulnerable
who cannot defend themselves. No injustice in court — neither favoring the
poor nor deferring to the rich (v.15). No slander; no standing against
your neighbor's life (v.16). No hatred in the heart; rebuke your neighbor
openly rather than harboring sin (v.17). No vengeance or grudge-bearing
(v.18). THE CLIMAX: "thou shalt love thy neighbour as thyself: I am the
LORD" (v.18). Love is the fulfillment of all these commands.

Laws of Separation (v.19): "Ye shall keep my statutes" — then three
prohibitions: no crossbreeding cattle, no sowing mixed seed, no garments
of mixed fabric (linen and wool). These laws teach the principle of
distinction — God separates, and His people maintain distinctions. They
are object lessons in holiness (separation/distinction).

Sexual Sin and Its Remedy (vv.20-22): A man who lies with a betrothed
slave woman — "she shall be scourged" (KJV) / "a distinction shall be
made" (ESV) (v.20). They are not put to death because she was not free
(v.20). He brings a guilt/trespass offering — a ram (v.21). The priest
makes atonement and he is forgiven (v.22). Even in cases of sexual sin,
there is a path to forgiveness through sacrifice.

Agricultural Laws (vv.23-25): Fruit trees in the land — the fruit is
"uncircumcised" (forbidden) for three years (v.23). In the fourth year,
all fruit is holy to the LORD (v.24). In the fifth year, they may eat it
(v.25). Patience, consecration, then enjoyment. First fruits belong to God.

Miscellaneous Holiness Laws (vv.26-31): No eating with blood (v.26). No
divination or fortune-telling (v.26). No rounding the corners of the head
or marring the beard (v.27) — likely prohibiting pagan mourning customs.
No cutting the body for the dead or tattoo marks (v.28). Do not prostitute
your daughter (v.29). Keep Sabbaths and reverence the sanctuary (v.30).
Do not consult mediums or necromancers (v.31).

Honor, Love, and Justice (vv.32-37): Rise before the elderly and honor
them (v.32). Do not wrong the stranger — love him as yourself, "for ye
were strangers in the land of Egypt" (vv.33-34). No dishonesty in
measurements — just balances, just weights, just measures (vv.35-36).
"I am the LORD your God, which brought you out of the land of Egypt" (v.36)
— the exodus is the motivation for justice. Final summary: "observe all my
statutes, and all my judgments, and do them: I am the LORD" (v.37).

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "You shall be holy, for I the LORD your God am holy." (ESV) — The
        foundation of all ethics. God's character is the standard.

v.10 — "You shall leave them for the poor and for the sojourner: I am the
        LORD your God." (ESV) — Institutionalized compassion. The poor
        have a right to glean.

v.14 — "You shall not curse the deaf or put a stumbling block before the
        blind, but you shall fear your God: I am the LORD." (ESV) —
        Protect those who cannot protect themselves.

v.15 — "You shall do no injustice in court. You shall not be partial to
        the poor or defer to the great, but in righteousness shall you
        judge your neighbor." (ESV) — Impartial justice. Neither
        favoritism nor reverse favoritism.

v.17 — "You shall not hate your brother in your heart, but you shall
        reason frankly with your neighbor, lest you incur sin because of
        him." (ESV) — Honest confrontation rather than hidden hatred.

v.18 — "You shall not take vengeance or bear a grudge against the sons of
        your own people, but you shall love your neighbor as yourself: I
        am the LORD." (ESV) — The second greatest commandment.

v.34 — "You shall treat the stranger who sojourns with you as the native
        among you, and you shall love him as yourself, for you were
        strangers in the land of Egypt." (ESV) — Love extends beyond your
        own people.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/leviticus-19/
  Guzik calls this chapter "a remarkable collection of laws" that shows
  holiness touching every area of life. He emphasizes that "love your
  neighbor as yourself" is not a New Testament invention but an Old
  Testament command — Jesus was quoting Moses. He notes the balance of
  justice in v.15: neither favoring the poor nor the rich.

Charles Spurgeon:
  Sermon: "Love Thy Neighbour"
  "Love thy neighbour as thyself — this is not a suggestion, it is a
  COMMAND. And it is not a new command — it is as old as Leviticus. Our
  Lord did not invent it; He quoted it. Moses wrote it by the Spirit of
  God a thousand years before Christ walked the earth. And what does it
  mean? It means: treat your neighbour's interests as you treat your own.
  His reputation — guard it as you guard yours. His property — respect it
  as you respect yours. His feelings — consider them as you consider yours.
  His needs — attend to them as you attend to yours. This is not natural
  to fallen man. It requires a new heart, a new nature, the very Spirit
  of God within. But it is the standard — and Christ both commands it and
  enables it."

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.2  — Hebrew "Q'doshim tih'yu ki qadosh ani YHWH Eloheikhem" — You shall be holy, for I the LORD your God am holy.
       KJV: "Ye shall be holy: for I the LORD your God am holy"
       ESV: "You shall be holy, for I the LORD your God am holy"
       ASV: "Ye shall be holy; for I Jehovah your God am holy"
       NET: "You must be holy because I, the Lord your God, am holy"
       WEB: "You shall be holy; for I Yahweh your God, am holy"
       Analysis: Identical in both KJV and ESV. "Q'doshim" (holy ones) — plural, addressed to all. "Qadosh" (holy) — set apart, distinct, other. God's holiness is the ground and model of Israel's holiness. NET adds "because" to make the causal logic explicit.

v.3  — Hebrew "ish immo v'aviv tira'u" — each person his mother and his father you shall fear/revere.
       KJV: "Ye shall fear every man his mother, and his father"
       ESV: "Every one of you shall revere his mother and his father"
       ASV: "Ye shall fear every man his mother, and his father"
       NET: "Each of you must respect his mother and his father"
       Analysis: "Yare" = fear/reverence (same word used for fearing God). NET softens "fear" to "respect." Mother listed FIRST — unusual in patriarchal context. Perhaps because reverence for the mother is more easily neglected.

v.9  — Hebrew "kalah" — to complete/finish.
       KJV: "thou shalt not wholly reap the corners of thy field"
       ESV: "you shall not reap your field right up to its edge"
       ASV: "thou shalt not wholly reap the corners of thy field"
       NET: "you must not harvest your field to its very edge"
       Analysis: The farmer must intentionally leave portions unharvested for the poor and alien. NET is clearer; ASV uses the archaic "wholly reap."

v.14 — Hebrew "lifnei iver lo titen mikhshol" — before a blind person do not place an obstacle.
       KJV: "put a stumblingblock before the blind"
       ESV: "put a stumbling block before the blind"
       Analysis: Taken literally AND metaphorically in Jewish tradition: do not mislead the ignorant, do not give bad advice to the naive, do not enable sin in the vulnerable.

v.17 — Hebrew "hokhe'ach tokhiach et amitekha v'lo tissa alav chet."
       KJV: "thou shalt in any wise rebuke thy neighbour, and not suffer sin upon him"
       ESV: "you shall reason frankly with your neighbor, lest you incur sin because of him"
       Analysis: KJV: rebuke him so that sin does not remain on him. ESV: reason with him lest YOU bear sin because of him. The Hebrew allows both: failure to rebuke makes you complicit in his sin. Honest confrontation is an act of love.

v.18 — Hebrew "v'ahavta l're'akha kamokha" — love your neighbor as yourself.
       KJV: "love thy neighbour as thyself"
       ESV: "love your neighbor as yourself"
       ASV: "thou shalt love thy neighbor as thyself"
       NET: "you must love your neighbor as yourself"
       WEB: "you shall love your neighbor as yourself"
       Analysis: All translations agree on this crucial verse. "Ahav" = love (the same word used in "love the LORD your God" — Deuteronomy 6:5). "Re'a" = neighbor, companion, fellow. "Kamokha" = as yourself. Jesus identifies this as the second greatest commandment (Matthew 22:39). The divine signature "I am the Lord" follows.

v.19 — Hebrew "sha'atnez" — a technical term for mixed fabric.
       KJV: "a garment mingled of linen and woollen"
       ESV: "a garment of cloth made of two kinds of material"
       Analysis: KJV names the specific fabrics; ESV generalizes. The prohibition is specifically linen + wool (Deuteronomy 22:11). Interestingly, the priestly garments DID combine linen and wool — what is forbidden to the common person is prescribed for the priest.

v.26 — Hebrew "lo t'nachashu v'lo t'onenu" — divination and fortune-telling prohibited.
       KJV: "neither shall ye use enchantment, nor observe times"
       ESV: "You shall not interpret omens or tell fortunes"
       ASV: "neither shall ye use enchantments, nor practice augury"
       NET: "You must not practice divination or soothsaying"
       WEB: "You shall not use enchantments, nor practice sorcery"
       Analysis: KJV's "observe times" = divination by times/seasons (astrology). ESV's "tell fortunes" captures the meaning. NET separates the blood prohibition from the divination prohibition more clearly than ASV/WEB.

v.27 — Hebrew "lo takifu pe'at rosh'khem" — rounding the corners of the head.
       KJV: "Ye shall not round the corners of your heads"
       ESV: "You shall not round off the hair on your temples"
       Analysis: Refers to cutting the hair in a specific pattern associated with pagan mourning or worship customs. Not a prohibition of haircuts generally, but of pagan-associated styles.

v.28 — Hebrew "u-kh'tovet qa'aqa lo tit'nu ba-khem" — a writing/inscription of incision.
       KJV: "nor print any marks upon you"
       ESV: "or tattoo yourselves"
       Analysis: KJV's "print marks" is literal; ESV's "tattoo" uses the modern term. The context is pagan mourning rituals — marking the body for the dead.

v.35 — Hebrew "ba-middah ba-mishqal u-va-m'surah" — in measure, weight, and quantity.
       KJV: "in meteyard, in weight, or in measure"
       ESV: "in measures of length or weight or quantity"
       ASV: "in measures of length, of weight, or of quantity"
       NET: "You must not do injustice in measurements of length, weight, or volume"
       Analysis: KJV's "meteyard" is archaic for a measuring rod (length). ESV modernizes. NET's "injustice" replaces ASV's "unrighteousness" — same Hebrew "avel." Honest commerce is a holiness issue, not merely an ethics issue.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
"Be holy" (Q'doshim tih'yu) — The imperative that governs the entire
               chapter. "Qadosh" means set apart, distinct, other. To be
               holy is to be like God — distinct from the world, set apart
               for His purposes. Not merely moral purity but total
               consecration.

"Love thy neighbour as thyself" — The second greatest commandment (Matthew
               22:39). Not a feeling but an action: treat others' interests
               as you treat your own. The standard is not perfection but
               self-interest redirected outward. "As yourself" = with the
               same concern, attention, and care you naturally give to your
               own needs.

Gleaning       — The practice of leaving the edges of fields unharvested
               and not picking up dropped grain or fallen fruit, so that
               the poor and foreigners may gather food. An institutionalized
               form of welfare that preserves the dignity of the poor (they
               work for their food) while requiring generosity from the
               landowner.

"I am the LORD" — The refrain that punctuates the chapter (16 times).
               Functions as both authority ("I command this") and
               motivation ("because of who I am"). God's identity is the
               ground of all ethics. Every command flows from His character.

Stumbling block (Mikhshol) — An obstacle placed in someone's path. Used
               literally (tripping the blind) and metaphorically (causing
               someone to sin). The principle: do not exploit another's
               weakness or ignorance for your advantage.

Mixed kinds (Kilayim) — The prohibition of mixing species (cattle),
               seeds (agriculture), and fabrics (clothing). Teaches the
               principle of distinction — God creates categories and His
               people respect them. A living parable of holiness
               (separation/distinction).

"Fear thy God" — Hebrew "v'yareta me-Elohekha" — and you shall fear your
               God. Appears after commands that could be violated secretly
               (cursing the deaf, misleading the blind, unjust judgment).
               When no human witness can hold you accountable, remember:
               God sees. Fear HIM.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Be ye holy: for I the LORD your God am holy" (v.2). Holiness is not
   optional for believers — it is the family resemblance. God's children
   bear His character. And holiness is not abstract — this chapter shows
   it is intensely practical: how you treat your parents, your employees,
   the disabled, the poor, the foreigner, your neighbor. Where is your
   holiness merely theoretical rather than practical?

2. "Leave them for the poor and stranger" (v.10). God builds compassion
   into the economic system. Generosity is not merely voluntary charity —
   it is structural justice. Are you using all your resources for yourself,
   or leaving "edges" for those in need?

3. "Thou shalt not curse the deaf, nor put a stumblingblock before the
   blind" (v.14). Do not exploit those who cannot defend themselves or
   detect your exploitation. God sees what the deaf cannot hear and what
   the blind cannot see. "Fear thy God" — He is the witness when no one
   else is watching.

4. "Love thy neighbour as thyself" (v.18). The standard is not "love your
   neighbor as much as you can manage" but "as yourself." With the same
   urgency, the same concern, the same investment you naturally give to
   your own interests. This is impossible without the Spirit of God. But
   it is the standard — and it is the fulfillment of the entire law
   (Romans 13:9; Galatians 5:14).

5. "The stranger that dwelleth with you shall be unto you as one born
   among you" (v.34). Love is not limited to your own people, your own
   tribe, your own kind. The foreigner is to be loved "as yourself" —
   the same standard as the neighbor. "For ye were strangers in the land
   of Egypt" — remember where you came from. You were once the outsider.
