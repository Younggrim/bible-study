Leviticus - Chapter 5
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Section 1 — Specific Sins Requiring a Sin Offering (vv.1-4):
 Four cases are named: (1) Failing to testify when one has witnessed
 something and hears a public adjuration (v.1); (2) Touching an unclean
 carcass — wild animal, livestock, or swarming thing — unknowingly (v.2);
 (3) Touching human uncleanness unknowingly (v.3); (4) Making a rash oath
 to do evil or good and forgetting it (v.4). In each case, when the person
 "realizes his guilt," he must act.

Section 2 — Confession and the Sin Offering (vv.5-6):
 When guilt is realized, the person must confess the specific sin and
 bring a female lamb or goat as a sin offering. The priest makes
 atonement.

Section 3 — Provision for the Poor: Two Birds (vv.7-10):
 If the offender cannot afford a lamb, he brings two turtledoves or two
 pigeons — one for a sin offering, one for a burnt offering. The priest
 wrings the neck of the first (sin offering), sprinkles blood on the
 altar side, drains the rest at the base. The second is offered as a
 burnt offering. Atonement is made; forgiveness is granted.

Section 4 — Provision for the Poorest: Flour (vv.11-13):
 If even two birds are unaffordable, the offender brings a tenth of an
 ephah of fine flour — no oil, no frankincense (because it is a sin
 offering, not a grain offering). The priest burns a memorial portion.
 Atonement is made; forgiveness is granted. The remainder goes to the
 priest.

Section 5 — The Guilt/Trespass Offering: Sacred Things (vv.14-16):
 If anyone commits a "breach of faith" in the holy things of the LORD
 (misusing tithes, firstfruits, or dedicated items), he brings a ram
 without blemish, valued in silver shekels. He also makes restitution:
 the full value plus one-fifth (20%) given to the priest. Atonement is
 made; forgiveness is granted.

Section 6 — The Guilt Offering: Unknown Violations (vv.17-19):
 If anyone sins by violating God's commandments without knowing it, he
 is still guilty and must bear his iniquity. He brings a ram without
 blemish (or its equivalent value) as a guilt offering. The priest makes
 atonement for his unintentional error. "It is a guilt offering; he has
 indeed incurred guilt before the LORD."

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses. Leviticus is the third book of the Pentateuch, written during
the wilderness period (approximately 1445-1405 BC). Hebrew title: "Vayikra"
— "And He called." God speaks from the Tabernacle, giving instructions for
worship, sacrifice, and holy living.

Historical Context: Leviticus 5 continues the sin offering regulations from
chapter 4 (vv.1-13) and then introduces the trespass or guilt offering
(Hebrew: "asham") in vv.14-19. The chapter bridges two categories: sins of
omission and contamination (the sin offering) and sins involving violation of
sacred things (the guilt offering). The distinction is important — the sin
offering (chattat) addresses the condition of sin and defilement, while the
guilt offering (asham) addresses specific acts of trespass that require
restitution.

The guilt offering introduces a principle absent from the other offerings:
restitution plus a penalty of one-fifth (20%). The offender must not only
atone for his sin but make the wronged party whole — and then some. This
reflects God's justice: sin creates a debt that must be paid in full with
interest. Christ is our guilt offering — He bore our guilt, paid our debt,
and restored more than what was lost. Isaiah 53:10 explicitly calls Christ's
sacrifice an "asham": "When his soul makes an offering for guilt [asham],
he shall see his offspring."

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1 — "If anyone sins in that he hears a public adjuration to testify, and
 though he is a witness... yet does not speak, he shall bear his
 iniquity." — Silence when truth is required is sin. Failing to act is
 as guilty as acting wrongly.

v.5 — "When he realizes his guilt in any of these and confesses the sin he
 has committed." — Confession is required. Awareness of guilt must lead
 to acknowledgment. No atonement without confession.

v.7 — "But if he cannot afford a lamb, then he shall bring... two
 turtledoves or two pigeons." — God makes provision for the poor. No
 one is excluded from atonement by poverty. Grace meets people where
 they are.

v.11 — "But if he cannot afford two turtledoves or two pigeons, then he
 shall bring... a tenth of an ephah of fine flour for a sin offering."
 — Even the poorest can come. God will not turn away anyone who seeks
 atonement. The offering is scaled to ability, not eliminated.

v.16 — "He shall also make restitution for what he has done amiss in the
 holy thing and shall add a fifth to it." — Restitution plus 20%. Sin
 creates a debt; the debt must be paid with interest. Christ paid our
 debt and restored more than what was lost.

v.17 — "If anyone sins, doing any of the things that by the LORD's
 commandments ought not to be done, though he did not know it, then
 realizes his guilt, he shall bear his iniquity." — Ignorance does not
 remove guilt. The standard is God's law, not our awareness of it.

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1  — Hebrew "qol alah" — a voice of an oath/curse. A public call for
       witnesses to come forward under oath.
       KJV: "hear the voice of swearing"
       ESV: "hears a public adjuration to testify"
       ASV: "heareth the voice of adjuration"
       NET: "hears a public curse against one who fails to testify"
       WEB: "hears the voice of adjuration"
       Analysis: NET expands the Hebrew into a full explanation: a public
       oath demanding testimony. ASV/WEB retain the technical "adjuration."
       KJV's "swearing" means a solemn oath, not profanity. ESV's
       "adjuration" clarifies: a formal demand for testimony. This refers
       to a public summons to testify, not casual swearing.

v.2  — Hebrew distinguishes "chayyah" (wild animal), "behemah" (domestic
       livestock), and "sherets" (swarming/creeping creatures).
       KJV: "carcase of an unclean beast... unclean cattle... unclean
       creeping things"
       ESV: "carcass of an unclean wild animal... unclean livestock...
       unclean swarming things"
       ASV: "carcass of an unclean beast... unclean cattle... unclean
       creeping things"
       NET: "carcass of an unclean wild animal... unclean domesticated
       animal... unclean swarming thing"
       WEB: "carcass of an unclean animal... unclean livestock... unclean
       creeping things"
       Analysis: Three categories covering all unclean animals. ESV and NET
       are the most precise in distinguishing the categories for modern
       readers.

v.6  — Hebrew "asham" — guilt/trespass/compensation.
       KJV: "trespass offering"
       ESV: "compensation"
       ASV: "trespass-offering"
       NET: "penalty for guilt"
       WEB: "trespass offering"
       Analysis: NET renders "asham" as "penalty for guilt" rather than
       "trespass offering," emphasizing the reparation aspect. This offering
       requires compensation, not just atonement. ESV's "compensation" in
       this context is distinct because the sacrifice in vv.1-13 is
       technically still the sin offering (chattat). The true guilt/trespass
       offering begins in v.14.

v.6  — Hebrew YHWH — the divine name in context of guilt and confession.
       KJV: "unto the LORD"
       ESV: "to the LORD"
       ASV: "unto Jehovah"
       NET: "to the Lord"
       WEB: "to Yahweh"
       Analysis: In a chapter about guilt and confession, the personal name
       of God reminds that sin is ultimately against a Person, not just a
       law.

v.7  — Hebrew "tor" (turtledove) and "ben yonah" (young pigeon).
       KJV: "two turtledoves, or two young pigeons"
       ESV: "two turtledoves or two pigeons"
       ASV: "two turtle-doves, or two young pigeons"
       NET: "two turtledoves or two young pigeons"
       WEB: "two turtledoves or two young pigeons"
       Analysis: The same offering Mary brought at Jesus' purification (Luke
       2:24), indicating the holy family's poverty. All translations are
       essentially identical.

v.11 — Hebrew "soleth" — fine flour. The tenth of an ephah (approximately
       2 quarts/2 liters).
       KJV: "the tenth part of an ephah of fine flour"
       ESV: "a tenth of an ephah of fine flour"
       ASV: "the tenth part of an ephah of fine flour"
       NET: "a tenth of an ephah of choice wheat flour"
       WEB: "a tenth part of an ephah of fine flour"
       Analysis: NET again specifies "choice wheat flour" for the sin
       offering of the poorest person. Even flour offered for sin must be
       the best quality available. The absolute minimum offering — yet still
       accepted by God. No oil or frankincense is added because this is a
       sin offering, not a joyful grain offering.

v.15 — Hebrew "ma'al ma'al" — to act unfaithfully, to trespass. Hebrew
       "shekel ha-qodesh" — shekel of the sanctuary.
       KJV: "sin through ignorance, in the holy things... with thy
       estimation by shekels of silver, after the shekel of the sanctuary"
       ESV: "sins unintentionally in any of the holy things... valued in
       silver shekels, according to the shekel of the sanctuary"
       ASV: "commit a trespass... in the holy things of Jehovah"
       NET: "commits a trespass and sins by straying unintentionally from
       the regulations about the Lord's holy things"
       WEB: "commits a trespass... in the holy things of Yahweh"
       Analysis: NET expands the Hebrew "ma'al" (trespass/unfaithfulness)
       to show this involves straying from God's regulations about sacred
       things. The ram's value had to be assessed by the sanctuary standard
       (approximately 11.5 grams of silver per shekel).

v.16 — Hebrew "chata" — to sin/miss. The one-fifth (20%) surcharge.
       KJV: "make amends for the harm that he hath done... add the fifth
       part thereto"
       ESV: "make restitution for what he has done amiss... and shall add
       a fifth to it"
       ASV: "make restitution for that which he hath done amiss... and
       shall add the fifth part thereto"
       NET: "whatever holy thing he violated he must restore and must add
       one-fifth"
       WEB: "make restitution for that which he has done amiss... add a
       fifth part to it"
       Analysis: NET uses "violated" for Hebrew "chata" in this context.
       The 20% surcharge shows that sin against God's holy things costs more
       than the original value — a principle of restitution.

v.19 — Hebrew "asham" — guilt offering.
       KJV: "It is a trespass offering"
       ESV: "It is a guilt offering"
       ASV: "It is a trespass-offering"
       NET: "It is a guilt offering"
       WEB: "It is a trespass offering"
       Analysis: KJV/ASV/WEB "trespass" and ESV/NET "guilt" translate the
       same word. "Guilt offering" captures the idea of liability and debt;
       "trespass offering" captures the idea of violation and encroachment.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Asham (Guilt/Trespass Offering) — From a root meaning "to be guilty, to
 incur liability." A mandatory offering for specific violations involving
 sacred things or the rights of others. Distinctive feature: restitution
 plus one-fifth (20%) penalty. Christ is our asham (Isa 53:10).

Adjuration (Qol Alah) — A public, sworn demand for witnesses to come
 forward with testimony. Failure to respond made one guilty of the sin
 of silence — bearing the iniquity of withholding truth.

Restitution — Making the wronged party whole. The guilt offering required
 full repayment of what was taken or misused, plus an additional 20%.
 This principle of "restoration plus interest" reflects God's justice.

Breach of Faith (Ma'al) — Unfaithfulness, treachery, misappropriation.
 Used specifically for violations involving holy things — taking what
 belongs to God or misusing what is consecrated to Him.

Rash Oath — A hasty, unconsidered vow or promise. Once spoken, it binds
 the speaker. Even forgotten oaths create guilt when remembered. This
 teaches the weight of words before God.

Shekel of the Sanctuary — The standard weight used for sacred transactions
 (approximately 11.5 grams of silver). Ensured that offerings met a
 minimum value and prevented fraud in sacred matters.

Tenth of an Ephah — Approximately 2 quarts (2 liters) of fine flour. The
 minimum acceptable offering for the poorest person — God's provision
 ensuring no one is excluded from atonement by poverty.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Silence when truth is required is sin (v.1). Is there a situation where
 you know the truth but have remained silent — out of fear, convenience,
 or self-protection? God calls withholding testimony a sin that must be
 confessed and atoned for. Where is He calling you to speak?

2. God scales the offering to the offerer's ability (vv.7, 11) — lamb, birds,
 or flour. He never turns away the poor, but He never eliminates the need
 for atonement either. How does this balance of grace and justice shape
 your understanding of God? He meets you where you are but never pretends
 sin doesn't matter.

3. Restitution plus one-fifth (v.16). When you wrong someone, is it enough
 to simply stop? Or does God require you to make it right — and then some?
 Is there a relationship where you owe restitution? What would "restoration
 plus 20%" look like in that situation?

4. "Though he did not know it, then realizes his guilt, he shall bear his
 iniquity" (v.17). Ignorance does not remove guilt. What sins might you be
 committing that you are not yet aware of? Pray for the Spirit to reveal
 hidden faults (Ps 139:23-24) and trust that Christ's blood covers even
 what you cannot see.

5. Confession is the hinge between guilt and forgiveness (v.5). "When he
 realizes his guilt... and confesses." Is there unconfessed sin in your
 life — something you have realized but not yet brought to God? What
 keeps you from confessing? Fear? Shame? Pride? "If we confess our sins,
 He is faithful and just to forgive" (1 John 1:9).
