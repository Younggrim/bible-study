Leviticus - Chapter 6
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
God gives further instructions for the priests on maintaining the offerings — the fire on the altar must never go out.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses. Leviticus is the third book of the Pentateuch, written during
the wilderness period (approximately 1445-1405 BC). Hebrew title: "Vayikra"
— "And He called." God speaks from the newly completed Tabernacle, giving
Israel instructions for worship, sacrifice, and holy living.

Historical Context: Chapter 6 transitions from the worshipper's perspective
(chapters 1-5) to the priest's perspective — how the priests are to
administer the offerings. The chapter opens with a final case of the guilt/
trespass offering (vv.1-7) involving sins against a neighbor that are also
sins against God, then provides priestly regulations for the burnt offering
(vv.8-13), grain offering (vv.14-23), and sin offering (vv.24-30). The
central image of this chapter is the perpetual fire: "The fire shall ever be
burning upon the altar; it shall never go out" (v.13). This unquenchable
flame represents God's unceasing readiness to receive worship, the
continuous need for atonement, and the call to unbroken devotion. Note the
Hebrew numbering differs — in Hebrew Bibles, 6:1-7 is actually 5:20-26,
and 6:8-30 begins at 6:1.

The Guilt Offering for Fraud (vv.1-7): Sins against a neighbor ARE sins
against God — "commit a trespass against the LORD" by lying to a neighbor
(v.2). Restitution is required: full restoration plus one-fifth (20%) added
(v.5). The sacrifice alone is insufficient without making things right with
the wronged party. Both restitution AND sacrifice are needed for forgiveness
(vv.5-7). This principle reappears in Matthew 5:23-24.

The Law of the Burnt Offering for Priests (vv.8-13): The burnt offering
burns all night until morning (v.9). The priest changes garments for ash
removal — linen for sacred duty, other garments for carrying ashes out
(vv.10-11). Ashes taken to a "clean place" outside the camp (v.11). The
fire must NEVER go out (vv.12-13) — stated three times for emphasis. Wood
added every morning (v.12). The perpetual fire: God is always ready to
receive the worshipper.

The Law of the Grain Offering for Priests (vv.14-23): The memorial portion
burned on the altar; the rest eaten by priests (vv.15-16). Must be eaten
unleavened in a holy place (v.16). "Most holy" — same status as sin and
guilt offerings (v.17). Whatever touches it becomes holy (v.18). The
priest's own grain offering on the day of anointing (vv.19-20): entirely
burned, not eaten (vv.22-23). The priest cannot benefit from his own
offering — it must be wholly given to God.

The Law of the Sin Offering for Priests (vv.24-30): Killed in the same
place as the burnt offering (v.25). The priest who offers it eats it in a
holy place (v.26). Whatever touches the flesh becomes holy (v.27). Blood
on garments must be washed in a holy place (v.27). Earthen vessels broken;
bronze vessels scoured (v.28) — holiness is contagious and must be
contained. If blood is brought into the Tabernacle for atonement, the
offering is burned, not eaten (v.30).

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/leviticus-6/
  Guzik emphasizes the perpetual fire as representing God's constant
  readiness to receive worship and the believer's call to unceasing
  devotion. He notes the restitution principle: sacrifice without making
  things right with your neighbor is insufficient.

Charles Spurgeon:
  Sermon: "The Fire That Never Goes Out"
  "The fire shall NEVER go out — here is the heart of worship. God does not
  sleep. His altar is never cold. His readiness to receive the sinner never
  wanes. And so our devotion must be: not a flash of enthusiasm that dies
  by Tuesday, but a steady flame that burns through every season. Keep the
  fire burning, Christian. Add the wood of prayer every morning. Let the
  fat of praise ascend continually. The altar of your heart must never grow
  cold."

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Commit a trespass against the LORD, and lie unto his neighbour" (v.2).
   Sin against people IS sin against God. You cannot wrong your neighbor
   and be right with God. Is there anyone you need to make restitution to?

2. "He shall restore it in the principal, and shall add the fifth part"
   (v.5). God requires MORE than what was taken — not just making it even,
   but making it MORE than even. Repentance costs something. Are you
   willing to go beyond "fair" in making things right?

3. "The fire shall ever be burning upon the altar; it shall never go out"
   (v.13). Is the fire of your devotion still burning? Or has it gone cold?
   What "wood" do you need to add this morning — prayer, Scripture, worship,
   obedience — to keep the flame alive?

4. The priest's grain offering is wholly burned — he cannot eat his own
   offering (v.23). Those who serve God cannot serve themselves with what
   belongs to God. Ministry is not self-serving.

5. "First be reconciled to thy brother" (Matthew 5:23-24). The guilt
   offering teaches that worship without reconciliation is empty. God
   refuses the sacrifice of someone who won't make things right with
   their neighbor.
