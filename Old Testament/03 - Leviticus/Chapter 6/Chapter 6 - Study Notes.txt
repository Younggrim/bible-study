Leviticus - Chapter 6
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
God gives further instructions for the priests on maintaining the offerings — the fire on the altar must never go out.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses. Leviticus is the third book of the Pentateuch, written during
the wilderness period (approximately 1445-1405 BC). Hebrew title: "Vayikra"
— "And He called." God speaks from the newly completed Tabernacle, giving
Israel instructions for worship, sacrifice, and holy living.

Historical Context: Chapter 6 transitions from the worshipper's perspective
(chapters 1-5) to the priest's perspective — how the priests are to
administer the offerings. The chapter opens with a final case of the guilt/
trespass offering (vv.1-7) involving sins against a neighbor that are also
sins against God, then provides priestly regulations for the burnt offering
(vv.8-13), grain offering (vv.14-23), and sin offering (vv.24-30). The
central image of this chapter is the perpetual fire: "The fire shall ever be
burning upon the altar; it shall never go out" (v.13). This unquenchable
flame represents God's unceasing readiness to receive worship, the
continuous need for atonement, and the call to unbroken devotion. Note the
Hebrew numbering differs — in Hebrew Bibles, 6:1-7 is actually 5:20-26,
and 6:8-30 begins at 6:1.

The Guilt Offering for Fraud (vv.1-7): Sins against a neighbor ARE sins
against God — "commit a trespass against the LORD" by lying to a neighbor
(v.2). Restitution is required: full restoration plus one-fifth (20%) added
(v.5). The sacrifice alone is insufficient without making things right with
the wronged party. Both restitution AND sacrifice are needed for forgiveness
(vv.5-7). This principle reappears in Matthew 5:23-24.

The Law of the Burnt Offering for Priests (vv.8-13): The burnt offering
burns all night until morning (v.9). The priest changes garments for ash
removal — linen for sacred duty, other garments for carrying ashes out
(vv.10-11). Ashes taken to a "clean place" outside the camp (v.11). The
fire must NEVER go out (vv.12-13) — stated three times for emphasis. Wood
added every morning (v.12). The perpetual fire: God is always ready to
receive the worshipper.

The Law of the Grain Offering for Priests (vv.14-23): The memorial portion
burned on the altar; the rest eaten by priests (vv.15-16). Must be eaten
unleavened in a holy place (v.16). "Most holy" — same status as sin and
guilt offerings (v.17). Whatever touches it becomes holy (v.18). The
priest's own grain offering on the day of anointing (vv.19-20): entirely
burned, not eaten (vv.22-23). The priest cannot benefit from his own
offering — it must be wholly given to God.

The Law of the Sin Offering for Priests (vv.24-30): Killed in the same
place as the burnt offering (v.25). The priest who offers it eats it in a
holy place (v.26). Whatever touches the flesh becomes holy (v.27). Blood
on garments must be washed in a holy place (v.27). Earthen vessels broken;
bronze vessels scoured (v.28) — holiness is contagious and must be
contained. If blood is brought into the Tabernacle for atonement, the
offering is burned, not eaten (v.30).

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "If a soul sin, and commit a trespass against the LORD, and lie unto
        his neighbour" — Sin against man IS sin against God.

v.5  — "He shall even restore it in the principal, and shall add the fifth
        part more thereto" — Restitution plus 20%. Grace costs the sinner
        something.

v.7  — "The priest shall make an atonement for him before the LORD: and it
        shall be forgiven him" — After restitution, full forgiveness.

v.12 — "The fire upon the altar shall be burning in it; it shall not be put
        out" — The perpetual fire, first mention.

v.13 — "The fire shall ever be burning upon the altar; it shall never go
        out." — Emphatic repetition. Unceasing devotion. God never stops
        being ready to receive worship.

v.17 — "It is most holy, as is the sin offering, and as the trespass
        offering." — The grain offering shares the same sacred status.

v.30 — "No sin offering, whereof any of the blood is brought into the
        tabernacle of the congregation to reconcile withal in the holy
        place, shall be eaten: it shall be burnt in the fire." — When
        atonement reaches the Holy Place, the offering is entirely consumed.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/leviticus-6/
  Guzik emphasizes the perpetual fire as representing God's constant
  readiness to receive worship and the believer's call to unceasing
  devotion. He notes the restitution principle: sacrifice without making
  things right with your neighbor is insufficient.

Charles Spurgeon:
  Sermon: "The Fire That Never Goes Out"
  "The fire shall NEVER go out — here is the heart of worship. God does not
  sleep. His altar is never cold. His readiness to receive the sinner never
  wanes. And so our devotion must be: not a flash of enthusiasm that dies
  by Tuesday, but a steady flame that burns through every season. Keep the
  fire burning, Christian. Add the wood of prayer every morning. Let the
  fat of praise ascend continually. The altar of your heart must never grow
  cold."

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1  — Note: NET includes Hebrew verse numbering in parentheses. In the
       Hebrew Bible, Leviticus 6:1-7 (English) is 5:20-26 (Hebrew). This
       chapter division difference affects cross-referencing.

v.2  — Hebrew "ma'al ma'al" — to act unfaithfully/treacherously. Hebrew
       "amith" — fellow citizen/neighbor.
       KJV: "If a soul sin, and commit a trespass against the LORD... in
       fellowship"
       ESV: "If anyone sins and commits a breach of faith against the
       LORD... or security"
       ASV: "commit a trespass against Jehovah, and deal falsely with his
       neighbor"
       NET: "commits a trespass against the Lord by deceiving his fellow
       citizen"
       WEB: "commits a trespass against Yahweh, and deals falsely with his
       neighbor"
       Analysis: ESV's "breach of faith" captures the covenant-breaking
       nature. KJV's "trespass" is more general. NET's "fellow citizen"
       (Hebrew "amith") is more specific than KJV/ASV's "neighbor." The
       Hebrew emphasizes betrayal of trust. KJV's "in fellowship" is
       misleading; ESV's "security" (a financial pledge, Hebrew "tesumeth
       yad") is more accurate.

v.5  — Hebrew "b'rosho" — in its head/chief amount.
       KJV: "in the principal"
       ESV: "in full"
       ASV: "in full"
       NET: "its full value"
       WEB: "in full"
       Analysis: All convey full restitution of the original amount plus
       one-fifth (20%).

v.6  — Hebrew "asham" — guilt/trespass offering.
       KJV: "trespass offering"
       ESV: "guilt offering"
       ASV: "trespass-offering"
       NET: "guilt offering"
       WEB: "trespass offering"
       Analysis: NET/ESV translate "asham" as "guilt offering" (emphasizing
       the guilt that requires reparation), while ASV/WEB/KJV use "trespass
       offering" (emphasizing the violation). Both capture different aspects
       of the same Hebrew term.

v.9  — Hebrew "torath ha'olah" — the law/instruction of the burnt offering.
       Hebrew "moqdah" — the hearth/burning surface of the altar.
       KJV: "the law of the burnt offering... on the hearth"
       ESV: "the law of the burnt offering... on the hearth"
       ASV: "the burnt-offering shall be on the hearth"
       NET: "The burnt offering is to remain on the hearth"
       WEB: "the burnt offering shall be on the hearth"
       Analysis: All translations agree on "hearth" (Hebrew "moqdah") — the
       burning surface of the altar. "Torah" here means instruction/
       regulation, not the whole Law. The perpetual fire symbolizes
       unceasing worship.

v.10 — Hebrew "miknesei bad" — linen undergarments. Hebrew "deshen" —
       fatty ashes.
       KJV: "linen breeches... the ashes"
       ESV: "linen undergarment... the ashes"
       ASV: "linen breeches... the ashes whereto the fire hath consumed"
       NET: "linen leggings over his bare flesh... the fatty ashes"
       WEB: "linen trousers... the ashes from where the fire has consumed"
       Analysis: ESV modernizes "breeches"; NET uses "leggings" and adds
       "bare" to clarify priestly modesty. NET's "fatty ashes" (Hebrew
       "deshen") is more precise — these ashes are mixed with sacrificial
       fat, not ordinary ash.

v.14 — Hebrew "minchah" — grain offering.
       KJV: "the meat offering"
       ESV: "the grain offering"
       ASV: "the meal-offering"
       NET: "the grain offering"
       WEB: "the meal offering"
       Analysis: KJV's "meat" is archaic English for "food" (not flesh).
       ESV/NET "grain offering" is far clearer for modern readers. This is
       flour, oil, and frankincense — no animal flesh involved.

v.15 — Hebrew "re'ach nichoach... azkaratah" — pleasing aroma... its
       memorial.
       KJV: "a sweet savour, even the memorial of it"
       ESV: "a pleasing aroma to the LORD"
       ASV: "for a sweet savor, the memorial thereof"
       NET: "for a soothing aroma, its memorial portion"
       WEB: "for a pleasant aroma, the memorial of it"
       Analysis: KJV/ASV/NET/WEB preserve "memorial" (azkarah) — the
       portion that brings the worshipper to God's remembrance. ESV omits
       this nuance.

v.21 — Hebrew "al machabath" — on a flat plate/griddle.
       KJV: "in a pan"
       ESV: "on a griddle"
       ASV: "upon a baking-pan"
       NET: "on a griddle"
       WEB: "on a griddle"
       Analysis: ESV/NET/WEB are more precise; KJV's "pan" suggests
       sides/depth.

v.25 — Hebrew "torath ha-chatta'th" — the law of the sin offering.
       KJV: "the law of the sin offering"
       ESV: "the law of the sin offering"
       ASV: "the law of the sin-offering"
       NET: "the law of the sin offering"
       WEB: "the law of the sin offering"
       Analysis: All identical. "Sin offering" — Hebrew "chatta'th" — from
       the root "to miss/fail."

v.28 — Hebrew "bushlah" — boiled/cooked. Hebrew "n'chosheth" — bronze.
       KJV: "sodden... brasen vessel"
       ESV: "boiled... bronze vessel"
       ASV: "boiled... brazen vessel"
       NET: "boiled... bronze vessel"
       WEB: "boiled... bronze vessel"
       Analysis: KJV's "sodden" is archaic past participle of "seethe" (to
       boil). KJV's "brasen" = ESV/NET/WEB's "bronze" — same metal,
       updated term.

v.30 — Hebrew "l'khapper ba-qodesh" — to atone in the holy place.
       KJV: "to reconcile withal in the holy place"
       ESV: "to make atonement in the Holy Place"
       ASV: "to make atonement in the holy place"
       NET: "to make atonement in the holy place"
       WEB: "to make atonement in the holy place"
       Analysis: KJV's "reconcile" and ESV/ASV/NET/WEB's "atonement" are
       both valid renderings of "kaphar."

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Trespass/Guilt offering (Asham) — The offering for sins requiring
               compensation. Addresses both the guilt before God and the
               debt owed to the wronged party. Requires restitution plus
               20% before the sacrifice is offered.

Perpetual fire — The fire on the altar that must never go out (v.13).
               Represents God's unceasing readiness to receive worship,
               the continuous need for atonement, and the call to
               unbroken devotion. Maintained by priests adding wood
               every morning.

Restitution    — Full repayment plus one-fifth (20%) to the wronged party.
               Sacrifice without restitution is unacceptable. God requires
               both vertical reconciliation (with Him) and horizontal
               reconciliation (with neighbor).

"Most holy" (qodesh qodashim) — The highest degree of sanctity. Applied
               to the grain offering, sin offering, and guilt offering.
               Must be eaten in a holy place by priests only.

Memorial portion (azkarah) — The handful of grain offering burned on the
               altar. It "memorializes" the worshipper before God — brings
               them to God's attention/remembrance.

"Clean place"  — The location outside the camp where ashes are deposited
               (v.11). Not a garbage dump but a designated pure location.
               Even the remains of holy things are treated with respect.

Contagious holiness — Whatever touches the sin offering becomes holy (v.27).
               Holiness in Leviticus is powerful and must be contained.
               Earthen vessels are broken; bronze is scoured. Holiness
               cannot be treated casually.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Commit a trespass against the LORD, and lie unto his neighbour" (v.2).
   Sin against people IS sin against God. You cannot wrong your neighbor
   and be right with God. Is there anyone you need to make restitution to?

2. "He shall restore it in the principal, and shall add the fifth part"
   (v.5). God requires MORE than what was taken — not just making it even,
   but making it MORE than even. Repentance costs something. Are you
   willing to go beyond "fair" in making things right?

3. "The fire shall ever be burning upon the altar; it shall never go out"
   (v.13). Is the fire of your devotion still burning? Or has it gone cold?
   What "wood" do you need to add this morning — prayer, Scripture, worship,
   obedience — to keep the flame alive?

4. The priest's grain offering is wholly burned — he cannot eat his own
   offering (v.23). Those who serve God cannot serve themselves with what
   belongs to God. Ministry is not self-serving.

5. "First be reconciled to thy brother" (Matthew 5:23-24). The guilt
   offering teaches that worship without reconciliation is empty. God
   refuses the sacrifice of someone who won't make things right with
   their neighbor.
