Leviticus - Chapter 9
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Aaron offers the first sacrifices as high priest; fire comes out from the Lord's presence and consumes the offering — God accepts the worship.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses. Leviticus is the third book of the Pentateuch, written during
the wilderness period (approximately 1445-1405 BC). Hebrew title: "Vayikra"
— "And He called." God speaks from the newly completed Tabernacle, giving
Israel instructions for worship, sacrifice, and holy living.

Historical Context: Chapter 9 is the climax of everything that has come
before. The offerings have been prescribed (chapters 1-7). The priests have
been consecrated (chapter 8). Now, on the eighth day — the day after the
seven days of consecration — Aaron begins his ministry. The eighth day in
Scripture represents new beginnings (circumcision on the eighth day, the
resurrection on the first day of the new week). This is the inauguration of
the sacrificial system. Aaron offers first for himself (sin offering and
burnt offering), then for the people (sin offering, burnt offering, grain
offering, and peace offerings). The chapter builds to its stunning
conclusion: the glory of the LORD appears to all the people, fire comes out
from before the LORD and consumes the offering on the altar, and the people
shout and fall on their faces (vv.23-24). This is the divine validation of
the entire system — God Himself accepts the sacrifice with supernatural
fire. Everything in Leviticus 1-8 has been building to this moment.

The Eighth Day — Aaron's First Ministry (vv.1-4): Moses calls Aaron, his
sons, and the elders (v.1). Aaron instructed to take a calf for sin
offering and a ram for burnt offering — both without blemish (v.2). The
people instructed to bring a goat (sin offering), calf and lamb (burnt
offering), ox and ram (peace offerings), and grain offering (vv.3-4).
The reason: "for to day the LORD will appear unto you" (v.4). The
sacrifices prepare the way for God's manifest presence.

Moses' Charge (vv.5-7): The congregation draws near and stands before the
LORD (v.5). Moses: "This is the thing which the LORD commanded that ye
should do: and the glory of the LORD shall appear unto you" (v.6). Moses
tells Aaron: "Go unto the altar, and offer thy sin offering, and thy burnt
offering, and make an atonement for thyself, and for the people" (v.7).
The order matters: first for himself, then for the people. The priest must
be right with God before he can represent others.

Aaron's Offering for Himself (vv.8-14): The sin offering — a calf (v.8).
Aaron's sons assist with the blood; Aaron applies it to the altar horns and
pours it at the base (v.9). Fat, kidneys, and liver lobe burned on the
altar (v.10). Flesh and skin burned outside the camp (v.11). The burnt
offering — a ram (v.12). Blood thrown against the altar sides (v.12).
Pieces and head burned on the altar (v.13). Entrails and legs washed and
burned (v.14). Aaron must atone for himself first — he is a sinner serving
sinners. (Contrast with Christ: Hebrews 7:27.)

Aaron's Offering for the People (vv.15-21): The sin offering — a goat
(v.15). Offered "as the first" — same procedure as his own sin offering.
The burnt offering — offered "according to the manner" (v.16). The grain
offering — a handful burned on the altar "beside the burnt sacrifice of
the morning" (v.17). The peace offerings — ox and ram (v.18). Fat pieces
burned on the altar (vv.19-20). Breasts and right thigh waved before the
LORD (v.21). The full range of offerings: sin dealt with, consecration
made, fellowship established.

Aaron's Blessing and the Glory of God (vv.22-24): Aaron lifts his hands
and blesses the people (v.22) — the priestly benediction (see Numbers
6:24-26). He comes down from the altar (v.22). Moses and Aaron enter the
Tabernacle together, then come out and bless the people (v.23). "And the
glory of the LORD appeared unto all the people" (v.23). "And there came a
fire out from before the LORD, and consumed upon the altar the burnt
offering and the fat" (v.24). The people's response: "they shouted, and
fell on their faces" (v.24). Divine fire = divine acceptance. God validates
the entire sacrificial system with supernatural fire. The people respond
with awe, joy, and worship.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.4  — "For to day the LORD will appear unto you." — The purpose of all
        the preparation: God's manifest presence.

v.6  — "This is the thing which the LORD commanded that ye should do: and
        the glory of the LORD shall appear unto you." — Obedience precedes
        glory. Do what God says, and His presence will come.

v.7  — "Go to the altar and offer your sin offering and your burnt
        offering and make atonement for yourself and for the people." (ESV)
        — First for himself, then for others. The priest must be clean.

v.22 — "And Aaron lifted up his hand toward the people, and blessed them."
        — The priest mediates God's blessing to the people.

v.23 — "And the glory of the LORD appeared unto all the people." — The
        climax: God's visible presence among His people.

v.24 — "And there came a fire out from before the LORD, and consumed upon
        the altar the burnt offering and the fat: which when all the people
        saw, they shouted, and fell on their faces." — Divine acceptance.
        Supernatural fire. Awe and worship.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/leviticus-9/
  Guzik highlights the eighth day as a new beginning, the necessity of
  Aaron offering for himself first (unlike Christ), and the fire from God
  as divine validation. He connects the people's response (shouting and
  falling) to genuine worship that combines joy and reverence.

Charles Spurgeon:
  Sermon: "Fire From Heaven"
  "Fire came out from before the LORD — this is the seal of heaven upon the
  sacrifice. God does not leave His people guessing whether He accepts their
  worship. He answers by fire. At Carmel, Elijah prayed and fire fell. At
  Calvary, God accepted the sacrifice of His Son — and the proof is the
  resurrection, the fire of divine approval upon the Lamb. When the people
  saw it, they SHOUTED — here is joy — and FELL ON THEIR FACES — here is
  reverence. True worship holds both: the shout of gladness and the
  prostration of awe. If you have only joy without reverence, you have
  presumption. If you have only reverence without joy, you have slavish
  fear. But fire from God produces both."

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1  — Hebrew "vay'hi ba-yom ha-sh'mini" — and it came to pass on the
       eighth day.
       KJV: "And it came to pass on the eighth day"
       ESV: "On the eighth day"
       ASV: "And it came to pass on the eighth day"
       NET: "On the eighth day"
       WEB: "On the eighth day"
       Analysis: KJV/ASV preserve the Hebrew narrative formula "vay'hi."
       ESV/NET/WEB streamline. The "eighth day" — after seven days of
       consecration. A new beginning.

v.2  — Hebrew "egel ben baqar" — a calf, son of the herd.
       KJV: "a young calf"
       ESV: "a bull calf"
       ASV: "a calf of the herd"
       NET: "a bull calf for a sin offering"
       WEB: "a calf of the herd for a sin offering"
       Analysis: NET specifies "bull calf" to clarify gender and age. Some
       see significance: Aaron's sin offering is a CALF — recalling the
       golden calf incident (Exodus 32). The very animal of his sin becomes
       his sin offering.

v.4  — Hebrew "minchah b'lulah ba-shamen" — grain offering mixed with oil.
       KJV: "a meat offering mingled with oil"
       ESV: "a grain offering mixed with oil"
       ASV: "a meal-offering mingled with oil"
       NET: "a grain offering mixed with olive oil"
       WEB: "a meal offering mixed with oil"
       Analysis: Three different terms for "minchah": NET/ESV's "grain
       offering," ASV/WEB's "meal-offering," and KJV's archaic "meat
       offering." NET specifies "olive oil" (Hebrew "shemen") for clarity.

v.4  — Hebrew "ki ha-yom YHWH nir'ah aleikhem" — for today the LORD will
       appear to you.
       KJV: "for to day the LORD will appear unto you"
       ESV: "for today the LORD will appear to you"
       ASV: "Jehovah appeareth unto you"
       NET: "the Lord is going to appear to you"
       WEB: "Yahweh appears to you"
       Analysis: NET uses future tense to build anticipation. The promise:
       God will make Himself visible/manifest. This is the purpose of all
       the preparation. The divine name difference is visible.

v.7  — Hebrew "q'rav el ha-mizbe'ach" — draw near/approach to the altar.
       KJV: "Go unto the altar"
       ESV: "Draw near to the altar"
       ASV: "Draw near unto the altar"
       NET: "make atonement on behalf of yourself and on behalf of the
       people"
       WEB: "Go to the altar"
       Analysis: ESV/ASV are more literal: "draw near" — the same root as
       "qorban" (offering). To offer is to draw near to God. NET's "on
       behalf of" (Hebrew "be'ad") emphasizes the representative nature of
       priestly atonement.

v.12 — Hebrew "vay'zar'qu" — to dash/throw.
       KJV: "sprinkled round about upon the altar"
       ESV: "threw it against the sides of the altar"
       ASV: "sprinkled upon the altar round about"
       NET: "splashed it against the altar's sides"
       WEB: "sprinkled it around on the altar"
       Analysis: ESV/NET are more accurate. The blood is dashed vigorously,
       not delicately sprinkled. Hebrew "zaraq" is distinct from "nazah"
       (to sprinkle with finger).

v.17 — Hebrew "minchah" — grain offering. Hebrew "tamid" — daily/continual.
       KJV: "the meat offering... beside the burnt sacrifice of the morning"
       ESV: "the grain offering... besides the burnt offering of the morning"
       ASV: "the meal-offering... besides the burnt-offering of the morning"
       NET: "the grain offering... in addition to the morning burnt offering"
       WEB: "the meal offering... besides the burnt offering of the morning"
       Analysis: This indicates the regular daily burnt offering (tamid) was
       already being offered. Aaron's inaugural offerings are IN ADDITION to
       the daily sacrifice. NET's "in addition to" is the clearest phrasing.

v.21 — Hebrew "shoq ha-yamin" — the right thigh/leg.
       KJV: "the right shoulder"
       ESV: "the right thigh"
       ASV: "the right thigh"
       NET: "the right thigh"
       WEB: "the right thigh"
       Analysis: As in chapter 7, ESV/ASV/NET/WEB's "thigh" is anatomically
       more accurate than KJV's "shoulder."

v.22 — Hebrew "yadav" (his hands, plural) — raised in blessing.
       KJV: "Aaron lifted up his hand"
       ESV: "Aaron lifted up his hands"
       ASV: "Aaron lifted up his hands toward the people, and blessed them"
       NET: "Aaron lifted his hands toward the people and blessed them"
       WEB: "Aaron lifted up his hands toward the people, and blessed them"
       Analysis: ESV/ASV/NET/WEB correctly render the plural. KJV's
       singular "hand" is a textual variant but the Hebrew is plural — both
       hands raised in blessing. The priestly blessing with uplifted hands
       is the visible sign of God's favor.

v.23 — Hebrew "kavod YHWH" — the glory of the LORD.
       KJV: "the glory of the LORD appeared unto all the people"
       ESV: "the glory of the LORD appeared to all the people"
       ASV: "the glory of Jehovah appeared unto all the people"
       NET: "the glory of the Lord appeared to all the people"
       WEB: "Yahweh's glory appeared to all the people"
       Analysis: All identical in meaning. "Kavod" — glory, weight, honor.
       The weighty, visible manifestation of God's presence.

v.24 — Hebrew "va-tetse esh mil'lifnei YHWH" — fire came out from before
       the LORD. Hebrew "vay'rannu vay'yiplu al p'neihem" — they shouted
       and fell on their faces.
       KJV: "there came a fire out from before the LORD... they shouted,
       and fell on their faces"
       ESV: "fire came out from before the LORD... they shouted and fell on
       their faces"
       ASV: "there came forth fire from before Jehovah, and devoured them"
       NET: "fire went out from the presence of the Lord and consumed them"
       WEB: "fire came out from before Yahweh, and consumed"
       Analysis: NET's "from the presence of the Lord" interprets "miliphne
       YHWH" (from before the face of YHWH). "Rannu" — to shout/sing for
       joy (not a scream of fear). "Fell on their faces" — prostration in
       awe. Joy AND reverence together. Divine fire consumes the offering —
       God's acceptance is visible and dramatic.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Eighth day     — The day after the seven days of consecration. In Scripture,
               eight represents new beginnings: circumcision on the eighth
               day (Genesis 17:12), resurrection on the first day of the
               new week (the "eighth day"). Aaron's ministry begins on a
               day of new beginning.

Glory of the LORD (Kavod YHWH) — The visible, weighty manifestation of
               God's presence. Appeared as cloud and fire at Sinai, filled
               the Tabernacle (Exodus 40:34), and here appears to all the
               people. "Kavod" means weight/heaviness — God's presence has
               substance and gravity.

Fire from God  — Supernatural fire that consumes the sacrifice. Divine
               acceptance made visible. Occurs at key moments: here, at
               Elijah's contest (1 Kings 18), at Solomon's Temple
               (2 Chronicles 7:1), and at David's altar (1 Chronicles
               21:26). God answers by fire.

"Fell on their faces" — Prostration in worship. The natural human response
               to God's manifest presence. Combines awe, reverence, fear,
               and joy. Not a casual posture but total submission before
               the Holy One.

Priestly blessing — Aaron lifts his hands and blesses the people (v.22).
               The content is likely the Aaronic benediction of Numbers
               6:24-26: "The LORD bless thee, and keep thee; the LORD make
               his face shine upon thee..." The priest mediates God's
               blessing to the people.

"According to the manner" — (v.16) Following the prescribed procedure of
               chapters 1-7. Aaron does not innovate; he follows God's
               instructions exactly. Legitimate worship follows God's
               pattern.

Atonement for himself — Aaron must offer for his own sin before he can
               represent the people (v.7). This distinguishes the Levitical
               priesthood from Christ's: "Who needeth not daily... to offer
               up sacrifice, first for his own sins" (Hebrews 7:27). Our
               High Priest is sinless.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "For to day the LORD will appear unto you" (v.4). God promises His
   manifest presence — but it comes AFTER obedience, AFTER sacrifice,
   AFTER preparation. Are you doing what God commands and then expecting
   His presence? Or are you demanding His presence without obedience?

2. Aaron must offer for himself first (v.7). Before you can minister to
   others, you must deal with your own sin. A pastor, teacher, parent, or
   friend who has not dealt with their own sin before God cannot effectively
   help others deal with theirs. Is your own house in order?

3. "The glory of the LORD appeared unto all the people" (v.23). God's
   presence is not hidden or private here — it is PUBLIC, visible to ALL.
   God wants to be known. He reveals Himself to those who seek Him through
   the appointed means.

4. "They shouted, and fell on their faces" (v.24). Joy AND reverence. The
   shout of gladness AND the prostration of awe. Is your worship balanced?
   Do you have the joy without the reverence (casual presumption)? Or the
   reverence without the joy (cold formalism)? True worship holds both.

5. Fire from God consumes the offering (v.24). God Himself accepts the
   sacrifice. You don't have to wonder if your offering through Christ is
   accepted — God has already answered by fire. The resurrection is God's
   "fire from heaven" on the sacrifice of Calvary. It is finished. It is
   accepted. Shout and fall on your face.
