Leviticus - Chapter 7
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
God completes the instructions for the guilt offering and peace offering, specifying the priests' portions and forbidding eating fat or blood.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses. Leviticus is the third book of the Pentateuch, written during
the wilderness period (approximately 1445-1405 BC). Hebrew title: "Vayikra"
— "And He called." God speaks from the newly completed Tabernacle, giving
Israel instructions for worship, sacrifice, and holy living.

Historical Context: Chapter 7 concludes the entire offering section that
began in chapter 1. It provides priestly regulations for the guilt/trespass
offering (vv.1-10), then gives detailed laws for the peace offering
(vv.11-36), and closes with a summary statement covering all the offerings
(vv.37-38). The peace offering is unique among the sacrifices because it is
the only offering where the worshipper eats a portion — it is a communal
meal shared between God (the fat burned on the altar), the priest (breast
and right thigh), and the worshipper (the remaining flesh). This makes it a
picture of fellowship and communion. Three categories of peace offering are
distinguished: thanksgiving (todah), vow (neder), and freewill (nedabah).
The chapter also contains absolute prohibitions against eating fat and blood
— commands that carry the penalty of being "cut off" from the people.

The Law of the Guilt/Trespass Offering (vv.1-10): Killed where the burnt
offering is killed (v.2). Blood sprinkled on the altar (v.2). All fat
offered: fat tail, fat covering entrails, kidneys, liver lobe (vv.3-4).
Burned on the altar as a fire offering (v.5). Eaten by male priests in a
holy place (v.6). Same law as sin offering — priest who atones receives it
(v.7). Priest receives the skin of burnt offerings (v.8). Priest receives
baked/prepared grain offerings; dry/oil-mixed grain offerings shared equally
among all priests (vv.9-10).

The Law of the Peace Offering (vv.11-21): Three categories:
  (1) Thanksgiving (vv.12-15) — offered with unleavened cakes, wafers, and
      fried cakes, PLUS leavened bread (v.13). Must be eaten the same day
      (v.15). The urgency: gratitude should be immediate and shared.
  (2) Vow or Freewill (vv.16-17) — may be eaten over two days. Remainder
      burned on the third day (v.17).
  (3) Penalty for violation: eating on the third day — the offering is not
      accepted, not credited, becomes an "abomination" (v.18).
Unclean flesh must be burned (v.19). Eating while unclean results in being
"cut off from his people" (vv.20-21).

Prohibition of Fat and Blood (vv.22-27): No fat of ox, sheep, or goat to
be eaten (v.23). Fat of dead/torn animals may be used for other purposes
but not eaten (v.24). Penalty: cut off from the people (v.25). No blood of
any kind — fowl or beast (v.26). Penalty: cut off (v.27). The fat belongs
to God (burned on the altar). The blood belongs to God (it is the life —
Leviticus 17:11). Man may not consume what belongs to God.

The Wave Offering and Heave Offering (vv.28-36): The worshipper brings the
offering with his own hands (v.30) — personal involvement required. The fat
burned on the altar; the breast waved before the LORD and given to Aaron's
sons (vv.30-31). The right thigh/shoulder given as a heave offering to the
officiating priest (vv.32-33). Wave breast and heave thigh: a perpetual
statute for priestly provision (v.34). This is their "portion of the
anointing" (v.35).

Summary Statement (vv.37-38): "This is the law of the burnt offering, of
the meat offering, and of the sin offering, and of the trespass offering,
and of the consecrations, and of the sacrifice of the peace offerings"
(v.37). Given at Mount Sinai in the wilderness (v.38). This closes the
entire sacrificial legislation of Leviticus 1-7.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.11 — "This is the law of the sacrifice of peace offerings, which he shall
        offer unto the LORD." — The peace offering: fellowship with God.

v.13 — "He shall offer for his offering leavened bread with the sacrifice
        of thanksgiving" — Unique: leavened bread permitted here.

v.15 — "The flesh of the sacrifice of his peace offerings for thanksgiving
        shall be eaten the same day" — Gratitude is urgent and shared.

v.20 — "The soul that eateth of the flesh... having his uncleanness upon
        him, even that soul shall be cut off from his people." — Holiness
        required for communion with God.

v.26 — "You shall not eat any blood, whether of fowl or of animal, in any
        of your dwelling places." (ESV) — Absolute prohibition. Blood is
        life; life belongs to God.

v.30 — "His own hands shall bring the offerings of the LORD made by fire"
        — Personal, hands-on worship. No proxies.

v.37 — "This is the law of the burnt offering, of the meat offering, and of
        the sin offering, and of the trespass offering, and of the
        consecrations, and of the sacrifice of the peace offerings" —
        Summary of all offerings (Leviticus 1-7).

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/leviticus-7/
  Guzik highlights the peace offering as the only sacrifice where the
  worshipper eats — making it a picture of communion and fellowship. He
  notes the three categories (thanksgiving, vow, freewill) and the urgency
  of the same-day eating requirement for thanksgiving offerings.

Charles Spurgeon:
  Sermon: "The Peace Offering and the Feast"
  "The peace offering is the feast of reconciliation. God takes His portion
  — the fat upon the altar. The priest takes his — the breast and the
  shoulder. And the worshipper sits down to eat with God. This is
  fellowship. This is communion. This is what the Lord's Table pictures for
  us: Christ our peace offering has been slain, and now we feast together
  with God upon the sacrifice. His own hands shall bring it — you cannot
  worship by proxy. Your own hands, your own heart, your own will must be
  engaged in the offering."

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1  — KJV: "the trespass offering" / ESV: "the guilt offering" — Hebrew
       "asham." Same distinction as chapter 6. ESV's "guilt offering"
       better conveys the compensatory nature. KJV's "trespass" focuses on
       the act of violation.

v.2  — KJV: "sprinkle round about upon the altar" / ESV: "thrown against
       the sides of the altar" — Hebrew "yizroq" — to dash/throw. ESV is
       more accurate: the blood is dashed/splashed, not carefully sprinkled.
       This is a vigorous action.

v.4  — KJV: "the caul that is above the liver" / ESV: "the long lobe of
       the liver" — Hebrew "yothereth ha-kaved." The exact anatomical
       referent is debated. ESV interprets it as the caudate lobe of the
       liver itself; KJV as a membrane (caul) above the liver. Modern
       scholarship favors ESV's reading.

v.9  — KJV: "meat offering that is baken in the oven" / ESV: "grain
       offering baked in the oven" — Again, KJV's "meat" = food (archaic).
       ESV's "grain" is accurate and clear.

v.14 — KJV: "an heave offering unto the LORD" / ESV: "a gift to the LORD"
       — Hebrew "terumah" — a contribution/portion lifted up. KJV preserves
       the technical term "heave offering" (from the lifting motion). ESV
       simplifies to "gift," losing the ritual gesture but gaining clarity.

v.16 — KJV: "a vow, or a voluntary offering" / ESV: "a vow offering or a
       freewill offering" — Hebrew "neder" (vow) and "nedabah" (freewill).
       Both translations are clear. The distinction: a vow is promised in
       advance; a freewill offering is spontaneous generosity.

v.18 — KJV: "it shall be an abomination" / ESV: "It is tainted" — Hebrew
       "piggul" — a foul/rejected thing. KJV's "abomination" is stronger
       than the Hebrew warrants here. ESV's "tainted" is more precise —
       the offering has become ritually repulsive/rejected.

v.23 — KJV: "Ye shall eat no manner of fat, of ox, or of sheep, or of
       goat" / ESV: "You shall eat no fat, of ox or sheep or goat" — Both
       essentially identical. The prohibition applies specifically to
       sacrificial animals (ox, sheep, goat), not all animal fat.

v.30 — KJV: "His own hands shall bring the offerings" / ESV: "His own
       hands shall bring the LORD's food offerings" — Hebrew "yadav
       tevi'enah." Both capture the personal nature. The worshipper
       physically carries the offering — worship requires personal
       involvement.

v.32 — KJV: "the right shoulder" / ESV: "the right thigh" — Hebrew
       "shoq ha-yamin." The Hebrew "shoq" refers to the upper leg/thigh,
       not the shoulder. ESV is anatomically more accurate. KJV's
       "shoulder" may reflect older English usage.

v.37 — KJV: "the meat offering... the trespass offering... the
       consecrations" / ESV: "the grain offering... the guilt offering...
       the ordination offering" — ESV consistently uses modern equivalents.
       "Consecrations" (KJV) / "ordination offering" (ESV) — Hebrew
       "millu'im" — literally "fillings" (filling the hands for service).

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Peace offering (Shelamim) — From "shalom" (peace/wholeness). The offering
               of fellowship and communion. Unique because God, priest, and
               worshipper all share in eating. Represents restored
               relationship and joyful communion. Three types: thanksgiving,
               vow, and freewill.

Thanksgiving (Todah) — A peace offering motivated by gratitude for God's
               deliverance or blessing. Must be eaten the same day —
               gratitude is urgent and meant to be shared immediately.

Vow offering (Neder) — A peace offering fulfilling a promise made to God.
               "If you do X, I will offer Y." May be eaten over two days.

Freewill offering (Nedabah) — A spontaneous, voluntary peace offering with
               no obligation or prior promise. Pure generosity. May be
               eaten over two days.

Wave offering (Tenufah) — The breast of the peace offering, waved
               (moved back and forth) before the LORD, then given to the
               priests. The waving motion symbolizes presenting it to God
               and receiving it back for the priests.

Heave offering (Terumah) — The right thigh/shoulder, lifted up before the
               LORD, then given to the officiating priest. "Heave" = to
               lift/raise. A contribution separated for sacred use.

"Cut off" (karath) — The penalty for eating fat, blood, or sacred food
               while unclean. Debated meaning: excommunication from the
               community, premature death by God's hand, or loss of
               covenant standing. Regardless, it is the most severe
               penalty short of execution.

Fat (chelev)   — The internal fat (suet) of sacrificial animals. Belongs
               exclusively to God — burned on the altar. Represents the
               best/richest portion. Man may not consume what belongs to God.

Blood (dam)    — "The life of the flesh is in the blood" (Lev 17:11).
               Blood belongs to God because life belongs to God. The
               prohibition predates Moses (Genesis 9:4) and continues into
               the NT (Acts 15:20).

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. The peace offering is a MEAL with God (vv.11-15). The Lord's Supper is
   the NT fulfillment — we feast with God on the sacrifice of Christ. Do
   you approach communion as a meal of fellowship, or merely a ritual?

2. "His own hands shall bring the offerings" (v.30). Worship cannot be
   delegated. You cannot hire someone to pray for you, give for you, or
   worship for you. Your own hands must be involved. Is your worship
   personal and engaged, or passive and distant?

3. Three motivations for peace offerings: thanksgiving (gratitude), vow
   (faithfulness), freewill (spontaneous love). Which motivates your
   worship most? Which is lacking?

4. Eating while unclean brings judgment (v.20). Paul warns: "He that eateth
   and drinketh unworthily, eateth and drinketh damnation to himself"
   (1 Cor 11:29). Communion requires self-examination. Do you examine
   yourself before approaching God's table?

5. Fat and blood belong to God alone (vv.23-27). The best belongs to Him.
   The life belongs to Him. Are you keeping for yourself what rightfully
   belongs to God — your best time, your best energy, your very life?
