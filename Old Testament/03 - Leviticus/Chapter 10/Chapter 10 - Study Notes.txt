Leviticus - Chapter 10
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Nadab and Abihu offer unauthorized fire before the Lord and are consumed; God declares He must be regarded as holy by those who approach Him.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses. Leviticus is the third book of the Pentateuch, written during
the wilderness period (approximately 1445-1405 BC). Hebrew title: "Vayikra"
— "And He called." God speaks from the newly completed Tabernacle, giving
Israel instructions for worship, sacrifice, and holy living.

Historical Context: Chapter 10 is the immediate and devastating sequel to
chapter 9. The fire that came from God to consume the offering in divine
acceptance (9:24) now comes from God to consume two priests in divine
judgment (10:2). The same fire that blesses obedience destroys presumption.
Nadab and Abihu, Aaron's two eldest sons — newly consecrated priests —
offer "strange fire" (unauthorized fire) before the LORD. They are killed
instantly. This chapter establishes a principle that runs through all of
Scripture: God is holy, and those who approach Him must do so on HIS terms,
not their own. The chapter also reveals the cost of priestly service: Aaron
is forbidden to mourn publicly, God speaks directly to Aaron (the only time
in Leviticus outside of chapter 16) about sobriety, and the chapter ends
with a dispute about proper handling of the sin offering — resolved by
Aaron's grief-stricken but theologically sound reasoning.

The Sin of Nadab and Abihu (vv.1-3): Nadab and Abihu each take his censer,
put fire in it, lay incense on it, and offer "strange fire" (KJV) /
"unauthorized fire" (ESV) before the LORD — "which he commanded them not"
(v.1). Fire comes out from before the LORD and devours them; they die before
the LORD (v.2). Moses speaks to Aaron: "This is it that the LORD spake,
saying, I will be sanctified in them that come nigh me, and before all the
people I will be glorified" (v.3). Aaron's response: silence. "Aaron held
his peace" (v.3). The nature of their sin: they offered what God had NOT
commanded. The issue is not that they did something explicitly forbidden —
it is that they innovated. They approached God on their own terms. The fire
that consumed the offering (9:24) now consumes the offerers (10:2). Same
source, opposite result. Obedience brings blessing; presumption brings
death.

Removal of the Bodies and Mourning Restrictions (vv.4-7): Moses calls
Mishael and Elzaphan (sons of Uzziel, Aaron's uncle — Levites, not priests)
to carry the bodies out of the camp (v.4). They carry them in their coats
(v.5) — the priestly garments became their burial shrouds. Moses commands
Aaron, Eleazar, and Ithamar: do NOT uncover your heads, do NOT tear your
clothes — "lest ye die, and lest wrath come upon all the people" (v.6).
The rest of Israel may mourn, but the priests must not (v.6). They must not
leave the Tabernacle entrance — "for the anointing oil of the LORD is upon
you" (v.7). They obey (v.7). The anointing takes priority over personal
grief. The priest belongs to God before he belongs to his family.

God Speaks Directly to Aaron — Wine and Discernment (vv.8-11): The LORD
speaks to Aaron directly (v.8) — rare in Leviticus. The command: "Do not
drink wine nor strong drink, thou, nor thy sons with thee, when ye go into
the tabernacle of the congregation, lest ye die" (v.9). A statute forever
(v.9). The purpose: "that ye may put difference between holy and unholy,
and between unclean and clean" (v.10), and "that ye may teach the children
of Israel all the statutes" (v.11). The placement of this command
immediately after Nadab and Abihu's sin strongly implies intoxication may
have contributed to their presumption. Discernment requires sobriety —
both literal and spiritual.

Instructions for the Remaining Priests (vv.12-15): Moses instructs Aaron,
Eleazar, and Ithamar regarding their priestly portions: the grain offering
eaten unleavened beside the altar — "it is most holy" (v.12). Eaten in the
holy place — their due (v.13). The wave breast and heave thigh eaten in a
clean place — by the priests, their sons, and daughters (v.14). These are
their perpetual due from the peace offerings (v.15). Even in the midst of
tragedy, the priestly duties continue. God's service does not stop for
personal grief.

The Dispute over the Sin Offering (vv.16-20): Moses searches for the goat
of the sin offering and finds it has been burned up entirely (v.16). He is
angry with Eleazar and Ithamar (v.16). His charge: "Why have ye not eaten
the sin offering in the holy place?" — it was most holy, given to them to
"bear the iniquity of the congregation, to make atonement for them" (v.17).
The blood was not brought inside the sanctuary, so they should have eaten it
(v.18). Aaron answers: they offered their sin offering and burnt offering
today, "and such things have befallen me" — if he had eaten the sin
offering today, "should it have been accepted in the sight of the LORD?"
(v.19). Moses hears this and is satisfied (v.20). Aaron's reasoning: in
his state of grief and defilement (his sons' sin and death), eating the
holy offering would not have honored God. This is not disobedience — it is
pastoral wisdom. Moses accepts it.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/leviticus-10/
  Guzik emphasizes that Nadab and Abihu's sin was offering what God had NOT
  commanded — the principle of worship by divine prescription, not human
  innovation. He notes the possible connection to intoxication (vv.8-9
  placed immediately after their death). He highlights Aaron's silence as
  one of the most powerful moments in the Old Testament — a father who
  submits to God's justice even in the loss of his sons.

Charles Spurgeon:
  Sermon: "Strange Fire"
  "Nadab and Abihu brought fire which God had not commanded. They did not
  bring something God had forbidden — they brought something God had not
  authorized. Here is the principle: in the worship of God, silence is
  prohibition. What God has not commanded, He has forbidden. Men say, 'Where
  does the Bible say we CANNOT do this?' God says, 'Where did I say you
  COULD?' The fire on the altar was God's fire — kindled from heaven (9:24).
  Any other fire is 'strange' — foreign, unauthorized, man-made. And
  man-made worship, however sincere, is an abomination. Aaron held his
  peace. What could he say? God was right. His sons were wrong. And a
  father's grief does not change God's holiness."

REFLECTION
------------------------------------
1. "Which he commanded them not" (v.1). Nadab and Abihu's sin was not doing
   something God explicitly forbade — it was doing something God had not
   authorized. In worship, silence is prohibition. Are you approaching God
   on HIS terms or your own? Is your worship shaped by "what does God say?"
   or "what do I feel like doing?"

2. "Aaron held his peace" (v.3). Sometimes the godliest response to
   suffering is silence. Not because you have no questions, but because you
   trust God's character even when you cannot understand His actions. Can
   you be silent before God when His providence is painful?

3. "Do not drink wine nor strong drink... that ye may put difference
   between holy and unholy" (vv.9-10). Sobriety enables discernment.
   Intoxication — whether literal or spiritual (distraction, worldliness,
   entertainment) — blurs the lines between holy and common. What is
   dulling your spiritual discernment?

4. "I will be sanctified in them that come nigh me" (v.3). The closer you
   are to God, the higher the standard. Privilege brings responsibility.
   Nadab and Abihu were not outsiders — they were priests, sons of the high
   priest, men who had seen God on Sinai (Exodus 24:1,9). Greater access
   means greater accountability.

5. Aaron's reasoning (v.19) — even correct ritual can be wrong in wrong
   circumstances. The letter of the law without the spirit of the law is
   not obedience. Moses accepted this. God values the heart behind the
   action, not merely the action itself.
