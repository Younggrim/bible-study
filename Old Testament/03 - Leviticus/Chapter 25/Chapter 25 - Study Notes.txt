Leviticus - Chapter 25
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
God institutes the Sabbath year and the Year of Jubilee — every fifty years, land returns, slaves go free, and debts are canceled.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses. Leviticus is the third book of the Pentateuch, written during
the wilderness period (approximately 1445-1405 BC). Hebrew title: "Vayikra"
— "And He called." God speaks from the newly completed Tabernacle, giving
Israel instructions for worship, sacrifice, and holy living.

Historical Context: Chapter 25 is one of the most socially revolutionary
chapters in the Bible. It establishes two extraordinary institutions: the
Sabbatical Year (every seventh year the land rests) and the Year of Jubilee
(every fiftieth year — liberty proclaimed, land returns to original owners,
slaves freed). The chapter opens uniquely: "The LORD spake unto Moses in
mount Sinai" (v.1) — connecting these economic laws directly to the Sinai
covenant and emphasizing their divine authority. The underlying theology is
stated plainly: "The land is mine; for ye are strangers and sojourners with
me" (v.23). Israel does not OWN the land — God does. They are tenants,
stewards, not proprietors. This prevents permanent accumulation of wealth
by some and permanent poverty for others. Every 50 years, the economic
slate is wiped clean. The Jubilee is profoundly Christological: when Jesus
began His public ministry, He read Isaiah 61:1-2 in the synagogue at
Nazareth — "to preach the acceptable year of the Lord" (Luke 4:18-19) —
the language of Jubilee. Christ IS the Jubilee: He proclaims liberty to
captives, restores what was lost, and cancels the debt of sin. The chapter
also introduces the concept of the "kinsman-redeemer" (go'el) — the near
relative who buys back what a poor brother has lost — pointing directly
to Christ our Redeemer (Ruth 3-4; Galatians 3:13; 4:4-5).

The Sabbatical Year — Rest for the Land (vv.1-7): Six years: sow, prune,
gather (v.3). The seventh year: "a sabbath of rest unto the land" (v.4).
No sowing, no pruning, no organized harvest (vv.4-5). Whatever grows of
itself is available to all — owner, servant, hired worker, sojourner,
cattle, and wild animals (vv.6-7). The Sabbath principle extends from
persons (weekly Sabbath) to the land itself. The land is not a machine
to be exploited endlessly — it belongs to God and must rest. This requires
FAITH: trusting God to provide without human effort for an entire year.

God's Provision for the Sabbath Year (vv.18-22): The anticipated question:
"What shall we eat the seventh year?" (v.20). God's answer: "I will
command my blessing upon you in the sixth year, and it shall bring forth
fruit for three years" (v.21). They sow in the eighth year and eat old
store until the ninth year's harvest comes in (v.22). God promises
supernatural provision — a triple harvest in the sixth year. This is a
test of faith: will you trust God enough to obey when obedience seems
economically irrational?

The Year of Jubilee (vv.8-17): Count seven sabbaths of years (7x7 = 49
years) (v.8). On the tenth day of the seventh month — the Day of
Atonement — sound the trumpet throughout the land (v.9). The Jubilee
begins on the Day of Atonement: liberty flows from atonement. "Proclaim
liberty throughout all the land unto all the inhabitants thereof" (v.10)
— this verse is inscribed on the Liberty Bell. Every man returns to his
property; every man returns to his family (v.10). No sowing or reaping
in the Jubilee year (vv.11-12). All land sales revert — everyone returns
to their original inheritance (v.13). Land prices are calculated based on
years remaining until Jubilee — you are buying harvests, not land
permanently (vv.14-16). "Ye shall not therefore oppress one another; but
thou shalt fear thy God" (v.17).

The Land Belongs to God (v.23): "The land shall not be sold for ever: for
the land is mine; for ye are strangers and sojourners with me." This is
the theological foundation of the entire chapter. God is the ultimate
landowner. Israel holds the land in trust. No one can permanently
dispossess another because no one permanently owns anything — it all
belongs to God. This prevents both permanent wealth concentration and
permanent poverty.

Redemption of Land (vv.24-34): In all the land, redemption must be
allowed (v.24). If a brother becomes poor and sells land, his nearest
kinsman (go'el — redeemer) shall redeem it (v.25). If no redeemer, and
the man himself becomes able, he may redeem it by calculating years and
paying the balance (vv.26-27). If neither can redeem, the land returns
at Jubilee regardless (v.28). Exception: houses in walled cities may be
redeemed within one year; after that, they belong permanently to the
buyer and do NOT return at Jubilee (vv.29-30). Village houses (unwalled)
are treated as fields — they DO return at Jubilee (v.31). Levitical
cities: Levites may always redeem their houses; their houses return at
Jubilee; their pasture fields may never be sold (vv.32-34).

Care for the Poor Brother (vv.35-38): If your brother becomes poor, you
shall support him — even if he is a stranger or sojourner (v.35). Take
no interest (usury) or profit from him (v.36). Do not lend money at
interest or food for profit (v.37). The basis: "I am the LORD your God,
which brought you forth out of the land of Egypt" (v.38). God redeemed
them from slavery; they must not enslave their brothers through debt.

Redemption of Persons (vv.39-55): If a brother becomes poor and sells
himself to you: do NOT treat him as a slave (v.39). He is a hired worker
and sojourner until Jubilee, then he goes free with his children (vv.40-41).
The reason: "They are MY servants, which I brought forth out of Egypt:
they shall not be sold as bondmen" (v.42). Do not rule with rigor (v.43).
If a brother sells himself to a foreigner: he may be redeemed by a
relative — uncle, cousin, or any close kin (vv.47-49). The price is
calculated by years remaining until Jubilee (vv.50-52). He must be
treated as a yearly hired worker, not ruled ruthlessly (v.53). If not
redeemed, he goes free at Jubilee with his children (v.54). Final
declaration: "For unto me the children of Israel are servants; they are
my servants whom I brought forth out of the land of Egypt: I am the LORD
your God" (v.55).

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.4  — "But in the seventh year there shall be a Sabbath of solemn rest
        for the land, a Sabbath to the LORD." (ESV) — Even the land must
        rest. God's Sabbath principle extends to all creation.

v.9  — "Then you shall sound the loud trumpet on the tenth day of the
        seventh month. On the Day of Atonement you shall sound the trumpet
        throughout all your land." (ESV) — Jubilee begins on the Day of
        Atonement. Liberty flows from atonement.

v.10 — "And you shall consecrate the fiftieth year, and proclaim liberty
        throughout the land to all its inhabitants. It shall be a jubilee
        for you." (ESV) — The great proclamation of freedom. Inscribed on
        the Liberty Bell.

v.17 — "You shall not wrong one another, but you shall fear your God, for
        I am the LORD your God." (ESV) — Economic justice rooted in the
        fear of God.

v.23 — "The land shall not be sold in perpetuity, for the land is mine.
        For you are strangers and sojourners with me." (ESV) — God owns
        everything. We are stewards, not owners.

v.25 — "If your brother becomes poor and sells part of his property, then
        his nearest redeemer shall come and redeem what his brother has
        sold." (ESV) — The kinsman-redeemer. Points to Christ.

v.38 — "I am the LORD your God, who brought you out of the land of Egypt
        to give you the land of Canaan, and to be your God." (ESV) —
        Redemption from Egypt is the basis for all these laws.

v.42 — "For they are my servants, whom I brought out of the land of Egypt;
        they shall not be sold as slaves." (ESV) — God's people belong
        to HIM. No one else may permanently own them.

v.55 — "For it is to me that the people of Israel are servants. They are
        my servants whom I brought out of the land of Egypt: I am the LORD
        your God." (ESV) — The chapter's final word: they belong to God.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/leviticus-25/
  Guzik emphasizes the Jubilee as a picture of the gospel: debts cancelled,
  slaves freed, inheritance restored. He notes that there is no biblical
  record of Israel ever fully observing the Jubilee — making Christ's
  fulfillment all the more significant.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "The Year of Jubilee! What a sound that trumpet must have made! Every
  slave heard it and knew — I am free. Every debtor heard it and knew —
  I am released. Every dispossessed man heard it and knew — my inheritance
  is restored. And so it is with the gospel trumpet. Christ has proclaimed
  the acceptable year of the Lord. Your slavery to sin is ended. Your debt
  is cancelled. Your lost inheritance is restored. The trumpet has sounded.
  Believe it and go free!"

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.2  — KJV: "then shall the land keep a sabbath unto the LORD" / ESV:
       "the land shall keep a Sabbath to the LORD" — Hebrew "v'shavtah
       ha-aretz shabbat la-YHWH" — the land shall sabbath a sabbath to
       the LORD. The land itself is personified as keeping Sabbath. The
       verb "shavat" (to cease, rest) is applied to the ground.

v.9  — KJV: "the trumpet of the jubile" / ESV: "the loud trumpet" —
       Hebrew "shofar teru'ah" — the ram's horn of blasting/shouting.
       KJV inserts "jubile" (from "yovel" — ram/ram's horn); ESV
       translates descriptively. The shofar blast on the Day of Atonement
       inaugurates the Jubilee.

v.10 — KJV: "jubile" / ESV: "jubilee" — Hebrew "yovel" — originally
       meaning "ram" or "ram's horn." The year is named for the trumpet
       that announces it. English "jubilee" comes through Latin "jubilaeus"
       from Hebrew "yovel." The word carries connotations of joy and
       celebration, though its root is simply the instrument.

v.10 — KJV: "proclaim liberty throughout all the land unto all the
       inhabitants thereof" / ESV: "proclaim liberty throughout the land
       to all its inhabitants" — Hebrew "uqratem deror ba-aretz l'khol
       yoshveha." "Deror" means liberty, release, free-flowing. This
       exact phrase is inscribed on the Liberty Bell in Philadelphia.

v.23 — KJV: "The land shall not be sold for ever" / ESV: "The land shall
       not be sold in perpetuity" — Hebrew "lo timakher litzmitut" —
       shall not be sold to cutting off (permanently). KJV's "for ever"
       and ESV's "in perpetuity" both capture the meaning: no permanent,
       irrevocable land sales.

v.25 — KJV: "if any of his kin come to redeem it" / ESV: "his nearest
       redeemer shall come and redeem" — Hebrew "go'alo ha-qarov" — his
       redeemer, the near one. "Go'el" is the kinsman-redeemer — a near
       relative with the right and responsibility to buy back what a poor
       brother has lost. This is the same term used of Boaz (Ruth 3:9, 12)
       and of God Himself (Isaiah 41:14; 43:14; 44:6).

v.35 — KJV: "fallen in decay with thee" / ESV: "cannot maintain himself
       with you" — Hebrew "u-matah yado" — his hand has slipped/failed.
       KJV's "fallen in decay" is archaic for economic decline. ESV
       modernizes. The image is of someone whose grip has failed — they
       can no longer hold on economically.

v.36 — KJV: "Take thou no usury of him, or increase" / ESV: "Take no
       interest from him or profit" — Hebrew "neshekh" (usury/interest —
       literally "a bite") and "tarbit/marbit" (increase/profit). KJV's
       "usury" originally meant any interest (not just excessive interest).
       ESV's "interest" and "profit" are clearer for modern readers.

v.39 — KJV: "thou shalt not compel him to serve as a bondservant" / ESV:
       "you shall not make him serve as a slave" — Hebrew "lo ta'avod bo
       avodat aved" — you shall not work him with the work of a slave.
       An Israelite who sells himself due to poverty must be treated as a
       hired worker, not a slave. His dignity as God's servant is preserved.

v.43 — KJV: "Thou shalt not rule over him with rigour" / ESV: "You shall
       not rule over him ruthlessly" — Hebrew "lo tirdeh vo b'farekh" —
       the same word "farekh" (harshness, ruthlessness) used of Egyptian
       slavery (Exodus 1:13-14). Israel must never replicate Egypt's
       oppression.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Sabbatical Year (Shemitah) — Every seventh year: the land rests, debts
               are released (Deuteronomy 15:1-2), and the law is read
               publicly (Deuteronomy 31:10-13). A test of faith in God's
               provision and a recognition that the land belongs to Him.

Jubilee (Yovel) — Every fiftieth year (after seven cycles of seven years).
               Announced by trumpet on the Day of Atonement. All land
               returns to original tribal allotments; all Israelite slaves
               go free; all debts are cancelled. A complete economic and
               social reset. Fulfilled in Christ who proclaims liberty to
               captives (Luke 4:18-19).

Kinsman-Redeemer (Go'el) — A near relative with the right and
               responsibility to: (1) buy back land a poor brother sold;
               (2) redeem a relative from slavery; (3) avenge blood
               (Numbers 35:19). Christ is our Go'el — our near kinsman
               (He became man) who redeems us from sin's slavery and
               restores our lost inheritance.

"The land is mine" — God's declaration of ultimate ownership (v.23). All
               human "ownership" is stewardship. This prevents both
               idolatry of possessions and exploitation of the poor.
               Everything belongs to God; we manage it for Him.

Usury (Neshekh) — Interest on a loan, literally "a bite." Forbidden when
               lending to a poor brother. The prohibition is against
               profiting from another's desperation. God's people are to
               be generous, not exploitative.

Liberty (Deror) — Freedom, release. The Jubilee proclamation. The same
               word used in Isaiah 61:1 — "to proclaim liberty to the
               captives" — which Jesus reads in Luke 4:18. True liberty
               comes through Christ's atonement.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "The land is mine" (v.23). Everything you have belongs to God. Your
   house, your savings, your career, your time — all of it is His,
   entrusted to you as a steward. This is not a burden but a freedom:
   if it all belongs to God, then losing it is not ultimate loss, and
   gaining it is not ultimate gain. Hold everything with open hands.

2. The Jubilee begins on the Day of Atonement (v.9). Liberty flows from
   atonement. You cannot have freedom without forgiveness first. The
   trumpet of Jubilee sounds on the day blood is shed for sin. In Christ:
   first the cross (atonement), then freedom (Jubilee). "If the Son
   therefore shall make you free, ye shall be free indeed" (John 8:36).

3. The kinsman-redeemer (v.25). When you could not redeem yourself, Christ
   — your near kinsman who took on flesh — paid the price to buy you back.
   He restored your lost inheritance. He freed you from slavery. He is
   your Go'el. "Ye are bought with a price" (1 Corinthians 6:20).

4. "What shall we eat?" (v.20). God anticipates the faith question. His
   answer: trust Me — I will provide supernaturally. The Sabbath year and
   Jubilee require faith that God's provision exceeds human calculation.
   Where is God asking you to trust Him in ways that seem economically
   irrational? "Seek ye first the kingdom of God... and all these things
   shall be added unto you" (Matthew 6:33).

5. "Thou shalt not rule over him with rigour" (v.43). The word "rigour"
   (farekh) is the same word used for Egyptian slavery. God's people must
   never replicate the oppression from which God delivered them. If you
   have authority over others — employees, family, those in need — do you
   exercise it with compassion or with harshness? Remember: you were once
   a slave, and God redeemed you with mercy.
