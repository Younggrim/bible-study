Leviticus - Chapter 14
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
God prescribes the cleansing ritual for healed lepers — involving blood, water, oil, and sacrifice — restoring them to community and worship.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses. Leviticus is the third book of the Pentateuch, written during
the wilderness period (approximately 1445-1405 BC). Hebrew title: "Vayikra"
— "And He called." God speaks from the newly completed Tabernacle, giving
Israel instructions for worship, sacrifice, and holy living.

Historical Context: Chapter 14 is the counterpart to chapter 13. Where
chapter 13 dealt with the DIAGNOSIS of leprosy and the declaration of
uncleanness, chapter 14 deals with the CLEANSING of the healed leper and
his restoration to the community. This is one of the most richly
typological chapters in Leviticus — the cleansing ritual is saturated with
pictures of Christ's redemptive work. The two-bird ceremony (vv.4-7)
parallels the two-goat ceremony of the Day of Atonement (chapter 16): one
dies, one goes free — death and resurrection, substitution and release. The
blood applied to the ear, thumb, and toe (v.14) mirrors the priestly
consecration of chapter 8 — the cleansed leper is restored to a status
parallel to a priest. The cedar wood, scarlet yarn, and hyssop (v.4) appear
together at the cross (the wooden cross, the scarlet blood, the hyssop that
lifted the sponge — John 19:29). The chapter also addresses "leprosy" in
houses (vv.33-53) — sin that infects not just persons but environments and
institutions. The entire chapter moves from exclusion to restoration, from
death to life, from outside the camp to full participation in worship. It
is the gospel in ceremonial form.

The Law of the Leper's Cleansing — The Priest Goes Out (vv.1-3): The LORD
speaks to Moses (v.1). "This shall be the law of the leper in the day of
his cleansing" (v.2). The leper is brought to the priest (v.2). Critical
detail: "the priest shall go forth OUT OF THE CAMP" (v.3). The priest goes
to where the leper is — outside the camp. The leper cannot come in; the
priest must go out. The priest examines: if the leprosy is healed (v.3).
Note: the healing has ALREADY occurred before the ceremony begins. The
ritual does not heal — it declares clean what God has already healed. The
priest going outside the camp = Christ leaving heaven's glory to come to
where sinners dwell (Hebrews 13:12; Philippians 2:5-8).

The Two-Bird Ceremony (vv.4-7): The priest commands: take two live clean
birds, cedar wood, scarlet, and hyssop (v.4). One bird is killed in an
earthen vessel over running (living) water (v.5). The living bird, cedar
wood, scarlet, and hyssop are dipped in the blood of the killed bird mixed
with the running water (v.6). The priest sprinkles the leper seven times
and pronounces him clean (v.7). The living bird is released into the open
field (v.7). The typology: TWO BIRDS — one dies, one lives. The killed bird
= Christ's death. The living bird = Christ's resurrection. The living bird
is dipped in the blood of the dead bird — resurrection is connected to
death; the risen Christ still bears the marks of crucifixion. The living
bird released into the open field = the ascension; freedom; life released
into the heavens. CEDAR WOOD — durable, incorruptible wood. The cross.
SCARLET — the color of blood, of royalty, of sin (Isaiah 1:18). HYSSOP —
the lowly plant used to apply blood (Exodus 12:22; Psalm 51:7; John 19:29).
EARTHEN VESSEL — humanity; the body of Christ (2 Corinthians 4:7). RUNNING
WATER — living water; the Holy Spirit (John 7:38-39). SEVEN TIMES — divine
completeness. The cleansing is total and perfect.

The Leper's Washing and Waiting (vv.8-9): The cleansed person washes
clothes, shaves ALL hair, bathes in water — he is clean (v.8). He may enter
the camp but must stay outside his tent seven days (v.8). On the seventh
day: shave ALL hair again — head, beard, eyebrows, everything (v.9). Wash
clothes, bathe — he is clean (v.9). The complete shaving = removal of
everything that grew during the period of disease. The old life is stripped
away entirely. A new beginning — like a newborn, hairless and fresh.

The Eighth-Day Offerings (vv.10-20): On the eighth day (new beginnings):
two male lambs without blemish, one ewe lamb (first year, without blemish),
three-tenths ephah of fine flour mixed with oil (grain offering), and one
log of oil (v.10). The priest presents the man and offerings before the LORD
at the Tabernacle entrance (v.11). The GUILT/TRESPASS offering — one male
lamb waved before the LORD with the oil (v.12). Killed in the holy place
where sin and burnt offerings are killed — it is most holy (v.13). BLOOD
APPLICATION: the priest puts blood of the guilt offering on the tip of the
RIGHT EAR, the thumb of the RIGHT HAND, and the great toe of the RIGHT
FOOT of the one being cleansed (v.14). This is IDENTICAL to the priestly
consecration (Leviticus 8:23-24). The cleansed leper receives the same
treatment as a newly consecrated priest. Ear = hearing consecrated to God.
Hand = work consecrated to God. Foot = walk consecrated to God. OIL
APPLICATION: oil poured into the priest's left hand (v.15). Right finger
dips in oil, sprinkles seven times before the LORD (v.16). Oil placed on
the right ear, right thumb, right great toe — ON TOP OF the blood (v.17).
Blood first, then oil. Atonement first, then the Spirit. The cross before
Pentecost. Remaining oil poured on the head (v.18) — anointing, like a
king or priest. Atonement made (v.18). SIN OFFERING offered — atonement for
uncleanness (v.19). BURNT OFFERING and GRAIN OFFERING offered (v.20).
Atonement complete — "he shall be clean" (v.20). The full range: guilt
offering (for specific trespass), sin offering (for the condition of
uncleanness), burnt offering (total consecration), grain offering
(dedication of daily life). Complete restoration.

Provision for the Poor (vv.21-32): If poor — one male lamb for guilt
offering (still required — the blood on ear/thumb/toe cannot be reduced),
one-tenth ephah flour with oil, one log of oil, and two turtledoves or
pigeons (one for sin offering, one for burnt offering) (vv.21-22). Brought
on the eighth day (v.23). Same procedure: wave offering (v.24), blood on
ear/thumb/toe (v.25), oil sprinkled seven times (v.27), oil on ear/thumb/
toe on top of blood (v.28), oil on head (v.29), birds offered (vv.30-31).
Atonement made (v.31). Summary: "This is the law of him in whom is the
plague of leprosy, whose hand is not able to get that which pertaineth to
his cleansing" (v.32). Poverty does not prevent restoration. The essential
elements (blood, oil, ear/thumb/toe) remain unchanged regardless of wealth.

Leprosy in Houses (vv.33-53): God speaks to Moses and Aaron (v.33). "When
ye be come into the land of Canaan" — this is future legislation for the
Promised Land (v.34). Remarkable: "I PUT the plague of leprosy in a house"
(v.34) — God claims sovereignty over this. The homeowner reports to the
priest (v.35). The house is emptied before examination — to prevent
everything inside from becoming unclean (v.36). The priest examines:
greenish or reddish spots deeper than the surface in the walls (v.37).
Quarantine seven days (v.38). Seventh day: if spread — remove affected
stones, cast them outside the city in an unclean place (vv.39-40). Scrape
the house inside, pour scrapings in an unclean place (v.41). Replace stones
and plaster (v.42). If it returns after repair — "fretting leprosy" /
"persistent leprous disease"; unclean (vv.43-44). Break down the entire
house — stones, timber, plaster — carry outside the city (v.45). Anyone
entering during quarantine — unclean until evening (v.46). Sleeping or
eating in it — wash clothes (v.47). If NOT spread after replastering —
pronounce clean; healed (v.48). CLEANSING CEREMONY for the house: same
two-bird ceremony as for the person — two birds, cedar wood, scarlet,
hyssop (v.49). One bird killed over running water in earthen vessel (v.50).
Dip cedar, hyssop, scarlet, and living bird in blood and water; sprinkle
house seven times (v.51). House cleansed (v.52). Living bird released
outside the city into open country (v.53). Atonement made for the house
(v.53). Sin infects environments, not just individuals. Institutions,
families, churches, and cultures can become "leprous" — and the same
remedy applies: blood, death, resurrection, and release.

Summary of All Leprosy Laws (vv.54-57): This is the law for all cases of
tsara'at: the itch/scall, leprosy in garments, leprosy in houses, swelling,
eruption, and bright spot (vv.54-56). Purpose: "To teach when it is unclean,
and when it is clean: this is the law of leprosy" (v.57). The entire
section (chapters 13-14) exists to teach discernment — when something is
diseased and when it is whole.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.3  — "And the priest shall go forth out of the camp." — The priest goes
        to where the leper is. Christ left glory to come to where sinners
        dwell. He went outside the camp (Hebrews 13:12).

v.7  — "And he shall sprinkle upon him that is to be cleansed from the
        leprosy seven times, and shall pronounce him clean, and shall let
        the living bird loose into the open field." — Seven = complete
        cleansing. The living bird released = resurrection and freedom.

v.14 — "And the priest shall put it upon the tip of the right ear of him
        that is to be cleansed, and upon the thumb of his right hand, and
        upon the great toe of his right foot." — Blood on ear, hand, foot.
        Hearing, working, walking — all consecrated. Same as priestly
        ordination (8:23-24). The cleansed sinner is made a priest.

v.17 — "Upon the blood of the trespass offering." — Oil ON TOP OF blood.
        The Spirit comes upon the foundation of atonement. No oil without
        blood first. No Pentecost without Calvary.

v.18 — "And the priest shall make an atonement for him before the LORD."
        — Full atonement. Complete restoration. The leper who was outside
        the camp is now standing before the LORD at the Tabernacle entrance.

v.34 — "I put the plague of leprosy in a house." — God's sovereignty even
        over disease and judgment. Nothing happens outside His will.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/leviticus-14/
  Guzik emphasizes the two-bird ceremony as a picture of Christ's death and
  resurrection. He notes that the priest going outside the camp pictures
  Christ coming to where sinners are. He highlights the blood on ear/thumb/
  toe as identical to priestly consecration — the cleansed leper is
  restored to priestly status. He connects cedar, scarlet, and hyssop to
  the cross.

Charles Spurgeon:
  Sermon: "The Cleansing of the Leper"
  "Two birds — one killed, one set free. Here is the gospel in a picture.
  One dies — that is Christ on the cross. One lives and flies away into the
  open field, dipped in the blood of the slain — that is Christ risen,
  ascending, bearing the marks of His death into the heavens. The living
  bird carries the blood upward and outward — so Christ carries His
  atoning blood into the presence of God and applies it to the farthest
  reaches of creation. And the leper? He is sprinkled seven times —
  perfectly, completely cleansed. Then blood on his ear — he hears
  differently now. Blood on his thumb — he works differently now. Blood on
  his toe — he walks differently now. And oil on top of the blood — the
  Spirit comes where the blood has been. No blood, no oil. No cross, no
  Pentecost. No atonement, no anointing. But where the blood is applied,
  the oil follows. Always."

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.2  — Hebrew "torat ha-m'tsora b'yom tahorato" — the law of the leprous
       one in the day of his purification.
       KJV: "the law of the leper in the day of his cleansing"
       ESV: "the law of the leprous person for the day of his cleansing"
       ASV: "the law of the leper in the day of his cleansing"
       NET: "the law of the diseased person on the day of his cleansing"
       WEB: "the law of the leper in the day of his cleansing"
       Analysis: NET consistently avoids "leper/leprosy" (since "tsara'at"
       is not modern leprosy) and uses "diseased person" — removing stigma
       while retaining ceremonial reality. "Tahorato" = his purification/
       cleansing. "The day of his cleansing" — a specific, appointed day.

v.4  — Hebrew "sh'tei tsipporim chayyot t'horot, v'etz erez, u-sh'ni
       tola'at, v'ezov" — two live clean birds, cedar wood, scarlet of the
       worm, and hyssop.
       KJV: "two birds alive and clean, and cedar wood, and scarlet, and
       hyssop"
       ESV: "two live clean birds and cedarwood and scarlet yarn and hyssop"
       ASV: "two living clean birds, and cedar wood, and scarlet, and
       hyssop"
       NET: "two live clean birds, a piece of cedar wood, a scrap of
       crimson fabric, and some twigs of hyssop"
       WEB: "two living clean birds, cedar wood, scarlet, and hyssop"
       Analysis: NET specifies "crimson fabric" for Hebrew "sheni tola'at"
       (literally "scarlet of the worm" — a dye from crushed insects). ESV
       adds "yarn" for clarity. The scarlet, cedar, and hyssop together
       picture Christ's blood, cross, and humility.

v.5  — Hebrew "al mayim chayyim" — over living water. Hebrew "el k'li
       cheres" — in an earthen vessel.
       KJV: "over running water... in an earthen vessel"
       ESV: "over fresh water... in an earthenware vessel"
       ASV: "over running water... in an earthen vessel"
       NET: "over fresh water... in a clay vessel"
       WEB: "over running water... in an earthen vessel"
       Analysis: "Chayyim" = living, alive. KJV/ASV/WEB's "running" captures
       the flowing aspect. ESV/NET's "fresh" is less evocative. The Hebrew
       is "living water" — the same phrase Jesus uses in John 4:10 and 7:38.
       The bird is killed over living water — death over life. "Cheres" =
       clay, earthenware — Christ's body was an "earthen vessel" (2 Cor
       4:7).

v.7  — Hebrew "penei hasadeh" — face of the field.
       KJV: "into the open field"
       ESV: "into the open field"
       ASV: "into the open field"
       NET: "over the open countryside"
       WEB: "into the open field"
       Analysis: NET's "open countryside" is more descriptive. The released
       bird flies free — a picture of sin carried away and resurrection
       freedom (cf. the scapegoat in ch.16).

v.10 — Hebrew "sh'nei k'vasim" — two male lambs.
       KJV: "two he lambs"
       ESV: "two male lambs"
       ASV: "two he-lambs"
       NET: "two flawless male lambs"
       WEB: "two male lambs"
       Analysis: KJV/ASV's "he lambs" is archaic for male lambs. NET adds
       "flawless" (Hebrew "tamim") directly into the text for clarity.

v.10 — Hebrew "sh'loshah esronim solet l'minchah" — three-tenths of fine
       flour for a grain offering.
       KJV: "three tenth deals of fine flour for a meat offering"
       ESV: "a grain offering of three tenths of an ephah of fine flour"
       ASV: "three tenth parts of an ephah of fine flour for a meal-offering"
       NET: "three-tenths of an ephah of choice wheat flour"
       WEB: "three tenth parts of an ephah of fine flour for a meal offering"
       Analysis: KJV's "meat offering" = grain offering (archaic). "Tenth
       deals" = tenth parts of an ephah. ESV specifies the unit. NET uses
       "choice wheat flour" for Hebrew "soleth."

v.12 — Hebrew "asham" — guilt/trespass offering.
       KJV: "a trespass offering"
       ESV: "a guilt offering"
       ASV: "a guilt-offering"
       NET: "a guilt offering"
       WEB: "a trespass offering"
       Analysis: KJV/WEB's "trespass" and ESV/ASV/NET's "guilt" both
       capture aspects of the Hebrew. The leper's disease was a kind of
       "debt" to God that must be repaid.

v.14 — Hebrew "t'nukh ozen y'manit" — the tip/lobe of the right ear.
       KJV: "the tip of the right ear... the thumb of his right hand...
       the great toe of his right foot"
       ESV: "the lobe of the right ear... the thumb of his right hand...
       the big toe of his right foot"
       ASV: "the tip of the right ear... the thumb of his right hand...
       the great toe of his right foot"
       NET: "the lobe of the right ear... right hand thumb... right foot
       big toe"
       WEB: "the tip of the right ear... the thumb of his right hand...
       the great toe of his right foot"
       Analysis: NET/ESV use anatomical "lobe" and "big toe" where ASV/KJV/
       WEB use "tip" and "great toe." The same three-point application as
       priestly consecration (ch.8) — the cleansed leper is re-consecrated
       like a priest. Blood on ear (hearing), thumb (work), toe (walk).

v.34 — Hebrew "v'natatti nega tsara'at b'veit" — I give/put/place a plague
       of skin disease in a house.
       KJV: "I put the plague of leprosy in a house"
       ESV: "I put a case of leprous disease in a house"
       ASV: "I put the plague of leprosy in a house"
       NET: "I assign an infection in a house"
       WEB: "I put a plague of leprosy in a house"
       Analysis: God claims direct agency. "Natatti" = I give/put/place.
       NET uses "assign" to emphasize divine sovereignty. NET also renders
       "the land that I am about to give you for a possession" — emphasizing
       the promise as imminent. The land laws apply to future possession.

v.44 — Hebrew "tsara'at mam'eret" — malignant/persistent leprosy.
       KJV: "a fretting leprosy"
       ESV: "a persistent leprous disease"
       ASV: "a fretting leprosy"
       NET: "a malignant disease"
       WEB: "a spreading plague"
       Analysis: Malignant, corroding, actively destructive. The disease
       that returns after treatment is the most dangerous — it requires
       total destruction of the house.

v.51 — The house cleansing ceremony mirrors the personal ceremony (vv.6-7).
       KJV: "sprinkle the house seven times"
       ESV: "sprinkle the house seven times"
       ASV: "sprinkle the house seven times"
       NET: "sprinkle the house seven times"
       WEB: "sprinkle the house seven times"
       Analysis: All identical. Blood cleanses environments as well as
       individuals. Seven times = complete, perfect cleansing.

v.57 — Hebrew "l'horot b'yom ha-tamei u-v'yom ha-tahor" — to teach/
       instruct in the day of unclean and the day of clean.
       KJV: "to teach when it is unclean, and when it is clean"
       ESV: "to show when it is unclean and when it is clean"
       ASV: "to teach when it is unclean, and when it is clean"
       NET: "to teach when something is unclean and when it is clean"
       WEB: "to teach when it is unclean, and when it is clean"
       Analysis: "L'horot" = to teach, instruct, show (same root as "Torah"
       = instruction). The entire leprosy legislation is TORAH —
       instruction. Its purpose is pedagogical: to teach discernment between
       disease and health, sin and holiness, death and life.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Two birds — The central symbol of the cleansing ceremony. One killed (death/
               substitution), one released alive (resurrection/freedom).
               Parallels the two goats of the Day of Atonement (Leviticus
               16). Together they picture the complete work of Christ:
               death for sin AND resurrection to life. The living bird
               carries the blood of the dead bird into the open field —
               the risen Christ carries His atoning work into heaven.

Cedar wood (etz erez) — A durable, aromatic, rot-resistant wood. Symbolizes
               strength, permanence, and incorruptibility. At the cross:
               the wooden beam. In the cleansing: that which does not decay
               — the eternal nature of Christ's work.

Scarlet (sh'ni tola'at) — Literally "scarlet of the worm." A crimson dye
               produced from the crushed body of the tola'at worm (coccus
               ilicis). Psalm 22:6 — "I am a worm (tola'at), and no man."
               The color of blood, of sin (Isaiah 1:18), and of royalty.
               Christ was robed in scarlet (Matthew 27:28) before
               crucifixion.

Hyssop (ezov) — A small, common, bushy plant (likely marjoram or similar).
               Used to apply blood at Passover (Exodus 12:22). David's
               prayer: "Purge me with hyssop, and I shall be clean" (Psalm
               51:7). At the cross: vinegar lifted to Jesus on hyssop (John
               19:29). Represents the humble means by which God's cleansing
               is applied — not grand or impressive, but effective.

Earthen vessel — A common clay pot. Fragile, ordinary, breakable. The bird
               is killed in it — Christ died in a human body, an "earthen
               vessel" (2 Corinthians 4:7). The treasure of divine life
               contained in common clay.

Running/Living water (mayim chayyim) — Water from a flowing source (spring
               or stream), not stagnant. "Chayyim" = living. Jesus: "If any
               man thirst, let him come unto me, and drink... rivers of
               living water" (John 7:37-38). The Holy Spirit. The blood
               mingles with living water — atonement and the Spirit work
               together.

Blood on ear/thumb/toe — The application of atoning blood to the organs of
               hearing (ear), working (hand), and walking (foot). The whole
               person is consecrated through blood. Identical to priestly
               ordination (Leviticus 8:23-24) — the cleansed sinner is
               made a priest. Every believer is a priest (1 Peter 2:9).

Oil on top of blood — Oil (representing the Holy Spirit) applied to the
               same places where blood was applied — but ON TOP of the
               blood. The Spirit's work rests on the foundation of
               atonement. No anointing without atonement. No Pentecost
               without Calvary. The order is fixed: blood first, oil second.

Eighth day — The day of the leper's full restoration through sacrifice.
               Eight = new beginnings (circumcision on day 8, resurrection
               on the first day of the new week). The leper begins a
               completely new life.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "The priest shall go forth out of the camp" (v.3). The leper cannot come
   to God — God comes to the leper. Christ did not wait for sinners to
   clean themselves up and come to Him. He went OUTSIDE THE CAMP — to where
   the unclean dwell. He came to you in your uncleanness. Have you
   understood the initiative of grace? You did not find God; He came
   looking for you.

2. Two birds — one dies, one flies free (vv.4-7). This is the gospel: death
   AND resurrection. Christ died for your sin AND rose for your
   justification (Romans 4:25). The living bird carries the blood into the
   open field — your freedom is purchased by His death and guaranteed by
   His life. Are you living in the freedom the living bird represents? Or
   are you still acting like someone trapped in the camp of the unclean?

3. Blood on ear, thumb, and toe (v.14). Your hearing, your work, and your
   walk all belong to God through the blood of Christ. What are you
   listening to? What are your hands doing? Where are your feet going? The
   blood claims all three.

4. Oil ON TOP OF blood (v.17). The Spirit's work always rests on the
   foundation of Christ's atonement. You cannot have the Spirit's power
   without the cross. You cannot have Pentecost without Calvary. Are you
   seeking spiritual experience apart from the gospel? The oil only goes
   where the blood has already been.

5. The cleansed leper receives the SAME treatment as a consecrated priest
   (v.14; cf. 8:23-24). In Christ, you are not merely forgiven — you are
   made a priest. "Ye are a royal priesthood" (1 Peter 2:9). Your
   restoration is not to second-class citizenship but to priestly service.
   The leper who was outside the camp now stands at the Tabernacle entrance
   before the LORD. That is your position in Christ.
