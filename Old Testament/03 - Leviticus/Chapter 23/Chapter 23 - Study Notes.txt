Leviticus - Chapter 23
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
God establishes Israel's sacred calendar — Sabbath, Passover, Firstfruits, Pentecost, Trumpets, Day of Atonement, and Tabernacles — each pointing to Christ.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses. Leviticus is the third book of the Pentateuch, written during
the wilderness period (approximately 1445-1405 BC). Hebrew title: "Vayikra"
— "And He called." God speaks from the newly completed Tabernacle, giving
Israel instructions for worship, sacrifice, and holy living.

Historical Context: Chapter 23 is one of the most prophetically significant
chapters in the entire Old Testament. It presents the seven "feasts of the
LORD" (Hebrew: "mo'adei YHWH" — appointed times/seasons of the LORD) — not
Israel's feasts, but GOD'S feasts. These are divine appointments on God's
prophetic calendar, each pointing to a specific aspect of Christ's
redemptive work. The chapter is structured around two groups of feasts
separated by a long gap: the Spring Feasts (Passover, Unleavened Bread,
Firstfruits, Pentecost/Weeks) — all fulfilled in Christ's first coming;
and the Fall Feasts (Trumpets, Day of Atonement, Tabernacles) — to be
fulfilled at Christ's second coming. The gap between the spring and fall
feasts corresponds to the Church Age — the present era between Christ's
first and second advents. The feasts are called "holy convocations"
(miqra'ei qodesh) — sacred assemblies, rehearsals, or dress rehearsals
of coming events. Israel rehearsed these feasts annually; Christ fulfilled
them precisely on their calendar dates. This chapter is God's prophetic
timeline written in festival form.

The Sabbath — Foundation of All Feasts (v.3): Before listing the seven
feasts, God establishes the Sabbath as the foundational principle. Six
days of work, seventh day of rest — "a sabbath of rest, an holy
convocation" (KJV) / "a Sabbath of solemn rest, a holy convocation" (ESV).
No work. The Sabbath principle underlies all the feasts: God's work leads
to God's rest. Ultimate fulfillment: Christ is our Sabbath rest (Hebrews
4:9-10) — we cease from our own works and rest in His finished work.

FEAST 1: PASSOVER — 14th of Nisan (vv.4-5): "The LORD's passover" (v.5).
The fourteenth day of the first month, at evening. Commemorates the
Exodus — the lamb slain, blood on the doorposts, the angel of death
passing over (Exodus 12). Prophetic fulfillment: Christ our Passover
Lamb, sacrificed for us (1 Corinthians 5:7). Jesus was crucified on
Passover — the exact day, at the exact time the lambs were being slain
in the temple. "Behold the Lamb of God, which taketh away the sin of
the world" (John 1:29).

FEAST 2: UNLEAVENED BREAD — 15th-21st of Nisan (vv.6-8): Begins the day
after Passover, lasts seven days. Eat unleavened bread — no leaven
(symbol of sin) for seven days. First and seventh days are holy
convocations with no servile work. Prophetic fulfillment: Christ's
sinless life and burial. Leaven represents sin (1 Corinthians 5:6-8);
unleavened bread represents sinlessness. Christ's body in the grave did
not see corruption (Psalm 16:10; Acts 2:27). Believers are called to
"purge out the old leaven" and keep the feast "with the unleavened bread
of sincerity and truth" (1 Corinthians 5:7-8).

FEAST 3: FIRSTFRUITS — The Day After the Sabbath (vv.9-14): When Israel
enters the land and reaps the harvest, they bring a sheaf of the
firstfruits to the priest (v.10). The priest waves it before the LORD
"on the morrow after the sabbath" (v.11) — the day after the Sabbath
during Unleavened Bread. A burnt offering of a male lamb without blemish
accompanies it (v.12), plus grain and drink offerings (v.13). They may
not eat of the new harvest until this offering is made (v.14). Prophetic
fulfillment: Christ's RESURRECTION. "But now is Christ risen from the
dead, and become the firstfruits of them that slept" (1 Corinthians
15:20). Christ rose on the day of Firstfruits — the day after the
Sabbath (Sunday) during Passover week. He is the firstfruits; we who
believe will follow in resurrection.

FEAST 4: PENTECOST / FEAST OF WEEKS — 50 Days After Firstfruits (vv.15-22):
Count seven complete weeks (49 days) from Firstfruits; on the fiftieth
day, present a new grain offering (vv.15-16). Unique features: TWO loaves
baked WITH LEAVEN (v.17) — the only offering with leaven. Seven lambs,
one bull, two rams for burnt offering; one goat for sin offering; two
lambs for peace offering (vv.18-19). Holy convocation, no servile work
(v.21). Gleaning provision for the poor inserted here (v.22). Prophetic
fulfillment: The coming of the Holy Spirit at Pentecost (Acts 2). Exactly
50 days after Christ's resurrection (Firstfruits), the Spirit descended.
The TWO leavened loaves represent Jew and Gentile — both with sin still
present — united in one body by the Spirit. A sin offering accompanies
because the Church, though redeemed, still contains sin. The harvest
imagery: the Church is God's harvest from the earth.

THE GAP — Between Spring and Fall Feasts: Between Pentecost (late spring)
and Trumpets (early fall) there is a long silence — no feasts. This
corresponds to the Church Age, the present era. Israel's prophetic clock
paused; God is gathering a people from all nations. When the fall feasts
begin, Israel's program resumes.

FEAST 5: TRUMPETS (Rosh Hashanah) — 1st of Tishri (vv.23-25): The seventh
month, first day — "a memorial of blowing of trumpets" (KJV) / "a
memorial proclaimed with blast of trumpets" (ESV) (v.24). A sabbath rest,
holy convocation, no servile work, fire offering to the LORD (v.25).
Prophetic fulfillment: The rapture/regathering of Israel. "The trumpet
shall sound, and the dead shall be raised" (1 Corinthians 15:52). "The
Lord himself shall descend from heaven with a shout, with the voice of
the archangel, and with the trump of God" (1 Thessalonians 4:16). Trumpets
signal gathering, warning, and the announcement of the King.

FEAST 6: DAY OF ATONEMENT (Yom Kippur) — 10th of Tishri (vv.26-32):
The tenth day of the seventh month — "ye shall afflict your souls" (v.27).
Holy convocation, fire offering. Absolutely NO work — anyone who works is
destroyed; anyone who does not afflict himself is cut off (vv.28-30). A
"sabbath of rest" from the ninth day evening to the tenth day evening
(v.32). Prophetic fulfillment: Israel's national repentance and salvation.
"They shall look upon me whom they have pierced, and they shall mourn"
(Zechariah 12:10). When Christ returns, Israel will recognize their
Messiah and be saved in a day (Isaiah 66:8; Romans 11:26). The affliction
of soul represents national mourning and repentance. (See Leviticus 16
for the full Day of Atonement ritual.)

FEAST 7: TABERNACLES / BOOTHS (Sukkot) — 15th-22nd of Tishri (vv.33-43):
The fifteenth day of the seventh month, seven days. First day: holy
convocation, no servile work (v.35). Seven days of fire offerings; eighth
day: solemn assembly, holy convocation (v.36). Israel dwells in booths
(sukkot) for seven days (v.42) — remembering the wilderness wandering
(v.43). They take branches of palm, willow, and leafy trees and rejoice
before the LORD seven days (v.40). Prophetic fulfillment: The Millennial
Kingdom — Christ dwelling (tabernacling) with His people. "The Word was
made flesh, and dwelt [tabernacled] among us" (John 1:14). Zechariah
14:16-19 prophesies that all nations will celebrate Tabernacles during
the Millennium. The eighth day points to the eternal state — the new
heaven and new earth beyond the Millennium.

Summary Statement (vv.37-38, 44): These are the appointed feasts of the
LORD — holy convocations for presenting offerings (v.37). They are IN
ADDITION TO regular Sabbaths, gifts, vows, and freewill offerings (v.38).
Moses declared these feasts to Israel (v.44).

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "These are my feasts." — Not Israel's feasts — GOD'S appointed
        times. He sets the calendar; He determines the fulfillment.

v.3  — "The seventh day is the sabbath of rest, an holy convocation; ye
        shall do no work therein: it is the sabbath of the LORD." — The
        Sabbath principle underlies all feasts: God's work, God's rest.

v.5  — "The LORD's passover." — The foundational feast. Everything begins
        with the blood of the lamb.

v.11 — "On the morrow after the sabbath the priest shall wave it." —
        Firstfruits on the day after the Sabbath — resurrection day.

v.17 — "They shall be baken with leaven; they are the firstfruits unto
        the LORD." — Two leavened loaves at Pentecost: Jew and Gentile,
        still with sin, united in one offering.

v.24 — "A memorial of blowing of trumpets, an holy convocation." —
        Trumpets announce the King's coming and the gathering of His people.

v.27 — "On the tenth day of this seventh month there shall be a day of
        atonement... ye shall afflict your souls." — National repentance
        and atonement.

v.42 — "You shall dwell in booths for seven days." (ESV) — Remembering
        dependence on God; pointing to God dwelling with man.

v.43 — "That your generations may know that I made the children of Israel
        to dwell in booths, when I brought them out of the land of Egypt."
        — Memory and hope: what God did, what God will do.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/leviticus-23/
  Guzik provides an excellent overview of the prophetic significance of
  each feast, noting that the spring feasts were fulfilled precisely on
  their calendar dates at Christ's first coming, giving confidence that
  the fall feasts will be fulfilled with equal precision at His return.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "These feasts are God's great object lessons written on the calendar of
  Israel. Passover — the blood that saves. Unleavened Bread — the life
  that is purged of sin. Firstfruits — the resurrection that guarantees
  ours. Pentecost — the Spirit poured out upon the Church. And then
  silence — a long summer of waiting. But the trumpets will sound again.
  The great Day of Atonement for the nation will come. And then — oh,
  then! — the great feast of Tabernacles, when God shall dwell with His
  people forever. Every feast is a prophecy. Every prophecy points to
  Christ."

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.2  — Hebrew "mo'adei YHWH" — appointed times/seasons of the LORD.
       KJV: "the feasts of the LORD"
       ESV: "the appointed feasts of the LORD"
       ASV: "The set feasts of Jehovah"
       NET: "the Lord's appointed times which you must proclaim as holy assemblies"
       WEB: "The feasts of Yahweh"
       Analysis: From "mo'ed" (appointed time, meeting, season). These are not merely celebrations but divinely appointed times — scheduled meetings between God and His people. ESV's "appointed feasts" and NET's "appointed times" capture this better than KJV's simple "feasts." The possessive "my" emphasizes these are GOD'S feasts, not Israel's festivals.

v.2  — Hebrew "miqra'ei qodesh" — holy convocations/rehearsals.
       KJV: "holy convocations"
       ESV: "holy convocations"
       Analysis: From "qara" (to call, proclaim, read). A "miqra" is a calling together, a rehearsal, a proclamation. The feasts are sacred assemblies AND prophetic rehearsals.

v.3  — Hebrew "shabbat shabbaton" — an intensive form of sabbath.
       KJV: "the sabbath of rest"
       ESV: "a Sabbath of solemn rest"
       ASV: "a sabbath of solemn rest"
       NET: "a Sabbath of complete rest"
       WEB: "a Sabbath of solemn rest"
       Analysis: An intensive form: a sabbath of complete cessation. ESV/ASV/WEB's "solemn rest" captures the intensification. NET's "complete rest" captures the totality.

v.5  — Hebrew "bein ha-arbayim" — between the evenings.
       KJV: "at even"
       ESV: "at twilight"
       ASV: "Jehovah's passover"
       NET: "a Passover offering to the Lord"
       WEB: "Yahweh's Passover"
       Analysis: Debated: either between noon and sunset, or between sunset and dark. The Passover lamb was slain in the late afternoon. NET adds "offering" to clarify "pesach" refers both to the festival and the lamb sacrificed. The divine name attached to Passover marks it as God's own saving act.

v.7  — Hebrew "melekhet avodah" — work of labor/service.
       KJV: "no servile work"
       ESV: "no ordinary work"
       Analysis: Distinguished from "all work" (kol melakhah) used for Sabbath and Day of Atonement. On feast days, food preparation was permitted; on Sabbath/Atonement, NO work at all. KJV's "servile" is archaic; ESV's "ordinary" is clearer.

v.11 — Hebrew "mi-macharat ha-shabbat" — on the morrow after the sabbath.
       KJV: "on the morrow after the sabbath"
       ESV: "On the day after the Sabbath"
       Analysis: This phrase generated significant debate: which Sabbath? The Pharisees said the first day of Unleavened Bread (Nisan 16 always); the Sadducees said the weekly Sabbath during the feast (always a Sunday). Christ's resurrection on Sunday confirms the Sunday interpretation.

v.13 — Hebrew "minchah" — grain/food offering.
       KJV: "the meat offering"
       ESV: "the grain offering"
       Analysis: KJV's "meat" is archaic English for "food" (not flesh). ESV correctly renders "grain offering."

v.15 — Hebrew "sheva shabbatot temimot" — seven complete sabbaths/weeks.
       KJV: "seven sabbaths shall be complete"
       ESV: "seven full weeks"
       Analysis: "Shabbat" can mean either "sabbath" or "week." The count of 49 days (7x7) leads to the 50th day — Pentecost (Greek: "fiftieth").

v.17 — Hebrew "chametz te'afenah" — baked with leaven.
       KJV: "baken with leaven"
       ESV: "baked with leaven"
       Analysis: Remarkable: leaven is normally forbidden in offerings (2:11). These two loaves are the exception — because they represent the Church (Jew and Gentile), which still contains sin. A sin offering accompanies (v.19) for this reason.

v.24 — Hebrew "zikron teru'ah" — a memorial of shouting/trumpet blast.
       KJV: "a memorial of blowing of trumpets"
       ESV: "a memorial proclaimed with blast of trumpets"
       ASV: "a memorial of blowing of trumpets, a holy convocation"
       NET: "a memorial of trumpet blasting, a holy assembly"
       WEB: "a memorial of blowing of trumpets, a holy convocation"
       Analysis: "Teru'ah" can mean a shout, an alarm, or a trumpet blast. The specific purpose is not stated — it is a memorial, but of what? The mystery adds to its prophetic significance. NET's "trumpet blasting" specifies the sound.

v.27 — Hebrew "yom ha-kippurim" — day of coverings/atonements (plural).
       KJV: "a day of atonement"
       ESV: "the Day of Atonement"
       ASV: "the day of atonement"
       NET: "the Day of Atonement"
       WEB: "the Day of Atonement"
       Analysis: All translations render it identically. The most solemn day in Israel's calendar — the tenth day of the seventh month.

v.34 — Hebrew "chag ha-sukkot" — the festival of booths/shelters.
       KJV: "the feast of tabernacles"
       ESV: "the Feast of Booths"
       Analysis: "Sukkah" is a temporary shelter made of branches. KJV's "tabernacles" (from Latin "tabernaculum" — tent) and ESV's "booths" both translate correctly. The temporary nature is the point: life on earth is temporary; God's dwelling with us will be permanent.

v.40 — Hebrew four types of vegetation for Sukkot.
       KJV: "boughs of goodly trees, branches of palm trees, and the boughs of thick trees, and willows of the brook"
       ESV: "the fruit of splendid trees, branches of palm trees and boughs of leafy trees and willows of the brook"
       ASV: "ye shall take you on the first day the fruit of goodly trees, branches of palm-trees"
       NET: "you must take for yourselves branches from luxuriant trees"
       Analysis: Jewish tradition identifies these as citron (etrog), palm (lulav), myrtle (hadas), and willow (aravah) — the "four species" still used in Sukkot celebrations today. NET uses "luxuriant trees" where ASV/KJV use "goodly trees" (Hebrew "etz hadar" — trees of splendor/glory).

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Mo'ed (Appointed Time) — Hebrew for "appointed time, meeting, season."
               The feasts are God's scheduled appointments — fixed times
               when He meets with His people and when prophetic events
               occur. The Tabernacle itself is called "ohel mo'ed" — tent
               of meeting/appointed time.

Miqra (Holy Convocation) — A sacred assembly, a calling together. Also
               carries the sense of "rehearsal" — Israel rehearsed these
               events annually until Christ fulfilled them in reality.

Passover (Pesach) — 14th of Nisan. The lamb slain, blood applied, death
               passes over. Fulfilled: Christ crucified on Passover.

Unleavened Bread (Matzot) — 15th-21st of Nisan. Seven days without leaven.
               Fulfilled: Christ's sinless body in the grave; believers
               purging sin from their lives.

Firstfruits (Bikkurim) — Day after the Sabbath during Unleavened Bread.
               The first sheaf of harvest waved before God. Fulfilled:
               Christ's resurrection — "the firstfruits of them that slept."

Pentecost / Weeks (Shavuot) — 50 days after Firstfruits. Two leavened
               loaves. Fulfilled: The Holy Spirit poured out (Acts 2).

Trumpets (Yom Teru'ah / Rosh Hashanah) — 1st of Tishri. Trumpet blasts.
               Prophetic: The rapture/regathering; announcement of the King.

Day of Atonement (Yom Kippur) — 10th of Tishri. Affliction of soul, no
               work, complete atonement. Prophetic: Israel's national
               repentance when they see their pierced Messiah.

Tabernacles / Booths (Sukkot) — 15th-22nd of Tishri. Dwelling in
               temporary shelters, rejoicing. Prophetic: The Millennial
               Kingdom — God dwelling with man. The eighth day: eternity.

Leaven (chametz) — Yeast/fermentation. Consistently represents sin,
               corruption, and false doctrine in Scripture (Matthew 16:6;
               1 Corinthians 5:6-8). Its presence in the Pentecost loaves
               is theologically significant.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "These are MY feasts" (v.2). God sets the calendar. He determines the
   times and seasons. The feasts are not human inventions but divine
   appointments. Christ fulfilled the spring feasts on their EXACT dates
   — Passover (crucifixion), Unleavened Bread (burial), Firstfruits
   (resurrection), Pentecost (Spirit). This gives absolute confidence
   that the fall feasts will be fulfilled with equal precision. God is
   never early, never late.

2. The prophetic gap between spring and fall feasts. We live in the gap —
   between Pentecost and Trumpets. The Church Age was a mystery hidden in
   the Old Testament (Ephesians 3:4-6). But the trumpets WILL sound. The
   next feast on God's calendar is coming. Are you ready?

3. Firstfruits and resurrection (vv.10-11). Christ rose on the day of
   Firstfruits — and His resurrection GUARANTEES ours. "If the firstfruits
   be holy, the lump is also holy" (Romans 11:16). Because He rose, we
   will rise. The sheaf waved before God is the pledge of the full harvest
   to come.

4. Two leavened loaves (v.17). The Church is not yet perfect. We are
   redeemed but still contain sin (leaven). Yet God accepts us — with a
   sin offering (v.19). We are accepted not because we are sinless but
   because Christ's sacrifice covers our remaining sin. Grace does not
   wait for perfection.

5. "Ye shall dwell in booths" (v.42). Life is temporary. We are pilgrims
   and strangers (1 Peter 2:11). The booth reminds us: this world is not
   our home. But Tabernacles is also the feast of JOY — because the
   temporary leads to the permanent. God will dwell with us forever
   (Revelation 21:3).
