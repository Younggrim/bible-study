Nahum - Chapter 3
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Woe to Nineveh, the city of blood and endless plunder — her wound is incurable, and all who hear of her fall clap their hands, for who has not felt her unceasing cruelty?

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Nahum the Elkoshite. Prophesied approximately 663-612 BC.
Key theme: God's judgment on Nineveh/Assyria — the city that
repented under Jonah has returned to wickedness. God is both
'slow to anger' and one who 'will not at all acquit the wicked'
(1:3).

Classification: Prophetic Oracle — The Reasons and Finality of
Nineveh's Doom
Key Themes: The bloody city's sins exposed, divine justice as
public shaming, the precedent of Thebes' fall, the incurable
wound, universal rejoicing at Nineveh's destruction

Historical Context: Chapter 3 provides the indictment — the
charges against Nineveh that justify her sentence. After the
theological declaration (ch.1) and the battle description (ch.2),
Nahum now presents the REASONS for judgment: violence ("bloody
city"), deception ("full of lies"), rapacious greed ("robbery"),
and corrupting influence over nations ("witchcrafts/charms").

Nahum uses Thebes (No-Amon) as a precedent: this great Egyptian
city fell in 663 BC despite its strong defenses, allies, and
strategic position. If Thebes could fall, Nineveh is not immune.
The argument from history is devastating — Assyria itself
conquered Thebes under Ashurbanipal. Now Nineveh will receive
what she gave.

The chapter ends with one of the most complete pictures of divine
judgment in Scripture: Nineveh's wound is "incurable," her leaders
are useless, her people scattered, and the entire world CLAPS at
her downfall. The final question — "upon whom hath not thy
wickedness passed continually?" — explains why no one mourns.
Nineveh has victimized everyone; everyone celebrates her end.

Structure:
- The Indictment: Woe to the Bloody City (vv.1-4)
- The Sentence: Divine Shaming and Spectacle (vv.5-7)
- The Precedent: The Fall of Thebes (vv.8-10)
- The Application: Nineveh Will Share Thebes' Fate (vv.11-13)
- The Mockery: Futile Preparations (vv.14-17)
- The Epitaph: An Incurable Wound (vv.18-19)

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------

REFLECTION
------------------------------------
1. Sins have a cumulative weight (v.1). Nineveh's judgment came
   not for one act but for generations of unceasing cruelty —
   "the prey departeth not." Habitual sin accumulates a debt
   that will eventually come due. Repent before the account is
   full.

2. No one is exempt from God's justice (v.8). "Are you better
   than Thebes?" — Nineveh thought its power made it immune.
   No nation, institution, or person is above God's reach.
   Past success does not guarantee future immunity.

3. A life of cruelty produces complete isolation (v.7). When
   Nineveh falls, no one mourns. A life lived exploiting
   others builds no genuine relationships. On the day of your
   need, will anyone grieve for you — or will they clap?

4. Leaders who fail their people ensure destruction (v.18).
   "Thy shepherds slumber, O king of Assyria." When leaders
   sleep — spiritually, morally, strategically — the people
   scatter. Leadership carries eternal weight. Negligent
   shepherds will answer for scattered sheep.

5. Some wounds are incurable by human means (v.19). Nineveh's
   wound has "no healing." When God pronounces judgment final,
   no human remedy exists. But what is incurable for nations
   under judgment is curable for individuals who repent — the
   Cross heals what nothing else can (Isaiah 53:5).
