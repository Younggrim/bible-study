1 Kings - Chapter 14
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Jeroboam's son falls ill; Ahijah prophesies the total destruction of Jeroboam's house because of his idolatry. Rehoboam reigns in Judah with apostasy and shame—Shishak of Egypt plunders the temple treasures.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Unknown, traditionally attributed to Jeremiah or a prophetic
school. Originally one book with 2 Kings in the Hebrew Bible. Covers
approximately 120 years (970-850 BC) — from David's death through
Ahaziah's reign. Key themes: the glory and failure of Solomon, the
division of the kingdom as judgment for idolatry, the faithfulness of
God's prophets (especially Elijah), and the principle that a nation's
spiritual health depends on its leaders' faithfulness to God's covenant.

Historical Context: Chapter 14 is the chapter of consequences. The
kingdom has been divided (ch.12), Jeroboam has established his
counterfeit worship system with golden calves at Dan and Bethel
(ch.12-13), and now the bill comes due. The chapter divides into
two parallel narratives of judgment: Jeroboam's house in Israel
(vv.1-20) and Rehoboam's reign in Judah (vv.21-31). Both kings
fail — one through deliberate innovation of false worship, the
other through passive tolerance of pagan practices. The chapter
demonstrates that the division of the kingdom did not produce one
faithful nation and one unfaithful one — both are under judgment.
Jeroboam's sick son Abijah becomes the occasion for God's
devastating verdict on Jeroboam's dynasty. The prophet Ahijah —
the same man who promised Jeroboam the kingdom (11:29-39) — now
pronounces its destruction. Meanwhile in Judah, Rehoboam presides
over a nation that sinks into Canaanite abominations, and Pharaoh
Shishak's invasion strips away the golden glory of Solomon's era.
The gold shields become bronze — a perfect symbol of the entire
chapter: what was glorious has become cheap imitation.

Jeroboam's Son Falls Ill (vv.1-4): Jeroboam's son Abijah falls
sick, and Jeroboam sends his wife in disguise to the prophet
Ahijah at Shiloh. The disguise reveals Jeroboam's spiritual
condition: he wants God's prophet to help him, but he does not
want to face God's prophet honestly. He knows his relationship
with God is broken — the disguise is an admission of guilt. He
sends his wife rather than going himself. He sends gifts (ten
loaves, cracknels, honey) — a commoner's offering, not a king's,
to maintain the deception. Yet he still turns to Ahijah — the
true prophet — not to his own golden calves. In crisis, even
apostates know where truth resides. Ahijah is now old and blind
("his eyes were set by reason of his age"), but God's revelation
does not depend on physical sight.

Ahijah's Prophecy Against Jeroboam (vv.5-16): God tells Ahijah
the disguised woman is coming before she arrives — the disguise
is useless before omniscience. Ahijah's greeting is devastating:
"Come in, thou wife of Jeroboam; why feignest thou thyself to be
another? for I am sent to thee with heavy tidings" (v.6). The
prophecy recounts God's grace to Jeroboam (exalted from the
people, given the kingdom torn from David's house) and then
indicts him: "thou hast done evil above all that were before thee"
(v.9). The specific charge: making "other gods, and molten images"
and casting God "behind thy back." The judgment is total: every
male of Jeroboam's house will be cut off, their bodies left for
dogs and birds — the ultimate disgrace in the ancient world. Only
the sick child Abijah will receive proper burial "because in him
there is found some good thing toward the LORD" (v.13). The child's
death is actually mercy — he is spared from seeing the destruction
to come. The prophecy extends beyond Jeroboam to all Israel:
scattered "beyond the river" (v.15) — a prophecy of the Assyrian
exile fulfilled in 722 BC, over 200 years later.

The Child Dies (vv.17-20): Jeroboam's wife returns to Tirzah
(the capital), and "when she came to the threshold of the door,
the child died" (v.17). The timing is precise — exactly as Ahijah
said: "when thy feet enter into the city." God's word is fulfilled
to the letter. Jeroboam reigns 22 years total and dies; Nadab
succeeds him.

Rehoboam's Reign in Judah (vv.21-24): The narrative shifts south.
Rehoboam reigns 17 years in Jerusalem. His mother was Naamah, an
Ammonitess — one of Solomon's foreign wives. Under Rehoboam,
Judah descends into full Canaanite paganism: high places, images
(pillars), groves (Asherim) "on every high hill, and under every
green tree" (v.23). Most shocking: "there were also sodomites in
the land" (v.24) — male cult prostitutes associated with Canaanite
fertility worship. They did "according to all the abominations of
the nations which the LORD cast out." Judah has become what the
Canaanites were — the very people God displaced to give Israel
the land.

Shishak's Invasion (vv.25-28): In Rehoboam's fifth year, Pharaoh
Shishak (Shoshenq I, confirmed by Egyptian records and the
Bubastite Portal at Karnak) invades Jerusalem. He takes "the
treasures of the house of the LORD, and the treasures of the
king's house... all the shields of gold which Solomon had made"
(v.26). Rehoboam replaces them with bronze shields. This is the
chapter's central image: gold replaced by bronze. The glory of
Solomon's kingdom is gone — what remains is a cheap substitute
maintained with the same ceremony ("the guard bare them") but
without the substance. The nation keeps up appearances while the
reality has departed.

Rehoboam's Death (vv.29-31): Rehoboam dies after continual war
with Jeroboam. His son Abijam succeeds him. The author twice
mentions his mother Naamah the Ammonitess — emphasizing that
Solomon's foreign marriages continue to bear bitter fruit in the
next generation.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Jerusalem is central — site of Solomon's Temple and royal palace; capital of the united kingdom, then of Judah (southern kingdom) after the division.
- The kingdom divides after Solomon: Israel (north, capital eventually at Samaria) and Judah (south, capital Jerusalem).
- Key locations: Gibeon (Solomon's dream, ch.3), Tyre (Hiram's city in Phoenicia, chs.5-7), Mount Carmel (Elijah vs. Baal prophets, ch.18), Zarephath (Sidonian widow, ch.17), Mount Horeb/Sinai (Elijah flees, ch.19), Ramoth-gilead (Ahab's death, ch.22).

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.6  — "Come in, you wife of Jeroboam; why feignest you thyself
        to be another? for I am sent to you with heavy tidings."
        (ESV) — No disguise works before God. Jeroboam's wife
        cannot hide from the blind prophet because God has already
        revealed her identity. Human deception is futile before
        divine omniscience.

v.9  — "But have done evil above all that were before you: for
        you have gone and made you other gods, and molten images,
        to provoke me to anger, and have cast me behind your back."
        (ESV) — The indictment summarized. "Cast me behind your
        back" = deliberately put God out of sight and out of mind.
        Jeroboam did not merely neglect God — he actively pushed
        Him away.

v.13 — "For he only of Jeroboam shall come to the grave, because
        in him there is found some good thing toward the LORD God
        of Israel in the house of Jeroboam." (ESV) — A remarkable
        statement. In the entire house of Jeroboam, only this dying
        child has "some good thing" toward God. His early death is
        mercy — he is spared the coming judgment.

v.16 — "And he shall give Israel up because of the sins of
        Jeroboam, who did sin, and who made Israel to sin." (ESV)
        — The recurring refrain of 1 Kings. Jeroboam's sin was not
        private — he institutionalized idolatry and made an entire
        nation complicit. Leaders who lead others into sin bear
        double guilt.

v.26 — "And he took away the treasures of the house of the LORD...
        he even took away all: and he took away all the shields of
        gold which Solomon had made." (ESV) — The glory departs.
        What Solomon built in splendor is stripped away in one
        generation. Sin always costs more than we expect.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------

         kingdom. The same prophet who gave now takes away.
v.9   — 1 Kings 12:28-30 — Jeroboam's golden calves — the specific
         sin referenced here as "other gods and molten images."
v.10  — 1 Kings 15:29 — Fulfilled when Baasha destroys Jeroboam's
         entire house, leaving none alive.
v.13  — 2 Kings 22:20 — Similar mercy: Josiah is promised death
         before seeing Jerusalem's destruction. Early death can be
         divine mercy.
v.15  — 2 Kings 17:6, 23 — The Assyrian exile fulfills this
         prophecy: Israel scattered "beyond the river."
v.22  — Deuteronomy 32:16, 21 — "They provoked him to jealousy
         with strange gods." The same language used of Judah here.
v.24  — Deuteronomy 23:17 — "There shall be no whore of the
         daughters of Israel, nor a sodomite of the sons of Israel."
         Direct violation of Mosaic law.
v.25  — 2 Chronicles 12:2-9 — Parallel account of Shishak's
         invasion with additional detail: Shishak came because
         "they had transgressed against the LORD."
v.26  — 1 Kings 10:16-17 — Solomon's original gold shields,
         now lost. The contrast between chapters 10 and 14 is
         the contrast between glory and judgment.
v.31  — 1 Kings 15:1-2 — Abijam's reign continues the pattern
         of unfaithfulness in Judah.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/1-kings-14/
  Guzik highlights the irony of Jeroboam's disguise: the man who
  created a false religion now sends his wife in a false identity
  to consult the true prophet. Jeroboam knows his golden calves
  cannot help — in crisis, he turns to the God he rejected. Guzik
  also emphasizes the gold-to-bronze replacement as a picture of
  spiritual decline: maintaining religious forms while the glory
  has departed.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "The shields of gold are gone, and shields of brass are put in
  their place. This is the way of declension. We do not at first
  give up all religion; we find a substitute — something that will
  look like the genuine article, and yet costs us nothing. The
  forms are maintained, the ceremonies are observed, but the gold
  is gone. How many churches carry brazen shields today and think
  themselves rich! How many Christians keep up the outward show of
  devotion while the heart has long since departed from God!"

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.3  — KJV: "cracknels" / ESV: "some cakes" — Hebrew "niqqudim"
       = a type of hard biscuit or crumbled cake. KJV uses the
       archaic English "cracknels" (a crisp wafer). The ESV
       generalizes to "cakes." These were simple, common foods —
       deliberately humble to maintain the disguise.

v.6  — KJV: "heavy tidings" / ESV: "unbearable news" — Hebrew
       "qashah" = hard, severe, harsh. KJV's "heavy" captures the
       weight; ESV's "unbearable" captures the emotional impact.
       Both convey that the message will be devastating.

v.10 — KJV: "him that pisseth against the wall" / ESV: "every
       male" — Hebrew "mashtin beqir" = literally "one who urinates
       against a wall" — a crude idiom for males. The KJV preserves
       the literal Hebrew; the ESV gives the meaning. The vulgarity
       of the expression conveys contempt for Jeroboam's house.

v.15 — KJV: "groves" / ESV: "Asherim" — Hebrew "asherim" = wooden
       poles or carved images representing the Canaanite goddess
       Asherah. KJV consistently translates as "groves" (the
       objects were often set up in groves of trees). ESV
       transliterates the Hebrew term directly.

v.23 — KJV: "images" / ESV: "pillars" — Hebrew "matstseboth" =
       standing stones, sacred pillars used in Canaanite worship.
       These were upright stone monuments, not carved images in the
       modern sense. The ESV "pillars" is more precise.

v.24 — KJV: "sodomites" / ESV: "male cult prostitutes" — Hebrew
       "qedeshim" = literally "consecrated ones" — men dedicated
       to ritual sexual acts in pagan worship. KJV's "sodomites"
       reflects the association with Genesis 19; ESV clarifies the
       cultic/religious context of the practice.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. In crisis, we reveal what we truly believe (vv.2-3). Jeroboam
   did not send to his golden calves when his son was dying — he
   sent to God's prophet. His false religion was useful for
   politics but useless for real need. What do you turn to when
   life falls apart? That reveals your true faith. The things we
   trust in prosperity often fail us in adversity.

2. You cannot disguise yourself before God (vv.5-6). Jeroboam's
   wife could fool a blind man — but not a man with God's
   revelation. God told Ahijah she was coming before she arrived.
   We may hide our true condition from others, even from ourselves,
   but God sees through every pretense. "All things are naked and
   opened unto the eyes of him with whom we have to do" (Hebrews
   4:13).

3. Leaders who make others sin bear compounded guilt (v.16).
   Jeroboam's sin was not merely personal — he "made Israel to
   sin." This phrase echoes through the entire book. A leader's
   influence multiplies both good and evil. Parents, pastors,
   teachers — anyone with influence over others — will answer not
   only for their own sin but for the sin they enabled in others.

4. Spiritual decline often preserves the forms while losing the
   substance (vv.26-28). Bronze shields replaced gold shields, but
   the ceremony continued unchanged. The guards still carried them;
   the king still processed to the temple. Everything looked the
   same — but the glory was gone. Beware of maintaining religious
   routine after the heart has departed. Form without power is
   self-deception.

5. God's mercy appears even in judgment (v.13). The child Abijah
   dies — but his death is framed as mercy: he alone receives
   proper burial because "some good thing" was found in him. God
   takes him before the greater horror comes. Sometimes what looks
   like loss is actually God's protection from something worse.
