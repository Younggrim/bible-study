1 Kings - Chapter 3
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
God appears to Solomon at Gibeon and offers him anything; Solomon asks for wisdom to govern. God grants unparalleled wisdom plus riches and honor, and Solomon demonstrates it by judging the case of two mothers and one living child.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Unknown, traditionally attributed to Jeremiah or a prophetic
school. Originally one book with 2 Kings in the Hebrew Bible. Covers
approximately 120 years (970-850 BC) — from David's death through
Ahaziah's reign. Key themes: the glory and failure of Solomon, the
division of the kingdom as judgment for idolatry, the faithfulness of
God's prophets (especially Elijah), and the principle that a nation's
spiritual health depends on its leaders' faithfulness to God's covenant.

Historical Context: Chapter 3 marks the beginning of Solomon's
golden age. The kingdom is secure (chapter 2), and now the narrative
turns to Solomon's character and wisdom. The chapter opens with two
facts held in tension: "Solomon loved the LORD" (v.3) and "Solomon
made affinity with Pharaoh king of Egypt" (v.1). The Egyptian
marriage alliance was a diplomatic masterstroke — no previous
Israelite king had been considered worthy of a pharaoh's daughter.
It demonstrates Israel's rising power. But the seeds of Solomon's
eventual fall are already present: the foreign wife, the high
places, the compromise mixed with genuine devotion. The chapter's
centerpiece is God's extraordinary offer at Gibeon: "Ask what I
shall give thee" (v.5). Solomon's request for wisdom rather than
wealth, long life, or military victory pleases God and becomes the
foundation of everything that follows. The judgment of the two
mothers then demonstrates that this wisdom is not merely theoretical
— it works in practical, human situations. The chapter establishes
Solomon as the wisest man who ever lived and prepares for the
golden age described in chapters 4-10.

Solomon's Marriage and Worship (vv.1-4): The chapter opens with
apparent contradiction. Solomon marries Pharaoh's daughter — a
foreign alliance that will prove characteristic of his reign and
ultimately his downfall (11:1-4). Yet "Solomon loved the LORD"
(v.3). The author notes a qualification: "only he sacrificed and
burnt incense in high places" (v.3). This was not necessarily
pagan worship — the high places were legitimate Yahweh-worship
sites before the Temple was built. The tabernacle itself was at
Gibeon (2 Chronicles 1:3). But the "only" signals that this was
less than ideal. Solomon's faith is genuine but already contains
the seeds of compromise. His thousand burnt offerings at Gibeon
demonstrate extravagant devotion — and it is precisely there that
God meets him.

God's Offer and Solomon's Request (vv.5-14): "Ask what I shall give
thee" — perhaps the most remarkable open-ended offer in Scripture.
Solomon's response reveals his heart at this early stage: he
acknowledges God's faithfulness to David (v.6), confesses his own
inadequacy ("I am but a little child," v.7), recognizes the weight
of his responsibility (v.8), and asks for "an understanding heart
to judge thy people" (v.9). The Hebrew "lev shomea" = a listening
heart. Solomon asks not for knowledge but for receptivity — the
ability to hear what is true and act accordingly. God's response
exceeds the request: wisdom is granted, plus riches and honor that
were not asked for. But note the conditional promise of long life
(v.14): "IF thou wilt walk in my ways." Solomon will not fulfill
this condition, and his life will be cut short compared to David's.

The Two Mothers: Wisdom Demonstrated (vv.16-28): The famous
judgment serves a narrative purpose: it proves that Solomon's
God-given wisdom is real and effective. Two prostitutes — the
lowest social class, with no witnesses or evidence to support
either claim — present an impossible case. Solomon's solution is
brilliant not because he intended to kill the child, but because
he understood maternal love: the true mother would rather lose her
child to a rival than see him die. The false mother, driven by
grief-twisted justice ("Let it be neither mine nor thine"), reveals
herself. The result: "all Israel feared the king: for they saw
that the wisdom of God was in him" (v.28). Wisdom is validated
by its fruit.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Jerusalem is central — site of Solomon's Temple and royal palace; capital of the united kingdom, then of Judah (southern kingdom) after the division.
- The kingdom divides after Solomon: Israel (north, capital eventually at Samaria) and Judah (south, capital Jerusalem).
- Key locations: Gibeon (Solomon's dream, ch.3), Tyre (Hiram's city in Phoenicia, chs.5-7), Mount Carmel (Elijah vs. Baal prophets, ch.18), Zarephath (Sidonian widow, ch.17), Mount Horeb/Sinai (Elijah flees, ch.19), Ramoth-gilead (Ahab's death, ch.22).

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/1-kings-3/
  Guzik notes that Solomon's request for wisdom was itself wise —
  showing he already possessed the seed of what he asked for. He
  also emphasizes the contrast between Solomon's humility here
  ("I am but a little child") and his later arrogance. Wisdom is
  a gift that must be continually stewarded, not a one-time
  acquisition. Solomon received wisdom but eventually stopped
  living by it.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "Solomon asked for an understanding heart — not a clever head,
  but a hearing heart. The original word means 'a listening heart.'
  This is where all true wisdom begins: not in speaking but in
  hearing, not in knowing but in receiving. We must hear God before
  we can judge men. We must listen to wisdom before we can speak it.
  Give me, O Lord, not knowledge that puffs up, but the hearing
  heart that knows Your voice and obeys it."

REFLECTION
------------------------------------
1. What would you ask for if God said "Ask what I shall give thee"?
   (v.5). Solomon's request reveals his priorities at their best:
   not personal comfort but capacity to serve. Our prayers reveal
   our hearts. If God gave you a blank check, would you ask for
   something that benefits others or something that benefits only
   yourself? The answer exposes what we truly value.

2. Humility is the prerequisite for wisdom (v.7). "I am but a
   little child" — Solomon knew what he didn't know. Those who
   think themselves wise rarely grow wiser. Those who acknowledge
   their inadequacy position themselves to receive God's help.
   Pride says "I can handle this." Wisdom says "I cannot — but God
   can, through me."

3. Wisdom is validated by its fruit, not by credentials (vv.16-28).
   Solomon didn't cite his education or his father's reputation.
   He simply made a wise judgment, and the people recognized God's
   wisdom in the result. True wisdom proves itself in practice. If
   your "wisdom" doesn't produce just, compassionate, effective
   outcomes — question whether it's wisdom at all.

4. God gives beyond what we ask (v.13). Solomon asked for wisdom;
   God added riches and honor unsolicited. "Seek first the kingdom
   of God, and his righteousness; and all these things shall be
   added unto you" (Matthew 6:33). When our priorities align with
   God's, He handles the rest. The error is seeking the "things"
   first and hoping righteousness will follow.

5. Seeds of future failure can coexist with present faithfulness
   (vv.1, 3). Solomon loved the LORD — genuinely. But he also
   married Pharaoh's daughter and worshipped at high places. Small
   compromises don't feel dangerous at the time. They coexist with
   real devotion. But left unaddressed, they grow. Guard the "small"
   areas of compromise — they may become the doorways to collapse.
