1 Kings - Chapter 11
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Solomon's foreign wives turn his heart after other gods in his old age, and God raises adversaries against him. God tells Jeroboam through Ahijah the prophet that He will tear ten tribes from Solomon's son.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Unknown, traditionally attributed to Jeremiah or a prophetic
school. Originally one book with 2 Kings in the Hebrew Bible. Covers
approximately 120 years (970-850 BC) — from David's death through
Ahaziah's reign. Key themes: the glory and failure of Solomon, the
division of the kingdom as judgment for idolatry, the faithfulness of
God's prophets (especially Elijah), and the principle that a nation's
spiritual health depends on its leaders' faithfulness to God's covenant.

Historical Context: Chapter 11 is the great reversal — the wisest
man who ever lived becomes a fool. After ten chapters of ascending
glory, Solomon falls into idolatry through his foreign wives. The
chapter is devastating precisely because of what preceded it. This
is not a weak man failing — this is the man who asked for wisdom,
received it supernaturally, and then abandoned the God who gave it.
The fall is gradual: "when Solomon was old" (v.4) — this was not a
sudden collapse but a slow drift over decades. The 700 wives and
300 concubines were accumulated over years of political marriages,
each one a small compromise that eventually produced catastrophic
apostasy. God's response is both just and merciful: the kingdom
will be torn away, but not in Solomon's lifetime (for David's sake),
and not entirely (one tribe preserved for David's lamp in
Jerusalem). The chapter introduces the adversaries God raises:
Hadad the Edomite, Rezon of Damascus, and most significantly
Jeroboam — to whom the prophet Ahijah promises ten tribes. Solomon
dies having reigned 40 years, and the stage is set for the
kingdom's division.

Solomon's Apostasy (vv.1-8): The opening word "But" signals the
reversal. Solomon "loved many strange women" — the same verb used
in 3:3 ("Solomon loved the LORD"). His love has been redirected.
The nations listed (Moabites, Ammonites, Edomites, Sidonians,
Hittites) are precisely those God had forbidden intermarriage with
(Deuteronomy 7:1-4). God's reason was explicit: "they will turn
away your heart after their gods." And that is exactly what happens.
Seven hundred wives and 300 concubines — these were political
marriages cementing alliances, but each brought her gods. "When
Solomon was old, his wives turned away his heart" (v.4). He builds
high places for Ashtoreth (Sidonian fertility goddess), Milcom/
Molech (Ammonite deity associated with child sacrifice), and
Chemosh (Moabite god). These shrines stood on the Mount of Olives
— visible from the Temple. The wisest man builds altars to the
most abominable gods within sight of Yahweh's house.

God's Judgment (vv.9-13): "The LORD was angry with Solomon" (v.9).
The anger is intensified by the privilege Solomon had received: God
"had appeared unto him twice" and "had commanded him concerning this
thing." Solomon sinned against direct revelation and personal
encounter with God. The judgment: "I will surely rend the kingdom
from thee" (v.11). But mercy tempers justice: not in Solomon's
lifetime (for David's sake), and one tribe preserved (for David
and Jerusalem's sake). God's faithfulness to David's covenant
limits the consequences of Solomon's sin — but does not eliminate
them.

The Adversaries (vv.14-25): God "stirred up" adversaries — the
peace of Solomon's reign begins to crumble. Hadad the Edomite
(a royal survivor of David's conquest, raised in Egypt) returns to
harass Israel's southern border. Rezon of Damascus establishes an
independent Syrian kingdom to the north. These external threats
are God's discipline — the "rest on every side" (5:4) is being
withdrawn because the condition of faithfulness has been violated.

Ahijah's Prophecy to Jeroboam (vv.26-40): The most significant
adversary is internal. Jeroboam — an able, industrious Ephraimite
whom Solomon had promoted — receives a dramatic prophetic sign.
Ahijah tears a new garment into twelve pieces and gives Jeroboam
ten: "I will rend the kingdom out of the hand of Solomon" (v.31).
The prophecy includes a conditional promise to Jeroboam (v.38):
"if thou wilt hearken... I will build thee a sure house, as I
built for David." Jeroboam receives the same offer Solomon received
— and will also fail it. Solomon's response to the prophecy is
telling: he tries to kill Jeroboam (v.40). The man who began with
wisdom ends with murder attempts. Jeroboam flees to Egypt — to
Shishak, who will later invade Judah (14:25-26).

Solomon's Death (vv.41-43): Solomon reigns 40 years and dies. The
author's summary is notably restrained — no evaluation of his
character, no "he did right/evil in the sight of the LORD." The
silence is eloquent. Solomon's legacy is ambiguous: unmatched
wisdom and unmatched folly, supreme glory and supreme failure.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Jerusalem is central — site of Solomon's Temple and royal palace; capital of the united kingdom, then of Judah (southern kingdom) after the division.
- The kingdom divides after Solomon: Israel (north, capital eventually at Samaria) and Judah (south, capital Jerusalem).
- Key locations: Gibeon (Solomon's dream, ch.3), Tyre (Hiram's city in Phoenicia, chs.5-7), Mount Carmel (Elijah vs. Baal prophets, ch.18), Zarephath (Sidonian widow, ch.17), Mount Horeb/Sinai (Elijah flees, ch.19), Ramoth-gilead (Ahab's death, ch.22).

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "You shall not go in to them, neither shall they come in to
        you: for surely they will turn away your heart after their
        gods: Solomon clave to these in love." (ESV) — God's
        command was explicit and the reason was stated. Solomon knew
        the danger and embraced it anyway. Knowledge without
        obedience is worthless.

v.4  — "When Solomon was old, that his wives turned away his heart
        after other gods: and his heart was not perfect with the
        LORD his God, as was the heart of David his father." (ESV)
        — The tragic summary. The heart that once asked for wisdom
        is now divided. "Not perfect" = not whole, not undivided.
        Solomon's heart belongs to many gods now.

v.9  — "The LORD was angry with Solomon, because his heart was
        turned from the LORD God of Israel, which had appeared to
        him twice." (ESV) — Privilege increases accountability.
        Solomon had two direct encounters with God — more than most
        people in Scripture. His sin is worse because of what he
        knew and experienced.

v.11 — "I will surely rend the kingdom from you, and will give it
        to your servant." (ESV) — The consequence of covenant
        violation. What God built, God can tear apart. The kingdom
        given in chapter 2 is taken in chapter 11.

v.36 — "That David my servant may have a light alway before me in
        Jerusalem." (ESV) — God preserves one tribe for David's
        sake. The "lamp" metaphor = the continuation of David's
        line. Even in judgment, God maintains His covenant promise.
        The Messianic line must continue.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.1   — Deuteronomy 7:3-4 — "Neither shall you make marriages with
         them... for they will turn away your son from following me."
         The exact warning Solomon violates.
v.2   — Deuteronomy 17:17 — "Neither shall he multiply wives to
         himself, that his heart turn not away." The law of the king,
         directly violated.
v.4   — 1 Kings 9:4 — God's condition: "if you will walk before me,
         as David your father walked." Solomon fails this condition.
v.7   — 2 Kings 23:13 — Josiah destroys these high places 300 years
         later. Solomon's sin endured for centuries.
v.11  — 1 Kings 12:16-17 — The kingdom is torn exactly as God
         declared.
v.29  — 1 Kings 14:2 — Ahijah appears again, now blind, to
         pronounce judgment on Jeroboam's house. The prophet who
         gave the kingdom also takes it away.
v.36  — 2 Samuel 7:16 — "Your throne shall be established for ever."
         The Davidic covenant ensures one tribe remains — the lamp
         cannot be extinguished until Messiah comes.
v.38  — 1 Kings 14:7-10 — Jeroboam fails the same condition Solomon
         failed. The "sure house" is never built for him.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/1-kings-11/
  Guzik emphasizes the gradual nature of Solomon's fall: "his wives
  turned away his heart" — it was not sudden but incremental. Each
  compromise made the next one easier. Guzik notes that Solomon
  never formally renounced Yahweh — he simply added other gods.
  Syncretism (mixing true worship with false) is more common than
  outright apostasy, and equally deadly.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "Solomon's fall is the most solemn warning in all Scripture. If
  the wisest man can fall, who is safe? If the man who talked with
  God twice can turn to idols, what hope have we in our own
  strength? None. Our only safety is daily dependence on God's
  grace. The moment we think ourselves secure, we are in the
  greatest danger. 'Let him that thinketh he stands take heed
  lest he fall' (1 Corinthians 10:12). Solomon proves that no
  amount of past experience with God guarantees future faithfulness."

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1  — KJV: "strange women" / ESV: "foreign women" — Hebrew
       "nashim nokhriyot" = foreign/alien women. KJV "strange" is
       archaic for "foreign/unfamiliar." The issue is not ethnicity
       per se but religious allegiance — these women brought their
       gods.

v.3  — KJV: "his wives turned away his heart" / ESV: "his wives
       turned away his heart" — Hebrew "natah" = to stretch out,
       extend, turn aside, bend. The image is of a heart being
       pulled/bent away from its proper direction. It was gradual
       — a slow bending, not a sudden break.

v.5  — KJV: "Milcom the abomination" / ESV: "Milcom the
       abomination" — Hebrew "shiqquts" = detestable thing,
       abomination. A deliberately derogatory term for a foreign
       deity. Milcom/Molech was associated with child sacrifice
       (Leviticus 18:21; 2 Kings 23:10). Solomon built a shrine
       to a god that demanded children be burned alive.

v.14 — KJV: "the LORD stirred up an adversary" / ESV: "the LORD
       raised up an adversary" — Hebrew "heqim... satan." "Satan"
       here means "adversary/opponent" (not the proper name of the
       devil). God actively raises opposition as discipline. The
       peace Solomon enjoyed was God's gift; its removal is God's
       judgment.

v.36 — KJV: "a light alway" / ESV: "a lamp" — Hebrew "nir" = lamp,
       light. The metaphor of David's "lamp" in Jerusalem represents
       the continuation of his dynasty. A lamp that goes out means
       the line has ended. God ensures the lamp keeps burning —
       ultimately until Christ, the true Light, comes.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Ashtoreth     — The Sidonian/Canaanite goddess of fertility, love,
              and war. The plural "Ashtaroth" was used generically
              for female deities. Her worship involved sexual rituals
              and cult prostitution. Solomon built her a shrine on
              the Mount of Olives.

Molech/       — The Ammonite deity whose worship included child
Milcom          sacrifice — children "passed through the fire"
              (burned alive) as offerings. Called "the abomination"
              in Scripture. That Solomon built a shrine to this god
              represents the absolute nadir of his apostasy.

Chemosh       — The national god of Moab. The Moabite Stone (Mesha
              Stele, discovered 1868) confirms Chemosh's central
              role in Moabite religion. His worship likely included
              human sacrifice. Solomon built his shrine on "the hill
              that is before Jerusalem" — the Mount of Olives.

Ahijah        — "Brother of Yahweh" — a prophet from Shiloh who
              delivers God's message to Jeroboam through the dramatic
              sign of the torn garment. He appears again in chapter
              14 to pronounce judgment on Jeroboam's house. He
              represents the prophetic voice that both gives and
              takes away kingdoms.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. No one is too wise to fall (vv.1-4). Solomon — supernaturally
   gifted with wisdom, twice visited by God — fell into idolatry.
   If he could fall, so can you. Past spiritual experiences do not
   immunize against future failure. Daily dependence on God is not
   optional — it is survival. "Let him that thinketh he standeth
   take heed lest he fall" (1 Corinthians 10:12).

2. The fall was gradual, not sudden (v.4). "When Solomon was old"
   — this took decades. Each foreign wife was a small compromise.
   Each shrine was a small accommodation. Sin rarely announces
   itself as catastrophe. It arrives as a series of small decisions
   that seem manageable at the time. Guard the small compromises —
   they are the path to ruin.

3. Disobedience to clear commands has no excuse (v.2). God said
   explicitly: "You shall not." Solomon knew the command and the
   reason. He disobeyed anyway. We cannot claim ignorance when
   Scripture is clear. The issue is not knowledge but will. Solomon
   had all the wisdom in the world — and chose to ignore it.

4. God's mercy limits judgment but does not eliminate it (vv.12-13).
   For David's sake, the kingdom is not torn away immediately or
   completely. Grace delays and reduces consequences — but does not
   remove them entirely. Do not presume on God's patience. His mercy
   gives time for repentance, not permission for continued sin.

5. Privilege increases accountability (v.9). God was angry because
   He "had appeared unto him twice." Solomon had more direct
   revelation than almost anyone in the Old Testament. Greater
   knowledge means greater responsibility. "Unto whomsoever much is
   given, of him shall be much required" (Luke 12:48). What you
   know of God obligates you to live accordingly.
