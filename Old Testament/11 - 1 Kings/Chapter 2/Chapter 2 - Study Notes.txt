1 Kings - Chapter 2
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
David gives Solomon his final charge: be strong, keep God's law, and settle accounts with Joab and Shimei. Solomon consolidates his kingdom by executing Adonijah, Joab, and Shimei, and removing Abiathar from the priesthood.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Unknown, traditionally attributed to Jeremiah or a prophetic
school. Originally one book with 2 Kings in the Hebrew Bible. Covers
approximately 120 years (970-850 BC) — from David's death through
Ahaziah's reign. Key themes: the glory and failure of Solomon, the
division of the kingdom as judgment for idolatry, the faithfulness of
God's prophets (especially Elijah), and the principle that a nation's
spiritual health depends on its leaders' faithfulness to God's covenant.

Historical Context: Chapter 2 records the death of David and
Solomon's consolidation of power. This is approximately 970 BC.
David's final charge to Solomon combines spiritual exhortation
("keep the charge of the LORD thy God") with political instruction
(dealing with Joab and Shimei). The chapter may trouble modern
readers who see a dying king settling old scores. But the ancient
context is essential: Joab was a murderer who had killed two
commanders (Abner and Amasa) treacherously in peacetime, and David
had been unable to bring him to justice because of Joab's military
power. Shimei had cursed God's anointed king — a capital offense
under the covenant (Exodus 22:28). David's oath not to kill Shimei
personally did not bind Solomon. Adonijah's request for Abishag —
seemingly innocent — was understood as a claim to the throne, since
a king's concubine or attendant was royal property (cf. 2 Samuel
16:21-22, where Absalom takes David's concubines as a political
act). Solomon's decisive actions in this chapter are not cruelty
but the establishment of justice and the removal of threats to the
kingdom's stability. The chapter ends with the statement: "the
kingdom was established in the hand of Solomon" — the transition
is complete.

David's Charge to Solomon (vv.1-9): David's deathbed charge has
two parts. First, the spiritual foundation (vv.2-4): "Be strong,
and shew thyself a man" — not mere physical courage but covenantal
faithfulness. The charge echoes God's words to Joshua (Joshua 1:6-9):
strength is defined by obedience to the Law of Moses. David makes
the dynastic promise conditional: "If thy children take heed to
their way... there shall not fail thee a man on the throne of
Israel" (v.4). This is the Deuteronomic principle — blessing follows
obedience. Second, the political instructions (vv.5-9): deal with
Joab (who murdered Abner and Amasa in peacetime), reward Barzillai's
sons (who helped David during Absalom's revolt), and deal with
Shimei (who cursed David but was spared by oath). David entrusts
these matters to Solomon's "wisdom" — not commanding specific
actions but trusting Solomon to act justly.

David's Death (vv.10-12): "David slept with his fathers" — the
standard formula for a king's death. Forty years of reign: 7 in
Hebron over Judah alone, 33 in Jerusalem over all Israel. David's
legacy is complex — warrior, poet, adulterer, penitent, man after
God's own heart. But the author's focus here is simple transition:
"Solomon sat on the throne of David his father; and his kingdom
was established greatly."

Adonijah's Fatal Request (vv.13-25): Adonijah approaches Bathsheba
— not Solomon directly — asking for Abishag as his wife. His
opening words reveal lingering resentment: "Thou knowest that the
kingdom was mine" (v.15). His request appears modest, but Solomon
immediately sees through it: "Ask for him the kingdom also" (v.22).
In the ancient Near East, taking a king's woman was a claim to his
throne (cf. Absalom with David's concubines, 2 Samuel 16:21-22).
Whether Adonijah was consciously making a political move or merely
desired Abishag is debated — but Solomon's interpretation is
decisive. Adonijah had been warned: "if wickedness shall be found
in him, he shall die" (1:52). Solomon sees this as the wickedness
foretold.

Abiathar, Joab, and Shimei (vv.26-46): Solomon deals with each
threat systematically. Abiathar the priest is exiled to Anathoth
— spared from death because of his long service to David, but
removed from the priesthood. The author notes this fulfills the
prophecy against Eli's house (1 Samuel 2:27-36). Joab flees to
the altar's horns, but unlike Adonijah in chapter 1, he is not
spared — his murders of Abner and Amasa disqualify him from
sanctuary (Exodus 21:14: "if a man come presumptuously upon his
neighbour, to slay him with guile; thou shalt take him from mine
altar, that he may die"). Shimei is given a conditional arrangement
— confined to Jerusalem on pain of death. For three years he
complies. Then two slaves escape to Gath, and Shimei's desire for
his property overrides his fear of death. He breaks the agreement
and dies for it. Each case demonstrates the same principle:
mercy was offered, conditions were set, the conditions were
violated, and justice followed.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Jerusalem is central — site of Solomon's Temple and royal palace; capital of the united kingdom, then of Judah (southern kingdom) after the division.
- The kingdom divides after Solomon: Israel (north, capital eventually at Samaria) and Judah (south, capital Jerusalem).
- Key locations: Gibeon (Solomon's dream, ch.3), Tyre (Hiram's city in Phoenicia, chs.5-7), Mount Carmel (Elijah vs. Baal prophets, ch.18), Zarephath (Sidonian widow, ch.17), Mount Horeb/Sinai (Elijah flees, ch.19), Ramoth-gilead (Ahab's death, ch.22).

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.3   — Joshua 1:7-8 — God's charge to Joshua uses nearly identical
         language: "Be strong and very courageous... observe to do
         according to all the law." Same principle: obedience is
         the path to prosperity.
v.4   — 2 Samuel 7:12-16 — The Davidic covenant. God's promise to
         David of an eternal dynasty — ultimately fulfilled in Christ.
v.5   — 2 Samuel 3:27 — Joab murdered Abner by treachery under
         pretense of a private conversation.
v.5   — 2 Samuel 20:9-10 — Joab murdered Amasa while greeting him
         with apparent friendship.
v.7   — 2 Samuel 17:27-29 — Barzillai provided food and supplies
         to David during Absalom's rebellion.
v.8   — 2 Samuel 16:5-13 — Shimei cursed David and threw stones as
         David fled Jerusalem during Absalom's revolt.
v.22  — 2 Samuel 16:21-22 — Absalom took David's concubines publicly
         as a claim to the throne. Same principle applies to
         Adonijah's request for Abishag.
v.27  — 1 Samuel 2:30-36 — God's judgment on Eli's house: "I will
         cut off your arm." Abiathar was the last of Eli's line to
         serve as priest.
v.35  — Ezekiel 44:15 — Zadok's descendants will serve in the
         eschatological temple. His faithfulness here establishes
         his priestly line permanently.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/1-kings-2/
  Guzik emphasizes that David's charge to Solomon shows the proper
  priority: spiritual obedience first, then political wisdom.
  David's instructions about Joab and Shimei were not personal
  vengeance but unfinished justice — matters David could not resolve
  during his reign because of political constraints. Guzik notes
  that Adonijah's request for Abishag was "either incredibly naive
  or deliberately provocative."

Charles Spurgeon:
  "David said, 'Be strong and shew thyself a man.' But how? By
  keeping the charge of the LORD. This is the biblical definition
  of manhood — not the sword but the Scripture, not conquest but
  obedience. The strongest man in Israel is the one who walks in
  God's ways. Solomon was never stronger than when he prayed for
  wisdom; never weaker than when he married foreign wives. Strength
  and godliness are not two things but one."

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. The foundation of all leadership is obedience to God's Word
   (vv.2-3). David's primary charge to Solomon is not military or
   political — it is spiritual. "Keep the charge of the LORD thy
   God." Everything else flows from this. When leaders abandon
   Scripture, the kingdom crumbles. When they hold fast to it,
   they prosper. This principle applies to homes, churches, and
   nations equally.

2. God's promises are often conditional in application (v.4). The
   Davidic dynasty was guaranteed ultimately (Christ will come), but
   each generation's experience of blessing depended on faithfulness.
   God's promises to us in Christ are secure — but our daily
   experience of His blessing is tied to our daily obedience. The
   unconditional and conditional aspects of grace are not
   contradictory — they operate on different levels.

3. Unresolved justice eventually demands resolution (vv.5-6, 8-9).
   David could not deal with Joab or Shimei during his reign — the
   political costs were too high. But injustice does not expire.
   Blood-guilt cannot simply be ignored or forgotten. Sometimes
   justice waits — but it always arrives. "Be sure your sin will
   find you out" (Numbers 32:23).

4. Small compromises reveal unchanged hearts (vv.39-40). Shimei
   lived faithfully in Jerusalem for three years. But when his
   slaves escaped, his desire for property overrode his commitment.
   The restriction seemed so small — just stay in Jerusalem. But
   the small test revealed that his submission was circumstantial,
   not internal. How we handle small restrictions reveals whether
   our obedience is genuine or merely convenient.

5. God fulfills His word across centuries (v.27). The prophecy
   against Eli's house was spoken in 1 Samuel 2 — perhaps 100 years
   before Solomon's reign. Yet it found exact fulfillment when
   Abiathar was expelled. God is never in a hurry, but He is never
   late. His word accomplishes its purpose on His schedule.
