2 Chronicles - Chapter 1
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Solomon worships at the tabernacle in Gibeon, where God appears to him at night offering whatever he asks. Solomon requests wisdom and knowledge to govern God's people, and God grants him unparalleled wisdom along with riches and honor.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Traditionally attributed to Ezra the scribe. Originally one
book with 1 Chronicles. Written post-exile (approximately 450-400 BC).
2 Chronicles covers Solomon's reign through the Babylonian exile and
Cyrus's decree of return (approximately 970-538 BC). The Chronicler
focuses exclusively on Judah (ignoring the northern kingdom) and
emphasizes Temple worship, reform movements, and the principle of
2 Chronicles 7:14: "If my people... shall humble themselves, and
pray... then will I hear from heaven, and will forgive their sin, and
will heal their land."

Historical Context: 2 Chronicles opens where 1 Chronicles ended — with
Solomon established on the throne. The Chronicler's account of Solomon
omits entirely the political intrigue of 1 Kings 1-2 (Adonijah's
rebellion, Joab's execution, Shimei's death) and begins immediately
with Solomon's spiritual act: seeking God at Gibeon. This deliberate
editorial choice presents Solomon as the ideal king whose first act
is worship. The high place at Gibeon was legitimate because it housed
the Tabernacle of Moses, including the bronze altar made by Bezalel
during the Exodus. The ark, however, was in Jerusalem in the tent
David had prepared — creating a temporary split between the Tabernacle
structure and its most sacred object. Solomon's journey to Gibeon with
"all Israel" — commanders, judges, governors, and family heads —
demonstrates that this was a national act of worship, not a private
devotion. The thousand burnt offerings represent extraordinary
generosity and devotion before God even speaks. God's nighttime
appearance and open-ended offer ("Ask what I shall give thee") tested
Solomon's heart. His request for wisdom to govern — rather than wealth,
long life, or victory over enemies — revealed a heart oriented toward
responsibility rather than privilege. God's response is lavish: wisdom
granted, plus everything Solomon did not ask for. The chapter closes
with evidence of that material blessing — wealth so abundant that
silver became as common as stones. For the post-exilic community, this
chapter established a pattern: seek God first, prioritize His purposes,
and trust Him to provide everything else. It echoed the principle Jesus
would later articulate: "Seek ye first the kingdom of God... and all
these things shall be added unto you" (Matthew 6:33).

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Parallels 1-2 Kings but focuses exclusively on Judah (the southern kingdom). Jerusalem and the Temple are the theological center throughout.
- Key locations include various battle sites, reform locations, and high places destroyed or rebuilt by successive kings.
- The book ends with exile to Babylon (586 BC) and Cyrus's decree permitting return — the geographic arc moves from Jerusalem to Babylon and back.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "Solomon the son of David established himself in his kingdom,
        and the LORD his God was with him and made him exceedingly
        great." (ESV) — The Chronicler's opening statement
        attributes Solomon's strength entirely to God's presence.
        "The LORD his God was with him" — this phrase is the
        Chronicler's key to understanding success or failure
        throughout 2 Chronicles. Every king who prospers does so
        because God is with him; every king who falls does so
        because he forsakes God.

v.7  — "In that night God appeared to Solomon, and said to him, 'Ask
        what I shall give you.'" (ESV) — God initiates this
        encounter. Solomon did not ask for an audience; God came to
        him after Solomon demonstrated devotion through sacrifice.
        The open-ended offer reveals God's generosity — He places no
        limitations on what Solomon might request. This is a test of
        the heart: what a man asks for reveals what he values most.

v.10 — "Give me now wisdom and knowledge to go out and come in before
        this people, for who can govern this people of yours, which
        is so great?" (ESV) — Solomon's request reveals humility
        ("who can judge?"), awareness of responsibility ("this thy
        people"), and proper priority (capability for service rather
        than personal gain). He asks for wisdom not to be admired but
        to govern faithfully. The phrase "go out and come in"
        describes the totality of public life — all administrative
        and judicial activity.

v.12 — "Wisdom and knowledge are granted to you. I will also give you
        riches, possessions, and honor, such as none of the kings had
        who were before you, and none after you shall have the like."
        (ESV) — God rewards
        selfless requests with both what was asked and what was not.
        The principle is clear: those who seek God's purposes above
        personal gain receive both. This is not a prosperity formula
        but a revelation of God's character — He is generous to those
        whose hearts align with His priorities.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.1  — 1 Chronicles 29:25 — "The LORD made Solomon very great in the
        sight of all Israel." (ESV) The ending of 1 Chronicles
        connects directly to the opening of 2 Chronicles — continuity
        of narrative showing God's faithfulness to His promise
        regarding David's successor.

v.7  — 1 Kings 3:5-14 — The parallel account in Kings provides
        additional detail: Solomon was at Gibeon, God appeared in a
        dream, and the request is framed slightly differently. The
        Chronicler abbreviates some details but preserves the
        essential theological exchange.

v.10 — James 1:5 — "If any of you lacks wisdom, let him ask God, who
        gives generously to all without reproach, and it will be
        given him." (ESV) Solomon's experience becomes the paradigm
        for all believers — God gives wisdom generously to those who
        ask in faith.

v.12 — Matthew 6:33 — "But seek first the kingdom of God and his
        righteousness, and all these things will be added to you."
        (ESV) Solomon sought God's purpose (governing well) and
        received everything else as addition. Jesus articulates this
        same principle as a universal spiritual law.

v.14 — Deuteronomy 17:16 — "Only he must not acquire many horses for
        himself or cause the people to return to Egypt in order to
        acquire many horses." (ESV) Solomon's accumulation of horses
        from Egypt hints at tension with the Mosaic ideal for
        kingship — a foreshadowing that material blessing, when
        pursued beyond divine boundaries, becomes a snare.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/2-chronicles-1/
  Guzik notes that the Chronicler's omission of Solomon's political
  consolidation (1 Kings 1-2) highlights the theological purpose of
  2 Chronicles: it is a spiritual history, not a political one.
  Solomon's request for wisdom at Gibeon establishes the priority
  principle that governs the entire book — seek God first.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "Solomon asked for wisdom, and God gave him wisdom and all things
  beside. It is ever so: seek the highest good, and all lesser goods
  are added. The man who seeks gold first loses both gold and God;
  the man who seeks God first gains both God and gold. A thousand
  burnt offerings preceded the request — Solomon did not come to God
  with empty hands and greedy prayers. He worshipped before he asked.
  Let those who would receive from God learn first to give to God.
  The altar precedes the answer."

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1  — KJV: "was strengthened in his kingdom" / ESV: "established
       himself in his kingdom" — Hebrew "yithchazzeq" (strengthened
       himself / became strong). KJV uses the passive voice suggesting
       God's action; ESV uses a reflexive construction. Both are
       grammatically possible from the Hithpael form. The immediate
       context ("the LORD his God was with him") clarifies that the
       strength was divinely enabled.

v.8  — KJV: "Thou hast shewed great mercy" / ESV: "You have shown
       great and steadfast love" — Hebrew "chesed gadol" (great
       covenant-faithfulness/lovingkindness). ESV's "steadfast love"
       captures the covenantal dimension of chesed — this is not
       generic mercy but faithful love rooted in God's promises to
       David. KJV's "mercy" is adequate but less specific.

v.10 — KJV: "wisdom and knowledge" / ESV: "wisdom and knowledge" —
       Hebrew "chokmah" (wisdom — practical skill for living/ruling)
       and "madda" (knowledge — understanding of facts and situations).
       Solomon asks for both theoretical understanding and practical
       capability — head knowledge united with applied skill.

v.15 — KJV: "the sycomore trees that are in the vale" / ESV: "the
       sycamore of the Shephelah" — The Shephelah is the low-lying
       foothill region between the Judean highlands and the coastal
       plain. Sycamore trees (ficus sycomorus) were the common,
       inexpensive wood of the lowlands — Solomon made precious cedar
       as common as this everyday timber.

v.16 — KJV: "linen yarn" / ESV: "Kue" — Hebrew "miqveh" is difficult.
       KJV understood it as "linen yarn" (a commodity); ESV follows
       modern scholarship identifying it as Kue (Cilicia, in modern
       Turkey), a region known for horse breeding. Most modern
       scholars favor the geographical reading.

v.1 — ASV: uses "Jehovah" for KJV's "LORD" (Hebrew YHWH).
       NET: "royal authority, for" for KJV's "kingdom, and"
       WEB: uses "Yahweh" for KJV's "LORD" (Hebrew YHWH).
v.8 — ASV: "king" for KJV's "to reign"
       NET: "loyalty to my father" for KJV's "mercy unto"
       WEB: "loving kindness to" for KJV's "mercy unto"
v.10 — NET: "great nation of yours.”" for KJV's "thy people, that is so great?"
       WEB: "great people of yours?”" for KJV's "thy people, that is so great?"
v.15 — ASV: "sycomore-trees" for KJV's "sycomore trees"
       NET: "plentiful as sycamore fig" for KJV's "stones, and cedar"
       WEB: "lowland." for KJV's "vale for abundance."
v.16 — ASV: "them in droves, each drove" for KJV's "the linen yarn"
       NET: "traders purchased them from Que." for KJV's "merchants received the linen yarn at a price."
       WEB: "purchased them from Kue." for KJV's "received the linen yarn at a price."

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Gibeon      — A city approximately 6 miles northwest of Jerusalem,
             assigned to the Levites (Joshua 21:17). It housed the
             Tabernacle of Moses and the bronze altar during the
             period between the ark's separation from the Tabernacle
             and Solomon's construction of the Temple. Its "high
             place" was the most legitimate worship site in Israel
             at this time because of the Tabernacle's presence.

Brasen/     — The large altar constructed by Bezalel under Moses'
Bronze        direction (Exodus 38:1-7), measuring 5 cubits square
Altar         and 3 cubits high. It was the altar of burnt offering
             where sacrifices were made. Its presence at Gibeon gave
             that location its sacred significance.

Burnt       — Hebrew "olah" (that which ascends). A sacrifice
Offering      entirely consumed by fire — nothing was retained by the
             offerer. It represented complete dedication and
             consecration to God. Solomon's thousand burnt offerings
             expressed total devotion with extraordinary generosity.

Wisdom      — Hebrew "chokmah" — not merely intellectual capacity
             but practical skill for living and governing rightly.
             Biblical wisdom is always moral and relational — it
             includes discernment, justice, and the ability to apply
             truth to complex situations. Solomon sought the ability
             to govern God's people faithfully, not abstract
             philosophical knowledge.

Shephelah   — The low-lying foothills between Judah's central
             highlands and the coastal plain of Philistia. A fertile
             region known for agriculture (especially sycamore
             trees). KJV translates as "the vale" (valley/lowland).

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Your first act reveals your first priority (vv.3-6). Solomon's
   first recorded act as king was worship — not policy-making,
   military review, or palace construction, but sacrifice at God's
   altar. What is your first act when you receive new responsibility,
   a new position, or a new opportunity? Those who begin with God
   establish a foundation that sustains everything that follows.

2. God tests the heart with open-ended offers (v.7). "Ask what I
   shall give thee" — this reveals what you truly value. If God
   made this offer to you tonight, what would you ask? Your answer
   reveals the condition of your heart. Solomon's request for
   wisdom to serve others well — rather than personal advancement
   — reveals a heart aligned with God's purposes.

3. Humility before great responsibility is wisdom (v.10). Solomon
   recognized that governing God's people was beyond his natural
   ability — "who can judge this thy people, that is so great?"
   Those who recognize their insufficiency are candidates for God's
   sufficiency. Self-confidence disqualifies; God-dependence qualifies.

4. God's generosity exceeds our requests (v.12). Solomon asked for
   one thing and received everything. God is not stingy with those
   whose hearts are right. When you seek His purposes, He provides
   His resources — often exceeding what you thought to ask. But this
   only works when the heart is genuinely oriented toward service
   rather than self-enrichment.

5. Material blessing comes with responsibility (vv.14-17). The
   chapter ends with Solomon's enormous wealth — but compared with
   Deuteronomy 17:16, there are already warning signs. Horses from
   Egypt, trade relationships with pagan nations, accumulation of
   military might — these blessings, when they become the focus
   rather than the fruit, lead to decline. Enjoy God's provision
   without making provision your god.
