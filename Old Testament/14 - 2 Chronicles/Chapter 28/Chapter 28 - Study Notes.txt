2 Chronicles - Chapter 28
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
King Ahaz plunges Judah into unprecedented wickedness, practicing child sacrifice and worshipping at every high place. God allows Syria and Israel to defeat him severely, and even when facing destruction, Ahaz turns to Assyrian gods rather than repenting.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Traditionally attributed to Ezra the scribe. Originally one
book with 1 Chronicles. Written post-exile (approximately 450-400 BC).
2 Chronicles covers Solomon's reign through the Babylonian exile and
Cyrus's decree of return (approximately 970-538 BC). The Chronicler
focuses exclusively on Judah (ignoring the northern kingdom) and
emphasizes Temple worship, reform movements, and the principle of
2 Chronicles 7:14: "If my people... shall humble themselves, and
pray... then will I hear from heaven, and will forgive their sin, and
will heal their land."

Historical Context: Chapter 28 records the catastrophic reign of Ahaz
(approximately 735-715 BC), perhaps Judah's worst king — a man who
systematically dismantled every element of faithful worship and
replaced it with comprehensive paganism. The Chronicler's account is
a relentless catalog of spiritual atrocity: Baal worship (v.2), child
sacrifice in the Valley of Hinnom (v.3), high-place worship (v.4),
alliance with Assyria against God's will (vv.16-21), worship of
Damascus's gods (v.23), destruction of Temple vessels (v.24), closure
of the Temple doors (v.24), and establishment of pagan altars
throughout Jerusalem and every city of Judah (vv.24-25). No other
king so comprehensively attacked the worship of the LORD.

The historical context is the Syro-Ephraimite crisis (approximately
735-732 BC), when Syria and Israel allied against Judah to force Ahaz
into an anti-Assyrian coalition. Isaiah 7-12 provides the prophetic
perspective on this same period — Isaiah offered Ahaz a sign from God
(the Immanuel prophecy), but Ahaz refused divine help in favor of
Assyrian alliance. The Chronicler focuses on the devastating military
consequences: Syria took captives to Damascus (v.5); Pekah of Israel
killed 120,000 in a single day (v.6); 200,000 women and children were
taken captive (v.8); Edom invaded (v.17); Philistines seized cities
(v.18). Judah was attacked from every direction simultaneously — the
Chronicler's signature portrait of divine judgment against apostasy.

One remarkable interlude breaks the darkness: the prophet Oded's
intervention in Samaria (vv.9-15). When Israel's army brought 200,000
Judean captives north, Oded rebuked them — "are there not with you,
even with you, sins against the LORD?" Four Ephraimite leaders stood
with the prophet, and the captives were clothed, fed, anointed, and
returned to Jericho. This stunning act of mercy from the northern
kingdom — often condemned in Chronicles — demonstrates that God's
prophetic word can produce compassion even among the apostate when
hearts are willing to listen. It also foreshadows Jesus' parable of
the Good Samaritan, where a despised northerner shows mercy.

The chapter's most damning phrase is verse 22: "And in the time of
his distress did he trespass yet more against the LORD: this is that
king Ahaz." Where righteous kings turned to God in distress (cf.
Manasseh, 2 Chronicles 33:12-13), Ahaz responded to suffering by
deepening his rebellion. Distress that should have driven him to
repentance instead drove him further into paganism. His reasoning
(v.23) represents the ultimate theological inversion: "Because the
gods of Damascus beat me, they must be powerful — I'll worship them."
He concluded that the gods who defeated him were stronger than the
God who was disciplining him. The chapter ends with Ahaz denied royal
burial — and the arrival of Hezekiah, whose great reform in chapter
29 will undo his father's comprehensive destruction.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Parallels 1-2 Kings but focuses exclusively on Judah (the southern kingdom). Jerusalem and the Temple are the theological center throughout.
- Key locations include various battle sites, reform locations, and high places destroyed or rebuilt by successive kings.
- The book ends with exile to Babylon (586 BC) and Cyrus's decree permitting return — the geographic arc moves from Jerusalem to Babylon and back.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.3  — "and he made offerings in the Valley of the Son of Hinnom and
        burned his sons as an offering, according to the abominations of
        the nations whom the LORD drove out before the people of Israel." (ESV)—
        The most abhorrent sin in the Old Testament — child sacrifice to
        Molech. The Valley of Hinnom ("ge-hinnom") later became
        "Gehenna" — Jesus' term for hell. Ahaz sacrificed his own
        children in the very valley that would become the Bible's symbol
        of eternal judgment.
v.19 — "For the LORD humbled Judah because of Ahaz king of Israel, for
        he had made Judah act sinfully and had been very unfaithful to
        the LORD." (ESV)— Note the Chronicler calls Ahaz "king of
        Israel" (not Judah) — a devastating theological statement. Ahaz
        had made Judah indistinguishable from apostate Israel in
        behavior, so the Chronicler strips him of Judah's name. He was
        king of "Israel" in the worst sense — following the northern
        kingdom's pattern of comprehensive Baal worship.
v.22 — "In the time of his distress he became yet more faithless to the
        LORD—this same King Ahaz." (ESV)— The editorial comment "this is
        that king Ahaz" is the Chronicler's expression of astonished
        disgust. Distress normally drives people toward God (cf. Psalm
        107); Ahaz uniquely responded to divine discipline with deeper
        rebellion. He is the anti-pattern of repentance.
v.24 — "And Ahaz gathered together the vessels of the house of God and
        cut in pieces the vessels of the house of God, and he shut up
        the doors of the house of the LORD, and he made himself altars
        in every corner of Jerusalem." (ESV)— The climactic act: closing
        the Temple. Every other wicked king added pagan worship
        alongside Temple worship. Ahaz alone shut down the Temple
        entirely — cutting off Judah's access to the God who had
        covenanted with David. This required Hezekiah's dramatic
        reopening in chapter 29.
v.15 — "And the men who have been mentioned by name rose and took the
        captives, and with the spoil they clothed all who were naked
        among them. They clothed them, gave them sandals, provided them
        with food and drink, and anointed them, and carrying all the
        feeble among them on donkeys, they brought them to their
        kinsfolk at Jericho, the city of palm trees. Then they returned
        to Samaria." (ESV)— A stunning oasis of compassion in a chapter
        of darkness. Northern Israelites — supposedly more apostate than
        Judah — showed mercy that Judah's own king would not. Obedience
        to prophetic word transcends national and tribal boundaries.
        CROSS-REFERENCES ------------------------------------
v.3  — Leviticus 18:21 — "Thou shalt not let any of thy seed pass
        through the fire to Molech." The specific prohibition Ahaz
        violated. Child sacrifice was the boundary sin that defined
        Canaanite religion — the very abomination for which God
        dispossessed the original inhabitants. Ahaz brought Israel
        down to the level of those nations God had judged.
v.9  — Luke 10:30-37 — The Good Samaritan parable. The Ephraimite
        leaders who clothed, fed, anointed, and transported the
        wounded Judean captives to Jericho prefigure Jesus' famous
        story. Both accounts feature despised northerners/Samaritans
        showing mercy that the "righteous" party failed to provide.
v.16 — Isaiah 7:1-9 — Isaiah's prophetic counsel during the same
        crisis. Isaiah urged Ahaz to trust God rather than Assyria:
        "If ye will not believe, surely ye shall not be established."
        Ahaz chose political alliance over divine promise — and both
        prophet and Chronicler record the disastrous consequences.
v.20 — Isaiah 8:7-8 — Isaiah predicted that the Assyrian "helper"
        would become an invader: "he shall overflow and go over, he
        shall reach even to the neck." The alliance Ahaz purchased
        became the instrument of Judah's future suffering — the
        very power invited to help would eventually threaten
        Jerusalem itself.
v.23 — Romans 1:21-23 — "When they knew God, they glorified him not
        as God... and changed the glory of the uncorruptible God
        into an image." Ahaz's theological reasoning — worshiping
        the gods that defeated him — mirrors the Romans 1 pattern:
        suppressing knowledge of the true God to pursue falsehood.
v.24 — 2 Chronicles 29:3 — Hezekiah's first act as king: "He in
        the first year of his reign, in the first month, opened
        the doors of the house of the LORD." What Ahaz closed,
        Hezekiah reopened. The depth of Ahaz's destruction measures
        the magnitude of Hezekiah's reform.


COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/2-chronicles-28/
  Guzik highlights the remarkable contrast between Ahaz's response
  to distress (deeper sin) and the biblical norm (repentance). He
  emphasizes the Oded episode as showing that even in the darkest
  national moment, God's prophetic word could produce compassion
  from unexpected sources. Guzik also notes that Ahaz's Assyrian
  alliance represents the recurring temptation to seek worldly
  power structures rather than divine provision when under pressure.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "'This is that king Ahaz' — the Holy Spirit sets a brand upon
  this wretch's name. He burned his children, closed God's house,
  set up altars to demons in every corner, and when God smote him
  for it, he sinned yet more. Here is a man whom affliction could
  not soften — whom mercy could not melt — whom judgment could not
  terrify. In all his distress he trespassed yet more. Let this
  stand as warning: it is possible so to harden the heart that
  even God's heaviest rod produces not penitence but defiance. The
  same sun that melts wax hardens clay. Distress is not salvation;
  it is only opportunity for salvation, and that opportunity can
  be forever wasted."

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.3  — KJV: "burnt his children in the fire" / ESV: "burned his
       sons as an offering" — Hebrew "va-yav'er eth-banav ba-esh"
       (and he caused his sons to pass through the fire). KJV's
       "children" (broader) vs. ESV's "sons" (more specific to
       "banav" — his sons). The Hebrew "he'evir ba-esh" (to cause
       to pass through fire) is the technical term for child
       sacrifice to Molech. ESV adds "as an offering" to clarify
       the sacrificial nature; KJV lets the phrase speak for itself.

v.15 — KJV: "the men which were expressed by name" / ESV: "the
       men who have been mentioned by name" — Hebrew "ha-anashim
       asher niqbu be-shemoth" (the men who were designated/
       specified by names). The four Ephraimite leaders named in
       v.12 are here recalled by their specific designation. KJV's
       archaic "expressed by name" means "specifically named/
       designated"; ESV clarifies with "mentioned by name."

v.19 — KJV: "Ahaz king of Israel" / ESV: "Ahaz king of Israel" —
       Both translations accurately render the Hebrew. The
       Chronicler deliberately calls Ahaz "king of Israel"
       instead of "king of Judah" — either a scribal error (some
       suggest) or, more likely, a theological statement: Ahaz
       behaved as the kings of apostate Israel, so the Chronicler
       gives him their title. This interpretation is supported by
       v.2: "he walked in the ways of the kings of Israel."

v.22 — KJV: "this is that king Ahaz" / ESV: "this same King Ahaz"
       — Hebrew "hu ha-melekh Achaz" (he is the king Ahaz). Both
       translations capture the demonstrative emphasis — the
       Chronicler points his finger, as it were, identifying Ahaz
       as uniquely and specifically guilty. This is editorial
       commentary unusual in Chronicles — the historian breaking
       his narrative voice to express moral judgment.

v.24 — KJV: "shut up the doors of the house of the LORD" / ESV:
       "shut up the doors of the house of the LORD" — Hebrew
       "va-yisgor eth-dalthoth beth-YHVH" (and he closed/locked
       the doors of the house of the LORD). Both render identically.
       "Sagar" means to close, shut, or lock — indicating complete
       termination of Temple worship, not mere restriction.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Valley of   — Hebrew "ge ben-Hinnom" (Valley of the son of Hinnom).
Hinnom        A valley south of Jerusalem where child sacrifices were
(Gehenna)     offered to Molech. Later defiled by Josiah (2 Kings
             23:10) and used as a garbage dump with perpetual fires.
             By Jesus' time, "Gehenna" (Greek form) became the term
             for hell — eternal judgment. Ahaz's abominations in
             this valley contributed to its association with divine
             wrath.

Molten       — Hebrew "massekoth" (cast/poured images). Metal idols
Images        formed by melting metal and pouring it into molds.
             Distinct from carved images ("pesel"), these required
             significant craft and expense — demonstrating Ahaz's
             deliberate investment in organized paganism, not casual
             syncretism.

Oded        — A prophet of the LORD stationed in Samaria (northern
             Israel) who rebuked the Israelite army for excessive
             violence against Judah. His intervention saved 200,000
             captives and represents one of the few positive
             prophetic episodes in the northern kingdom during this
             period. His name means "restorer" — fitting his role
             in restoring the captives to their homes.

Tilgath-    — Tiglath-pileser III, king of Assyria (745-727 BC).
pilneser      One of Assyria's greatest conquerors who established
             the neo-Assyrian empire. Ahaz voluntarily submitted to
             him and paid massive tribute from the Temple and royal
             treasuries — but received only oppression in return.
             The "helper" became an affliction (v.20).

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Distress is an opportunity for repentance, not an excuse for
   deeper rebellion (v.22). Ahaz responded to God's discipline by
   sinning more — the exact opposite of what suffering is designed
   to produce. When hardship comes, you face the same choice: will
   pain drive you toward God or away from Him? Suffering is not
   neutral — it either softens or hardens. Choose now how you will
   respond before the pressure comes.

2. Worldly alliances cannot substitute for divine protection
   (vv.16-21). Ahaz sought Assyria's help and received only
   further oppression. When you seek security in worldly systems
   — financial strategies without God, relationships without God,
   political power without God — you invite the very forces that
   will eventually oppress you. The "helper" you purchase apart
   from God becomes your next problem.

3. Closing the doors of worship is the final step of apostasy
   (v.24). Ahaz didn't merely add pagan worship — he eliminated
   access to the true God entirely. When you close the "doors"
   of your spiritual life — stopping prayer, abandoning Scripture,
   leaving fellowship — you remove your only means of return. Keep
   the doors open, even in your weakest seasons. A closed door
   requires someone else to reopen it (Hezekiah in chapter 29).

4. Compassion can come from unexpected sources (vv.9-15). The
   northern Israelites — condemned throughout Chronicles — showed
   more mercy than Judah's own king. Don't assume you know where
   God's compassion will manifest. The "wrong" people sometimes
   obey God's word more readily than the "right" people. And when
   God's word confronts you with someone else's need, respond like
   those Ephraimite leaders — immediately, practically, and
   thoroughly.

5. One person's unfaithfulness can corrupt an entire community
   (v.19). Ahaz "made Judah naked" — his personal sin stripped the
   nation of divine protection. Leadership carries this terrifying
   weight: your choices don't affect only you. Whether you lead a
   family, a ministry, or a workplace, your spiritual condition
   radiates outward. Guard your faithfulness not only for yourself
   but for everyone under your influence.
