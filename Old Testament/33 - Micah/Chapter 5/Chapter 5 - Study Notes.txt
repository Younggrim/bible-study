Micah - Chapter 5
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
From Bethlehem Ephrathah — though small among Judah's clans — will come a ruler whose origin is from of old, from ancient days; He will shepherd His flock in the strength of the LORD and be their peace.


AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Micah of Moresheth. Prophesied approximately 735-700 BC to both Israel and Judah. Contemporary with Isaiah. Key theme: social justice, true religion, and Messianic hope. Famous verses: 'But thou, Bethlehem Ephratah... out of thee shall he come forth' (5:2) and 'What doth the LORD require of thee, but to do justly, and to love mercy, and to walk humbly with thy God?' (6:8).

Classification: Messianic Prophecy — The Ruler from Bethlehem
Key Themes: The Bethlehem prophecy, Messiah's eternal origin, shepherd-king, peace, the remnant's dual nature, purification from idolatry

Historical Context: Chapter 5 contains one of the most significant Messianic prophecies in the entire Old Testament. Written approximately 700 years before Christ's birth, Micah identifies the tiny village of Bethlehem Ephrathah as the birthplace of Israel's ultimate Ruler—one whose origins are "from of old, from everlasting." This prophecy was so well-known that when the Magi came seeking the newborn King, the chief priests and scribes immediately quoted Micah 5:2 to identify Bethlehem as the location (Matthew 2:4-6).

The chapter opens with present humiliation—Israel's judge struck on the cheek (v. 1)—and immediately pivots to future glory through the Bethlehem-born Ruler. The contrast between the smallness of Bethlehem and the greatness of the One who comes from it is deliberate: God characteristically works through the small, the overlooked, and the unlikely.

After the Messianic prophecy, the chapter describes the Ruler's work: He will shepherd His people in divine strength (v. 4), He will be their peace (v. 5), and the remnant will function as both refreshing dew and conquering lion among the nations (vv. 7-8). The chapter concludes with God's purification of His people—removing all forms of false trust (military power, sorcery, idols) so that they depend on Him alone.

Structure:
- Present humiliation of Israel's ruler (v. 1)
- THE BETHLEHEM PROPHECY — The eternal Ruler's birthplace (v. 2)
- The interim period and the birth (v. 3)
- The Shepherd-King's reign and greatness (v. 4)
- He shall be their peace (vv. 5-6)
- The remnant as dew and as lion (vv. 7-9)
- Purification: removal of false securities (vv. 10-15)

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
*** VERSE 2 — THE BETHLEHEM PROPHECY ***
KJV: "But thou, Bethlehem Ephratah, though thou be little among the thousands of Judah, yet out of thee shall he come forth unto me that is to be ruler in Israel; whose goings forth have been from of old, from everlasting."
ESV: "But you, O Bethlehem Ephrathah, who are too little to be among the clans of Judah, from you shall come forth for me one who is to be ruler in Israel, whose coming forth is from of old, from ancient days."
Commentary: This is one of the most precisely fulfilled prophecies in Scripture. Key elements: (1) Bethlehem is identified as the birthplace—a village so small it wasn't even listed among Judah's cities in Joshua 15. (2) The Ruler comes forth "unto me"—He comes for God's purposes. (3) His "goings forth" are "from everlasting" (Hebrew: "mimei olam")—this is not merely an ancient lineage but eternal preexistence. The One born in Bethlehem existed before time itself. This verse establishes both the humanity (born in Bethlehem) and deity (from everlasting) of the Messiah. Matthew 2:4-6 records its fulfillment when the scribes directed the Magi to Bethlehem based on this very passage.

Verse 3 (KJV): "Therefore will he give them up, until the time that she which travaileth hath brought forth: then the remnant of his brethren shall return unto the children of Israel."
Commentary: Between the prophecy and its fulfillment lies an interim of divine silence—God "gives them up" until the appointed birth. "She which travaileth" points to the virgin birth (cf. Isaiah 7:14). After the birth, the regathering begins—"the remnant of his brethren shall return."

Verse 4 (KJV): "And he shall stand and feed in the strength of the LORD, in the majesty of the name of the LORD his God; and they shall abide: for now shall he be great unto the ends of the earth."
ESV: "And he shall stand and shepherd his flock in the strength of the LORD, in the majesty of the name of the LORD his God. And they shall dwell secure, for now he shall be great to the ends of the earth."
Commentary: The Ruler is a shepherd—He "feeds" (shepherds) His flock. His authority is not self-derived but exercised "in the strength of the LORD." His greatness extends to "the ends of the earth"—this is no mere local king but a universal sovereign. Under His care, His people "abide" (dwell secure).

Verse 5a (KJV): "And this man shall be the peace."
Commentary: He is not merely a peacemaker or peace-bringer—He IS the peace. His very person is the source of peace. This echoes Isaiah 9:6 ("Prince of Peace") and Paul's declaration in Ephesians 2:14: "He is our peace." Peace is not merely something He gives but something He embodies.

Verse 7 (KJV): "And the remnant of Jacob shall be in the midst of many people as a dew from the LORD, as the showers upon the grass, that tarrieth not for man, nor waiteth for the sons of men."
Commentary: The remnant's first function is to be refreshing, life-giving blessing to the nations—like dew that comes from God alone, independent of human effort. This is the calling of God's people: to be a source of divine refreshment wherever they are placed.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Matthew 2:4-6 — The scribes quote Micah 5:2 to identify Bethlehem as Messiah's birthplace for the Magi
John 7:42 — The crowd debates Jesus: "Hath not the scripture said, That Christ cometh... out of the town of Bethlehem?"
Isaiah 7:14 — "A virgin shall conceive"—the birth Micah refers to in "she which travaileth"
Isaiah 9:6-7 — "Unto us a child is born... Prince of Peace"—parallel Messianic prophecy of eternal rule
Luke 2:4-7 — The historical fulfillment: Joseph and Mary travel to Bethlehem for the birth of Jesus
John 10:11-14 — Jesus as the Good Shepherd—fulfillment of the shepherd imagery in v. 4
Ephesians 2:14 — "He is our peace"—Paul's echo of Micah 5:5
Genesis 35:19 — Rachel buried near Bethlehem Ephrathah—connecting the town to Israel's matriarchal history
Revelation 19:15 — The returning King who rules the nations—ultimate fulfillment of v. 4's "great unto the ends of the earth"

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. God works through smallness. Bethlehem was insignificant by every human measure, yet God chose it for His greatest work. Never despise small beginnings, small places, or small callings—God is not limited by size or status.

2. The Bethlehem prophecy establishes both Christ's humanity and deity in a single verse. He is genuinely human (born in a specific town) and genuinely eternal (His goings forth are from everlasting). This is the mystery of the incarnation—and the foundation of our salvation.

3. "This man shall be the peace." In a world of anxiety, conflict, and turmoil, peace is not found in circumstances, strategies, or escape—it is found in a Person. Do you know Him as your peace, or are you still seeking peace elsewhere?

4. The dual nature of the remnant (dew and lion, vv. 7-8) suggests that God's people are called to be both gentle blessing and confident strength. We are to refresh the world like dew while standing firm like a lion. These are not contradictions but complementary aspects of faithfulness.

5. God's purification (vv. 10-15) removes everything we might trust instead of Him—military might, superstition, crafted images. What "false securities" do you cling to? God's stripping is not punishment but preparation for deeper dependence on Him alone.
