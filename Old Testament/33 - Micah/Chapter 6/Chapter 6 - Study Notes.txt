Micah - Chapter 6
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
God brings a covenant lawsuit against Israel, asking what He has done to weary them, and declares what He truly requires: "to do justly, love mercy, and walk humbly with your God."


AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Micah of Moresheth. Prophesied approximately 735-700 BC to both Israel and Judah. Contemporary with Isaiah. Key theme: social justice, true religion, and Messianic hope. Famous verses: 'But thou, Bethlehem Ephratah... out of thee shall he come forth' (5:2) and 'What doth the LORD require of thee, but to do justly, and to love mercy, and to walk humbly with thy God?' (6:8).

Classification: Covenant Lawsuit — God's Case Against His People
Key Themes: God's controversy with Israel, true vs. false worship, the requirements of God, social corruption, futility curses, what God truly desires

Historical Context: Chapter 6 employs the literary form of a "covenant lawsuit" (Hebrew: rib)—a legal proceeding in which God brings His case against His covenant people. The mountains and foundations of the earth are summoned as witnesses (vv. 1-2), echoing the ancient Near Eastern practice of calling cosmic witnesses to treaty violations. This is not God as distant judge but as grieved covenant partner asking, "What have I done to you?"

God rehearses His saving acts—the exodus, the gift of Moses, Aaron, and Miriam, and the deliverance from Balak and Balaam (vv. 3-5). Against this backdrop of relentless grace, the people's unfaithfulness becomes inexcusable. The people then ask what they should bring to appease God—escalating from burnt offerings to thousands of rams to rivers of oil to even their firstborn children (vv. 6-7).

The answer comes in verse 8—one of the most famous and beloved verses in the entire Old Testament. God does not want more elaborate rituals or extreme sacrifices. He requires justice in dealings with others, mercy (hesed—covenant love) as a way of life, and humble walk with Him. This is the essence of true religion, stripped of all pretense and performance.

Structure:
- The covenant lawsuit summoned (vv. 1-2)
- God's case: remembering His gracious acts (vv. 3-5)
- The people's question: what does God want? (vv. 6-7)
- THE ANSWER: What the LORD requires (v. 8)
- Indictment of commercial dishonesty and violence (vv. 9-12)
- The sentence: futility curses (vv. 13-16)

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
Verse 3 (KJV): "O my people, what have I done unto thee? and wherein have I wearied thee? testify against me."
ESV: "O my people, what have I done to you? How have I wearied you? Answer me!"
Commentary: The tenderness of God's voice here is striking. He is not thundering in anger but pleading with broken-hearted love. "What have I done to you?" is not defensive—it is an invitation to examine whether God has ever failed them. The implied answer is devastating in its silence: God has done nothing but good.

Verse 6-7 (KJV): "Wherewith shall I come before the LORD, and bow myself before the high God? shall I come before him with burnt offerings, with calves of a year old? Will the LORD be pleased with thousands of rams, or with ten thousands of rivers of oil? shall I give my firstborn for my transgression, the fruit of my body for the sin of my soul?"
Commentary: The escalation reveals a fundamental misunderstanding of God. The people assume God wants more—more expensive sacrifices, more extreme devotion, more costly offerings. The final suggestion—child sacrifice—shows how far pagan thinking had corrupted Israel's theology. They had confused the character of YHWH with the demands of Molech.

*** VERSE 8 — WHAT THE LORD REQUIRES ***
KJV: "He hath shewed thee, O man, what is good; and what doth the LORD require of thee, but to do justly, and to love mercy, and to walk humbly with thy God?"
ESV: "He has told you, O man, what is good; and what does the LORD require of you but to do justice, and to love kindness, and to walk humbly with your God?"
Commentary: This verse is often called the summary of Old Testament ethics and one of the most important verses in all of prophetic literature. Three requirements, each building on the last: (1) DO JUSTLY — right action in human relationships, fairness, integrity, giving others what is due; (2) LOVE MERCY — not merely practice mercy reluctantly but love it, delight in hesed (covenant faithfulness/lovingkindness); (3) WALK HUMBLY WITH THY GOD — ongoing relationship characterized by humility, recognizing one's need for God. Note the progression: outward conduct (justice), inward disposition (love mercy), upward relationship (walk with God). This is not an alternative to faith but its expression. Jesus affirmed these as "the weightier matters of the law" (Matthew 23:23).

Verse 8 (additional note): The word "require" (Hebrew: doresh) means "to seek" or "to demand." God is not asking for something optional—this is His non-negotiable expectation. Yet compared to thousands of rams or rivers of oil, these requirements are accessible to every person. God's demands are not about wealth or grand gestures but about character and relationship.

Verse 11 (KJV): "Shall I count them pure with the wicked balances, and with the bag of deceitful weights?"
Commentary: After stating what He requires, God exposes what they actually practice. Dishonest business dealings—rigged scales, false weights—reveal hearts that do the opposite of justice. Their worship and their commerce tell completely different stories about their character.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
"Controversy" (KJV) vs. "indictment" (ESV) — Hebrew: "rib." This is the technical term for a legal dispute or lawsuit. In prophetic literature, the "rib pattern" is a covenant lawsuit where God prosecutes His people for treaty violations, with creation as witness.

"Mercy" (KJV) vs. "kindness" (ESV) — Hebrew: "hesed." This is one of the richest words in the Hebrew Bible. It encompasses covenant loyalty, steadfast love, lovingkindness, mercy, and faithfulness all at once. Neither English translation fully captures it. To "love hesed" is to find delight in covenant faithfulness—both receiving it from God and extending it to others.

"Walk humbly" — Hebrew: "hatsnea lechet." The word "hatsnea" appears only here in the Old Testament. It carries the sense of modesty, circumspection, and careful attentiveness. To walk "hatsnea" with God is to walk carefully, modestly, and with full awareness of who He is and who you are.

"The statutes of Omri" (v. 16) — Omri was the father of Ahab and founder of a dynasty notorious for introducing systematic Baal worship and commercial exploitation to Israel (1 Kings 16:25-26). To follow "the statutes of Omri" means to embrace a system of governance based on pagan values rather than covenant faithfulness.

"Hissing" (v. 16) — Hebrew: "shreqah." This describes the horrified whistle or gasp of passersby who see a devastated city. It is an expression of shock and contempt—the ruined city becomes a cautionary tale for others.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. God's question "What have I done unto thee?" invites personal inventory. When we drift from God, it is never because He has failed us. Take time to rehearse God's faithfulness in your own life—His "exodus moments" of deliverance and provision.

2. We often make the same mistake as Israel in vv. 6-7—assuming God wants more activity, more programs, more religious performance. But God's requirements are relational and ethical, not ritualistic. He wants who you are, not just what you do.

3. Micah 6:8 is a daily checklist for the Christian life: Am I dealing justly with others today? Am I choosing mercy when I could choose harshness? Am I walking attentively with God rather than rushing ahead on my own?

4. The connection between worship and ethics is inseparable. Verses 6-8 (what God requires in worship) flow directly into verses 9-12 (how they cheat in business). You cannot separate Sunday worship from Monday conduct. Integrity means integration—the same person in every setting.

5. "He hath shewed thee" — God's requirements are not hidden or mysterious. He has already revealed what He wants. The problem is never lack of information but lack of obedience. What has God clearly shown you that you are hesitating to do?
