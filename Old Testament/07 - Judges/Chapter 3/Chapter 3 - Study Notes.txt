Judges - Chapter 3
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
God leaves Canaanite nations to test Israel, and Israel intermarries with them and serves their gods. Othniel delivers Israel from Mesopotamia, Ehud assassinates the Moabite king Eglon, and Shamgar strikes down 600 Philistines.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Unknown, traditionally attributed to Samuel. Judges covers
approximately 350 years (1380-1050 BC) between Joshua's death and the
rise of the monarchy. Hebrew title: 'Shophetim' — 'Judges' (deliverers/
rulers raised by God). The book records Israel's repeated cycle: sin →
oppression → crying out → deliverance → rest → sin again. The refrain:
'In those days there was no king in Israel: every man did that which was
right in his own eyes' (17:6, 21:25). The book demonstrates humanity's
need for a righteous king — ultimately fulfilled in Christ.

Historical Context: Chapter 3 presents the first three judges of Israel:
Othniel, Ehud, and Shamgar. Othniel serves as the "model judge" — his
narrative follows the cycle perfectly and concisely: sin (v.7), oppression
(v.8), crying out (v.9), deliverance (v.9-10), rest (v.11). Every
subsequent judge narrative is measured against this template. Ehud's story
is told in vivid, almost humorous detail — a left-handed Benjamite who
assassinates the obese Moabite king Eglon with a hidden dagger. Shamgar
receives only a single verse but his feat is remarkable: 600 Philistines
killed with an ox goad. The chapter also completes the theological
introduction by listing the nations God left to test Israel (vv.1-6) and
recording Israel's intermarriage with the Canaanites — the very thing God
had forbidden (Deuteronomy 7:3-4).

Nations Left to Test Israel (vv.1-6): God left nations for two purposes:
(1) to teach the new generation war (v.2) — they needed to learn
dependence on God in battle, and (2) to test whether Israel would obey
(v.4). The nations: five lords of the Philistines, Canaanites, Sidonians,
and Hivites of Lebanon (v.3). Israel's response to the test: total
failure. They "dwelt among" the nations (v.5), intermarried with them
(v.6), and "served their gods" (v.6). The progression: proximity →
intermarriage → idolatry. This is exactly what Deuteronomy 7:3-4 warned
would happen.

Othniel — The Model Judge (vv.7-11): Israel "did evil... and forgat the
LORD" and served Baal and the Asherah poles (v.7). God "sold them" to
Cushan-rishathaim king of Mesopotamia for eight years (v.8). Israel cried
out; God raised Othniel (v.9) — Caleb's nephew, already introduced in
1:13 as a man of valor. "The Spirit of the LORD came upon him" (v.10) —
the empowerment for the task. He judged Israel, went to war, and prevailed
(v.10). The land rested forty years (v.11). Othniel's narrative is the
template: every element of the cycle is present in its purest form.

Ehud — The Left-Handed Assassin (vv.12-30): Israel sins again (v.12).
God strengthens Eglon king of Moab, who allies with Ammon and Amalek and
conquers "the city of palm trees" (Jericho) (v.13). Eighteen years of
servitude (v.14). Israel cries out; God raises Ehud — "a man lefthanded"
(v.15). Literally "restricted in his right hand" — possibly meaning his
right hand was bound or disabled, or simply that he was naturally
left-handed. This "weakness" becomes his weapon: guards would check the
left thigh for weapons, but Ehud hides his cubit-long dagger on his RIGHT
thigh (v.16). He presents tribute to Eglon — described as "a very fat
man" (v.17). After sending the tribute-bearers away, Ehud returns alone
claiming "a message from God" (v.20). When Eglon rises, Ehud thrusts the
dagger into his belly with his left hand — the fat closes over the blade
(vv.21-22). He locks the doors and escapes while servants assume Eglon is
"covering his feet" (relieving himself) (v.24). Ehud rallies Israel,
seizes the fords of Jordan, and kills 10,000 Moabites (v.29). Eighty
years of rest follow (v.30).

Shamgar — One Verse, One Ox Goad (v.31): Shamgar son of Anath kills 600
Philistines with an ox goad — a farming implement about 8 feet long with
a metal tip. He "also delivered Israel." His brevity in the text does not
diminish his significance: God uses unlikely weapons and unlikely people.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Othniel: Delivers the southern tribes from Cushan-rishathaim of Mesopotamia (far northeast — Aram-naharaim)
- Ehud: A Benjamite who delivers Israel from Moab; the action centers on Jericho ("city of palm trees") and the fords of the Jordan
- Shamgar: Operates in the southwestern coastal region against the Philistines

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.7  — "And the people of Israel did what was evil in the sight of the
        LORD. They forgot the LORD their God and served the Baals and the
        Asheroth." (ESV) — The cycle begins: forgetting God leads to
        serving idols. Memory is the first casualty of apostasy.

v.9  — "But when the people of Israel cried out to the LORD, the LORD
        raised up a deliverer for the people of Israel, who saved them,
        Othniel the son of Kenaz." (ESV) — The pattern of grace: they cry,
        God delivers. Not because they deserve it but because God is
        merciful.

v.10 — "The Spirit of the LORD was upon him, and he judged Israel. He
        went out to war." (ESV) — The Spirit's empowerment is essential.
        No judge delivers in his own strength. The Spirit equips for the
        task God assigns.

v.15 — "The LORD raised up for them a deliverer, Ehud, the son of Gera,
        the Benjaminite, a left-handed man." (ESV) — God chooses the
        unlikely. A left-handed man in a right-handed world — his
        "limitation" becomes his advantage. God uses what the world
        overlooks.

v.20 — "And Ehud said, 'I have a message from God for you.'" (ESV) —
        Ironic and deadly. The "message" is a dagger. Yet there is
        theological truth: God's judgment IS His message to the wicked.
        Eglon received God's word — in steel.

v.31 — "After him was Shamgar the son of Anath, who killed 600 of the
        Philistines with an oxgoad, and he also saved Israel." (ESV) —
        God does not need conventional weapons. An ox goad, a jawbone,
        a sling — God's power is displayed through inadequate instruments.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/judges-3/
  Guzik notes that Othniel is the "ideal judge" — his story contains
  every element of the cycle in perfect form. He highlights the humor in
  the Ehud narrative (the servants' embarrassment, the graphic details)
  as characteristic of Hebrew storytelling that mocks Israel's enemies.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "Ehud had a message from God for the king of Moab — and he delivered
  it with his left hand. God's messages come in unexpected ways. Sometimes
  His word is comfort; sometimes it is a sword. The wicked who will not
  hear God's word of grace will feel God's word of judgment. Better to
  receive His message on your knees than in your belly."
TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.2  — Hebrew "milchamah" — war/battle.
       KJV: "to teach them war"
       ESV: "that he might teach war to those who had not known it before"
       ASV: "to teach them war"
       NET: "he wanted to teach the subsequent generations of Israelites...how to conduct holy war"
       WEB: "to teach them war"
       Analysis: NET uniquely adds "holy war" — Hebrew simply says
       "milchamah" (war/battle). This is an interpretive addition — NET
       clarifies the theological dimension that Israel's warfare was
       divinely sanctioned, but the Hebrew text does not include the word
       "holy." KJV/ASV/WEB retain the concise Hebrew form.

v.7  — Hebrew "ha-Be'alim v'ha-Asherot" — the Baals and the Asherah
       poles.
       KJV: "served Baalim and the groves"
       ESV: "served the Baals and the Asheroth"
       ASV: "served the Baalim and the Asheroth"
       NET: "worshiped the Baals and the Asherahs"
       WEB: "served the Baals and the Asheroth"
       Analysis: KJV translates "Asherot" as "groves" because Asherah
       poles were wooden pillars set up in groves. ESV/ASV/WEB retain the
       proper name. NET uses "worshiped" instead of "served" (Hebrew
       "avad" can mean both). The Asherah was a Canaanite goddess; her
       worship symbol was a wooden pole or tree.

v.8  — Hebrew "Kushan-Rish'atayim" — Cushan of double wickedness.
       KJV: "Chushan-rishathaim king of Mesopotamia"
       ESV: "Cushan-rishathaim king of Mesopotamia"
       ASV: "Cushan-rishathaim king of Mesopotamia"
       NET: "King Cushan-Rishathaim of Aram-Naharaim"
       WEB: "Cushan Rishathaim king of Mesopotamia"
       Analysis: The name likely means "Cushan of double wickedness" —
       possibly a derogatory Hebrew nickname. NET retains "Aram-Naharaim"
       (Hebrew: "Aram of the two rivers" — Tigris and Euphrates) while
       others translate it as "Mesopotamia." NET's retention of the
       Hebrew place name is more precise geographically.

v.9  — Hebrew "moshia" — savior/deliverer.
       KJV: "the LORD raised up a deliverer"
       ESV: "the LORD raised up a deliverer for the people of Israel"
       ASV: "Jehovah raised up a saviour to the children of Israel"
       NET: "he raised up a deliverer for the Israelites"
       WEB: "Yahweh raised up a savior to the children of Israel"
       Analysis: ASV and WEB use "saviour/savior" (stronger theological
       term connecting to Christ as ultimate Savior); NET/KJV/ESV use
       "deliverer." The Hebrew term "moshia" is the same root as "Yeshua/
       Joshua/Jesus" — "one who saves." The choice between "savior" and
       "deliverer" carries significant theological weight.

v.10 — Hebrew "vattehi alav ruach Adonai" — the Spirit of the LORD was
       upon him.
       KJV: "the Spirit of the LORD came upon him"
       ESV: "The Spirit of the LORD was upon him"
       ASV: "the Spirit of Jehovah came upon him"
       NET: "The Lord's Spirit empowered him"
       WEB: "Yahweh's Spirit came on him"
       Analysis: NET interprets "came upon" as "empowered" — adding
       functional explanation. The Hebrew "vattehi alav" (was upon him)
       is more mysterious; NET makes the purpose explicit but loses the
       sense of divine presence resting on a person. The Spirit's
       empowerment is essential — no judge delivers in his own strength.

v.15 — Hebrew "itter yad-y'mino" — restricted/bound of his right hand.
       KJV: "a man lefthanded"
       ESV: "a left-handed man"
       ASV: "a man left-handed"
       NET: "a left-handed man"
       WEB: "a left-handed man"
       Analysis: Literally "restricted/bound of his right hand." This
       could mean naturally left-handed, or that his right hand was
       disabled. The tribe of Benjamin (meaning "son of the right hand")
       ironically produces left-handed warriors (cf. 20:16). All
       translations simplify the Hebrew idiom to "left-handed."

v.19 — Hebrew "ha-p'silim" — graven images/idols.
       KJV: "the quarries that were by Gilgal"
       ESV: "the idols near Gilgal"
       ASV: "the quarries that were by Gilgal"
       NET: "the carved images at Gilgal"
       WEB: "the stone idols that were by Gilgal"
       Analysis: "Pesel" typically means "graven image/idol." KJV/ASV's
       "quarries" follows an older interpretation (carved stones). ESV's
       "idols" is the standard meaning. WEB says "stone idols"; NET says
       "carved images." The context — Ehud turns back at these objects,
       perhaps motivated by righteous anger at pagan images near Israel's
       sacred site — supports the "idols" reading.

v.20 — Hebrew "d'var-Elohim" — a word/message from God.
       KJV: "I have a message from God for you"
       ESV: "I have a message from God for you"
       ASV: "I have a message from God unto thee"
       NET: "I have a message from God for you"
       WEB: "I have a message from God for you"
       Analysis: All translations render this identically in substance.
       The Hebrew "d'var-sether" (v.19) = "a word of secrecy." The deadly
       irony is preserved across all versions — the "message" is a
       dagger. God's judgment IS His message to the wicked.

v.22 — Hebrew "ha-parshedonah" — excrement/intestinal contents.
       KJV: "the dirt came out"
       ESV: "the dung came out"
       ASV: "the dirt came out"
       NET: "the fecal matter came out"
       WEB: "the handle also went in after the blade; and the fat closed upon the blade"
       Analysis: This rare word likely refers to excrement. KJV/ASV
       euphemize with "dirt"; ESV uses "dung"; NET is most explicit with
       "fecal matter." The graphic detail serves to humiliate Eglon and
       mock the enemy — a common feature of ancient Near Eastern victory
       narratives.

v.24 — Hebrew "mesikh et-raglav" — covering his feet.
       KJV: "he covereth his feet in his summer chamber"
       ESV: "he is relieving himself in the closet of the cool chamber"
       ASV: "he is covering his feet in the upper chamber"
       NET: "He must be relieving himself in the well-ventilated inner room"
       WEB: "Surely he is covering his feet in the upper room"
       Analysis: "Covering his feet" is a euphemism for using the toilet
       (the robe would cover the feet when squatting). ESV/NET make the
       euphemism explicit; KJV/ASV/WEB retain the literal idiom. NET also
       adds "well-ventilated" (interpreting Hebrew "maqerah" as referring
       to the room's coolness/ventilation).

v.29 — Hebrew "kol-shamen v'kol-ish chayil" — every robust man and
       every man of valor.
       KJV: "all lusty, and all men of valour"
       ESV: "all strong, able-bodied men"
       ASV: "every lusty man, and every man of valor"
       NET: "all strong, capable warriors"
       WEB: "every strong man, and every man of valor"
       Analysis: ASV/KJV's "lusty" (archaic for "robust/vigorous")
       corresponds to "shamen" (fat/robust). The Hebrew literally means
       "fat/well-fed" — implying well-provisioned soldiers. WEB uses
       "strong"; NET combines both terms into "strong, capable warriors."

v.30 — Hebrew "sh'monim shanah" — eighty years.
       KJV: "the land had rest fourscore years"
       ESV: "the land had rest eighty years"
       ASV: "the land had rest fourscore years"
       NET: "the land was undisturbed for eighty years"
       WEB: "the land had rest eighty years"
       Analysis: KJV/ASV use archaic "fourscore" (4 × 20 = 80). NET's
       "undisturbed" interprets the Hebrew "shaqat" (to be quiet, at rest)
       with a slightly different nuance than "rest." All agree on the
       duration.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Othniel        — "God is my strength/lion of God." Son of Kenaz, Caleb's
               younger brother (or nephew). First judge of Israel. The
               model deliverer — empowered by the Spirit, victorious in
               battle, bringing rest to the land. Previously won Achsah
               as wife by capturing Kiriath-sepher (1:13).

Ehud           — "Strong/united." Son of Gera, from the tribe of
               Benjamin. A left-handed man who assassinated Eglon king
               of Moab. His story is told with vivid, almost comic
               detail — God's deliverance through an unlikely hero using
               an unexpected advantage.

Eglon          — "Calf-like/round." King of Moab described as "very fat."
               He oppressed Israel for 18 years from "the city of palm
               trees" (Jericho). His name and description may be
               deliberately mocking — he is like a fattened calf ready
               for slaughter.

Shamgar        — Son of Anath (possibly indicating he was from
               Beth-anath, or that his family served the goddess Anath).
               Killed 600 Philistines with an ox goad. Mentioned again
               in Deborah's song (5:6). A farmer-warrior who delivered
               Israel with a farming tool.

Ox Goad        — A long wooden pole (6-8 feet) with a metal point on one
               end for prodding oxen and a flat blade on the other for
               cleaning the plow. An agricultural implement turned weapon
               — demonstrating that God's power does not depend on
               conventional military equipment.

Cubit          — A unit of measurement approximately 18 inches (the
               length from elbow to fingertip). Ehud's dagger was "a
               cubit length" — about 18 inches, short enough to conceal
               but long enough to be lethal.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. God uses unlikely instruments (vv.15,31). A left-handed man. An ox
   goad. God consistently chooses what the world overlooks or despises.
   Our "limitations" may be God's chosen instruments. What we consider
   our weakness, God may consider His opportunity (2 Corinthians 12:9).

2. The Spirit empowers for the task (v.10). Othniel did not deliver
   Israel in his own strength — "the Spirit of the LORD came upon him."
   Every act of genuine spiritual service requires the Spirit's
   empowerment. Natural ability is insufficient for supernatural work.

3. Intermarriage leads to idolatry (v.6). The progression is predictable:
   living among pagans → marrying pagans → serving pagan gods. Intimate
   relationships with those who reject God inevitably pull the heart away
   from God. This is why Paul warns against being "unequally yoked"
   (2 Corinthians 6:14).

4. "I have a message from God for you" (v.20). Ehud's words are
   simultaneously literal and ironic. God does have a message for the
   wicked — and sometimes that message is judgment. Those who refuse
   God's word of grace will eventually receive God's word of judgment.
   Better to hear Him in mercy than to feel Him in wrath.

5. The cycle repeats because the heart does not change (vv.7,12). Othniel
   delivers — and within a generation, Israel sins again. External
   deliverance without internal transformation produces only temporary
   relief. This is why the judges are insufficient — Israel needs not
   just a deliverer but a new heart (Ezekiel 36:26). The book points
   forward to Christ who delivers AND transforms.
