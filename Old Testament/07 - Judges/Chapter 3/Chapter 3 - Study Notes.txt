Judges - Chapter 3
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
God leaves Canaanite nations to test Israel, and Israel intermarries with them and serves their gods. Othniel delivers Israel from Mesopotamia, Ehud assassinates the Moabite king Eglon, and Shamgar strikes down 600 Philistines.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Unknown, traditionally attributed to Samuel. Judges covers
approximately 350 years (1380-1050 BC) between Joshua's death and the
rise of the monarchy. Hebrew title: 'Shophetim' — 'Judges' (deliverers/
rulers raised by God). The book records Israel's repeated cycle: sin →
oppression → crying out → deliverance → rest → sin again. The refrain:
'In those days there was no king in Israel: every man did that which was
right in his own eyes' (17:6, 21:25). The book demonstrates humanity's
need for a righteous king — ultimately fulfilled in Christ.

Historical Context: Chapter 3 presents the first three judges of Israel:
Othniel, Ehud, and Shamgar. Othniel serves as the "model judge" — his
narrative follows the cycle perfectly and concisely: sin (v.7), oppression
(v.8), crying out (v.9), deliverance (v.9-10), rest (v.11). Every
subsequent judge narrative is measured against this template. Ehud's story
is told in vivid, almost humorous detail — a left-handed Benjamite who
assassinates the obese Moabite king Eglon with a hidden dagger. Shamgar
receives only a single verse but his feat is remarkable: 600 Philistines
killed with an ox goad. The chapter also completes the theological
introduction by listing the nations God left to test Israel (vv.1-6) and
recording Israel's intermarriage with the Canaanites — the very thing God
had forbidden (Deuteronomy 7:3-4).

Nations Left to Test Israel (vv.1-6): God left nations for two purposes:
(1) to teach the new generation war (v.2) — they needed to learn
dependence on God in battle, and (2) to test whether Israel would obey
(v.4). The nations: five lords of the Philistines, Canaanites, Sidonians,
and Hivites of Lebanon (v.3). Israel's response to the test: total
failure. They "dwelt among" the nations (v.5), intermarried with them
(v.6), and "served their gods" (v.6). The progression: proximity →
intermarriage → idolatry. This is exactly what Deuteronomy 7:3-4 warned
would happen.

Othniel — The Model Judge (vv.7-11): Israel "did evil... and forgat the
LORD" and served Baal and the Asherah poles (v.7). God "sold them" to
Cushan-rishathaim king of Mesopotamia for eight years (v.8). Israel cried
out; God raised Othniel (v.9) — Caleb's nephew, already introduced in
1:13 as a man of valor. "The Spirit of the LORD came upon him" (v.10) —
the empowerment for the task. He judged Israel, went to war, and prevailed
(v.10). The land rested forty years (v.11). Othniel's narrative is the
template: every element of the cycle is present in its purest form.

Ehud — The Left-Handed Assassin (vv.12-30): Israel sins again (v.12).
God strengthens Eglon king of Moab, who allies with Ammon and Amalek and
conquers "the city of palm trees" (Jericho) (v.13). Eighteen years of
servitude (v.14). Israel cries out; God raises Ehud — "a man lefthanded"
(v.15). Literally "restricted in his right hand" — possibly meaning his
right hand was bound or disabled, or simply that he was naturally
left-handed. This "weakness" becomes his weapon: guards would check the
left thigh for weapons, but Ehud hides his cubit-long dagger on his RIGHT
thigh (v.16). He presents tribute to Eglon — described as "a very fat
man" (v.17). After sending the tribute-bearers away, Ehud returns alone
claiming "a message from God" (v.20). When Eglon rises, Ehud thrusts the
dagger into his belly with his left hand — the fat closes over the blade
(vv.21-22). He locks the doors and escapes while servants assume Eglon is
"covering his feet" (relieving himself) (v.24). Ehud rallies Israel,
seizes the fords of Jordan, and kills 10,000 Moabites (v.29). Eighty
years of rest follow (v.30).

Shamgar — One Verse, One Ox Goad (v.31): Shamgar son of Anath kills 600
Philistines with an ox goad — a farming implement about 8 feet long with
a metal tip. He "also delivered Israel." His brevity in the text does not
diminish his significance: God uses unlikely weapons and unlikely people.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Othniel: Delivers the southern tribes from Cushan-rishathaim of Mesopotamia (far northeast — Aram-naharaim)
- Ehud: A Benjamite who delivers Israel from Moab; the action centers on Jericho ("city of palm trees") and the fords of the Jordan
- Shamgar: Operates in the southwestern coastal region against the Philistines

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/judges-3/
  Guzik notes that Othniel is the "ideal judge" — his story contains
  every element of the cycle in perfect form. He highlights the humor in
  the Ehud narrative (the servants' embarrassment, the graphic details)
  as characteristic of Hebrew storytelling that mocks Israel's enemies.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "Ehud had a message from God for the king of Moab — and he delivered
  it with his left hand. God's messages come in unexpected ways. Sometimes
  His word is comfort; sometimes it is a sword. The wicked who will not
  hear God's word of grace will feel God's word of judgment. Better to
  receive His message on your knees than in your belly."
REFLECTION
------------------------------------
1. God uses unlikely instruments (vv.15,31). A left-handed man. An ox
   goad. God consistently chooses what the world overlooks or despises.
   Our "limitations" may be God's chosen instruments. What we consider
   our weakness, God may consider His opportunity (2 Corinthians 12:9).

2. The Spirit empowers for the task (v.10). Othniel did not deliver
   Israel in his own strength — "the Spirit of the LORD came upon him."
   Every act of genuine spiritual service requires the Spirit's
   empowerment. Natural ability is insufficient for supernatural work.

3. Intermarriage leads to idolatry (v.6). The progression is predictable:
   living among pagans → marrying pagans → serving pagan gods. Intimate
   relationships with those who reject God inevitably pull the heart away
   from God. This is why Paul warns against being "unequally yoked"
   (2 Corinthians 6:14).

4. "I have a message from God for you" (v.20). Ehud's words are
   simultaneously literal and ironic. God does have a message for the
   wicked — and sometimes that message is judgment. Those who refuse
   God's word of grace will eventually receive God's word of judgment.
   Better to hear Him in mercy than to feel Him in wrath.

5. The cycle repeats because the heart does not change (vv.7,12). Othniel
   delivers — and within a generation, Israel sins again. External
   deliverance without internal transformation produces only temporary
   relief. This is why the judges are insufficient — Israel needs not
   just a deliverer but a new heart (Ezekiel 36:26). The book points
   forward to Christ who delivers AND transforms.
