Judges - Chapter 8
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Gideon pursues and captures the Midianite kings Zebah and Zalmunna, punishing the towns that refused to help. He declines the offer of kingship but makes a golden ephod that becomes a snare of idolatry for Israel.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Unknown, traditionally attributed to Samuel. Judges covers
approximately 350 years (1380-1050 BC) between Joshua's death and the
rise of the monarchy. Hebrew title: 'Shophetim' — 'Judges' (deliverers/
rulers raised by God). The book records Israel's repeated cycle: sin →
oppression → crying out → deliverance → rest → sin again. The refrain:
'In those days there was no king in Israel: every man did that which was
right in his own eyes' (17:6, 21:25). The book demonstrates humanity's
need for a righteous king — ultimately fulfilled in Christ.

Historical Context: Chapter 8 concludes the Gideon narrative with a
complex portrait of a flawed hero. The chapter records: Ephraim's
complaint and Gideon's diplomatic response (vv.1-3), the pursuit of the
Midianite kings Zebah and Zalmunna (vv.4-12), Gideon's punishment of
Succoth and Penuel for refusing aid (vv.13-17), the execution of the
Midianite kings (vv.18-21), Gideon's noble refusal of kingship (vv.22-23),
and his tragic creation of a golden ephod that becomes an idol (vv.24-27).
The chapter reveals the complexity of the judges: Gideon rightly refuses
the crown ("the LORD shall rule over you") but then lives like a king
(many wives, 70 sons, a concubine in Shechem). He refuses the title but
assumes the lifestyle. His ephod — perhaps intended as a memorial — becomes
"a snare unto Gideon, and to his house" (v.27). The chapter ends with
Gideon's death and Israel's immediate return to Baal worship (vv.33-35).

Ephraim's Complaint (vv.1-3): Ephraim — the dominant tribe — is angry
that Gideon did not call them to the initial battle (v.1). Gideon's
response is masterful diplomacy: "Is not the gleaning of the grapes of
Ephraim better than the vintage of Abi-ezer?" (v.2). Translation: your
minor role (capturing Oreb and Zeeb) was greater than my major role.
Flattery defuses the conflict (v.3). Compare this with Jephthah's
handling of the same tribe in 12:1-6 — very different outcome.

Faint Yet Pursuing (vv.4-12): Gideon and his 300 cross the Jordan,
"faint, yet pursuing" (v.4) — exhausted but relentless. He requests bread
from Succoth and Penuel — Israelite cities east of Jordan — but both
refuse, fearing Midianite retaliation (vv.5-9). Gideon promises
retribution and continues the pursuit. He finds the remaining 15,000
Midianites at Karkor (v.10) — all that remain of 135,000 (120,000 already
fallen, v.10). He attacks and captures the kings Zebah and Zalmunna (v.12).

Punishment of Succoth and Penuel (vv.13-17): Gideon returns and fulfills
his threats: he "teaches" Succoth's elders with thorns and briers (v.16)
and breaks down Penuel's tower, killing the men (v.17). This punishment
is severe — these were Israelite cities that refused to support God's
deliverer. Their neutrality was treated as treason.

The Kings' Execution (vv.18-21): Gideon reveals a personal motive: Zebah
and Zalmunna had killed his brothers at Tabor (v.18-19). He offers his
firstborn son Jether the honor of executing them, but the youth is afraid
(v.20). The kings taunt: "as the man is, so is his strength" — asking for
a warrior's death (v.21). Gideon kills them himself.

Refusal of Kingship (vv.22-23): Israel offers Gideon dynastic rule: "Rule
thou over us, both thou, and thy son, and thy son's son" (v.22). Gideon's
response is theologically perfect: "I will not rule over you, neither
shall my son rule over you: the LORD shall rule over you" (v.23). This is
the correct answer — God is Israel's king. Yet Gideon's subsequent
behavior contradicts his words.

The Ephod — A Snare (vv.24-27): Gideon requests gold earrings from the
spoil — 1,700 shekels (approximately 43 pounds of gold) (v.26). He makes
"an ephod" and places it in Ophrah (v.27). The ephod was a priestly
garment used for divine inquiry — but Gideon was not a priest, and Ophrah
was not the tabernacle. "All Israel went thither a whoring after it:
which thing became a snare unto Gideon, and to his house" (v.27). Good
intentions do not sanctify unauthorized worship.

Gideon's Later Life and Death (vv.28-35): Forty years of rest (v.28).
Gideon has 70 sons and many wives — living like a king despite refusing
the title (v.30). His concubine in Shechem bears Abimelech — whose name
means "my father is king" (v.31). After Gideon's death, Israel
immediately returns to Baal worship and shows no loyalty to Gideon's
family (vv.33-35).

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Pursuit of Midian: Eastward across the Jordan into Transjordan — through Succoth and Penuel (in Gad's territory)
- Karkor: Far east in the desert where Gideon catches the fleeing Midianite kings Zebah and Zalmunna
- Ophrah: Where Gideon returns, makes the golden ephod, and is buried — his hometown in Manasseh

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.4  — "Faint, yet pursuing." — Three words that capture the essence of
        faithful perseverance. Exhausted but not quitting. Weak but still
        obedient. This is the Christian life: pressing on despite
        weariness.

v.23 — "I will not rule over you, and my son will not rule over you; the
        LORD will rule over you." (ESV) — Gideon's finest moment. The
        correct theological answer: God alone is Israel's king. No human
        deliverer should replace divine sovereignty.

v.27 — "And Gideon made an ephod of it and put it in his city, in
        Ophrah. And all Israel whored after it there, and it became a
        snare to Gideon and to his family." (ESV) — Gideon's worst
        moment. A memorial becomes an idol. Good intentions produce
        spiritual disaster when they bypass God's prescribed worship.

v.33 — "As soon as Gideon died, the people of Israel turned again and
        whored after the Baals." (ESV) — The cycle resumes immediately.
        External deliverance without internal transformation produces
        only temporary faithfulness.

v.34 — "And the people of Israel did not remember the LORD their God, who
        had delivered them from the hand of all their enemies." (ESV) —
        Forgetfulness is the root of apostasy. They forgot God AND they
        forgot Gideon's family (v.35). Ingratitude toward God produces
        ingratitude toward His servants.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/judges-8/
  Guzik notes the tragic irony: Gideon refuses the crown but lives like
  a king. He refuses the title but assumes the privileges. The ephod
  parallels the golden calf — gold from spoil fashioned into an object
  of false worship. Gideon's story shows that even great faith can end
  in compromise.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "'The LORD shall rule over you' — noble words from Gideon's lips! But
  then he made an ephod of gold and set it up in his city. He refused the
  crown but kept the gold. He rejected the title but assumed the power.
  How many of us say the right things while doing the wrong things! Our
  theology is sound but our practice is corrupt. Gideon's mouth honored
  God while his hands built an idol."
TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.2  — KJV: "the gleaning of the grapes of Ephraim" / ESV: "the gleaning
       of the grapes of Ephraim" — Hebrew "olelot Ephrayim." "Olelot" =
       gleanings (what remains after harvest). Gideon's metaphor: even
       Ephraim's leftovers (capturing Oreb and Zeeb) exceed Abiezer's
       full harvest (the initial battle). Diplomatic flattery.

v.4  — KJV: "faint, yet pursuing them" / ESV: "exhausted yet pursuing" —
       Hebrew "ayefim v'rod'fim." "Ayef" = faint, exhausted, weary.
       "Radaph" = to pursue, chase. The juxtaposition is powerful:
       physical exhaustion does not stop spiritual determination.

v.16 — KJV: "with them he taught the men of Succoth" / ESV: "with them
       taught the men of Succoth a lesson" — Hebrew "vayoda bahem."
       "Yada" here means "to make known/teach" — but the "lesson" was
       physical punishment with thorns. A grim euphemism: he "educated"
       them with briers.

v.21 — KJV: "the ornaments that were on their camels' necks" / ESV: "the
       crescent ornaments that were on the necks of their camels" —
       Hebrew "ha-saharonim." "Sahar" = crescent moon. These were
       crescent-shaped gold ornaments — likely connected to moon worship.
       Gideon takes them as spoil, but they may have contributed to the
       idolatrous ephod.

v.26 — KJV: "a thousand and seven hundred shekels of gold" / ESV: "1,700
       shekels of gold" — Approximately 43 pounds (19.5 kg) of gold. An
       enormous amount — enough to make a substantial golden object. The
       parallel to Exodus 32 (golden calf from earrings) is unmistakable.

v.27 — KJV: "made an ephod thereof" / ESV: "made an ephod of it" —
       Hebrew "vaya'as oto l'ephod." The ephod was normally a priestly
       garment with the Urim and Thummim for divine inquiry (Exodus
       28:6-30). Gideon's ephod was likely a gold-plated garment or
       image used for seeking God's will — but outside the authorized
       priesthood and tabernacle. Unauthorized worship, however sincere,
       becomes idolatry.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Ephod          — A priestly garment used for divine inquiry, containing
               the Urim and Thummim. Gideon's ephod was unauthorized —
               made from battle spoil and placed in his hometown rather
               than at the tabernacle. It became an object of worship
               rather than a means of worship. A warning: religious
               objects can become idols.

Succoth        — "Booths." An Israelite city east of the Jordan (in Gad's
               territory). Its leaders refused bread to Gideon's exhausted
               army, fearing Midianite retaliation. Gideon punished them
               with thorns and briers upon his return.

Penuel         — "Face of God." Named by Jacob after wrestling with God
               (Genesis 32:30). An Israelite city east of Jordan whose
               men also refused Gideon aid. He destroyed their tower and
               killed the men — severe punishment for refusing to support
               God's deliverer.

Zebah and Zalmunna — The two kings of Midian. "Zebah" may mean
               "sacrifice/slaughter." "Zalmunna" may mean "shade
               withheld/protection refused." They had killed Gideon's
               brothers at Tabor. Their execution completes the Midianite
               defeat.

Abimelech      — "My father is king." Gideon's son by a concubine in
               Shechem. The name contradicts Gideon's stated refusal of
               kingship (v.23). Abimelech will seize power violently in
               Chapter 9, killing his 70 brothers.

Baal-berith    — "Lord of the covenant." The specific Baal worshipped at
               Shechem after Gideon's death. The name parodies God's
               covenant relationship with Israel — they replace the true
               covenant God with a false "lord of the covenant."

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.4 — ASV: "faint, yet pursuing" / WEB: "faint, yet pursuing" / NET: "even though they were exhausted, they were still chasing the Midianites" — This famous phrase in KJV/ASV/WEB ("faint, yet pursuing") is Hebrew "ayefim v'rod'fim" — a terse, powerful construction. NET expands into a full explanatory clause, losing the poetic compression that has made this phrase memorable for centuries.

- v.16 — ASV: "with them he taught the men of Succoth" / WEB: "with them he taught the men of Succoth" / NET: "he then 'threshed' the men of Sukkoth with them" — Hebrew "vayyoda" can mean "to teach/make known" or (from a different root) "to thresh." KJV/ASV/WEB use "taught" (grimly ironic — he "taught" them with thorns). NET uses "threshed" in quotes, connecting to Gideon's threat in v.7. Both readings are linguistically possible.

- v.21 — NET: "a man is judged by his strength" / ASV: "as the man is, so is his strength" / WEB: "as the man is, so is his strength" — Hebrew "ki k'ish g'vurato" = "for as the man, so is his strength." Zebah and Zalmunna prefer death at a warrior's hand rather than a boy's. NET interprets this as a proverb about judgment; ASV/WEB retain the more literal construction linking identity to power.

- v.27 — ASV: "all Israel played the harlot after it there" / WEB: "all Israel played the prostitute with it there" / NET: "All the Israelites prostituted themselves to it by worshiping it there" — Hebrew "vayyiznu... acharav" = whored after it. NET adds "by worshiping it" — explanatory gloss not in Hebrew. The spiritual adultery metaphor is preserved in all versions; NET makes explicit what the metaphor implies.

- v.33 — ASV: "played the harlot after the Baalim, and made Baal-berith their god" / WEB: "played the prostitute following the Baals, and made Baal Berith their god" / NET: "prostituted themselves to the Baals. They made Baal Berith their god" — "Baal-berith" = "Lord of the Covenant" — a deeply ironic name. Israel replaces their covenant LORD with a false "lord of the covenant." All translations preserve the name; the theological irony transcends translation.

- v.22 — ASV: "Rule thou over us... for thou hast saved us" / WEB: "Rule over us... for you have saved us" / NET: "Rule over us... For you have delivered us from Midian's power" — Hebrew "moshal banu... ki hoshata-nu" = rule over us, for you have saved us. NET adds "from Midian's power" for clarity. Gideon's refusal ("Jehovah/Yahweh shall rule over you") is theologically crucial — he acknowledges divine kingship, though his subsequent actions (ephod, many wives, naming a son "my father is king") contradict his words.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Faint, yet pursuing" (v.4). The Christian life is often this: weary
   but not quitting, exhausted but still obedient. Faithfulness is not
   the absence of fatigue — it is perseverance despite fatigue. When you
   are faint, keep pursuing. God honors endurance.

2. Words without matching actions (vv.23,27). Gideon says "the LORD shall
   rule" but then makes an ephod that rivals God's prescribed worship. He
   refuses the crown but lives like a king. Our theology must match our
   practice. It is possible to say all the right things while doing all
   the wrong things.

3. Good intentions can produce idols (v.27). Gideon likely meant the
   ephod as a memorial or means of worship — but it became "a snare."
   Sincerity does not sanctify unauthorized worship. God prescribes HOW
   He is to be worshipped, not just THAT He is to be worshipped. Good
   motives with wrong methods produce idolatry.

4. The danger of the aftermath (vv.30-31). Gideon's greatest failures
   come after his greatest victory. Many wives, a concubine, a son named
   "my father is king." Success is more dangerous than struggle. In
   battle, Gideon depended on God; in peace, he depended on himself.

5. Forgetfulness leads to apostasy (vv.33-34). "As soon as Gideon was
   dead" — immediately. They forgot God and forgot Gideon. Memory is the
   foundation of faithfulness. When we forget what God has done, we
   inevitably turn to other gods. This is why Scripture commands
   remembrance (Deuteronomy 6:12; 1 Corinthians 11:24-25).
