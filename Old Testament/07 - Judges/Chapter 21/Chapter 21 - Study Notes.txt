Judges - Chapter 21
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Israel grieves over the near-extinction of Benjamin and devises ways to provide wives for the 600 survivors without breaking their oath. The book ends: "In those days there was no king in Israel: every man did that which was right in his own eyes."

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Unknown, traditionally attributed to Samuel. Judges covers
approximately 350 years (1380-1050 BC) between Joshua's death and the
rise of the monarchy. Hebrew title: 'Shophetim' — 'Judges' (deliverers/
rulers raised by God). The book records Israel's repeated cycle: sin →
oppression → crying out → deliverance → rest → sin again. The refrain:
'In those days there was no king in Israel: every man did that which was
right in his own eyes' (17:6, 21:25). The book demonstrates humanity's
need for a righteous king — ultimately fulfilled in Christ.

Historical Context: Chapter 21 is the final chapter of Judges and
concludes both the Gibeah narrative (chapters 19-21) and the entire book.
It records Israel's desperate — and morally bankrupt — attempts to
provide wives for the 600 surviving Benjamite men at the rock of Rimmon.
The chapter reveals the cascading consequences of rash oaths and
unchecked violence. Israel had sworn two oaths at Mizpah: (1) no one
would give their daughter in marriage to Benjamin (v.1), and (2) anyone
who failed to assemble at Mizpah would be put to death (v.5). Now,
having nearly annihilated an entire tribe, they weep over the
consequences of their own actions. Their "solution" involves two further
atrocities: destroying Jabesh-gilead (killing all men, married women,
and children) to obtain 400 virgins for Benjamin, and then authorizing
the abduction of young women dancing at the feast of Shiloh to supply
the remaining 200 wives needed. The chapter is a masterpiece of dark
irony: Israel began by seeking justice for the rape of one woman (chapter
19) and ends by orchestrating the mass abduction of women for forced
marriage. They have become what they condemned. The legal casuistry they
employ — technically they did not "give" their daughters since the women
were "taken" — reveals the moral bankruptcy of a people who manipulate
the letter of the law while violating its spirit. The book's final verse
delivers the author's verdict on the entire period: "In those days there
was no king in Israel: every man did that which was right in his own
eyes" (v.25). This is not merely a political observation — it is a
theological indictment. Without righteous authority, even well-
intentioned people produce chaos, violence, and injustice. The book ends
not with resolution but with a cry for the king who will bring order out
of moral anarchy — pointing forward to David and ultimately to Christ.

Israel's Grief and the Oath Problem (vv.1-7): The men of Israel had
sworn at Mizpah: "There shall not any of us give his daughter unto
Benjamin to wife" (v.1). Now, with only 600 Benjamite men surviving and
all Benjamite women dead (20:48), the tribe faces extinction. Israel
comes to Bethel (the house of God), weeps until evening, and cries out:
"O LORD God of Israel, why is this come to pass in Israel, that there
should be to day one tribe lacking in Israel?" (v.3). The question is
remarkable in its lack of self-awareness — they themselves caused this
through their excessive violence. They built an altar and offered
sacrifices (v.4), seeking God's guidance. But their "solution" will not
come from God — it will come from human scheming that produces more
violence. The oath creates an impossible situation: they cannot give
wives to Benjamin without breaking their vow, yet without wives Benjamin
will perish. Rather than acknowledging their oath was rash and seeking
God's release from it, they seek loopholes.

The Destruction of Jabesh-gilead (vv.8-14): Israel discovers that no one
from Jabesh-gilead attended the assembly at Mizpah. The second oath
activates: those who did not come must die. They send 12,000 warriors
with orders to "smite the inhabitants of Jabesh-gilead with the edge of
the sword, with the women and the children" (v.10) — sparing only
virgin women. They find 400 young virgins and bring them to Shiloh. The
600 Benjamites are called from the rock of Rimmon and offered peace.
The 400 women are given to them — "and yet so they sufficed them not"
(v.14). Two hundred men remain without wives. The moral calculus is
staggering: to preserve one tribe, they destroy an entire city. To
provide wives for men who defended rapists, they kill families and take
their daughters. The "solution" replicates the very evil that started
the crisis — violence against the vulnerable to serve the interests of
men.

The Abduction at Shiloh (vv.15-23): Still 200 wives short, the elders
devise another scheme. They identify the annual feast of the LORD at
Shiloh — a legitimate religious festival — and instruct the Benjamites:
"Go and lie in wait in the vineyards; and see, and, behold, if the
daughters of Shiloh come out to dance in dances, then come ye out of the
vineyards, and catch you every man his wife" (vv.20-21). The plan
includes a prepared excuse for the fathers and brothers who will
complain: "Be favourable unto them for our sakes: because we reserved
not to each man his wife in the war: for ye did not give unto them at
this time, that ye should be guilty" (v.22). The legal fiction is
transparent: since the women were "seized" rather than "given," the oath
is technically unbroken. This is casuistry at its worst — the letter of
the law preserved while its spirit is demolished. Young women at worship
are abducted into forced marriages, and the elders of Israel authorize
it. The feast of the LORD — meant for worship and joy — becomes the
setting for organized kidnapping.

The Book's Conclusion (vv.23-25): Benjamin takes wives, returns to their
inheritance, and rebuilds their cities. Israel disperses — "every man to
his tribe and to his family" (v.24). Then the final verdict: "In those
days there was no king in Israel: every man did that which was right in
his own eyes" (v.25). This closing statement frames the entire book. It
appeared first in 17:6, introducing the appendix. Now it closes the
book. The repetition is deliberate: everything recorded in chapters 17-21
— Micah's idolatry, Dan's apostasy, the rape at Gibeah, the civil war,
the destruction of Jabesh-gilead, the abduction at Shiloh — all of it
flows from the absence of righteous authority. "Right in his own eyes"
is the author's diagnosis of the human condition without God's rule. It
is not freedom — it is chaos. It is not autonomy — it is moral anarchy.
The book of Judges ends with a question hanging in the air: Who will be
the king that Israel needs? The answer unfolds through Ruth, Samuel, and
ultimately David — and beyond David to David's greater Son.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Jabesh-gilead: A city in Transjordan (Gilead) that did not join the assembly — its unmarried women are taken for Benjamin
- Shiloh: Where the remaining Benjamite men seize wives during the annual feast/dance in the vineyards
- The remnant of Benjamin: 600 survivors hiding at the rock of Rimmon in the wilderness east of Bethel

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.3  — "And they said, 'O LORD, the God of Israel, why has this happened
        in Israel, that today there should be one tribe lacking in
        Israel?'" (ESV) — Israel blames God for consequences they
        created. They nearly destroyed Benjamin through their own
        excessive violence, then ask God why a tribe is missing.
        Self-awareness is absent.

v.7  — "What shall we do for wives for those who are left, since we have
        sworn by the LORD that we will not give them any of our daughters
        for wives?" (ESV) — The rash oath creates an impossible dilemma.
        Rather than repenting of the oath, they seek loopholes — producing
        more violence to solve the problem their violence created.

v.15 — "And the people had compassion on Benjamin because the LORD had
        made a breach in the tribes of Israel." (ESV) — "The LORD had
        made a breach" — they attribute to God what their own hands did.
        Yet there is genuine grief here. Regret without repentance
        produces schemes, not solutions.

v.21 — "And watch. If the daughters of Shiloh come out to dance in the
        dances, then come out of the vineyards and snatch each man his
        wife from the daughters of Shiloh, and go to the land of
        Benjamin." (ESV) — Organized abduction authorized by Israel's
        elders. The book that began with outrage over one woman's rape
        ends with the institutional kidnapping of 200 women. Israel has
        become what it condemned.

v.22 — "Grant them graciously to us, because we did not take for each
        man of them his wife in battle, neither did you give them to
        them, else you would now be guilty." (ESV) — Legal fiction.
        Technical compliance with the oath while violating its spirit.
        When people use God's name to bind themselves, then scheme to
        circumvent their own vows, they dishonor God doubly.

v.25 — "In those days there was no king in Israel. Everyone did what was
        right in his own eyes." (ESV) — The book's final word. The thesis
        statement of Judges. Without righteous authority, human autonomy
        produces not freedom but chaos, not justice but violence. This
        verse cries out for the King.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/judges-21/
  Guzik emphasizes the tragic irony: Israel's "solution" to the crisis
  they created involves more violence and the very abuse of women that
  triggered the war. He notes that the book ends without resolution —
  deliberately pointing forward to the need for a righteous king.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "Every man did that which was right in his own eyes.' Here is the
  epitaph of a nation in ruins. When every man becomes his own god, his
  own lawgiver, his own standard of right and wrong — then there is no
  right at all, only competing selfishnesses tearing the fabric of
  society to pieces. The book of Judges does not end with a period but
  with a question mark: Where is the King? And the answer echoes down
  the centuries until a child is born in Bethlehem of whom it is said,
  'He shall reign over the house of Jacob for ever.'"

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.2  — Hebrew "vayyis'u qolam vayyivku b'khi gadol" — they lifted their voice and wept a great weeping.
       KJV: "came to the house of God"
       ESV: "came to Bethel"
       ASV: "the people came to Beth-el, and sat there till even before God, and lifted up their voices, and wept sore"
       NET: "So the people came to Bethel and sat there before God until evening, weeping loudly and uncontrollably"
       WEB: "the people came to Bethel, and sat there till evening before God, and lifted up their voices, and wept sore"
       Analysis: Hebrew "Beit-El." Same ambiguity as in 20:26. KJV
       translates descriptively ("house of God"); ESV uses the proper noun
       ("Bethel"). Both are valid renderings of the same Hebrew. NET adds
       "uncontrollably" (not in Hebrew). The Hebrew is literally "they
       lifted their voice and wept a great weeping." The corporate grief
       is genuine but ironic — they weep over consequences they created
       through their own excessive violence.

v.6  — Hebrew "vayyinnachamu" — they were sorry/repented/had compassion.
       KJV: "repented them for Benjamin"
       ESV: "had compassion for Benjamin"
       ASV: "the children of Israel repented them for Benjamin"
       NET: "The Israelites regretted what had happened to their brother Benjamin"
       WEB: "The children of Israel grieved for Benjamin their brother"
       Analysis: From "nacham" = to be sorry, to comfort oneself, to
       relent. KJV/ASV's "repented" uses the older English sense of "felt
       regret/sorrow" (not moral repentance). ESV's "had compassion"
       captures the emotional dimension. NET's "regretted" and WEB's
       "grieved" suggest emotional response without moral change. The same
       root describes God's compassion in 2:18. The Hebrew conveys grief
       over consequences — they are sorry for what happened, though not
       necessarily for their role in causing it.

v.11 — Hebrew "tachariymu" / "hacharim" — devote to destruction.
       KJV: "Ye shall utterly destroy every male"
       ESV: "every male and every woman that has lain with a male you shall devote to destruction"
       ASV: "ye shall utterly destroy every male, and every woman that hath lain by man"
       NET: "Exterminate every male, as well as every woman who has experienced a man's bed. But spare the lives of any virgins"
       WEB: "every male, and every woman who has lain with a man, you shall utterly destroy"
       Analysis: From "charam" = to devote to destruction, to place under
       the ban. This is the same "herem" language used for the Canaanite
       conquest (Joshua 6:17-21). Its application to a fellow Israelite
       city is extraordinary and reveals how far the situation has
       deteriorated — Israel treats its own people as it was commanded to
       treat the Canaanites. NET adds "But spare the lives of any virgins"
       — not in the Hebrew at this point (it's implied from v.12). The
       application of cherem against fellow Israelites marks the complete
       moral collapse of the period.

v.15 — Hebrew "parets" — breach/gap.
       KJV: "the LORD had made a breach in the tribes of Israel"
       ESV: "the LORD had made a breach in the tribes of Israel"
       ASV: "Jehovah had made a breach in the tribes of Israel"
       NET: "the Lord had weakened the Israelite tribes"
       WEB: "Yahweh had made a breach in the tribes of Israel"
       Analysis: "Parets" = breach/gap. KJV/ESV/ASV/WEB retain the
       architectural metaphor — God made a breach (gap) in the tribal
       structure. NET's "weakened" loses the imagery. The "breach"
       acknowledges divine sovereignty even in civil war — God permitted
       the near-annihilation of Benjamin as judgment. They attribute to
       God what their own hands did, yet there is genuine grief here.

v.19 — Hebrew "chag-Adonai b'Shiloh miyamim yamimah" — a feast of the LORD in Shiloh annually.
       KJV: "a feast of the LORD in Shiloh yearly"
       ESV: "the yearly feast of the LORD at Shiloh"
       ASV: "a feast of Jehovah from year to year in Shiloh"
       NET: "an annual festival to the Lord is being held at Shiloh"
       WEB: "a feast of Yahweh from year to year in Shiloh"
       Analysis: "Chag" = a pilgrimage feast. "Miyamim yamimah" =
       literally "from days to days" = annually. This is likely one of the
       three required annual feasts (Passover, Weeks, or Tabernacles). The
       dancing suggests Tabernacles (Sukkot), the harvest celebration.
       NET's "annual festival" is more accessible; ASV/WEB's "from year
       to year" preserves the Hebrew idiom.

v.21 — Hebrew "vachataphtem" — seize/snatch/carry off.
       KJV: "catch you every man his wife"
       ESV: "snatch each man his wife"
       ASV: "come ye out of the vineyards, and catch you every man his wife of the daughters of Shiloh"
       NET: "jump out from the vineyards. Each one of you, catch yourself a wife from among the daughters of Shiloh"
       WEB: "and catch every man his wife of the daughters of Shiloh"
       Analysis: From "chataph" = to seize, snatch, carry off. The word
       implies violent seizure — not courtship or consent. It is the
       language of predation. The same root appears in Job 9:12 ("who can
       hinder him?") and Psalm 10:9 (the lion snatching prey). NET's
       "catch yourself a wife" is more colloquial. The institutional
       sanctioning of bride-kidnapping from a religious festival reveals
       how far Israel has fallen — they solve one injustice (oath-
       breaking) with another (abduction). The authorized "catching" of
       women at worship is linguistically indistinguishable from
       kidnapping.

v.25 — Hebrew "ish hayashar b'einav ya'aseh" — each man did the straight/right in his eyes.
       KJV: "every man did that which was right in his own eyes"
       ESV: "Everyone did what was right in his own eyes"
       ASV: "In those days there was no king in Israel: every man did that which was right in his own eyes"
       NET: "In those days Israel had no king. Each man did what he considered to be right"
       WEB: "In those days there was no king in Israel. Everyone did that which was right in his own eyes"
       Analysis: "Yashar" = straight, right, upright. "B'einav" = in his
       eyes. The irony: "yashar" is the word for moral uprightness, yet
       here it describes moral chaos. Each person defines "right" for
       themselves — which means there is no objective right at all. The
       word also connects to "Jeshurun" (the upright one) — Israel's ideal
       name. They claim uprightness while practicing wickedness. The book's
       final and most devastating statement. ASV/WEB/KJV preserve the
       iconic phrasing "right in his own eyes." NET's "what he considered
       to be right" loses the visual/moral metaphor. This refrain frames
       the entire book (17:6, 18:1, 19:1, 21:25) and points forward to
       the monarchy — and ultimately to Christ as the righteous King.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Jabesh-gilead  — A city in the Transjordan territory of Gilead (modern
               Jordan). Its failure to attend the Mizpah assembly led to
               its destruction. Later, Jabesh-gilead develops a special
               bond with Benjamin/Saul: Saul rescues them from Nahash
               the Ammonite (1 Samuel 11), and they retrieve his body
               after his death (1 Samuel 31:11-13).

Shiloh         — The central worship site of Israel during the Judges
               period, where the tabernacle was located (Joshua 18:1).
               Located in Ephraim, approximately 20 miles north of
               Jerusalem. The annual feast held there was a legitimate
               religious observance corrupted by the Benjamite abduction
               scheme. God later destroyed Shiloh as judgment (Jeremiah
               7:12-14).

Rash Oath      — A vow made hastily, often in the heat of emotion,
               without considering consequences. Israel's oath not to
               give daughters to Benjamin (v.1) and the death oath
               against non-participants (v.5) are both examples. Cf.
               Jephthah's vow (Judges 11:30-31), Saul's oath
               (1 Samuel 14:24). Ecclesiastes 5:2-5 warns against
               hasty vows before God.

Feast of the LORD — Hebrew "chag Adonai." One of the three annual
               pilgrimage festivals required of all Israelite males
               (Exodus 23:14-17): Passover/Unleavened Bread (spring),
               Weeks/Pentecost (early summer), and Tabernacles/Booths
               (autumn). The dancing at Shiloh suggests the joyful
               harvest celebration of Tabernacles.

"Right in his own eyes" — Hebrew "hayashar b'einav." The book's
               defining phrase. It describes moral relativism — each
               person as their own standard of right and wrong. It is
               the opposite of Deuteronomy 6:18: "thou shalt do that
               which is right in the sight of the LORD" (hayashar
               b'einei Adonai). The contrast: God's eyes vs. human eyes
               as the standard of righteousness.

Rock of Rimmon — The stronghold where 600 Benjamite survivors hid for
               four months (20:47). Their emergence and reintegration
               into Israel (v.13-14) represents the preservation of the
               tribe — God's providence ensuring that no tribe is
               permanently lost, despite human excess.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Rash oaths create impossible situations (vv.1,7). Israel swore in
   anger what they could not sustain in wisdom. Vows made in the heat of
   emotion — whether in war, conflict, or religious fervor — often
   produce consequences worse than the problem they addressed. Jesus
   taught: "Let your yes be yes and your no be no" (Matthew 5:37).
   Better to speak carefully than to bind yourself rashly before God.

2. Human "solutions" without God's wisdom multiply evil (vv.10-11,20-21).
   Israel's two schemes — destroying Jabesh-gilead and abducting women
   at Shiloh — solve one problem by creating others. When we act from
   human cleverness rather than divine wisdom, our solutions often
   generate new sins. The pattern: sin → consequence → human scheme →
   more sin. Only God's wisdom breaks the cycle.

3. Legal technicalities cannot mask moral failure (v.22). The elders'
   argument — "you didn't give them, so you're not guilty" — is
   transparent manipulation. God is not fooled by loopholes. He looks at
   the heart, not the technicality. Those who use clever reasoning to
   justify what they know is wrong deceive only themselves.

4. The absence of righteous authority produces chaos, not freedom (v.25).
   "Every man did that which was right in his own eyes" sounds like
   liberty but produces atrocity. True freedom requires a framework of
   righteousness. Without God as King — without submission to His
   authority — human autonomy degenerates into competing selfishnesses
   that destroy community. The book of Judges is the Bible's strongest
   argument against moral relativism.

5. The book ends crying out for a King (v.25). Judges does not resolve
   — it aches. It presents the problem without providing the solution.
   The solution comes in stages: Samuel, then David, then ultimately
   Christ — the righteous King under whose rule "every man doing right
   in his own eyes" is replaced by every knee bowing and every tongue
   confessing that Jesus Christ is Lord (Philippians 2:10-11).
