Judges - Chapter 20
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
All Israel unites against Benjamin for the atrocity at Gibeah, and after two devastating defeats, God gives them victory on the third day. Benjamin is nearly wiped out—only 600 men survive, fleeing to the rock of Rimmon.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Unknown, traditionally attributed to Samuel. Judges covers
approximately 350 years (1380-1050 BC) between Joshua's death and the
rise of the monarchy. Hebrew title: 'Shophetim' — 'Judges' (deliverers/
rulers raised by God). The book records Israel's repeated cycle: sin →
oppression → crying out → deliverance → rest → sin again. The refrain:
'In those days there was no king in Israel: every man did that which was
right in his own eyes' (17:6, 21:25). The book demonstrates humanity's
need for a righteous king — ultimately fulfilled in Christ.

Historical Context: Chapter 20 records the civil war that erupted in
response to the atrocity at Gibeah (chapter 19). All Israel assembles —
"from Dan even to Beer-sheba" (v.1) — 400,000 armed men united against
Benjamin's refusal to surrender the guilty men of Gibeah. This is the
only time in the Judges period that all Israel acts in concert, and
ironically it is not against a foreign oppressor but against one of their
own tribes. The chapter presents a complex theological narrative: Israel
is right to demand justice for the crime at Gibeah, yet their execution
of that justice is deeply flawed. They inquire of God, yet their first
two attacks result in devastating defeats — 22,000 dead on the first day,
18,000 on the second. Only after fasting, weeping, and offering
sacrifices does God grant them victory on the third day. The question
arises: why did God allow Israel to suffer 40,000 casualties while
pursuing justice? Several answers emerge. First, Israel's own sin needed
purging — they were not morally superior to Benjamin. Second, their
initial inquiry of God was superficial: "Which of us shall go up first?"
(v.18) — they asked about tactics, not whether they should fight at all.
Third, the escalating devotion (weeping in v.23, then fasting and
sacrifices in v.26) suggests God was driving them to deeper dependence.
The presence of Phinehas son of Eleazar (v.28) — Aaron's grandson —
places this event early in the Judges period, despite its position at
the end of the book. The military strategy of the third day mirrors
Joshua's conquest of Ai (Joshua 8): a feigned retreat drawing the enemy
out, followed by an ambush and the burning of the city. The near-
annihilation of Benjamin — from 26,700 warriors to just 600 survivors —
raises the specter of a tribe being erased from Israel entirely, setting
up the crisis of chapter 21.

Israel Assembles at Mizpah (vv.1-11): "All the children of Israel went
out, and the congregation was gathered together as one man, from Dan even
to Beer-sheba" (v.1). This phrase — "from Dan to Beer-sheba" — describes
the full extent of Israel, north to south. The assembly at Mizpah
includes 400,000 armed men. The Levite presents his case (vv.4-7),
though notably he omits his own role in pushing his concubine outside.
He claims the men "thought to have slain me" (v.5) — emphasizing the
threat to himself rather than the actual crime against her. His account
is self-serving, yet the underlying crime is real. Israel responds
unanimously: "We will not any of us go to his tent, neither will we any
of us turn into his house" (v.8) — they will not rest until justice is
done. They send messengers to Benjamin demanding the surrender of "the
children of Belial, which are in Gibeah, that we may put them to death,
and put away evil from Israel" (v.13). This demand is consistent with
Deuteronomy 13:12-15 and 17:12 — the requirement to purge evil from the
community. But Benjamin refuses. Tribal loyalty overrides moral duty.
They choose to protect their guilty rather than submit to justice.

Benjamin's Defiance and Military Strength (vv.12-17): Benjamin musters
26,000 swordsmen plus 700 chosen men from Gibeah itself. Among them are
700 left-handed slingers who "could sling stones at an hair breadth, and
not miss" (v.16). This detail recalls Ehud, the left-handed Benjamite
judge (Judges 3:15). Benjamin was small but fierce — their warriors were
elite. Israel's 400,000 against Benjamin's 26,700 represents roughly a
15-to-1 advantage, yet the first two battles go to Benjamin. Numbers
alone do not determine outcomes.

The First Two Defeats (vv.18-25): Israel inquires of God: "Which of us
shall go up first?" (v.18). God answers: "Judah shall go up first" —
the same answer given at the book's opening (Judges 1:2). But the first
attack fails catastrophically: Benjamin kills 22,000 Israelites. Israel
"encouraged themselves" (v.22) and asks again: "Shall I go up again to
battle against the children of Benjamin my brother?" (v.23). God says
"Go up against him." The second day: 18,000 more Israelites fall. Total
losses: 40,000 men — nearly 10% of their army. Why does God permit this?
The text does not explain directly, but the progression of Israel's
response is instructive. After the first defeat: they "encouraged
themselves" (v.22) — self-reliance. After the second: they "went up, and
came unto the house of God, and wept, and sat there before the LORD, and
fasted that day until even, and offered burnt offerings and peace
offerings" (v.26). The defeats drive them from self-confidence to genuine
dependence on God.

The Third Day — Victory Through Ambush (vv.26-48): After fasting and
sacrifice, Israel inquires again — but now with a different posture:
"Shall I yet again go out to battle against the children of Benjamin my
brother, or shall I cease?" (v.28). They are now willing to stop if God
says stop. This is the posture God was waiting for. His answer: "Go up;
for to morrow I will deliver them into thine hand" (v.28). The strategy
mirrors Ai (Joshua 8): Israel sets an ambush, feigns retreat to draw
Benjamin out of the city, then the ambush force strikes Gibeah and burns
it. The signal — "a great flame with smoke rise up out of the city"
(v.38) — tells the retreating Israelites to turn and fight. Benjamin,
seeing the smoke, realizes "evil was come upon them" (v.41). The rout is
total: 25,100 Benjamites fall. The survivors flee to the rock of Rimmon
— only 600 men escape. Israel then systematically destroys every
Benjamite city, killing men, women, children, and livestock (v.48). The
punishment, while responding to genuine evil, becomes excessive —
approaching genocide of an entire tribe.

The Theological Complexity: This chapter resists simple moral readings.
Israel is right to demand justice. Benjamin is wrong to protect the
guilty. Yet Israel's execution of justice becomes disproportionate
destruction. God authorizes the battle but allows Israel to suffer first
— purging their self-righteousness through defeat before granting
victory. The 40,000 Israelite dead remind us that pursuing justice in a
fallen world always costs something. And the near-extinction of Benjamin
shows that righteous anger, unchecked by mercy, can become its own form
of evil.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Mizpah: Assembly point for the eleven tribes (likely Mizpah in Benjamin, north of Jerusalem)
- Gibeah (Benjamin): The target city — located on the central ridge road between Jerusalem and Ramah
- Bethel: Where Israel inquires of God (the ark is there); the battle takes place in the narrow passes around Gibeah

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/judges-20/
  Guzik emphasizes that Israel's initial defeats were God's discipline
  for their own sin — they were not morally superior to Benjamin. He
  notes the progression from casual inquiry to genuine brokenness, and
  that God required humility before granting victory.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "Forty thousand men of Israel fell before God gave the victory. Why?
  Because God will not be used as a rubber stamp for human plans. They
  asked 'Who shall go first?' — a question of strategy. They should have
  asked 'Are we right with Thee?' God sometimes lets His people fail in
  a righteous cause until they learn that the cause alone does not
  guarantee success. Right doctrine with wrong hearts still falls before
  the enemy. Only when they wept, fasted, and offered sacrifice — only
  when they were broken — did God say, 'Tomorrow I will deliver them.'"
REFLECTION
------------------------------------
1. Right cause does not guarantee right outcome without right hearts
   (vv.18-25). Israel pursued justice — a righteous cause. Yet they
   suffered 40,000 casualties before victory. Having the right position
   is not enough; God requires the right posture. Self-righteous
   crusaders often fail even when their cause is just, because God will
   not bless pride even in pursuit of justice.

2. God uses defeat to produce dependence (vv.22-26). The progression is
   clear: after the first defeat, Israel "encouraged themselves" (self-
   reliance). After the second, they wept, fasted, and sacrificed
   (God-reliance). Sometimes God allows us to fail — even in righteous
   endeavors — to break our self-sufficiency and drive us to genuine
   dependence on Him.

3. Protecting the guilty implicates the protectors (v.13). Benjamin's
   refusal to surrender the men of Gibeah made the entire tribe
   complicit. When communities, institutions, or families protect
   wrongdoers from accountability, they share in the guilt. Loyalty
   that shields evil is not virtue — it is complicity.

4. The cost of justice in a fallen world (vv.21,25,35). Pursuing
   righteousness cost Israel 40,000 men before it cost Benjamin 25,000.
   Justice is never free. Standing against evil always exacts a price.
   Those who think righteousness should be easy or costless have not
   understood the nature of the battle.

5. Righteous anger without mercy becomes its own evil (v.48). Israel's
   response to Gibeah's sin — destroying every Benjamite city, killing
   women and children — exceeds proportional justice. Vengeance that
   knows no restraint becomes indistinguishable from the evil it
   punishes. Even justified wrath must be tempered by the recognition
   that we too are sinners in need of mercy.
