Judges - Chapter 5
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Deborah and Barak sing a victory song celebrating God's triumph over Sisera and praising those tribes who fought. The song ends with Sisera's mother peering through the window, waiting in vain for her son's return.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Unknown, traditionally attributed to Samuel. Judges covers
approximately 350 years (1380-1050 BC) between Joshua's death and the
rise of the monarchy. Hebrew title: 'Shophetim' — 'Judges' (deliverers/
rulers raised by God). The book records Israel's repeated cycle: sin →
oppression → crying out → deliverance → rest → sin again. The refrain:
'In those days there was no king in Israel: every man did that which was
right in his own eyes' (17:6, 21:25). The book demonstrates humanity's
need for a righteous king — ultimately fulfilled in Christ.

Historical Context: Chapter 5 is the Song of Deborah — one of the oldest
and most celebrated poems in the Hebrew Bible. It is a victory hymn
celebrating the defeat of Sisera and the Canaanite army, composed by
Deborah and Barak on the day of victory. The song is considered by
scholars to be among the most ancient Hebrew poetry preserved in
Scripture, possibly composed within hours of the battle itself. It
provides details not found in the prose account of Chapter 4: the cosmic
dimension of the battle ("the stars in their courses fought against
Sisera," v.20), the flooding of the Kishon that swept away the chariots
(v.21), the roll call of tribes who fought and those who refused (vv.14-18),
the curse on Meroz for not helping (v.23), and the poignant scene of
Sisera's mother waiting for a son who will never return (vv.28-30). The
song moves from praise to God (vv.2-5), to the desperate conditions
before deliverance (vv.6-8), to the battle itself (vv.19-22), to Jael's
heroism (vv.24-27), to the enemy's grief (vv.28-30), and concludes with
a prayer for God's enemies to perish and His friends to shine like the
sun (v.31).

Praise to the Warrior God (vv.1-5): The song opens with praise for
leaders who led and people who volunteered (v.2). God is addressed as the
Divine Warrior who marches from Seir/Edom (v.4) — recalling His
appearance at Sinai. When God moves, "the earth trembled, and the heavens
dropped... the mountains melted" (vv.4-5). This theophanic language
connects the Kishon victory to the Exodus and Sinai — the same God who
delivered from Egypt delivers now.

The Desperate Conditions (vv.6-8): Before Deborah arose, "the highways
were unoccupied" — travel was too dangerous (v.6). Villages were
abandoned (v.7). Israel had chosen "new gods" and war came to their gates
(v.8). They were disarmed: "was there a shield or spear seen among forty
thousand in Israel?" (v.8). The situation was hopeless by human standards.

The Tribal Roll Call (vv.14-18): The song praises tribes who fought:
Ephraim, Benjamin, Machir (Manasseh), Zebulun, Issachar, Naphtali
(vv.14-15,18). It rebukes tribes who refused: Reuben deliberated but
stayed with his flocks (vv.15-16), Gilead (Gad) stayed beyond Jordan,
Dan stayed with ships, Asher remained on the coast (v.17). Zebulun and
Naphtali receive highest praise: they "jeoparded their lives unto the
death" (v.18).

The Battle — Cosmic and Earthly (vv.19-22): The kings of Canaan fought
at Taanach by Megiddo's waters — "they took no gain of money" (v.19) —
they gained nothing. "They fought from heaven; the stars in their courses
fought against Sisera" (v.20). The cosmos itself opposed Sisera — God
marshaled creation against His enemies. "The river of Kishon swept them
away" (v.21) — a flash flood turned the river into a weapon, bogging
down chariots and drowning soldiers.

Jael Celebrated (vv.24-27): "Blessed above women shall Jael... be" (v.24).
The poetic account is more graphic than the prose: "she smote off his
head, when she had pierced and stricken through his temples" (v.26). The
threefold repetition — "at her feet he bowed, he fell, he lay down" (v.27)
— emphasizes the totality of his defeat and the completeness of her
victory.

Sisera's Mother (vv.28-30): A haunting scene: Sisera's mother peers
through the lattice, wondering why her son's chariot delays. Her ladies
comfort her with imagined spoils — "to every man a damsel or two" (v.30).
The dramatic irony is devastating: while she imagines victory, her son
lies dead at a woman's feet. The Hebrew word for "damsel" (racham) literally
means "womb" — reducing women to objects. The song contrasts this with
Jael and Deborah — women as agents, not objects.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- The Song of Deborah describes the battle geography: Mount Tabor, the Kishon River (which flooded and swept away Sisera's chariots), and the plain of Megiddo
- Tribes praised for responding: Ephraim, Benjamin, Machir (Manasseh), Zebulun, Issachar, Naphtali
- Tribes rebuked for not coming: Reuben (beyond Jordan), Dan (stayed with ships), Asher (on the coast)

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "That the leaders took the lead in Israel, that the people
        offered themselves willingly, bless the LORD!" (ESV) — Victory
        comes from two sources: God's power and the people's willing
        participation. Divine sovereignty and human responsibility work
        together.

v.7  — "The villagers ceased in Israel; they ceased to be until I arose;
        I, Deborah, arose as a mother in Israel." (ESV) — Deborah's
        self-description: "a mother in Israel." Not a warrior or general
        but a mother — one who nurtures, protects, and leads her children.
        Leadership as maternal care.

v.20 — "From heaven the stars fought, from their courses they fought
        against Sisera." (ESV) — The cosmic dimension of the battle. God
        marshals all creation against His enemies. The stars themselves
        fight for Israel. When God is for you, the universe is for you.

v.21 — "The torrent Kishon swept them away, the ancient torrent, the
        torrent Kishon. March on, my soul, with might!" (ESV) — The
        Kishon becomes God's weapon. Iron chariots — Israel's terror
        — are swept away by water. God uses nature itself to defeat the
        enemy's greatest strength.

v.23 — "'Curse Meroz,' says the angel of the LORD, 'curse its inhabitants
        utterly, because they did not come to the help of the LORD.'" (ESV)
        — Neutrality in God's battles is cursed. Those who refuse to help
        when God calls are not innocent bystanders — they are guilty of
        abandoning God's cause.

v.31 — "So may all your enemies perish, O LORD! But your friends be like
        the sun as he rises in his might." (ESV) — The song's conclusion:
        a prayer for the destruction of God's enemies and the radiance of
        God's friends. Those who love God shine with increasing glory.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/judges-5/
  Guzik emphasizes the antiquity and literary power of this song. He
  notes the contrast between tribes who risked everything (Zebulun,
  Naphtali) and those who stayed home (Reuben, Dan, Asher). The curse
  on Meroz shows that neutrality in God's battles is not an option.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "'The stars in their courses fought against Sisera.' When God fights,
  all creation is His army. The stars in their orbits, the rivers in
  their channels, the storms in their fury — all serve His purposes.
  What folly to fight against the God who commands the universe! And what
  comfort to know that when He is for us, even the stars fight on our
  behalf."
TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.2  — KJV: "for the avenging of Israel, when the people willingly
       offered themselves" / ESV: "That the leaders took the lead in
       Israel, that the people offered themselves willingly" — Hebrew
       "biphroa' p'ra'ot b'Yisrael b'hitnaddeyv am." The first phrase
       is difficult. KJV reads "pera'" as "vengeance/avenging." ESV
       reads it as "leaders leading" (from "para'" = to let loose hair,
       hence leaders letting their hair loose in battle). Both are
       legitimate readings of a notoriously difficult verse.

v.7  — KJV: "a mother in Israel" / ESV: "a mother in Israel" — Hebrew
       "em b'Yisrael." Deborah's self-designation. The title "mother in
       Israel" denotes a protector and nurturer of the nation — a
       leadership role defined by care rather than conquest.

v.20 — KJV: "the stars in their courses fought against Sisera" / ESV:
       "from their courses they fought against Sisera" — Hebrew
       "mimm'sillotam nilchamu im-Sisera." "M'sillot" = courses, tracks,
       highways. The stars fought from their ordained paths — cosmic
       order itself opposed Sisera. This may be poetic/metaphorical or
       may suggest actual meteorological phenomena (storms, hail) that
       accompanied the battle.

v.26 — KJV: "she smote off his head" / ESV: "she crushed his head" —
       Hebrew "machaqah rosho." "Machaq" = to crush, shatter. KJV's
       "smote off" suggests decapitation; ESV's "crushed" is more
       accurate to the Hebrew. She crushed/shattered his skull with the
       hammer blow that drove the peg through his temple.

v.28 — KJV: "The mother of Sisera looked out at a window" / ESV: "Out
       of the window she peered, the mother of Sisera" — Hebrew
       "be'ad hachallon nishq'phah." "Shaqaph" = to look out, peer
       through. The image of a mother anxiously watching for her son's
       return is universal and poignant — even for the enemy. The song
       acknowledges the human cost of war.

v.30 — KJV: "to every man a damsel or two" / ESV: "A womb or two for
       every man" — Hebrew "racham rachmatayim l'rosh gaver." "Rechem"
       = womb. KJV softens this to "damsel." ESV's literal "womb"
       captures the brutal objectification — women reduced to body parts
       as spoils of war. The song exposes the enemy's dehumanizing view
       of women, contrasting it with Deborah and Jael's agency.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Song of Deborah — One of the oldest poems in the Hebrew Bible, likely
               composed on the day of victory. A victory hymn combining
               praise to God, historical narrative, tribal roll call,
               and prophetic curse/blessing. Its archaic Hebrew grammar
               and vocabulary confirm its antiquity.

Theophany      — A visible manifestation of God. Verses 4-5 describe God
               marching from Seir with cosmic upheaval — earth trembling,
               heavens dropping, mountains melting. This connects the
               Kishon victory to God's earlier appearances at Sinai and
               in the wilderness.

Meroz          — An unknown Israelite town cursed for refusing to help in
               the battle. Its exact location is lost — perhaps
               deliberately, as the curse may have led to its destruction.
               It serves as a warning: neutrality in God's battles is
               not innocence but guilt.

Megiddo        — A strategic city in the Jezreel Valley controlling major
               trade routes. "The waters of Megiddo" (v.19) refers to the
               streams near Megiddo/Taanach where the battle occurred.
               Megiddo (Armageddon) becomes a symbol of decisive battle
               throughout Scripture (Revelation 16:16).

Seir/Edom      — The mountainous region southeast of the Dead Sea. God
               "marching from Seir" recalls His journey with Israel from
               Sinai through the wilderness. The theophanic language
               connects past deliverance (Exodus) with present victory.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.2 — ASV: "For that the leaders took the lead in Israel, For that the people offered themselves willingly, Bless ye Jehovah" / WEB: "Because the leaders took the lead in Israel, because the people offered themselves willingly, be blessed, Yahweh!" / NET: "When the leaders took the lead in Israel, When the people answered the call to war—Praise the Lord!" — NET diverges significantly: "offered themselves willingly" becomes "answered the call to war" — losing the voluntary nature emphasized in Hebrew "b'hitnadev am" (when the people volunteered themselves). The Hebrew stresses willing self-offering, not merely responding to a summons.

- v.7 — ASV: "Until that I Deborah arose, That I arose a mother in Israel" / WEB: "Until I, Deborah, arose; Until I arose a mother in Israel" / NET: "until you arose, Deborah, until you arose as a motherly protector in Israel" — NET changes first person to second person ("you arose") and expands "mother" to "motherly protector." The Hebrew "em" simply means "mother" — Deborah's role as national mother-figure needs no expansion.

- v.11 — ASV: "there shall they rehearse the righteous acts of Jehovah" / WEB: "there they will rehearse Yahweh's righteous acts" / NET: "there they tell of the Lord's victorious deeds" — Hebrew "tsidqot Adonai" = "righteousnesses/righteous acts of the LORD." NET renders "tsidqot" as "victorious deeds" — an interpretive shift from moral character (righteousness) to military outcome (victory). The Hebrew encompasses both: God's victories ARE His righteous acts.

- v.21 — ASV: "O my soul, march on with strength" / WEB: "My soul, march on with strength" / NET: "Step on the necks of the strong!" — Dramatically different renderings of Hebrew "tidr'ki nafshi oz." ASV/WEB: self-exhortation to courage. NET: violent imagery of trampling enemies. The Hebrew is ambiguous but the self-address ("my soul") favors ASV/WEB's reading.

- v.25 — ASV: "She brought him butter in a lordly dish" / WEB: "She brought him butter in a lordly dish" / NET: "in a bowl fit for a king, she served him curds" — Hebrew "chem'ah" = curds/butter/cream. KJV/ASV/WEB say "butter"; NET says "curds" (more accurate for the dairy product in ancient Near East). "Lordly dish" (Hebrew "sephel addiyrim") = ASV/WEB retain the majestic language; NET expands to "bowl fit for a king."

- v.30 — NET: "a girl or two for each man to rape!" / ASV: "A damsel, two damsels to every man" / WEB: "A lady, two ladies to every man" — Hebrew "racham rachamatayim" literally means "a womb, two wombs per warrior." NET makes the brutal sexual violence explicit ("to rape"); ASV/WEB use euphemistic "damsel/lady." NET's rendering is interpretively bold but captures the degradation Sisera's mother casually contemplates for Israelite women.

- v.23 — ASV: "Curse ye Meroz, said the angel of Jehovah" / WEB: "'Curse Meroz,' said Yahweh's angel" / NET: "'Call judgment down on Meroz,' says the angel of the Lord" — Hebrew "oru" = curse. NET softens "curse" to "call judgment down on" — a dynamic equivalence that loses the direct imprecatory force of the Hebrew curse formula.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Willing participation in God's work (v.2). The song praises those who
   "willingly offered themselves." God's victories require human
   participation — not because God needs us but because He honors us by
   including us. The question is: am I willing? Do I volunteer for God's
   work or wait to be drafted?

2. The curse of neutrality (v.23). Meroz is cursed not for fighting
   against God but for refusing to fight FOR God. In spiritual warfare,
   there is no neutral ground. Those who sit out God's battles are not
   innocent — they are complicit in the enemy's cause. "He that is not
   with me is against me" (Matthew 12:30).

3. All creation serves God's purposes (v.20). Stars, rivers, storms —
   all fight for God's people when God commands. If the Creator is on
   your side, all creation is on your side. Romans 8:28 — all things
   work together for good for those who love God.

4. The contrast of two mothers (vv.7,28). Deborah — "a mother in Israel"
   who leads her people to victory. Sisera's mother — who waits in vain
   for a son destroyed by his own arrogance. One mother rises in faith;
   the other is left in grief. The choices of leaders affect not only
   themselves but those who love them.

5. "Let them that love him be as the sun" (v.31). The song ends with a
   vision: those who love God shine with increasing brightness, like the
   sun rising to full strength. Faithfulness leads to radiance. The
   trajectory of the righteous is upward — from glory to glory
   (2 Corinthians 3:18).
