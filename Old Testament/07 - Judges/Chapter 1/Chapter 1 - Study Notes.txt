Judges - Chapter 1
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
After Joshua's death, Judah and Simeon lead the attack on the remaining Canaanites and achieve partial victories. Multiple tribes fail to fully drive out the inhabitants, setting the stage for future compromise.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Unknown, traditionally attributed to Samuel. Judges covers
approximately 350 years (1380-1050 BC) between Joshua's death and the
rise of the monarchy. Hebrew title: 'Shophetim' — 'Judges' (deliverers/
rulers raised by God). The book records Israel's repeated cycle: sin →
oppression → crying out → deliverance → rest → sin again. The refrain:
'In those days there was no king in Israel: every man did that which was
right in his own eyes' (17:6, 21:25). The book demonstrates humanity's
need for a righteous king — ultimately fulfilled in Christ.

Historical Context: Chapter 1 opens immediately after Joshua's death and
records the incomplete conquest of Canaan, tribe by tribe. This chapter
serves as a prologue to the entire book — explaining WHY Israel fell into
the cycles of sin and oppression that follow. The answer: they failed to
obey God's command to drive out the Canaanites completely. Each tribe's
failure is catalogued with devastating repetition: "did not drive out."
The chapter moves from initial success (Judah's victories in the south)
to progressive failure (tribe after tribe compromising with the enemy).
The pattern is clear: partial obedience is disobedience. What Israel
tolerated eventually enslaved them. The Canaanites they allowed to remain
became the source of idolatry, intermarriage, and spiritual corruption
that defines the rest of the book.

Judah's Campaign — Initial Success (vv.1-20): After Joshua's death,
Israel inquires of the LORD who should lead the attack. God designates
Judah — the tribe of kingship and the line of Christ. Judah invites
Simeon as an ally (v.3). They defeat 10,000 at Bezek and capture
Adoni-bezek, cutting off his thumbs and great toes (v.6). Adoni-bezek
acknowledges divine justice: "As I have done, so God hath requited me"
(v.7). Judah captures Jerusalem (v.8), fights in the hill country, the
Negeb, and the lowland (v.9). Caleb's conquest of Hebron and Debir is
recounted (vv.10-15), paralleling Joshua 15:13-19. Othniel — who will
become the first judge (3:9) — wins Caleb's daughter Achsah by capturing
Kiriath-sepher. The Kenites (descendants of Moses' father-in-law) settle
in Judah's territory (v.16). Judah and Simeon destroy Zephath/Hormah
(v.17) and capture Gaza, Ashkelon, and Ekron (v.18). The LORD was with
Judah (v.19) — but even Judah "could not drive out the inhabitants of
the valley, because they had chariots of iron" (v.19). This is the first
crack: iron chariots intimidated them more than God's promise empowered
them.

Benjamin's Failure (v.21): Benjamin "did not drive out the Jebusites that
inhabited Jerusalem." The Jebusites remained until David's conquest
(2 Samuel 5:6-7). This failure persisted for centuries.

The House of Joseph (vv.22-26): Joseph (Ephraim and Manasseh) attacks
Bethel with the LORD's help. They use a spy who shows them the city's
entrance — reminiscent of Rahab at Jericho. But unlike Jericho, they let
the man go and he builds another pagan city called Luz (v.26). Incomplete
destruction leads to pagan reproduction.

The Catalogue of Failure (vv.27-36): The chapter's tone shifts
dramatically. A litany of failure: Manasseh "did not drive out" Beth-shean,
Taanach, Dor, Ibleam, Megiddo (v.27). Ephraim "did not drive out" Gezer
(v.29). Zebulun "did not drive out" Kitron and Nahalol (v.30). Asher "did
not drive out" Acco, Sidon, Ahlab, Achzib, Helbah, Aphik, Rehob (v.31).
Note the devastating reversal in v.32: "the Asherites dwelt AMONG the
Canaanites" — not the Canaanites among them. Asher became the minority in
their own inheritance. Naphtali "did not drive out" Beth-shemesh and
Beth-anath (v.33). Dan was actually "forced into the mountain" by the
Amorites (v.34) — pushed back, losing ground. The progression: from
victory (Judah) to compromise (forced labor) to coexistence to being
dominated. This is the trajectory of tolerated sin.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Bezek: Located in the central hill country, where Judah and Simeon defeat Adoni-bezek
- Jerusalem: Captured and burned by Judah (v.8) but Jebusites remain in Benjamin's territory (v.21)
- Hebron/Debir: Southern hill country — Caleb's inheritance; Othniel captures Kiriath-sepher (Debir)
- Catalogue of unconquered territory spans from Beth-shean and Megiddo (Jezreel Valley) to coastal cities (Gaza, Ashkelon, Ekron)

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "And the LORD said, 'Judah shall go up; behold, I have given
        the land into his hand.'" (ESV) — God designates Judah as leader. The
        tribe of kingship, the line of David, the line of Christ. God's
        choice of Judah is consistent throughout Scripture.

v.7  — "And Adoni-bezek said, 'Seventy kings with their thumbs and
        their great toes cut off used to pick up scraps under my table.
        As I have done, so God has repaid me.'" (ESV) — Even a pagan
        king recognizes divine justice. The principle of sowing and
        reaping (Galatians 6:7) is acknowledged by Israel's enemies.

v.19 — "And the LORD was with Judah, and he took possession of the hill
        country, but he could not drive out the inhabitants of the plain
        because they had chariots of iron." (ESV) — A troubling verse.
        Was God unable? No — Israel's faith failed. They trusted God in
        the hills but feared iron chariots in the plains. The limitation
        was in their faith, not in God's power.

v.28 — "When Israel grew strong, they put the Canaanites to forced
        labor, but did not drive them out completely." (ESV) —
        Israel's compromise: using the enemy for profit rather than
        obeying God's command to destroy them. Pragmatism replaced
        obedience. They chose economic benefit over spiritual purity.

v.34 — "The Amorites pressed the people of Dan back into the hill
        country, for they did not allow them to come down to the
        plain." (ESV) — The ultimate reversal: instead of Israel driving
        out the enemy, the enemy drives out Israel. Dan is pushed back,
        dominated, confined. This is where tolerated sin leads — from
        compromise to captivity.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/judges-1/
  Guzik notes the progressive deterioration: Judah conquers with God's
  help, but even Judah fails with iron chariots. Each subsequent tribe
  does worse. The chapter is a catalogue of incomplete obedience — and
  incomplete obedience is disobedience. He emphasizes that Israel chose
  economic benefit (forced labor) over spiritual obedience (driving out).

Charles Spurgeon:
  "The Canaanites whom Israel spared became thorns in their sides and
  snares to their souls. So it is with the sins we tolerate. We think
  we can manage them, use them, profit from them — but they will manage
  us. Every sin left alive in the heart will grow stronger while we grow
  weaker. Drive them out utterly, or they will drive you out."
TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1  — KJV: "the children of Israel asked the LORD" / ESV: "the people
       of Israel inquired of the LORD" — Hebrew "vayish'alu b'nei-Yisrael
       ba'Adonai." "Sha'al" = to ask, inquire. This likely involved the
       Urim and Thummim through the high priest — the appointed means of
       divine inquiry.

v.7  — KJV: "Threescore and ten kings" / ESV: "Seventy kings" — Same
       Hebrew number. KJV uses the archaic English counting system.
       "Requited" (KJV) vs. "repaid" (ESV) — Hebrew "shillem" = to
       repay, recompense. Both translations capture the lex talionis
       (law of retribution) that Adoni-bezek acknowledges.

v.14 — KJV: "she lighted from off her ass" / ESV: "she dismounted from
       her donkey" — Hebrew "vatitsnach me'al hachamor." "Tsanach" = to
       alight, dismount. The action signals she has a request — a gesture
       of respect and petition before her father.

v.15 — KJV: "the nether springs" / ESV: "the lower springs" — Hebrew
       "gullot tachtiyyot." "Nether" is archaic English for "lower."
       Caleb gives generously — both upper and lower springs, ensuring
       adequate water in the arid Negeb.

v.19 — KJV: "could not drive out" / ESV: "could not drive out" — Hebrew
       "lo horishh." The Hiphil of "yarash" = to dispossess. The text
       says Judah could not — but the theological question is whether
       this was inability or unwillingness. Given that God promised the
       entire land (v.2), the failure was in faith, not in God's power.
       Iron chariots are nothing to the God who parted the Red Sea.

v.27 — KJV: "the Canaanites would dwell in that land" / ESV: "the
       Canaanites persisted in dwelling in that land" — Hebrew "vayo'el
       haK'na'ani lashevet." "Ya'al" = to be determined, to persist.
       The Canaanites were determined to stay — and Israel lacked the
       determination to remove them.

v.28 — KJV: "did not utterly drive them out" / ESV: "did not drive them
       out completely" — Hebrew "v'horeysh lo horisho." The emphatic
       construction (infinitive absolute + finite verb) stresses the
       completeness of the failure. They did NOT completely dispossess.

v.32 — KJV: "the Asherites dwelt among the Canaanites" / ESV: "the
       Asherites lived among the Canaanites" — Note the reversal from
       earlier verses where "the Canaanites dwelt among them." Now
       Asher is the minority — they live among the Canaanites rather
       than the Canaanites living among them. The language signals
       complete failure.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Adoni-bezek    — "Lord of Bezek." A Canaanite king who had mutilated 70
               other kings. His name may also mean "lord of lightning."
               His acknowledgment of divine justice (v.7) shows that even
               pagans recognize God's retributive righteousness.

Chariots of Iron — Advanced military technology of the Canaanites. Iron
               was superior to bronze and gave significant advantage in
               flat terrain. Israel's failure to overcome them reveals
               a faith problem, not a military one — God had promised
               the entire land regardless of enemy technology.

Hormah         — Hebrew "cherem" = devoted to destruction, ban. The city
               of Zephath was renamed Hormah after being "utterly
               destroyed" (devoted to God through total destruction).
               This was the proper response to Canaanite cities.

Forced Labor   — Hebrew "mas" = tribute, forced labor. Israel's
               compromise: instead of destroying the Canaanites as God
               commanded, they enslaved them for economic benefit. This
               pragmatic disobedience seemed wise but led to spiritual
               disaster.

Kenites        — Descendants of Moses' father-in-law (Jethro/Hobab).
               A nomadic people who allied with Israel and settled in
               Judah's territory. They remained faithful allies
               throughout Israel's history (cf. 1 Samuel 15:6).

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.1 — ASV: "asked of Jehovah" / WEB: "asked of Yahweh" / NET: "asked the Lord" — The divine name YHWH is rendered distinctly in each: ASV uses "Jehovah," WEB uses "Yahweh" (closest to Hebrew pronunciation), NET/KJV use "the LORD/Lord." This distinction recurs throughout Judges wherever the covenant name appears.

- v.17 — KJV: "utterly destroyed it" / NET: "wiped out Zephath" / ASV/WEB: "utterly destroyed it" — Hebrew "hecharim" (devoted to destruction, cherem/herem). NET uses dynamic equivalence ("wiped out") losing the covenantal warfare concept of devoting a city to God through total destruction. The term herem is central to Judges' theology of holy war.

- v.19 — NET: "they had chariots with iron-rimmed wheels" / KJV/ASV/WEB: "chariots of iron" — NET adds interpretive detail ("iron-rimmed wheels") not explicit in Hebrew "rikbey barzel." The Hebrew simply says "chariots of iron." NET's expansion clarifies the military technology but goes beyond the literal text.

- v.24 — NET: "the spies spotted a man" / ASV/WEB: "the watchers saw a man" / KJV: "the spies saw a man" — Hebrew "shomerim" literally means "watchers/guards." NET and KJV interpret as "spies" while ASV/WEB retain the more literal "watchers." The distinction matters: were they intelligence operatives or sentries observing?

- v.28 — ASV: "put the Canaanites to taskwork" / WEB: "put the Canaanites to forced labor" / NET: "forced the Canaanites to do hard labor" / KJV: "put the Canaanites to tribute" — Hebrew "mas" = corvée labor. KJV's "tribute" suggests financial payment; ASV's "taskwork" and WEB/NET's "forced labor" better capture the Hebrew concept of compulsory labor service.

- v.34 — NET: "The Amorites forced the people of Dan to live in the hill country" / WEB: "forced the children of Dan into the hill country" — NET adds interpretive "to live in," making explicit what is implied. Both capture the devastating reversal: the enemy pushes Israel back rather than Israel driving out the enemy.

- v.14 — NET: "she charmed her father so she could ask him for some land" / KJV: "she moved him to ask of her father a field" / ASV: "she moved him to ask of her father a field" — Hebrew "vatesithehu" is debated. NET interprets Achsah as persuading her father directly; KJV/ASV suggest she urged her husband to ask. The subject and object of persuasion differ significantly between translations.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Partial obedience is disobedience (v.28). Israel obeyed partially —
   they fought, they conquered some cities, they put enemies to forced
   labor. But God said "utterly drive them out." Anything less than
   complete obedience is rebellion dressed in religious clothing. What
   sins do I tolerate, manage, or profit from rather than utterly
   destroying?

2. What we tolerate will dominate us (vv.32,34). The progression is
   clear: first Israel dwells with the Canaanites, then the Canaanites
   dwell among Israel, then Israel dwells among the Canaanites, then
   the enemy forces Israel out. Sin follows the same trajectory — first
   we tolerate it, then it tolerates us, then it dominates us.

3. Iron chariots vs. God's promise (v.19). Israel trusted God in the
   hills but feared technology in the plains. We do the same — trusting
   God in familiar territory but fearing when circumstances seem
   overwhelming. The God who parted the Red Sea is not intimidated by
   iron chariots. Our limitations are in faith, not in God's power.

4. Pragmatism is not obedience (v.28). Forced labor seemed wise —
   economic benefit from the enemy. But God didn't say "profit from
   them." He said "drive them out." When we substitute our wisdom for
   God's commands, we call it pragmatism. God calls it disobedience.

5. The first chapter sets the trajectory for the whole book. Every
   failure recorded here bears fruit in the chapters that follow.
   Compromise in one generation becomes captivity in the next. The sins
   of the fathers are visited upon the children — not as punishment but
   as consequence. What we fail to deal with, our children will have to
   fight.
