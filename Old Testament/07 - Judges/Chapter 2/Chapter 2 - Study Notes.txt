Judges - Chapter 2
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
The Angel of the LORD rebukes Israel for making covenants with Canaanites and not tearing down their altars. A new generation arises that does not know the LORD, and the cycle of apostasy, oppression, and deliverance begins.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Unknown, traditionally attributed to Samuel. Judges covers
approximately 350 years (1380-1050 BC) between Joshua's death and the
rise of the monarchy. Hebrew title: 'Shophetim' — 'Judges' (deliverers/
rulers raised by God). The book records Israel's repeated cycle: sin →
oppression → crying out → deliverance → rest → sin again. The refrain:
'In those days there was no king in Israel: every man did that which was
right in his own eyes' (17:6, 21:25). The book demonstrates humanity's
need for a righteous king — ultimately fulfilled in Christ.

Historical Context: Chapter 2 is the theological introduction to the
entire book of Judges. It contains three critical elements: (1) The Angel
of the LORD's rebuke at Bochim (vv.1-5), (2) the death of Joshua's
generation and the rise of a generation "which knew not the LORD" (vv.6-10),
and (3) the introduction of the recurring cycle that defines the book:
sin → oppression → crying out → deliverance → rest → sin again (vv.11-23).
This chapter explains the theological framework for everything that
follows. Israel's failure in Chapter 1 (incomplete conquest) now receives
divine commentary — God declares He will no longer drive out the nations
before them. The remaining Canaanites will serve as a test and a
consequence. The chapter also introduces the concept of the "judges" —
deliverers raised by God in response to Israel's groaning under
oppression. Yet even this grace is met with ingratitude: each generation
becomes "more corrupt than their fathers" (v.19).

The Angel's Rebuke at Bochim (vv.1-5): "An angel of the LORD came up
from Gilgal to Bochim" (v.1). This is likely a theophany — the Angel of
the LORD (pre-incarnate Christ) speaks in the first person as God: "I
made you to go up out of Egypt... I said, I will never break my covenant
with you" (v.1). He moves from Gilgal (the place of Israel's first
encampment and circumcision — a place of covenant renewal) to Bochim
(which means "weepers"). God's indictment: "Ye shall make no league with
the inhabitants of this land; ye shall throw down their altars: but ye
have not obeyed my voice" (v.2). The consequence: "I will not drive them
out from before you; but they shall be as thorns in your sides, and their
gods shall be a snare unto you" (v.3). The people weep — hence "Bochim"
— but their tears produce no lasting repentance. They sacrifice to the
LORD (v.5) but do not change their behavior.

Joshua's Death and the Lost Generation (vv.6-10): The narrative flashes
back to Joshua's dismissal of the people (v.6; cf. Joshua 24:28-31).
Israel served the LORD during Joshua's lifetime and during the lifetime
of the elders who had witnessed God's mighty works (v.7). Joshua dies at
110 (v.8) and is buried in Timnath-heres (v.9). Then the devastating
statement: "there arose another generation after them, which knew not the
LORD, nor yet the works which he had done for Israel" (v.10). This is the
hinge of the book — the failure to transmit faith to the next generation.
The generation that saw God's works believed; the generation that only
heard about them did not. This indicts the parents who failed to teach
(cf. Deuteronomy 6:6-9).

The Cycle Introduced (vv.11-23): Israel "did evil in the sight of the
LORD, and served Baalim" (v.11). They "forsook the LORD" and followed
the gods of surrounding nations (v.12). God's anger burns; He delivers
them to "spoilers" and "enemies" (v.14). They cannot stand before their
enemies (v.14). Yet in mercy, "the LORD raised up judges, which delivered
them" (v.16). God was "moved to pity by their groaning" (v.18, ESV). But
when the judge died, "they returned, and corrupted themselves more than
their fathers" (v.19). Each cycle spirals downward — not a circle but a
descending spiral. God's response: He will leave the nations as a test
(v.22) — to prove whether Israel will walk in His ways or not.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Gilgal to Bochim: The Angel of the LORD moves from Gilgal (Israel's first camp near Jericho) to Bochim (likely near Bethel in the central highlands)
- The territories of the unconquered nations surround Israel on all sides — a test of obedience
- Bochim ("weepers"): Named for Israel's weeping when confronted with their failure to drive out the Canaanites

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "Now the angel of the LORD went up from Gilgal to Bochim. And he
        said, 'I brought you up from Egypt and brought you into the land
        that I swore to give to your fathers. I said, "I will never break
        my covenant with you."'" (ESV) — God's faithfulness contrasted with
        Israel's unfaithfulness. He kept His covenant; they broke theirs.

v.2  — "But you have not obeyed my voice. What is this you have done?" (ESV)
        — God's question to His people. Not ignorance but grief. He asks
        them to confront their own disobedience. The question demands an
        answer they cannot give.

v.10 — "And all that generation also were gathered to their fathers. And
        there arose another generation after them who did not know the LORD
        or the work that he had done for Israel." (ESV) — The most
        devastating verse in the chapter. One generation's failure to
        transmit faith produces a generation of apostasy. "Did not know" =
        had no personal, experiential knowledge of God.

v.16 — "Then the LORD raised up judges, who saved them out of the hand of
        those who plundered them." (ESV) — "Then" — the word of grace.
        Despite their sin, God raises deliverers. This is the gospel
        pattern: undeserved rescue.

v.19 — "But whenever the judge died, they turned back and were more
        corrupt than their fathers." (ESV) — The downward spiral. Each
        generation does not merely repeat the sin but exceeds it. Sin is
        progressive — it never stays at the same level.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/judges-2/
  Guzik emphasizes that the Angel of the LORD speaks as God Himself —
  this is a Christophany. He notes that Bochim's tears were emotional
  but not transformative — sorrow without repentance. The failure to
  teach the next generation (v.10) is the root cause of all that follows.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "A generation arose that knew not the LORD. How terrible! Their fathers
  had seen the Jordan part and Jericho fall — and yet they raised children
  who knew nothing of it. The greatest miracle God ever works is the
  transmission of living faith from one generation to the next. And the
  greatest tragedy is when that transmission fails. You may conquer
  Canaan and still lose your children."
TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1  — KJV: "an angel of the LORD" / ESV: "the angel of the LORD" —
       Hebrew "malakh Adonai." The Hebrew lacks the definite article but
       the context (speaking as God in first person) strongly suggests
       this is THE Angel of the LORD — a theophany, the pre-incarnate
       Christ. ESV's "the" is interpretive but contextually justified.

v.3  — KJV: "thorns in your sides" / ESV: "thorns in your sides" —
       Hebrew "l'tsiddim" = "for sides" (i.e., adversaries at your
       sides). Some manuscripts read "l'tsarim" = "for distresses/
       adversaries." The image is of constant irritation and pain from
       the nations left among them.

v.11 — KJV: "served Baalim" / ESV: "served the Baals" — Hebrew
       "ha-Be'alim" (plural of Baal). KJV retains the Hebrew plural
       ending "-im." Baal = "lord/master." The plural indicates the
       various local manifestations of Baal worship throughout Canaan.

v.13 — KJV: "Ashtaroth" / ESV: "the Ashtaroth" — Hebrew "ha-Ashtarot"
       (plural of Ashtoreth). The Canaanite fertility goddess, consort
       of Baal. Worship involved sexual immorality and cult prostitution.
       The plural indicates multiple local shrines and images.

v.17 — KJV: "they went a whoring after other gods" / ESV: "they whored
       after other gods" — Hebrew "zanu acharey." "Zanah" = to commit
       fornication, to be unfaithful. The metaphor of spiritual adultery
       — Israel as God's unfaithful wife pursuing other lovers. This
       imagery runs throughout the prophets (Hosea, Ezekiel 16, 23).

v.18 — KJV: "it repented the LORD because of their groanings" / ESV:
       "the LORD was moved to pity by their groaning" — Hebrew "yinnachem
       Adonai." "Nacham" = to be sorry, to relent, to have compassion.
       KJV's "repented" does not mean God changed His mind about sin but
       that He was moved with compassion. ESV's "moved to pity" captures
       the emotional sense more clearly for modern readers.

v.19 — KJV: "corrupted themselves more than their fathers" / ESV: "were
       more corrupt than their fathers" — Hebrew "hishchitu" (Hiphil of
       "shachat" = to corrupt, destroy). The intensifying pattern: each
       generation exceeds the previous in corruption. The spiral descends.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Bochim         — Hebrew "weepers." The place where Israel wept at the
               Angel's rebuke. Location uncertain — possibly near Bethel
               or Shiloh. Named for the weeping that occurred there, but
               the tears did not produce lasting change.

The Angel of the LORD — Hebrew "malakh Adonai." In the Old Testament,
               this figure often speaks as God, accepts worship, and is
               identified with God Himself (Genesis 16:13; Exodus 3:2-6;
               Judges 13:18-22). Understood by Christian theology as a
               pre-incarnate appearance of Christ (Christophany).

Baalim/Baals   — Plural of Baal ("lord/master"). The chief male deity of
               Canaan — a storm/fertility god. Worshipped at local high
               places with altars, pillars, and sacred groves. Worship
               involved ritual prostitution and sometimes child sacrifice.

Ashtaroth      — Plural of Ashtoreth. The chief female deity of Canaan —
               goddess of fertility, love, and war. Consort of Baal.
               Equivalent to Babylonian Ishtar and Greek Aphrodite.
               Worship involved cult prostitution.

Gilgal         — "Rolling" — where God "rolled away the reproach of
               Egypt" (Joshua 5:9). Israel's first camp in Canaan, place
               of circumcision and Passover. A place of covenant
               beginnings — the Angel moves FROM Gilgal (covenant) TO
               Bochim (weeping) — from grace to judgment.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.1 — ASV: "the angel of Jehovah" / WEB: "Yahweh's angel" / NET: "The angel of the Lord" — The divine name distinction continues. WEB uniquely uses the possessive "Yahweh's angel" rather than "angel of Yahweh," slightly altering the construction but preserving the covenant name.

- v.10 — NET: "a new generation grew up that had not personally experienced the Lord's presence or seen what he had done for Israel" / ASV/WEB: "knew not Jehovah/Yahweh, nor yet the work which he had wrought for Israel" — NET expands "knew not" into "had not personally experienced the Lord's presence," adding interpretive clarity. The Hebrew "yada" (to know) implies experiential, covenantal knowledge — not mere intellectual awareness. NET makes this explicit.

- v.11 — ASV: "did that which was evil in the sight of Jehovah" / WEB: "did that which was evil in Yahweh's sight" / NET: "did evil before the Lord" — This key Judges formula ("evil in the sight of the LORD") is rendered differently: ASV/KJV retain the literal "in the sight of," WEB uses "in Yahweh's sight," while NET simplifies to "before the Lord," losing the anthropomorphic "eyes/sight" metaphor.

- v.16 — NET: "The Lord raised up leaders who delivered them from these robbers" / ASV: "Jehovah raised up judges, who saved them" / WEB: "Yahweh raised up judges, who saved them" — NET renders "shophetim" as "leaders" rather than "judges" — a significant interpretive choice acknowledging these figures were military deliverers, not merely judicial officials. This captures the Hebrew sense better but loses the traditional book title connection.

- v.17 — ASV: "they played the harlot after other gods" / WEB: "they prostituted themselves to other gods" / NET: "they prostituted themselves to other gods" — Hebrew "zanu acharey." KJV's "went a whoring" is archaic; ASV's "played the harlot" retains the adultery metaphor; WEB and NET modernize to "prostituted themselves." All preserve the spiritual infidelity imagery central to the prophetic tradition.

- v.18 — ASV: "it repented Jehovah because of their groaning" / WEB: "it grieved Yahweh because of their groaning" / NET: "The Lord felt sorry for them when they cried out in agony" — Hebrew "yinnachem." ASV retains KJV's "repented" (potentially misleading); WEB's "grieved" captures divine compassion; NET's "felt sorry" is most dynamic but risks trivializing divine pathos. The Hebrew conveys deep emotional response, not regret over a mistake.

- v.14 — ASV: "he delivered them into the hands of spoilers that despoiled them" / WEB: "he delivered them into the hands of raiders who plundered them" / NET: "handed them over to robbers who plundered them" — Hebrew "shosim" = plunderers/raiders. KJV/ASV use "spoilers"; WEB uses "raiders"; NET uses "robbers." Each carries different connotations — "robbers" (NET) implies criminality, while "raiders/spoilers" suggests military pillaging, which better fits the context of national enemies.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. God's faithfulness exposes our unfaithfulness (vv.1-2). God kept every
   promise — "I brought you up... I brought you in... I said I will never
   break my covenant." Against this backdrop, Israel's failure is stark.
   God's consistency reveals our inconsistency. His faithfulness is both
   our comfort and our conviction.

2. Tears without repentance change nothing (vv.4-5). Israel wept at
   Bochim — but the rest of the book shows they did not change. Emotional
   responses to conviction are not the same as repentance. Godly sorrow
   produces repentance (2 Corinthians 7:10); worldly sorrow produces
   only tears.

3. The failure to transmit faith (v.10). One generation saw God's works
   and believed. The next generation "knew not the LORD." The gap between
   these two statements is a parenting failure. Faith must be actively
   transmitted — it is not inherited automatically. Deuteronomy 6:6-9
   commands intentional, daily, relentless teaching. When parents assume
   their children will "catch" faith, they lose them.

4. The downward spiral (v.19). Sin never stays at the same level. Each
   generation was "more corrupt than their fathers." Tolerated sin grows.
   Compromise deepens. What one generation tolerates, the next embraces.
   What one generation embraces, the next celebrates. The trajectory is
   always downward unless interrupted by grace.

5. "Nevertheless" — the word of grace (v.16). Despite everything, God
   raises up deliverers. He is "moved to pity by their groaning" (v.18).
   God's mercy is not earned by Israel's repentance — it flows from His
   own compassionate nature. This is the gospel: God rescues the
   undeserving because of who HE is, not because of who we are.
