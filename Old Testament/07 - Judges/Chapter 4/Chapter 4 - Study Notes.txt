Judges - Chapter 4
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Deborah the prophetess summons Barak to fight Sisera's army, and God routs the Canaanite chariots at the river Kishon. Jael drives a tent peg through Sisera's skull, ending twenty years of oppression.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Unknown, traditionally attributed to Samuel. Judges covers
approximately 350 years (1380-1050 BC) between Joshua's death and the
rise of the monarchy. Hebrew title: 'Shophetim' — 'Judges' (deliverers/
rulers raised by God). The book records Israel's repeated cycle: sin →
oppression → crying out → deliverance → rest → sin again. The refrain:
'In those days there was no king in Israel: every man did that which was
right in his own eyes' (17:6, 21:25). The book demonstrates humanity's
need for a righteous king — ultimately fulfilled in Christ.

Historical Context: Chapter 4 records the deliverance of Israel through
Deborah and Barak from the oppression of Jabin king of Canaan and his
military commander Sisera. This is the only narrative in Judges where a
woman serves as judge — Deborah is both prophetess and judge, the
spiritual and civil leader of Israel. The chapter features two remarkable
women: Deborah, who commissions and accompanies Barak to battle, and
Jael, who kills Sisera with a tent peg. Deborah's prophecy is fulfilled:
"the LORD shall sell Sisera into the hand of a woman" (v.9). The military
situation is dire — Sisera commands 900 iron chariots, the ancient
equivalent of tanks. Israel has been "mightily oppressed" for twenty
years (v.3). Yet God draws Sisera to the river Kishon where his chariots
become a liability (cf. 5:21 — the river sweeps them away). The chapter
demonstrates that God's deliverance is not limited by gender, military
technology, or human weakness — He uses whomever He chooses.

Deborah the Judge (vv.1-5): After Ehud's death, Israel sins again (v.1).
God sells them to Jabin king of Canaan (v.2) — a dynasty name; an earlier
Jabin was defeated by Joshua (Joshua 11:1-11). Sisera, his general, has
900 iron chariots and oppresses Israel cruelly for twenty years (v.3).
Deborah — "a prophetess, the wife of Lapidoth" — judges Israel (v.4).
She sits under a palm tree between Ramah and Bethel, and Israel comes to
her for judgment (v.5). She is the only judge described as actually
adjudicating disputes — she functions as both spiritual leader (prophetess)
and civil authority (judge).

Barak's Commission and Condition (vv.6-10): Deborah summons Barak and
delivers God's command: gather 10,000 men from Naphtali and Zebulun at
Mount Tabor (v.6). God promises to draw Sisera to the river Kishon and
deliver him into Barak's hand (v.7). Barak's response: "If thou wilt go
with me, then I will go: but if thou wilt not go with me, then I will not
go" (v.8). This is not necessarily cowardice — it may reflect his desire
for the prophetess's presence as assurance of God's presence. But Deborah
pronounces the consequence: "the journey that thou takest shall not be for
thine honour; for the LORD shall sell Sisera into the hand of a woman"
(v.9). The glory of the final kill will go to a woman — not Deborah, but
Jael.

The Battle (vv.12-16): Sisera musters his 900 chariots at the river
Kishon (v.13). Deborah gives the command: "Up; for this is the day in
which the LORD hath delivered Sisera into thine hand: is not the LORD gone
out before thee?" (v.14). Barak descends from Mount Tabor with 10,000 men.
"The LORD discomfited Sisera, and all his chariots" (v.15). The word
"discomfited" (Hebrew "vayaham") suggests supernatural confusion and panic
— God Himself routs the enemy. Sisera abandons his chariot and flees on
foot (v.15). Barak pursues and destroys the entire army — "there was not
a man left" (v.16).

Jael Kills Sisera (vv.17-22): Sisera flees to the tent of Jael, wife of
Heber the Kenite — there was peace between Jabin and Heber's house (v.17).
Jael invites him in: "Turn in, my lord... fear not" (v.18). She covers
him, gives him milk (v.19), and he falls asleep from exhaustion. Then
"Jael Heber's wife took a nail of the tent, and took an hammer in her
hand, and went softly unto him, and smote the nail into his temples, and
fastened it into the ground" (v.21). When Barak arrives pursuing Sisera,
Jael shows him the dead commander (v.22). Deborah's prophecy is fulfilled
— Sisera is sold "into the hand of a woman."

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Hazor: Jabin's capital in upper Galilee (rebuilt since Joshua 11) — oppressing the northern tribes
- Harosheth-hagoiim: Sisera's base, likely at the western end of the Jezreel Valley near the Kishon River
- Kedesh (Naphtali): Where Barak is summoned; Mount Tabor: The staging ground overlooking the Jezreel plain

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.3  — "Then the people of Israel cried out to the LORD for help, for he
        had 900 chariots of iron and he oppressed the people of Israel
        cruelly for twenty years." (ESV) — The cry of desperation that
        precedes deliverance. Twenty years of cruel oppression — God allows
        the consequences to drive them back to Himself.

v.8  — "Barak said to her, 'If you will go with me, I will go, but if
        you will not go with me, I will not go.'" (ESV) — Barak's
        condition. He wants the prophetess present — wanting assurance of
        God's presence. His faith is real but incomplete.

v.9  — "The LORD will sell Sisera into the hand of a woman." (ESV) —
        Deborah's prophecy. The ultimate honor of the victory will go to
        a woman — a double shame for Sisera (killed by a woman) and a
        rebuke to Barak (the glory he could have had goes to another).

v.14 — "'Up! For this is the day in which the LORD has given Sisera into
        your hand. Does not the LORD go out before you?'" (ESV) — Deborah's
        battle cry. Note the past tense: "has given" — it is already
        done in God's purposes. Faith speaks of future victory as
        accomplished fact.

v.21 — "But Jael the wife of Heber took a tent peg, and took a hammer
        in her hand. Then she went softly to him and drove the peg into
        his temple." (ESV) — Jael's decisive act. A tent peg and hammer
        — the tools of a nomadic woman. God uses what is at hand.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/judges-4/
  Guzik notes that Deborah is unique among the judges — she is already
  functioning as judge before the crisis, not raised up in response to
  it. He sees Barak's request not as cowardice but as incomplete faith.
  The chapter shows God using women in leadership when men fail to lead.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "Sisera had nine hundred chariots of iron, but God had the river Kishon.
  All the iron in the world cannot stand against the waters of God's
  providence. The mightiest armies are nothing when God fights against
  them. Sisera fled on foot — his chariots became his coffin. So it is
  with every weapon formed against God's people: what the enemy trusts in
  becomes the instrument of his destruction."
TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.2  — KJV: "Harosheth of the Gentiles" / ESV: "Harosheth-hagoyim" —
       Hebrew "Charoshet ha-Goyim." KJV translates "ha-Goyim" as "of the
       Gentiles." ESV transliterates the full Hebrew name. "Harosheth"
       may mean "woodland" or "craftsman's place." The location suggests
       a mixed population area.

v.4  — KJV: "the wife of Lapidoth" / ESV: "the wife of Lappidoth" —
       Hebrew "eshet Lappidot." "Lappidot" means "torches/flames." Some
       scholars suggest this could be a description ("woman of torches/
       fiery woman") rather than a husband's name, but the traditional
       reading as her husband's name is standard.

v.9  — KJV: "the LORD shall sell Sisera into the hand of a woman" / ESV:
       "the LORD will sell Sisera into the hand of a woman" — Hebrew
       "b'yad ishah yimkor Adonai et-Sisera." "Makar" = to sell, hand
       over. The same verb used of God "selling" Israel to their enemies
       (v.2) — now God "sells" the enemy to a woman. Poetic justice.

v.15 — KJV: "the LORD discomfited Sisera" / ESV: "the LORD routed
       Sisera" — Hebrew "vayaham Adonai." "Hamam" = to confuse, throw
       into panic, rout. This word is used of God's supernatural
       intervention in battle (Exodus 14:24; Joshua 10:10; 1 Samuel 7:10).
       It implies divine action beyond natural military engagement —
       God Himself caused the panic and confusion.

v.18 — KJV: "she covered him with a mantle" / ESV: "she covered him with
       a rug" — Hebrew "ba-s'mikhah." This word occurs only here in the
       Old Testament. It likely refers to a thick covering — a blanket,
       rug, or heavy cloth. The covering conceals Sisera and gives him
       false security.

v.21 — KJV: "a nail of the tent" / ESV: "a tent peg" — Hebrew "y'ted
       ha-ohel." "Yated" = a peg, pin, or stake. These were large wooden
       or metal stakes used to anchor tent ropes — substantial enough to
       be driven through a man's skull into the ground. Nomadic women
       were skilled at driving tent pegs — this was Jael's expertise.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Deborah        — "Bee." A prophetess and judge — the only female judge in
               the book. She judged Israel from under a palm tree between
               Ramah and Bethel. Both spiritual authority (prophetess) and
               civil authority (judge). Her name suggests industriousness
               and the ability to sting.

Barak          — "Lightning." Son of Abinoam from Kedesh-naphtali. The
               military commander who defeats Sisera's army. Listed in
               Hebrews 11:32 as a hero of faith despite his conditional
               response to Deborah. He obeyed — even if imperfectly.

Sisera         — Commander of Jabin's army. Commanded 900 iron chariots.
               Oppressed Israel for 20 years. Fled the battle on foot
               and was killed by Jael with a tent peg. His name may be
               non-Semitic, suggesting foreign (possibly Philistine or
               Sea Peoples) origin.

Jael           — "Mountain goat." Wife of Heber the Kenite. Killed Sisera
               with a tent peg while he slept in her tent. Celebrated as
               "blessed above women" in Deborah's song (5:24). She
               fulfilled Deborah's prophecy that Sisera would fall "into
               the hand of a woman."

Iron Chariots  — The dominant military technology of the period. Each
               chariot carried a driver and warrior, with iron fittings
               for durability. Effective on flat terrain but vulnerable
               near rivers and in mud. God drew Sisera to the Kishon
               where his chariots became a liability.

Mount Tabor    — A prominent dome-shaped mountain (1,843 feet) in the
               Jezreel Valley. Barak gathered his forces here — the high
               ground gave tactical advantage and forced Sisera's chariots
               to fight uphill or wait below.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.2 — ASV: "Jehovah sold them into the hand of Jabin" / WEB: "Yahweh sold them into the hand of Jabin" / NET: "The Lord turned them over to King Jabin" — Hebrew "vayyimk'rem." KJV/ASV/WEB retain the stark "sold" metaphor; NET softens to "turned them over." The "selling" language portrays God as sovereign over Israel's fate — they become slaves because He transfers ownership as judgment.

- v.3 — NET: "900 chariots with iron-rimmed wheels" / ASV/WEB/KJV: "nine hundred chariots of iron" — NET again adds interpretive detail ("iron-rimmed wheels") to Hebrew "rikbey barzel." This is the same expansion seen in 1:19. The Hebrew simply says "chariots of iron" without specifying which component was iron.

- v.4 — NET: "Deborah, a prophetess, wife of Lappidoth, was leading Israel at that time" / ASV: "Deborah, a prophetess, the wife of Lappidoth, she judged Israel" / WEB: "Deborah, a prophetess, the wife of Lappidoth, judged Israel" — NET renders "shophetah" (was judging) as "was leading" — again avoiding "judge" in favor of "leader." This obscures the judicial function emphasized in v.5 where people come for "judgment" (mishpat).

- v.9 — ASV: "Jehovah will sell Sisera into the hand of a woman" / WEB: "Yahweh will sell Sisera into a woman's hand" / NET: "the Lord will turn Sisera over to a woman" — NET again avoids "sell" (Hebrew "yimkor"), using "turn over." The selling language is theologically significant — it parallels God "selling" Israel to Jabin (v.2). God both sells Israel to enemies and sells enemies to Israel's deliverers.

- v.15 — ASV: "Jehovah discomfited Sisera" / WEB: "Yahweh confused Sisera" / NET: "The Lord routed Sisera" — Hebrew "vayyahom" = to confuse, throw into panic, discomfit. ASV retains archaic "discomfited"; WEB uses "confused"; NET interprets as "routed" (result rather than means). The same word describes God's action at the Red Sea (Exodus 14:24) and against the Philistines (1 Samuel 7:10).

- v.21 — ASV: "smote the pin into his temples, and it pierced through into the ground; for he was in a deep sleep; so he swooned and died" / WEB: "struck the pin into his temples, and it pierced through into the ground; for he was in a deep sleep; so he fainted and died" / NET: "drove the tent peg through his temple into the ground while he was asleep from exhaustion, and he died" — NET streamlines the narrative; ASV/WEB preserve the Hebrew's sequential detail. ASV's "swooned" (archaic for fainted/expired) adds pathos absent from NET's direct "he died."

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. God uses women when men fail to lead (vv.4,9). Deborah's leadership
   is not presented as the ideal but as God's provision when men abdicate.
   She is faithful, courageous, and Spirit-led. God is not limited by
   human conventions — He uses whoever is willing and available.

2. Incomplete faith is still faith (v.8). Barak's condition ("if you go,
   I'll go") shows imperfect faith — but he still went. He still obeyed.
   He still fought. Hebrews 11:32 lists him among the faithful. God uses
   imperfect faith. He does not demand perfection before He acts — He
   demands willingness.

3. God turns the enemy's strength into weakness (v.15). Nine hundred iron
   chariots — Israel's greatest fear (cf. 1:19). But God draws them to
   the river where chariots become death traps. What the enemy trusts in
   becomes the instrument of his defeat. God is never intimidated by
   human technology or power.

4. "Is not the LORD gone out before thee?" (v.14). Deborah's question to
   Barak is the question for every believer facing overwhelming odds. If
   God goes before you, what can stand against you? The issue is never
   the size of the enemy but the presence of God.

5. Jael's decisive action (v.21). She did not hesitate, deliberate, or
   second-guess. When the moment came, she acted with precision and
   courage. Sometimes faithfulness requires decisive, uncomfortable
   action — not endless deliberation.
