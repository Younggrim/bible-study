Judges - Chapter 13
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
The Angel of the LORD appears to Manoah's barren wife, announcing she will bear a son—Samson—who will be a Nazirite from the womb. The child is born and the Spirit of the LORD begins to stir him.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Unknown, traditionally attributed to Samuel. Judges covers
approximately 350 years (1380-1050 BC) between Joshua's death and the
rise of the monarchy. Hebrew title: 'Shophetim' — 'Judges' (deliverers/
rulers raised by God). The book records Israel's repeated cycle: sin →
oppression → crying out → deliverance → rest → sin again. The refrain:
'In those days there was no king in Israel: every man did that which was
right in his own eyes' (17:6, 21:25). The book demonstrates humanity's
need for a righteous king — ultimately fulfilled in Christ.

Historical Context: Chapter 13 introduces the Samson narrative (chapters
13-16) with the announcement of his birth. This is the longest oppression
in Judges — forty years under the Philistines (v.1). Notably, there is no
record of Israel "crying out" to God — suggesting they had become
comfortable in their subjugation. The birth announcement follows the
pattern of other miraculous births in Scripture: a barren woman, a divine
messenger, a child set apart for God's purposes (cf. Isaac, Samuel, John
the Baptist). The Angel of the LORD appears to Manoah's wife (unnamed in
the text) and announces that her son will be a Nazirite from the womb —
set apart to "begin to deliver Israel" (v.5). Note: only "begin" — Samson
will not complete the deliverance. The chapter features a remarkable
theophany: the Angel ascends in the flame of the altar (v.20), reveals
His name as "Wonderful/Secret" (v.18), and Manoah recognizes they have
"seen God" (v.22). His wife's faith exceeds his — she reasons that God
would not have accepted their offering if He intended to kill them (v.23).

The Philistine Oppression (v.1): Israel sins again; God delivers them to
the Philistines for forty years. The Philistines were a Sea Peoples group
who settled along the Mediterranean coast (Gaza, Ashkelon, Ashdod, Ekron,
Gath). They possessed iron technology and superior military organization.
Unlike previous oppressions, there is no record of Israel crying out —
they seem to have accepted Philistine dominance.

The Angel's Announcement (vv.2-7): Manoah's wife is barren (v.2). The
Angel of the LORD appears to her (not to Manoah) with the announcement:
"thou shalt conceive, and bear a son" (v.3). Instructions: no wine,
strong drink, or unclean food (v.4) — the Nazirite restrictions apply to
the mother during pregnancy. The child: "no razor shall come on his head:
for the child shall be a Nazarite unto God from the womb" (v.5). His
mission: "he shall begin to deliver Israel out of the hand of the
Philistines" (v.5).

Manoah's Prayer and the Second Appearance (vv.8-14): Manoah prays for
the Angel to return and "teach us what we shall do unto the child" (v.8).
God answers — the Angel appears again to the woman (v.9). She fetches
Manoah (v.10). The Angel repeats the instructions but adds nothing new
(vv.13-14) — the original message to the woman was sufficient.

The Theophany Revealed (vv.15-23): Manoah offers hospitality — a young
goat (v.15). The Angel redirects: "if thou wilt offer a burnt offering,
thou must offer it unto the LORD" (v.16). Manoah asks His name (v.17).
The answer: "Why askest thou thus after my name, seeing it is secret?"
(v.18) — Hebrew "peli" = wonderful, incomprehensible (same root as Isaiah
9:6, "Wonderful"). Manoah offers the sacrifice on a rock, and "the angel
of the LORD ascended in the flame of the altar" (v.20). They fall on
their faces. Manoah fears death: "We shall surely die, because we have
seen God" (v.22). His wife's superior faith: "If the LORD were pleased to
kill us, he would not have received a burnt offering... neither would he
have shewed us all these things" (v.23).

Samson's Birth (vv.24-25): The son is born and named Samson (Hebrew
"Shimshon" — related to "shemesh" = sun). "The child grew, and the LORD
blessed him" (v.24). "The Spirit of the LORD began to move him" (v.25) —
stirring him for his future mission.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Zorah and Eshtaol: Towns in Dan's territory on the border with Philistia — Samson's homeland (Shephelah/western foothills)
- Mahaneh-dan: "Camp of Dan" between Zorah and Eshtaol — where the Spirit first stirs Samson
- The Danite region sits on the frontier between Israelite highlands and the Philistine coastal plain

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.5  — "For behold, you shall conceive and bear a son. No razor shall
        come upon his head, for the child shall be a Nazirite to God from
        the womb, and he shall begin to save Israel from the hand of the
        Philistines." (ESV) — The announcement. Note "begin" — not
        complete. Samson starts what Samuel and David will finish.

v.18 — "Why do you ask my name, seeing it is wonderful?" (ESV) — The
        Angel's name is "Wonderful" (peli) — beyond human comprehension.
        This connects to Isaiah 9:6: "His name shall be called Wonderful."
        The pre-incarnate Christ reveals His nature: incomprehensible,
        transcendent, wonderful.

v.20 — "The angel of the LORD ascended in the flame of the altar." —
        The theophany's climax. The Angel does not merely depart — He
        ascends in the sacrifice's flame. He is both the recipient of
        worship and the one who ascends to heaven. This is deity.

v.22 — "We shall surely die, for we have seen God." (ESV) — Manoah's
        recognition: the Angel IS God. To see God is to die (Exodus
        33:20). Yet they live — because God chose to reveal Himself in
        grace, not judgment.

v.23 — "If the LORD were pleased to kill us, he would not have received
        a burnt offering and a meat offering at our hands." — The wife's
        theological reasoning. God's acceptance of their worship proves
        His favorable intent. A God who receives your offering does not
        intend your destruction.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/judges-13/
  Guzik notes the absence of Israel's cry — they have become comfortable
  in oppression. He emphasizes that Manoah's wife shows greater faith and
  perception than her husband throughout the chapter. The Angel's name
  "Wonderful" connects this theophany to the Messianic prophecy of
  Isaiah 9:6.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "The Angel's name is 'Wonderful' — secret, incomprehensible, beyond
  human grasp. This is Christ before Bethlehem — the eternal Son
  appearing in temporary form. His name cannot be contained in human
  language because His nature cannot be contained in human thought. He
  is wonderful in His person, wonderful in His works, wonderful in His
  grace. Ask not His name — worship His nature."
TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.5  — KJV: "a Nazarite unto God from the womb" / ESV: "a Nazirite to
       God from the womb" — Hebrew "n'zir Elohim min-ha-beten." "Nazir"
       = one separated/consecrated. The Nazirite vow (Numbers 6) normally
       was voluntary and temporary. Samson's is unique: imposed before
       birth and lifelong. Three restrictions: no grape products, no
       contact with the dead, no cutting of hair.

v.18 — KJV: "seeing it is secret" / ESV: "seeing it is wonderful" —
       Hebrew "v'hu peli." "Peli" can mean "wonderful, incomprehensible,
       hidden, beyond understanding." KJV's "secret" emphasizes the
       hiddenness; ESV's "wonderful" emphasizes the transcendence. Both
       are valid — the name is beyond human comprehension. The same root
       appears in Isaiah 9:6 ("Wonderful") and Psalm 139:6 ("too
       wonderful for me").

v.19 — KJV: "the angel did wondrously" / ESV: "to the one who works
       wonders" — Hebrew "maphli la'asot." This phrase is difficult.
       KJV reads it as describing the Angel's action (He did wonderfully).
       ESV reads it as a divine title (the One who works wonders). Both
       connect to the "wonderful" name of v.18.

v.22 — KJV: "we have seen God" / ESV: "we have seen God" — Hebrew
       "Elohim ra'inu." Manoah identifies the Angel as God Himself. This
       confirms the Angel of the LORD as a theophany — a visible
       manifestation of God (pre-incarnate Christ).

v.25 — KJV: "the Spirit of the LORD began to move him" / ESV: "the
       Spirit of the LORD began to stir him" — Hebrew "vatachel ruach
       Adonai l'fa'amo." "Pa'am" = to impel, stir, trouble. The Spirit
       began to agitate/stir Samson — creating restlessness and impulse
       toward his divine mission.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Samson         — Hebrew "Shimshon," related to "shemesh" (sun). A
               Nazirite from birth, from the tribe of Dan. The last and
               most complex of the major judges. Gifted with supernatural
               strength but plagued by moral weakness. He "began to
               deliver" Israel from the Philistines but did not complete
               the task.

Nazirite       — Hebrew "nazir" = separated/consecrated one. A person
               set apart to God by special vow (Numbers 6). Three
               restrictions: (1) no grape products (wine, vinegar,
               grapes, raisins); (2) no contact with dead bodies; (3) no
               cutting of hair. Samson's Nazirite status was lifelong and
               imposed before birth — unique in Scripture.

Manoah         — "Rest." A Danite from Zorah. Samson's father. He shows
               faith (praying for the Angel's return) but also fear
               (expecting death after seeing God). His wife demonstrates
               superior theological reasoning throughout the chapter.

Angel of the LORD — The pre-incarnate Christ appearing to Manoah and his
               wife. Identified as God (v.22), has a "wonderful" name
               (v.18), ascends in the altar flame (v.20), and accepts
               worship (v.20). A Christophany — God the Son appearing
               before the Incarnation.

Zorah          — A town in the Shephelah (lowlands) on the border between
               Dan and Judah. Samson's hometown. Located in the foothills
               facing Philistine territory — the front line of conflict.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.5 — ASV: "the child shall be a Nazirite unto God from the womb" / NET: "the child will be dedicated to God from birth" — Hebrew "nazir Elohim" = Nazirite of God. NET replaces the specific term "Nazirite" with the generic "dedicated to God," losing the connection to the Numbers 6 Nazirite vow (no wine, no razor, no corpse contact). The Nazirite identity is central to Samson's story — each violation drives the plot.

- v.18 — ASV: "Wherefore askest thou after my name, seeing it is wonderful?" / NET: "You should not ask me my name because you cannot comprehend it" — Hebrew "peliy" = wonderful/incomprehensible. ASV retains "wonderful" (connecting to Isaiah 9:6 — "Wonderful Counselor"). NET interprets as "you cannot comprehend it" — a valid reading but losing the messianic resonance. The Angel's name is "beyond comprehension" precisely because He is divine.

- v.22 — ASV: "We shall surely die, because we have seen God" / NET: "We will certainly die because we have seen a supernatural being!" — NET avoids saying Manoah recognized "God" directly, using "supernatural being" instead. Hebrew "Elohim" here clearly means God — Manoah's fear echoes the ancient belief that seeing God means death (Exodus 33:20). NET weakens this theological claim.

- v.25 — ASV: "the Spirit of Jehovah began to move him" / WEB: "Yahweh's Spirit began to move him" / NET: "The Lord's Spirit began to control him" — Hebrew "l'fa'amo" = to stir/impel/move. ASV/WEB use "move" (gentle stirring); NET uses "control" (domination). The Hebrew root "pa'am" suggests repeated impulses — the Spirit stirring Samson like wind stirring a bell. NET's "control" implies coercion not in the Hebrew.

- v.6 — ASV: "his countenance was like the countenance of the angel of God, very terrible" / NET: "He looked like God's angel—he was very awesome" — Hebrew "nora m'od" = very fearsome/terrible. ASV's "terrible" (in the old sense of inspiring terror/awe) matches the Hebrew. NET's "awesome" has been diluted in modern English. The divine messenger's appearance inspires holy dread.

- v.24 — ASV: "Jehovah blessed him" / WEB: "Yahweh blessed him" / NET: "the Lord empowered him" — Hebrew "vay'varekhehu Adonai" = the LORD blessed him. NET changes "blessed" to "empowered" — an interpretive shift from general divine favor to specific enablement. The Hebrew "barak" (bless) is broader than empowerment; it includes growth, protection, and favor.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. God works even when His people don't cry out (v.1). Unlike previous
   cycles, Israel does not cry to God — yet God still initiates
   deliverance. His grace is not dependent on our asking. He moves even
   when we have become comfortable in our bondage.

2. God's name is "Wonderful" — beyond comprehension (v.18). We cannot
   fully know God — He transcends our categories. This should produce
   humility in our theology and wonder in our worship. The God we serve
   is always greater than our understanding of Him.

3. A wife's faith can exceed her husband's (vv.22-23). Manoah fears
   death; his wife reasons from God's actions to God's intentions. She
   demonstrates superior theological logic. God speaks to her first and
   she perceives more clearly. Spiritual insight is not determined by
   gender but by faith.

4. "He shall BEGIN to deliver" (v.5). Samson's mission is partial — he
   starts what others will finish. Not every calling is to complete the
   work. Some are called to begin, others to continue, others to finish.
   Faithfulness is measured by obedience to YOUR assignment, not by
   completing the entire task.

5. Great potential does not guarantee great faithfulness. Samson has
   everything: divine announcement, Nazirite consecration, the Spirit's
   stirring, supernatural strength. Yet his story will be one of wasted
   potential and moral failure. Privilege does not ensure obedience.
   The greater the gift, the greater the responsibility — and the greater
   the tragedy when it is squandered.
