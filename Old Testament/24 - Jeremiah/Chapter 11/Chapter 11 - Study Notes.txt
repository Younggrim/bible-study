Jeremiah - Chapter 11
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
God reminds Judah of the original covenant ("Obey my voice") which they have broken. A conspiracy of rebellion exists throughout Judah and Jerusalem. God tells Jeremiah again: don't pray for them. Then Jeremiah discovers his own hometown (Anathoth) is plotting to kill him: "Let us destroy the tree with his fruit."

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Jeremiah son of Hilkiah (see Chapter 1 notes for full
details).

Classification: Prophetic Oracle + Personal Narrative
Key Themes: The broken covenant, "cursed be the man that obeyeth
not," don't pray for them (repeated), the conspiracy against
Jeremiah from his own town, the cost of faithful ministry

Historical Context: This chapter likely dates to Josiah's reforms
(621 BC) — Jeremiah supporting the covenant renewal based on the
rediscovered Book of the Law (2 Kings 22-23). But the response
is external only; the people's hearts remain unchanged. Then
comes personal crisis: Jeremiah's OWN FAMILY AND TOWN (Anathoth)
plots to kill him (v.21). The prophets of Anathoth may have
resented Jeremiah's support for centralizing worship in Jerusalem
(which would eliminate their local priestly income). This is the
first of Jeremiah's "confessions" — personal prayers revealing
the prophet's inner anguish.

Structure:
- The Covenant Remembered: "Obey My Voice" (vv.1-5)
- Proclaim These Words in Judah (vv.6-8)
- The Conspiracy Against the Covenant (vv.9-13)
- Don't Pray for Them (vv.14-17)
- The Plot Against Jeremiah from Anathoth (vv.18-23)

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.3  — "Cursed be the man that obeyeth not the words of this
        covenant" (KJV) — The covenant curse (Deuteronomy 27-28)
        invoked. The covenant isn't optional — violation brings
        the curse that was agreed to at Sinai.

v.4  — "Obey my voice, and do them... so shall ye be my people,
        and I will be your God" (KJV) — The covenant in its
        simplest form: obey → you're my people → I'm your God.
        The relationship conditioned on responsive hearing.

v.11 — "I will bring evil upon them, which they shall not be able
        to escape; and though they shall cry unto me, I will not
        hearken unto them" (KJV) — Coming judgment with no exit.
        Even their prayers will be unheard — the same God who
        normally listens will close His ears.

v.14 — "Therefore pray not thou for this people" (KJV) — Second
        time God forbids Jeremiah from interceding (see 7:16).
        The decision is FINAL for this generation.

v.19 — "But I was like a lamb or an ox that is brought to the
        slaughter; and I knew not that they had devised devices
        against me" (KJV) — Jeremiah as innocent lamb — unaware
        his own people plotted his death. Messianic echo: Isaiah
        53:7 — "as a lamb to the slaughter."

v.21 — "Prophesy not in the name of the LORD, that thou die not
        by our hand" (KJV) — The men of Anathoth's threat: STOP
        preaching or we kill you. The cost of faithfulness:
        death threats from your own community.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.4  — "iron furnace" (KJV/ESV) — Hebrew "kur ha-barzel" =
      the iron-smelting furnace. Egypt described as a refining
      furnace for Israel — intense heat that purified them into
      a nation.

v.16 — "green olive tree, fair, and of goodly fruit" (KJV) —
      Hebrew metaphor: Israel was a beautiful, fruitful olive
      tree planted by God. Now He lights it on fire — the very
      tree He planted, He burns.

v.19 — "I was like a lamb" (KJV/ESV) — Hebrew "kebes alluph" =
      a gentle/trusting lamb. Jeremiah's innocence and
      vulnerability — he didn't know the plot until God revealed it.

v.20 — "reins and the heart" (KJV) / "heart and the mind" (ESV)
      — Hebrew "kelayot va-lev" = kidneys and heart. In Hebrew
      anatomy, kidneys = seat of emotion/desires, heart = seat of
      mind/will. God tests BOTH.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Covenant Curse — The curse pronounced at Sinai/Moab on those who
              break the covenant (Deuteronomy 27-28). Not arbitrary
              punishment but the AGREED consequence of violation.

Anathoth      — Jeremiah's hometown, 3 miles NE of Jerusalem.
              A priestly town (Joshua 21:18). The priests there
              may have resented Jeremiah's support for Jerusalem-
              centered worship that eliminated their local role.

Confessions of Jeremiah — Personal prayers scattered through
              the book (11:18-23; 12:1-6; 15:10-21; 17:14-18;
              18:19-23; 20:7-18) revealing the prophet's inner
              struggle with his calling.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Obey my voice" (v.4). The covenant was always simple at its
   core: LISTEN and RESPOND. All the complexity we add to faith
   doesn't change the fundamental requirement: hear God and obey.

2. Persecution from your own people (v.21). The most painful
   opposition comes from INSIDE your community — family, church,
   hometown. If faithfulness costs you belonging, you're in
   prophetic company.

3. "I was like a lamb" (v.19). Jeremiah didn't know about the
   plot until God revealed it. Sometimes God lets you be naive
   for a season — and then opens your eyes when you're ready.
   His timing in revealing threats is sovereign.

4. "Pray not for this people" (v.14). Sometimes the most faithful
   thing is to STOP interceding for a situation God has already
   judged — and instead adjust your life to the reality of
   judgment. Discern what's worth praying for and what requires
   acceptance.

5. The green olive tree burned (v.16). God will destroy what He
   Himself planted if it becomes corrupt. Even your spiritual
   fruitfulness doesn't make you immune from discipline. Continued
   faithfulness — not past fruitfulness — is what God requires.
