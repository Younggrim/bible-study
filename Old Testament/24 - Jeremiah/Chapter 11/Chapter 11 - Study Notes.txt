Jeremiah - Chapter 11
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
God reminds Judah of the original covenant ("Obey my voice") which they have broken. A conspiracy of rebellion exists throughout Judah and Jerusalem. God tells Jeremiah again: don't pray for them. Then Jeremiah discovers his own hometown (Anathoth) is plotting to kill him: "Let us destroy the tree with his fruit."

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Jeremiah son of Hilkiah (see Chapter 1 notes for full
details).

Classification: Prophetic Oracle + Personal Narrative
Key Themes: The broken covenant, "cursed be the man that obeyeth
not," don't pray for them (repeated), the conspiracy against
Jeremiah from his own town, the cost of faithful ministry

Historical Context: This chapter likely dates to Josiah's reforms
(621 BC) — Jeremiah supporting the covenant renewal based on the
rediscovered Book of the Law (2 Kings 22-23). But the response
is external only; the people's hearts remain unchanged. Then
comes personal crisis: Jeremiah's OWN FAMILY AND TOWN (Anathoth)
plots to kill him (v.21). The prophets of Anathoth may have
resented Jeremiah's support for centralizing worship in Jerusalem
(which would eliminate their local priestly income). This is the
first of Jeremiah's "confessions" — personal prayers revealing
the prophet's inner anguish.

Structure:
- The Covenant Remembered: "Obey My Voice" (vv.1-5)
- Proclaim These Words in Judah (vv.6-8)
- The Conspiracy Against the Covenant (vv.9-13)
- Don't Pray for Them (vv.14-17)
- The Plot Against Jeremiah from Anathoth (vv.18-23)

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Obey my voice" (v.4). The covenant was always simple at its
   core: LISTEN and RESPOND. All the complexity we add to faith
   doesn't change the fundamental requirement: hear God and obey.

2. Persecution from your own people (v.21). The most painful
   opposition comes from INSIDE your community — family, church,
   hometown. If faithfulness costs you belonging, you're in
   prophetic company.

3. "I was like a lamb" (v.19). Jeremiah didn't know about the
   plot until God revealed it. Sometimes God lets you be naive
   for a season — and then opens your eyes when you're ready.
   His timing in revealing threats is sovereign.

4. "Pray not for this people" (v.14). Sometimes the most faithful
   thing is to STOP interceding for a situation God has already
   judged — and instead adjust your life to the reality of
   judgment. Discern what's worth praying for and what requires
   acceptance.

5. The green olive tree burned (v.16). God will destroy what He
   Himself planted if it becomes corrupt. Even your spiritual
   fruitfulness doesn't make you immune from discipline. Continued
   faithfulness — not past fruitfulness — is what God requires.
