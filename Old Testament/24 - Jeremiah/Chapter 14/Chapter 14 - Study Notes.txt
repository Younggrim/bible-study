Jeremiah - Chapter 14
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A devastating drought hits Judah — even animals abandon their young. Jeremiah intercedes, but God says: don't pray for them. False prophets promise rain and peace; God says "I sent them not." Judgment by sword, famine, and pestilence is decreed. Jeremiah pours out his grief and God promises the lying prophets will face the very judgment they denied.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Jeremiah son of Hilkiah (see Chapter 1 notes for full
details).

Classification: Prophetic Oracle — Drought Liturgy + False
Prophet Condemnation
Key Themes: Drought as divine judgment, intercessory prayer
rejected, false prophets exposed, the contrast between Jeremiah's
genuine intercession and false prophets' empty promises, God's
sovereignty over weather

Historical Context: A severe drought devastates Judah — the
cisterns are empty, ground is cracked, deer abandon their fawns,
wild donkeys gasp on bare hills (vv.2-6). The drought is God's
judgment, but the people treat it as mere bad luck. Jeremiah
intercedes powerfully (vv.7-9), but for the THIRD time God says:
don't pray for them (v.11). False prophets say "no drought will
come" (v.15) — yet it's already here. God's sentence: sword,
famine, pestilence. The chapter alternates between Jeremiah's
lament-prayers and God's refusals — creating a liturgy of
rejected intercession.

Structure:
- The Drought Described (vv.1-6)
- Jeremiah's First Intercession (vv.7-9)
- God's First Refusal (vv.10-12)
- The False Prophets Condemned (vv.13-16)
- Jeremiah's Lament (vv.17-18)
- Jeremiah's Second Intercession (vv.19-22)

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.7  — "O LORD, though our iniquities testify against us, do thou
        it for thy name's sake" (KJV) — Jeremiah's prayer
        strategy: he can't appeal to Israel's goodness (there is
        none) so he appeals to GOD'S NAME. "We don't deserve help
        — but Your reputation is at stake."

v.9  — "thou art in the midst of us, and we are called by thy
        name; leave us not" (KJV) — The intercessor's argument:
        God's presence and God's name are connected to this people.
        If they're destroyed, God's reputation suffers.

v.11 — "Then said the LORD unto me, Pray not for this people for
        their good" (KJV) — Third prohibition against intercession
        (see 7:16; 11:14). Even the greatest intercessor's prayers
        are refused when judgment is final.

v.14 — "The prophets prophesy lies in my name: I sent them not,
        neither have I commanded them" (KJV) — God's explicit
        denial: these prophets are UNAUTHORIZED. Their credentials
        are self-issued. Their message is self-invented.

v.22 — "Art not thou he, O LORD our God? therefore we will wait
        upon thee: for thou hast made all these things" (KJV) —
        Despite God's refusal, Jeremiah KEEPS praying. The
        intercessor's persistence — even when told to stop. His
        theology: you're the Creator; weather is yours to command.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "gates" (KJV/ESV) — Hebrew "she'areyha" = the city gates.
      Metonymy: the gates represent the cities. "The gates languish"
      = the cities are in mourning/distress.

v.4  — "chapt" (KJV) / "cracked" (ESV) — Hebrew "chatah" =
      dismayed/cracked. The ground itself is distressed — even
      soil has no moisture.

v.6  — "snuffed up the wind like dragons" (KJV) / "panting for
      air like jackals" (ESV) — Hebrew "tannim" = jackals (not
      dragons). Wild donkeys gasping on hilltops like panting
      jackals — desperate for any air or moisture.

v.14 — "divination" (KJV/ESV) — Hebrew "qesem" = the practice of
      seeking omens or supernatural knowledge apart from God.
      False prophets' messages come from divination, not revelation.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Drought as Judgment — In a land dependent on seasonal rains,
              drought is God withholding the most basic provision.
              Not natural disaster but divine discipline — God
              controls the weather.

Pray Not     — God's command to stop interceding for a specific
              generation that has passed the point of no return.
              Not a permanent rule but a specific sentence on a
              specific group.

False Prophets — Those claiming divine authorization without
              possessing it. Their message: what people want to
              hear. Their source: divination or imagination, not
              God's Spirit. Their credentials: self-issued.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Do it for thy name's sake" (v.7). When you can't appeal to
   your own goodness (and you usually can't), appeal to God's
   REPUTATION. His name is at stake in your life. Pray on the
   basis of who HE is, not who you are.

2. False prophets say what people want to hear (v.14). In any
   era, there are voices claiming God's authority while
   contradicting God's word. Test: does the message match
   Scripture, or just your preferences?

3. God controls weather (v.22). Modern ears resist this — but
   biblically, weather is in God's hands. Drought, flood, storm
   — God is sovereign over nature. Whatever you're experiencing
   externally may have spiritual dimensions.

4. Even refused prayer matters. Jeremiah was told THREE TIMES to
   stop praying — and he kept praying anyway (v.19). Sometimes
   the intercessor can't stop, even when heaven says "no."
   Persistent prayer honors the intercessor's calling even when
   the answer is "no."

5. "I sent them not" (v.14). Not everyone who says "God told me"
   was actually told by God. False spiritual authority is real and
   dangerous. Question credentials. Check messages against
   Scripture. Guard yourself.
