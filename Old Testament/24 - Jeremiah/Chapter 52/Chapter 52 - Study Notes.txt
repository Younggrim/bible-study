Jeremiah - Chapter 52
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
The historical appendix — a detailed account of Jerusalem's fall (paralleling 2 Kings 24:18-25:30). Zedekiah's rebellion, the siege, the breach, Zedekiah's capture and blinding, the temple burned, walls demolished, population deported. The chapter ends with a note of hope: Evil-Merodach releases Jehoiachin from prison in Babylon and gives him a seat at the king's table.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Likely a later editor/compiler (not Jeremiah himself), as
44:30 appears to be his last prophecy. This chapter closely
parallels 2 Kings 24:18-25:30.

Classification: Historical Appendix — Jerusalem's Fall and
Jehoiachin's Release
Key Themes: Prophecy fulfilled (everything Jeremiah predicted came
true), the temple destroyed, the Davidic monarchy ended (seemingly),
yet Jehoiachin's release — a spark of hope for David's line

Historical Context: This chapter was likely added by an editor to
provide the FULFILLMENT record: everything Jeremiah prophesied
actually happened. The detailed inventory of temple furnishings
carried to Babylon (vv.17-23) emphasizes the magnitude of loss.
The deportation numbers (vv.28-30: 4,600 total over three
deportations) may count only adult males. The chapter's FINAL
note (vv.31-34) is the release of King Jehoiachin from prison
by Evil-Merodach (Amel-Marduk) of Babylon in 562 BC — 37 years
after his exile. This tiny note of royal favor preserves hope:
David's line SURVIVES. The book ends not with destruction but
with a king at a royal table — a whisper of messianic hope.

Structure:
- Zedekiah's Reign and Rebellion (vv.1-3)
- The Siege: 18 Months (vv.4-5)
- The Breach, Flight, and Capture (vv.6-11)
- The Temple Burned, Walls Demolished (vv.12-14)
- The Deportation (vv.15-16)
- Temple Furnishings Catalogued and Taken (vv.17-23)
- Leaders Executed at Riblah (vv.24-27)
- Deportation Numbers (vv.28-30)
- Jehoiachin Released: A King at the Table (vv.31-34)

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.12-13 — "Now in the fifth month, in the tenth day of the month...
        came Nebuzar-adan... and burned the house of the LORD, and
        the king's house; and all the houses of Jerusalem" (KJV) —
        The temple of Solomon — 400 years standing — burned to the
        ground. The king's palace. Every great house. Total
        destruction.

v.11 — "Then he put out the eyes of Zedekiah; and the king of
        Babylon bound him in chains, and carried him to Babylon"
        (KJV) — Fulfilling both Jeremiah's prophecy (34:3: "thine
        eyes shall behold the eyes of the king of Babylon") AND
        Ezekiel's (12:13: "he shall not see it" — Babylon). Both
        true: he SAW the king of Babylon, then was blinded and
        DIDN'T see Babylon.

v.31 — "Evil-merodach king of Babylon in the first year of his
        reign lifted up the head of Jehoiachin king of Judah, and
        brought him forth out of prison" (KJV) — After 37 YEARS in
        prison, the Davidic king is released. Not restored to
        power but given dignity. A king at a royal table — David's
        line survives.

v.33 — "And changed his prison garments: and he did eat bread
        before him continually all the days of his life" (KJV) —
        Prison clothes exchanged for royal garments. Daily bread at
        the king's table. Small mercies — but a SIGN: God's
        covenant with David isn't dead. The line continues.

v.34 — "his allowance was a continual allowance given him of the
        king of Babylon, every day a portion until the day of his
        death" (KJV) — DAILY provision until death. Sustained by
        the conqueror's table. A Davidic king preserved in exile.
        From him, the line continues to Christ.

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.12 — "fifth month, tenth day" (KJV) / cf. 2 Kings 25:8 "seventh day" — Minor date variation between accounts. Both refer to the same event: Nebuzaradan's arrival and the burning of Jerusalem in August 586 BC.

v.28-30 — "4,600" total deportees — Much smaller than "10,000" in 2 Kings 24:14. Likely different counting methods: Jeremiah counts adult male citizens; Kings counts broader categories.

v.31 — "Evil-merodach" (KJV/ESV) — Babylonian "Amel-Marduk" = "man of Marduk." Son of Nebuchadnezzar, reigned 562-560 BC. Released Jehoiachin in his accession year — possibly a political gesture of new-reign amnesty.

v.34 — "until the day of his death" (KJV/ESV) — Jehoiachin was sustained at the Babylonian court until he died. Archaeological evidence: the "Jehoiachin tablets" from Babylon list rations allocated to "Yaukin, king of Judah" — confirming this biblical detail.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Historical Appendix — Chapter 52 serves as the PROOF that Jeremiah's prophecies were fulfilled — added to validate the book's claims against Jerusalem, Zedekiah, and the temple.

Jehoiachin's Release — The final note of hope: David's line preserved in Babylonian exile. A king released from prison, given dignity and daily bread. The messianic line SURVIVES.

Temple Furnishings — The detailed inventory of bronze pillars, lavers, and implements (vv.17-23) emphasizes what was LOST — and what Ezra later partially recovered (Ezra 1:7-11).

Jehoiachin Tablets — Archaeological discovery: Babylonian administrative documents listing ration allocations to "Yaukin king of the land of Judah" — extrabiblical confirmation of 52:34.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Everything Jeremiah predicted came true (entire chapter). 40 years of rejected prophecy, every word fulfilled precisely. God's word is RELIABLE — even when it takes decades.

2. The temple burned (v.13). Not even Solomon's magnificent temple was permanent. Buildings, institutions, and traditions can ALL be destroyed if God withdraws His presence. Put your faith in GOD, not His house.

3. Jehoiachin released after 37 years (v.31). 37 YEARS in prison — then release. Never assume God's timing matches yours. Hope can arrive after DECADES of darkness.

4. The book ends with bread at a table (v.34). After 52 chapters of judgment, the final image is: daily bread, sustained, alive. Even the darkest book in the Bible ends with a note of provision. God's last word is never destruction — it's preservation.

5. David's line survives. From this released prisoner descends JESUS (Matthew 1:12). The seemingly dead dynasty produces the eternal King. God's promises are not defeated by prisons, exiles, or empires.
