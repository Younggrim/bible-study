Jeremiah - Chapter 2
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
God's first message through Jeremiah: Israel's spiritual adultery exposed. God remembers their honeymoon love in the wilderness, then indicts them: "My people have committed two evils — they have forsaken ME the fountain of living waters, and hewn them out cisterns, broken cisterns, that can hold no water."

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Jeremiah son of Hilkiah (see Chapter 1 notes for full
details).

Classification: Prophetic Indictment — The Great Accusation
Key Themes: Israel's early love forsaken, God as a fountain
abandoned, broken cisterns that fail, spiritual adultery with
idols, "Where is the LORD?" no longer asked, even the priests
don't seek God

Historical Context: This chapter begins Jeremiah's public
preaching (likely during Josiah's reign, c. 627-621 BC). The
structure is a courtroom speech — God brings charges against
Israel. He begins tenderly ("I remember the devotion of your
youth") then builds a devastating case. Israel abandoned the
living water (God) for cracked cisterns (idols) — a metaphor
powerfully relevant in water-scarce Palestine. No nation has ever
changed its gods (v.11) — yet Israel exchanged Glory for
worthlessness. The adultery language intensifies: "a wild donkey
in heat" (v.24). Even the stains of guilt can't be washed away
with soap (v.22).

Structure:
- The Honeymoon: Early Devotion Remembered (vv.1-3)
- The Indictment: What Wrong Did I Do? (vv.4-8)
- The Unprecedented Exchange (vv.9-13)
- The Consequences of Forsaking God (vv.14-19)
- The Wild Vine, the Wild Donkey (vv.20-25)
- Shame Coming: Their Gods Cannot Save (vv.26-37)

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "I remember thee, the kindness of thy youth, the love of
        thine espousals, when thou wentest after me in the
        wilderness" (KJV) — God's memory: the early love, the
        wilderness honeymoon (Sinai journey). Israel was once
        devoted. God remembers what THEY have forgotten.

v.5  — "What iniquity have your fathers found in me, that they
        are gone far from me?" (KJV) — God's hurt question: what
        did I DO wrong? What deficiency in Me drove you away?
        The answer: NOTHING. They left without cause.

v.11 — "Hath a nation changed their gods, which are yet no gods?
        but my people have changed their glory for that which doth
        not profit" (KJV) — Even PAGAN nations don't switch gods.
        Only Israel trades Glory for emptiness. The exchange is
        unprecedented and irrational.

v.13 — "For my people have committed two evils; they have forsaken
        me the fountain of living waters, and hewed them out
        cisterns, broken cisterns, that can hold no water" (KJV)
        — The double sin: (1) leaving the fountain (the SOURCE),
        (2) building broken replacements (that DON'T WORK). Not
        one sin but two.

v.19 — "Thine own wickedness shall correct thee, and thy
        backslidings shall reprove thee: know therefore and see
        that it is an evil thing and bitter, that thou hast
        forsaken the LORD thy God" (KJV) — Sin is self-punishing.
        You don't need God to actively judge — your own wickedness
        will correct you. Consequence is built into the rebellion.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "kindness" (KJV) / "devotion" (ESV) — Hebrew "chesed" =
      covenant love, loyal-love, devotion. Israel's early chesed
      toward God (not just God's toward them). The reciprocal
      love of the honeymoon period.

v.13 — "fountain of living waters" (KJV/ESV) — Hebrew "meqor
      mayim chayyim" = source/spring of living (flowing) water.
      In contrast to stagnant cistern water, living water is
      fresh, flowing, constantly renewed. Jesus claims this
      title (John 4:10; 7:38).

v.13 — "cisterns" (KJV/ESV) — Hebrew "borot" = pits dug to
      collect rainwater. In Palestine's dry climate, cisterns were
      essential — but they cracked, leaked, and grew algae. A poor
      substitute for a natural spring.

v.23 — "dromedary" (KJV) / "restless young camel" (ESV) —
      Hebrew "bikrah" = a young she-camel. Running aimlessly in
      heat — frantic, unfocused, driven by appetite rather than
      direction.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Fountain of Living Waters — God as the SOURCE of fresh, flowing,
              life-giving water. Not stagnant, not stored — fresh
              from the spring. The opposite of human-made cisterns.

Broken Cisterns — Human substitutes for God: carved out with
              effort, plastered to hold water, but inevitably
              cracking and leaking. Idols, systems, and ambitions
              that CANNOT satisfy.

Spiritual Adultery — Israel's covenant with God described as a
              marriage; idol worship as adultery. The personal
              betrayal intensifies the legal violation.

Backsliding  — (Hebrew: meshuvah) Turning back, apostasy, sliding
              away from God. Not a sudden fall but a gradual
              departure — and self-punishing (v.19).

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "What iniquity have your fathers found in me?" (v.5). God asks
   YOU: what did I do wrong? What drove you away? Most
   backsliding isn't caused by God's failure but by our
   forgetfulness. If you've drifted, the problem isn't God.

2. Two evils, not one (v.13). Leaving God is sin #1. Building
   replacements is sin #2. We don't just abandon God — we replace
   Him. What broken cisterns have you built? Career? Relationships?
   Substances? Achievements? They all crack.

3. "A fountain of living waters" (v.13). God is not a stagnant
   resource you visit occasionally. He's a FOUNTAIN — constantly
   flowing, always fresh, inexhaustible. Are you drinking from
   the fountain or hauling water from a cracked cistern?

4. Sin corrects itself (v.19). You don't always need God's active
   judgment — the CONSEQUENCES of sin are built in. The
   bitterness, the emptiness, the shame — those ARE the
   correction. Learn from them.

5. No nation changes its gods — except you (v.11). The irony:
   pagans are more faithful to FAKE gods than Israel is to the
   REAL one. Loyalty to worthless things comes naturally; loyalty
   to God requires intentionality. Fight for it.
