Jeremiah - Chapter 26
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
The trial of Jeremiah: after preaching the temple sermon (ch.7), priests and prophets seize him shouting "Thou shalt surely die!" Jeremiah is put on trial for blasphemy against the temple. He defends himself: "The LORD sent me." The princes side with Jeremiah; the precedent of Micah (who prophesied doom and wasn't killed) saves him. But Urijah, another prophet, is killed for the same message.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Jeremiah son of Hilkiah (see Chapter 1 notes for full details).

Classification: Historical Narrative — The Prophet's Trial
Key Themes: The cost of truth-telling, religious authorities
opposing God's prophet, the trial process, the precedent of Micah,
God's protection through unlikely allies, the murder of Urijah

Historical Context: This chapter narrates the REACTION to the temple sermon of ch.7. Dated to "the beginning of the reign of Jehoiakim" (609 BC). The religious establishment charges Jeremiah with death-worthy blasphemy: "This man is worthy to die; for he hath prophesied against this city" (v.11). The trial moves from priests/prophets (who want him dead) to the princes/elders (who use the Micah precedent to spare him). A secondary narrative (vv.20-23) records that Urijah son of Shemaiah preached the same message but was hunted down in Egypt and executed. Jeremiah survived; Urijah didn't. God's protection is sovereign and selective.

Structure:
- The Sermon and the Seizure (vv.1-9)
- The Trial Before the Princes (vv.10-11)
- Jeremiah's Defense (vv.12-15)
- The Verdict: Not Guilty (vv.16-19)
- Urijah: The Prophet Who Wasn't Spared (vv.20-24)

Charles Spurgeon:
  "'The LORD sent me.' Jeremiah's defense was not eloquence or self-justification but simple commission: God sent me. When the world puts you on trial for speaking truth, your defense is the same: 'I didn't choose this message — it was given to me.'"

REFLECTION
------------------------------------
1. "The LORD sent me" (v.12). Your defense against opposition isn't eloquence — it's commission. If God sent you, that's all the authority you need. The question isn't "can I say this?" but "did God send me to say it?"

2. Religious people opposing God's word (v.11). The priests and prophets — the religious ESTABLISHMENT — wanted Jeremiah dead. Throughout history, organized religion has often opposed God's fresh word. Don't assume religious authority equals spiritual correctness.

3. Jeremiah kept preaching AT HIS OWN TRIAL (v.13). Even facing death, he didn't soften the message. He used his defense speech as an evangelistic opportunity. What would YOU preach if your life depended on it?

4. Urijah died; Jeremiah lived (vv.20-24). Same message, different outcomes. Faithfulness doesn't guarantee safety. God sovereignly protects some and allows others to be martyred. Both are faithful.

5. Unlikely allies (v.24). Ahikam — a political figure — protected Jeremiah. God uses unlikely people in unlikely positions to protect His servants. Don't dismiss potential allies because they're "not spiritual enough."
