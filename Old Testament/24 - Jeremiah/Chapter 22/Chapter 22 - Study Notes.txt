Jeremiah - Chapter 22
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Oracle to the royal house of David: execute justice or lose the throne. God pronounces judgment on three kings — Shallum (Jehoahaz), Jehoiakim ("Woe unto him that buildeth his house by unrighteousness"), and Coniah (Jehoiachin). Jehoiakim will have the burial of a donkey; Coniah is a broken pot whose seed will never sit on David's throne.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Jeremiah son of Hilkiah (see Chapter 1 notes for full
details).

Classification: Prophetic Oracle — Against the Royal House
Key Themes: Justice as the condition for monarchy's survival,
three kings judged, Jehoiakim's exploitation of workers,
Coniah/Jehoiachin's rejection and exile, the broken Davidic line

Historical Context: This chapter addresses Judah's last kings
in sequence. Shallum/Jehoahaz (609 BC, reigned 3 months, exiled
to Egypt by Pharaoh Necho). Jehoiakim (609-598 BC, built a
lavish palace with forced labor, burned Jeremiah's scroll). Coniah/
Jehoiachin (598-597 BC, reigned 3 months, surrendered to
Nebuchadnezzar and was exiled to Babylon). The judgments escalate:
Jehoahaz won't return from Egypt, Jehoiakim gets a donkey's burial,
and Coniah's line is cut from the throne. Yet Matthew 1 traces
Jesus through Jehoiachin — the curse is lifted through the virgin
birth (Jesus is legal heir through Joseph but biologically through
Mary, bypassing the curse).

Structure:
- Oracle to the Royal House: Do Justice (vv.1-9)
- Shallum/Jehoahaz: Won't Return from Egypt (vv.10-12)
- Jehoiakim: Woe to the Unjust Builder (vv.13-19)
- Coniah/Jehoiachin: Cast Out Like a Broken Pot (vv.20-30)

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.3  — "Execute ye judgment and righteousness, and deliver the
        spoiled out of the hand of the oppressor" (KJV) — The
        king's ONLY job that matters: JUSTICE. Everything else is
        secondary to protecting the vulnerable from the powerful.

v.13 — "Woe unto him that buildeth his house by unrighteousness,
        and his chambers by wrong; that useth his neighbour's
        service without wages, and giveth him not for his work"
        (KJV) — Jehoiakim's crime: forced labor without pay. The
        king building a palace on the backs of unpaid workers.
        Wage theft under divine curse.

v.15 — "Shalt thou reign, because thou closest thyself in cedar?
        did not thy father eat and drink, and do judgment and
        justice, and then it was well with him?" (KJV) — Contrast
        with Josiah: he had simple pleasures AND justice. Jehoiakim
        has cedar palaces AND injustice. Which king does God
        commend?

v.16 — "He judged the cause of the poor and needy; then it was
        well with him: was not this to know me? saith the LORD"
        (KJV) — Stunning definition: KNOWING GOD = judging the
        cause of the poor. Justice for the needy IS knowledge of
        God. Not theology but JUSTICE.

v.30 — "Write ye this man childless... for no man of his seed
        shall prosper, sitting upon the throne of David" (KJV) —
        Coniah's curse: no descendant will sit on David's throne.
        Yet Jesus is in Coniah's line (Matt 1:12) — resolved by
        the virgin birth (legal heir through Joseph, biological
        lineage through Mary).

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.10 — "Shallum" (KJV/ESV) — Jehoahaz's personal name (2 Kings
      23:30-34). He reigned only 3 months before Pharaoh Necho
      deposed him and installed Jehoiakim.

v.15 — "closest thyself in cedar" (KJV) / "compete in cedar"
      (ESV) — Hebrew "tachtareh ba-erez" = do you think paneling
      in cedar makes you a king? Cedar was the luxury building
      material. Jehoiakim competed in architecture, not justice.

v.19 — "burial of an ass" (KJV) / "burial of a donkey" (ESV) —
      Hebrew "qevurat chamor" = dragged and dumped outside the
      city like a dead animal. No funeral, no honor, no tomb.
      The ultimate disgrace for a king.

v.24 — "Coniah" (KJV) / "Coniah" (ESV) — Shortened form of
      "Jeconiah/Jehoiachin." God strips even the divine element
      ("Yeho-") from his name — calling him just "Coniah."

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Knowing God = Justice — (v.16) Jeremiah's radical definition:
              genuine knowledge of God is demonstrated through
              justice for the poor and needy. Theology without
              ethics is not true knowledge.

Donkey's Burial — (v.19) The most disgraceful end for a king:
              no funeral, dragged and dumped like an animal carcass.
              Jehoiakim's punishment fits his contempt for others.

Coniah's Curse — (v.30) No descendant sits on David's throne.
              Creates a theological puzzle resolved by Christ's
              virgin birth: legal Davidic heir through Joseph
              without biological descent from Coniah through Mary.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Was not THIS to know me?" (v.16). Stop separating theology
   from justice. Knowing God means DOING justice for the vulnerable.
   If your faith doesn't produce fairness for the poor, you don't
   know God as well as you think.

2. Woe to those who don't pay workers (v.13). Wage theft is
   CURSED BY GOD. If you employ people, PAY THEM fairly and
   promptly. God notices exploitation — even by kings.

3. Cedar panels don't make you king (v.15). Luxury doesn't equal
   legitimacy. The trappings of success don't indicate divine
   blessing. Josiah was a good king with modest taste; Jehoiakim
   was a bad king with expensive taste. Character > aesthetics.

4. Coniah's curse and Christ (v.30). God cursed the royal line —
   yet through it came Messiah. God's curses have expiration dates
   when redemption intervenes. The virgin birth resolves what
   seemed impossible. Don't assume God's "no" is permanent.
