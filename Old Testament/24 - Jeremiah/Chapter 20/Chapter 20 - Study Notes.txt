Jeremiah - Chapter 20
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Pashhur the priest beats Jeremiah and puts him in stocks overnight. Upon release, Jeremiah renames him "Magor-missabib" (Terror on Every Side). Then comes Jeremiah's darkest confession: "Cursed be the day wherein I was born... Wherefore came I forth out of the womb to see labour and sorrow?" The prophet's most extreme emotional crisis.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Jeremiah son of Hilkiah (see Chapter 1 notes for full
details).

Classification: Historical Narrative + Prophetic Confession (the
darkest of Jeremiah's confessions)
Key Themes: Physical persecution by religious authorities, God's
word as fire in the bones, the prophet's suicidal despair, cursing
the day of birth, the tension between calling and despair

Historical Context: After the flask-smashing sermon (ch.19),
Pashhur (chief officer of the temple, not to be confused with
other Pashhurs in Jeremiah) has Jeremiah beaten and placed in
stocks at the Upper Benjamin Gate overnight. Upon release,
Jeremiah pronounces judgment on Pashhur: his new name is "Terror
on Every Side" — he and his friends will fall by Babylon's sword.
Then comes Jeremiah's deepest personal crisis (vv.7-18) — the
SIXTH and final confession. He feels deceived by God, mocked by
everyone, yet unable to stop preaching because God's word is
FIRE in his bones. The chapter ends with a birth-curse more
extreme than Job 3.

Structure:
- Pashhur Beats and Stocks Jeremiah (vv.1-2)
- Jeremiah's Prophecy Against Pashhur (vv.3-6)
- The Confession: "Thou Hast Deceived Me" (vv.7-10)
- Faith Resurfaces: "The LORD Is with Me" (vv.11-13)
- The Birth-Curse: The Darkest Moment (vv.14-18)

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.7  — "O LORD, thou hast deceived me, and I was deceived: thou
        art stronger than I, and hast prevailed" (KJV) — The most
        shocking accusation a prophet makes against God: "You
        DECEIVED me." God overpowered him into a calling that
        brought only suffering. Raw, almost blasphemous honesty.

v.9  — "Then I said, I will not make mention of him, nor speak
        any more in his name. But his word was in mine heart as a
        burning fire shut up in my bones, and I was weary with
        forbearing, and I could not stay" (KJV) — Jeremiah tries
        to QUIT — and can't. God's word is FIRE in his bones. He
        can't NOT preach. The calling won't release him.

v.11 — "But the LORD is with me as a mighty terrible one:
        therefore my persecutors shall stumble" (KJV) — Faith
        returns. After the accusation (v.7) and the burning (v.9),
        Jeremiah remembers: God is a "dread warrior" on his side.

v.14 — "Cursed be the day wherein I was born: let not the day
        wherein my mother bare me be blessed" (KJV) — The birth-
        curse. Not suicidal (he doesn't want to die NOW) but wishes
        he'd never existed. Deeper than death-wish — existence-
        wish reversal.

v.18 — "Wherefore came I forth out of the womb to see labour and
        sorrow, that my days should be consumed with shame?" (KJV)
        — The question with no answer: WHY was I born for this?
        The chapter ends here — no resolution, no comfort, no
        divine response. Raw despair, unresolved.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.3  — "Magor-missabib" (KJV/ESV) — Hebrew "Magor mi-Saviv" =
      "Terror on Every Side." Pashhur's prophetic renaming: his
      life will be surrounded by terror.

v.7  — "deceived" (KJV) / "deceived" (ESV) — Hebrew "pittitani"
      (from "patah") = to entice, persuade, seduce. Can mean
      "deceived" or "persuaded/seduced." Jeremiah felt LURED into
      a calling whose cost wasn't disclosed upfront.

v.9  — "burning fire shut up in my bones" (KJV/ESV) — Hebrew
      "esh bo'eret atsur be-atsmotai" = fire burning, confined in
      my bones. The word of God experienced as PHYSICAL sensation
      — heat, pressure, combustion that must be released.

v.14 — "cursed" (KJV/ESV) — Hebrew "arur" = the formal curse
      formula. Not casual frustration but LITURGICAL cursing of
      the day — the same word used in Deuteronomy's curses.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Magor-Missabib — "Terror on Every Side" — the prophetic name
              given to Pashhur (meaning his future: surrounded by
              terror). Jeremiah renames the man who persecuted him.

Fire in the Bones — God's word experienced as uncontainable
              internal combustion. The prophet CANNOT hold it in —
              the pressure of unspoken revelation is physically
              painful. The compulsion to preach.

Birth-Curse  — Wishing one had never been born. Not suicidal
              (wanting to die) but existential (wanting never to
              have existed). The deepest despair possible.

Stocks       — (Hebrew: mahpekhet) A wooden frame that locked the
              body in a bent/distorted position. Public humiliation
              plus physical pain.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "His word was in mine heart as a burning fire" (v.9). If you
   have a calling from God, you KNOW the fire. You try to quit
   and can't. The burning won't let you rest. That's not
   obligation — that's ANOINTING. The fire is the proof.

2. "Thou hast deceived me" (v.7). Honest accusation of God is in
   Scripture. Jeremiah felt misled about the cost. God didn't
   strike him down for saying it. If you feel deceived by God —
   SAY IT. He can handle the accusation. Silence is worse.

3. The birth-curse (vv.14-18). Jeremiah was in extreme emotional
   anguish — and God included it in Scripture WITHOUT resolving it.
   No neat answer arrives. Some chapters of life end in
   unresolved pain. It doesn't mean God abandoned you — it means
   some pain has no immediate explanation.

4. Faith surfaces between despair (v.11). Even in his darkest
   chapter, Jeremiah has a flash of faith: "the LORD is with me."
   It doesn't last (vv.14-18 follow) — but it's there. Faith in
   darkness isn't constant sunshine; it's occasional lightning.

5. Persecution by religious people (vv.1-2). Pashhur was the
   TEMPLE OFFICER — the religious establishment. Jeremiah's worst
   enemies weren't pagans but RELIGIOUS LEADERS. If you face
   opposition from "the church," you're in ancient prophetic
   company.
