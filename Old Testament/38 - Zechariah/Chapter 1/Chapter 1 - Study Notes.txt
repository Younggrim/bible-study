Zechariah - Chapter 1
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
God calls Judah to return to Him (unlike their fathers who ignored the prophets), and Zechariah sees a night vision of horsemen patrolling the earth — the nations are at ease while Jerusalem remains unrestored, prompting God's jealous compassion.


AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Zechariah son of Berechiah. Prophesied 520-480 BC.
Contemporary with Haggai. Key theme: Messianic hope and the
coming kingdom. The most Messianic of the Minor Prophets.
Famous passages: the Branch (3:8, 6:12), "Not by might... but
by my spirit" (4:6), the triumphal entry (9:9), "they shall
look upon me whom they have pierced" (12:10), the shepherd
struck (13:7), and the LORD's return to the Mount of Olives
(14:4).

Classification: Prophetic Vision — The Call to Return and the
Horsemen Among the Myrtles
Key Themes: A call to repentance, God's jealousy for Jerusalem,
angelic patrols reporting on the earth, God's comfort and
restoration, the four horns and four craftsmen

Historical Context: Zechariah begins his ministry in the eighth
month of the second year of Darius (October/November 520 BC) —
two months after Haggai's first oracle. Like Haggai, Zechariah
encourages the returned exiles to rebuild the Temple, but his
approach is vastly different: where Haggai is direct and
practical, Zechariah is visionary and apocalyptic.

The opening call (vv.1-6) establishes the book's foundation:
"Return to me, and I will return to you." God reminds this
generation not to repeat their fathers' mistakes. The fathers
ignored the prophets and suffered exile. But God's word outlasted
them all — it "overtook" them (v.6). The lesson: human life is
short; God's word endures forever.

The first night vision (vv.7-17) reveals horsemen patrolling the
earth and reporting peace. But the peace of the nations contrasts
with Jerusalem's suffering. The Angel of the LORD intercedes:
"How long?" God responds with "good and comforting words" —
He is jealous for Zion and angry with the complacent nations.
The second vision (vv.18-21) shows four horns (empires that
scattered Israel) matched by four craftsmen who will cast them
down.

Structure:
- The Call to Repentance: "Return to Me" (vv.1-6)
- The First Night Vision: Horsemen Among the Myrtles (vv.7-11)
- The Angel's Intercession and God's Answer (vv.12-17)
- The Second Vision: Four Horns and Four Craftsmen (vv.18-21)

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.3   — James 4:8 — "Draw nigh to God, and he will draw nigh to
         you." The NT echo of Zechariah's call to return.
v.3   — Malachi 3:7 — "Return unto me, and I will return unto
         you" — the same invitation repeated by the last prophet.
v.5   — Isaiah 40:6-8 — "The grass withereth... but the word of
         our God shall stand for ever."
v.8   — Revelation 6:1-8 — The four horsemen of Revelation echo
         Zechariah's colored horses patrolling the earth.
v.12  — Daniel 9:2 — Daniel also understood the seventy years
         from Jeremiah and prayed for restoration.
v.14  — Exodus 20:5 — "I the LORD thy God am a jealous God" —
         God's jealousy is a covenant attribute.
v.16  — Haggai 1:8 — "Build the house" — the same command
         through Zechariah's contemporary.
v.21  — Daniel 2:44 — God raises up kingdoms to cast down the
         empires that oppose His people.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------

REFLECTION
------------------------------------
1. "Return to me, and I will return to you" (v.3). God does not
   say "Clean up first, then come." He says: take one step toward
   Me, and I will come running toward you. The initiative is His;
   the response is yours. Have you drifted? The way back is
   simpler than you think.

2. "Your fathers, where are they?" (v.5). One generation's
   disobedience does not have to define the next. God gives this
   generation a fresh start. You are not bound by your parents'
   failures — you can choose differently. But you must choose.

3. God is "jealous for Zion" (v.14). His jealousy means He will
   not give up on you. He will not share you with lesser loves.
   He pursues with fierce, protective affection. That jealousy
   is not a flaw — it is the intensity of His commitment.

4. For every "horn" there is a "craftsman" (vv.18-21). Whatever
   power has scattered, broken, or opposed you — God has already
   prepared the agent of its overthrow. The horns seem powerful,
   but the craftsmen are coming. God always has the last move.
