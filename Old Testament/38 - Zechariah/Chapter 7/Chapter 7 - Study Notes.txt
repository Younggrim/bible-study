Zechariah - Chapter 7
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
A delegation asks about continuing fast days, and God responds that their fasting was self-serving — what He truly requires is justice, mercy, and compassion, which their fathers refused and reaped exile.


AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Zechariah son of Berechiah. Prophesied 520-480 BC.
Contemporary with Haggai. Key theme: Messianic hope and the
coming kingdom. The most Messianic of the Minor Prophets.
Famous passages: the Branch (3:8, 6:12), "Not by might... but
by my spirit" (4:6), the triumphal entry (9:9), "they shall
look upon me whom they have pierced" (12:10), the shepherd
struck (13:7), and the LORD's return to the Mount of Olives
(14:4).

Classification: Prophetic Oracle — The Question of Fasting and
the Demand for Justice
Key Themes: Empty ritual versus genuine obedience, self-centered
religion exposed, God's true requirements (justice, mercy,
compassion), the hard heart that refuses to hear, the
consequences of persistent disobedience

Historical Context: Two years after the night visions (now the
fourth year of Darius, 518 BC), a delegation from Bethel arrives
with a practical question: should they continue the fasts
commemorating Jerusalem's fall? They had fasted in the fifth
month (the Temple's destruction) and the seventh month
(Gedaliah's assassination) for seventy years. Now that the
Temple is being rebuilt, are these fasts still required?

God's answer is devastating: "When you fasted... did you really
fast for ME?" (v.5). Their fasting was self-referential — about
THEIR grief, THEIR national identity, THEIR religious routine.
It was never truly about God. Similarly, "when you eat and
drink, do you not eat for YOURSELVES?" (v.6). Whether fasting
or feasting, they were self-centered.

God then reminds them what He actually requires: true justice,
mercy, compassion — not ritual. Their fathers refused this and
made their hearts like "adamant stone" (diamond-hard). The
consequence: God scattered them and desolated their land. The
lesson for this generation is clear: don't repeat the pattern.
Ritual without righteousness is worthless.

Structure:
- The Delegation's Question: Should We Still Fast? (vv.1-3)
- God's Probing Response: Was It Really for Me? (vv.4-6)
- The True Requirement: Justice and Mercy (vv.7-10)
- The Fathers' Stubborn Refusal (vv.11-12)
- The Terrible Consequence: As I Called, They Refused (vv.13-14)

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.5  — "When ye fasted and mourned in the fifth and seventh
        month, even those seventy years, did ye at all fast unto
        me, even to me?" (KJV) — God's piercing question exposes
        the heart. Religious activity can be entirely self-
        referential — done for ourselves, our reputation, our
        comfort — while bypassing God entirely. The repetition
        "unto ME, even to ME" emphasizes that God was never the
        true audience of their worship.

v.9  — "Execute true judgment, and shew mercy and compassions
        every man to his brother" (KJV) / "Render true judgments,
        show kindness and mercy to one another" (ESV) — What God
        ACTUALLY wants. Not fasting rituals but relational
        righteousness. True religion is measured by how you treat
        people — especially the vulnerable.

v.10 — "oppress not the widow, nor the fatherless, the stranger,
        nor the poor" (KJV) — The four categories of the
        vulnerable that recur throughout the prophets. God
        measures a society by its treatment of those who cannot
        defend themselves.

v.12 — "they made their hearts as an adamant stone, lest they
        should hear" (KJV) / "They made their hearts diamond-hard
        lest they should hear" (ESV) — The ultimate hardness:
        deliberate, chosen resistance. They didn't fail to hear —
        they refused to hear. The hardening was voluntary. This is
        the most dangerous spiritual state.

v.13 — "as he cried, and they would not hear; so they cried, and
        I would not hear" (KJV) — A terrifying reciprocity. Those
        who refuse to listen to God will find that God refuses to
        listen to them. The day of grace has limits.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.5   — Isaiah 58:3-7 — God's exposé of empty fasting: "Is not
         this the fast that I have chosen? to loose the bands of
         wickedness."
v.5   — Matthew 6:16-18 — Jesus' teaching on fasting for God's
         approval, not human attention.
v.9   — Micah 6:8 — "What doth the LORD require of thee, but to
         do justly, and to love mercy, and to walk humbly?"
v.10  — James 1:27 — "Pure religion... is this, To visit the
         fatherless and widows in their affliction."
v.12  — Hebrews 3:7-8 — "Harden not your hearts, as in the
         provocation."
v.13  — Proverbs 1:24-28 — "Then shall they call upon me, but I
         will not answer" — the consequence of rejecting wisdom.
v.14  — Deuteronomy 28:64 — The scattering among nations as
         covenant consequence for disobedience.

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.3 — "separating myself" (KJV) / "abstain" (ESV) — Hebrew
      "hinnazar" = to consecrate oneself by abstaining (same root
      as "Nazirite"). The fasting involved specific acts of self-
      denial — but the motivation was wrong.

v.11 — "pulled away the shoulder" (KJV) / "turned a stubborn
       shoulder" (ESV) — Hebrew "nathan kateph sorerot" =
       literally "gave a turning/rebellious shoulder." The image
       of an ox refusing the yoke — turning its shoulder away from
       the load. Willful resistance to God's direction.

v.12 — "adamant stone" (KJV) / "diamond-hard" (ESV) — Hebrew
       "shamir." Used elsewhere for the hardest cutting stone.
       The heart became impenetrable — no word could break through.
       A self-inflicted condition with devastating consequences.

v.14 — "pleasant land" — Hebrew "eretz chemdah" = the land of
       delight/desire. The Promised Land — so beautiful, so
       blessed — made desolate by disobedience. The land reflects
       the spiritual state of its people.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Did ye fast unto ME?" (v.5). Examine your spiritual
   disciplines. Is your prayer life about connecting with GOD
   or about feeling religious? Is your church attendance for HIM
   or for your social identity? The crucial question is not
   whether you do spiritual things but WHO they are actually for.

2. God wants justice and mercy, not just worship services
   (vv.9-10). You cannot sing on Sunday and oppress on Monday.
   True worship produces just behavior. If your religion doesn't
   change how you treat the vulnerable, it isn't real religion
   (James 1:27).

3. A hard heart is chosen, not inherited (v.12). They MADE their
   hearts diamond-hard. Hardness is a decision — repeated refusals
   to listen calcify into inability to hear. Every time you dismiss
   God's conviction, your heart gets a little harder. Respond NOW
   while your heart is still soft.

4. God's patience has limits (v.13). He called; they refused. Then
   THEY called; HE refused. This is sobering. God is patient — but
   persistent rejection exhausts even divine patience. Don't
   presume on grace. Respond to God while He is still calling.

5. Disobedience desolates what was once beautiful (v.14). The
   "pleasant land" became wasteland. Sin ALWAYS desolates. What
   was once flourishing — your marriage, your joy, your calling —
   can become wasteland through persistent disobedience. Choose
   repentance before desolation.
