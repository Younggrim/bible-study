Zechariah - Chapter 11
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Zechariah enacts a parable of two shepherd staffs — Favor and Union — breaking them to symbolize God's rejected covenant and the breaking of brotherhood between Judah and Israel, valued at thirty pieces of silver.


AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Zechariah son of Berechiah. Prophesied 520-480 BC.
Contemporary with Haggai. Key theme: Messianic hope and the
coming kingdom. The most Messianic of the Minor Prophets.
Famous passages: the Branch (3:8, 6:12), "Not by might... but
by my spirit" (4:6), the triumphal entry (9:9), "they shall
look upon me whom they have pierced" (12:10), the shepherd
struck (13:7), and the LORD's return to the Mount of Olives
(14:4).

Classification: Prophetic Oracle — The Rejected Shepherd and
the Thirty Pieces of Silver
Key Themes: The Good Shepherd rejected, the flock doomed to
slaughter, the breaking of covenant, thirty pieces of silver
(the price of Messiah's betrayal), the foolish shepherd who
destroys the flock, judgment for rejecting the true Shepherd

Historical Context: Chapter 11 is one of the most remarkable
prophetic passages in the OT. Zechariah enacts a prophetic drama
as a shepherd over a "flock doomed to slaughter." He takes two
staffs — "Beauty" (Favor) and "Bands" (Union) — representing
God's gracious covenant and the unity of the people. But the
flock despises the shepherd, and the shepherd breaks both staffs
in sequence: first Favor (the covenant annulled) then Union
(national brotherhood dissolved).

The most astonishing detail: when asked for his wages, the
shepherd receives THIRTY PIECES OF SILVER — described with
bitter irony as "a goodly price" (the price of a gored slave,
Exodus 21:32). God says: "Cast it to the potter" — and the
prophet throws the silver into the Temple, to the potter. This
was fulfilled with eerie precision when Judas received thirty
silver pieces for betraying Christ (Matt 26:15) and later threw
them into the Temple, after which the priests used them to buy
the potter's field (Matt 27:3-10).

Structure:
- The Devastation of the Land (vv.1-3)
- The Shepherd of the Doomed Flock (vv.4-7)
- The Shepherd Rejected by the Flock (vv.8-9)
- The Staff "Beauty" Broken: Covenant Annulled (vv.10-11)
- Thirty Pieces of Silver: The Shepherd's Price (vv.12-13)
- The Staff "Bands" Broken: Brotherhood Dissolved (v.14)
- The Rise of the Foolish Shepherd (vv.15-17)

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.4  — "Feed the flock of the slaughter" (KJV) / "Become
        shepherd of the flock doomed to slaughter" (ESV) — God
        commands Zechariah to shepherd a flock whose owners exploit
        and kill them without guilt. This pictures Israel under
        corrupt leaders who profit from the people's suffering.
        The Good Shepherd comes to a people already being destroyed.

v.7  — "I took unto me two staves; the one I called Beauty, and
        the other I called Bands" (KJV) / "one I named Favor, the
        other I named Union" (ESV) — Two aspects of God's
        relationship with Israel: His gracious favor (Beauty/Favor)
        and the unity He provides (Bands/Union). When the shepherd
        is rejected, BOTH are broken.

v.12 — "they weighed for my price thirty pieces of silver" (KJV)
        — The exact sum Judas received for betraying Christ (Matt
        26:15). Thirty shekels was the compensation for a slave
        gored by an ox (Exod 21:32) — the lowest valuation
        possible. Israel valued the Good Shepherd at slave-price.
        This is what the world thought Jesus was worth.

v.13 — "Cast it unto the potter: a goodly price that I was prised
        at of them" (KJV) — Dripping with divine sarcasm: "a
        lordly price!" The silver is thrown to the potter in the
        Temple — precisely what happened with Judas's blood money
        (Matt 27:5-10). Zechariah wrote this 500+ years before
        the event.

v.16 — "I will raise up a shepherd in the land, which shall not
        visit those that be cut off, neither shall seek the young
        one, nor heal that that is broken" (KJV) — After
        rejecting the Good Shepherd, Israel receives a FOOLISH
        shepherd who devours rather than feeds. When you reject
        Christ, you get anti-Christ. History confirms this
        tragically in Israel's suffering under corrupt and
        oppressive leadership.

v.17 — "Woe to the idol shepherd that leaveth the flock!" (KJV)
        / "Woe to my worthless shepherd, who deserts the flock!"
        (ESV) — The foolish shepherd's judgment is certain. His
        arm (strength) will wither; his eye (perception) will be
        blinded. Judgment comes to those who exploit God's people.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------

         even my servant David" — God's solution for bad shepherds.
v.12  — Matthew 26:15 — "They covenanted with him for thirty
         pieces of silver" — Judas's price for Christ.
v.13  — Matthew 27:3-10 — Judas throwing the silver into the
         Temple; the priests buying the potter's field.
v.13  — Exodus 21:32 — Thirty shekels: the price of a dead slave.
v.10  — Romans 11:1-10 — The partial hardening/rejection of

         sheep" — the false shepherd who abandons the flock.
v.16  — 2 Thessalonians 2:3-4 — The man of lawlessness — the
         ultimate "foolish shepherd."

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.7 — "Beauty" (KJV) / "Favor" (ESV) — Hebrew "no'am" = grace,
      pleasantness, beauty, favor. Represents God's gracious
      covenant relationship with His people.

v.7 — "Bands" (KJV) / "Union" (ESV) — Hebrew "choblim" = cords,
      bands, binding together. Represents the brotherhood that
      holds Judah and Israel together as one people.

v.12 — "thirty pieces of silver" — Hebrew "sheloshim kasef." The
       exact compensation for a slave (Exod 21:32). The insult is
       deliberate: the Good Shepherd is valued at the cheapest
       possible rate. This is contempt, not payment.

v.13 — "the potter" — Hebrew "yotser." Why "the potter"? Perhaps
       connected to the potter's field (Matt 27:7) or to God as
       the Potter who forms vessels (Jer 18:1-6). The connection
       between Zechariah's prophecy and the NT fulfillment is
       remarkably precise.

v.17 — "idol shepherd" (KJV) / "worthless shepherd" (ESV) —
       Hebrew "ro'i ha'elil" = shepherd of nothingness/worthless-
       ness. "Elil" is related to idols (worthless things). A
       shepherd who is himself an idol — empty, powerless, and
       destructive.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Rejecting the Good Shepherd has consequences (vv.8-10). When
   Israel despised Christ, they received oppressive leaders
   instead. Whom you reject determines whom you receive. If you
   reject Christ's gentle rule, you will end up under harsher
   masters — sin, self, or the world.

2. God was valued at slave-price (v.12). Thirty pieces of silver
   — that's what the world thought Jesus was worth. How do YOU
   value Christ? Is He slave-price to you — an afterthought, a
   convenience? Or is He the Pearl of great price for whom you
   sell everything? (Matt 13:46)

3. When "Beauty" is broken, grace departs (v.10). God's favor is
   not automatic. When persistently rejected, He withdraws His
   gracious protection. Don't take God's patience for granted.
   Covenant blessings have covenant conditions.

4. When "Bands" is broken, unity dissolves (v.14). Rejection of
   Christ fractures community. Without the true Shepherd, the
   flock divides against itself. Unity among God's people is
   maintained by shared submission to Christ. When He is rejected,
   brotherhood disintegrates.

5. The foolish shepherd devours what the Good Shepherd would feed
   (v.16). False leaders consume the people they should serve.
   Be discerning about who shepherds you. A true shepherd heals,
   seeks, and feeds. A false one exploits, abandons, and devours.
