Zechariah - Chapter 4
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
The vision of the golden lampstand and two olive trees teaches that the temple will be rebuilt "not by might nor by power, but by My Spirit" — Zerubbabel's hands laid the foundation and will complete it.


AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Zechariah son of Berechiah. Prophesied 520-480 BC.
Contemporary with Haggai. Key theme: Messianic hope and the
coming kingdom. The most Messianic of the Minor Prophets.
Famous passages: the Branch (3:8, 6:12), "Not by might... but
by my spirit" (4:6), the triumphal entry (9:9), "they shall
look upon me whom they have pierced" (12:10), the shepherd
struck (13:7), and the LORD's return to the Mount of Olives
(14:4).

Classification: Prophetic Vision — The Golden Lampstand, the
Two Olive Trees, and the Spirit's Power
Key Themes: The sufficiency of the Spirit, human weakness and
divine power, the day of small things, Zerubbabel's
completion of the Temple, continuous supply of God's anointing

Historical Context: The fifth night vision reveals a golden
lampstand (menorah) with a bowl on top and seven lamps — each
with seven channels (49 total openings for light). Flanking it
are two olive trees that continuously feed oil into the bowl.
The lamp never goes out because the supply never ceases.

The interpretation centers on Zerubbabel's task of rebuilding the
Temple. The mountain of opposition seems insurmountable. But God
declares one of the most famous verses in Scripture: "Not by
might, nor by power, but by my spirit, saith the LORD of hosts."
Human resources cannot complete God's work. Only the Spirit can.
The mountain will become a plain before Zerubbabel.

The "day of small things" (v.10) directly addresses the
discouragement of the remnant. The work seems insignificant. The
Temple seems modest. But God delights in small beginnings
empowered by His Spirit. The two olive trees (v.14) are "the
two anointed ones" — Joshua and Zerubbabel (priest and king) —
who stand before the Lord, continually supplied with His oil.

Structure:
- The Vision: A Golden Lampstand with Olive Trees (vv.1-3)
- Zechariah's Question and the Angel's Challenge (vv.4-5)
- "Not by Might... But by My Spirit" (v.6)
- The Mountain Becomes a Plain (v.7)
- Zerubbabel Will Finish the Temple (vv.8-9)
- The Day of Small Things (v.10)
- The Two Olive Trees Explained (vv.11-14)

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.6  — "Not by might, nor by power, but by my spirit, saith the
        LORD of hosts" (KJV) / "Not by might, nor by power, but
        by my Spirit, says the LORD of hosts" (ESV) — THE key
        verse of the chapter and one of the most quoted in all
        Scripture. "Might" (Hebrew "chayil") = army, strength,
        wealth. "Power" (Hebrew "koach") = individual force,
        ability. Neither collective resources nor personal talent
        accomplish God's work — ONLY His Spirit. This is the
        antidote to all self-reliance.

v.7  — "Who art thou, O great mountain? before Zerubbabel thou
        shalt become a plain: and he shall bring forth the
        headstone thereof with shoutings, crying, Grace, grace
        unto it" (KJV) — Every obstacle becomes flat ground when
        God's Spirit is at work. And the capstone is set with
        shouts of "Grace!" — the completion of God's work is
        attributed to GRACE, not human effort.

v.9  — "The hands of Zerubbabel have laid the foundation of this
        house; his hands shall also finish it" (KJV) — A divine
        guarantee. What God starts, He finishes — through the same
        instrument. Philippians 1:6 echoes this: "He which hath
        begun a good work in you will perform it."

v.10 — "For who hath despised the day of small things?" (KJV) —
        A rebuke to those who looked at the modest rebuilding and
        scoffed. God does not despise small beginnings. The plumb
        line in Zerubbabel's hand IS the work of God. Faithfulness
        in the small is what God honors.

v.14 — "These are the two anointed ones, that stand by the LORD
        of the whole earth" (KJV) — The two olive trees represent
        the priestly and royal offices (Joshua and Zerubbabel)
        through whom God's Spirit flows to Israel. Ultimately,
        Christ unites both offices — Prophet, Priest, and King.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------

         (menorah) — the pattern for Zechariah's vision.
v.6   — 2 Corinthians 12:9 — "My grace is sufficient for thee:
         for my strength is made perfect in weakness."
v.6   — 1 Samuel 17:47 — "The battle is the LORD's" — David's
         declaration against Goliath echoes this principle.
v.7   — Isaiah 40:4 — "Every mountain and hill shall be made low"
         — the same leveling imagery.
v.7   — Matthew 17:20 — "If ye have faith... ye shall say unto
         this mountain, Remove hence."

         will perform it until the day of Jesus Christ."
v.10  — 1 Corinthians 1:27-28 — God chooses the weak and small
         things to shame the mighty.
v.14  — Revelation 11:3-4 — The two witnesses described as "two
         olive trees... standing before the God of the earth."

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.2 — "candlestick" (KJV) / "lampstand" (ESV) — Hebrew
      "menorah." Not a candle but an oil lamp. The distinction
      matters: oil is consistently a symbol of the Holy Spirit in
      Scripture. The lamp burns by OIL, not wax — by Spirit, not
      human fuel.

v.6 — "might" — Hebrew "chayil" = force, army, wealth, resources,
      efficiency. "Power" — Hebrew "koach" = individual strength,
      vigor, capacity. Together they cover every human resource —
      collective and individual. NEITHER suffices for God's work.

v.7 — "headstone" (KJV) / "top stone" (ESV) — Hebrew "ha'eben
      harosha" = the head/chief stone. The capstone or
      cornerstone that completes the building. Its placement amid
      shouts of "Grace!" means the entire project — start to
      finish — is credited to God's grace, not human achievement.

v.10 — "plummet" (KJV) / "plumb line" (ESV) — Hebrew "ha'eben
       habedil" = the tin/lead stone used to ensure straight
       construction. Zerubbabel holds the measuring tool — he is
       actively building. The work is real, but the power is
       God's.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Not by might, nor by power, but by my Spirit" (v.6). This
   is the death sentence to self-reliance. Your talent, your
   resources, your strategic plans — none of them accomplish
   God's purposes. Only His Spirit does. The question is not
   "How much can I do?" but "How much of the Spirit am I
   depending on?"

2. Your "great mountain" will become a plain (v.7). That
   insurmountable obstacle — the debt, the addiction, the broken
   relationship, the impossible task — is addressed to directly:
   "Who are YOU, O great mountain?" Before God's anointed
   servant, mountains flatten. Speak to your mountain in faith.

3. Don't despise the day of small things (v.10). If your ministry
   is small, your progress is slow, your resources are limited —
   do not despise it. God sees the plumb line in your hand. He
   rejoices over faithfulness in the small. Small + the Spirit =
   unstoppable.

4. The supply is continuous (vv.11-12). The olive trees feed oil
   CONSTANTLY into the lampstand. You don't need to stockpile
   anointing — you need to stay CONNECTED to the source. The
   Spirit flows continuously to those who remain positioned
   beside the Lord.

5. "Grace, grace!" (v.7). The shout at completion is not
   "Achievement!" or "Finally!" — it is "Grace!" Every finished
   work of God in your life will be attributed to His grace, not
   your grit. Live in grace. Finish in grace. Shout grace.
