Ecclesiastes - Chapter 5
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
The vanity of rash vows and wealth — guard your steps before God, let your words be few, and recognize that the lover of money is never satisfied; naked we came and naked we return.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: 'The Preacher' (Qoheleth), traditionally identified as Solomon.
Written late in life as a reflection on life's meaning. Hebrew title:
'Qoheleth' — 'The Assembler/Preacher.' Key themes: the vanity (hebel =
vapor/breath) of life 'under the sun' (without God's perspective), the
search for meaning in pleasure/wisdom/work/wealth, the certainty of
death, and the conclusion: 'Fear God, and keep his commandments: for
this is the whole duty of man' (12:13).

Historical Context: Chapter 5 divides into two major sections: proper
conduct before God in worship (vv.1-7) and the vanity of wealth
(vv.8-20). The worship section is remarkable within Ecclesiastes —
here the Preacher moves from "under the sun" observations to direct
instruction about relating to God. His counsel is striking: be
reverent, be brief, keep your vows. "God is in heaven, and thou upon
earth" (v.2) — the distance between creature and Creator demands
humility. The wealth section (vv.8-20) contains some of the most
penetrating observations about money in all Scripture: the lover of
silver is never satisfied (v.10), increased wealth means increased
dependents (v.11), riches are lost in bad ventures (v.14), and we
leave the world as naked as we entered it (v.15). Yet the chapter
ends with another enjoyment passage (vv.18-20): receiving one's
portion with joy is God's gift — and God keeps the heart occupied
with gladness.

Structure:
- Reverence in Worship: Words Before God (vv.1-7)
- The Vanity of Wealth: Never Enough (vv.8-17)
- Third Enjoyment Conclusion: Receive Your Portion from God (vv.18-20)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "Be not rash with your mouth, nor let your heart be hasty
        to utter a word before God, for God is in heaven and you
        are on earth. Therefore let your words be few." (ESV)
        — The theological foundation for reverence: God is
        transcendent; you are finite. The gap between heaven and
        earth should govern your speech in worship. Hasty words
        before God reveal a shrunken view of His majesty. Fewer
        words, more weight.

v.4  — "When you vow a vow to God, do not delay paying it, for he
        has no pleasure in fools. Pay what you vow." (ESV) — Vows
        are serious because God hears them.
        Better to never vow than to vow and break it (v.5). This
        applies to every promise made before God — marriage vows,
        dedications, commitments to serve. God holds you to your
        word.

v.10 — "He who loves money will not be satisfied with money, nor
        he who loves wealth with his income; this also is vanity."
        (ESV) — The fundamental law of materialism:
        wealth cannot satisfy the desire for wealth. The appetite
        for money grows with feeding. This is not merely a moral
        judgment but a psychological observation — the nature of
        greed is insatiability.

v.15 — "As he came from his mother's womb he shall go again, naked
        as he came, and shall take nothing for his toil that he may
        carry away in his hand." (ESV) —
        The ultimate argument against hoarding: you will leave
        exactly as you arrived — with nothing. Every possession
        is temporary. No U-Haul follows a hearse.

v.19 — "Everyone also to whom God has given wealth and possessions
        and power to enjoy them, and to accept his lot and rejoice
        in his toil — this is the gift of God." (ESV) — The key
        distinction: having wealth is not
        the problem — inability to enjoy it is. When God gives both
        wealth AND the capacity to enjoy it, that enjoyment itself
        is a divine gift. Many have riches without this second gift.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.1   — James 1:19 — "Let every person be quick to hear, slow to
         speak." (ESV) NT echoes the Preacher's counsel on
         restraint before God.
v.2   — Matthew 6:7 — "Do not heap up empty phrases as the Gentiles
         do." (ESV) Jesus teaches the same economy of words in
         prayer.
v.4   — Deuteronomy 23:21-23 — "If you make a vow to the LORD your
         God, you shall not delay fulfilling it." (ESV)
v.10  — 1 Timothy 6:9-10 — "For the love of money is a root of all
         kinds of evils." (ESV) Paul's famous expansion of
         Solomon's observation.
v.12  — Proverbs 3:24 — "If you lie down, you will not be afraid;
         when you lie down, your sleep will be sweet." (ESV) The
         peace of the simple life.
v.15  — Job 1:21 — "Naked I came from my mother's womb, and naked
         shall I return." (ESV) Job and Solomon agree.
v.15  — 1 Timothy 6:7 — "For we brought nothing into the world,
         and we cannot take anything out of the world." (ESV)
v.19  — James 1:17 — "Every good gift and every perfect gift is
         from above." (ESV) Enjoyment is always God's gift.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/ecclesiastes-5/
  Guzik highlights the unusual nature of vv.1-7 within
  Ecclesiastes — here the Preacher speaks directly about
  relating to God rather than merely observing life "under the
  sun." The instruction is severe: approach God with the
  reverence due to One who is infinitely above you. On wealth,
  Guzik notes that v.10 is not condemning riches but exposing
  the impossibility of satisfaction through accumulation. The
  problem is not having money but loving it — making it the
  object of your heart's desire.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "He that loveth silver shall not be satisfied with silver.
  Here is a law as fixed as gravity. The man who makes gold his
  god will find that gold is a hard master and a poor comforter.
  It promises everything and delivers nothing. It cannot buy a
  good night's sleep — the labouring man sleeps sweetly while
  the rich man tosses and turns counting what he might lose.
  Set your heart on the eternal riches of Christ, which can
  never be stolen, never depreciate, and never disappoint."
TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1  — KJV: "Keep thy foot" / ESV: "Guard your steps" — Hebrew
       "shamar ragleka" = guard/watch your foot. The image is of
       careful approach — enter God's house with deliberate
       reverence, not careless haste. "Sacrifice of fools" = the
       thoughtless religious ritual performed without genuine
       engagement.

v.6  — KJV: "before the angel" / ESV: "before the messenger" —
       Hebrew "malak" can mean angel or messenger (human). Some
       interpret this as a temple priest (God's messenger); others
       as a literal angel witnessing your vow. Either way: your
       words before God are witnessed and accountable.

v.8  — KJV: "he that is higher than the highest regardeth" / ESV:
       "the high official is watched by a higher" — This
       difficult verse likely describes the hierarchy of
       bureaucratic corruption: each official is watched by a
       higher one — but ultimately God watches over all. The
       system of oppression is not the final word.

v.20 — KJV: "God answereth him in the joy of his heart" / ESV:
       "God keeps him occupied with joy" — Hebrew "ma'aneh" can
       mean answers or occupies. The idea: God fills the grateful
       person's heart with enough joy that they do not obsess over
       life's brevity. Joy is God's distraction from vanity.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Vow (neder) — A solemn promise made to God, often conditional
              ("If God does X, I will do Y"). In ancient Israel,
              vows were voluntary but absolutely binding once
              spoken (Numbers 30:2). The Preacher warns: it is
              better never to vow than to vow and fail to fulfill.

Silver (keseph) — Money, wealth (literally silver, the primary
              currency). In v.10, it represents the object of
              materialistic desire. The Preacher's observation is
              that the desire for wealth is structurally
              insatiable — more always demands more.

Portion (cheleq) — One's allotted share in life. The Preacher
              repeatedly counsels receiving one's daily "portion"
              with gratitude rather than grasping for the whole.
              Contentment is finding joy in what God has given
              rather than mourning what He has withheld.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Approach God with reverence, not casualness (vv.1-2). He is in
   heaven; you are on earth. Let that distance govern your prayer
   life. Quality of attention matters more than quantity of words.
   Come to worship ready to listen, not merely to speak.

2. Keep your promises to God (vv.4-5). Every vow made before Him
   is binding. If you have made commitments — to your spouse, to
   your church, to serve, to give — fulfill them. It is better to
   promise nothing than to promise and break faith.

3. Money will never satisfy the desire for money (v.10). This is
   not a moral platitude but a structural truth. The appetite for
   wealth grows with feeding. If you are waiting until you have
   "enough" to be content, you will wait forever. Choose
   contentment now.

4. You will leave with nothing (v.15). Every material possession
   is on temporary loan. This truth should liberate, not depress.
   Hold things loosely. Invest in what cannot be lost — faith,
   character, relationships, eternal reward.

5. The ability to enjoy what you have is itself God's gift (v.19).
   Some people have everything and enjoy nothing. Enjoyment is not
   automatic — it requires God's grace. Thank Him not only for
   provision but for the capacity to receive it with gladness.
