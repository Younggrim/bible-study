Ecclesiastes - Chapter 6
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
The vanity of wealth without enjoyment — God may give a man riches, honor, and long life, yet not the ability to enjoy them; this is a grievous evil, for the appetite is never satisfied.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: 'The Preacher' (Qoheleth), traditionally identified as Solomon.
Written late in life as a reflection on life's meaning. Hebrew title:
'Qoheleth' — 'The Assembler/Preacher.' Key themes: the vanity (hebel =
vapor/breath) of life 'under the sun' (without God's perspective), the
search for meaning in pleasure/wisdom/work/wealth, the certainty of
death, and the conclusion: 'Fear God, and keep his commandments: for
this is the whole duty of man' (12:13).

Historical Context: Chapter 6 continues and deepens the meditation on
wealth's inability to satisfy. Where chapter 5 ended with the gift
of enjoyment, chapter 6 presents its dark counterpart: the curse of
having everything yet being unable to enjoy it. The Preacher describes
a man whom God gives riches, wealth, and honor — "so that he wanteth
nothing" — yet God does not give him the power to enjoy it (v.2). A
stranger consumes his wealth instead. This is described as "an evil
disease" — a sickness of the soul. The chapter's most shocking
statement: a stillborn child is better off than such a man (v.3),
because at least the stillborn finds rest. Chapter 6 marks the
approximate midpoint of Ecclesiastes and closes the first half of
the book. The repeated question "who knoweth what is good for man?"
(v.12) transitions to the second half, which will attempt to answer
that very question with practical wisdom.

Structure:
- The Evil of Wealth Without Enjoyment (vv.1-6)
- The Insatiability of Desire (vv.7-9)
- Human Limitation: Who Knows What Is Good? (vv.10-12)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "A man to whom God gives wealth, possessions, and honor, so
        that he lacks nothing of all that he desires, yet God does
        not give him power to enjoy them, but a stranger enjoys
        them. This is vanity; it is a grievous evil." (ESV) — The
        darkest form of vanity: abundance
        without capacity for enjoyment. The problem is not poverty
        but the inability to receive what one has. God gives wealth
        — but the "power to eat thereof" is a separate gift that
        may or may not accompany it.

v.3  — "If a man fathers a hundred children and lives many years,
        so that the days of his years are many, but his soul is not
        satisfied with life's good things, and he also has no
        burial, I say that a stillborn child is better off than
        he." (ESV) — A
        deliberately shocking comparison. Long life, many children
        — the highest blessings in ancient Israel — are worthless
        without the ability to enjoy them. A stillborn, though
        never experiencing life, at least has rest (v.5).

v.7  — "All the toil of man is for his mouth, yet his appetite is
        not satisfied." (ESV) — A profound observation on
        human desire: we work to satisfy appetite, but appetite is
        never permanently satisfied. The cycle of desire-fulfillment-
        desire never ends "under the sun." The soul has hungers that
        food cannot feed.

v.9  — "Better is the sight of the eyes than the wandering of the
        appetite: this also is vanity and a striving after wind."
        (ESV) — Better to enjoy what is before you (sight of the
        eyes =
        present reality) than to endlessly pursue what you do not
        have (wandering desire = restless craving). Contentment with
        the present is superior to perpetual yearning for more.

v.12 — "For who knows what is good for man while he lives the few
        days of his vain life, which he passes like a shadow? For
        who can tell man what will be after him under the sun?"
        (ESV) — The transitional question that closes the first
        half of Ecclesiastes. Two limitations: (1) we do not know
        what is truly good for us; (2) we cannot know the future.
        Both drive us toward dependence on God.

         wealth.

         (ESV) The positive counterpart — enjoyment as gift.
v.3   — Job 3:16 — "Or why was I not as a hidden stillborn child?"
         (ESV) Job also compares his suffering unfavorably to
         stillbirth.

         is not bread?" (ESV) Only God satisfies true hunger.
v.9   — Philippians 4:11 — "I have learned in whatever situation I
         am to be content." (ESV) Paul learned what the Preacher

         him." (ESV) Contending with God is futile.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/ecclesiastes-6/
  Guzik observes that chapter 6 is the "dark twin" of chapter
  5's enjoyment passage. Having wealth is one thing; the ability
  to enjoy it is another entirely — and the second is wholly in
  God's hands. The comparison to a stillborn (v.3) is deliberately
  extreme: Solomon forces us to confront that length of life and
  material abundance mean nothing without the God-given capacity
  for contentment. Verse 12 closes the first half of the book
  with an honest confession: we do not know what is truly good
  for us — only God does.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "A man may have the whole world in his hand and not one crumb
  of it in his heart. Riches without the grace to enjoy them are
  like a banquet spread before a man with no appetite — torment,
  not blessing. The power to enjoy is a distinct gift from God.
  Pray for it as earnestly as you pray for provision. What good
  is a full table if you cannot eat?"
TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.2  — KJV: "an evil disease" / ESV: "a grievous evil" — Hebrew
       "choli ra" literally = a sickness/evil. The inability to
       enjoy one's wealth is described in medical terms — it is a
       disease of the soul, a pathology of the spirit. This is not
       mere misfortune but something deeply wrong.

v.3  — KJV: "an untimely birth" / ESV: "a stillborn child" —
       Hebrew "nephel" = a miscarriage or stillborn. The comparison
       is deliberately provocative: the stillborn never experienced
       life, yet has "more rest" than the wealthy man who cannot
       enjoy his abundance.

v.9  — KJV: "sight of the eyes" / ESV: "sight of the eyes" —
       Hebrew "mar'eh enayim" = what the eyes can see, i.e.,
       present reality. Contrasted with "halak nephesh" (wandering
       of the desire/soul) — the restless roaming of unsatisfied
       appetite. Present gratitude vs. perpetual longing.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Evil Disease (choli ra) — A sickness or affliction of the soul.
              In v.2, it describes the condition of having
              abundance without the capacity for enjoyment — a
              spiritual pathology that turns blessing into curse.
              Only God can heal this disease by granting the gift
              of contentment.

Shadow (tsel) — A fleeting, insubstantial image. In v.12, human
              life is compared to a shadow — brief, passing,
              without permanent substance. Like hebel (vapor),
              shadow imagery emphasizes the transience of earthly
              existence.

Appetite (nephesh) — Literally "soul" or "throat" — the seat of
              desire and longing. In v.7, all labor serves the
              appetite, yet the appetite is never permanently
              filled. The soul's desire transcends what physical
              provision can satisfy.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Wealth without enjoyment is a curse, not a blessing (v.2).
   Do not assume that accumulation equals fulfillment. Some of
   the wealthiest people are the most miserable. Ask God not
   only for provision but for the grace to enjoy what He provides.

2. Long life without goodness is not a gift (v.3). Years without
   joy, accomplishments without satisfaction, children without
   love — these are not blessings. Quality of soul matters more
   than quantity of years. Pursue depth with God, not mere
   longevity.

3. Your appetite will never be permanently satisfied by earthly
   things (v.7). Food satisfies hunger temporarily; tomorrow you
   will be hungry again. This is the nature of all temporal
   goods. Only the Bread of Life satisfies permanently. Stop
   expecting finite things to provide infinite satisfaction.

4. Enjoy what you have rather than craving what you lack (v.9).
   "The sight of the eyes" — present reality — is better than
   "wandering desire" — the restless pursuit of more. Practice
   gratitude for what is in front of you. Contentment is a
   discipline, not a feeling.

5. Confess your limitations honestly (v.12). You do not know
   what is truly good for you. You cannot see the future. This
   is not weakness — it is wisdom. Surrender your plans to the
   One who knows, and trust Him to give what is genuinely good.
