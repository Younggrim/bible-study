Ezekiel - Chapter 2
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
God commissions Ezekiel: "Son of man, I send thee to the children of Israel, to a rebellious nation." They may or may not listen — but they'll KNOW a prophet has been among them. Don't be afraid of them or their words (though they're "briers and thorns"). God gives Ezekiel a scroll written on both sides with "lamentations, and mourning, and woe" — and tells him to eat it.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Ezekiel son of Buzi (see Chapter 1 notes for full details).

Classification: Prophetic Call Narrative — The Commission
Key Themes: "Son of man" as divine address, sent to a rebellious
people, success not guaranteed (just faithfulness), don't fear
faces, the scroll of lamentation eaten

Historical Context: Following the overwhelming throne-vision
(ch.1), Ezekiel receives his commission. The address "son of man"
(Hebrew "ben adam") appears 93 times in Ezekiel — emphasizing the
prophet's HUMANITY before God's overwhelming glory. The commission
is stark: you're sent to rebels. They're "impudent" (hard-faced)
and "stiffhearted." They probably WON'T listen. But preach anyway
— so they'll KNOW a prophet was among them. The scroll symbolizes
God's message: Ezekiel must INTERNALIZE it (eat it) before
externalizing it (preaching it). The scroll contains only "lamentations,
mourning, and woe" — Ezekiel's first message is entirely JUDGMENT.

Structure:
- "Stand Up: I Will Speak to You" (vv.1-2)
- The Commission: Sent to a Rebellious Nation (vv.3-5)
- Don't Be Afraid: Briers and Thorns (vv.6-7)
- The Scroll: Eat What I Give You (vv.8-10)

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "And he said unto me, Son of man, stand upon thy feet, and
        I will speak unto thee" (KJV) — From facedown (1:28) to
        standing up (2:1). God doesn't leave you prostrate — He
        raises you to receive your assignment.

v.3  — "Son of man, I send thee to the children of Israel, to a
        rebellious nation that hath rebelled against me" (KJV) —
        Sent not to Babylon, not to the Gentiles — but to ISRAEL.
        God's own people, described as "rebellious nation" (same
        word as "Gentiles/nations" — they've become like pagans).

v.5  — "And they, whether they will hear, or whether they will
        forbear... yet shall know that there hath been a prophet
        among them" (KJV) — Success is not measured by RESPONSE
        but by PRESENCE. Whether they listen or not — they'll KNOW
        a prophet was there. That's enough.

v.6  — "Be not afraid of them, neither be afraid of their words,
        though briers and thorns be with thee, and thou dost dwell
        among scorpions" (KJV) — The environment: briers (sharp
        words), thorns (painful opposition), scorpions (dangerous
        people). Don't fear ANY of it.

v.10 — "and it was written within and without: and there was
        written therein lamentations, and mourning, and woe" (KJV)
        — Both sides full (an unusual scroll — completely filled
        with judgment). The message is heavy: lamentation, mourning,
        woe. No comfort yet — that comes later (chs.33-48).

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1  — "Son of man" (KJV/ESV) — Hebrew "ben adam" = son of Adam,
      human being. Emphasizes Ezekiel's CREATURELINESS before the
      Creator. 93 times in Ezekiel. Jesus uses it as His primary
      self-title (in Aramaic "bar enash") — connecting to both
      Ezekiel and Daniel 7:13.

v.3  — "rebellious nation" (KJV/ESV) — Hebrew "goyim ha-moredim"
      = rebellious nations/peoples. Shockingly: "goyim" (usually
      = Gentiles/pagans) used for ISRAEL. They've become as
      pagan as the nations.

v.6  — "scorpions" (KJV/ESV) — Hebrew "aqrabbim." Literal
      scorpions or figurative: people who sting with deadly
      venom. The prophet lives AMONG danger.

v.9  — "roll of a book" (KJV) / "scroll" (ESV) — Hebrew
      "megillat sepher" = scroll of writing. Unrolled before
      Ezekiel's eyes so he can see its contents (lamentations)
      before ingesting them.

v.1 — NET: "on your feet" for KJV's "upon thy feet,"
       WEB: "with you.”" for KJV's "unto thee."
v.3 — ASV: "nations" for KJV's "a rebellious nation"
       NET: "nations who have" for KJV's "nation that hath"
       WEB: "of rebels who have" for KJV's "that hath"
v.6 — NET: adds "surround you and you live among scorpions—do not fear the..."
       WEB: "you do" for KJV's "thou dost"
v.9 — NET: "stretched out to me, and in it was" for KJV's "sent unto me; and, lo,"
       WEB: "stretched out to" for KJV's "sent unto"

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Son of Man   — God's address to Ezekiel 93 times. Emphasizes
              human frailty before divine glory. Jesus' favorite
              self-designation — connecting Himself to the prophetic
              tradition and Daniel's heavenly figure.

Rebellious Nation — Israel described with "goyim" language —
              they've become functionally pagan despite covenant
              identity. Rebellion has erased the distinction.

Eating the Scroll — Internalizing God's message before delivering
              it. The prophet doesn't merely READ the word — he
              CONSUMES it. It becomes part of him before it comes
              out of him.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Stand upon thy feet" (v.1). God raises you from prostration
   to commission. Worship precedes service. You can't serve
   effectively until you've been on your face — but you can't
   serve at all if you STAY there. Get up.

2. "Whether they hear or forbear" (v.5). Stop measuring your
   faithfulness by others' responses. Your job: deliver truth.
   Their response: their responsibility. Detach your obedience
   from their reception.

3. "Briers, thorns, and scorpions" (v.6). The environment will
   be hostile. God doesn't promise comfortable surroundings — He
   promises His PRESENCE in hostile ones. Don't expect ministry
   to feel safe.

4. The scroll must be EATEN (vv.8-10). Before you preach God's
   word, you must INTERNALIZE it. Don't deliver messages you
   haven't digested. God's word must be in your gut before it's
   in your mouth.

5. Lamentations, mourning, and woe (v.10). Sometimes God's
   message is ENTIRELY heavy. No silver lining, no feel-good
   ending (yet). Can you deliver a hard word faithfully without
   sugar-coating it?
