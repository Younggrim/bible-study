Ezekiel - Chapter 45
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
God commands the allotment of a "holy district" within the promised land — a sacred reserve for the temple, the priests, the Levites, and the city. The prince receives land flanking this district. The chapter then establishes just weights and measures (correcting the economic corruption condemned earlier in Ezekiel), and prescribes the prince's responsibilities for providing offerings at the feasts — Passover, Feast of Unleavened Bread, and Tabernacles.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Ezekiel son of Buzi (see Chapter 1 notes for full details).

Classification: Prophetic Vision — Sacred Land Division and
Worship Ordinances
Key Themes: The holy district (sacred geography), just weights
and measures, the prince's responsibility for public worship,
the restored feasts, God's concern for economic justice alongside
spiritual faithfulness

Historical Context: Having described the temple structure
(chs. 40-42), the glory's return (ch. 43), and priestly
regulations (ch. 44), Ezekiel now addresses the LAND. Where will
the temple sit? Who gets surrounding territory? How will worship
be funded? These practical questions receive divine answers.

The "holy district" (terumah) is a strip of land 25,000 cubits
long and 20,000 cubits wide, divided into three horizontal
bands: one for the Zadokite priests (with the temple at center),
one for the Levites, and one for the city and its inhabitants.
The prince receives the land on both sides (east and west).
This arrangement places the TEMPLE at the literal center of the
nation — geographically declaring what is theologically true:
God's presence is at the heart of everything.

The section on just weights (vv.9-12) is striking. In the
middle of temple legislation, God demands economic justice.
Holiness is not just liturgical — it is marketplace integrity.
Princes who exploit the people with crooked scales profane God's
name as surely as those who bring idols into the temple.

Structure:
- The Holy District: Temple, Priests, Levites, City (vv.1-6)
- The Prince's Portion (vv.7-8)
- Just Weights and Measures Demanded (vv.9-12)
- The People's Offerings to the Prince (vv.13-17)
- The Feasts: Passover and Tabernacles (vv.18-25)

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "Moreover, when ye shall divide by lot the land for
        inheritance, ye shall offer an oblation unto the LORD, a
        holy portion of the land" (KJV) — Before anyone receives
        their inheritance, GOD receives His. The first portion
        goes to the temple and those who serve it. God's claim on
        the land precedes every human claim. First fruits — even
        of real estate.

v.8  — "In the land shall be his possession in Israel: and my
        princes shall no more oppress my people; and the rest of
        the land shall they give to the house of Israel according
        to their tribes" (KJV) — God gives the prince ENOUGH —
        and then commands: "No more oppression." The land
        provision for rulers eliminates their excuse for
        exploiting the people. Greedy rulers were a persistent
        plague in Israel's history (cf. Ezekiel 34:1-10).

v.9  — "Thus saith the Lord GOD; Let it suffice you, O princes
        of Israel: remove violence and spoil, and execute judgment
        and justice, and take away your exactions from my people,
        saith the Lord GOD" (KJV) — "LET IT SUFFICE YOU" — be
        content with what God has given! Stop taking from the
        people. This echoes John the Baptist's command to
        soldiers: "Be content with your wages" (Luke 3:14).
        Leadership is service, not extraction.

v.10 — "Ye shall have just balances, and a just ephah, and a
        just bath" (KJV) — Honest weights. Fair measures. God
        CARES about the marketplace as much as the altar. You
        cannot worship with integrity on Sabbath and cheat
        customers on Monday. Economic justice IS spiritual
        obedience.

v.22 — "And upon that day shall the prince prepare for himself
        and for all the people of the land a bullock for a sin
        offering" (KJV) — The prince provides the sin offering
        for the entire nation at Passover. He bears the cost of
        national atonement. Leadership means absorbing the
        expense of the people's spiritual needs.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "oblation" (KJV) / "portion" (ESV) — Hebrew "terûmāh" =
      a contribution lifted up, a sacred reserve set apart for
      God. The same word used for the heave-offering. The land
      itself becomes an offering to God.

v.10 — "ephah" / "bath" — Hebrew dry and liquid measures
      respectively. An ephah ≈ 22 liters (dry goods); a bath ≈
      22 liters (liquids). Both are one-tenth of a homer. God
      standardizes measurements to prevent exploitation.

v.12 — "shekel" — Hebrew "sheqel" = a unit of weight (not a
      coin in Ezekiel's time). God defines: 20 gerahs per
      shekel, and specifies the mina as 60 shekels. Fixed,
      non-negotiable, tamper-proof standards.

v.18 — "first month" — Hebrew "ḥōḏesh hārī'shôn" = Nisan/Abib
      (March-April). The month of Passover and new beginnings.
      The religious calendar begins with redemption — always.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Holy District  — Hebrew "terûmāh" — a sacred strip of land
              25,000 x 20,000 cubits set apart for the temple,
              priests, and Levites. Located at the geographic
              center of the nation, declaring God's centrality.

The Prince    — The civil ruler of the restored community (nāśî').
              He has defined territory, defined responsibilities
              (funding offerings), and defined limits (no
              oppression). A steward-leader, not an absolute
              monarch.

Just Weights  — Standardized measurements (ephah, bath, shekel,
              mina) mandated by God to prevent economic
              exploitation. Honest commerce is a form of worship;
              cheating in the marketplace profanes God's name.

Passover      — The annual feast commemorating Israel's
              deliverance from Egypt (Exodus 12). In Ezekiel's
              temple, the prince provides the sin offering for
              the nation — a seven-day observance beginning on
              the 14th of the first month.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. God gets the first portion (v.1). Before tribal allotments,
   before personal inheritance — the LORD's portion is set apart.
   Do you give God the FIRST of your time, resources, and energy?
   Or does He get the leftovers after everything else is claimed?

2. Economic justice is worship (vv.9-12). You cannot separate
   Sunday faith from Monday business. How you handle money, treat
   employees, price your goods, and fulfill contracts — ALL of it
   is before God's face. "Just balances" are not business advice;
   they are divine commands.

3. "Let it suffice you" (v.9) — contentment is commanded for
   leaders. If you have authority over others — in business,
   ministry, family — God says: be content with your provision
   and stop extracting from those under your care. Leadership is
   for giving, not taking.

4. The prince bears the cost of worship (vv.16-17). The leader
   provides the offerings on behalf of the people. True
   leadership means absorbing costs others cannot bear. It means
   funding the spiritual needs of your community. It is expensive
   to lead like God intends.

5. God places the temple at the CENTER (vv.1-6). The holy
   district is the geographic heart of the nation. Is God's
   presence at the CENTER of your life — or pushed to the
   periphery? Everything should orbit around His dwelling, not
   the other way around.
