Ezekiel - Chapter 5
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
God commands Ezekiel to shave his head and beard with a sword, then divide the hair into thirds: one third burned in the city-model, one third struck with the sword around the city, one third scattered to the wind. A tiny remnant is bound in the prophet's garment — but even some of THOSE are thrown into fire. Jerusalem will be made a horror: "I will do in thee that which I have not done."

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Ezekiel son of Buzi (see Chapter 1 notes for full details).

Classification: Prophetic Sign-Act + Judgment Oracle
Key Themes: The divided hair (thirds of Jerusalem's population),
famine/sword/scattering, a tiny remnant barely preserved,
Jerusalem worse than surrounding nations, unprecedented judgment,
"then shall they know that I am the LORD"

Historical Context: A SWORD used as a razor — shaving the prophet's
head and beard. In priestly law, shaving was associated with
mourning and shame; for a PRIEST (Ezekiel was one), it was
normally FORBIDDEN (Leviticus 21:5). God overrides priestly purity
FOR the message. The three portions represent Jerusalem's
population: 1/3 die of famine/plague (burned), 1/3 killed by
the sword (struck), 1/3 scattered among nations (wind). A few
hairs bound in the garment = tiny remnant. But even SOME of those
are cast into fire (the remnant is further refined). The oracle
that follows (vv.5-17) explains: Jerusalem is WORSE than pagan
nations — she had God's laws and REJECTED them. Nations without
law are more righteous than Israel WITH law.

Structure:
- Shave with a Sword, Divide the Hair (vv.1-4)
- Jerusalem's Sin: Worse Than the Nations (vv.5-8)
- Unprecedented Judgment (vv.9-10)
- Three Judgments: Plague, Sword, Scatter (vv.11-12)
- God's Fury Completed (vv.13-17)

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.5  — "This is Jerusalem: I have set it in the midst of the
        nations" (KJV) — Jerusalem's PRIVILEGE: placed at the
        CENTER of nations, given God's law as a light to others.
        Greater privilege = greater accountability.

v.6  — "And she hath changed my judgments into wickedness more
        than the nations" (KJV) — Not just as bad as the pagans —
        WORSE. Having God's law and rejecting it is worse than
        never having it at all.

v.9  — "And I will do in thee that which I have not done, and
        whereunto I will not do any more the like, because of all
        thine abominations" (KJV) — UNPRECEDENTED judgment.
        Never before, never again in the same way. The siege of
        586 BC was uniquely terrible because of uniquely great sin.

v.11 — "Surely, because thou hast defiled my sanctuary with all
        thy detestable things... therefore will I also diminish
        thee; neither shall mine eye spare" (KJV) — The trigger:
        defiling GOD'S SANCTUARY. They brought abominations into
        the HOLY PLACE. God's eye will NOT spare — no mercy clause.

v.13 — "Thus shall mine anger be accomplished, and I will cause
        my fury to rest upon them, and I will be comforted" (KJV)
        — God's fury has an ENDPOINT: it will be "accomplished"
        (completed), and God Himself will be "comforted" (satisfied
        that justice is done). Wrath is not infinite.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1 — "take thee a sharp knife, take thee a barber's razor" (KJV)
    / "take a sharp sword... use it as a barber's razor" (ESV) —
    A SWORD used as a razor. The instrument of WAR becomes the
    instrument of SHAMING. Military destruction and personal
    humiliation merged in one image.

v.3 — "bind... in thy skirts" (KJV) / "bind in the skirts of
    your robe" (ESV) — Hebrew "kanfey" = the wings/corners of the
    garment. A few hairs tucked into the robe's hem = the tiny
    remnant preserved close to the prophet (close to God).

v.10 — "fathers shall eat the sons" (KJV/ESV) — Siege
    cannibalism: parents eating children AND children eating
    parents. The most extreme horror of prolonged starvation.

v.13 — "I will be comforted" (KJV) / "I will be satisfied" (ESV)
    — Hebrew "ve-hinachamti" = I will be comforted/settled/
    satisfied. God's wrath reaches its GOAL and STOPS. It's not
    eternal or purposeless.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Hair Divided by Sword — Ezekiel's shaved hair representing
              Jerusalem's population divided into three fates:
              famine-death, sword-death, and scattering. The sword
              as razor = military judgment as personal humiliation.

Worse Than Nations — (v.6) Israel's unique guilt: having God's
              law and being MORE disobedient than nations who never
              had it. Privilege INCREASES accountability.

Unprecedented Judgment — (v.9) A one-time-only level of
              punishment matched to a one-time-only level of sin.
              The 586 BC destruction was unique in its horror.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Greater privilege = greater accountability (vv.5-6). You have
   the FULL Bible, church history, Christian community — more
   spiritual privilege than ancient Israel had. Are you using it
   or ignoring it? The nations are more excusable than you.

2. WORSE than pagans (v.6). A professing Christian who lives
   worse than non-Christians is in a UNIQUELY dangerous position.
   Having light and choosing darkness is graver than never having
   light at all.

3. God's fury has an endpoint (v.13). Wrath is not purposeless
   rage — it reaches its goal and STOPS. If you're under
   discipline, it won't last forever. God's anger is measured
   and completed.

4. A few hairs bound in the garment (v.3). Even in the most
   severe judgment, God preserves a tiny remnant. YOU might be
   one of those tucked into God's garment — spared while
   everything around you burns.

5. They defiled God's sanctuary (v.11). What TRIGGERED this
   extreme judgment: bringing abominations into the holy place.
   Is there anything unholy that you've brought into your
   relationship with God — your worship, your prayer life, your
   inner sanctuary?
