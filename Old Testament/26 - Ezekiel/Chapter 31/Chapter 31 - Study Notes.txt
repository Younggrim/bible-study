Ezekiel - Chapter 31
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Pharaoh is compared to the great cedar of Assyria — the tallest, most magnificent tree in the garden of God. Its top reached into the clouds, all nations sheltered in its branches, and even Eden's trees envied it. But because its heart was lifted up in pride, God cut it down and cast it into Sheol. The lesson for Pharaoh: if ASSYRIA — greater than you — was felled, what hope do you have?

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Ezekiel son of Buzi (see Chapter 1 notes for full details).

Classification: Oracle Against the Nations (Egypt Series: 3 of 7)
Key Themes: Pharaoh compared to Assyria's cedar, the greatest
tree in Eden, pride in height and beauty, God cuts down the
tallest tree, descent to Sheol, the lesson of Assyria's fall
for Egypt, no tree is too great for God's axe

Historical Context: Dated to June 587 BC — just weeks before
Jerusalem's fall. Ezekiel delivers a parable aimed at Pharaoh
Hophra by using the historical example of Assyria's fall. The
logic: "You think you're great, Pharaoh? Consider Assyria — she
was GREATER than you. A cedar so tall it reached the clouds. And
I cut her down. What makes you think you're safe?"

Assyria had fallen to Babylon in 612 BC (the fall of Nineveh)
— less than 25 years before this prophecy. The memory was fresh.
The greatest empire the world had ever seen, ruling from Egypt
to Persia, was suddenly gone. Ezekiel uses this as a cautionary
tale: if Assyria couldn't survive God's axe, Egypt certainly
cannot.

The "garden of God" language (vv.8-9) echoes Eden — the cedar
of Assyria is depicted as a tree IN Eden, surpassing all others.
This Eden imagery connects to chapter 28 (the king of Tyre in
Eden) and creates a pattern: pride in the garden leads to
expulsion from the garden. The same story repeating.

Structure:
- The Question: Whom Are You Like? (vv.1-2)
- The Great Cedar: Unmatched Beauty and Height (vv.3-9)
- The Sin: Heart Lifted Up (vv.10-11)
- The Fall: Cut Down and Cast Out (vv.12-14)
- The Mourning: Sheol and the Nations (vv.15-18)

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.3  — "Behold, the Assyrian was a cedar in Lebanon with fair
        branches, and with a shadowing shroud, and of an high
        stature; and his top was among the thick boughs" (KJV) —
        Assyria as the GREATEST tree. Fair branches, enormous
        canopy, height among the clouds. The most impressive
        empire anyone had ever seen — made into a tree metaphor
        to teach a lesson.

v.8  — "The cedars in the garden of God could not hide him: the
        fir trees were not like his boughs, and the chestnut
        trees were not like his branches; nor any tree in the
        garden of God was like unto him in his beauty" (KJV) —
        Even the trees of EDEN couldn't match Assyria. "The
        garden of God" — this is supernatural comparison. Every
        kingdom in history was smaller than this one.

v.10 — "Therefore thus saith the Lord GOD; Because thou hast
        lifted up thyself in height, and he hath shot up his top
        among the thick boughs, and his heart is lifted up in his
        height" (KJV) — THE pivotal verse: "his heart is lifted
        up." The cause of the fall is always the same — PRIDE.
        Height became the occasion for arrogance. Greatness
        produced self-worship.

v.14 — "To the end that none of all the trees by the waters exalt
        themselves for their height... for they are all delivered
        unto death, to the nether parts of the earth" (KJV) —
        The moral of the story stated plainly: NO tree should
        boast in its height. ALL are headed to death. This is
        meant for Pharaoh — and for everyone.

v.18 — "To whom art thou thus like in glory and in greatness among
        the trees of Eden? yet shalt thou be brought down with the
        trees of Eden unto the nether parts of the earth" (KJV)
        — The direct application to Pharaoh: "This is YOU." You
        will share Assyria's fate. Eden's glory didn't prevent
        the fall; neither will yours.

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.3  — "shadowing shroud" (KJV) — Hebrew "choreshet metzel" =
      a forest-shade/shadowing canopy. The branches created a
      covering so vast that all nations rested under it. ESV:
      "forest shade." Imagery of empire as protection — nations
      sheltered under Assyria's power.

v.8  — "garden of God" (KJV/ESV) — Hebrew "gan elohim" = God's
      garden. Direct reference to Eden (confirmed by "trees of
      Eden" in vv.9, 16, 18). The greatest empires are compared
      to Eden's primal beauty — and found superior. Astonishing
      praise before devastating judgment.

v.14 — "nether parts of the earth" (KJV) — Hebrew "erets
      tachtit" = the lower/lowest earth = Sheol, the realm of
      the dead. ESV: "the world below." Descent to Sheol =
      national death, joining the dead empires of history.

v.15 — "I caused Lebanon to mourn for him" (KJV) — Hebrew
      "he'ebalti" = I caused mourning/made to be dark. Even
      NATURE mourns the fall of Assyria. The cedars of Lebanon
      grieve the loss of their greatest representative. Cosmic
      disruption accompanying national judgment.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
The Cedar of Assyria — The metaphor for Assyria as the tallest,
              most magnificent tree in God's garden. Represents
              the greatest empire anyone had known — used as a
              warning to Egypt.

Garden of God / Eden — The primordial garden of perfection. Used
              here as the standard of comparison — Assyria
              surpassed even Eden's trees. Connects to ch.28
              (Tyre in Eden) and the theme of pride-in-paradise
              leading to expulsion.

Descent to Sheol — National death. When an empire "goes down to
              the pit," it joins the dead civilizations of
              history — never to rise again. Sheol is the
              graveyard of kingdoms.

"Heart Lifted Up" — The consistent diagnosis for fallen empires
              in Ezekiel: the heart that looks at its own height
              and worships itself instead of God. The universal
              mechanism of catastrophic fall.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "To whom art thou thus like?" (v.18). God asks Pharaoh to
   COMPARE himself to Assyria's fate. Are you comparing yourself
   to the right examples? Look at those who fell before you and
   ask: "Am I repeating their pattern?" History teaches those
   willing to learn.

2. Height produces pride produces fall (v.10). It's a consistent
   formula: the taller you grow, the more your heart is tempted
   to "lift up." Success is the most dangerous season. The
   bigger the blessing, the bigger the temptation to self-worship.

3. No tree is immune (v.14). "NONE of all the trees" — no
   exception. Not Assyria, not Egypt, not Babylon, not Rome, not
   America, not YOU. Every human system is mortal. Every human
   achievement is temporary. Only God's kingdom endures.

4. Even the garden of God provides no immunity. Being IN Eden
   didn't prevent the fall (Adam). Being LIKE Eden didn't prevent
   Assyria's fall. Proximity to beauty, privilege, or blessing
   doesn't protect against pride. Only HUMILITY protects.

5. God comforts in Sheol (v.16). A strange note: "all the trees
   of Eden... shall be comforted in the nether parts." Even in
   judgment, there's a bitter "comfort" — you're not alone in
   your fall. Every empire eventually joins you. Cold comfort,
   but God notes it.
