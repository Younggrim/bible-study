Ezekiel - Chapter 23
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
The allegory of two sisters: Oholah (Samaria/Northern Kingdom) and Oholibah (Jerusalem/Southern Kingdom). Both are depicted as unfaithful wives who committed spiritual adultery through political alliances and idolatry — first with Assyria, then Egypt, then Babylon. Oholah was judged and destroyed (722 BC), yet Oholibah saw her sister's fate and became WORSE. God sentences Oholibah to the same fate at the hands of her former "lovers."

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Ezekiel son of Buzi (see Chapter 1 notes for full details).

Classification: Prophetic Allegory / Extended Metaphor
Key Themes: Spiritual adultery through political alliances, two
sisters representing two kingdoms, unfaithfulness despite
witnessing judgment, escalating sin, the lovers become the
executioners, jealous judgment of a betrayed God

Historical Context: This chapter parallels Ezekiel 16 but with a
different focus. Chapter 16 traced Jerusalem's history from
infancy; chapter 23 focuses specifically on the political
alliances that constituted spiritual adultery. The two sisters
represent the two kingdoms that resulted from the division after
Solomon (1 Kings 12).

Oholah (meaning "her own tent/tabernacle" — she set up her OWN
worship) represents Samaria/the Northern Kingdom, which pursued
alliances with Assyria (2 Kings 15:19; 17:3) and then was
destroyed by Assyria in 722 BC. Oholibah (meaning "my tabernacle
is in her" — God's true temple was in Jerusalem) represents
Jerusalem/the Southern Kingdom. Despite witnessing her sister's
destruction, Oholibah pursued the same path — allying with
Assyria (2 Kings 16:7), Egypt (Isaiah 30:1-3), and Babylon
(2 Kings 20:12-19).

The shocking graphic language of this chapter is intentional —
God wants His people to feel the REVULSION of their unfaithfulness
the way a betrayed spouse feels it. The political alliances were
not merely diplomatic errors; they were acts of spiritual
prostitution — trusting foreign powers instead of the LORD.

Structure:
- Introduction: Two Sisters, One Mother (vv.1-4)
- Oholah (Samaria) and Her Lovers (vv.5-10)
- Oholibah (Jerusalem): Worse Than Her Sister (vv.11-21)
- Judgment Pronounced on Oholibah (vv.22-35)
- Both Sisters Judged Together (vv.36-49)

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.4  — "And the names of them were Aholah the elder, and Aholibah
        her sister: and they were mine, and they bare sons and
        daughters" (KJV) — "They were MINE." God's possessive
        claim. These were not strangers who went astray — they
        were His covenant wives who bore His children. Betrayal
        by a spouse cuts deeper than rejection by a stranger.

v.11 — "And when her sister Aholibah saw this, she was more
        corrupt in her inordinate love than she, and in her
        whoredoms more than her sister" (KJV) — The tragedy
        intensified: Oholibah SAW her sister's destruction and
        learned NOTHING. She became WORSE. Witnessing judgment
        without repentance increases guilt.

v.22 — "Therefore, O Aholibah, thus saith the Lord GOD; Behold,
        I will raise up thy lovers against thee, from whom thy
        mind is alienated, and I will bring them against thee on
        every side" (KJV) — Devastating irony: the "lovers"
        become the executioners. The nations Jerusalem trusted
        for protection become the instruments of her destruction.

v.35 — "Therefore thus saith the Lord GOD; Because thou hast
        forgotten me, and cast me behind thy back, therefore bear
        thou also thy lewdness and thy whoredoms" (KJV) — "Cast
        me behind thy back" — God thrown away like garbage.
        Forgotten and discarded. The consequence: bear your own
        sin without His protection.

v.49 — "And they shall recompense your lewdness upon you, and ye
        shall bear the sins of your idols: and ye shall know that
        I am the Lord GOD" (KJV) — The recognition formula: even
        in judgment, the purpose is KNOWLEDGE of God. "Ye SHALL
        know" — whether through blessing or disaster, God WILL
        be known.

         his own brothers.

         the alliance begins.

         same idiom for total rejection of God.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.4  — "Aholah" (KJV) — Hebrew "Oholah" = "her tent" or "her
      own tabernacle." The name indicates the Northern Kingdom
      set up its OWN worship centers (Dan and Bethel — 1 Kings
      12:28-29) rather than worshiping at God's temple.

v.4  — "Aholibah" (KJV) — Hebrew "Oholibah" = "my tent is in
      her" or "my tabernacle is in her." God's temple was in
      Jerusalem — His presence dwelt THERE. This makes her
      adultery worse: she had God's actual presence and STILL
      chased after foreign lovers.

v.14 — "portrayed upon the wall" (KJV) — Hebrew "m'chuqqeh al
      haqqir" = carved/engraved on the wall. Refers to Babylonian
      relief carvings that depicted warriors in vermilion-dyed
      uniforms. Jerusalem was seduced by the IMAGE of Babylon's
      power — lust through visual propaganda.

v.25 — "jealousy" (KJV/ESV) — Hebrew "qin'ah" = passionate
      jealousy, the burning possessiveness of a betrayed spouse.
      Not petty envy but the righteous fury of violated covenant
      love.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Oholah — "Her own tent" — the Northern Kingdom (Samaria), which
              created its own worship system apart from God's
              temple. Destroyed by Assyria in 722 BC.

Oholibah — "My tent is in her" — the Southern Kingdom
              (Jerusalem), which housed God's temple yet still
              pursued foreign alliances and idolatry.

Spiritual Adultery — The prophetic metaphor for Israel's
              unfaithfulness: political alliances with pagan
              nations = sexual unfaithfulness to God the Husband.
              Not merely diplomatic — covenantally treasonous.

The Lovers Become Executioners — The poetic justice of God's
              judgment: the very nations Israel trusted for
              protection become the armies that destroy her.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Witnessing someone else's judgment and learning nothing (v.11).
   Oholibah SAW her sister destroyed and became worse. Are you
   watching others face consequences for sin and assuming you're
   immune? Proximity to judgment without repentance is not safety
   — it's accumulated guilt.

2. "They were MINE" (v.4). The depth of God's pain is
   proportional to the depth of His love. He is not a distant
   judge — He is a betrayed Husband. If you have trusted anything
   or anyone more than God, understand what that does to HIM.

3. Your "lovers" will turn on you (v.22). Whatever you trust
   instead of God will eventually become the instrument of your
   pain. The career, the relationship, the substance, the
   ideology — what you run TO instead of God will someday be
   used AGAINST you.

4. "Cast me behind thy back" (v.35). What a phrase. God
   discarded. Thrown behind you like something irrelevant. Is God
   behind your back right now? Behind your career, your
   entertainment, your comfort? Turn around.

5. The purpose is still "ye shall KNOW that I am the Lord" (v.49).
   Even this graphic judgment has a redemptive aim. God would
   rather be known through disaster than not known at all. He
   refuses to be ignored.
