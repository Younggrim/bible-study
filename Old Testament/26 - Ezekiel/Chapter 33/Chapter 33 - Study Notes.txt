Ezekiel - Chapter 33
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
The great turning point of the book. Ezekiel is recommissioned as a watchman — the same call from chapter 3, but now with renewed urgency. God reaffirms individual responsibility: the wicked who turn will live, the righteous who fall will die. Then comes the moment the entire book has been building toward: a fugitive arrives from Jerusalem with the news — "The city is smitten!" Ezekiel's mouth is opened, his silence ends, and a new era of prophetic ministry begins. The chapter closes with God's rebuke of the people who HEAR Ezekiel's words but treat him as entertainment rather than truth.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Ezekiel son of Buzi (see Chapter 1 notes for full details).

Classification: Prophetic Recommissioning / Narrative / Oracle
Key Themes: The watchman's responsibility, individual moral
accountability, "turn ye, turn ye!" — God's desire that the
wicked live, the fugitive arrives with news of Jerusalem's fall,
Ezekiel's silence broken, hearing without obeying, treating the
prophet as entertainment

Historical Context: Chapter 33 marks the structural center of
the book. Chapters 1-24 were judgment on Jerusalem (spoken
BEFORE the fall). Chapters 25-32 judged the nations. Now
chapters 33-48 turn to restoration and hope. The hinge is verse
21: "The city is smitten."

The recommissioning as watchman (vv.1-9) echoes chapter 3:16-21
almost verbatim — but the repetition is not redundant. After the
long silence (since chapter 24:27), Ezekiel needs fresh
authorization for a new phase of ministry. Before the fall, his
job was WARNING. After the fall, his job shifts to RESTORATION
and HOPE.

The fugitive's arrival (v.21) is dated to January 585 BC — about
18 months after Jerusalem fell (July 586 BC). The delay is
explained by the distance: Babylon is over 900 miles from
Jerusalem. The night before the fugitive arrives, God opens
Ezekiel's mouth (v.22), ending the enforced silence of chapter
24:27. The prophet who was mute can now speak freely.

The chapter's final section (vv.30-33) is both humorous and
tragic: the people flock to hear Ezekiel — but as entertainment.
They treat his prophecies like "a very lovely song of one that
hath a pleasant voice" (v.32). They listen with pleasure but
don't obey. They are prophetic groupies — fans who follow but
never change.

Structure:
- The Watchman Recommissioned (vv.1-9)
- Individual Responsibility Reaffirmed (vv.10-20)
- The Fugitive Arrives: "The City Is Smitten!" (vv.21-22)
- Those Left in Judah's Ruins: False Confidence (vv.23-29)
- The Entertaining Prophet: Hearers Who Don't Obey (vv.30-33)

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.6  — "But if the watchman see the sword come, and blow not the
        trumpet, and the people be not warned; if the sword come,
        and take any person from among them... his blood will I
        require at the watchman's hand" (KJV) — The watchman's
        burden: if you see danger and stay silent, you're guilty
        of their death. Warning is not optional — it's a life-
        or-death responsibility.

v.11 — "Say unto them, As I live, saith the Lord GOD, I have no
        pleasure in the death of the wicked; but that the wicked
        turn from his way and live: turn ye, turn ye from your
        evil ways; for why will ye die, O house of Israel?" (KJV)
        — The heart of God revealed. He does NOT delight in
        judgment. He PLEADS: "Turn! Turn! WHY will you die?" The
        repetition ("turn ye, turn ye") is passionate urgency.

v.21 — "And it came to pass in the twelfth year of our captivity,
        in the tenth month, in the fifth day of the month, that
        one that had escaped out of Jerusalem came unto me, saying,
        The city is smitten" (KJV) — THE moment. Everything in
        chapters 1-24 pointed to this. The fugitive arrives.
        Five words that change everything: "The city is smitten."
        Ezekiel's warnings are vindicated.

v.22 — "Now the hand of the LORD was upon me in the evening,
        afore he that was escaped came; and had opened my mouth,
        until he came to me in the morning: and my mouth was
        opened, and I was no more dumb" (KJV) — God opens
        Ezekiel's mouth the NIGHT BEFORE the news arrives. He's
        prepared to speak before the message comes. God always
        prepares the messenger before the moment.

v.32 — "And, lo, thou art unto them as a very lovely song of one
        that hath a pleasant voice, and can play well on an
        instrument: for they hear thy words, but they do them not"
        (KJV) — The prophet as entertainer. They love his
        preaching but won't obey it. They're fans of the
        performance, not followers of the message. The ancient
        version of treating church as a podcast you enjoy but
        never apply.

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "watchman" (KJV/ESV) — Hebrew "tsopheh" = one who looks
      out, a sentinel on a wall. The watchman's job: see danger
      before others do and sound the alarm. Applied to prophets:
      they see what's coming and must warn, regardless of
      reception.

v.11 — "turn" (KJV/ESV) — Hebrew "shuvu" = return, repent, turn
      back. The fundamental call of all prophets: TURN. Change
      direction. Repeated twice for emphasis — "shuvu shuvu" =
      "turn, turn!" — God's passionate double plea.

v.21 — "smitten" (KJV) — Hebrew "hukkethah" = has been struck/
      defeated. From "nakah" = to strike, smite, defeat. Past
      tense: it's DONE. The city is ALREADY fallen. The verdict
      has been executed.

v.32 — "a very lovely song" (KJV) — Hebrew "shir agavim" = a
      song of loves/sensual songs. The idea: they treat Ezekiel's
      prophecy like a love ballad — aesthetically pleasing,
      emotionally moving, but not requiring obedience. Entertainment
      religion.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
The Watchman — The prophet's role as sentinel: seeing danger
              before others and sounding the alarm. The watchman
              is accountable if he fails to warn — but NOT
              accountable if people hear and ignore.

Individual Responsibility — The principle that each person is
              judged on THEIR OWN repentance or rebellion, not
              their parents' faith or their own past record.
              Today's turn determines today's verdict.

"The City Is Smitten" — The fugitive's five-word report that
              changes everything. Confirms Ezekiel's prophecies,
              ends his silence, and opens the door to restoration
              oracles.

The Entertaining Prophet — The tragic phenomenon of people who
              enjoy prophetic preaching as performance without
              letting it change their behavior. Spiritual
              consumers rather than obedient disciples.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. The watchman's blood-guilt (v.6). If you see someone heading
   toward destruction and stay silent — out of fear, politeness,
   or indifference — their blood is on YOUR hands. Faithful
   warning is not judgmental; it's life-saving. Speak up.

2. "WHY will ye die?" (v.11). God is genuinely baffled by human
   stubbornness. Life is offered freely; death is chosen
   unnecessarily. If you're choosing a destructive path right
   now — WHY? God is asking you directly. Turn.

3. God prepares you BEFORE the moment arrives (v.22). Ezekiel's
   mouth was opened the night BEFORE the fugitive came. God
   equips you in advance for what He knows is coming. If He's
   preparing you for something now — the moment is about to
   arrive.

4. Don't be an entertained non-doer (v.32). Do you consume
   sermons, podcasts, and study notes like entertainment? Do you
   enjoy the performance of spiritual content without obeying
   it? "Lovely song" Christianity is not Christianity at all.
   Be a DOER (James 1:22).

5. Past righteousness doesn't cover present sin (v.13). You
   cannot coast on yesterday's obedience. The question is always:
   what are you doing TODAY? Your spiritual resume doesn't save
   you — your present faith does.
