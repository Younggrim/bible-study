Ezekiel - Chapter 29
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
The oracles against Egypt begin. Pharaoh is depicted as a great dragon (crocodile/sea monster) lying in the Nile, boasting "My river is mine own, and I have made it for myself." God promises to put hooks in his jaws, drag him from the waters, and scatter Egypt for forty years. Egypt will never again rule over nations — reduced to "the basest of kingdoms." The chapter ends with God giving Egypt to Nebuchadnezzar as wages for his service against Tyre.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Ezekiel son of Buzi (see Chapter 1 notes for full details).

Classification: Oracle Against the Nations (Egypt Series: 1 of 7)
Key Themes: Pharaoh as the great dragon, "My river is mine own,"
the dragon dragged from the water, Egypt scattered forty years,
the basest of kingdoms, Nebuchadnezzar's wages, God's sovereignty
over empires

Historical Context: Chapters 29-32 contain seven oracles against
Egypt — more than any other nation receives in Ezekiel. This
reflects Egypt's special significance: she was the false hope
that Judah trusted instead of God. Every time Judah faced
Babylonian pressure, she looked south to Egypt for military
help (cf. Isaiah 30:1-3; 31:1; Jeremiah 37:5-10). Egypt
repeatedly promised aid and repeatedly failed to deliver.

The first oracle (29:1-16) is dated to January 587 BC — during
the siege of Jerusalem, when Egypt's Pharaoh Hophra (Apries)
had actually marched north, briefly lifting the siege, before
retreating (Jeremiah 37:5-11). This apparent rescue was a cruel
mirage: Egypt came, then left, and Babylon returned stronger.

Pharaoh is depicted as a "tannin" — a great sea monster/dragon/
crocodile in the Nile. This imagery draws on both the literal
crocodile of the Nile and the mythological chaos-monster that
God subdues (cf. Isaiah 51:9; Psalm 74:13). Pharaoh claims to
be master of the Nile — "I have made it" — which is creation
language. He claims to be the creator of Egypt's prosperity.
God responds by treating him like a caught fish: hooks in the
jaws, dragged from the water, left in the desert to rot.

Structure:
- The Great Dragon in the Nile (vv.1-5)
- Egypt: A Staff of Reed to Israel (vv.6-7)
- Judgment: The Sword and Forty Years of Desolation (vv.8-12)
- Restoration as the Basest Kingdom (vv.13-16)
- Nebuchadnezzar Receives Egypt as Wages (vv.17-21)

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.3  — "Speak, and say, Thus saith the Lord GOD; Behold, I am
        against thee, Pharaoh king of Egypt, the great dragon
        that lieth in the midst of his rivers, which hath said,
        My river is mine own, and I have made it for myself"
        (KJV) — Pharaoh's claim: "I MADE the Nile." This is
        not economics — it's theology. He claims to be the
        creator. The dragon in the river is a self-made god.

v.4  — "But I will put hooks in thy jaws, and I will cause the
        fish of thy rivers to stick unto thy scales, and I will
        bring thee up out of the midst of thy rivers" (KJV) —
        God as FISHERMAN. The great dragon caught on a hook,
        dragged from the river with lesser fish stuck to his
        scales. The terrifying monster is merely a catch.

v.6  — "And all the inhabitants of Egypt shall know that I am the
        LORD, because they have been a staff of reed to the house
        of Israel" (KJV) — Egypt's crime against Israel: being a
        REED staff — something that looks supportive but snaps
        under pressure, driving splinters into the hand that
        leans on it. False hope that wounds.

v.15 — "It shall be the basest of the kingdoms; neither shall it
        exalt itself any more above the nations: for I will
        diminish them, that they shall no more rule over the
        nations" (KJV) — Egypt's permanent reduction: once the
        greatest empire, now "the basest of kingdoms." Never
        again a world power. Historically fulfilled — Egypt has
        never again been an independent imperial power.

v.20 — "I have given him the land of Egypt for his labour
        wherewith he served against it, because they wrought for
        me, saith the Lord GOD" (KJV) — Nebuchadnezzar as God's
        laborer. He besieged Tyre for 13 years (for GOD's
        purposes) and gained no plunder. So God pays him with
        Egypt instead. God employs even pagan kings and
        compensates them.

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.3  — "dragon" (KJV) — Hebrew "tannin" (or "tannim") = a great
      sea creature, crocodile, or mythological sea monster. In
      context, primarily the Nile crocodile — Pharaoh sprawled in
      his waters. But the word carries echoes of the primordial
      chaos monster God defeats (cf. Isaiah 27:1; 51:9).

v.4  — "hooks in thy jaws" (KJV/ESV) — Hebrew "chachim
      bil'chayekha" = hooks/rings in your jaw-cheeks. Ancient
      Egyptians actually used hooks to catch crocodiles. God uses
      the method Egyptians knew well — on their own king.

v.6  — "staff of reed" (KJV) — Hebrew "mish'enet qaneh" = a
      support staff made of reed/papyrus. Looks solid but is
      hollow; when leaned upon, it snaps and the sharp broken end
      pierces the hand. A perfect image of false allies.

v.14 — "Pathros" (KJV/ESV) — Hebrew "Patros" = Upper Egypt
      (southern Egypt). Pharaoh's ancestral homeland. The
      prophecy promises return TO Egypt — but as a reduced,
      permanently diminished state.

v.3 — ASV: uses "Jehovah" for KJV's "LORD" (Hebrew YHWH).
       NET: "Tell them, ‘This is what" for KJV's "Speak, and say, Thus saith"
       WEB: uses "Yahweh" for KJV's "LORD" (Hebrew YHWH).
v.4 — ASV: "And I will put hooks in thy jaws, and I will cause the fish of thy rivers to sti..."
       NET: "your waterways will" for KJV's "thy rivers shall"
       WEB: "your scales." for KJV's "stick unto thy scales, and"
v.6 — ASV: uses "Jehovah" for KJV's "LORD" (Hebrew YHWH).
       NET: "those living in" for KJV's "the inhabitants of"

v.14 — NET: "an insignificant" for KJV's "there a base"
       WEB: "lowly kingdom, there." for KJV's "base kingdom."

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Tannin (The Great Dragon) — The Hebrew sea monster/crocodile.
              Applied to Pharaoh as the terrifying creature
              lurking in the Nile. Carries both literal (crocodile)
              and mythological (chaos monster) connotations.

Staff of Reed — A walking staff made from river reed that looks
              supportive but is hollow and brittle. When weight is
              applied, it snaps and pierces the hand. Egypt's
              metaphor as a false ally who wounds those who
              lean on her.

"Basest of Kingdoms" — Egypt's permanent status after judgment:
              she will exist but never again dominate. Reduced
              from world empire to minor regional state. A
              historical prophecy remarkably fulfilled.

Wages for Service — God's payment to Nebuchadnezzar: Egypt as
              compensation for his 13-year siege of Tyre that
              yielded no plunder. God employs pagan kings and
              pays them on His terms.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "My river is mine own, and I have made it" (v.3). The lie of
   self-creation. Everything you have — talent, resources,
   opportunity, even the breath in your lungs — belongs to God.
   When you say "I built this," you're playing Pharaoh. The
   river is not yours.

2. False support wounds you (v.6). The "reed staff" — whatever
   you lean on instead of God — will not just fail to support
   you; it will INJURE you when it breaks. False security is
   worse than no security because it stabs you when it fails.

3. God hooks dragons (v.4). Whatever terrifies you — whatever
   seems too powerful, too entrenched, too monstrous to defeat —
   is merely a fish to God. He has hooks for every dragon. Don't
   be intimidated by crocodiles when you serve the Fisherman.

4. "The basest of kingdoms" (v.15). Permanent reduction is a real
   consequence. Egypt never recovered imperial status. Some
   losses from pride are PERMANENT. Don't assume God will always
   restore everything to its former glory. Humility NOW prevents
   permanent diminishment LATER.

5. God pays His workers (v.20). Even Nebuchadnezzar — an
   unwitting servant — receives compensation. How much more will
   God reward those who serve Him KNOWINGLY and willingly?
   Nothing done for God is unrewarded (1 Corinthians 15:58).
