Ezekiel - Chapter 28
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
The oracle against the king of Tyre — one of the most theologically significant passages in Ezekiel. The chapter begins by addressing the human "prince of Tyre" who says "I am a God" in his pride (vv.1-10), then shifts to a lament over the "king of Tyre" with language that transcends any human ruler: "Thou hast been in Eden the garden of God," "thou art the anointed cherub that covereth," "thou wast perfect in thy ways from the day that thou wast created, till iniquity was found in thee" (vv.11-19). Many theologians see here a description of Satan's original glory and fall. The chapter concludes with a brief oracle against Sidon and a promise of Israel's restoration.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Ezekiel son of Buzi (see Chapter 1 notes for full details).

Classification: Prophetic Oracle / Lament / Satan Typology
Key Themes: The pride of the prince of Tyre, "I am a God,"
the king of Tyre in Eden, the anointed cherub, perfection
corrupted by pride, beauty becoming the cause of the fall,
cast from the mountain of God, Satan typology, judgment on
Sidon, Israel's restoration promise

Historical Context: The chapter addresses two figures: the
"prince" (nagid — v.2) and the "king" (melek — v.12) of Tyre.
The first is clearly the human ruler — likely Ethbaal III (also
called Ithobaal), who reigned during the Babylonian siege. He
is condemned for claiming deity: "I am a God, I sit in the seat
of God" (v.2). Ancient Near Eastern kings often claimed divine
status; Ethbaal took this to an extreme.

The second oracle (vv.12-19) shifts dramatically. The language
goes beyond any human king: "Thou hast been in Eden," "every
precious stone was thy covering," "thou art the anointed cherub
that covereth," "thou wast upon the holy mountain of God,"
"perfect... till iniquity was found in thee." Many interpreters
(church fathers, reformers, and modern scholars) see this as
using the king of Tyre as a TYPE of Satan — the original
guardian cherub whose beauty and perfection became the source
of his pride, leading to his expulsion from God's mountain.

Whether read as a literal description of Satan's fall or as
hyperbolic language about Tyre's king, the theological point is
identical: beauty and privilege, when they produce pride instead
of worship, lead to catastrophic fall.

Structure:
- The Prince of Tyre: "I Am a God" (vv.1-10)
- The King of Tyre: The Anointed Cherub Falls (vv.11-19)
- Oracle Against Sidon (vv.20-23)
- Promise of Israel's Restoration (vv.24-26)

Charles Spurgeon:
  "Here is the biography of the devil before he was the devil.
  A cherub — anointed, covering, perfect, beautiful, wise,
  walking in fire and glory on God's holy mountain. And pride
  was born in that breast before any serpent slithered. The
  highest angel fell the furthest because the brightest light
  casts the darkest shadow when it turns from the Source."

REFLECTION
------------------------------------
1. "I am a God" (v.2). Every human being is tempted to self-
   deification. It doesn't require literal words — it's the
   attitude of self-sufficiency, self-worship, self-determination
   apart from God. You are a MAN (or woman). Not God. Live
   accordingly.

2. Beauty became the cause of the fall (v.17). Your greatest
   GIFTS are your greatest DANGER. Intelligence, talent, beauty,
   influence — all can become mirrors that replace the face of
   God. The more gifted you are, the more vigilant you must be
   against self-admiration.

3. "Perfect... TILL" (v.15). Created perfection does not
   guarantee permanent faithfulness. Angels fell. Adam fell. You
   can fall. Don't trust your track record — trust God's
   sustaining grace daily.

4. The highest can fall the furthest. The anointed cherub had the
   most intimate access to God — and fell the most catastrophically.
   Proximity to God without humility is the most dangerous
   position in the universe. Spiritual privilege requires
   corresponding vigilance.

5. God will restore His people (vv.25-26). After all the judgment
   oracles — against Tyre, against Sidon, against the world's
   pride — God promises to bring Israel back, plant them safely,
   and vindicate His holiness. Judgment is not God's last word.
   Restoration is.
