Ezekiel - Chapter 35
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
An oracle against Mount Seir — the territory of Edom. God declares judgment on Edom because of her perpetual hatred against Israel and her rejoicing when the two kingdoms fell. Edom said "These two nations shall be mine" — attempting to inherit the land God promised to Israel. Because Edom gloated "They are laid desolate, they are given us to consume," God will make Edom herself a perpetual desolation. The mountain that celebrated Israel's emptiness will itself become permanently empty.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Ezekiel son of Buzi (see Chapter 1 notes for full details).

Classification: Oracle Against the Nations / Covenant Lawsuit
Key Themes: Edom's perpetual hatred, gloating over Israel's fall,
attempting to seize God's land, "These two nations shall be mine,"
blood-guilt, speaking against the mountains of Israel, God's
jealousy for His land, perpetual desolation for perpetual hatred

Historical Context: This chapter seems oddly placed — the oracles
against the nations ended at chapter 32. But chapter 35 is
strategically positioned as a CONTRAST to chapter 36 (the
restoration of Israel's mountains). The structure is deliberate:
Mount Seir judged (ch.35) / Mountains of Israel restored (ch.36).
Edom DOWN, Israel UP. The contrast makes the restoration
promises brighter.

Edom (descendants of Esau, Jacob's twin brother — Genesis 25:30;
36:1) occupied the mountainous region south of the Dead Sea
(Mount Seir). The hatred between Edom and Israel was ancient,
going back to the twins' rivalry in the womb (Genesis 25:22-23).
But Edom's specific crimes here are: (1) perpetual hatred
(v.5), (2) actively shedding Israelite blood during Jerusalem's
fall (v.5), (3) coveting the land of both kingdoms for herself
(v.10), and (4) speaking blasphemously against "the mountains
of Israel" — which are God's mountains (v.12).

Edom's attempt to seize Israel's land was a direct challenge to
God's covenant promises. By saying "These two nations... shall
be mine" (v.10), Edom was claiming what God had given to Abraham,
Isaac, and Jacob. This is not merely political ambition — it's
theological rebellion: seizing what God pledged to another.

Historically, Edom was progressively displaced by Nabatean Arabs
from the 6th century BC onward, completely losing their
territory and eventually disappearing as a distinct people.

Structure:
- God Against Mount Seir: The Sentence (vv.1-4)
- The Charge: Perpetual Hatred and Blood (vv.5-9)
- The Claim: "These Two Nations Shall Be Mine" (vv.10-13)
- The Verdict: Edom's Desolation Is Israel's Joy (vv.14-15)

Charles Spurgeon:
  "'Whereas the LORD was there.' What a sentence! Edom looked at
  the smoking ruins of Jerusalem and saw vacancy. God looked at
  the same ruins and saw His own presence. The land was not
  abandoned — it was temporarily disciplined. And the Brother who
  tried to steal what God had not given found that God's absence
  from the temple did not mean God's absence from the land. HE
  WAS THERE."

REFLECTION
------------------------------------
1. "Perpetual hatred" destroys (v.5). Unforgiveness held across
   years becomes a defining characteristic — and eventually a
   death sentence. What ancient grudge are you carrying? It will
   consume YOU long before it harms them. Release it before it
   becomes your identity.

2. "Whereas the LORD was there" (v.10). When you look at a
   difficult situation and see only emptiness — God is THERE. The
   church may look weak, the land may look desolate, your life
   may feel empty — but God has not vacated. He is present in
   what others write off.

3. Don't rejoice over others' discipline (v.15). When God
   corrects someone else — even if they deserve it — your job is
   NOT to gloat. Celebration of another's suffering invites the
   same suffering to your door. Weep with those who weep
   (Romans 12:15), even when their tears are deserved.

4. God hears your words ABOUT His people (v.13). Every mocking
   comment about the church, every gleeful observation about a
   believer's failure, every "Aha!" over God's people — He
   hears. You're not speaking about them; you're speaking
   against HIM.

5. Trying to seize what God gave to another will fail (v.10).
   Coveting someone else's calling, position, blessing, or
   inheritance — it won't work. God's gifts are assigned. What
   He gave to another is not available for your hostile takeover.
   Find your OWN calling instead.
