Ezekiel - Chapter 17
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
The parable of two eagles and a vine: the great eagle (Babylon/Nebuchadnezzar) takes the top of a cedar (Jehoiachin) to Babylon and plants a seed (Zedekiah) which grows as a low vine. But the vine turns toward ANOTHER eagle (Egypt/Pharaoh) for water. God asks: will it prosper? NO — it will be plucked up. Yet God Himself will plant a tender twig on a high mountain that becomes a noble cedar — Messianic hope.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Ezekiel son of Buzi (see Chapter 1 notes for full details).

Classification: Prophetic Parable/Riddle + Messianic Promise
Key Themes: Political allegory (Babylon, Zedekiah, Egypt), covenant-breaking punished, the futility of trusting Egypt against Babylon, God's own planting of the Messiah

Historical Context: The "riddle" (v.2) tells Judah's recent history in code: First eagle (Nebuchadnezzar) took the cedar-top (Jehoiachin, 597 BC) and planted a seed (installed Zedekiah as vassal). Zedekiah swore a covenant with Babylon — then broke it by reaching toward the second eagle (Pharaoh Hophra of Egypt) for military help. God's verdict: covenant-breaking is punished — even a covenant made with a PAGAN king. Zedekiah swore by GOD (v.19: "mine oath... my covenant") and broke it. The chapter ends with GOD planting His own "tender twig" (Messiah) on a high mountain — the true and lasting Davidic king that human politics couldn't produce.

Structure:
- The Riddle: Two Eagles and a Vine (vv.1-10)
- The Interpretation: Babylon, Zedekiah, Egypt (vv.11-21)
- God's Own Planting: The Tender Twig (vv.22-24)

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.15 — "But he rebelled against him in sending his ambassadors into Egypt... Shall he prosper? shall he escape that doeth such things? or shall he break the covenant, and be delivered?" (KJV) — Rhetorical questions: can a covenant-BREAKER escape? NO. Even breaking a treaty with a pagan is punished when God's name was invoked.

v.19 — "Mine oath that he hath despised, and my covenant that he hath broken, even it will I recompense upon his own head" (KJV) — God calls Zedekiah's oath to Babylon "MINE oath" and "MY covenant." Because it was sworn in God's name, breaking it offends GOD — not just Nebuchadnezzar.

v.22 — "I will also take of the highest branch of the high cedar, and will set it; I will crop off from the top of his young twigs a tender one, and will plant it upon an high mountain" (KJV) — GOD'S planting: the Messiah. Taken from David's line ("highest branch"), tender (humble beginnings), planted on a high mountain (Zion).

v.24 — "I the LORD have brought down the high tree, have exalted the low tree, have dried up the green tree, and have made the dry tree to flourish" (KJV) — God's sovereign reversal: high brought low, low exalted. The proud humbled, the humble raised. The summary of kingdom-logic.

v.3 — "great eagle" (KJV/ESV) — Nebuchadnezzar: great wings, long pinions, full plumage, many colors = vast empire, military reach, splendor.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Two Eagles — Babylon (first eagle, dominant) and Egypt (second eagle, secondary). Judah's vine turns from one to the other — neither of which is GOD.

Broken Covenant — Zedekiah's oath of loyalty to Babylon, sworn in God's name, then broken by seeking Egyptian alliance. God holds oath-breakers accountable regardless of who the oath was made to.

Tender Twig — The Messiah: planted by GOD (not human politics), from David's line, humble in origin but growing into a great cedar. Christ as God's alternative to political failure.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Mine oath that he hath despised" (v.19). God takes your promises seriously — even ones made to unbelievers. If you swore in God's name, God holds you accountable for the oath. Keep your word.

2. The vine turned to the wrong eagle (v.7). When your "water source" (sustenance/protection) shifts from God to human powers, you're repeating Zedekiah's fatal error. Where are you looking for security?

3. GOD plants the Messiah (v.22). After all human political machinations fail — God acts. The solution to the world's problems doesn't come from better politics but from God's own planting.

4. "I bring down the high tree, exalt the low" (v.24). God's kingdom operates by REVERSAL. If you're currently "low" — take hope. If you're currently "high" — take warning. God inverts human hierarchies.
