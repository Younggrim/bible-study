Ezekiel - Chapter 32
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
The final two oracles against Egypt. First, a funeral lament over Pharaoh — the dragon caught in God's net, his blood poured on the mountains, the sun darkened at his fall (vv.1-16). Second, the most haunting passage in Ezekiel: a guided tour of Sheol where Pharaoh descends and finds the fallen empires already there — Assyria, Elam, Meshech-Tubal, Edom, Sidon — all lying uncircumcised among the slain. Egypt joins them in the pit, and the chapter closes the entire "oracles against nations" section with a vision of death.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Ezekiel son of Buzi (see Chapter 1 notes for full details).

Classification: Oracle Against the Nations (Egypt Series: 6 & 7)
Key Themes: Pharaoh's funeral lament, the dragon caught in a
net, cosmic darkness at Egypt's fall, descent to Sheol, the
catalogue of dead empires, lying with the uncircumcised, the
pit as equalizer, the terror of the nations silenced

Historical Context: Two oracles, both dated. The first (vv.1-16)
is from March 585 BC — about a year after Jerusalem's fall. The
second (vv.17-32) is from April 586 BC (or 585 BC, depending on
textual variants). Together they form the capstone of the seven
Egypt oracles (chs. 29-32).

The first oracle (vv.1-16) reprises the dragon imagery from
chapter 29: Pharaoh as the sea monster caught in God's net. But
now the language becomes cosmic — stars darkened, sun covered
with cloud, the land made dark (vv.7-8). This cosmic imagery
suggests that Pharaoh's fall has supernatural dimensions: the
spiritual powers behind Egypt are being dismantled.

The second oracle (vv.17-32) is one of the most extraordinary
passages in Old Testament literature: a descent into Sheol (the
underworld) where all the great empires of history lie dead
together. Assyria is there. Elam is there. Meshech and Tubal are
there. Edom and the Sidonians — all there. Each once caused
"terror in the land of the living" and now lies among "the
uncircumcised" — the ultimate shame for ancient Near Eastern
warriors. Pharaoh descends and is "comforted" by the company of
other dead empires. Cold comfort indeed.

Structure:
- Lament for Pharaoh: The Dragon Caught (vv.1-10)
- The Sword of Babylon (vv.11-16)
- The Descent to Sheol: A Tour of Dead Empires (vv.17-21)
- Assyria in the Pit (vv.22-23)
- Elam in the Pit (vv.24-25)
- Meshech-Tubal in the Pit (vv.26-28)
- Edom in the Pit (vv.29)
- Sidon in the Pit (vv.30)
- Pharaoh Joins Them: "Comforted" in Death (vv.31-32)

Charles Spurgeon:
  "Sheol is the great democratic graveyard of empires. Here lies
  Assyria, who terrified the world. Here lies Elam, who shook
  the nations. Here lies Meshech, here Edom, here Sidon — all
  neighbors now in death who were rivals in life. And Pharaoh
  arrives last and is 'comforted' by their company. What comfort!
  The comfort of the damned: 'At least we are not alone.'"

REFLECTION
------------------------------------
1. Cosmic shaking at God's judgment (vv.7-8). When God acts, the
   entire created order responds. Stars darken. The sun hides.
   If NATURE trembles at God's wrath, how much more should you?
   Don't take divine judgment lightly.

2. The pit is the great equalizer (vv.22-30). Assyria, Elam,
   Meshech, Edom — all equal in death. Your power, your status,
   your resume — irrelevant in Sheol. Only one thing matters: do
   you know the One who conquered the grave?

3. "Comforted" by the company of the dead (v.31). What terrible
   comfort — knowing others share your fate. Don't let "everyone
   does it" comfort you in sin. The broad road leads to
   destruction, and having company doesn't make the destination
   better (Matthew 7:13).

4. The terror of the nations is SILENCED (v.32). Every empire
   that terrified others is eventually silenced. Whatever
   terrifies you today — whatever seems unstoppable — it has a
   grave waiting. Only God's kingdom has no end.

5. God's lament accompanies the descent (v.18). Even as God
   judges, He commands a WAIL — mourning. Judgment is never
   joyful for God. He takes no pleasure in the death of the
   wicked (Ezekiel 33:11). His heart breaks even as His hand
   strikes.
