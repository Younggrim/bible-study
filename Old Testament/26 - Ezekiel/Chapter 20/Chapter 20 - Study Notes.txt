Ezekiel - Chapter 20
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
The elders of Israel come to inquire of the LORD, but God refuses to be consulted by them. Instead, He rehearses the entire history of Israel's rebellions — in Egypt, in the wilderness, and in the Promised Land — showing a pattern of idolatry at every stage. Yet at each point of deserved judgment, God stayed His hand "for my name's sake." The chapter ends with God's promise to purge the rebels and restore a purified remnant through a second exodus.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Ezekiel son of Buzi (see Chapter 1 notes for full details).

Classification: Prophetic Historical Review / Covenant Lawsuit
Key Themes: Israel's repeated rebellions, God's restraint "for
my name's sake," idolatry in every generation, the purging of
rebels, a second exodus, God's holiness vindicated

Historical Context: Dated to the seventh year of Jehoiachin's
exile (August 591 BC — v.1). The elders of the exile community
come to Ezekiel to "inquire of the LORD" — likely wanting to
know when they'll return home or whether an alliance with Egypt
might deliver them. God's response is devastating: "I will not
be inquired of by you" (v.3). Instead of answering their
question, He puts THEM on trial.

What follows is a comprehensive review of Israel's history of
rebellion — not the triumphant version (as in Psalm 78 or
Nehemiah 9) but the ugly version. God shows that Israel was
idolatrous EVEN IN EGYPT before the exodus (v.8), that they
rebelled in the wilderness TWICE (vv.13, 21), and that every
generation repeated the same pattern. Yet God restrained His
wrath each time "for my name's sake, that it should not be
polluted before the heathen" (vv.9, 14, 22).

The chapter climaxes with God's promise of a future purging: He
will bring Israel through a second wilderness experience, sort
the rebels from the faithful (vv.34-38), and ultimately restore
pure worship on His holy mountain (vv.40-44).

Structure:
- The Elders Come; God Refuses to Answer (vv.1-4)
- Rebellion in Egypt (vv.5-9)
- First Wilderness Rebellion (vv.10-17)
- Second Wilderness Rebellion (vv.18-26)
- Rebellion in the Land (vv.27-29)
- Present Generation Repeats the Pattern (vv.30-32)
- The Second Exodus and Purging (vv.33-38)
- Future Restoration on God's Holy Mountain (vv.39-44)
- The Fire from the South (vv.45-49)

REFLECTION
------------------------------------
1. God sometimes refuses to answer (v.3). Not every prayer gets
   a response. Persistent, unrepentant rebellion can silence
   heaven. If God seems silent, check whether you're living in a
   way that makes inquiry hypocritical.

2. "For my name's sake" (v.9). God's faithfulness depends on HIS
   character, not yours. Your survival as a believer is not based
   on your performance but on God's investment of His name in you.
   This is grace — scandalous, undeserved, and entirely about HIM.

3. The danger of "blending in" (v.32). Israel wanted to be "like
   the nations." God says: that will NEVER happen. He refuses to
   release His people into comfortable paganism. If you belong to
   Him, He will not let you assimilate — even if the road back
   involves painful discipline.

4. God purges before He restores (vv.37-38). The second exodus
   includes a wilderness — a sorting process. Not everyone who
   leaves bondage enters the promise. Are you willing to be
   purged, or are you a rebel who will "not enter the land"?

5. History repeats when lessons go unlearned (vv.18-21). The
   children repeat their parents' sins. Break the cycle. What
   generational patterns of disobedience are you perpetuating?
