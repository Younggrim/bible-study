Nehemiah - Chapter 6
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Sanballat and Tobiah try four times to lure Nehemiah into a meeting to harm him, then attempt to intimidate him through a false prophet. Nehemiah discerns the schemes and refuses to stop, and the wall is completed in just fifty-two days, astonishing the surrounding nations.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Nehemiah, with possible editorial additions by Ezra. Written
approximately 445-400 BC. Nehemiah covers the rebuilding of
Jerusalem's walls (445 BC) and the spiritual renewal of the
community. Key themes: prayer and action together, opposition
overcome by faith, the importance of God's Word (chapter 8),
covenant renewal, and the principle that spiritual rebuilding must
accompany physical rebuilding.

Historical Context: Chapter 6 records the final attempts to stop
the wall before its completion — and its triumphant finish in just
52 days. With the physical wall nearly complete (only the gates
needed doors), the enemies shifted from military threats to
sophisticated personal attacks against Nehemiah himself. Three
schemes are recorded: (1) Four invitations to "meet" at the plain
of Ono — actually assassination attempts (vv.1-4); (2) An open
letter accusing Nehemiah of rebellion and aspiring to kingship —
political blackmail (vv.5-9); (3) A hired false prophet who urged
Nehemiah to hide in the Temple — designed to make him sin and lose
credibility (vv.10-14). Nehemiah saw through each scheme with
remarkable discernment. His response to the meeting invitations
became one of Scripture's great leadership statements: "I am doing
a great work, so that I cannot come down" (v.3). The wall was
completed on Elul 25 (approximately September/October 445 BC) in
52 days — an astonishing feat that even the enemies recognized as
divine: "they perceived that this work was wrought of our God"
(v.16). The chapter closes with the disturbing reality that Tobiah
had extensive family connections among the Jewish aristocracy
(vv.17-19), creating an espionage network within the community
itself. External enemies often have internal allies.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Susa (Shushan) — the Persian winter capital in modern Iran, where Nehemiah serves as cupbearer to King Artaxerxes.
- Jerusalem — the city whose walls Nehemiah rebuilds. Chapter 3 names specific gates (Sheep Gate, Fish Gate, Valley Gate, Dung Gate, etc.) tracing the wall circuit.
- The surrounding enemies occupy identifiable regions: Sanballat (Samaria/north), Tobiah (Ammon/east), Geshem (Arabia/south).

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.3  — "I am doing a great work and I cannot come down. Why should
        the work stop while I leave it and come down to you?"
        (ESV) — Nehemiah's classic response to distraction
        disguised as opportunity. He refused to be lured from his
        God-given assignment by any invitation, however reasonable
        it appeared. Focus requires saying no to things that seem
        good but would divert from what is great.

v.9  — "But now, O God, strengthen my hands." (ESV) —
        Nehemiah's brief prayer amid intimidation. He acknowledged
        the enemy's intention ("they made us afraid") and
        immediately turned to God for strengthening. Short prayers
        in crisis moments reveal a soul habitually turned toward
        God.

v.11 — "Should such a man as I run away? And what man such as I
        could go into the temple and live? I will not go in."
        (ESV) — A masterful response to the false
        prophet. Two arguments: (1) A leader cannot flee — his
        position requires courage. (2) A non-priest entering the
        inner Temple would sin — the "spiritual" advice was
        actually a trap to violate God's law. Nehemiah discerned
        that true prophets never counsel disobedience to Scripture.

v.15 — "So the wall was finished on the twenty-fifth day of the
        month Elul, in fifty-two days." (ESV) — Fifty-two
        days. An engineering miracle that enemies attributed to
        divine aid (v.16). What seemed impossible was accomplished
        through united effort, faithful leadership, prayerful
        dependence, and divine empowerment. The speed itself was
        a testimony.

v.16 — "They perceived that this work had been accomplished with
        the help of our God." (ESV) — Even enemies recognized the supernatural element.
        When God's people accomplish the impossible through faith
        and obedience, it becomes self-evidently divine. The wall
        was its own apologetic — no human explanation sufficed.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.3  — Hebrews 12:1-2 — "Let us run with endurance the race that
        is set before us, looking to Jesus." (ESV) Nehemiah's
        refusal to be distracted from his "great work" parallels
        the Christian call to focused perseverance, refusing the
        entanglements that would pull us off course.

v.11 — Numbers 18:7 — "And if any outsider comes near, he shall
        be put to death." (ESV) Only priests could enter the
        Temple's inner rooms. Nehemiah, a layman, would have sinned
        by taking refuge there. False counsel that violates
        Scripture — no matter how spiritual it sounds — is not from
        God.

v.14 — Jeremiah 23:21-22 — "I did not send the prophets, yet they
        ran." (ESV) False prophets are not a new problem. Nehemiah
        discerned that Shemaiah and Noadiah were hired opponents,
        not divine messengers. Discernment requires knowing
        Scripture well enough to recognize when "prophecy"
        contradicts God's word.

v.15 — Psalm 127:1 — "Unless the LORD builds the house, those who
        build it labor in vain." (ESV) The 52-day completion
        demonstrated that God was building — human effort was real
        but divine enablement was the ultimate cause.

v.16 — Joshua 2:9-11 — Rahab testified that the nations feared
        Israel because they perceived God's work. The same pattern
        here — enemies' fear confirmed divine involvement. When
        God acts, even opponents recognize it.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/nehemiah-6/
  Guzik highlights Nehemiah's discernment in three tests: he
  recognized assassination disguised as diplomacy, political
  blackmail designed to create fear, and false prophecy designed
  to produce sin. Each attack required different discernment but
  the same principle: stay focused on God's assignment.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "'I am doing a great work, and I cannot come down.' Here is the
  answer to every distraction, every secondary invitation, every
  clever attempt to pull you from your calling. The devil rarely
  tempts busy workers with obvious evil — he invites them to
  conferences, to meetings, to things that seem reasonable but
  would remove them from their post. Nehemiah saw through it:
  'Why should the work cease?' Ask that question of every
  invitation that would pull you from God's assigned task. And when
  a prophet counseled him to flee into the Temple, Nehemiah asked
  the killer question: 'Should such a man as I flee?' Character
  and calling together produced an unshakeable refusal. The wall
  was finished in fifty-two days — and the heathen fell in their
  own eyes. When God's people finish what God started, even enemies
  confess it is divine."

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.2  — KJV: "the villages in the plain of Ono" / ESV: "Hakkephirim
       in the plain of Ono" — Hebrew "ba-kephirim be-biq'ath Ono."
       KJV translates "kephirim" as "villages"; ESV treats it as a
       place name. The plain of Ono was neutral territory between
       Judah and Samaria — a seemingly reasonable meeting place,
       but far from Nehemiah's security.

v.5  — KJV: "an open letter" / ESV: "an open letter" — Hebrew
       "iggeret petuchah" (an opened/unsealed letter). Normally
       diplomatic correspondence was sealed for privacy. An open
       letter was a deliberate insult — its contents would be read
       by everyone who handled it, publicly spreading the
       accusation of rebellion.

v.11 — KJV: "Should such a man as I flee?" / ESV: "Should such a
       man as I run away?" — Hebrew "ha-ish kamoni yivrach" (would
       a man like me flee?). Not arrogance but awareness of
       responsibility — a governor fleeing would demoralize the
       entire community. Position carries obligation to courage.

v.16 — KJV: "they were much cast down in their own eyes" / ESV:
       "they fell greatly in their own esteem" — Hebrew "yippelu
       me'od be'eineihem" (they fell very much in their own eyes).
       The enemies' self-confidence collapsed when they saw the
       completed wall. Their own estimation of themselves diminished
       because they recognized a power greater than themselves at
       work.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Plain of    — A valley northwest of Jerusalem, on the border
Ono           between Judah and Samaria. The meeting invitation to
             this neutral location seemed reasonable but would have
             removed Nehemiah from his protected position in
             Jerusalem and placed him within easy reach of
             assassination.

Open Letter — A diplomatic communication deliberately left
             unsealed so its contents could be read publicly.
             Used as a form of political pressure — the accusation
             of rebellion would spread as gossip throughout the
             region, creating pressure on Nehemiah through public
             opinion and reaching Persian authorities through
             informal channels.

False       — Shemaiah (v.10) and Noadiah (v.14) were prophets
Prophet       hired by Tobiah and Sanballat to manipulate Nehemiah
             through false spiritual counsel. Their advice seemed
             pious ("meet in the house of God") but would have
             caused Nehemiah to sin (entering forbidden Temple
             precincts) and lose credibility (appearing to flee
             in cowardice).

Fifty-two   — The total construction time for the entire wall.
Days          This extraordinary speed (mid-August to early
             October 445 BC) was possible because: (1) much of the
             existing foundation remained, (2) the entire community
             participated, (3) they worked from dawn to stars
             (4:21), and (4) God's hand empowered the work.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Know your great work and refuse to come down (v.3). What has God
   assigned to you? Identify it clearly and protect it fiercely.
   Every distraction — however reasonable — that pulls you from your
   calling is an enemy invitation to "the plain of Ono." Practice
   saying: "I am doing a great work and cannot come down."

2. Discern false spiritual counsel by testing it against Scripture
   (vv.10-13). The false prophet's advice sounded spiritual but
   contradicted God's law. True guidance never violates Scripture —
   no matter how urgent or pious it sounds. When someone gives you
   "spiritual" counsel that would require you to disobey God's
   word, recognize it as the enemy's voice, however religious the
   packaging.

3. Courage is not the absence of fear but refusal to be governed by
   it (v.11). "Should such a man as I flee?" Nehemiah was afraid
   (v.9 admits it), but he would not let fear dictate action. Your
   position, your calling, your responsibility — these demand
   courage even when you're scared. Fear is a feeling; courage is
   a choice.

4. God makes His work self-evident (v.16). The enemies "perceived
   that this work was wrought of our God." You don't always need to
   argue for God's involvement — sometimes the finished work speaks
   for itself. Focus on faithful completion; God will make the
   divine origin evident to observers.

5. Internal compromise is ongoing (vv.17-19). Even after the wall
   was done, Tobiah maintained an espionage network through family
   connections. Victory doesn't eliminate infiltration. Stay
   vigilant against internal compromise even after external
   opposition is defeated.
