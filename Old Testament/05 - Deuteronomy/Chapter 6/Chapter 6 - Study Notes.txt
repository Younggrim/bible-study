Deuteronomy - Chapter 6
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Moses commands Israel to love the Lord with all their heart, soul, and strength — the Shema — and to teach God's words diligently to their children.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses. Deuteronomy is the fifth and final book of the Pentateuch,
written at the end of the 40 years of wilderness wandering (approximately
1406 BC). Hebrew title: "Devarim" — "Words/Things." Greek title:
"Deuteronomion" — "Second Law" (a repetition/expansion of the Law for the
new generation). The book consists of Moses' farewell speeches to Israel on
the plains of Moab, just before they enter the Promised Land. Moses will
not enter with them — these are his final words to the nation he has led
for 40 years.

Historical Context: Chapter 6 contains the most important passage in
Judaism — the Shema (vv.4-5): "Hear, O Israel: The LORD our God is one
LORD: And thou shalt love the LORD thy God with all thine heart, and with
all thy soul, and with all thy might." This is the passage Jesus identified
as "the first and great commandment" (Matthew 22:37-38). The chapter flows
from the Ten Commandments (chapter 5) into their practical application:
love God totally, teach your children constantly, and do not forget God
when prosperity comes. Moses knows the greatest danger Israel will face is
not military enemies but spiritual amnesia — forgetting God in the comfort
of the Promised Land. The chapter establishes the pattern of faith
transmission: from heart (v.6) to home (v.7) to hand and eyes (v.8) to
doorposts and gates (v.9). God's word must saturate every dimension of
life. When the next generation asks "What mean these things?" the answer is
always the story of redemption (vv.20-25).

The Purpose of the Commandments (vv.1-3): These commandments are given "that
ye might do them in the land" (v.1) — they are for life in the Promised
Land. The purpose: fear God across generations — "thou, and thy son, and
thy son's son" (v.2). The result: prolonged days, well-being, and
multiplication "in the land that floweth with milk and honey" (v.3).
Obedience is not burdensome duty but the path to flourishing.

THE SHEMA (vv.4-5): "Hear, O Israel: The LORD our God is one LORD: And
thou shalt love the LORD thy God with all thine heart, and with all thy
soul, and with all thy might." This is the foundational confession of
Israel's faith. "Hear" (Shema) — not merely auditory but responsive
hearing: hear and obey. "The LORD our God is one LORD" — YHWH alone is God;
He is undivided, unique, singular in His deity. "Love the LORD thy God" —
the first and greatest command is not a prohibition but a positive demand:
love. "With all thine heart" — the center of will and thought. "With all
thy soul" — the whole life/being. "With all thy might" — with all strength
and resources. Total, undivided, wholehearted devotion.

The Word in Every Sphere of Life (vv.6-9): "These words... shall be in
thine heart" (v.6) — internalized, not merely external. "Teach them
diligently unto thy children" (v.7) — Hebrew "shinnantam" means to sharpen,
to repeat, to impress. Talk of them constantly: sitting, walking, lying
down, rising up (v.7) — every posture, every moment. "Bind them... upon
thine hand" (v.8) — they govern your actions. "Frontlets between thine
eyes" (v.8) — they govern your vision/thinking. "Write them upon the posts
of thy house, and on thy gates" (v.9) — they govern your home and your
public life. Later Judaism literalized this with phylacteries (tefillin)
and mezuzot, but the original intent is comprehensive: God's word must
permeate everything.

The Danger of Prosperity (vv.10-15): When God brings them into the land —
cities they did not build, houses they did not fill, wells they did not
dig, vineyards they did not plant (vv.10-11) — "when thou shalt have eaten
and be full" (v.11) — "then beware lest thou forget the LORD" (v.12). This
is Moses' great warning: prosperity breeds forgetfulness. Full stomachs
produce empty memories. The antidote: fear God, serve Him, swear by His
name alone (v.13). Do not follow other gods (v.14) — "for the LORD thy God
is a jealous God among you" (v.15).

Do Not Test God (v.16): "Ye shall not tempt the LORD your God, as ye
tempted him in Massah." At Massah (Exodus 17:1-7), Israel tested God by
demanding: "Is the LORD among us, or not?" Testing God means demanding
proof of His presence or faithfulness rather than trusting His word. Jesus
quoted this verse when Satan tempted Him to jump from the temple (Matthew
4:7).

Teaching the Next Generation (vv.20-25): "When thy son asketh thee in time
to come, saying, What mean the testimonies...?" (v.20). The answer is not
abstract theology but redemptive history: "We were Pharaoh's bondmen in
Egypt; and the LORD brought us out" (v.21). He showed signs and wonders
(v.22). He brought us out to bring us in (v.23). He commanded us "for our
good always" (v.24). Obedience is "our righteousness" (v.25). The faith is
transmitted through story — the story of what God has done.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Moses speaks from the plains of Moab. These commands anticipate life "in the land" — "when thou comest into the land" (v.10).

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.4  — "Hear, O Israel: The LORD our God, the LORD is one." (ESV) — The
        Shema. The foundational confession of monotheism. God is one —
        undivided, unique, alone in His deity.

v.5  — "You shall love the LORD your God with all your heart and with all
        your soul and with all your might." (ESV) — The greatest
        commandment. Total love for God — heart, soul, strength. Nothing
        held back.

v.6  — "And these words that I command you today shall be on your heart."
        (ESV) — Not merely on tablets of stone but written on the heart.
        Internal before external.

v.7  — "You shall teach them diligently to your children, and shall talk
        of them when you sit in your house, and when you walk by the way,
        and when you lie down, and when you rise." (ESV) — Constant,
        pervasive, life-integrated teaching.

v.12 — "Then take care lest you forget the LORD, who brought you out of
        the land of Egypt, out of the house of slavery." (ESV) — The
        greatest spiritual danger: forgetting God in prosperity.

v.16 — "You shall not put the LORD your God to the test, as you tested
        him at Massah." (ESV) — Do not demand proof from the God who has
        already proven Himself.

v.23 — "He brought us out from there, that he might bring us in." (ESV) —
        Redemption has a destination. God saves FROM in order to bring TO.

v.24 — "The LORD commanded us to do all these statutes... for our good
        always." (ESV) — God's commands are not arbitrary restrictions but
        provisions for human flourishing.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES

Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/deuteronomy-6/
  Guzik emphasizes that the Shema is not merely a theological statement
  but a call to action — hearing that produces loving obedience. He notes
  that the command to love God with "all" (heart, soul, might) leaves no
  compartment of life outside God's claim. He highlights the danger of
  prosperity as the most relevant warning for Western Christians.

  "Love the Lord thy God with ALL. Not with part of your heart while the
  world has the rest. Not with your soul on Sundays while your strength
  belongs to mammon on weekdays. ALL. This is the command that no man has
  ever fully kept except One — and He kept it for us. Christ loved the
  Father with all His heart, all His soul, all His might. His perfect
  obedience is credited to all who believe."

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.4  — KJV: "The LORD our God is one LORD" / ESV: "The LORD our God, the
       LORD is one" — Hebrew "YHWH Eloheinu YHWH echad." This can be
       translated multiple ways: "The LORD our God is one LORD" / "The LORD
       our God, the LORD is one" / "The LORD is our God, the LORD alone."
       "Echad" means one/unified/alone. The statement affirms both God's
       unity and His exclusivity.

v.5  — KJV: "with all thy might" / ESV: "with all your might" — Hebrew
       "b'chol m'odecha." "M'od" literally means "very/exceedingly" — with
       all your "very-ness," your utmost capacity, your everything. Some
       render it "resources" or "abundance" — love God with everything you
       have and are.

v.7  — KJV: "teach them diligently" / ESV: "teach them diligently" —
       Hebrew "v'shinnantam." From "shanan" = to sharpen, to whet. The
       image is of sharpening a blade by repeated strokes — teaching by
       constant repetition until the words are sharp and clear in the
       child's mind.

v.8  — KJV: "frontlets between thine eyes" / ESV: "frontlets between your
       eyes" — Hebrew "totafot bein einecha." "Totafot" is a rare word,
       possibly meaning "bands" or "ornaments." Later Judaism interpreted
       this literally as tefillin (phylacteries) — small boxes containing
       Scripture worn on the forehead and arm during prayer.

v.12 — KJV: "beware lest thou forget" / ESV: "take care lest you forget"
       — Hebrew "hishamer l'cha pen tishkach." "Hishamer" = guard yourself.
       "Pen" = lest. "Tishkach" = you forget. Active vigilance against
       spiritual amnesia.

v.16 — KJV: "Ye shall not tempt the LORD" / ESV: "You shall not put the
       LORD your God to the test" — Hebrew "lo t'nassu et YHWH." "Nasah" =
       to test, to try, to prove. Testing God means demanding He prove
       Himself rather than trusting His revealed word.

v.25 — KJV: "it shall be our righteousness" / ESV: "it will be
       righteousness for us" — Hebrew "ts'daqah tihyeh lanu." Obedience
       constitutes righteousness under the old covenant. Paul will later
       contrast this with righteousness by faith (Romans 10:5-6; Galatians
       3:11-12).

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Shema          — Hebrew "Hear!" — the first word of verse 4 and the name
               given to this passage (6:4-9). Recited twice daily in
               Jewish practice. The foundational confession of Israel's
               faith: God is one, and He demands total love.

Echad          — Hebrew "one." Can mean numerically one, unified, or alone/
               unique. In the Shema, it affirms God's unity (He is not
               divided), His uniqueness (there is no other), and His
               exclusivity (He alone is to be worshiped).

Frontlets/Tefillin — Phylacteries. Small leather boxes containing
               Scripture passages (Deut 6:4-9; 11:13-21; Exodus 13:1-10,
               11-16), worn on the forehead and left arm during prayer.
               A literal interpretation of v.8.

Mezuzah        — A small case containing the Shema (Deut 6:4-9; 11:13-21)
               affixed to doorposts. A literal interpretation of v.9.
               The word means "doorpost."

Massah         — "Testing/Temptation" — the place where Israel tested God
               by demanding water and questioning His presence (Exodus
               17:1-7). The name became a byword for faithless demanding
               of proof from God.

Diligently (Hebrew: "shanan") — To sharpen, to whet, to repeat. Teaching
               by constant repetition — like sharpening a blade with
               repeated strokes. Not casual mention but intentional,
               persistent instruction.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.4 — NET: "Listen, Israel: The Lord is our God, the Lord is one!" / ASV: "Hear, O Israel: Jehovah our God is one Jehovah" / WEB: "Hear, Israel: Yahweh is our God. Yahweh is one" / KJV: "Hear, O Israel: The LORD our God is one LORD" — The Shema — Israel's foundational confession. The divine name appears twice in ASV/WEB. The precise meaning is debated: "YHWH is one" (numeric unity), "YHWH alone" (exclusive devotion), or "YHWH is unique" (incomparable). NET places the exclamation mark for emphasis.

- v.4 — The word "Hear" (Hebrew "Shema") means more than passive listening — it means obey, respond, act. This single word gives the passage its name and defines Israel's covenantal posture: attentive obedience.

- v.5 — NET: "with all your heart, with all your soul, and with all your strength" / ASV: "with all thy heart, and with all thy soul, and with all thy might" / WEB: "with all your heart, with all your soul, and with all your might" — NET uses "strength" where ASV/WEB use "might" (Hebrew "me'od" — literally "muchness/exceedingly"). The totality of love: will (heart), being (soul), resources (strength/might).

- v.7 — NET: "You must teach them diligently to your children" / ASV: "thou shalt teach them diligently unto thy children" / WEB: "you shall teach them diligently to your children" — Hebrew "shinnantam" (from "shanan" — to sharpen/whet). Teaching is like sharpening a blade — repetitive, intentional, producing a keen edge. NET/ASV/WEB all capture the diligence.

- v.8 — NET: "Tie them as a reminder on your forearm" / ASV: "thou shalt bind them for a sign upon thy hand" / WEB: "You shall bind them for a sign on your hand" — The basis for Jewish phylacteries (tefillin). NET interprets "oth" as "reminder"; ASV/WEB as "sign." "Forearm" (NET) vs. "hand" (ASV/WEB) — Hebrew "yad" includes both.

- v.9 — NET: "write them on the doorframes of your houses and on your gates" / ASV: "thou shalt write them upon the door-posts of thy house, and upon thy gates" / WEB: "and you shall write them on the door posts of your house, and on your gates" — The basis for the Jewish mezuzah. "Doorposts" (Hebrew "mezuzot") — God's word marks the boundaries of daily life: going out and coming in.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Hear, O Israel" (v.4). The Shema begins with hearing — but biblical
   hearing is never passive. To hear is to respond, to obey, to act.
   Are you hearing God's word, or merely listening?

2. "With all thine heart, and with all thy soul, and with all thy might"
   (v.5). God demands totality. Not 90% — all. No compartment of life is
   exempt from His claim. Love that holds something back is not the love
   God commands.

3. "Teach them diligently unto thy children" (v.7). Faith is transmitted
   through intentional, constant, life-integrated teaching. Not merely
   Sunday school but sitting, walking, lying down, rising up. Every moment
   is a teaching moment.

4. "When thou shalt have eaten and be full — then beware lest thou forget"
   (vv.11-12). Prosperity is more dangerous than poverty. Want drives us
   to God; abundance tempts us to forget Him. The full stomach is the
   enemy of the grateful heart.

5. "He brought us out... that he might bring us in" (v.23). Salvation has
   a purpose beyond escape. God does not merely save FROM — He saves FOR.
   He brought Israel out of Egypt to bring them into the land. He saves
   you from sin to bring you into His purposes.

6. "For our good always" (v.24). God's commands are not arbitrary
   restrictions imposed by a tyrant. They are the loving instructions of
   a Father who knows what leads to life. Obedience is always "for our
   good."
