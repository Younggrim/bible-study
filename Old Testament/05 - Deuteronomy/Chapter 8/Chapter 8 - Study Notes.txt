Deuteronomy - Chapter 8
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Moses warns Israel not to forget God when they prosper in the land — remember it is He who gives the power to get wealth.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses. Deuteronomy is the fifth and final book of the Pentateuch,
written at the end of the 40 years of wilderness wandering (approximately
1406 BC). Hebrew title: "Devarim" — "Words/Things." Greek title:
"Deuteronomion" — "Second Law" (a repetition/expansion of the Law for the
new generation). The book consists of Moses' farewell speeches to Israel on
the plains of Moab, just before they enter the Promised Land. Moses will
not enter with them — these are his final words to the nation he has led
for 40 years.

Historical Context: Chapter 8 is one of the most personally searching
chapters in Deuteronomy. Moses commands Israel to remember the wilderness —
not as punishment but as education. God humbled them, tested them, let them
hunger, then fed them with manna — all to teach one lesson: "man doth not
live by bread only, but by every word that proceedeth out of the mouth of
the LORD" (v.3). This is the verse Jesus quoted when Satan tempted Him in
the wilderness (Matthew 4:4). The chapter then describes the abundant land
they are about to enter — and immediately warns: when you are full, do not
forget God. Do not say "My power and the might of mine hand hath gotten me
this wealth" (v.17). Remember: "it is he that giveth thee power to get
wealth" (v.18). The chapter is a masterful sermon on the spiritual dangers
of prosperity — more relevant to comfortable Western Christians than
perhaps any other Old Testament passage.

Remember the Wilderness (vv.1-6): Keep the commandments "that ye may live,
and multiply, and go in and possess the land" (v.1). "Remember all the way
which the LORD thy God led thee these forty years in the wilderness" (v.2).
Three purposes of the wilderness: (1) "to humble thee" — to break self-
sufficiency; (2) "to prove thee" / "testing you" — to reveal what was in
their hearts; (3) "to know what was in thine heart, whether thou wouldest
keep his commandments, or no" (v.2). God let them hunger, then fed them
with manna — food they had never known (v.3). The lesson: "man doth not
live by bread only, but by every word that proceedeth out of the mouth of
the LORD doth man live" (v.3). Physical provision is not enough — man needs
God's word. Their clothes did not wear out; their feet did not swell (v.4).
"As a man chasteneth his son, so the LORD thy God chasteneth thee" (v.5) —
the wilderness was fatherly discipline, not abandonment.

The Good Land Described (vv.7-10): God is bringing them into "a good land"
(v.7) — described in lavish detail: brooks of water, fountains, springs
flowing from valleys and hills (v.7). Wheat, barley, vines, fig trees,
pomegranates, olive oil, honey (v.8). Bread without scarcity, lacking
nothing, iron in the stones, copper in the hills (v.9). "When thou hast
eaten and art full, then thou shalt bless the LORD thy God for the good
land which he hath given thee" (v.10). The proper response to abundance is
gratitude, not self-congratulation.

The Danger of Forgetting (vv.11-17): "Beware that thou forget not the LORD
thy God" (v.11). The progression of forgetfulness: eating and being full
(v.12), building good houses (v.12), herds multiplying, silver and gold
multiplying, everything multiplying (v.13) — "then thine heart be lifted
up, and thou forget the LORD thy God" (v.14). They will forget the God who
brought them from Egypt (v.14), who led them through the terrible
wilderness with fiery serpents, scorpions, and drought (v.15), who brought
water from rock and fed them manna (v.16). The ultimate expression of
forgetfulness: "My power and the might of mine hand hath gotten me this
wealth" (v.17). Self-made man theology — the denial of God as source.

Remember — He Gives Power to Get Wealth (vv.18-20): The antidote: "thou
shalt remember the LORD thy God: for it is he that giveth thee power to get
wealth" (v.18). Even the ability to produce wealth is God's gift. The
purpose: "that he may establish his covenant" (v.18). The warning: if you
forget God and follow other gods, "ye shall surely perish" (v.19). "As the
nations which the LORD destroyeth before your face, so shall ye perish"
(v.20) — Israel will receive the same judgment as the Canaanites if they
commit the same sins.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Moses speaks from the plains of Moab. He recalls the "great and terrible wilderness" (v.15) — the route where God sustained them with manna and water from flint rock.
- "A land of brooks of water, of fountains and depths" (v.7): The Promised Land described in contrast to the barren wilderness.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/deuteronomy-8/
  Guzik emphasizes that the wilderness was God's school — designed to
  teach dependence. He notes that verse 3 is not anti-material but
  pro-word: physical bread sustains the body, but God's word sustains the
  whole person. He highlights the progression in vv.12-17 as the anatomy
  of spiritual decline: prosperity → comfort → pride → forgetfulness →
  self-credit → idolatry.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "'Man doth not live by bread only.' The body lives by bread, but the man
  — the whole man, the soul — lives by every word from God's mouth. You
  may feed the body and starve the soul. You may be rich in this world's
  goods and bankrupt in heaven's currency. The manna taught Israel that
  their true sustenance came not from the ground but from God's mouth.
  Every morning, fresh from heaven — that is how God's word must come to
  us."

REFLECTION
------------------------------------
1. "Man doth not live by bread only" (v.3). Physical provision is not
   enough for human flourishing. You need God's word as surely as you need
   food. Are you feeding your soul as faithfully as you feed your body?

2. "To humble thee, and to prove thee" (v.2). Your wilderness seasons have
   purpose. God uses hardship to break self-sufficiency and reveal what is
   truly in your heart. The test is not for God's information — it is for
   yours.

3. "As a man chasteneth his son" (v.5). God's discipline is fatherly, not
   punitive. A father who never disciplines does not love (Hebrews 12:8).
   If God is correcting you, it is because you are His child, not because
   He has abandoned you.

4. "Then thine heart be lifted up, and thou forget" (v.14). The anatomy of
   spiritual decline: abundance → pride → forgetfulness → self-credit →
   idolatry. Prosperity is more spiritually dangerous than poverty. Guard
   your heart in seasons of plenty.

5. "My power and the might of mine hand hath gotten me this wealth" (v.17).
   The self-made man is a myth. Every ability, every opportunity, every
   breath is God's gift. The moment you take credit for what God has given,
   you have begun to forget Him.

6. "It is he that giveth thee power to get wealth" (v.18). Even your
   capacity to work, to think, to create, to earn — all of it comes from
   God. Wealth is not evil, but it is never self-generated. Acknowledge
   the Source.
