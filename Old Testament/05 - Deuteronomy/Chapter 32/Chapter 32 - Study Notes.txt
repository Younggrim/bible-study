Deuteronomy - Chapter 32
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Moses teaches Israel a song recounting God's faithfulness and Israel's rebellion — a witness against them for future generations.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses. Deuteronomy is the fifth and final book of the Pentateuch,
written at the end of the 40 years of wilderness wandering (approximately
1406 BC). Hebrew title: "Devarim" — "Words/Things." Greek title:
"Deuteronomion" — "Second Law" (a repetition/expansion of the Law for the
new generation). The book consists of Moses' farewell speeches to Israel on
the plains of Moab, just before they enter the Promised Land. Moses will
not enter with them — these are his final words to the nation he has led
for 40 years.

Historical Context: Chapter 32 contains the Song of Moses — one of the
greatest poems in the Old Testament and one of the longest songs in
Scripture. Commanded by God in 31:19 as "a witness for me against the
children of Israel," this song was designed to be memorized and sung by
future generations so that when judgment came, they could not claim
ignorance. The song follows a clear structure: (1) Introduction — call to
heaven and earth as witnesses (vv.1-3), (2) God's character — the
faithful Rock (vv.4-6), (3) God's past grace — choosing, finding, and
nurturing Israel (vv.7-14), (4) Israel's apostasy — Jeshurun grew fat and
kicked (vv.15-18), (5) God's judgment — hiding His face, provoking them
with a foolish nation (vv.19-27), (6) Israel's foolishness and their
enemies' false confidence (vv.28-33), (7) God's vengeance stored up and
His vindication of His people (vv.34-43). The song ends with a call to
rejoice because God will avenge His servants and cleanse His land. After
the song, God commands Moses to ascend Mount Nebo to view the land and die
(vv.48-52). The Song of Moses is referenced in Revelation 15:3 where the
victorious saints sing "the song of Moses the servant of God, and the song
of the Lamb." Key theological themes: God as "the Rock" (used 5 times),
divine faithfulness versus human unfaithfulness, the jealousy of God, and
the certainty of both judgment and ultimate mercy.

Introduction — Heaven and Earth as Witnesses (vv.1-3): "Give ear, O ye
heavens, and I will speak; and hear, O earth, the words of my mouth" (v.1).
Moses summons the cosmos as witnesses — the same witnesses called in 30:19.
His teaching is compared to rain and dew: "My doctrine shall drop as the
rain, my speech shall distil as the dew" (v.2). The purpose: "I will
publish the name of the LORD: ascribe ye greatness unto our God" (v.3).

God the Faithful Rock (vv.4-6): "He is the Rock, his work is perfect: for
all his ways are judgment: a God of truth and without iniquity, just and
right is he" (v.4). Five attributes: perfect work, just ways, truthful,
without iniquity, upright. In contrast: "They have corrupted themselves,
their spot is not the spot of his children: they are a perverse and
crooked generation" (v.5). The rebuke: "Do ye thus requite the LORD, O
foolish people and unwise? is not he thy father that hath bought thee?"
(v.6).

God's Past Grace (vv.7-14): "Remember the days of old" (v.7). God chose
Israel: "the LORD'S portion is his people; Jacob is the lot of his
inheritance" (v.9). God found and nurtured them: "He found him in a desert
land... he led him about, he instructed him, he kept him as the apple of
his eye" (v.10). The eagle metaphor: "As an eagle stirreth up her nest,
fluttereth over her young, spreadeth abroad her wings, taketh them, beareth
them on her wings: so the LORD alone did lead him" (vv.11-12). God gave
abundance: honey from the rock, oil from flint, butter, milk, fat of
lambs, finest wheat, pure blood of the grape (vv.13-14).

Jeshurun's Apostasy (vv.15-18): "But Jeshurun waxed fat, and kicked"
(v.15). "Jeshurun" = "the upright one" — an ironic name for Israel. They
grew prosperous and rebelled: "thou art waxen fat, thou art grown thick,
thou art covered with fatness; then he forsook God which made him, and
lightly esteemed the Rock of his salvation" (v.15). "They provoked him to
jealousy with strange gods" (v.16). "They sacrificed unto devils, not to
God" (v.17). "Of the Rock that begat thee thou art unmindful, and hast
forgotten God that formed thee" (v.18).

God's Judgment (vv.19-27): "I will hide my face from them" (v.20). "They
have moved me to jealousy with that which is not God... I will move them
to jealousy with those which are not a people; I will provoke them to
anger with a foolish nation" (v.21) — Paul quotes this in Romans 10:19
regarding the Gentiles provoking Israel to jealousy. "A fire is kindled in
mine anger, and shall burn unto the lowest hell" (v.22). Disasters heaped
upon them (vv.23-25). God would have destroyed them completely except: "I
feared the wrath of the enemy, lest their adversaries should behave
themselves strangely, and lest they should say, Our hand is high, and the
LORD hath not done all this" (v.27) — God restrains total destruction so
enemies cannot claim credit.

God's Vengeance and Mercy (vv.34-43): "To me belongeth vengeance, and
recompence" (v.35) — quoted in Romans 12:19 and Hebrews 10:30. "Their
foot shall slide in due time" (v.35). "The LORD shall judge his people,
and repent himself for his servants" (v.36). God mocks false gods: "Where
are their gods, their rock in whom they trusted?" (v.37). The great
declaration: "See now that I, even I, am he, and there is no god with me:
I kill, and I make alive; I wound, and I heal: neither is there any that
can deliver out of my hand" (v.39). God will avenge His servants and "be
merciful unto his land, and to his people" (v.43).

Moses Commanded to Ascend Nebo (vv.44-52): Moses recites the song to all
Israel (v.44). "Set your hearts unto all the words which I testify among
you this day" (v.46). "For it is not a vain thing for you; because it is
your life" (v.47). God commands: "Get thee up into this mountain Abarim,
unto mount Nebo... and behold the land of Canaan" (v.49). "And die in the
mount" (v.50). The reason: "Because ye trespassed against me... at the
waters of Meribah-Kadesh... because ye sanctified me not in the midst of
the children of Israel" (v.51). "Yet thou shalt see the land before thee;
but thou shalt not go thither" (v.52).

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Moses speaks from the plains of Moab. The Song of Moses references Israel's geographic story: the wilderness as "a waste howling wilderness" (v.10), the "high places of the earth" (v.13), and future judgment.
- Mount Nebo / Abarim (vv.49-52): God tells Moses to ascend Nebo to view the land before dying — "the land of Canaan, which I give unto the children of Israel for a possession."

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.4  — "He is the Rock, his work is perfect: for all his ways are
        judgment: a God of truth and without iniquity, just and right is
        he." (ESV) — The foundational declaration of God's character. Five
        attributes in one verse. Everything that follows — Israel's sin,
        God's judgment, God's mercy — flows from this perfect character.

v.10 — "He found him in a desert land, and in the waste howling
        wilderness; he led him about, he instructed him, he kept him as
        the apple of his eye." (ESV) — God's tender care for Israel. Found in
        desolation, kept as the most precious thing. "Apple of his eye" =
        the pupil — the most protected part of the body.

v.15 — "But Jeshurun waxed fat, and kicked: you are waxen fat, you are
        grown thick, you art covered with fatness; then he forsook God
        which made him, and lightly esteemed the Rock of his salvation." (ESV) —         Prosperity produces apostasy. The well-fed rebel against the One
        who fed them. This is the pattern of human history.

v.21 — "They have moved me to jealousy with that which is not God; they
        have provoked me to anger with their vanities: and I will move
        them to jealousy with those which are not a people; I will provoke
        them to anger with a foolish nation." (ESV) — Measure for measure. Israel
        provoked God with non-gods; God provokes Israel with non-people.
        Paul applies this to Gentile inclusion (Romans 10:19; 11:11).

v.35 — "To me belongs vengeance, and recompence; their foot shall slide
        in due time: for the day of their calamity is at hand, and the
        things that shall come upon them make haste." (ESV) — Vengeance belongs
        to God alone. Quoted in Romans 12:19 and Hebrews 10:30. Human
        patience is warranted because divine justice is certain.

v.39 — "See now that I, even I, am he, and there is no god with me: I
        kill, and I make alive; I wound, and I heal: neither is there any
        that can deliver out of my hand." (ESV) — Absolute divine sovereignty.
        God alone has power over life and death. No rival exists. None can
        resist His hand.

v.47 — "For it is not a vain thing for you; because it is your life: and
        through this thing you shall prolong your days in the land." (ESV) — God's
        word is not empty or irrelevant — it is life itself. The word is
        not optional but essential for survival.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/deuteronomy-32/
  Guzik emphasizes the Song as a covenant lawsuit — God presenting His
  case against Israel before cosmic witnesses. He notes the "Rock"
  metaphor as central: God is stable, unchanging, reliable — Israel is
  the one who moved. He highlights Paul's use of verse 21 in Romans to
  explain Gentile inclusion as God's means of provoking Israel to jealousy.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "'The Rock, his work is perfect.' When all else shifts and crumbles,
  God remains. His work needs no revision, His ways need no correction.
  If your life seems imperfect, it is not because the Rock has cracked
  but because you have wandered from it. Return to the Rock and you will
  find His work as perfect as ever it was."

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.2  — Hebrew "ya'arof ka-matar liqchi" — may my teaching drop like rain.
       KJV: "My doctrine shall drop as the rain"
       ESV: "May my teaching drop as the rain"
       ASV: "My doctrine shall drop as the rain"
       NET: "My teaching will drop like the rain"
       WEB: "My doctrine will drop as the rain"
       Analysis: "Leqach" = teaching, doctrine, what is received. KJV/ASV/
       WEB use "doctrine"; ESV uses "teaching"; NET uses "teaching." Both
       are valid translations. KJV/ASV/NET/WEB use indicative ("shall/will
       drop"); ESV uses optative ("May... drop"). The image: God's word
       falls gently and produces growth, like rain on grass.

v.4  — Hebrew "El emunah" — God of faithfulness/truth.
       KJV: "a God of truth"
       ESV: "A God of faithfulness"
       ASV: "a God of faithfulness"
       NET: "a reliable God"
       WEB: "a God of faithfulness"
       Analysis: "Emunah" = faithfulness, reliability, steadfastness.
       KJV alone uses "truth" — capturing God's veracity. ESV/ASV/WEB use
       "faithfulness" — capturing His reliability. NET uniquely uses
       "reliable" — an adjective rather than a genitive construction. God
       is both truthful (never lies) and faithful (never fails).

v.8  — Hebrew "b'nei Yisrael" (MT) / "b'nei Elohim" (DSS/LXX) — children of Israel / sons of God.
       KJV: "according to the number of the children of Israel"
       ESV: "according to the number of the sons of God"
       ASV: "according to the number of the children of Israel"
       NET: "according to the number of the heavenly assembly"
       WEB: "according to the number of the children of Israel"
       Analysis: A major textual variant. The Masoretic Text reads "b'nei
       Yisrael" — KJV/ASV/WEB follow this. The Dead Sea Scrolls (4QDeutj)
       and LXX read "b'nei Elohim" — ESV follows this. NET interprets as
       "heavenly assembly." The ESV/NET reading suggests God assigned
       nations to angelic beings but kept Israel for Himself. Both readings
       are ancient and theologically significant.

v.15 — Hebrew "vayishman Yeshurun vayiv'at" — and Jeshurun grew fat and kicked.
       KJV: "Jeshurun waxed fat, and kicked"
       ESV: "Jeshurun grew fat, and kicked"
       ASV: "Jeshurun waxed fat, and kicked"
       NET: "Israel grew fat and kicked"
       WEB: "Jeshurun grew fat, and kicked"
       Analysis: "Jeshurun" (from "yashar" = upright) is a poetic name for
       Israel — ironic here because the "upright one" has become rebellious.
       NET alone replaces "Jeshurun" with "Israel" — losing the irony of
       the poetic name but gaining immediate clarity. "Waxed fat" = grew
       prosperous. "Kicked" = rebelled (like a well-fed animal).

v.17 — Hebrew "la-shedim" — to the shedim/demons.
       KJV: "They sacrificed unto devils"
       ESV: "They sacrificed to demons"
       ASV: "They sacrificed unto demons"
       NET: "They sacrificed to demons"
       WEB: "They sacrificed to demons"
       Analysis: "Shedim" = demons, harmful spirits. KJV alone uses
       "devils" — a term that in modern English typically refers to Satan
       specifically. ESV/ASV/NET/WEB all use "demons" — the more precise
       term for malevolent spiritual beings. Paul quotes this concept in
       1 Corinthians 10:20: pagan sacrifice is offered to demons.

v.35 — Hebrew "li naqam v'shillem" — to me belongs vengeance and recompense.
       KJV: "To me belongeth vengeance, and recompence"
       ESV: "Vengeance is mine, and recompense"
       ASV: "Vengeance is mine, and recompense"
       NET: "I will get revenge and pay them back"
       WEB: "Vengeance is mine, and recompense"
       Analysis: "Naqam" = vengeance, retribution. "Shillem" = recompense,
       repayment. KJV/ESV/ASV/WEB all preserve the declaration form. NET
       uniquely converts to first-person action: "I will get revenge" —
       more colloquial but losing the formal declaration. Paul (Romans
       12:19) and Hebrews (10:30) quote this to forbid personal revenge
       and assure divine justice.

v.43 — Hebrew "harninu goyim ammo" (MT) — rejoice, O nations, his people.
       KJV: "Rejoice, O ye nations, with his people"
       ESV: "Rejoice with him, O heavens; bow down to him, all gods"
       ASV: "Rejoice, O ye nations, with his people"
       NET: "Cry out in joy, O nations, with his people"
       WEB: "Rejoice, you nations, with his people"
       Analysis: Another major textual variant. The Masoretic Text has
       "harninu goyim ammo" — KJV/ASV/NET/WEB follow this. The LXX and
       Dead Sea Scrolls have a longer reading including heavens and gods —
       ESV follows this expanded text. Paul quotes the KJV/MT reading in
       Romans 15:10 to show Gentile inclusion in praise.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
The Rock       — Hebrew "ha-Tzur." A title for God used five times in this
               song (vv.4,15,18,30,31). Emphasizes God's stability,
               reliability, unchangeableness, and strength. Israel's "Rock"
               is contrasted with the enemies' "rock" (v.31) — "their rock
               is not as our Rock."

Jeshurun       — Hebrew "the upright one" — a poetic/affectionate name for
               Israel (from "yashar" = straight, upright). Used ironically
               in v.15: the "upright one" has become crooked. Also appears
               in 33:5,26.

Apple of His Eye — Hebrew "ishon eino" — the pupil of the eye. The most
               sensitive, protected part of the body. Instinctively guarded.
               God protects Israel with the same instinctive care one gives
               to protecting one's own eye.

Vengeance      — Hebrew "naqam" — divine retribution, the execution of
               justice. Not petty revenge but righteous recompense. Belongs
               to God alone (v.35). Humans must not take it; God will
               execute it perfectly and in His timing.

Song of Moses  — This poem (vv.1-43), commanded by God in 31:19 as a
               witness against Israel. Designed to be memorized and sung
               so that when judgment came, Israel could not claim ignorance.
               Referenced in Revelation 15:3.

Meribah-Kadesh — The place where Moses struck the rock instead of speaking
               to it (Numbers 20:1-13). His sin that barred him from
               entering the Promised Land. He failed to sanctify God before
               the people.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. God is the Rock — perfect, faithful, just (v.4). When everything else
   shifts, God remains. His character is the foundation of all theology.
   If we doubt His perfection, justice, or faithfulness, everything else
   collapses. Start here: God is good, God is right, God is faithful.

2. Prosperity is spiritually dangerous (v.15). "Jeshurun waxed fat, and
   kicked." Abundance produces forgetfulness. The well-fed forget who fed
   them. This is the perennial danger of prosperity: it breeds
   self-sufficiency and spiritual amnesia. Guard against it.

3. Forgetting God is the root of all apostasy (v.18). "Thou art unmindful
   of the Rock that begat thee, and hast forgotten God that formed thee."
   Sin begins with forgetting. Before Israel worshiped idols, they forgot
   God. Memory is a spiritual discipline — remembrance prevents rebellion.

4. Vengeance belongs to God (v.35). We are not to take revenge because God
   will. Our patience is not passivity — it is trust that divine justice
   will be executed perfectly. "Their foot shall slide in due time" — God's
   timing is certain even when it seems delayed.

5. God's word is life, not a vain thing (v.47). "It is not a vain thing
   for you; because it is your life." Scripture is not academic, optional,
   or merely informational. It is the difference between life and death.
   Treat it accordingly.

6. Consequences for leaders are real (vv.48-52). Moses — the greatest
   prophet in Israel — is barred from the land for one act of
   disobedience at Meribah. Leadership does not exempt from accountability.
   Greater privilege brings greater responsibility and greater consequence.
