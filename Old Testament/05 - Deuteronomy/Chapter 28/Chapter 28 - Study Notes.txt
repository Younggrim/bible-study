Deuteronomy - Chapter 28
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
God pronounces blessings for obedience and devastating curses for disobedience — the consequences of covenant faithfulness and unfaithfulness.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses. Deuteronomy is the fifth and final book of the Pentateuch,
written at the end of the 40 years of wilderness wandering (approximately
1406 BC). Hebrew title: "Devarim" — "Words/Things." Greek title:
"Deuteronomion" — "Second Law" (a repetition/expansion of the Law for the
new generation). The book consists of Moses' farewell speeches to Israel on
the plains of Moab, just before they enter the Promised Land. Moses will
not enter with them — these are his final words to the nation he has led
for 40 years.

Historical Context: Chapter 28 is the climactic chapter of Deuteronomy's
covenant structure and one of the most remarkable chapters in all of
Scripture. It presents the blessings for obedience (vv.1-14) and the
curses for disobedience (vv.15-68) in devastating detail. The structure
is strikingly asymmetric: 14 verses of blessing versus 54 verses of
cursing. This is not because God delights in cursing but because Moses
knows prophetically what Israel will choose. The curses read like a
preview of Israel's entire subsequent history: siege (fulfilled in
Samaria, Jerusalem under Nebuchadnezzar, and Jerusalem under Titus in
AD 70), exile (Assyrian captivity 722 BC, Babylonian captivity 586 BC),
scattering among the nations (the Diaspora), and the specific horrors of
cannibalism during siege (fulfilled in 2 Kings 6:28-29 and Josephus'
account of the siege of Jerusalem). The chapter follows ancient Near
Eastern treaty/covenant patterns where blessings and curses accompany
covenant ratification. But the specificity and prophetic accuracy of these
curses is unparalleled in ancient literature. Verse 47 gives the root
cause of all judgment: "Because thou servedst not the LORD thy God with
joyfulness, and with gladness of heart, for the abundance of all things."
Joyless religion provokes God's judgment as surely as outright rebellion.

Blessings for Obedience (vv.1-14): The condition: "if thou shalt hearken
diligently unto the voice of the LORD thy God, to observe and to do all
his commandments" (v.1). The blessings are comprehensive: "Blessed shalt
thou be in the city, and blessed shalt thou be in the field" (v.3) —
everywhere. "Blessed shall be the fruit of thy body, and the fruit of thy
ground, and the fruit of thy cattle" (v.4) — everything productive.
"Blessed shalt thou be when thou comest in, and blessed shalt thou be when
thou goest out" (v.6) — always. Military victory: enemies "shall come out
against thee one way, and flee before thee seven ways" (v.7). Economic
dominance: "thou shalt lend unto many nations, and thou shalt not borrow"
(v.12). National preeminence: "the LORD shall make thee the head, and not
the tail" (v.13). The blessings "shall come on thee, and overtake thee"
(v.2) — they pursue the obedient as if chasing them down.

Curses for Disobedience (vv.15-68): The curses mirror and reverse the
blessings: "Cursed shalt thou be in the city, and cursed shalt thou be in
the field" (v.16). Disease (vv.21-22,27,35), drought (vv.23-24), military
defeat (v.25), madness and blindness (v.28), loss of family and property
(vv.30-34), exile (v.36), agricultural failure (vv.38-42), economic
reversal (vv.43-44). The siege prophecy (vv.49-57) is horrifyingly
specific: a nation "from far, from the end of the earth, as swift as the
eagle flieth; a nation whose tongue thou shalt not understand" (v.49) —
fulfilled by Babylon and Rome. Cannibalism during siege: "thou shalt eat
the fruit of thine own body, the flesh of thy sons and of thy daughters"
(v.53) — fulfilled in 2 Kings 6:28-29 and during the Roman siege of
Jerusalem (AD 70). The final curse: return to Egypt in ships (v.68) and
offered as slaves with no buyers — utter worthlessness. The scattering:
"the LORD shall scatter thee among all people, from the one end of the
earth even unto the other" (v.64). The psychological torment: "thy life
shall hang in doubt before thee; and thou shalt fear day and night" (v.66).

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Moses speaks from the plains of Moab. The blessings and curses span the entire land: "blessed in the city... in the field" (v.3). Disobedience brings siege, exile, and scattering "among all people, from the one end of the earth even unto the other" (v.64).

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.1  — "And it shall come to pass, if you shall listen diligently to
        the voice of the LORD your God, to observe and to do all his
        commandments which I command you this day, that the LORD your God
        will set you on high above all nations of the earth." (ESV) — The
        condition for blessing: diligent obedience. The promise: national
        preeminence.

v.2  — "And all these blessings shall come on you, and overtake you, if
        you shall listen to the voice of the LORD your God." (ESV) — Blessings
        pursue the obedient. They don't have to chase blessing; blessing
        chases them.

v.13 — "And the LORD shall make you the head, and not the tail; and you
        shall be above only, and you shall not be beneath." (ESV) — God's
        design for His obedient people: leadership, not subjugation.

v.15 — "But it shall come to pass, if you will not listen to the voice
        of the LORD your God... that all these curses shall come upon you,
        and overtake you." (ESV) — The same pursuit language: curses chase the
        disobedient as blessings chase the obedient.

v.47 — "Because you servedst not the LORD your God with joyfulness, and
        with gladness of heart, for the abundance of all things." (ESV) — The
        root cause of judgment: joyless, ungrateful service. God demands
        not mere compliance but joyful worship.

v.63 — "And it shall come to pass, that as the LORD rejoiced over you to
        do you good, and to multiply you; so the LORD will rejoice over
        you to destroy you." (ESV) — Terrifying: God will take the same delight
        in judgment that He took in blessing. His justice is as passionate
        as His mercy.

v.64 — "And the LORD shall scatter you among all people, from the one end
        of the earth even to the other." (ESV) — Prophetic of the Diaspora.
        Fulfilled historically in the scattering of Israel among all
        nations.

v.66 — "And your life shall hang in doubt before you; and you shall fear
        day and night, and shall have none assurance of your life." (ESV) —         Psychological torment of exile: constant fear, no security, no
        rest. Fulfilled throughout Jewish history in persecution.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/deuteronomy-28/
  Guzik notes the stunning prophetic accuracy of these curses — fulfilled
  in the Assyrian and Babylonian captivities, the Roman destruction of
  Jerusalem, and the subsequent Diaspora. He emphasizes verse 47: the root
  sin is not serving God with joy. Grudging obedience is itself a form of
  rebellion.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "Because thou servedst not the LORD thy God with joyfulness.' Mark this
  well! It is not enough to serve God — you must serve Him with joy. A
  sullen, reluctant obedience is no obedience at all in God's eyes. He
  who gives grudgingly might as well not give. He who obeys with a heavy
  heart insults the God he pretends to serve. Joy in God is not optional
  — it is commanded, and its absence is judged."

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.5  — KJV: "Blessed shall be thy basket and thy store" / ESV: "Blessed
       shall be your basket and your kneading bowl" — Hebrew "tan'akha
       u-mish'artekha." "Tene" = basket (for gathering). "Mish'eret" =
       kneading trough/bowl. ESV is more precise; KJV generalizes
       "mish'eret" as "store." The blessing covers both harvest (basket)
       and food preparation (kneading bowl).

v.20 — KJV: "cursing, vexation, and rebuke" / ESV: "curses, confusion,
       and frustration" — Hebrew "m'erah, m'humah, mig'eret." Three
       Hebrew words beginning with "m" — alliterative in the original.
       "M'humah" = confusion, panic, discomfiture. KJV's "vexation" and
       ESV's "confusion" both capture aspects of this word.

v.25 — KJV: "shalt be removed into all the kingdoms of the earth" / ESV:
       "you shall be a horror to all the kingdoms of the earth" — Hebrew
       "l'za'avah." "Za'avah" = an object of horror/trembling. ESV is
       more accurate: Israel becomes something that horrifies other
       nations, not merely "removed" to them.

v.27 — KJV: "the botch of Egypt, and with the emerods" / ESV: "the boils
       of Egypt, and with tumors" — "Sh'chin" = boils (same word as the
       sixth plague, Exodus 9:9). "Opalim" = tumors/hemorrhoids. KJV's
       archaic "botch" = boil; "emerods" = hemorrhoids.

v.42 — KJV: "All thy trees and fruit of thy land shall the locust
       consume" / ESV: "The cricket shall possess all your trees and the
       fruit of your ground" — Hebrew "tz'latzel." This word is debated:
       it may refer to a cricket, cicada, or type of locust. KJV
       translates as "locust"; ESV as "cricket."

v.47 — KJV: "with joyfulness, and with gladness of heart, for the
       abundance of all things" / ESV: "with joyfulness and gladness of
       heart, because of the abundance of all things" — The Hebrew "me-rov
       kol" can mean either "because of the abundance" (ESV — joy should
       arise FROM abundance) or "amid the abundance" (during prosperity).
       Both readings condemn ingratitude during blessing.

v.49 — KJV: "as swift as the eagle flieth" / ESV: "swooping down like the
       eagle" — Hebrew "ka'asher yid'eh ha-nesher." "Nesher" = eagle or
       vulture. "Da'ah" = to swoop, fly swiftly. The image is of a raptor
       diving on prey — sudden, swift, inescapable.

v.53 — KJV: "thou shalt eat the fruit of thine own body" / ESV: "you
       shall eat the fruit of your womb" — Hebrew "p'ri bitnekha." Both
       translations are accurate. The horror is explicit: parents eating
       their own children during siege. Fulfilled historically.

v.68 — KJV: "no man shall buy you" / ESV: "there will be no buyer" —
       Hebrew "v'ein qoneh." The ultimate degradation: offered as slaves
       but so worthless that no one will purchase them. Below the value
       of a slave.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Blessings & Curses — The two possible outcomes of covenant relationship.
               Blessings follow obedience; curses follow disobedience.
               Standard ancient Near Eastern treaty format, but here with
               prophetic specificity unmatched in secular treaties.

Head and Tail  — Metaphor for national status. "Head" = leader among
               nations, dominant, prosperous. "Tail" = subordinate,
               dependent, following. God promises Israel will be head if
               obedient; tail if disobedient.

Siege          — Military blockade of a city, cutting off food and water
               to force surrender. The curses describe siege conditions in
               horrifying detail, including cannibalism — all fulfilled
               historically in Israel's experience.

Diaspora       — Greek "scattering." The dispersion of Israel among the
               nations. Prophesied here (v.64) and fulfilled through
               Assyrian captivity (722 BC), Babylonian captivity (586 BC),
               and Roman destruction (AD 70).

Joyfulness     — Hebrew "simchah" — joy, gladness. Verse 47 identifies
               joyless service as the root cause of judgment. God demands
               not mere obedience but joyful, grateful obedience that
               recognizes His goodness.

Overtake       — Hebrew "hissig" — to catch up with, to reach. Both
               blessings and curses are described as pursuing and
               overtaking. The obedient cannot escape blessing; the
               disobedient cannot escape cursing.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Blessings pursue the obedient (v.2). "All these blessings shall come
   on thee, and overtake thee." The obedient person doesn't have to chase
   blessing — it chases him. God actively pursues His faithful people with
   goodness. Obedience positions us in the path of God's favor.

2. Joyless service is judged (v.47). "Because thou servedst not the LORD
   thy God with joyfulness." This is stunning: the root sin is not
   idolatry or murder but ungrateful, joyless religion. God is not honored
   by grudging compliance. He demands that we delight in Him. Joy is not
   optional — its absence is rebellion.

3. The asymmetry of curses (54 verses) versus blessings (14 verses). This
   reflects not God's preference for cursing but His knowledge of Israel's
   future choices. The extensive curses serve as warning — God goes to
   extraordinary lengths to make the consequences clear. He wants
   obedience, not judgment.

4. Prophetic accuracy confirms divine authorship. The specificity of these
   curses — siege, cannibalism, exile, scattering, psychological torment,
   persecution among nations — reads like a history of Israel written in
   advance. This is powerful evidence of divine inspiration.

5. Sin reverses every blessing (vv.15-19 mirror vv.3-6). What obedience
   builds, disobedience destroys. Every blessing has a corresponding curse.
   Sin doesn't merely stop blessing — it actively reverses it. What God
   gave in grace, He removes in judgment.

6. The eagle nation (v.49). "A nation from far... as swift as the eagle."
   Fulfilled by Babylon (whose standard was an eagle) and Rome (whose
   standard was also an eagle). God uses pagan nations as instruments of
   His judgment against His own disobedient people. No nation is exempt
   from accountability.
