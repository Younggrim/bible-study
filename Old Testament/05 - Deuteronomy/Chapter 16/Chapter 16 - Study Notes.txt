Deuteronomy - Chapter 16
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
God establishes the three annual pilgrimage feasts — Passover, Weeks, and Tabernacles — and appoints judges to administer justice.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses. Deuteronomy is the fifth and final book of the Pentateuch,
written at the end of the 40 years of wilderness wandering (approximately
1406 BC). Hebrew title: "Devarim" — "Words/Things." Greek title:
"Deuteronomion" — "Second Law" (a repetition/expansion of the Law for the
new generation). The book consists of Moses' farewell speeches to Israel on
the plains of Moab, just before they enter the Promised Land. Moses will
not enter with them — these are his final words to the nation he has led
for 40 years.

Historical Context: Chapter 16 establishes the three annual pilgrimage
feasts and then transitions to the appointment of judges. The three feasts
— Passover/Unleavened Bread (vv.1-8), Weeks/Pentecost (vv.9-12), and
Tabernacles/Booths (vv.13-15) — required all males to appear before God at
the chosen place (v.16). These feasts structured Israel's year around
worship and remembrance. Passover recalled the Exodus (deliverance from
Egypt). Weeks celebrated the grain harvest (firstfruits of labor). Booths
celebrated the full harvest and recalled wilderness dwelling. Each feast
was marked by joy and inclusion — sons, daughters, servants, Levites,
strangers, orphans, and widows all participated (vv.11,14). The chapter
emphasizes that no one appears before God "empty" (v.16) — each gives "as
he is able, according to the blessing of the LORD" (v.17). The transition
to judges (vv.18-20) connects worship to justice: the same God who demands
right worship demands right judgment. The famous command "That which is
altogether just shalt thou follow" (v.20) / "Justice, and only justice,
you shall follow" (ESV) establishes justice as the foundation of life in
the land. The chapter closes with prohibitions against Asherah poles and
pillars near God's altar (vv.21-22) — worship must be pure, unmixed with
pagan elements.

Passover and Unleavened Bread (vv.1-8): "Observe the month of Abib, and
keep the passover unto the LORD thy God" (v.1). Abib (later called Nisan)
— the month of the Exodus. The Passover sacrifice: "of the flock and the
herd, in the place which the LORD shall choose" (v.2). Eaten with
unleavened bread — "the bread of affliction" (v.3). Why? "For thou camest
forth out of the land of Egypt in haste" (v.3). Purpose: "that thou mayest
remember the day when thou camest forth out of the land of Egypt all the
days of thy life" (v.3). No leaven seen for seven days (v.4). The flesh
must not remain until morning (v.4). The Passover may NOT be sacrificed
locally — only "at the place which the LORD thy God shall choose" (vv.5-6).
Timing: "at even, at the going down of the sun" (v.6). "Thou shalt roast
and eat it" (KJV) / "cook it and eat it" (ESV) at the chosen place (v.7).
Six days of unleavened bread, seventh day a solemn assembly — no work
(v.8).

Feast of Weeks (vv.9-12): "Seven weeks shalt thou number unto thee: begin
to number the seven weeks from such time as thou beginnest to put the
sickle to the corn" (v.9). Seven weeks (49 days) from the start of grain
harvest = Pentecost (50th day). "Thou shalt keep the feast of weeks unto
the LORD thy God with a tribute of a freewill offering of thine hand"
(v.10). Give "according as the LORD thy God hath blessed thee" (v.10).
"Thou shalt rejoice before the LORD thy God" (v.11) — with your entire
household, the Levite, the stranger, the fatherless, the widow. At the
chosen place. "Thou shalt remember that thou wast a bondman in Egypt"
(v.12) — memory of slavery motivates generosity and inclusion.

Feast of Tabernacles/Booths (vv.13-15): "Thou shalt observe the feast of
tabernacles seven days, after that thou hast gathered in thy corn and thy
wine" (v.13). This is the harvest festival — celebrating God's full
provision. "Thou shalt rejoice in thy feast" (v.14) — with the same
inclusive list: household, servants, Levite, stranger, fatherless, widow.
Seven days at the chosen place (v.15). "Because the LORD thy God shall
bless thee in all thine increase, and in all the works of thine hands,
therefore thou shalt surely rejoice" (v.15). God's blessing demands
joyful response.

The Three Annual Appearances (vv.16-17): "Three times in a year shall all
thy males appear before the LORD thy God in the place which he shall
choose" (v.16): Unleavened Bread, Weeks, and Tabernacles. "They shall not
appear before the LORD empty" (v.16). "Every man shall give as he is able,
according to the blessing of the LORD thy God which he hath given thee"
(v.17). Proportional giving — not equal amounts but equal sacrifice.

Appointment of Judges (vv.18-20): "Judges and officers shalt thou make
thee in all thy gates" (v.18). They "shall judge the people with just
judgment" (v.18). Three prohibitions: "Thou shalt not wrest judgment" (do
not pervert justice); "thou shalt not respect persons" (no partiality);
"neither take a gift" (no bribes) (v.19). Why no bribes? "A gift doth
blind the eyes of the wise, and pervert the words of the righteous" (v.19).
The great command: "That which is altogether just shalt thou follow" (v.20)
/ "Justice, and only justice, you shall follow" (ESV). Purpose: "that thou
mayest live, and inherit the land" (v.20). Justice is the condition for
remaining in the land.

No Pagan Worship Elements (vv.21-22): "Thou shalt not plant thee a grove
of any trees near unto the altar of the LORD" (KJV) / "You shall not plant
any tree as an Asherah beside the altar" (ESV) (v.21). "Neither shalt thou
set thee up any image" (KJV) / "you shall not set up a pillar" (ESV)
(v.22). "Which the LORD thy God hateth" (v.22). God's altar must be free
from all pagan associations.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Moses speaks from the plains of Moab. Three pilgrimage festivals require travel to "the place which the LORD shall choose" (vv.2, 11, 16) — the future central sanctuary.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.3  — "That you may remember the day when you came forth out of
        the land of Egypt all the days of your life." (ESV) — The feasts exist
        for remembrance. Worship is structured memory of God's saving acts.

v.11 — "And you shall rejoice before the LORD your God, you, and your son,
        and your daughter, and your manservant, and your maidservant, and the
        Levite... and the stranger, and the fatherless, and the widow." (ESV) —         Worship is inclusive joy. No one is excluded from God's feast.

v.16 — "Three times in a year shall all your males appear before the LORD
        your God in the place which he shall choose." (ESV) — The rhythm of
        Israel's worship year. Regular, required, communal appearance
        before God.

v.17 — "Every man shall give as he is able, according to the blessing of
        the LORD your God which he has given you." (ESV) — Proportional giving.
        God does not demand equal amounts but faithful response to His
        blessing.

v.19 — "A gift does blind the eyes of the wise, and pervert the words of
        the righteous." (ESV) — Bribery corrupts even the wise and righteous.
        No one is immune to its influence.

v.20 — "That which is altogether just shall you follow, that you may
        live, and inherit the land which the LORD your God gives you." (ESV) —         Justice is the condition for life in the land. Injustice forfeits
        the inheritance.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/deuteronomy-16/
  Guzik notes that the three feasts correspond to three aspects of God's
  work: Passover = redemption (looking back to deliverance), Weeks =
  provision (celebrating present harvest), Booths = pilgrimage (remembering
  the journey). He emphasizes v.20 as one of the great justice texts of
  Scripture — "justice, and only justice" leaves no room for compromise.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "Three times a year all Israel gathered before God. Not once — that
  would be forgotten. Not constantly — that would become routine. Three
  times — enough to structure the year around worship, enough to prevent
  forgetfulness, enough to maintain the rhythm of grace. God knows what
  we need: regular, joyful, communal remembrance of His mighty acts."

TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1  — Hebrew "v'asita pesach." — "Pesach" = Passover.
       KJV: "keep the passover"
       ESV: "keep the Passover"
       Analysis: Hebrew "v'asita pesach." "Pesach" = Passover. From "pasach" = to pass over, to spare. The feast commemorates God passing over Israelite homes when He struck Egypt's firstborn.

v.2  — takes up residence.
       ASV: "in the place which Jehovah shall choose, to cause his name to dwell there"
       NET: "in the place he chooses to locate his name"
       WEB: "in the place which Yahweh shall choose, to cause his name to dwell there"
       Analysis: takes up residence — in one chosen place.

v.3  — Hebrew "lechem oni." — Unleavened bread = bread of poverty/affliction.
       KJV: "the bread of affliction"
       ESV: "the bread of affliction"
       ASV: "that thou mayest remember the day when thou camest forth out of the land of Egypt"
       NET: "that you may remember the day you came out of the land of Egypt"
       WEB: "that you may remember the day when you came out of the land of Egypt"
       Analysis: Hebrew "lechem oni." Unleavened bread = bread of poverty/affliction. It recalls the haste of departure (no time for dough to rise) and the affliction of slavery. active recollection that shapes present identity and future action.

v.6  — The repetition of "the place YHWH chooses" binds all three pilgrim...
       ASV: "the place which Jehovah thy God shall choose to cause his name to dwell in"
       NET: "at the place where the Lord your God chooses to locate his name"
       Analysis: The repetition of "the place YHWH chooses" binds all three pilgrim feasts (Passover, Weeks, Tabernacles) to centralized worship. God controls where and how He is worshiped.

v.7  — Hebrew "u-vishalta." — The verb "bashal" can mean "cook" or "boil." KJV follows the Exodus...
       KJV: "thou shalt roast and eat it"
       ESV: "you shall cook it and eat it"
       Analysis: Hebrew "u-vishalta." The verb "bashal" can mean "cook" or "boil." KJV follows the Exodus 12:9 specification (roasted with fire, not boiled). ESV uses the broader term. This apparent tension with Exodus 12:9 is resolved in 2 Chronicles 35:13: "they roasted the passover with fire according to the ordinance."

v.10  — Hebrew "missat nidvat yad'kha." — "Missah" = sufficiency, proportion. "N'davah" = freewill offering.
       KJV: "a tribute of a freewill offering"
       ESV: "the tribute of a freewill offering"
       Analysis: Hebrew "missat nidvat yad'kha." "Missah" = sufficiency, proportion. "N'davah" = freewill offering. The amount is voluntary but proportional to blessing received.

v.13  — Hebrew "chag ha-sukkot." — "Sukkot" = booths, temporary shelters.
       KJV: "the feast of tabernacles"
       ESV: "the Feast of Booths"
       Analysis: Hebrew "chag ha-sukkot." "Sukkot" = booths, temporary shelters. KJV's "tabernacles" (from Latin "tabernaculum" = tent) and ESV's "Booths" both refer to the temporary shelters Israel lived in during the wilderness and built during this feast.

v.16  — be seen.
       ASV: "Three times in a year shall all thy males appear before Jehovah thy God"
       NET: "Three times a year all your males must appear"
       WEB: "Three times in a year all your males shall appear before Yahweh your God"
       Analysis: literally "be seen" before God (not just "appear"). They come to be seen BY God — divine inspection of His people at the pilgrimage festivals.

v.18  — justice must be locally accessible, not centralized beyond reach.
       ASV: "judges and officers"
       NET: "judges and officials"
       WEB: "judges and officers"
       Analysis: justice must be locally accessible, not centralized beyond reach.

v.19  — Hebrew "lo tatteh mishpat." — "Natah" = to bend, turn aside, pervert.
       KJV: "Thou shalt not wrest judgment"
       ESV: "You shall not pervert justice"
       Analysis: Hebrew "lo tatteh mishpat." "Natah" = to bend, turn aside, pervert. Justice must be straight — not bent toward the powerful or away from the vulnerable.

v.20  — Hebrew "tzedeq tzedeq tirdof." — righteousness, righteousness you shall pursue.
       KJV: "That which is altogether just shalt thou follow"
       ESV: "Justice, and only justice, you shall follow"
       ASV: "That which is altogether just shalt thou follow"
       NET: "You must pursue justice alone"
       WEB: "You shall follow that which is altogether just"
       Analysis: Hebrew "tzedeq tzedeq tirdof." Literally "righteousness, righteousness you shall pursue." The doubling of "tzedeq" (justice/righteousness) is emphatic: justice alone, justice completely, justice without compromise. One of the most powerful justice statements in Scripture. "Justice, justice you shall pursue!" The doubling is emphatic. NET's "pursue" captures the active chase (Hebrew "radaph" — run after, hunt down).

v.21  — Hebrew "asherah kol etz." — KJV translates "asherah" as "grove"; ESV retains the proper name.
       KJV: "a grove of any trees"
       ESV: "any tree as an Asherah"
       Analysis: Hebrew "asherah kol etz." KJV translates "asherah" as "grove"; ESV retains the proper name. The Asherah was a sacred pole or tree associated with the Canaanite goddess. Planting one near God's altar would syncretize worship.

v.22  — Hebrew "matzevah." — A standing stone used in Canaanite worship.
       KJV: "any image"
       ESV: "a pillar"
       Analysis: Hebrew "matzevah." A standing stone used in Canaanite worship. KJV's "image" is too broad; ESV's "pillar" is more precise. These sacred stones were associated with Baal worship.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Passover (Pesach) — The feast commemorating God's deliverance of Israel
               from Egypt. The lamb's blood on the doorposts caused the
               destroyer to "pass over" Israelite homes (Exodus 12).
               Fulfilled in Christ, "our Passover" (1 Corinthians 5:7).

Unleavened Bread — Bread made without yeast/leaven. Eaten for seven days
               after Passover. Recalls the haste of the Exodus and
               symbolizes purity (leaven = sin in NT typology,
               1 Corinthians 5:6-8).

Feast of Weeks (Pentecost) — Celebrated 50 days after the start of grain
               harvest. A harvest thanksgiving. Fulfilled in Acts 2 when
               the Holy Spirit was poured out on the day of Pentecost —
               the "firstfruits" of the Spirit (Romans 8:23).

Feast of Tabernacles/Booths (Sukkot) — Seven-day harvest festival.
               Israel lived in temporary booths to remember wilderness
               dwelling. Celebrated God's full provision. Associated with
               joy more than any other feast.

Abib/Nisan     — The first month of the Hebrew religious calendar
               (March-April). The month of the Exodus and Passover.
               "Abib" means "green ears" (of grain) — spring month.

"Justice, Justice" — Hebrew "tzedeq tzedeq" — the emphatic doubling
               demands absolute, uncompromised justice. No bending, no
               partiality, no bribery. Justice is the foundation of
               society and the condition for remaining in God's land.

Asherah        — A Canaanite goddess and/or the wooden pole/tree that
               represented her. Placed beside altars in pagan worship.
               Strictly forbidden near God's altar. Represents the
               syncretism God abhors.


PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "That thou mayest remember" (v.3). The feasts exist to prevent
   forgetfulness. God knows that His people forget His mighty acts. So He
   builds remembrance into the calendar — regular, required, communal
   recollection. The Lord's Supper serves the same function: "This do in
   remembrance of me" (Luke 22:19).

2. "Thou shalt rejoice" (vv.11,14,15). Joy is commanded three times in
   this chapter. Biblical worship is not somber duty but commanded
   celebration. And it is inclusive — everyone participates: family,
   servants, Levites, strangers, orphans, widows. Joy that excludes the
   vulnerable is not God's joy.

3. "They shall not appear before the LORD empty" (v.16). You do not come
   to God with nothing. Worship involves giving — not because God needs it
   but because giving expresses gratitude and dependence. Coming empty-
   handed says "God has given me nothing worth acknowledging."

4. "Every man shall give as he is able" (v.17). Proportional giving. God
   does not demand what you do not have. He asks for faithful response to
   what He has given. The widow's mite and the rich man's abundance are
   both acceptable when given proportionally (Mark 12:41-44).

5. "Justice, and only justice, you shall follow" (v.20 ESV). The doubled
   "tzedeq" leaves no room for compromise. Not "justice when convenient"
   or "justice for my friends." Justice absolutely, justice for all,
   justice without exception. This is the foundation of godly society.

6. "A gift doth blind the eyes of the wise" (v.19). Even wise and
   righteous people are corrupted by bribes. No one is immune. This is
   why the prohibition is absolute — not "be careful with gifts" but
   "take no gift." The only defense against corruption is total refusal.
