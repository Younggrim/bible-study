Deuteronomy - Chapter 31
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Moses commissions Joshua, writes the law, and commands it be read every seven years; God foretells Israel's coming unfaithfulness.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses. Deuteronomy is the fifth and final book of the Pentateuch,
written at the end of the 40 years of wilderness wandering (approximately
1406 BC). Hebrew title: "Devarim" — "Words/Things." Greek title:
"Deuteronomion" — "Second Law" (a repetition/expansion of the Law for the
new generation). The book consists of Moses' farewell speeches to Israel on
the plains of Moab, just before they enter the Promised Land. Moses will
not enter with them — these are his final words to the nation he has led
for 40 years.

Historical Context: Chapter 31 is the beginning of the end — Moses'
final preparations before his death. The chapter records four critical
actions: (1) Moses' public charge to Israel and Joshua — "Be strong and
of a good courage" (vv.1-8), (2) the writing of the law and its
entrustment to the priests with instructions for public reading every
seven years (vv.9-13), (3) God's commissioning of Joshua in the tabernacle
and His sobering prophecy of Israel's future apostasy (vv.14-22), and (4)
the placement of the written law beside the ark as a witness against
Israel, with Moses' final prediction of their corruption (vv.23-30). The
chapter is pervaded by a tragic tension: Moses prepares for a future he
knows will go wrong. God Himself tells Moses: "this people will rise up,
and go a whoring after the gods of the strangers of the land" (v.16).
Moses tells the people: "I know that after my death ye will utterly
corrupt yourselves" (v.29). Yet the preparations are made anyway — the law
is written, Joshua is commissioned, the Song is taught. God provides for
His people even knowing they will rebel. The chapter also marks the
transition of leadership from Moses to Joshua — the end of one era and
the beginning of another.

Moses' Charge to Israel and Joshua (vv.1-8): "I am an hundred and twenty
years old this day; I can no more go out and come in" (v.2). Moses
acknowledges his age and God's decree: "Thou shalt not go over this
Jordan" (v.2). But God will go: "The LORD thy God, he will go over before
thee" (v.3). Joshua will lead: "Joshua, he shall go over before thee"
(v.3). The charge to all Israel: "Be strong and of a good courage, fear
not, nor be afraid of them: for the LORD thy God, he it is that doth go
with thee; he will not fail thee, nor forsake thee" (v.6). Then to Joshua
personally: "Be strong and of a good courage: for thou must go with this
people unto the land" (v.7). The promise repeated: "The LORD, he it is
that doth go before thee; he will be with thee, he will not fail thee,
neither forsake thee: fear not, neither be dismayed" (v.8). Hebrews 13:5
quotes this promise: "I will never leave thee, nor forsake thee."

The Law Written and Entrusted (vv.9-13): "Moses wrote this law, and
delivered it unto the priests the sons of Levi" (v.9). The law is to be
read publicly "at the end of every seven years, in the solemnity of the
year of release, in the feast of tabernacles" (v.10). The audience: "all
Israel" (v.11) — "men, and women, and children, and thy stranger" (v.12).
The purpose: "that they may hear, and that they may learn, and fear the
LORD your God, and observe to do all the words of this law" (v.12). And
for the next generation: "that their children, which have not known any
thing, may hear, and learn to fear the LORD your God" (v.13). The law must
be transmitted — each generation must hear it fresh.

God's Prophecy of Apostasy (vv.14-22): God summons Moses and Joshua to
the tabernacle (v.14). The LORD appears in a pillar of cloud (v.15). The
prophecy: "this people will rise up, and go a whoring after the gods of
the strangers of the land... and will forsake me, and break my covenant"
(v.16). God's response: "my anger shall be kindled against them... and I
will forsake them, and I will hide my face from them" (v.17). Israel will
then say: "Are not these evils come upon us, because our God is not among
us?" (v.17). But God will still hide His face "for all the evils which
they shall have wrought" (v.18). Therefore: "write ye this song for you,
and teach it the children of Israel" (v.19) — the Song of Moses (chapter
32) will serve as a witness against them when they rebel.

The Law Beside the Ark (vv.23-30): Joshua is charged: "Be strong and of
a good courage: for thou shalt bring the children of Israel into the land
which I sware unto them: and I will be with thee" (v.23). Moses commands
the Levites: "Take this book of the law, and put it in the side of the
ark of the covenant of the LORD your God, that it may be there for a
witness against thee" (v.26). Moses' sobering assessment: "For I know thy
rebellion, and thy stiff neck: behold, while I am yet alive with you this
day, ye have been rebellious against the LORD; and how much more after my
death?" (v.27). His prophecy: "I know that after my death ye will utterly
corrupt yourselves, and turn aside from the way which I have commanded
you; and evil will befall you in the latter days" (v.29).

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Moses speaks from the plains of Moab. He will "not go over this Jordan" (v.2). Joshua will lead Israel across. The book of the law is placed beside the ark of the covenant.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/deuteronomy-31/
  Guzik notes the tragic irony: Moses prepares Israel for a future he
  knows will go wrong. God commissions Joshua knowing Israel will rebel.
  Yet the preparations are still made — God's faithfulness is not
  contingent on Israel's faithfulness. He also emphasizes Hebrews 13:5
  as the New Testament application of verse 6.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "'He will not fail thee, nor forsake thee.' Here is the believer's
  anchor in every storm. Not 'He might not fail thee' but 'He WILL NOT.'
  The double negative is absolute: not fail, not forsake. Whatever else
  may change — leaders die, circumstances shift, enemies arise — this
  remains: God will not leave His people. Moses dies, but God does not."

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. God's presence is the foundation of courage (vv.6,8). "Be strong and
   of a good courage" is not a command to manufacture confidence from
   within. The reason for courage follows immediately: "for the LORD thy
   God, he it is that doth go with thee." Our strength is derivative —
   it comes from His presence. Without Him, courage is foolishness.

2. Leaders change; God doesn't (vv.2-3). Moses dies, but God continues.
   Joshua takes over, but God is the same. Human leadership is temporary;
   divine faithfulness is permanent. We must not put our trust in leaders
   but in the God who outlasts them all.

3. The law must be transmitted to every generation (vv.12-13). "That their
   children, which have not known any thing, may hear, and learn." Each
   generation must hear God's word fresh. Faith is not inherited
   automatically — it must be taught, heard, and embraced anew. Neglect
   of transmission produces apostasy.

4. God provides even knowing we will fail (vv.16-22). God knows Israel
   will rebel. He tells Moses plainly. Yet He still commissions Joshua,
   still writes the law, still teaches the Song. God's faithfulness is
   not contingent on our faithfulness. He provides means of grace even for
   those He knows will abuse them.

5. The law as witness against us (v.26). The written word placed beside
   the ark testifies against those who violate it. Scripture is not merely
   instruction — it is evidence. On the day of judgment, God's revealed
   word will testify that we knew what was right.

6. Moses' tragic realism (v.29). "I know that after my death ye will
   utterly corrupt yourselves." Moses has no illusions about human nature.
   Forty years of leading this people has taught him what they are. Yet he
   still serves, still prepares, still speaks. Realism about human sin
   does not excuse us from faithful service.
