Deuteronomy - Chapter 5
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Moses restates the Ten Commandments to the new generation, reminding them of the covenant at Horeb and their duty to obey.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses. Deuteronomy is the fifth and final book of the Pentateuch,
written at the end of the 40 years of wilderness wandering (approximately
1406 BC). Hebrew title: "Devarim" — "Words/Things." Greek title:
"Deuteronomion" — "Second Law" (a repetition/expansion of the Law for the
new generation). The book consists of Moses' farewell speeches to Israel on
the plains of Moab, just before they enter the Promised Land. Moses will
not enter with them — these are his final words to the nation he has led
for 40 years.

Historical Context: Chapter 5 begins Moses' second address (chapters 5-26),
which forms the heart of Deuteronomy. He opens by restating the Ten
Commandments — the foundation of the covenant — for the new generation.
Most of these people were children or not yet born when God spoke at Horeb.
Yet Moses insists: "Not with our fathers did the LORD make this covenant,
but with us, who are all of us here alive today" (v.3). The covenant is not
merely historical — it is present and binding on each generation. The
Deuteronomy version of the Ten Commandments is nearly identical to Exodus
20, with one significant difference: the Sabbath commandment. In Exodus,
the reason is creation ("for in six days the LORD made heaven and earth");
here, the reason is redemption ("remember that thou wast a servant in the
land of Egypt"). Same command, different motivation — reflecting
Deuteronomy's emphasis on God's saving acts. The chapter concludes with the
people's fear at Horeb and God's poignant response: "O that there were such
an heart in them, that they would fear me, and keep all my commandments
always!" (v.29) — a divine longing that anticipates the new covenant
(Jeremiah 31:33; Ezekiel 36:26).

The Covenant at Horeb (vv.1-5): Moses summons all Israel: "Hear, O Israel"
(v.1) — learn, keep, and do. The covenant was made at Horeb — "not with
our fathers... but with us" (v.3). This is remarkable: the previous
generation is dead, yet Moses says the covenant was made with those standing
before him. The covenant transcends generations. God spoke "face to face"
from the fire (v.4). Moses stood as mediator between God and the people
because they feared the fire (v.5).

The Ten Commandments Restated (vv.6-21):
  First: "I am the LORD thy God, which brought thee out of the land of
  Egypt" (v.6) — God's identity and saving act as the basis for all
  commands.
  Second: No other gods before Me (v.7).
  Third: No graven images (vv.8-10) — God is jealous, visiting iniquity
  to the third and fourth generation of those who hate Him, but showing
  mercy to thousands who love Him.
  Fourth: Do not take God's name in vain (v.11).
  Fifth: Keep the Sabbath holy (vv.12-15) — six days of labor, seventh
  day of rest for all, including servants and animals. The reason here:
  "remember that thou wast a servant in the land of Egypt" (v.15). You
  were slaves — now give rest to all, even your servants.
  Sixth: Honor father and mother — "that thy days may be prolonged, and
  that it may go well with thee" (v.16). The first commandment with
  promise.
  Seventh: Do not kill (v.17).
  Eighth: Do not commit adultery (v.18).
  Ninth: Do not steal (v.19).
  Tenth: Do not bear false witness (v.20).
  Eleventh: Do not covet — neighbor's wife, house, field, servants,
  animals, or anything (v.21).

God Spoke and Added No More (v.22): These words God spoke to the whole
assembly from fire, cloud, and thick darkness "with a great voice: and he
added no more" (KJV) / "with a loud voice; and he added no more" (ESV).
The Ten Commandments are complete — God Himself drew the boundary. He wrote
them on two tablets of stone and gave them to Moses.

The People's Fear and Request (vv.23-27): Hearing God's voice from the
fire, the people were terrified (v.23). They acknowledged: "we have seen
this day that God doth talk with man, and he liveth" (v.24). But they
feared death: "if we hear the voice of the LORD our God any more, then we
shall die" (v.25). Their request: let Moses be the mediator — "Go thou
near, and hear all... and speak thou unto us" (v.27). They promise: "we
will hear it, and do it."

God's Response — The Longing Heart (vv.28-33): God heard and approved their
words: "they have well said all that they have spoken" (v.28). Then the
most poignant verse: "O that there were such an heart in them, that they
would fear me, and keep all my commandments always, that it might be well
with them, and with their children for ever!" (v.29). God longs for what He
knows they cannot sustain. Their words are right; their hearts are weak.
This divine longing points forward to the new covenant — when God will
write His law on their hearts (Jeremiah 31:33). Moses is told to remain
and receive the full law to teach them (v.31). The charge: do not turn
aside to the right or left (v.32); walk in all God's ways "that ye may
live" (v.33).

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Moses speaks from the plains of Moab. He recounts the giving of the Ten Commandments at Horeb (Sinai) — "The LORD talked with you face to face in the mount out of the midst of the fire" (v.4).

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.3  — "Not with our fathers did the LORD make this covenant, but with us,
        who are all of us here alive today." (ESV) — The covenant is not
        merely historical. Each generation stands before God as if they
        were at Sinai.

v.6  — "I am the LORD your God, who brought you out of the land of Egypt,
        out of the house of slavery." (ESV) — God's identity precedes His
        commands. He saves first, then commands.

v.7  — "You shall have no other gods before me." (ESV) — The first and
        foundational commandment. All other sins flow from breaking this
        one.

v.15 — "You shall remember that you were a slave in the land of Egypt...
        therefore the LORD your God commanded you to keep the Sabbath day."
        (ESV) — The Sabbath as memorial of redemption. You were slaves;
        now rest.

v.22 — "These words the LORD spoke to all your assembly at the mountain
        out of the midst of the fire... with a loud voice; and he added no
        more." (ESV) — The Ten Commandments are complete. God Himself set
        the boundary of this revelation.

v.26 — "For who is there of all flesh, that has heard the voice of the
        living God speaking out of the midst of fire as we have, and has
        still lived?" (ESV) — The terror and privilege of hearing God
        directly.

v.29 — "Oh that they had such a heart as this always, to fear me and to
        keep all my commandments always, that it might go well with them
        and with their descendants forever!" (ESV) — God's longing for
        His people's obedience. He knows their weakness before they do.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/deuteronomy-5/
  Guzik emphasizes the difference between the Exodus and Deuteronomy
  Sabbath rationales — creation vs. redemption — showing that the same
  command can have multiple valid motivations. He highlights v.29 as one
  of the most moving verses in Scripture: God's own longing for obedient
  hearts, which He knows the old covenant cannot produce.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "'O that there were such an heart in them!' Here is the sigh of God
  over His people. He does not merely command obedience — He longs for it.
  He does not merely punish disobedience — He grieves over it. What a God
  is this, who yearns for the love of His creatures! And what a gospel is
  this, that what God longed for in Deuteronomy 5, He provides in the new
  covenant: 'I will put my law in their inward parts, and write it in
  their hearts' (Jeremiah 31:33)."

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.1  — KJV: "statutes and judgments" / ESV: "statutes and rules" — Hebrew
       "ha-chuqqim v'ha-mishpatim." Same distinction as 4:1. KJV
       consistently renders "mishpatim" as "judgments"; ESV as "rules."

v.5  — KJV: "to shew you the word of the LORD" / ESV: "to declare to you
       the word of the LORD" — Hebrew "l'haggid lachem et d'var YHWH."
       "Haggid" = to declare, to tell, to make known. Moses' role as
       mediator: receiving God's word and declaring it to the people.

v.9  — KJV: "visiting the iniquity of the fathers upon the children" /
       ESV: "visiting the iniquity of the fathers on the children" —
       Hebrew "poqed avon avot al banim." "Poqed" = visiting/attending to.
       Not arbitrary punishment of innocents but the natural consequences
       of sin extending through generations.

v.10 — KJV: "shewing mercy unto thousands" / ESV: "showing steadfast love
       to thousands" — Hebrew "oseh chesed la-alaphim." "Chesed" =
       covenant love/loyalty/mercy. ESV's "steadfast love" captures the
       covenantal faithfulness; KJV's "mercy" captures the undeserved
       nature. The contrast: iniquity visits 3-4 generations; mercy
       extends to thousands.

v.12 — KJV: "Keep the sabbath day to sanctify it" / ESV: "Observe the
       Sabbath day, to keep it holy" — Hebrew "shamor et yom ha-Shabbat
       l'qadd'sho." Note: Exodus 20:8 uses "zachor" (remember); here
       "shamor" (keep/guard). Jewish tradition holds both were spoken
       simultaneously — remember and keep.

v.21 — KJV: "Neither shalt thou desire... neither shalt thou covet" / ESV:
       "you shall not covet... you shall not desire" — Hebrew "lo
       tachmod... v'lo tit'avveh." Two different verbs: "chamad" (to
       desire/delight in) and "avah" (to crave/long for). The Exodus
       version uses only "chamad." Deuteronomy adds "avah" and separates
       the wife from the property — elevating her status.

v.29 — KJV: "O that there were such an heart in them" / ESV: "Oh that
       they had such a heart as this always" — Hebrew "mi yitten v'hayah
       l'vavam zeh lahem." Literally "who will give that their heart would
       be this to them." An idiom expressing deep longing. God Himself
       wishes for what He knows they lack.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Covenant (Hebrew: "b'rit") — A binding agreement between God and His
               people. At Horeb, God initiated the covenant with Israel:
               He would be their God; they would be His people, keeping
               His commandments. The Ten Commandments are the covenant
               terms.

Ten Commandments — Hebrew "aseret ha-d'varim" — literally "ten words."
               The foundational terms of God's covenant with Israel,
               spoken directly by God and written by His finger on stone.
               They govern relationship with God (1-4) and with neighbor
               (5-10).

Sabbath (Hebrew: "Shabbat") — "Cessation/rest." The seventh day, set
               apart as holy. In Exodus, grounded in creation (God rested
               on the seventh day). In Deuteronomy, grounded in redemption
               (you were slaves — now rest). Both reasons are valid and
               complementary.

Face to Face   — Hebrew "panim b'fanim" — indicating directness and
               intimacy of communication. Not that they saw God's face
               (4:12 — "no similitude") but that He spoke directly,
               without intermediary, to the whole assembly.

Mediator       — Moses' role: standing between God and the people (v.5).
               The people could not bear God's direct voice; Moses
               received and transmitted. This points to Christ as the
               ultimate mediator (1 Timothy 2:5; Hebrews 8:6).

Vain (Hebrew: "shav") — Emptiness, falsehood, worthlessness. Taking God's
               name "in vain" means using it for empty, false, or
               worthless purposes — false oaths, casual profanity, or
               claiming His authority without His commission.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.6 — NET: "I am the Lord your God, who brought you from the land of Egypt, from the place of slavery" / ASV: "I am Jehovah thy God, who brought thee out of the land of Egypt, out of the house of bondage" / WEB: "I am Yahweh your God, who brought you out of the land of Egypt, out of the house of bondage" — The Decalogue preamble. NET uses "place of slavery"; ASV/WEB use "house of bondage" (Hebrew "beth avadim"). God's self-identification grounds all commandments in redemption — He saved them BEFORE giving rules.

- v.7 — NET: "You must not have any other gods besides me" / ASV: "Thou shalt have no other gods before me" / WEB: "You shall have no other gods before me" / KJV: "Thou shalt have none other gods before me" — NET's "besides me" differs from ASV/KJV's "before me." Hebrew "al-panai" could mean "before my face" (in my presence), "in opposition to me," or "besides me." NET chooses exclusivity; ASV/KJV retain the spatial metaphor.

- v.12 — NET: "the Lord spoke to you from the middle of the fire" / ASV: "Jehovah spake unto you out of the midst of the fire" / WEB: "Yahweh spoke to you out of the middle of the fire" — Deuteronomy's retelling of Sinai. "Out of the midst of the fire" — God speaks from fire itself, demonstrating both His holiness (fire purifies) and His power (fire consumes). The divine name marks who spoke.

- v.15 — NET: "the Lord your God has commanded you" / ASV: "Jehovah thy God commanded thee" / WEB: "Yahweh your God commanded you" — The Sabbath command in Deuteronomy adds a humanitarian motivation (slaves must rest) not found in Exodus 20. The divine name appears more frequently in Deuteronomy's version.

- v.21 — NET: "You must not covet your neighbor's wife" / ASV: "Neither shalt thou covet thy neighbor's wife" / WEB: "Neither shall you covet your neighbor's wife" — In Deuteronomy, "wife" is listed separately from property (unlike Exodus 20:17 where she's grouped with possessions). This elevates her dignity — she is a person, not property.

- v.29 — NET: "if only they had such a heart" / ASV: "Oh that there were such a heart in them" / WEB: "Oh that there were such a heart in them" — God's poignant wish for Israel. Hebrew "mi-yitten" (who will give?) — an expression of divine longing. God desires their obedience but will not coerce it.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Not with our fathers... but with us" (v.3). The covenant is not
   ancient history — it is present reality. You stand before God today as
   surely as Israel stood at Sinai. His commands are addressed to you,
   now, personally.

2. "I am the LORD thy God, which brought thee out" (v.6). God's commands
   are grounded in His saving acts. He does not command strangers — He
   commands those He has already redeemed. Obedience is response to grace,
   not the means of earning it.

3. "Remember that thou wast a servant" (v.15). The Sabbath is not merely
   about rest — it is about remembering slavery and celebrating freedom.
   Give rest to others because you know what it is to have none.

4. "O that there were such an heart in them" (v.29). God's deepest desire
   is not mere external compliance but a heart that loves Him. The old
   covenant exposed the problem — the heart is unwilling. The new covenant
   provides the solution — God gives a new heart (Ezekiel 36:26).

5. "Ye shall not turn aside to the right hand or to the left" (v.32).
   Faithfulness is a straight path. Deviation in either direction — toward
   legalism or toward license — is departure from God's way.

6. The people's fear (vv.25-27). They heard God and were terrified. This
   is right — "the fear of the LORD is the beginning of wisdom." But fear
   alone does not produce obedience. They needed what v.29 longs for: a
   transformed heart. Fear without love produces only temporary compliance.
