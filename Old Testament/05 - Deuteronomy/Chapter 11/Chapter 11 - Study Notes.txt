Deuteronomy - Chapter 11
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Moses sets before Israel blessing and curse — blessing for obedience, curse for disobedience — urging them to choose life in the Promised Land.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses. Deuteronomy is the fifth and final book of the Pentateuch,
written at the end of the 40 years of wilderness wandering (approximately
1406 BC). Hebrew title: "Devarim" — "Words/Things." Greek title:
"Deuteronomion" — "Second Law" (a repetition/expansion of the Law for the
new generation). The book consists of Moses' farewell speeches to Israel on
the plains of Moab, just before they enter the Promised Land. Moses will
not enter with them — these are his final words to the nation he has led
for 40 years.

Historical Context: Chapter 11 concludes the exhortation section of Moses'
second address (chapters 5-11) before the specific laws begin in chapter
12. It is a final passionate appeal: love God, obey His commands, and
receive blessing — or turn aside and receive cursing. The chapter contrasts
Egypt (where you irrigated by foot) with Canaan (where God sends rain from
heaven) — making the point that life in the Promised Land requires constant
dependence on God. Rain is not guaranteed by irrigation technology but by
covenant faithfulness. Moses repeats the Shema's teaching commands
(vv.18-20, echoing 6:6-9) and concludes with the dramatic announcement:
"Behold, I set before you this day a blessing and a curse" (v.26). The
blessing and curse will be ceremonially enacted on Mount Gerizim and Mount
Ebal when they enter the land (fulfilled in Joshua 8:30-35). This chapter
is the hinge between exhortation and legislation — the final "why" before
the "what."

Love and Obey — Remember What You Saw (vv.1-7): "Love the LORD thy God,
and keep his charge... alway" (v.1). Moses addresses the adults who
personally witnessed God's acts — "I speak not with your children which
have not known" (v.2). Remember: God's discipline, His greatness, His
mighty hand (v.2). What He did in Egypt to Pharaoh (v.3). What He did to
Egypt's army at the Red Sea (v.4). What He did in the wilderness (v.5).
What He did to Dathan and Abiram — the earth swallowed them (v.6; cf.
Numbers 16). "Your eyes have seen all the great acts of the LORD" (v.7).
Eyewitness experience demands obedient response.

Obedience Brings Strength and Long Life (vv.8-9): "Keep all the
commandments... that ye may be strong, and go in and possess the land"
(v.8). Obedience produces strength for conquest. "That ye may prolong your
days in the land... a land that floweth with milk and honey" (v.9).

The Land Depends on God's Rain (vv.10-12): The Promised Land is "not as
the land of Egypt" (v.10). Egypt: you irrigated by foot (canal systems fed
by the Nile). Canaan: "a land of hills and valleys, and drinketh water of
the rain of heaven" (v.11). This means dependence on God — rain comes from
Him, not from human engineering. "A land which the LORD thy God careth for:
the eyes of the LORD thy God are always upon it, from the beginning of the
year even unto the end of the year" (v.12). God's constant attention is
both provision and accountability.

Conditional Blessings (vv.13-15): "If ye shall hearken diligently... to
love the LORD your God, and to serve him with all your heart and with all
your soul" (v.13) — then God will give rain in its season: "the first rain
and the latter rain" (v.14). The early rain (October-November) softens the
ground for plowing; the latter rain (March-April) fills the grain before
harvest. Result: grain, wine, oil, grass for cattle — "thou mayest eat and
be full" (v.15).

Warning Against Deception (vv.16-17): "Take heed to yourselves, that your
heart be not deceived" (v.16). The danger: turning aside to serve other
gods. The consequence: God's wrath kindled, heaven shut up, no rain, no
fruit, perishing from the land (v.17). The same God who sends rain can
withhold it. Blessing and curse flow from the same source — the difference
is obedience.

Repeat the Shema's Commands (vv.18-21): Nearly identical to 6:6-9: lay up
these words in heart and soul (v.18). Bind them on hand and forehead
(v.18). Teach them to children — sitting, walking, lying down, rising up
(v.19). Write them on doorposts and gates (v.20). Purpose: "that your days
may be multiplied... as the days of heaven upon the earth" (v.21) — as
long as heaven endures above earth.

Promise of Conquest (vv.22-25): If they diligently keep the commandments,
love God, walk in His ways, and "cleave unto him" (v.22) — God will drive
out all nations (v.23). "Every place whereon the soles of your feet shall
tread shall be yours" (v.24). The boundaries: wilderness to Lebanon,
Euphrates to the western sea (Mediterranean) (v.24). "There shall no man
be able to stand before you" (v.25). God will put fear and dread on the
land.

Blessing and Curse — Gerizim and Ebal (vv.26-32): "Behold, I set before
you this day a blessing and a curse" (v.26). The blessing: if you obey
(v.27). The curse: if you disobey and go after other gods (v.28). When
they enter the land: put the blessing on Mount Gerizim and the curse on
Mount Ebal (v.29). These two mountains face each other near Shechem — a
natural amphitheater for the covenant ceremony. The chapter closes: "ye
shall observe to do all the statutes and judgments which I set before you
this day" (v.32).

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Moses speaks from the plains of Moab. The Promised Land is described: watered by rain from heaven (vv.10-12), unlike Egypt where you irrigated by foot.
- Mount Gerizim and Mount Ebal (vv.29-30): Near Shechem — the mountains of blessing and cursing, where Israel will renew the covenant after crossing the Jordan.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/deuteronomy-11/
  Guzik emphasizes the contrast between Egypt and Canaan: Egypt's
  irrigation was human-controlled; Canaan's rain was God-controlled. This
  made Canaan a land of faith — you had to trust God for rain. He notes
  that the blessing/curse formula (v.26) is the fundamental structure of
  covenant theology: two ways, two outcomes, one choice.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "'I set before you this day a blessing and a curse.' There is no third
  option. No neutral ground. No middle path between obedience and
  disobedience, between blessing and curse. God sets before every soul
  the same choice: life or death, heaven or hell, Christ or the world.
  And He does not merely present the options — He urges: choose life.
  Choose blessing. Choose Me."

REFLECTION
------------------------------------
1. "A land which the LORD thy God careth for" (v.12). Canaan required
   faith — you could not control the rain. God designed the land to keep
   His people dependent on Him. Sometimes God puts us in situations where
   we cannot control the outcome — precisely so we will trust Him.

2. "Take heed... that your heart be not deceived" (v.16). Deception is
   the danger — not open rebellion but subtle drift. The heart is deceived
   gradually, not suddenly. Guard against the slow slide.

3. "I set before you this day a blessing and a curse" (v.26). There is no
   neutral ground. Every day you choose — obedience or disobedience,
   blessing or curse. The choice is real, and the consequences are real.

4. "The first rain and the latter rain" (v.14). God's provision has
   seasons. The early rain prepares; the latter rain completes. Trust
   God's timing — He knows when you need the early rain of preparation
   and when you need the latter rain of fulfillment.

5. "To love the LORD your God, to walk in all his ways, and to cleave
   unto him" (v.22). Love, walk, cleave — heart, feet, hands. Devotion
   that is felt (love), practiced (walk), and maintained (cleave). All
   three are necessary.

6. "Your eyes have seen" (v.7). Moses appeals to personal experience.
   You have seen God work. Let what you have witnessed produce faith for
   what lies ahead. Past faithfulness is the ground of future trust.
