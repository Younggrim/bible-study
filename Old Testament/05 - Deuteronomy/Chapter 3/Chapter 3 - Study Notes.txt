Deuteronomy - Chapter 3
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Moses recounts the defeat of King Og of Bashan, the distribution of Transjordan land, and God's refusal to let Moses enter the Promised Land.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses. Deuteronomy is the fifth and final book of the Pentateuch,
written at the end of the 40 years of wilderness wandering (approximately
1406 BC). Hebrew title: "Devarim" — "Words/Things." Greek title:
"Deuteronomion" — "Second Law" (a repetition/expansion of the Law for the
new generation). The book consists of Moses' farewell speeches to Israel on
the plains of Moab, just before they enter the Promised Land. Moses will
not enter with them — these are his final words to the nation he has led
for 40 years.

Historical Context: Chapter 3 continues Moses' historical review with the
defeat of Og king of Bashan — the second great Transjordan victory. Og was
the last of the Rephaim (giants), and his iron bed measured nine cubits by
four (approximately 13.5 feet by 6 feet). His defeat proved that even
giants fall before God. Moses then recounts the distribution of the
Transjordan territory to Reuben, Gad, and the half-tribe of Manasseh, with
the condition that their warriors must cross the Jordan to help their
brothers conquer the western land. The chapter concludes with one of the
most poignant moments in Scripture: Moses' personal plea to enter the
Promised Land — and God's firm refusal. "Speak no more unto me of this
matter." After forty years of faithful leadership, Moses will see the land
but never set foot in it. He must commission Joshua and let go.

The Defeat of Og King of Bashan (vv.1-7): Israel turns northward toward
Bashan. Og comes out with all his people to battle at Edrei (v.1). God
reassures Moses: "Fear him not: for I will deliver him" (v.2) — do to him
as you did to Sihon. The result: total victory. "We smote him until none
was left to him remaining" (KJV) / "we struck him down until he had no
survivor left" (ESV) (v.3). Sixty fortified cities taken — "there was not
a city which we took not from them" (v.4). All devoted to destruction
(v.6), livestock taken as plunder (v.7).

Og the Last Giant (vv.8-11): The conquered territory stretches from the
Arnon Valley to Mount Hermon (v.8). Hermon has multiple names: Sirion
(Sidonian) and Senir (Amorite) (v.9). Verse 11 provides a remarkable
detail: Og was the last of the Rephaim. His bed (or sarcophagus) of iron
was preserved in Rabbah of Ammon — nine cubits long, four cubits wide
(approximately 13.5 x 6 feet). This is not merely trivia; it proves the
reality of the giants the previous generation feared. Yet God defeated him
easily.

Distribution of Transjordan Land (vv.12-17): Moses distributes the
conquered territory: Reuben and Gad receive from Aroer (on the Arnon) to
half of Gilead (v.12). The half-tribe of Manasseh receives the rest of
Gilead and all Bashan — "the land of giants" (v.13). Jair of Manasseh
takes the region of Argob (v.14). Machir receives Gilead (v.15). The
boundaries are carefully defined (vv.16-17): from the Arnon to the Jabbok,
from Chinnereth (Sea of Galilee) to the Salt Sea (Dead Sea).

The Condition for the Transjordan Tribes (vv.18-20): Moses commands Reuben,
Gad, and half-Manasseh: you have received your land, but your warriors must
cross the Jordan armed and fight alongside their brothers (v.18). Wives,
children, and livestock may remain in the cities (v.19). They must fight
"until the LORD have given rest unto your brethren" (v.20) — then they may
return. Receiving blessing carries obligation to help others receive theirs.

Encouragement to Joshua (vv.21-22): Moses charges Joshua: "Thine eyes have
seen all that the LORD your God hath done unto these two kings: so shall
the LORD do unto all the kingdoms whither thou passest" (v.21). The past
victories are proof of future faithfulness. "Ye shall not fear them: for
the LORD your God he shall fight for you" (v.22).

Moses' Plea and God's Refusal (vv.23-29): Moses pleads with God: "Thou
hast begun to shew thy servant thy greatness" (v.24) — let me see the good
land, the mountain, Lebanon (v.25). His prayer is beautiful: "What God is
there in heaven or in earth, that can do according to thy works?" (v.24).
But God is "wroth" (KJV) / "angry" (ESV) with Moses "for your sakes"
(v.26) and refuses. The command is final: "Let it suffice thee; speak no
more unto me of this matter" (KJV) / "Enough from you; do not speak to me
of this matter again" (ESV) (v.26). Moses may look from Pisgah — westward,
northward, southward, eastward — but he shall not cross (v.27). Instead:
"charge Joshua, and encourage him, and strengthen him" (v.28). Moses must
invest in his successor. They remain in the valley opposite Beth-peor
(v.29).

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Bashan (vv.1-11): Og's kingdom in the north of Transjordan — from the Arnon to Mount Hermon. Sixty fortified cities.
- The Transjordan allotment (vv.12-17): Reuben and Gad receive from Aroer (Arnon) to Gilead; half-Manasseh receives Bashan to Hermon.
- Beth-peor (v.29): Israel camps in the valley opposite Beth-peor — the setting for the Deuteronomy speeches.

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "Do not fear him, for I have given him and all his people and his
        land into your hand." (ESV) — God's command to not fear is always
        backed by His promise to deliver.

v.11 — "For only Og the king of Bashan was left of the remnant of the
        Rephaim. Behold, his bed was a bed of iron... Nine cubits was its
        length, and four cubits its breadth." (ESV) — The last giant. His
        size was real — but God is bigger.

v.22 — "You shall not fear them, for it is the LORD your God who fights
        for you." (ESV) — The ground of courage: not human strength but
        divine presence.

v.24 — "O Lord GOD, you have only begun to show your servant your
        greatness and your mighty hand. For what god is there in heaven or
        on earth who can do such works and mighty acts as yours?" (ESV) —
        Moses' worship even in the face of personal disappointment.

v.26 — "Enough from you; do not speak to me of this matter again." (ESV)
        — God's final word. Some prayers are answered "no" — and the "no"
        is final. Grace to accept God's "no" is itself a gift.

v.28 — "But charge Joshua, and encourage and strengthen him, for he shall
        go over at the head of this people." (ESV) — When God says "not
        you," the faithful response is to invest in the one He has chosen.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/deuteronomy-3/
  Guzik highlights Moses' plea as one of the most moving prayers in
  Scripture — a man who gave everything for God's people, yet is denied
  his deepest desire. He notes that Moses' acceptance of God's "no" and
  his investment in Joshua demonstrate mature faith.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "Moses could not enter Canaan, but he could see it. He could not lead
  the people in, but he could prepare the man who would. There is a grace
  in letting go — in accepting that God's plan does not require your
  presence to succeed. Moses' greatest act of faith may not have been at
  the Red Sea but here, on Pisgah, when he accepted God's 'no' and
  blessed his successor."

TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.3  — KJV: "we smote him until none was left to him remaining" / ESV: "we
       struck him down until he had no survivor left" — Hebrew "va-nakkehu
       ad bilti hish'ir lo sarid." Complete destruction — no survivor
       (sarid) remained.

v.4  — KJV: "threescore cities" / ESV: "sixty cities" — Same number.
       KJV uses the archaic "threescore" (three times twenty = 60).

v.5  — KJV: "fenced with high walls, gates, and bars" / ESV: "fortified
       with high walls, gates, and bars" — Hebrew "b'tsurot chomah
       g'vohah, d'latayim u-v'riach." "B'tsurot" = fortified/inaccessible.
       These were not primitive villages but heavily defended cities — yet
       they fell.

v.11 — KJV: "his bedstead was a bedstead of iron" / ESV: "his bed was a
       bed of iron" — Hebrew "arso eres barzel." "Eres" can mean bed or
       couch; some scholars suggest it may refer to a sarcophagus (burial
       coffin). Either way, its size testifies to Og's enormous stature.

v.11 — KJV: "after the cubit of a man" / ESV: "according to the common
       cubit" — Hebrew "b'ammat ish." The standard cubit (approximately
       18 inches), not the royal cubit. Nine cubits = approximately 13.5
       feet.

v.17 — KJV: "Chinnereth" / ESV: "Chinnereth" — The ancient name for the
       Sea of Galilee. From "kinnor" (harp) — the lake is harp-shaped.

v.23 — KJV: "I besought the LORD" / ESV: "I pleaded with the LORD" —
       Hebrew "va-etchannan el YHWH." The verb "chanan" means to seek
       grace/favor. Moses pleads for undeserved mercy. This verb gives
       the name to the next Torah portion: "Va-etchannan."

v.26 — KJV: "Let it suffice thee" / ESV: "Enough from you" — Hebrew "rav
       lach." Literally "much/enough for you." A firm but not harsh
       closure. God does not explain further — He simply says: enough.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Bashan         — A fertile region in northern Transjordan (modern Golan
               Heights area), known for its rich pastures, oak forests,
               and strong cattle (Psalm 22:12; Amos 4:1). Og's kingdom.

Rephaim        — An ancient race of giants. Og was the last of them (v.11).
               The name may mean "shades" or "healers." They inhabited
               various parts of Canaan and Transjordan before being
               displaced.

Argob          — A region within Bashan containing sixty fortified cities.
               Taken by Jair of Manasseh and renamed Havvoth-jair
               ("villages of Jair").

Pisgah         — A mountain ridge in Moab overlooking the Jordan Valley
               and the Dead Sea. Part of the Abarim range. Moses viewed
               the Promised Land from its summit (also called Nebo).

Beth-peor      — "House of Peor" — a location in Moab opposite Jericho.
               Near where Israel camped, where Moses delivered his
               speeches, and where he was buried (34:6). Also associated
               with the Baal-peor incident (Numbers 25).

Chinnereth     — Ancient name for the Sea of Galilee. Also a city on its
               western shore. The name derives from "kinnor" (harp),
               describing the lake's shape.

ADDITIONAL TRANSLATION NOTES (ASV, NET, WEB)
------------------------------------
- v.2 — NET: "Do not be afraid of him" / ASV: "Fear him not" / WEB: "Don't fear him" — God's encouragement before battle with Og of Bashan. The same divine assurance formula: "al-tira" — do not fear. God has already determined the outcome; courage is obedience to promise.

- v.11 — NET: "his bed was made of iron" / ASV: "his bedstead was a bedstead of iron" / WEB: "his bedstead was a bedstead of iron" / KJV: "his bedstead was a bedstead of iron" — NET simplifies the redundant "bedstead was a bedstead." Og's iron bed (approximately 13.5 x 6 feet) testifies to his enormous size — the last of the Rephaim giants.

- v.22 — NET: "The Lord your God will fight for you" / ASV: "Jehovah your God, he it is that fighteth for you" / WEB: "Yahweh your God himself fights for you" — The divine warrior theme. ASV/WEB add "he it is" / "himself" for emphasis — YHWH personally engages in battle. Not just permission but active divine participation.

- v.24 — NET: "what god is there in heaven or on earth" / ASV: "what god is there in heaven or in earth" / WEB: "what god is there in heaven or in earth" — Moses' rhetorical question elevates YHWH above all competitors. No deity in any realm can match His mighty acts. This is practical monotheism expressed through experience.

- v.26 — NET: "Enough! Do not speak to me anymore about this matter" / ASV: "Let it suffice thee; speak no more unto me of this matter" / WEB: "Enough! Speak no more to me of this matter" — God refuses Moses' plea to enter Canaan. Hebrew "rav-lakh" (enough for you!) — the same phrase used to move Israel forward (1:6; 2:3). Now it silences Moses' appeals.

- v.27 — NET: "Go up to the top of Pisgah and look" / ASV: "get thee up into the top of Pisgah, and lift up thine eyes" — Moses may see but not enter the Promised Land. Seeing without entering — a poignant picture of law's limitation. The law shows the promise but cannot bring you in (Rom 8:3).

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "Fear him not" (v.2). Og was the last giant — enormous, terrifying,
   ruling sixty fortified cities. Yet God says: do not fear. The size of
   your enemy is irrelevant when God has already determined the outcome.

2. Moses' prayer denied (vv.23-26). Forty years of faithful service, and
   God still says no. Not every prayer is granted. Not every desire is
   fulfilled. Moses' response is not bitterness but obedience — he
   invests in Joshua. Can you accept God's "no" without resentment?

3. "Speak no more unto me of this matter" (v.26). There is a time to stop
   asking and start accepting. Persistent prayer is good (Luke 18:1-8),
   but when God gives a final answer, continued pleading becomes
   disobedience. Discernment knows the difference.

4. "Charge Joshua, and encourage him, and strengthen him" (v.28). When
   your season ends, invest in the next generation. Moses' legacy is not
   diminished by Joshua's succession — it is fulfilled by it. True
   leadership prepares successors, not dependents.

5. The Transjordan condition (vv.18-20). Receiving your inheritance does
   not exempt you from helping others receive theirs. Blessing carries
   responsibility. You cannot enjoy your rest while your brothers still
   fight.

6. Og's iron bed (v.11). The giants were real. The fear was understandable.
   But God defeated what terrified them. The things that seem impossibly
   large to you are not impossible to God.
