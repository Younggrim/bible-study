Deuteronomy - Chapter 17
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
God gives laws for judges, forbids idolatry, and prescribes rules for Israel's future king — he must write a copy of the law and read it daily.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Moses. Deuteronomy is the fifth and final book of the Pentateuch,
written at the end of the 40 years of wilderness wandering (approximately
1406 BC). Hebrew title: "Devarim" — "Words/Things." Greek title:
"Deuteronomion" — "Second Law" (a repetition/expansion of the Law for the
new generation). The book consists of Moses' farewell speeches to Israel on
the plains of Moab, just before they enter the Promised Land. Moses will
not enter with them — these are his final words to the nation he has led
for 40 years.

Historical Context: Chapter 17 addresses three critical areas of
governance: (1) the prosecution of idolaters with proper judicial
safeguards (vv.1-7), (2) the supreme court for difficult cases (vv.8-13),
and (3) the law of the king (vv.14-20). The chapter opens with a
prohibition against blemished sacrifices (v.1) — connecting worship
integrity to judicial integrity. The idolatry laws (vv.2-7) establish the
crucial requirement of multiple witnesses: no one may be executed on the
testimony of a single witness (v.6). The witnesses must cast the first
stones (v.7) — ensuring personal accountability for accusations. The
supreme court (vv.8-13) provides for cases too difficult for local judges:
they go to the central sanctuary where Levitical priests and the presiding
judge render binding decisions. The law of the king (vv.14-20) is
remarkable: Moses anticipates Israel's future request for a king (fulfilled
in 1 Samuel 8) and provides constitutional limits. The king must be God's
choice, an Israelite (v.15). He must NOT multiply horses (military power),
wives (political alliances), or silver and gold (wealth) (vv.16-17). Most
remarkably, he must write his own copy of the law and read it daily
(vv.18-19). The king is not above the law — he is under it. His heart
must not be "lifted up above his brethren" (v.20). Israel's king is a
brother under God's law, not an absolute monarch. Solomon violated every
prohibition in vv.16-17 (1 Kings 10-11), and his kingdom was torn apart.

Blemished Sacrifices Forbidden (v.1): "Thou shalt not sacrifice unto the
LORD thy God any bullock, or sheep, wherein is blemish, or any
evilfavouredness" (v.1). This is "an abomination unto the LORD thy God"
(v.1). God deserves the best, not the defective. A blemished sacrifice
insults God — it says He is not worth your best.

Prosecution of Idolaters (vv.2-7): "If there be found among you... man or
woman, that hath wrought wickedness... in transgressing his covenant" (v.2).
The specific crime: serving "other gods... either the sun, or moon, or any
of the host of heaven" (v.3) — astral worship, common in the ancient Near
East. Process: "enquired diligently" (v.4) — thorough investigation. If
confirmed: "stone them with stones, till they die" (v.5). Critical
safeguard: "At the mouth of two witnesses, or three witnesses, shall he
that is worthy of death be put to death; but at the mouth of one witness
he shall not be put to death" (v.6). The witnesses must throw first (v.7)
— they bear personal responsibility for the execution. "So thou shalt put
the evil away from among you" (v.7).

The Supreme Court for Difficult Cases (vv.8-13): "If there arise a matter
too hard for thee in judgment" (v.8) — cases involving homicide, legal
rights, or assault that are too complex for local courts. Solution: "arise,
and get thee up into the place which the LORD thy God shall choose" (v.8).
Consult "the priests the Levites, and unto the judge that shall be in
those days" (v.9). Their decision is binding: "thou shalt do according to
the sentence" (v.10). "Thou shalt not decline from the sentence... to the
right hand, nor to the left" (v.11). Penalty for contempt of court: "the
man that will do presumptuously, and will not hearken unto the priest...
or unto the judge, even that man shall die" (v.12). Purpose: "all the
people shall hear, and fear, and do no more presumptuously" (v.13).

The Law of the King (vv.14-20): "When thou art come unto the land... and
shalt say, I will set a king over me, like as all the nations" (v.14).
Moses anticipates the request. God permits it with conditions: "Thou shalt
in any wise set him king over thee, whom the LORD thy God shall choose"
(v.15). He must be an Israelite — "thou mayest not set a stranger over
thee" (v.15). Three prohibitions: (1) "he shall not multiply horses to
himself, nor cause the people to return to Egypt" (v.16) — no military
buildup through Egyptian alliances; (2) "Neither shall he multiply wives
to himself, that his heart turn not away" (v.17) — no political marriages
that introduce foreign gods; (3) "neither shall he greatly multiply to
himself silver and gold" (v.17) — no excessive wealth accumulation. The
king's primary duty: "he shall write him a copy of this law in a book"
(v.18). "He shall read therein all the days of his life" (v.19). Purpose:
"that he may learn to fear the LORD his God" (v.19), "that his heart be
not lifted up above his brethren" (v.20), and "that he turn not aside from
the commandment" (v.20). Promise: "he may prolong his days in his kingdom,
he, and his children" (v.20).

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Moses speaks from the plains of Moab. The future king will rule "in the land which the LORD thy God giveth thee" (v.14) — laws for Canaan's governance.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/deuteronomy-17/
  Guzik emphasizes that the law of the king is a constitutional monarchy
  — the king is under God's law, not above it. He notes that Solomon
  violated all three prohibitions (horses, wives, gold) and suffered the
  consequences. The requirement to copy and read the law daily made the
  king a student of Scripture first and a ruler second.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "The king must write out the law with his own hand and read it every day
  of his life. What a prescription for leadership! Not military strategy,
  not political cunning, not economic theory — but the daily reading of
  God's word. The leader who neglects Scripture will inevitably multiply
  what God forbids. Solomon is the proof: the wisest man who ever lived
  destroyed his kingdom because he stopped listening to God's word."

REFLECTION
------------------------------------
1. No blemished sacrifice (v.1). God deserves our best — not our
   leftovers, not our defective offerings. This applies to time, energy,
   attention, and resources given to God. Offering God what costs us
   nothing is an abomination (2 Samuel 24:24).

2. The two-witness rule (v.6). God builds safeguards into justice. No
   person's life depends on a single accusation. This protects against
   false testimony, personal vendettas, and hasty judgment. Justice
   requires patience and corroboration.

3. The king under the law (vv.18-20). The highest human authority is
   still under God's authority. No leader is above Scripture. The king's
   primary qualification is not charisma or competence but submission to
   God's word. Leaders who neglect Scripture inevitably abuse power.

4. "He shall read therein all the days of his life" (v.19). Daily
   Scripture reading is not optional for leaders — or for any believer.
   The king who stops reading becomes the king who starts multiplying
   what God forbids. Neglect of the word precedes every other failure.

5. "That his heart be not lifted up above his brethren" (v.20). Power
   tends toward pride. The antidote is daily immersion in God's word,
   which reminds the leader that he is a brother, not a lord. Humility
   is maintained by the word, not by willpower.

6. Solomon's warning. Every prohibition in vv.16-17 was violated by
   Solomon: horses from Egypt (1 Kings 10:28), 700 wives and 300
   concubines (1 Kings 11:3), silver and gold beyond measure (1 Kings
   10:14-23). The result: his heart turned away (1 Kings 11:4) and his
   kingdom was torn (1 Kings 11:11). The law of the king is not
   theoretical — it is prophetically precise.
