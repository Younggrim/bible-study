Job - Chapter 6
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Job's first reply to Eliphaz — defends the legitimacy of his anguish, compares his friends to unreliable wadis, and challenges them to show him where he has sinned.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Unknown. Candidates include Job himself, Moses, Solomon, or
an anonymous sage. Likely the oldest book in the Bible by setting
(patriarchal era, approximately 2000-1800 BC), though the date of
composition is debated. The book addresses the problem of innocent
suffering and God's sovereignty. Key themes: Why do the righteous
suffer? Is God just? Can faith survive without answers? The
inadequacy of simplistic theology ('you suffer because you sinned').
God's sovereignty transcends human understanding. True worship says
'Though he slay me, yet will I trust in him' (13:15).

Historical Context: Chapter 6 is Job's first reply to Eliphaz,
continuing through chapter 7. Job does not directly refute
Eliphaz's theology — instead he defends the intensity of his
complaint, expresses his desire for death, and rebukes his friends
for their failure as comforters. The speech is deeply emotional:
Job compares his grief to sand on a scale (immeasurably heavy),
describes God's arrows poisoning his spirit, and compares his
friends to seasonal streams that fail travelers when most needed.
The chapter reveals Job at his most vulnerable — asking not for
theological debate but for simple human kindness. His closing
challenge — "Show me where I have erred" (v.24) — goes unanswered
because the friends cannot actually point to specific sin.

The Weight of Grief (vv.1-7): Job justifies his earlier outburst
(ch. 3): if his grief were weighed, it would be "heavier than the
sand of the sea" (v.3). His words were "rash" (ESV) or "swallowed
up" (KJV) because his pain is incomprehensible. The "arrows of the
Almighty" are in him — God's terrors arrayed against him (v.4). He
uses an animal analogy (v.5): a donkey doesn't bray when it has
grass; an ox doesn't low over fodder — they cry out only when they
lack. Job's cries prove something is genuinely, desperately wrong.
His suffering is not drama — it is proportional response.

The Desire for Death (vv.8-13): Job wishes God would "crush me"
and "cut me off" (v.9). This would be his "comfort" — because even
in death, he would have the consolation of never having "denied the
words of the Holy One" (v.10). He asks: what strength do I have
to keep waiting? "Is my strength the strength of stones? or is my
flesh of brass?" (v.12). He is not superhuman — he is mortal, weak,
and running out of endurance. This is not self-pity but honest
assessment of human limits.

Friends Like Dried-Up Streams (vv.14-21): One of Job's most
powerful images. His friends are like wadis — seasonal streams in
the desert that are full in winter but dry up in summer heat.
Caravans from Tema and Sheba travel toward them expecting water
and find nothing — they "are ashamed because they were confident"
(v.20, ESV). The application is devastating: "For now ye are
nothing; ye see my casting down, and are afraid" (v.21). The
friends have no water to offer when Job needs it most. They are
afraid of his suffering — it threatens their theology.

Challenge to the Friends (vv.22-30): Job has not asked them for
money, rescue, or intervention — only for understanding. "Teach me,
and I will hold my tongue: and cause me to understand wherein I
have erred" (v.24). If they can show specific sin, he will be
silent. But they cannot — they deal in generalities and theory.
"How forcible are right words!" (v.25) — Job acknowledges that
truth has power, but asks: what does their "arguing" actually prove?
He closes with a plea: "Return, I pray you, let it not be
iniquity; yea, return again, my righteousness is in it" (v.29) —
look at me again with fresh eyes; my integrity is at stake.


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2-3 — "Oh that my vexation were weighed, and all my calamity
         laid in the balances! For then it would be heavier than
         the sand of the sea." (ESV) — Job's pain is not
         exaggerated — it is literally immeasurable. The image of
         sand outweighing grief explains why his words seem extreme.
         Proportional grief to proportional suffering.

v.4   — "For the arrows of the Almighty are in me; my spirit
         drinks their poison; the terrors of God are arrayed
         against me." (ESV) — Job attributes his suffering to God,
         not Satan. He experiences God as an
         archer targeting him. This is honest — and the book does
         not condemn him for this perception.

v.14  — "He who withholds kindness from a friend forsakes the fear
         of the Almighty." (ESV) — A profound principle: failing
         to show kindness to a suffering friend is itself a sin
         against God. Compassion is not optional.

v.24  — "Teach me, and I will be silent; make me understand how I
         have gone astray." (ESV) — Job's direct challenge: show
         me my specific sin. The friends never can.
         They speak in generalities because they have no evidence.

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.4   — Psalm 38:2 — "For your arrows have sunk into me, and your
         hand has come down on me." (ESV) — David uses the same
         image of God's arrows — the experience of God as
         adversary.
v.4   — Lamentations 3:12-13 — "He bent his bow and set me as a
         target for his arrow." (ESV) — Jeremiah echoes Job's
         experience.
v.14  — Proverbs 17:17 — "A friend loves at all times, and a
         brother is born for adversity." (ESV) — What Job's friends
         fail to provide.
v.15  — Jeremiah 15:18 — "Why is my pain unceasing, my wound
         incurable, refusing to be healed? Will you be to me like
         a deceitful brook, like waters that fail?" (ESV) —
         Jeremiah uses the same failed-stream image for God's
         apparent unfaithfulness.
v.24  — Job 13:23 — "How many are my iniquities and my sins? Make
         me know my transgression and my sin." (ESV) — Job will
         repeat this demand.
v.29  — Job 27:5-6 — "Till I die I will not put away my integrity
         from me. I hold fast my righteousness." (ESV) — Job
         maintains this position throughout.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/job-6/
  Guzik highlights the wadi metaphor as Job's most devastating
  rebuke of his friends. They appeared substantial — like full
  streams — but when he actually needed them, they had nothing to
  offer. Their theology was seasonal: adequate for fair weather but
  dry in the heat of real crisis. Guzik also notes that v.14 (the
  duty of kindness to the afflicted) is a rebuke that applies to
  the church today — many suffering people are failed not by God
  but by God's people who offer theology when they should offer
  tears.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "Job asked his friends one simple question: 'Show me where I
  have erred.' They could not do it. They had theories, systems,
  traditions, and night visions — but they could not point to a
  single specific sin that Job had committed. When you accuse a
  man, you must have evidence. General suspicion is not conviction.
  The friends condemned Job on principle rather than proof, and for
  this God later condemned them."
TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.3  — KJV: "my words are swallowed up" / ESV: "my words have
       been rash" — Hebrew "la'u" is difficult. KJV suggests his
       words fail him — he cannot adequately express his pain. ESV
       suggests his words were hasty/rash because of overwhelming
       grief. Both interpretations fit the context: extreme pain
       produces extreme speech.

v.6  — KJV: "the white of an egg" / ESV: "the juice of the
       mallow" — Hebrew "rir chalamuth" is uncertain. KJV follows
       Talmudic interpretation (egg white). ESV follows botanical
       interpretation (a tasteless plant sap). Both convey the
       same point: something flavorless and unappetizing — like the
       friends' empty words.

v.10 — KJV: "I have not concealed the words of the Holy One" /
       ESV: "I have not denied the words of the Holy One" — Hebrew
       "kichad" = to hide, deny, disown. Job's comfort in death
       would be this: he never denied or concealed God's truth.
       His integrity remained even in his desire to die.

v.14 — KJV: "To him that is afflicted pity should be shewed from
       his friend; but he forsaketh the fear of the Almighty." /
       ESV: "He who withholds kindness from a friend forsakes the
       fear of the Almighty." — The Hebrew is grammatically
       difficult. ESV reverses the subject: the one who fails to
       show kindness is the one who forsakes God. KJV could be read
       as Job forsaking God — but ESV's reading better fits the
       context: the friends fail Job, and in doing so, fail God.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Wadi          — A seasonal streambed in the desert, full during
              winter rains but completely dry in summer. Job uses
              this image (vv.15-20) to describe friends who are
              present in prosperity but absent in crisis.

Tema          — (v.19) A region in northwestern Arabia, associated
              with Ishmael's son Tema (Genesis 25:15). Caravans
              from Tema would travel desert routes depending on
              water sources — finding a dry wadi meant possible
              death.

Sheba         — (v.19) A region in southwestern Arabia (modern
              Yemen), famous for trade caravans. The "travelers of
              Sheba" were experienced merchants who knew the desert
              — even they were deceived by empty wadis.

Arrows of     — (v.4) A metaphor for divinely permitted suffering
the Almighty    that penetrates deep and poisons the spirit. Job
              attributes his pain to God's direct action — the
              arrows are not Satan's but God's (from Job's
              perspective).

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Grief has weight, and it deserves acknowledgment (vv.2-3).
   Job does not apologize for the intensity of his complaint —
   he explains it. When someone expresses extreme pain, do not
   minimize it. Ask: what is the weight of sand they are carrying?
   Proportional pain produces proportional cries. Honor that.

2. Friends fail when suffering frightens them (v.21). "Ye see my
   casting down, and are afraid." The friends are scared by Job's
   condition — it threatens their worldview. Many people abandon
   the suffering not from cruelty but from fear. They cannot face
   what they cannot explain. Be the friend who stays when it
   scares you.

3. Kindness to the afflicted is not optional (v.14). Withholding
   compassion from a suffering person is not neutrality — it is
   a failure in godliness. The ESV reading makes this explicit:
   to withhold kindness "forsakes the fear of the Almighty."
   How you treat the suffering reveals your relationship with God.

4. Demand evidence before accepting accusations (v.24). Job's
   challenge stands: show me the specific sin. Vague accusations
   of unworthiness are worthless. If you are being accused, ask
   for specifics. If they cannot provide them, their accusation
   has no substance. Resist the temptation to accept guilt you
   have not earned.

5. The duty of honest reassessment (v.29). "Return... my
   righteousness is in it." Job asks his friends to look again —
   to reconsider. Are you willing to reconsider a judgment you've
   already made about someone? Humility means being willing to
   say "I was wrong about you."
