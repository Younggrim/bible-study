Job - Chapter 19
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Job's reply to Bildad's second speech — describes his total alienation from family and friends, then reaches the book's climactic declaration of faith: 'I know that my redeemer liveth.'

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Unknown. Candidates include Job himself, Moses, Solomon, or
an anonymous sage. Likely the oldest book in the Bible by setting
(patriarchal era, approximately 2000-1800 BC), though the date of
composition is debated. The book addresses the problem of innocent
suffering and God's sovereignty. Key themes: Why do the righteous
suffer? Is God just? Can faith survive without answers? The
inadequacy of simplistic theology ('you suffer because you sinned').
God's sovereignty transcends human understanding. True worship says
'Though he slay me, yet will I trust in him' (13:15).

Historical Context: Chapter 19 is the theological summit of Job's
speeches and contains what is arguably the most remarkable
declaration of faith in the entire Old Testament: "I know that my
redeemer liveth" (v.25). The chapter moves from Job's deepest
isolation — abandoned by God, friends, family, and servants — to
his highest faith. The contrast is intentional: precisely when Job
has lost every human support, his faith reaches its apex. The
progression of Job's mediator-theology reaches its climax here:
daysman/arbiter (9:33), witness in heaven (16:19), and now REDEEMER
who lives and will stand upon the earth (19:25). This is not a
gradual improvement in Job's mood — it is a sudden eruption of
faith from the deepest possible despair. The chapter structure
mirrors the gospel: total loss, total abandonment, then
resurrection hope.

Job's Complaint Against Friends (vv.1-6): "How long will ye vex
my soul, and break me in pieces with words?" (v.2). Their words
are not neutral — they actively destroy. "These ten times have ye
reproached me" (v.3) — Job feels the cumulative weight of their
accusations. Then his bold claim: "Know now that God hath overthrown
me, and hath compassed me with his net" (v.6). Job directly states:
GOD has done this. Not sin, not Satan (from Job's perspective), not
natural causes — God. This is not blasphemy but Job's honest
theology of sovereignty.

Total Abandonment (vv.7-22): Job catalogues his complete isolation.
God has "fenced up my way" and "set darkness in my paths" (v.8),
"stripped me of my glory" (v.9), "destroyed me on every side" (v.10),
"kindled his wrath against me" (v.11). Then the human abandonment:
brothers estranged (v.13), relatives failed (v.14), household
servants treat him as a stranger (v.15), his own servant will not
respond (v.16), his breath is offensive to his wife (v.17), even
young children despise him (v.18), his intimate friends abhor him
(v.19). He is reduced to "skin and bones" — "escaped with the skin
of my teeth" (v.20). Then the desperate cry: "Have pity upon me,
have pity upon me, O ye my friends; for the hand of God hath
touched me" (v.21). This is Job at his most vulnerable — begging
for basic human compassion.

"I Know That My Redeemer Liveth" (vv.23-27): From the absolute
bottom, faith erupts. Job first wishes his words were permanently
recorded — written, inscribed, engraved in rock forever (vv.23-24).
Why? Because what follows is so important it must outlast him. Then
the declaration: "For I know that my redeemer liveth, and that he
shall stand at the latter day upon the earth: And though after my
skin worms destroy this body, yet in my flesh shall I see God: Whom
I shall see for myself, and mine eyes shall behold, and not another"
(vv.25-27). Job affirms: (1) his Redeemer is alive, (2) this
Redeemer will stand on earth, (3) even after bodily death/
destruction, Job will see God, (4) he will see God personally —
"for myself" — not through another. This is resurrection faith.
"My reins be consumed within me" (v.27) — the longing is so
intense it consumes him physically.

Warning to the Friends (vv.28-29): Job warns his friends: if you
continue pursuing me, "be ye afraid of the sword: for wrath
bringeth the punishments of the sword, that ye may know there is a
judgment" (v.29). The friends should fear God's judgment — not Job.
There IS a judgment coming, and it will vindicate Job and condemn
his accusers. (This is fulfilled in 42:7-8.)


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.6  — "Know then that God has put me in the wrong and closed his
        net about me." (ESV) — Job's unflinching attribution of his
        suffering to God. He does not blame secondary causes. God is
        the primary agent — and Job still worships. This is more
        honest than many theological systems allow.

v.20 — "My bones stick to my skin and to my flesh, and I have
        escaped by the skin of my teeth." (ESV) — The origin of
        the English idiom "by the skin of my teeth." Job is barely
        alive — reduced to skeleton and suffering. From THIS
        condition, he declares faith in a living Redeemer.

v.25 — "For I know that my Redeemer lives, and at the last he
        will stand upon the earth." (ESV) — The greatest
        declaration of faith in the Old Testament. "I KNOW" — not
        hope, not wish, but certainty. "MY Redeemer" — personal,
        possessive. "LIVES" — present tense, currently alive.
        "Will stand upon the earth" — eschatological: the Redeemer
        will appear on earth.

v.26 — "And after my skin has been thus destroyed, yet in my flesh
        I shall see God." (ESV) — Resurrection faith. After bodily
        death and decomposition, Job expects to see God in embodied
        existence. This is not survival of the soul but resurrection
        of the body.

v.27 — "Whom I shall see for myself, and my eyes shall behold,
        and not another. My heart faints within me!" (ESV) — Three
        emphases: for MYSELF (personal), MY eyes (embodied sight),
        not ANOTHER (unmediated). Job will see God face-to-face,
        personally, in his own body.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/job-19/
  Guzik identifies vv.25-27 as the climax of Job's entire
  theological journey. The progression from arbiter (9:33) to
  witness (16:19) to Redeemer (19:25) shows faith growing through
  suffering, not merely surviving it. Guzik notes the Hebrew "goel"
  (redeemer/kinsman-redeemer) implies one who has the RIGHT and
  OBLIGATION to redeem — like Boaz for Ruth. Job's Redeemer is not
  merely willing but obligated by relationship to vindicate him.
  Guzik also emphasizes the physicality of v.26: "in my flesh I
  shall see God" — this is bodily resurrection, not disembodied
  spiritual existence.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "'I know that my redeemer liveth!' Out of the depths comes the
  greatest confession of faith ever uttered by mortal man. Note
  the circumstances: abandoned by friends, cursed by his wife,
  covered in sores, scraping himself with pottery in an ash heap,
  told by his closest companions that God is punishing him for
  hidden sin — and from THIS position he declares: 'I KNOW that
  my Redeemer LIVETH.' Not 'I hope' or 'I think' or 'perhaps' —
  but I KNOW. This is faith that has been driven down to bedrock
  and found it solid. Strip away everything else and faith remains
  — not as a luxury but as the last and most essential thing."
TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.20 — KJV: "I am escaped with the skin of my teeth" / ESV: "I
       have escaped by the skin of my teeth" — The origin of the
       English proverb. Teeth have no skin — the point is: I have
       barely escaped with nothing. The margin between life and
       death is thinner than non-existent skin on teeth.

v.25 — KJV: "my redeemer" / ESV: "my Redeemer" — Hebrew "go'el" =
       kinsman-redeemer. In Israelite law, the go'el was a close
       relative obligated to buy back family property, avenge blood,
       or redeem from slavery (Leviticus 25:25; Numbers 35:19;
       Ruth 3:9). Job claims a cosmic Kinsman-Redeemer who will
       vindicate him. ESV capitalizes, recognizing the messianic
       significance.

v.26 — KJV: "though after my skin worms destroy this body" / ESV:
       "after my skin has been thus destroyed" — The KJV adds
       "worms" (italicized, meaning not in Hebrew). The Hebrew is
       simply: "after my skin is destroyed/struck off." ESV is more
       literal. Both affirm: after bodily death/destruction, Job
       expects embodied vision of God. The ambiguity of the Hebrew
       allows both "from within my flesh" (resurrected body) and
       "apart from my flesh" (disembodied). Context favors
       embodied resurrection given v.27's emphasis on personal
       sight.

v.27 — KJV: "though my reins be consumed within me" / ESV: "My
       heart faints within me!" — Hebrew "kalah kilyotay becheqi"
       = my kidneys (seat of emotion) are consumed within me. KJV
       preserves the literal organ; ESV interprets as emotional
       overwhelm. The point: this hope so overwhelms Job that his
       inner being is consumed with longing.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Redeemer      — Hebrew "go'el" — kinsman-redeemer. In Israelite
(Go'el)         law: (1) one who buys back family property sold in
              hardship (Leviticus 25:25), (2) one who avenges a
              murdered relative (Numbers 35:19), (3) one who
              redeems from slavery. Job's "go'el" is God Himself —
              or a divine figure who will stand on earth, vindicate
              Job, and enable him to see God face-to-face.

Skin of       — (v.20) A proverbial expression meaning the
My Teeth        narrowest possible margin of survival. Since teeth
              have no skin, the point is: Job has escaped death by
              an impossibly thin margin — he is barely alive.

Latter Day    — (v.25, KJV) Hebrew "acharon" = last, final. "At
              the latter day" suggests an eschatological time — the
              end of things, when God will make all things right.
              The Redeemer's appearance is future and climactic.

Stand Upon    — (v.25) Hebrew "qum al-aphar" = arise/stand upon
the Earth       the dust. The Redeemer will physically stand on
              earth. This is incarnation/return language: God's
              representative will be physically present, not merely
              spiritually active.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. "I know that my redeemer liveth" (v.25). This is the sentence
   that justifies the entire book. From total loss, total
   isolation, total pain — faith declares what it cannot see. Do
   you know this? Not "do you hope" or "do you believe in theory"
   — do you KNOW that your Redeemer lives? This is the faith that
   survives everything.

2. Faith reaches its highest point in the lowest place (vv.20-25).
   It is from the ash heap, not the palace, that Job makes his
   greatest declaration. You do not need favorable circumstances
   to possess the deepest faith. Sometimes the worst external
   conditions produce the most authentic internal conviction.

3. You will see God for yourself (v.27). Not through a mediator's
   report, not through theological abstraction, not secondhand —
   "mine eyes shall behold, and not another." You will personally,
   bodily, directly see God. This is the Christian hope: face-to-
   face communion. Everything between now and then is temporary.

4. Total human abandonment does not equal divine abandonment
   (vv.13-22). Every human relationship failed Job. Wife, brothers,
   servants, friends, children of his household — all gone. But
   the Redeemer lives. When every earthly support collapses, the
   vertical relationship remains. Loneliness is painful, but it is
   not the same as being forsaken by God.

5. Record your faith declarations (vv.23-24). Job wanted his words
   written in rock permanently. Why? Because declarations of faith
   made in darkness are worth preserving. When you have a moment of
   clarity in suffering — write it down. You will need it later when
   the darkness returns. Job's words WERE preserved — and they have
   sustained millions for three thousand years.
