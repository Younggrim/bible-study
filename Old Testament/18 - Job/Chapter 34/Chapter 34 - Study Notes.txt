Job - Chapter 34
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Elihu's second speech — defends God's justice, arguing it is unthinkable that the Almighty would pervert justice since He governs all nations impartially and sees all human deeds without need of investigation.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Unknown (see Chapter 1 notes for full discussion).

Historical Context: Chapter 34 is Elihu's second speech, and
here he turns to defend God's justice directly. Elihu addresses
"wise men" and "men of understanding" — possibly the three
friends or a broader audience. His argument centers on one
assertion: "Far be it from God, that he should do wickedness;
and from the Almighty, that he should commit iniquity" (v.10).
God repays according to works (v.11), does not pervert justice
(v.12), sees all (v.21), and is no respecter of persons (v.19).
Elihu's defense of God's justice is stronger than the three
friends' because he does not ground it in observable retribution
but in God's nature itself. God is just because God IS just —
not because we can always see justice operating.

Elihu Quotes Job (vv.5-9): Elihu paraphrases Job's claims: "I am
righteous: and God hath taken away my judgment" (v.5); "It
profiteth a man nothing that he should delight himself with God"
(v.9). These are fair, if reductive, summaries of Job's
positions. Elihu treats these as dangerous conclusions that must
be refuted.

God's Justice Defended (vv.10-15): God repays each according to
his ways (v.11). He cannot do wickedly (v.12). No one appointed
Him over the earth — He is self-authorized (v.13). If He withdrew
His breath, all flesh would perish (vv.14-15). God's justice is
inherent, not delegated. He answers to no one because there is
no one above Him.

God's Sovereignty Over All (vv.16-30): God governs nations and
individuals alike (v.29). He shows no partiality to princes or
the rich (v.19) because "they all are the work of his hands"
(v.19). He sees all — no darkness can hide the wicked (v.22). He
judges the mighty "without number" (v.24). His purpose: "That
the hypocrite reign not, lest the people be ensnared" (v.30).

Elihu's Harsh Conclusion (vv.34-37): Elihu concludes harshly:
Job "speaks without knowledge" (v.35), should be "tried unto the
end" (v.36), and "adds rebellion unto his sin" (v.37). This goes
too far — Elihu moves from defending God to attacking Job. His
desire that Job be tested further seems cruel and exceeds his
earlier empathy.


KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.10 — "Therefore, hear me, you men of understanding: far be it
        from God that he should do wickedness, and from the
        Almighty that he should do wrong." (ESV) — Elihu's central
        thesis: God's nature makes injustice impossible. This is a
        stronger argument than the friends' because it appeals to
        God's character rather than observable outcomes.

v.11 — "For according to the work of a man he will repay him, and
        according to his ways he will make it befall him." (ESV) —
        God does repay — but Elihu, unlike the friends, does not
        claim this repayment is always immediate or visible.

v.19 — "Who shows no partiality to princes, nor regards the rich
        more than the poor, for they are all the work of his
        hands." (ESV) — God's impartiality grounded in His role as
        Creator. All humans are His handiwork — none receives
        preferential treatment.

v.29 — "When he is quiet, who can condemn? When he hides his face,
        who can behold him, whether it be a nation or a man?"
        (ESV) — God's sovereignty in both giving peace and
        withholding His presence. No one can override either
        action.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/job-34/
  Guzik notes that Elihu's defense of God's justice (vv.10-12)
  is theologically sound and avoids the simplistic retribution
  logic of the three friends. However, his conclusion (vv.34-37)
  goes too far — wanting Job "tried unto the end" reveals a
  punitive streak that undermines his earlier compassion. Guzik
  observes that v.9 is a misquotation of Job: Job never said it
  profits nothing to delight in God — he said his righteous
  conduct had not been rewarded. The distinction matters.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "Elihu stands on solid ground when he declares God cannot do
  wickedness. This is not a hope — it is a certainty grounded in
  God's nature. Unlike the friends, Elihu does not argue backward
  from Job's suffering to Job's sin. He argues forward from God's
  character to God's trustworthiness. The logic is right: if God
  IS just, then even when we CANNOT SEE justice operating, we can
  trust it exists. But Elihu falls when he becomes prosecutor
  rather than theologian — when he desires Job's further testing
  rather than Job's restoration."
TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.10 — KJV: "far be it from God" / ESV: "far be it from God" —
       Hebrew "chalilah" — the same strong rejection used by Job
       in 27:5 ("God forbid"). Elihu uses Job's own language to
       make the opposite point.

v.14 — KJV: "gather unto himself his spirit and his breath" / ESV:
       "gather to himself his spirit and his breath" — If God
       withdrew the life-breath He loaned to creation, all would
       die instantly. Creation's existence depends moment by
       moment on God's sustaining will.

v.36 — KJV: "tried unto the end" / ESV: "tried to the end" —
       Hebrew "yibbachen" = tested/examined. Elihu wants Job's
       testing prolonged — a harsh wish that seems to lack
       compassion. This is arguably Elihu's worst moment.

v.37 — KJV: "clappeth his hands among us" / ESV: "claps his hands
       among us" — A gesture of mockery or defiance. Elihu accuses
       Job of adding arrogant rebellion to whatever sin originally
       caused his suffering.

v.10 — ASV: "wickedness, And" for KJV's "wickedness; and"
       NET: "“Therefore, listen to" for KJV's "Therefore hearken unto"
       WEB: "“Therefore listen to" for KJV's "Therefore hearken unto"
v.14 — ASV: "himself, [If]" for KJV's "man, if"
       NET: "on it, and" for KJV's "upon man, if he"
       WEB: "on himself, If" for KJV's "upon man, if"
v.36 — ASV: "answering like" for KJV's "answers for"
       NET: "tested to" for KJV's "tried unto"
       WEB: "answering like" for KJV's "answers for"
v.37 — ASV: "sin; He" for KJV's "sin, he"

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Respect of   — Hebrew "nasa panim" = to lift the face (of another),
persons        meaning to show favoritism based on status or
             appearance. God does NOT do this — He judges all
             equally regardless of wealth, power, or position.

Without hand — v.20: "the mighty shall be taken away without hand"
             — removed by God's direct action, not human agency.
             God's judgment needs no human instrument (cf. Daniel
             2:34, the stone "cut without hands").

Rebellion    — Hebrew "pesha" = revolt, transgression, breach of
             trust. Elihu's accusation in v.37 is that Job's
             continued protest constitutes rebellion against God —
             a charge stronger than the friends made.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. God's justice is grounded in His nature, not in our observation
   (v.10). We cannot always SEE justice operating, but we can trust
   it EXISTS because God cannot be unjust. Faith trusts God's
   character when God's actions are unclear.

2. All humanity is equally God's creation (v.19). Rich and poor,
   powerful and weak — "they all are the work of his hands." This
   is the ultimate basis for human equality and impartial justice.
   If God shows no favoritism, neither should His people.

3. God sustains existence moment by moment (vv.14-15). Every breath
   is borrowed. If God withdrew His sustaining power for one
   instant, all life would cease. Gratitude, not complaint, should
   be our default posture — we exist by God's continuous choice.

4. Defending God can become attacking people (vv.36-37). Elihu
   starts by defending God's justice (good) and ends by wishing
   prolonged suffering on Job (bad). Zeal for God's honor can
   become cruelty toward God's people if it loses compassion.
   Defend truth without destroying people.
