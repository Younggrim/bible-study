1 Samuel - Chapter 2
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Hannah prays a prophetic song of praise, celebrating God who lifts the poor and brings down the proud. Meanwhile, Eli's wicked sons abuse their priestly office and despise the offerings of the LORD.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Unknown, traditionally attributed to Samuel (for the early
chapters), Nathan, and Gad (1 Chronicles 29:29). Originally one book
with 2 Samuel in the Hebrew Bible. Written approximately 1050-950 BC.
The book covers the transition from the judges to the monarchy — from
the birth of Samuel through the death of Saul (approximately 1105-1010
BC). Key themes: God's sovereignty in raising and removing leaders, the
danger of rejecting God's rule, the contrast between Saul (man's choice)
and David (God's choice), and the principle that 'man looketh on the
outward appearance, but the LORD looketh on the heart' (16:7).

Historical Context: Chapter 2 presents one of Scripture's sharpest
contrasts — Hannah's song of praise alongside the corruption of Eli's
sons. Hannah's prayer (vv.1-10) is a prophetic hymn celebrating God's
power to reverse human circumstances: the barren bears, the hungry are
fed, the poor are exalted. It anticipates Mary's Magnificat (Luke
1:46-55) in both structure and theology. The chapter then exposes the
wickedness of Hophni and Phinehas — priests who steal from sacrifices,
sleep with women at the tabernacle entrance, and treat God's offerings
with contempt. Their sin is not merely personal immorality but
institutional corruption: they cause the people to "abhor the offering
of the LORD" (v.17). Eli's rebuke is too little, too late — he
"honoured his sons above" God (v.29). The unnamed "man of God" delivers
a devastating oracle: Eli's priestly line will be cut off, his sons
will die on the same day, and God will raise up "a faithful priest"
(v.35). This prophecy finds partial fulfillment in Samuel, further
fulfillment in Zadok (who replaces Eli's line under Solomon), and
ultimate fulfillment in Christ. Meanwhile, young Samuel "grew before
the LORD" (v.21) — a quiet counterpoint to the noise of corruption.
The chapter establishes the principle that governs the entire book:
"them that honour me I will honour, and they that despise me shall be
lightly esteemed" (v.30).

Hannah's Song of Praise (vv.1-10): This is not a simple thank-you for
a baby — it is a theological declaration about God's character. Hannah
moves from personal experience to cosmic truth. Her "horn is exalted"
(v.1) — horn symbolizes strength and dignity. She declares God's
holiness (v.2), His omniscience (v.3), and His sovereign power to
reverse all human conditions (vv.4-8). The mighty are broken; the weak
are strengthened. The full go hungry; the hungry are satisfied. The
barren bears seven; the fruitful is forlorn. God kills and makes alive,
impoverishes and enriches, humbles and exalts. Verse 8 reaches the
theological summit: God raises the poor from the dust to sit with
princes — "for the pillars of the earth are the LORD's." Verse 10
introduces the concept of God's "anointed" (mashiach/messiah) — the
first reference to a king in this book, spoken before Israel has a
king. Hannah prophesies what she cannot yet see: God will establish a
king and exalt his horn. This points forward to David and ultimately
to Christ.

The Sins of Eli's Sons (vv.12-17): "Sons of Belial" (KJV) / "worthless
men" (ESV) — they "knew not the LORD" (v.12). This is not ignorance
but willful rejection. Their sins are specific: (1) They took meat from
sacrifices before the fat was burned to God — the fat belonged to the
LORD (Leviticus 3:16-17), and taking it first was stealing from God.
(2) They demanded raw meat instead of boiled — wanting choice cuts for
roasting rather than accepting the priest's legitimate portion. (3)
They used force if refused (v.16). The result: "men abhorred the
offering of the LORD" (v.17). Corrupt leadership does not merely sin
privately — it destroys the people's relationship with God. When
priests make worship repulsive, the people stop coming.

Samuel's Growth and Eli's Failure (vv.18-26): The contrast is
deliberate. Samuel ministers faithfully in a linen ephod — the garment
of priestly service. Hannah brings him a "little coat" yearly — a
mother's love expressed in practical care despite the separation. Eli
blesses Elkanah and Hannah, and God gives them five more children
(v.21). Meanwhile, Eli hears of his sons' sexual sin with women at the
tabernacle (v.22). His rebuke (vv.23-25) is verbal but toothless — he
does not remove them from office. Verse 25 is sobering: "they hearkened
not... because the LORD would slay them." God's judicial hardening has
begun. Verse 26 echoes Luke 2:52 — Samuel grows "in favour both with
the LORD, and also with men," just as Jesus would.

The Prophecy Against Eli's House (vv.27-36): An unnamed "man of God"
delivers God's indictment. The charges: (1) God chose Eli's house for
priestly service and gave them the fire offerings (vv.27-28). (2) They
have "kicked at" God's sacrifices and honored sons above God (v.29).
The sentence: (1) God revokes His promise — "I said indeed... for ever:
but now the LORD saith, Be it far from me" (v.30). God's promises are
conditional on faithfulness. (2) Eli's house will be cut off — no old
men will survive (v.31). (3) Hophni and Phinehas will die on the same
day as a sign (v.34). (4) God will raise a "faithful priest" (v.35).
The governing principle is stated in v.30: "them that honour me I will
honour, and they that despise me shall be lightly esteemed."

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Shiloh: The tabernacle site where Samuel ministers and Eli's corrupt sons serve — in the hill country of Ephraim
- The "door of the tabernacle" (v.22): Where Eli sits and where the wickedness of Hophni and Phinehas occurs
- The annual circuit: Israelites travel to Shiloh from across the nation to worship and sacrifice

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/1-samuel-2/
  Guzik highlights Hannah's song as prophetic — she speaks of God's
  "anointed" before Israel has a king. He notes that Eli's failure was
  not ignorance but indulgence: he knew his sons' sins but honored them
  above God by refusing to remove them from service.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "Hannah's song teaches us that God delights in reversals. He takes
  the empty and fills them, the low and lifts them, the broken and
  strengthens them. But He also takes the full and empties them, the
  proud and humbles them, the strong and breaks them. Let no man trust
  in his present condition, for God is able to change it in a moment.
  And let no man despair of his present condition, for the same God who
  brings down is the God who lifts up."
PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Praise should be theological, not merely emotional (vv.1-10).
   Hannah's song moves from personal gratitude to cosmic truth about
   God's character. Our worship should do the same — not merely
   expressing how we feel but declaring who God is. The best praise
   is rooted in theology: God's holiness, sovereignty, justice, and
   faithfulness.

2. Corrupt leadership destroys more than the leaders themselves (v.17).
   Hophni and Phinehas did not merely sin — they caused others to
   "abhor the offering of the LORD." Leaders bear responsibility not
   only for their own souls but for the spiritual damage their sin
   inflicts on others. This is why James warns: "be not many masters,
   knowing that we shall receive the greater condemnation" (James 3:1).

3. Honoring children above God is a form of idolatry (v.29). Eli's
   fatal failure was not ignorance but misplaced loyalty — he honored
   his sons above God. When family loyalty overrides obedience to God,
   it becomes idolatry. God demands first place, even above our
   deepest human attachments (Matthew 10:37).

4. God's promises are conditional on faithfulness (v.30). "I said
   indeed... for ever: but now the LORD saith, Be it far from me."
   God revoked His promise to Eli's house because they despised Him.
   Privilege does not guarantee permanence. Position does not exempt
   from judgment. Only those who honor God will be honored by God.

5. God always raises up faithful servants (v.35). When leadership
   fails, God does not abandon His people — He raises up replacements.
   Samuel replaces Eli. David replaces Saul. Christ replaces all
   failed priests. God's purposes are never ultimately thwarted by
   human unfaithfulness.
