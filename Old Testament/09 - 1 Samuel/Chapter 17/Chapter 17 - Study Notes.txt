1 Samuel - Chapter 17
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
The young shepherd David defeats the giant Goliath with a sling and a stone, fighting in the name of the LORD of hosts. All Israel sees that the battle belongs to God, not to armor or military experience.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Unknown, traditionally attributed to Samuel (for the early
chapters), Nathan, and Gad (1 Chronicles 29:29). Originally one book
with 2 Samuel in the Hebrew Bible. Written approximately 1050-950 BC.
The book covers the transition from the judges to the monarchy — from
the birth of Samuel through the death of Saul (approximately 1105-1010
BC). Key themes: God's sovereignty in raising and removing leaders, the
danger of rejecting God's rule, the contrast between Saul (man's choice)
and David (God's choice), and the principle that 'man looketh on the
outward appearance, but the LORD looketh on the heart' (16:7).

Historical Context: Chapter 17 is the most famous chapter in 1 Samuel
— David and Goliath. This is the public demonstration of what chapter
16 established privately: God's Spirit is upon David, and God fights
for those who trust Him. The Philistines and Israel face each other
across the Valley of Elah, with a champion-combat challenge issued by
Goliath of Gath — a giant over nine feet tall, armed with massive
bronze weaponry. For forty days, morning and evening, Goliath defies
Israel and Israel's God. No one in Saul's army will face him — not
even Saul, who stands head and shoulders above all Israel (9:2) and
was supposed to be their warrior-king. The theological irony is
devastating: Israel asked for a king "like all the nations" to fight
their battles (8:20), and now that king cowers in his tent. Into this
vacuum steps David — the youngest son, a shepherd boy, sent by his
father with bread and cheese. David's response to Goliath is not
military bravado but theological outrage: "Who is this uncircumcised
Philistine, that he should defy the armies of the living God?" (v.26).
David sees what no one else sees: this is not a military problem but a
spiritual one. Goliath has defied God, and God will answer. David's
declaration to Goliath is the chapter's theological center: "Thou
comest to me with a sword, and with a spear, and with a shield: but I
come to thee in the name of the LORD of hosts" (v.45). The battle is
not between David and Goliath — it is between the LORD of hosts and
the gods of Philistia. David prevails with a sling and a stone — "but
there was no sword in the hand of David" (v.50). God wins with
weakness, not strength. This is the pattern of the gospel: God's power
made perfect in human weakness (2 Corinthians 12:9).

Goliath's Challenge (vv.1-11): The Philistines gather at Socoh (KJV:
Shochoh) in Judah's territory — an aggressive incursion. The armies
face each other across the Valley of Elah, neither willing to attack
across the valley floor. Goliath emerges as the Philistine champion —
"six cubits and a span" (approximately 9 feet 9 inches). His armor is
described in extraordinary detail (vv.5-7): bronze helmet, coat of
mail weighing 5,000 shekels (about 125 pounds), bronze greaves, bronze
javelin, spear shaft like a weaver's beam with a 600-shekel iron head
(about 15 pounds). He is a walking fortress. His challenge is
representative combat: one champion from each side, winner takes all
(vv.8-9). This was an ancient Near Eastern practice — reducing
casualties by letting champions decide the outcome. Goliath's defiance
is directed at Israel's God: "I defy the armies of Israel" (v.10).
Saul and all Israel are "dismayed, and greatly afraid" (v.11). The
king who was supposed to fight for Israel cannot face this challenge.

David Arrives (vv.12-30): The narrative reintroduces David as Jesse's
youngest son, going back and forth between Saul's court and his
father's sheep (v.15). Jesse sends David with provisions for his
brothers — an errand that places him at the battlefield by divine
providence. David arrives as Goliath issues his challenge (v.23) and
witnesses Israel's fear. He hears about the reward: riches, the king's
daughter, and tax exemption for his family (v.25). David's response
reveals his heart: "Who is this uncircumcised Philistine, that he
should defy the armies of the living God?" (v.26). He does not ask
about the reward first — he is offended at the blasphemy. Eliab, his
eldest brother, attacks David with contempt: "I know thy pride, and
the naughtiness of thine heart" (v.28 KJV). This echoes the pattern
of chapter 16 — the older brothers who look the part but lack the
heart. David's response is measured: "What have I now done? Is there
not a cause?" (v.29 KJV).

David Before Saul (vv.31-40): David volunteers: "Let no man's heart
fail because of him; thy servant will go and fight" (v.32). Saul
objects: "Thou art but a youth, and he a man of war from his youth"
(v.33). David's argument is theological, not military: God delivered
him from the lion and the bear while keeping sheep — the same God will
deliver him from this Philistine (vv.34-37). David's past faithfulness
in obscurity prepared him for this public moment. Saul offers his
armor (vv.38-39) — the king's armor on the shepherd boy. David cannot
use it: "I have not proved them" (v.39). He must fight with what God
has given him, not with borrowed equipment. He takes his staff, five
smooth stones from the brook, his shepherd's bag, and his sling (v.40).
Why five stones? Perhaps for Goliath and his four brothers (2 Samuel
21:22), or simply prudent preparation. The point is: David goes with
God's weapons, not man's.

The Confrontation (vv.41-47): Goliath disdains David — "he was but a
youth, and ruddy, and of a fair countenance" (v.42). He curses David
by his gods and threatens to feed his flesh to the birds (vv.43-44).
David's response is one of the greatest declarations of faith in
Scripture: "Thou comest to me with a sword, and with a spear, and with
a shield: but I come to thee in the name of the LORD of hosts, the God
of the armies of Israel, whom thou hast defied" (v.45). David then
prophesies the outcome: "This day will the LORD deliver thee into mine
hand" (v.46). The purpose is not David's glory but God's: "that all
the earth may know that there is a God in Israel" (v.46). The
theological summary: "the LORD saveth not with sword and spear: for
the battle is the LORD's" (v.47).

The Victory (vv.48-58): David runs toward Goliath — not away, but
toward the giant. He slings a single stone that strikes Goliath's
forehead and sinks in. The giant falls face-down. David then takes
Goliath's own sword and cuts off his head (v.51). The Philistines flee;
Israel pursues them to Gath and Ekron. The chapter ends with Saul
asking whose son David is (vv.55-58) — a question that has puzzled
commentators given chapter 16. Most likely Saul wants to know David's
family lineage for the promised reward (marriage to his daughter and
family tax exemption), or the evil spirit has clouded his memory.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Valley of Elah: A broad valley in the Shephelah (western foothills) between Israelite and Philistine territory
- Socoh and Azekah: Philistine-held towns on the south and north sides of the valley
- The brook: Between the two armies — where David selects five smooth stones before crossing to face Goliath

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.26 — "Who is this uncircumcised Philistine, that he should defy the
        armies of the living God?" — David's first words about Goliath
        reveal his perspective. Where others see a military threat,
        David sees a theological offense. The issue is not Goliath's
        size but his blasphemy. David's faith begins with a right view
        of God: if God is the living God, then no uncircumcised pagan
        can stand against His armies.

v.37 — "The LORD who delivered me from the paw of the lion and from the
        paw of the bear will deliver me from the hand of this
        Philistine." (ESV) — David's faith is built on experience. He
        reasons from past deliverance to present confidence. God's
        faithfulness yesterday is the ground of trust today. This is how
        faith grows: remembering what God has done.

v.45 — "You come to me with a sword and with a spear and with a javelin,
        but I come to you in the name of the LORD of hosts, the God of
        the armies of Israel, whom you have defied." (ESV) — The chapter's
        defining verse. Two combatants, two arsenals: Goliath trusts in
        visible weapons; David trusts in the invisible God. "The name of
        the LORD of hosts" = the character, authority, and power of the
        God who commands heaven's armies. This is the ultimate contrast
        between human strength and divine power.

v.47 — "For the battle is the LORD's, and he will give you into our
        hand." (ESV) — The theological summary of the entire chapter. The
        battle belongs to God — not to the strongest warrior, not to the
        best-equipped army, not to human strategy. God fights for His
        people, and He wins with whatever instrument He chooses — even a
        shepherd's sling.

v.50 — "So David prevailed over the Philistine with a sling and with a
        stone... but there was no sword in the hand of David." — The
        narrator emphasizes the absence of conventional weapons. David
        won without a sword. God's victories do not require human
        power. The weakness of the instrument magnifies the power of
        God.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/1-samuel-17/
  Guzik emphasizes that David's faith was not reckless presumption but
  was built on past experience with God. He had proven God faithful in
  private (with the lion and bear) before he trusted God in public
  (with Goliath). Guzik also notes that David refused Saul's armor
  because he had to fight with what God had given him, not with
  borrowed equipment.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "David did not say, 'I come to thee with a sling and a stone,' but
  'I come to thee in the name of the LORD of hosts.' The sling was
  nothing; the stone was nothing; but the name of the LORD of hosts
  was everything. So it is with us: our weapons are nothing in
  themselves, but when wielded in the name of the Lord, they become
  almighty. Let us never go forth to any conflict trusting in our own
  strength, but always in the name of Him who cannot fail."
TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.4  — KJV: "six cubits and a span" / ESV: "six cubits and a span" —
       Both translations agree. A cubit = approximately 18 inches; a
       span = approximately 9 inches. Total: approximately 9 feet 9
       inches. The Septuagint (LXX) reads "four cubits and a span"
       (about 6'9") — a significant textual variant. The Hebrew
       Masoretic text has "six cubits," which both KJV and ESV follow.

v.5  — KJV: "brass" / ESV: "bronze" — Hebrew "nechosheth." KJV
       consistently translates as "brass" (an alloy of copper and
       zinc), but the correct translation is "bronze" (copper and tin),
       which was the metal of the ancient world. ESV is more accurate
       here. This applies throughout the chapter.

v.20 — KJV: "he came to the trench" / ESV: "he came to the
       encampment" — Hebrew "ha-ma'galah." This word can mean a
       circular encampment (wagons arranged in a circle for defense)
       or a trench/barricade. ESV "encampment" is more likely correct;
       KJV "trench" reflects an older interpretation.

v.22 — KJV: "left his carriage in the hand of the keeper of the
       carriage" / ESV: "left the things in charge of the keeper of
       the baggage" — KJV "carriage" is archaic English for "baggage"
       or "things carried." ESV clarifies with modern English. David
       left his provisions with the supply guard.

v.28 — KJV: "I know thy pride, and the naughtiness of thine heart" /
       ESV: "I know your presumption and the evil of your heart" —
       Hebrew "zadon" = presumption, pride, insolence. KJV
       "naughtiness" is archaic for "wickedness" (not the modern sense
       of childish misbehavior). ESV "evil" is stronger but captures
       the Hebrew "ro'a" (badness, evil) more directly.

v.29 — KJV: "Is there not a cause?" / ESV: "Was it not but a word?" —
       Hebrew "halo davar hu." This is a significant translation
       difference. KJV reads it as "Is there not a cause/reason [for
       my concern]?" — David defending his right to speak. ESV reads
       it as "Was it not just a word/question?" — David minimizing his
       inquiry. Both are grammatically possible; the KJV reading has
       more theological weight and is the more traditional
       interpretation.

v.40 — KJV: "a shepherd's bag which he had, even in a scrip" / ESV:
       "his shepherd's pouch" — Hebrew "yalqut" (bag) and "keli"
       (vessel/container). KJV "scrip" is an archaic word for a small
       bag or wallet. ESV simplifies to "pouch."

v.45 — KJV: "a shield" / ESV: "a javelin" — Hebrew "kidon." This word
       is debated: it may mean a javelin, a scimitar, or a short sword.
       KJV translates as "shield" here but as "spear" in Joshua 8:18.
       ESV "javelin" is the more common modern scholarly translation.
       The point remains: Goliath comes with multiple weapons; David
       comes with God's name.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Champion       — Hebrew "ish ha-benayim" = literally "man of the
               between" or "man of the space between." This refers to
               the space between the two armies where representative
               combat took place. Goliath is the designated fighter
               who stands in the gap between the armies.

Valley of Elah — Hebrew "Emeq ha-Elah" = Valley of the Terebinth
               (a large oak-like tree). Located about 15 miles
               southwest of Jerusalem, this valley was a natural
               invasion route from Philistine territory into Judah's
               heartland. The brook where David chose his stones still
               flows through this valley.

Uncircumcised  — David's term for Goliath (vv.26, 36). Circumcision
               was the sign of God's covenant with Israel (Genesis
               17:10-14). To call someone "uncircumcised" meant they
               were outside God's covenant — without God's promises,
               protection, or relationship. It is a theological
               statement, not merely a physical observation.

Coat of mail   — Hebrew "shiryon qasqassim" = literally "armor of
               scales." A garment made of overlapping metal plates
               (like fish scales) sewn onto leather or fabric. At
               5,000 shekels (about 125 pounds), Goliath's coat was
               extraordinarily heavy — indicating both his size and
               the Philistines' superior metalworking technology.

Sling          — Not a child's toy but a serious military weapon in
               the ancient world. Made of leather or woven material
               with a pouch for the stone, a sling could hurl a stone
               at speeds over 100 mph with deadly accuracy. Judges
               20:16 mentions Benjamite slingers who could hit a
               hair's breadth target. David's weapon was legitimate
               and lethal.

LORD of hosts  — Hebrew "Yahweh Tseva'ot" = the LORD of armies. This
               title emphasizes God as the commander of heavenly
               armies (angels) and earthly armies (Israel). David
               invokes this name against Goliath — he is not fighting
               alone but with the God who commands all the armies of
               heaven and earth.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. The battle is the LORD's (v.47). This is the chapter's central
   truth. Every battle we face — spiritual, relational, vocational —
   ultimately belongs to God. Our job is not to win in our own
   strength but to show up in faith and let God fight through us. The
   outcome is His responsibility; obedience is ours.

2. Past faithfulness prepares for future challenges (vv.34-37). David
   fought the lion and bear in obscurity — no audience, no reward, no
   recognition. But those private victories built the faith that faced
   Goliath publicly. Do not despise small, hidden acts of faithfulness.
   God is preparing you in private for what He will do through you in
   public.

3. Fight with what God has given you, not borrowed equipment (vv.38-40).
   David could not use Saul's armor — it did not fit, and he had not
   tested it. We cannot fight our battles with someone else's gifts,
   methods, or calling. God has equipped each of us uniquely. Use what
   He has given you, even if it looks foolish to others.

4. Right perspective changes everything (v.26). Everyone else saw
   Goliath and thought "He's too big to fight." David saw Goliath and
   thought "He's too big to miss — and he's defying the living God."
   Same giant, different perspective. Faith does not deny the problem;
   it sees God as bigger than the problem.

5. God uses the weak to shame the strong (v.50). No sword in David's
   hand — just a stone and a sling. God deliberately chose the weakest
   instrument to defeat the strongest warrior, so that no one could
   credit the victory to human power. When you feel inadequate for
   what God has called you to do, remember: your weakness is the
   canvas on which God displays His strength.
