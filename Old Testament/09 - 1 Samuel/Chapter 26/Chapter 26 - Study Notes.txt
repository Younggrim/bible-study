1 Samuel - Chapter 26
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
David again spares Saul's life, this time stealing his spear and water jug while Saul sleeps in his camp. Saul again confesses his sin, but David wisely retreats rather than trust Saul's unstable repentance.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Unknown, traditionally attributed to Samuel (for the early
chapters), Nathan, and Gad (1 Chronicles 29:29). Originally one book
with 2 Samuel in the Hebrew Bible. Written approximately 1050-950 BC.
The book covers the transition from the judges to the monarchy — from
the birth of Samuel through the death of Saul (approximately 1105-1010
BC). Key themes: God's sovereignty in raising and removing leaders, the
danger of rejecting God's rule, the contrast between Saul (man's choice)
and David (God's choice), and the principle that 'man looketh on the
outward appearance, but the LORD looketh on the heart' (16:7).

Historical Context: Chapter 26 parallels chapter 24 — David spares
Saul a second time. The Ziphites again betray David's location (as in
23:19). Saul again pursues with 3,000 men. David again has the
opportunity to kill Saul and again refuses. But the circumstances
differ: in chapter 24, Saul entered David's cave unknowingly; here,
David deliberately infiltrates Saul's camp at night. God causes a
"deep sleep" to fall on Saul's army (v.12) — divine intervention that
places Saul completely in David's power. Abishai urges David to let
him kill Saul with one spear-thrust (v.8), but David refuses with the
same principle: "who can stretch forth his hand against the LORD's
anointed, and be guiltless?" (v.9). Instead of killing Saul, David
takes his spear and water jug as proof. He then calls out from a safe
distance, rebukes Abner for failing to guard the king, and confronts
Saul with the evidence of his mercy. David's theology is explicit:
"the LORD shall smite him; or his day shall come to die; or he shall
descend into battle, and perish" (v.10) — God will remove Saul in His
own time and way. David will not preempt God's judgment. Saul's
response — "I have sinned... I have played the fool" (v.21) — is his
last recorded words to David. They part permanently. David concludes
that Saul will never stop pursuing him and decides to flee to
Philistia (ch.27).

David Infiltrates Saul's Camp (vv.1-12): The Ziphites betray David
again (v.1). Saul pursues with 3,000 men and camps at Hachilah (v.3).
David scouts the camp and sees Saul sleeping in the center with Abner
nearby and the army around him (v.5). He asks for a volunteer: Abishai
(Joab's brother, David's nephew) agrees to go (v.6). They enter the
camp at night — Saul sleeps with his spear stuck in the ground at his
head and a water jug beside him (v.7). Abishai sees divine providence:
"God hath delivered thine enemy into thine hand this day" (v.8) — the
same interpretation David's men gave in the cave (24:4). He asks
permission to kill Saul with one stroke. David refuses absolutely:
"Destroy him not: for who can stretch forth his hand against the LORD's
anointed, and be guiltless?" (v.9). David's theology of God's timing
is explicit: God will strike Saul, or he will die naturally, or he
will fall in battle (v.10). David takes only the spear and water jug
(v.11). No one wakes — "a deep sleep from the LORD was fallen upon
them" (v.12). God Himself arranged this test and this protection.

David Confronts Abner and Saul (vv.13-20): From a safe distance,
David calls out to Abner (v.14). He rebukes Abner for failing to
guard the king: "ye are worthy to die, because ye have not kept your
master, the LORD's anointed" (v.16). The missing spear and water jug
prove David's point. Saul recognizes David's voice: "Is this thy
voice, my son David?" (v.17). David's speech raises two possibilities:
either God has stirred Saul against him (in which case, let God accept
an offering to end it) or men have stirred Saul (in which case, they
are cursed for driving David from "the inheritance of the LORD" — the
promised land — effectively saying "Go, serve other gods" (v.19). This
is a profound theological complaint: being driven from Israel means
being driven from God's presence and worship.

Saul's Final Words to David (vv.21-25): Saul confesses: "I have
sinned... I have played the fool, and have erred exceedingly" (v.21).
He invites David to return. David does not return — he has heard these
promises before (24:16-22). Instead, he sends for the spear (v.22)
and declares his principle: "The LORD render to every man his
righteousness and his faithfulness" (v.23). He trusts God to reward
his restraint. Saul's final words to David are a blessing: "Blessed
be thou, my son David: thou shalt both do great things, and also shalt
still prevail" (v.25). They part — permanently. David goes his way;
Saul returns to his place. They will never speak again.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Wilderness of Ziph: The hill of Hachilah, south of Jeshimon — Saul camps here with 3,000 men pursuing David
- David enters Saul's camp at night: The trench/barricade of wagons surrounds the sleeping army
- This is the second time David spares Saul (first was En-gedi, ch.24) — both in the Judean wilderness

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.9  — "Who can stretch forth his hand against the LORD's anointed,
        and be guiltless?" — David's consistent principle, now stated
        for the second time. No one can harm God's chosen and escape
        judgment. This protects Saul now — and will protect David
        later. The principle is universal: God defends those He has
        appointed.

v.10 — "The LORD shall smite him; or his day shall come to die; or he
        shall descend into battle, and perish." — David's theology of
        divine timing. God has three options for removing Saul: direct
        judgment, natural death, or death in battle. David does not
        need to be the instrument. This is prophetic — Saul will
        indeed "descend into battle, and perish" (ch.31).

v.12 — "A deep sleep from the LORD was fallen upon them." — God
        orchestrates the entire scene. The deep sleep (tardemah — the
        same word used of Adam in Genesis 2:21 and Abraham in Genesis
        15:12) is supernatural. God puts Saul's army to sleep so that
        David can demonstrate his mercy — and so that Saul can have no
        excuse.

v.21 — "I have sinned... I have played the fool, and have erred
        exceedingly." — Saul's most complete confession. He uses three
        terms: sinned (chata'ti), played the fool (niskalti), erred
        exceedingly (shagiti harbeh). Yet this confession, like his
        tears in chapter 24, produces no lasting change. Words of
        repentance without works of repentance are empty.

v.23 — "The LORD render to every man his righteousness and his
        faithfulness." — David's final word to Saul is a commitment to
        divine justice. He trusts God to evaluate and reward both his
        own faithfulness and Saul's unfaithfulness. This is the
        ultimate expression of faith: leaving the final verdict to God.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/1-samuel-26/
  Guzik notes that David's second refusal to kill Saul is even more
  remarkable than the first — he deliberately enters the camp, has
  complete control, and still refuses. He emphasizes David's three
  options for Saul's death (v.10) as showing David's trust that God
  has multiple ways to accomplish His purposes without David's help.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "'The LORD shall smite him; or his day shall come to die; or he
  shall descend into battle, and perish.' Here is David's creed
  concerning his enemy: God will deal with him. I need not. The Lord
  has a thousand ways to remove the wicked — by His own hand, by
  natural death, by the sword of battle. Why should I stain my hands
  when God has so many instruments at His disposal? Let us learn this
  holy patience. Our enemies are in God's hands, not ours. He will
  deal with them in His time and way — and His way is always better
  than ours."
TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.5  — KJV: "Saul lay in the trench" / ESV: "Saul was lying within
       the encampment" — Hebrew "ba-ma'gal." The same word as 17:20.
       It refers to the circular arrangement of the camp (wagons or
       soldiers in a circle with the commander in the center). KJV
       "trench" and ESV "encampment" reflect different interpretations
       of the same word.

v.7  — KJV: "at his bolster" / ESV: "at his head" — Hebrew
       "me-ra'ashotav" = at his head-place. KJV "bolster" (pillow/
       headrest) is an interpretation; ESV "at his head" is more
       literal. The spear was stuck in the ground near where Saul's
       head lay.

v.11 — KJV: "the cruse of water" / ESV: "the jar of water" — Hebrew
       "tsappachat ha-mayim." "Tsappachat" = a flask, jug, or jar.
       KJV "cruse" is archaic for a small container; ESV "jar" is
       clearer.

v.19 — KJV: "from abiding in the inheritance of the LORD" / ESV:
       "that I should have no share in the heritage of the LORD" —
       Hebrew "me-histappeach be-nachalat Yahweh." David's complaint
       is theological: being driven from Israel means being driven
       from God's land, God's worship, God's presence. Both
       translations capture this, though ESV's "no share in the
       heritage" emphasizes the disinheritance more strongly.

v.21 — KJV: "I have played the fool" / ESV: "I have acted foolishly"
       — Hebrew "niskalti" from "sakal" = to be foolish, to act
       stupidly. KJV's "played the fool" is more vivid and memorable;
       ESV's "acted foolishly" is more literal. Both capture Saul's
       self-assessment — he knows his pursuit of David is irrational.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Deep sleep     — Hebrew "tardemah" (v.12) = a supernatural deep sleep
               sent by God. Used of Adam (Genesis 2:21), Abraham
               (Genesis 15:12), and here of Saul's army. It is not
               natural exhaustion but divine intervention — God puts
               them to sleep for His purposes. No one can wake when
               God says "sleep."

LORD's anointed — Hebrew "meshiach Yahweh" (vv.9, 11, 16, 23) =
               the LORD's anointed one. David uses this title four
               times in this chapter — emphasizing that his refusal
               to harm Saul is based on Saul's divine appointment,
               not on Saul's character. The office is sacred even when
               the officeholder is corrupt.

Spear          — Saul's spear appears throughout 1 Samuel as a symbol
               of his violent, grasping kingship. He hurls it at David
               (18:11; 19:10), at Jonathan (20:33), and here it stands
               stuck in the ground at his head — a symbol of his
               authority. David takes it as proof of his mercy, not as
               a weapon.

Inheritance of the LORD — Hebrew "nachalat Yahweh" (v.19) = the land
               God gave to Israel as their inheritance. To be driven
               from this land is to be driven from God's presence and
               worship. David's complaint reveals his deepest pain:
               not the physical danger but the spiritual exile — being
               cut off from corporate worship of Yahweh.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Consistency in principle proves character (vv.9-11). David refuses
   to kill Saul a second time — same principle, same restraint, same
   trust in God. Character is not proven by a single decision but by
   consistent decisions over time. Are you as faithful to your
   principles the second time as the first?

2. God has many ways to accomplish His purposes (v.10). David lists
   three ways God might remove Saul — and refuses to add a fourth
   (his own hand). God does not need our help to execute His plans.
   When we try to "help God" through sinful means, we only complicate
   His work and stain our own hands.

3. Confession without change is not repentance (v.21). Saul says "I
   have sinned" — again. But nothing changes. True repentance is not
   words but transformation. If you find yourself confessing the same
   sin repeatedly without change, the problem is not your confession
   but your will. Repentance means turning, not just talking.

4. Being driven from God's presence is the deepest pain (v.19). David's
   greatest grief is not physical danger but spiritual exile — being
   cut off from "the inheritance of the LORD." When circumstances
   separate us from worship, community, and God's felt presence, that
   is the real suffering. Pursue God's presence above all else.

5. Trust God's timing even when it seems slow (v.23). David has now
   spared Saul twice. He could have ended his suffering years ago.
   But he trusts: "The LORD render to every man his righteousness."
   God's timing may seem slow, but it is never wrong. What He promises,
   He will perform — in His time, not ours.
