1 Samuel - Chapter 9
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
Saul, a tall Benjamite searching for lost donkeys, is directed by God to Samuel, who anoints him privately as Israel's first king. God gives Saul a new heart and confirms the anointing with signs.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Unknown, traditionally attributed to Samuel (for the early
chapters), Nathan, and Gad (1 Chronicles 29:29). Originally one book
with 2 Samuel in the Hebrew Bible. Written approximately 1050-950 BC.
The book covers the transition from the judges to the monarchy — from
the birth of Samuel through the death of Saul (approximately 1105-1010
BC). Key themes: God's sovereignty in raising and removing leaders, the
danger of rejecting God's rule, the contrast between Saul (man's choice)
and David (God's choice), and the principle that 'man looketh on the
outward appearance, but the LORD looketh on the heart' (16:7).

Historical Context: Chapter 9 introduces Saul — the man Israel wanted
and God permitted. The narrative is masterfully constructed: what
appears to be a mundane story about lost donkeys is actually God's
sovereign orchestration of a royal appointment. Saul is everything
Israel would choose in a king: tall, handsome, from a wealthy family.
He is "from his shoulders and upward higher than any of the people"
(v.2) — the kind of impressive physical specimen that commands human
respect. Yet the narrative subtly undermines confidence in appearances.
Saul cannot find his father's donkeys. He is ready to give up and go
home. His servant has to suggest visiting the prophet. He does not know
who Samuel is. He worries about having no gift to bring. At every turn,
Saul is passive, uncertain, and dependent on others' initiative. God
is working behind the scenes: He told Samuel the day before that He
would send "a man out of the land of Benjamin" (v.16). The lost donkeys,
the servant's suggestion, the timing of arrival — all are God's
providence directing Saul to Samuel. God calls Saul "captain" (nagid)
rather than "king" (melekh) — a subtle distinction suggesting that
even this king serves under God's authority. The chapter ends with
Samuel preparing to reveal "the word of God" to Saul — the private
anointing that will come in chapter 10.

Saul Introduced (vv.1-2): Saul's genealogy traces through Kish, a
"mighty man of power" (KJV) / "man of wealth" (ESV) — Hebrew "gibbor
chayil" can mean either a warrior of valor or a man of substance. The
family is Benjamite — from the smallest tribe (v.21), the tribe nearly
destroyed in Judges 19-21. Saul himself is described as "a choice young
man, and a goodly" (KJV) / "a handsome young man" (ESV). He is taller
than everyone else — "from his shoulders and upward" he towers over
Israel. This physical description is deliberate: Saul looks like a
king. He is what the people would choose based on appearance. The
contrast with David (16:7 — "man looketh on the outward appearance,
but the LORD looketh on the heart") is being set up from the beginning.

The Search for Lost Donkeys (vv.3-10): The narrative begins with the
mundane: Kish's donkeys are lost, and Saul is sent to find them. He
searches through multiple regions — hill country of Ephraim, Shalishah,
Shaalim, Benjamin — without success. He is ready to quit: "let us
return; lest my father leave caring for the asses, and take thought
for us" (v.5). It is the servant who suggests visiting "a man of God"
in the nearby city (v.6). Saul's concern is practical: they have no
gift to bring (v.7). The servant produces a quarter-shekel of silver
(v.8). The parenthetical note (v.9) explains that "prophet" and "seer"
are the same office — an editorial comment for later readers. The
entire sequence reveals Saul's character: passive, practical, lacking
spiritual initiative. He does not think to seek God; his servant does.
Yet God is using even Saul's aimless wandering to bring him to the
right place at the right time.

God's Revelation to Samuel (vv.15-17): The narrative reveals what
Samuel already knows: "the LORD had told Samuel in his ear a day
before" (v.15). God's instruction is specific: "To morrow about this
time I will send thee a man out of the land of Benjamin, and thou shalt
anoint him to be captain over my people Israel" (v.16). The word
"captain" (nagid) rather than "king" (melekh) is significant — nagid
means a designated leader, one appointed by a higher authority. Even
the king of Israel serves under God's kingship. God's stated purpose:
"that he may save my people out of the hand of the Philistines: for I
have looked upon my people, because their cry is come unto me" (v.16).
Despite Israel's rejection of His rule (chapter 8), God still hears
their cry and provides for their need. When Samuel sees Saul, God
confirms: "Behold the man" (v.17).

Samuel Receives Saul (vv.18-27): Saul approaches Samuel without
recognizing him — "Tell me, I pray thee, where the seer's house is"
(v.18). Samuel reveals himself and immediately demonstrates prophetic
knowledge: the donkeys are found, and "all the desire of Israel" is
upon Saul and his house (v.20). Saul's response shows genuine humility
(or perhaps insecurity): "Am not I a Benjamite, of the smallest of the
tribes?" (v.21). Samuel honors Saul at the feast — the chief seat among
thirty guests, a reserved portion of meat (the shoulder/leg — the
priest's portion, symbolizing authority). Samuel has prepared for this
moment. After the feast, they commune privately on the rooftop (v.25).
At dawn, Samuel sends Saul on his way but stops him at the city's edge:
"stand thou still a while, that I may shew thee the word of God" (v.27).
The private anointing is about to occur.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Land of Zuph: Where Saul finds Samuel — likely the area around Ramah (Ramathaim-zophim) in the central highlands
- Saul's search route: From Gibeah (Benjamin) through the hill country of Ephraim, land of Shalishah, land of Shaalim, land of Benjamin
- The "high place" (bamah): A worship site on the hilltop above the town where Samuel presides over a sacrificial feast

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "And he had a son, whose name was Saul, a choice young man, and
        a goodly: and there was not among the children of Israel a
        goodlier person than he: from his shoulders and upward he was
        higher than any of the people." — Saul is introduced by his
        appearance — tall, handsome, impressive. This is what Israel
        wanted: a king who looks the part. But appearance is deceptive.
        The book will later contrast this with God's choice of David:
        "the LORD looketh on the heart" (16:7).

v.16 — "Tomorrow about this time I will send to you a man from the
        land of Benjamin, and you shall anoint him to be prince over
        my people Israel, that he may save my people out of the hand
        of the Philistines: for I have looked upon my people, because
        their cry is come unto me." — God's sovereignty in the
        appointment. Despite Israel's sinful demand, God still provides
        for His people. He hears their cry even when they have rejected
        Him. Grace operates even within judgment.

v.17 — "Here is the man of whom I spoke to you! He it is who shall reign
        over my people." — God identifies Saul to Samuel. There is no
        ambiguity — God is directing this process. Saul's kingship is
        not accidental but divinely appointed, even though it arises
        from Israel's rebellion.

v.20 — "And for whom is all that is desirable in Israel? Is it not for
        you and for all your father's house?" — Samuel hints at Saul's
        destiny. "The desire of Israel" — the nation's longing for a
        king — will be fulfilled in Saul. The phrase is both honor and
        burden: Israel's expectations will rest on this man's shoulders.

v.21 — "Am not I a Benjamite, of the smallest of the tribes of Israel?
        and my family the least of all the families of the tribe of
        Benjamin?" — Saul's initial humility. He recognizes his
        insignificance. This is the right posture — but it will not
        last. The man who begins by saying "I am least" will end by
        building himself a monument (15:12).

  donkeys were God's means of bringing Saul to Samuel. What 'lost
  donkeys' is God using in your life to bring you to His appointed
  place?"
TRANSLATION COMPARISON
------------------------------------
v.1  — KJV: "a mighty man of power" / ESV: "a man of wealth" —
       Hebrew "gibbor chayil." This phrase can mean a mighty warrior,
       a man of valor, or a man of substance/wealth. KJV emphasizes
       military prowess; ESV emphasizes economic status. In context,
       Kish is a man of standing in his community — whether through
       military reputation or material wealth (or both).

v.2  — KJV: "a choice young man, and a goodly" / ESV: "a handsome
       young man" — Hebrew "bachur vatov." "Bachur" = choice, select,
       young man in his prime. "Tov" = good, in this context meaning
       good-looking. KJV preserves both descriptors; ESV combines them
       into "handsome." The emphasis is on Saul's physical
       impressiveness — he looks like royalty.

v.16 — KJV: "anoint him to be captain" / ESV: "anoint him to be
       prince" — Hebrew "nagid." This word means a designated leader,
       one appointed by a higher authority — distinct from "melekh"
       (king). KJV "captain" emphasizes military leadership; ESV
       "prince" emphasizes royal designation. The theological point:
       even Israel's king is subordinate to God — he is God's
       appointed leader, not an autonomous sovereign.

v.17 — KJV: "this same shall reign over my people" / ESV: "He it is
       who shall restrain my people" — Hebrew "ya'atsor." This verb
       means to restrain, hold back, or rule. KJV "reign" and ESV
       "restrain" reflect different nuances of the same word. The idea
       may be that Saul will "hold together" or "govern" the people —
       keeping them in order.

v.24 — KJV: "the shoulder" / ESV: "the leg" — Hebrew "ha-shoq." This
       refers to the upper leg/thigh portion — the priest's portion
       of the sacrifice (Leviticus 7:32-34). By giving Saul the
       priestly portion, Samuel honors him as God's chosen leader.
       KJV "shoulder" and ESV "leg" reflect different understandings
       of the cut of meat.

v.1 — ASV: adds "the son of"
       NET: "prominent person." for KJV's "Benjamite, a mighty man of power."
       WEB: adds "the son of"
v.2 — NET: "he was; he stood head and" for KJV's "he: from his"
       WEB: "an impressive" for KJV's "a choice"
v.16 — ASV: "Israel; and" for KJV's "Israel, that"

       WEB: "Israel. He will" for KJV's "Israel, that he may"

       NET: "told you about. He will rule" for KJV's "spake to thee of! this same shall reign"
       WEB: uses "Yahweh" for KJV's "LORD" (Hebrew YHWH).

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Nagid          — Hebrew "leader, prince, captain." A title for one
               designated by God to lead — distinct from "melekh"
               (king) in that it emphasizes divine appointment and
               subordination to God's authority. Used of Saul (v.16),
               David (2 Samuel 5:2), and others. The nagid rules under
               God; the melekh rules in his own right.

Seer           — Hebrew "ro'eh" = one who sees. An older title for a
               prophet (v.9), emphasizing the visionary aspect of
               prophetic ministry — the prophet "sees" what God reveals.
               Later replaced by "navi" (prophet = one who speaks for
               God), emphasizing the proclamation aspect.

Benjamin       — The smallest tribe of Israel, nearly destroyed in the
               civil war of Judges 19-21. Saul's tribe. Its smallness
               makes Saul's selection surprising — God often chooses
               from the least (Gideon from Manasseh, David from
               Bethlehem). Yet Benjamin's selection also reflects
               Israel's desire for a warrior-king — Benjamin was known
               as fierce fighters (Judges 20:16).

Kish           — Saul's father, a Benjamite of wealth/standing. His
               name means "bow" or "snare." He sends Saul to find lost
               donkeys — the mundane errand that God uses to bring Saul
               to his destiny.

Quarter-shekel — A small amount of silver (about 3 grams). The
               servant's offering to the prophet — a modest gift
               reflecting their limited resources on the journey. It
               was customary to bring a gift when consulting a prophet
               (2 Kings 5:5; 8:8-9).

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. God works through ordinary circumstances (vv.3-6). Lost donkeys
   led to a kingdom. God's providence operates through the mundane —
   a wrong turn, a chance meeting, a servant's suggestion. We often
   cannot see God's hand until we look backward. Trust that God is
   directing your "ordinary" days toward His purposes.

2. Appearance is not destiny (v.2). Saul looked like a king — tall,
   handsome, impressive. But looking the part does not mean being the
   part. External qualifications do not guarantee internal character.
   We must learn to evaluate people (and ourselves) by heart, not by
   appearance.

3. God provides even within judgment (v.16). Israel's demand for a
   king was sinful (chapter 8), yet God still hears their cry and
   provides a deliverer. Grace operates even within the consequences
   of our rebellion. God does not abandon His people even when they
   reject His rule.

4. Initial humility is no guarantee of lasting humility (v.21). Saul
   begins with genuine self-deprecation: "I am from the smallest
   tribe." But humility that depends on low position often disappears
   when position rises. True humility is not thinking less of yourself
   because you have less — it is thinking rightly of yourself
   regardless of what you have.

5. God prepares before He reveals (vv.15-17). God told Samuel about
   Saul the day before Saul arrived. God had prepared the feast, the
   reserved portion, the rooftop conversation. God works ahead of us,
   preparing what we will need before we know we need it. His
   providence is always ahead of our awareness.
