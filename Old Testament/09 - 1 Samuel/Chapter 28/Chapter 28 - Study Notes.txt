1 Samuel - Chapter 28
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
On the eve of battle, a terrified Saul consults the witch of Endor, and the spirit of Samuel appears with a devastating message: tomorrow Saul and his sons will die. Saul collapses in despair.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Unknown, traditionally attributed to Samuel (for the early
chapters), Nathan, and Gad (1 Chronicles 29:29). Originally one book
with 2 Samuel in the Hebrew Bible. Written approximately 1050-950 BC.
The book covers the transition from the judges to the monarchy — from
the birth of Samuel through the death of Saul (approximately 1105-1010
BC). Key themes: God's sovereignty in raising and removing leaders, the
danger of rejecting God's rule, the contrast between Saul (man's choice)
and David (God's choice), and the principle that 'man looketh on the
outward appearance, but the LORD looketh on the heart' (16:7).

Historical Context: Chapter 28 is one of the most dramatic and
theologically debated chapters in the Old Testament. It records Saul's
final descent — from fear (v.5) to divine silence (v.6) to forbidden
necromancy (v.7) to doom (v.19). The Philistines mass for a major
invasion, camping at Shunem in the Jezreel Valley. Saul gathers Israel
at Mount Gilboa. When he sees the Philistine army, he is terrified
(v.5). He seeks God — but God does not answer by any means: dreams,
Urim, or prophets (v.6). In desperation, Saul does what he himself
has forbidden: he seeks a medium (a woman with a "familiar spirit") at
Endor. He asks her to bring up Samuel from the dead. What happens next
is extraordinary: Samuel actually appears (v.12) — the medium herself
is shocked, suggesting this is not her usual experience. Samuel's
message is devastating: God has become Saul's enemy (v.16), the
kingdom is given to David (v.17), and "tomorrow shalt thou and thy
sons be with me" (v.19) — Saul and his sons will die in battle. Saul
collapses in terror (v.20). The chapter is Saul's darkest hour: the
king who rejected God's word through Samuel now seeks Samuel through
forbidden means — and receives only confirmation of his doom. The
theological irony is complete: Saul banned mediums from the land (v.3)
but now uses one himself; he could not hear God through legitimate
means, so he seeks Him through illegitimate ones; and the message he
receives is the same one Samuel delivered in life (15:28) — the
kingdom is torn from him.

David's Dilemma (vv.1-2): The chapter opens with David's crisis:
Achish expects him to fight against Israel (v.1). David's ambiguous
answer — "thou shalt know what thy servant can do" (v.2) — can be
read as loyalty to Achish or as a veiled threat. Achish takes it
positively and makes David his bodyguard (v.2). David is trapped by
his own deception — the consequences of chapter 27's faithless
decision. God will resolve this in chapter 29.

Saul's Desperation (vv.3-7): Samuel is dead (v.3). Saul has banned
mediums and wizards (v.3) — ironically, one of his few acts of
obedience to the law (Deuteronomy 18:10-12). The Philistines camp at
Shunem; Israel at Gilboa (v.4). Saul sees the enemy and is terrified
(v.5). He inquires of the LORD — but receives no answer by any channel:
dreams, Urim (the priestly oracle — but Abiathar and the ephod are
with David), or prophets (v.6). God's silence is judgment. Having
rejected God's word repeatedly, Saul now finds God will not speak to
him at all. In desperation, he seeks what he has forbidden: "Seek me
a woman that hath a familiar spirit" (v.7).

The Medium at Endor (vv.8-14): Saul disguises himself and goes to
Endor by night (v.8) — darkness, disguise, and disobedience. He asks
the woman to bring up Samuel (v.11). When Samuel appears, the woman
cries out in shock (v.12) — this is not her normal experience. She
recognizes Saul (perhaps because only a king would dare summon a
prophet). She sees "gods ascending out of the earth" (v.13 KJV) / "a
god coming up" (ESV) — Hebrew "elohim" (divine being/beings). Samuel
appears as "an old man... covered with a mantle" (v.14) — his
characteristic prophetic garment. The theological question: is this
really Samuel? The text says "Samuel said to Saul" (v.15) — the
narrator treats it as genuinely Samuel. Most conservative interpreters
conclude that God permitted Samuel's actual spirit to appear (not a
demonic impersonation) as a final act of judgment on Saul.

Samuel's Message of Doom (vv.15-19): Samuel's words are harsh and
final: "Why hast thou disquieted me?" (v.15). Saul explains his
desperation: the Philistines attack, God is silent (v.15). Samuel's
response: "Wherefore then dost thou ask of me, seeing the LORD is
departed from thee, and is become thine enemy?" (v.16). The message
repeats what Samuel said in life: the kingdom is torn from Saul and
given to David (v.17), because Saul disobeyed regarding Amalek (v.18).
Then the death sentence: "tomorrow shalt thou and thy sons be with me:
the LORD also shall deliver the host of Israel into the hand of the
Philistines" (v.19). "With me" = in the realm of the dead (Sheol).
Saul's doom is sealed.

Saul's Collapse (vv.20-25): Saul falls full-length on the ground,
terrified and physically spent — he has not eaten all day (v.20). The
medium shows him unexpected kindness: she urges him to eat, kills a
fattened calf, and bakes bread (vv.21-24). This is Saul's last meal —
a condemned man fed by a woman he should have executed. He eats and
leaves into the night (v.25) — walking toward his death at Gilboa.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Endor: A village on the north side of Mount Moreh, behind the Philistine camp — Saul crosses enemy lines to reach it at night
- Mount Gilboa: Where Saul's army camps facing the Philistines in the Jezreel Valley
- The Philistines camp at Shunem, across the valley from Gilboa — Saul is terrified by their numbers

CROSS-REFERENCES
------------------------------------
v.3   — Deuteronomy 18:10-12 — The law forbidding mediums, wizards,
         and necromancy — which Saul now violates.
v.6   — Proverbs 1:28 — "Then shall they call upon me, but I will not
         answer" — the consequence of rejecting wisdom.
v.6   — 1 Samuel 14:37 — An earlier time God did not answer Saul —
         due to Jonathan's unwitting oath violation.
v.15  — 1 Samuel 15:26-28 — Samuel's original prophecy of the kingdom
         being torn from Saul — now confirmed from beyond the grave.
v.17  — 1 Samuel 16:13 — David's anointing — the "neighbor" to whom
         the kingdom is given.
v.19  — 1 Samuel 31:2-6 — The fulfillment: Saul and his sons die at
         Gilboa the next day.
v.19  — 1 Chronicles 10:13-14 — The chronicler's summary: Saul died
         "for asking counsel of one that had a familiar spirit."

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/1-samuel-28/
  Guzik argues that this was genuinely Samuel (not a demon), based on
  the text's straightforward language and the medium's own shock. He
  notes that God permitted this extraordinary event as a final act of
  judgment — Saul sought the dead because the living God would not
  answer him.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "Saul enquired of the LORD, and the LORD answered him not. Here is
  the harvest of a life of disobedience. God will not be mocked. He
  who rejects God's word when it is freely given shall find God silent
  when he desperately needs to hear. Let us tremble at this: there is
  a point beyond which God's patience does not extend. Seek the Lord
  while He may be found; call upon Him while He is near. For the day
  may come when you call and He does not answer."
PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. Divine silence is the most terrifying judgment (v.6). God answered
   Saul "not... neither by dreams, nor by Urim, nor by prophets."
   Every channel closed. This is the consequence of persistently
   rejecting God's word: eventually He stops speaking. Do not take
   God's voice for granted. Respond when He speaks, lest He fall
   silent.

2. Desperation without repentance leads to deeper sin (vv.7-8). Saul
   is desperate — but not repentant. Instead of humbling himself
   before God, he seeks a medium. Desperation that drives us to God
   is healthy; desperation that drives us to forbidden alternatives is
   deadly. When you are desperate, the question is: where do you turn?

3. God's word does not change (vv.17-18). Samuel's message from beyond
   the grave is the same as his message in life: the kingdom is torn
   from Saul because of disobedience regarding Amalek. God does not
   revise His verdicts. What He said through His word remains true
   whether we accept it or not.

4. There is a point of no return (v.19). Saul receives no offer of
   repentance, no possibility of restoration. His doom is sealed.
   While God's patience is extraordinary, it is not infinite. Do not
   presume upon grace. Today is the day of salvation (2 Corinthians
   6:2).

5. Sin's consequences are generational (v.19). "Thou and thy sons"
   will die. Saul's rebellion costs not only his own life but his
   sons' lives — including Jonathan, the faithful friend of David.
   Our sin does not affect only us. It touches our children, our
   families, our communities. The stakes of obedience are higher than
   we think.
