1 Samuel - Chapter 22
Bible Study Notes
========================================

CHAPTER SUMMARY
------------------------------------
David gathers a band of 400 distressed and discontented men at the cave of Adullam. Saul massacres eighty-five priests at Nob for helping David; only Abiathar escapes to join David's company.

AUTHORSHIP & HISTORICAL BACKGROUND
------------------------------------
Author: Unknown, traditionally attributed to Samuel (for the early
chapters), Nathan, and Gad (1 Chronicles 29:29). Originally one book
with 2 Samuel in the Hebrew Bible. Written approximately 1050-950 BC.
The book covers the transition from the judges to the monarchy — from
the birth of Samuel through the death of Saul (approximately 1105-1010
BC). Key themes: God's sovereignty in raising and removing leaders, the
danger of rejecting God's rule, the contrast between Saul (man's choice)
and David (God's choice), and the principle that 'man looketh on the
outward appearance, but the LORD looketh on the heart' (16:7).

Historical Context: Chapter 22 contains two contrasting scenes: the
formation of David's band at Adullam (vv.1-5) and Saul's massacre of
the priests of Nob (vv.6-23). At Adullam, David becomes captain over
400 men who are "in distress, in debt, and discontented" (v.2) — the
rejected and marginalized of Israel gather to the rejected king. This
is a powerful type of Christ, who gathers to Himself the broken, the
burdened, and the outcast (Matthew 11:28). Meanwhile, Saul descends
into paranoid tyranny. Sitting under a tree with his spear (the
symbol of his violent kingship), he accuses his own servants of
conspiracy and self-pity: "there is none of you that is sorry for me"
(v.8). When Doeg reports David's visit to Nob, Saul summons the
priests and condemns them without fair hearing. His own soldiers refuse
to execute the priests — even Saul's guard recognizes the horror of
killing God's servants. But Doeg the Edomite (a foreigner with no
covenant loyalty to Israel's God) carries out the slaughter: 85
priests killed, and the entire city of Nob destroyed — men, women,
children, infants, and livestock (v.19). The irony is devastating:
Saul was commanded to destroy the Amalekites completely and refused
(ch.15); now he destroys an Israelite priestly city completely. What
he would not do to God's enemies, he does to God's servants. Only
Abiathar escapes, fleeing to David — bringing the priestly ephod and
the ability to inquire of God. David accepts responsibility: "I have
occasioned the death of all the persons of thy father's house" (v.22).

David at Adullam (vv.1-5): David escapes to the cave of Adullam —
a large cave in the Judean foothills, about 10 miles southeast of
Gath. His family joins him (v.1) — they are now targets of Saul's
rage. Then others gather: "every one that was in distress, and every
one that was in debt, and every one that was discontented" (v.2). Four
hundred men — society's rejects become David's army. He becomes their
"captain" (sar = commander, leader). David sends his parents to Moab
for safety (v.3) — his great-grandmother Ruth was a Moabitess (Ruth
4:17), giving him a family connection there. The prophet Gad instructs
David to return to Judah (v.5) — God directs David's movements even
in exile.

Saul's Paranoia (vv.6-10): Saul sits at Gibeah under a tree (KJV) /
tamarisk tree (ESV), spear in hand, surrounded by his Benjamite
servants (v.6). He appeals to tribal loyalty: "will the son of Jesse
give every one of you fields and vineyards?" (v.7). His argument is
purely political — David is from Judah, not Benjamin; he will not
reward Benjamites. Saul accuses everyone of conspiracy (v.8) and
self-pity dominates: "none of you is sorry for me." Doeg seizes the
opportunity and reports David's visit to Nob (vv.9-10), adding that
Ahimelech "enquired of the LORD for him" — which may or may not be
accurate (Ahimelech denies initiating it, v.15).

The Massacre of Nob (vv.11-19): Saul summons all the priests of Nob
(v.11). He accuses Ahimelech of conspiracy (v.13). Ahimelech's defense
is reasonable and truthful: David is the king's faithful servant, his
son-in-law, his bodyguard captain — why would helping him be treason?
(vv.14-15). Saul ignores the defense: "Thou shalt surely die,
Ahimelech, thou, and all thy father's house" (v.16). He commands his
guard to kill the priests — they refuse (v.17). This is significant:
even Saul's own soldiers recognize that killing God's priests is
unconscionable. Doeg alone obeys: 85 priests slaughtered, then the
entire city of Nob — total destruction (vv.18-19). This is the
"cherem" (ban of total destruction) that Saul was supposed to execute
against Amalek but refused. Now he executes it against God's own
priests.

Abiathar Escapes (vv.20-23): One son of Ahimelech survives — Abiathar.
He flees to David and reports the massacre (vv.20-21). David's response
shows genuine remorse: "I knew it that day, when Doeg the Edomite was
there, that he would surely tell Saul: I have occasioned the death of
all the persons of thy father's house" (v.22). David accepts
responsibility for the consequences of his deception at Nob. He offers
Abiathar protection: "with me thou shalt be in safeguard" (v.23).
Abiathar will serve as David's priest throughout his fugitive years
and into his reign.

MAP & GEOGRAPHY NOTES
------------------------------------
- Cave of Adullam: In the western Judean foothills (Shephelah) — David's hideout where 400 outcasts gather to him
- Nob: Where Saul massacres 85 priests — Doeg the Edomite carries out the slaughter
- Mizpeh of Moab: Where David sends his parents for safety — across the Dead Sea in Moab (his great-grandmother Ruth's homeland)

KEY VERSES
------------------------------------
v.2  — "And every one that was in distress, and every one that was in
        debt, and every one that was discontented, gathered themselves
        unto him; and he became a captain over them." — David's first
        followers are not the elite but the rejected. This is a
        picture of Christ's kingdom: He gathers the broken, the
        burdened, and the bitter, and transforms them into an army.
        David's 400 misfits will become his "mighty men" (2 Samuel
        23:8-39).

v.17 — "But the servants of the king would not put forth their hand to
        fall upon the priests of the LORD." — Even Saul's own soldiers
        have more conscience than their king. There are limits that
        even loyal servants will not cross. When a leader commands what
        God forbids, disobedience is faithfulness.

v.18 — "And Doeg the Edomite turned, and he fell upon the priests, and
        slew on that day fourscore and five persons that did wear a
        linen ephod." — 85 priests murdered. An Edomite does what no
        Israelite would do. This is Saul's darkest act — the
        slaughter of God's servants by a pagan mercenary at the king's
        command.

v.22 — "I have caused the death of all the persons of your father's
        house." — David accepts responsibility. He does not blame Saul
        alone or excuse his own deception. True repentance acknowledges
        the full chain of consequences. David's lie at Nob (21:2) led
        to this massacre — a sobering reminder that our sins have
        victims beyond ourselves.

COMMENTARY REFERENCES
------------------------------------
Enduring Word (David Guzik):
  https://enduringword.com/bible-commentary/1-samuel-22/
  Guzik emphasizes the typological significance of David at Adullam:
  as David gathered the distressed, indebted, and discontented, so
  Christ gathers sinners to Himself and transforms them. He also notes
  the devastating irony of Saul executing the "ban" against priests
  that he refused to execute against Amalek.

Charles Spurgeon:
  "To the cave of Adullam came the distressed, the debtors, and the
  discontented — and David became captain over them. What a picture of
  our Lord Jesus! To Him come the spiritually bankrupt, the soul-
  distressed, the heart-discontented with this world's emptiness. And
  He receives them all, makes them His own, and transforms them into
  soldiers of the cross. Are you in distress? Go to David's greater
  Son. Are you in debt to God's law? Go to Him who paid it all. Are
  you discontented with sin's wages? Go to Him who gives eternal life."
TRANSLATION NOTES
------------------------------------
v.2  — KJV: "every one that was discontented" / ESV: "everyone who
       was bitter in soul" — Hebrew "mar nephesh" = bitter of soul.
       KJV "discontented" captures the social dimension; ESV "bitter
       in soul" captures the emotional/spiritual dimension. These men
       were embittered by life under Saul's unjust rule.

v.6  — KJV: "Saul abode in Gibeah under a tree in Ramah" / ESV: "Saul
       was sitting at Gibeah under the tamarisk tree on the height" —
       Hebrew "tachat ha-eshel ba-ramah." "Eshel" = tamarisk tree (ESV)
       or simply "tree" (KJV). "Ba-ramah" can mean "in Ramah" (a place
       name) or "on the height" (a common noun). ESV reads it as a
       description of location (on the hill); KJV reads it as a place
       name. The ESV reading is more likely given the context of Gibeah.

v.8  — KJV: "my son hath made a league with the son of Jesse" / ESV:
       "my son makes a covenant with the son of Jesse" — Hebrew
       "karath berith" = cut a covenant. Both translations are
       accurate. Saul knows about Jonathan's covenant with David and
       sees it as betrayal.

v.18 — KJV: "fourscore and five persons that did wear a linen ephod" /
       ESV: "eighty-five persons who wore the linen ephod" — Both
       agree: 85 priests killed. The "linen ephod" (ephod bad)
       identifies them as serving priests — not the high priest's
       ornate ephod but the simple linen garment of priestly service.

v.19 — KJV: "children and sucklings" / ESV: "child and infant" —
       Hebrew "olel ve-yoneq" = child and nursing infant. The total
       destruction of Nob includes the most innocent — a horror that
       echoes the destruction Saul was supposed to bring on Amalek
       (15:3) but now brings on God's own people.

GLOSSARY
------------------------------------
Adullam        — A large cave (or cave complex) in the Judean
               foothills (Shephelah), about 10 miles southeast of
               Gath and 16 miles southwest of Jerusalem. It became
               David's first base of operations as a fugitive. The
               cave was large enough to shelter 400+ men. Psalms 57
               and 142 are associated with this location.

Distressed, in debt, discontented — The three categories of David's
               first followers (v.2). Hebrew: "matsoq" (in straits,
               pressed), "nosheh" (having a creditor, in debt),
               "mar nephesh" (bitter of soul). These represent the
               marginalized of Saul's kingdom — people failed by
               their king who find hope in God's anointed.

Linen ephod    — Hebrew "ephod bad" (v.18) = the simple white linen
               garment worn by priests during service. Distinguished
               from the high priest's ornate ephod with its
               breastplate and gems. The 85 victims are identified as
               serving priests by this garment — making the massacre
               explicitly an attack on God's ordained ministry.

Cherem         — Hebrew "ban" or "devoted thing" — total destruction
               as an act of divine judgment. Though the word is not
               used here, the description of Nob's destruction (v.19)
               mirrors the cherem language of 15:3. Saul performs
               against priests what he refused to perform against
               Amalek.

Prophet Gad    — One of David's prophetic advisors (v.5), who will
               serve him throughout his reign (2 Samuel 24:11-14;
               1 Chronicles 29:29). Gad's instruction to leave Moab
               and return to Judah shows that God actively guided
               David's movements during his fugitive years.

PERSONAL REFLECTION & APPLICATION
------------------------------------
1. God gathers the broken and transforms them (v.2). David's 400
   rejects became Israel's mighty men. Christ's church is built from
   the distressed, the indebted, and the discontented — people the
   world rejects. If you feel broken, you are exactly who God is
   looking for. He does not need the qualified; He qualifies the
   called.

2. Our sins have victims beyond ourselves (v.22). David's lie at Nob
   cost 85 priests their lives. Sin is never private — it always has
   collateral damage. When we deceive, cut corners, or act in fear
   rather than faith, others may pay the price. Consider the ripple
   effects of your choices.

3. There are orders that must be disobeyed (v.17). Saul's soldiers
   refused to kill the priests. When human authority commands what God
   forbids, faithful disobedience is the only righteous option. Not
   all obedience is virtue — obedience to evil is complicity.

4. Self-pity blinds us to our own wickedness (v.8). Saul sees himself
   as the victim: "none of you is sorry for me." He is about to
   murder 85 innocent priests, yet he feels sorry for himself. Self-
   pity is one of sin's most dangerous disguises — it makes the
   perpetrator feel like the victim and justifies any retaliation.

5. Accept responsibility for consequences (v.22). David does not
   deflect blame. He says plainly: "I have occasioned the death."
   This is the mark of a man after God's heart — not sinlessness but
   honest accountability. When your actions contribute to harm, own
   it. Confession is the beginning of restoration.
